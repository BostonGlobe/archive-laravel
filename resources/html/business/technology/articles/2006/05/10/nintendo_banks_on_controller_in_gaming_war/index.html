Nintendo banks on controller in gaming war

By Hiawatha Bray, Globe Staff  |  May 10, 2006

LOS ANGELES -- Executives from the Japanese video game company Nintendo turned the stage of the Kodak Theater into a tennis court yesterday, simply by waving their arms.

It was the coming-out party for the Wii, (pronounced wee) Nintendo's new video game console designed as the company's response to Microsoft Corp.'s Xbox 360 and the upcoming PlayStation 3 from Japan's Sony Computer Entertainment, all of which are being heavily promoted here at E3, the world's largest computer game trade show.

The Wii uses a game controller that's almost as unusual as its name to set itself apart. Nintendo -- with the help of Norwood's Analog Devices Inc. -- abandoned the standard one-piece video game controller for a two-piece setup that lets people play games by moving the controller in any direction.

Nintendo executive vice president Reggie Fils-Aime said inexperienced gamers would find it easy to control a game by making simple, intuitive arm movements, instead of having to master a host of pushbuttons and joysticks.

If it catches on with consumers, the motion-sensitive game controller could mean the end of ''couch potato" gaming, as software writers create games that force players to throw punches, swing swords or stroke a few well-placed backhand shots. It could also mean lots of new business for Analog Devices, which makes the ''micromechanical" chip that drives the Wii controller.

The chip is an ''accelerometer," a device that measures speed and direction of movement. Analog Devices produces accelerometer chips at its plant in Cambridge and sells millions of them to the world's automakers, which use them to detect car crashes and instantly deploy the car's airbags. The chips are also used in some cellphones in Japan to measure how far the user has walked, based on movements of the body.

William Giudice, vice president and general manager of Analog Devices' micromachined products division, said some games for the original Nintendo Game Boy had a similar motion control feature built in. But they were not successful because it was hard to appreciate the motion effect on the low-quality screen of the first Game Boy.

But with a Wii console plugged into a high-definition TV monitor, it was easy to see the impact of the Analog Devices chip. Nintendo president Satoru Iwata and three of his colleagues used the Wii controller to play a spirited doubles match of tennis. Their feet rooted to the stage, the four men flailed away with their arms, making a quartet of animated digital figures race around the court, swatting balls back and forth.

A software engineer for French game developer Ubisoft demonstrated the company's upcoming action game Red Steel, a violent action title that features gun battles and samurai sword fights. The player used the motion chip to pinpoint targets, but could instantly shift to samurai mode, slashing and jabbing with the game controller.

Sony sought to steal some thunder from Nintendo by disclosing on Monday that the controller for its upcoming PlayStation 3 console would also use motion-sensing technology. Giudice said that the news didn't take him by surprise, since Nintendo last year discussed its intention to offer the feature. ''This is a very competitive business," said Giudice. Analog does not supply motion sensing technology to Sony.

Sony says it will charge $499 for the PlayStation 3, or $599 for a model with a larger hard drive, when the machine goes on sale Nov. 17. Microsoft's Xbox 360, already available, costs $399 with a hard drive, or $299 for a driveless version. But yesterday Nintendo refused to reveal the Wii's price or release date. ''We will give you more fun for less money," Fils-Amie said, ''and you will be playing Wii in the fourth quarter of 2006."

The competition in the gaming arena was underscored yesterday when Microsoft Corp. chairman Bill Gates made his first appearance ever at the E3 computer trade show to announce Live Anywhere, a plan to create an integrated gaming network built around Microsoft's dominant Windows operating system. A Live Anywhere user with an Xbox video game console will be able to compete against someone else playing the same game on a desktop computer running Vista, the upcoming new version of Windows. Or he can use his Windows-based wireless phone to set up a match, purchase new Xbox software or download new game videos and accessories.

''Microsoft is probably the only company that could pull this off," said Gates. The surging popularity of video game consoles has led to the release of fewer games for Windows-based computers. Microsoft also announced a new initiative to encourage the development and marketing of Windows games. The company said it would push more simultaneous releases of new games on both the Xbox 360 and Windows computers.
