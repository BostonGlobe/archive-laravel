STEVE BAILEY

Gall in the family

By Steve Bailey, Globe Columnist, 9/10/2003

He is, as The New York Times once labeled him, the "Johnny Carson of the training circuit." John J. Donovan, part visionary, part showman, is considered an authority on the technology revolution, a man who preaches his gospel of change to top executives around the world. He has written seven books, been a tenured professor in engineering and business at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, and is a serial entrepreneur, whose start-ups have made him, if not the investors who followed, fabulously wealthy. He is a man with big-name admirers, and more than his share of enemies -- including former partners, colleagues, and investors who say he constantly promises more than he delivers.

Now add another group to the Donovan Enemies List: four of his five children.

In an ugly lawsuit that both sides threaten could get even uglier, four of Donovan's grown children have filed suit to enforce a March agreement that would ensure their control of trusts established for them by their father. There is big money at stake: The trusts include prime properties in Manchester, Essex, Ipswich, and Hamilton in Massachusetts and also in Vermont and Bermuda. The suit values the properties in the "tens of millions of dollars"; a lawyer for the children puts the number "well in excess of $60 million." That does not include the $18.5 million transferred to the children in November.

The kids from Donovan's first marriage, which ended more than 20 years ago -- James H. Donovan, Rebecca M. Brown, Maureen Donovan Lantz, and Carolyn Donovan Rosenbaum -- are in effect moving for a divorce from their dad. The question: Is John Donovan the father from hell who managed to alienate his children in the same way he did so many others in his business life, or are these simply spoiled rich kids trying to shake down their father for tens of millions of dollars they never earned? A court may eventually have to decide.

What is clear from documents filed in Suffolk Superior Court last week and available from lawyers is that something went terribly wrong in the Donovan family. Lawyers for the Donovan children -- the fifth and youngest child, John Jr., split with his siblings late last year -- say their clients simply want a final separation from their father. Donovan's team says he is being shaken down by his children, who have threatened to "ruin" him with false, personal allegations. The charges and demands run from the serious to the petty. Among the latter: The children, according to a summary prepared by a Donovan lawyer, asked that Donovan resign from the exclusive Myopia Hunt Club in Hamilton, where his oldest son is also a member. Donovan's current wife, Linda, "may attend events at Myopia Hunt Club if invited by a member. John may not set foot on Myopia for any purposes," says the memo.

"I'm terribly saddened by this dispute over money," Donovan, 61, said in a statement yesterday. "I set these funds aside to benefit my children and charities upon my passing. This is a father's worst nightmare come true." Said Nancy Sterling, a spokeswoman for Donovan's four unhappy children: "We are very hurt that our father has failed to honor the agreement that he made in the presence of counsel and in writing six months ago. The agreement requires him to give a large amount of property to charity at the children's request."

The cover of one of his books, "The Second Industrial Revolution," outlines the remarkable resume that has made Donovan such a success in the business world. A founder of Cambridge Technology Partners and other companies; educated at Yale, Tufts and MIT; a teacher at MIT, Tufts, and Harvard. And then it adds this line: "He is the father of five wonderful children."

Four of those five kids wouldn't call dad so wonderful.
