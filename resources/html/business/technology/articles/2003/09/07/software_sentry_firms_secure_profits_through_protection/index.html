Software sentry firms secure profits through protection

By Hiawatha Bray, Globe Staff, 9/7/2003

For millions of personal computer users, the rising tide of Internet-borne software worms presents a constant threat of potential disaster.

But for such companies as Symantec Corp., Network Associates Technology Inc., and Zone Labs Inc., potential digital catastrophe means potential profits.

These firms, which make the security software designed to protect homes and businesses from Internet attack, have traditionally built their businesses on "enterprise" users, the kind who buy antivirus and firewall software for millions of corporate or government PCs. It had been much harder to convince Mr. and Mrs. America that their home machine needs just as much protection.

Not any more. The Blaster and SoBig attack programs, which clogged corporate and home computer networks worldwide, have gotten everybody's attention. And computer security software firms are looking to cash in.

"When there is ever a major virus outbreak there is a tremendous increase in consumer awareness," said Bill Kerrigan, senior vice president of McAfee Security, the unit of Network Associates that makes its home computer security products. "In response to the virus attacks, we saw a very heavy increase in demand for people to update their antivirus service."

And not just antivirus programs either. The Blaster worm could attack any Internet-connected computer through an unprotected data port, an identification point used to manage data traffic. This vulnerability made millions of ordinary users realize for the first time that antivirus scanning isn't enough. They also need firewall software, programmed to close all data ports not carrying approved Internet traffic. "For the home user and small business, it's really going to turn into a story about comprehensive security," said Vincent Weafer, senior director of Symantec's security response department.

The upshot, says industry analyst Brian Burke of IDC Corp. in Framingham, is strong demand among consumers for software to protect their personal machines. "Consumer spending on antivirus was one of the only markets that exceeded our estimates from 2001 to 2002," Burke said.

Consumers spent $650 million on antivirus software in 2002, up from $473 million in 2001. Symantec, based in Cupertino, Calif., earned a $248 million profit in fiscal 2003, which ended in March, from a $28 million net loss in fiscal year 2002. Network Associates, in Santa Clara, Calif., earned a $90 million profit last year, up from a $100 million loss in 2001. Meanwhile, personal firewall sales were $65.2 million in 2001, and IDC estimates that consumers bought $78.4 million worth last year.

This robust growth all came before this year's seemingly endless round of computer attacks. The big security software companies are betting that the fury and frustration caused by these infections will mean continued strong demand for their products.

Symantec is by far the largest seller of consumer antivirus products, with about 64 percent of the market, according to IDC Corp. Network Associates' McAfee antivirus unit trails far behind with about 16 percent. But both firms spend millions on laboratories, where experts dismantle the latest pieces of malware, to find ways to disinfect tainted computers, and prevent future infections. Most of this information is made available for free on the companies' websites, along with emergency programs for cleaning infected computers.

In addition, the labs identify unique patterns in the malware's digital code. These patterns, or signatures, can be added to the database of known malware signatures and relayed to users of antivirus programs. Users of the company's antivirus software can download the latest signatures to protect themselves against new threats.

Here's where the companies make their money. The signatures only work for those who've bought the antivirus software package, which usually costs around $40. This buys the user one year of virus signature updates. To keep his machine protected, he must renew the antivirus subscription annually.

It makes for a nice, steady revenue stream -- if you can get consumers to pay it. IDC's Burke says many new computers purchased at retail include an introductory antivirus package, which allows the customer to sign up for future updates. According to Burke, only about 10 percent of them do. "Consumers are under a false sense of security," said Burke. But he adds that the signup rate has been increasing in recent months, because the fear of viruses is finally starting to sink in.

The malware boom has also meant boom times at the antivirus labs. When Vincent Cullotto, vice president of McAfee's antivirus emergency response team, joined the company in 1998, the team had five employees in two countries. Today, there are 90 in eight countries. Symantec and other antivirus firms make similar investments.

McAfee has 35 technicians who study viruses full time. Some are submitted by infected customers; some are found during regular visits to clandestine websites created by virus writers. Still other samples come from competing antivirus companies like Symantec or Sophos LLC. "We want to alert other folks and pass on samples to them," said Cullotto. While the virus hunters compete for business, they generally work together to smash the latest infections quickly.

Antivirus firms are also developing ways to block malware as soon as it appears. It's called behavioral analysis, and it's based on the idea that bad software will always try to do something suspicious. "We look at what the program does, not what it is," said Vincent Weafer of Symantec. For instance, the SoBig worm collects every e-mail address stored on the machine, and then tries to send copies of itself to these addresses. An antivirus program with behavioral analysis would identify this as inappropriate behavior and send a warning to the computer user. "If we notice something is trying to send out 50 e-mails in 10 seconds, a red flag goes up." said Bryson Gordon, senior product manager for McAfee consumer antivirus products. Even if the antivirus program lacked the correct signature to detect SoBig, the program's behavior would give it away.

Firewall vendors are also beefing up their offerings. Privately held Zone Labs Inc. of San Francisco, which makes a popular line of firewalls called Zone Alarm, made a name for itself by giving away a basic version of the product for home use. "It's been good for us, because a lot of people gain trust in us," said Zone Labs marketing vice president Fred Felman.

Zone Labs hopes its users will pay for more advanced versions of the product, such as the $60 Zone Alarm Pro 4. This version blocks annoying Web page popups and filters out offensive Internet content, along with the usual firewall functions.

The rise of high-speed always-on home Internet connections has made security more vital than ever. The major security software companies see another golden opportunity in alliances with broadband providers. McAfee, for instance, provides antivirus scanning services for users of America Online and Microsoft's MSN online services. It also runs special promotions with cable modem providers Comcast and Cox Communications to provide users with free firewall software for one year.

Zone Labs says it's in discussions with other Internet providers to offer its firewall software as part of a customer security package. Zone Labs' Felman says it's not an easy sell, because broadband companies don't want to be pestered by customers asking how to set up their firewalls. So his company must come up with software that works so smoothly that customers will barely know it's there. "It's a tough balance," said Felman. "It requires a very carefully crafted product."

But with millions of users threatened by Internet attack, Zone Labs and its rivals have plenty of reasons to get it right.
