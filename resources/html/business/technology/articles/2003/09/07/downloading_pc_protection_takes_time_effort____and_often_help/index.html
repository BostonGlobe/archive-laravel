Downloading PC protection takes time, effort -- and often help

By Peter J. Howe, Globe Staff, 9/7/2003

For computer owners terrified of such "worms" as SoBig and Blaster, Microsoft Corp.'s website promises a simple three-step process to "help ensure your PC is protected."

ADVERTISEMENT

Jay Fortin tripped on step one. Lead guitarist with the "acid mountain rock" band Scissorfight, Fortin was just beginning to download a software "patch" to protect the computer at his wife's Fenway T-shirt printing shop, NE Design, when an ominous warning popped up on the screen: "This type of file could harm your computer if it contains malicious code."

Sound familiar? It should. The warning is of a kind that snags almost every PC user -- from novice to MIT electrical engineering PhD graduate -- at some point. In the past you could try to ignore it, reboot and hope the mysterious onscreen presence went away. But as tens of millions of PC owners across the country struggle to deal -- often on their own -- with waves of new worms and viruses spread by cyber-sociopaths, they're being told the only way to cope is to download some protection.

The result: Fortin has plenty of company in finding the whole process an overwhelming headache.

"This thing is not too reassuring," said Fortin as he let a Globe photographer and reporter watch him grapple with the software download procedure to protect against recent "worm" programs (which spread to other computers through e-mail addresses, but don't damage other programs as a virus does).

"This does not make me comfortable if you are getting this from Microsoft's own website. I think this is where I would stop, right here."

Nor did he feel much better about following through on downloading a dozen Windows updates that a pop-up window from Microsoft's site tells him his machine needs after he has it scan the PC. Many of the updates, he said, sound "weird and confusing" and would require him to shut down the computer and restart it.

A Microsoft spokesman said that while specific figures have not been released, "tens and tens of millions of people were able to successfully download" the patch against this summer's Blaster worm. The spokesman said the warning Fortin got about "malicious code" was almost certainly triggered by his computer having its Internet Explorer "default" security setting at what is essentially the most paranoid level, even for software from the same company that makes Explorer.

"That's not to say that certain people don't have problems, but there hasn't been any widespread issue, and for people who have the automatic update feature for Windows, it's a fairly painless process," the Microsoft spokesman said.

Many consumers who just want to use a computer for e-mail and Web surfing have little interest in, or aptitude for, keeping up with any high-tech hygiene practices. But spending on software and devices to protect computers from attacks is soaring. All it takes is a small minority of unprotected Net surfers and e-mailers to create the electronic equivalent of a petri dish to incubate ongoing waves of viruses, worms, Trojan horses, and other malicious software havoc.

"There is a lot you have to remember to do," said Craig Rabe, owner of The Computer Cafe in Arlington, a PC dealer that operates a public Net access service as well.

Rabe advises customers who are serious about protecting themselves to complete a long checklist of precautions: Get and install all the software updates recommended by Microsoft, whose Windows software operates around 90 percent of all PCs, including the service that can automatically transmit software fixes to your PC in the middle of the night. Install an anti-virus software program that regularly updates itself and is programmed to sweep the machine once a week. Schedule a regular backup session for all the data stored on your computer. Use a device called a router -- a kind of Internet data traffic cop, a simple version of which can cost about $100 -- between your computer and broadband modem for extra protection.

Even with all those precautions in place, Rabe said, "Do I feel completely protected against this worm thing? Not really."

Christine Heckart, marketing vice president with Juniper Networks, a Silicon Valley network equipment maker with an office in Westford, said, "It's pathetic that it's still this hard for people to protect themselves, 20 years after the personal computer has become a mass-market phenomenon. This is the kind of thing that should be done by your Internet service provider automatically. Your machine is already connected to the network, so it should be totally transparent. I think a lot of people would be totally happy to pay $5 a month to just have this done automatically and not have to bother with it."

Kathleen McDermott, a fashion historian and instructor at the Massachusetts College of Art who lives in Boston's Bay Village section, copes with the mystifying drudgery needed to keep her computer secure through McAfee PCSecurity software, an $80 annual subscription from Network Associates Inc. "All I do is what the McAfee software tells me to do," McDermott said. "I have no idea whether it really protects me, but I just do what they say. I download and I scan."

McDermott said she also will "never, ever, ever open an e-mail from anybody I don't know. That's religion with me."

But, she said, following through on the seemingly weekly updates on what patches to install and software to upgrade holds no appeal. "I'm totally untechie. I'm an artist. There's enough to do in life without chasing around every last technological hiccup You would drive yourself nuts," McDermott said. "You would spend your whole life on the computer, and that's not what life is all about."

Robert M. Krim of Newton Centre, editor of an upcoming history of 400 years of innovations born in Boston called "Innovation Odyssey," has no fewer than four graduate degrees, including an MBA-PhD from Boston College. But when he decided last month he had to get serious about protecting his home computer from the latest epidemic, Krim called for help, in the form of Network Solutions, a computer service consulting firm in Dorchester.

With a wife who works long hours as a physician and two children ages 3 and 5, Krim said, "For me to get 15 or 20 minutes alone to do this would almost require hiring a babysitter. I've learned to know what I can do well and where I have problems, and this is one of these times when I can't be self-reliant," Krim said.

While he doesn't rule out the possibility he could figure out how to get through the steps of upgrading his system, Krim said the risks of doing something wrong are too high. Life as they know it in the Krim household would not exist if he couldn't use the computer to pay bills and order online groceries, and "wiping out my address book is worth more than $50" -- the cost of a visit from a Network Solutions technician -- not to mention the hassle of having to reload dozens of favorite "bookmarked" websites if an upgrade scrubs them out.

Those who have gotten in the habit of installing and regularly using antivirus software seem to have a leg up. Nate Walker, owner of El Pelon Taqueria on Peterborough Street in Boston, had his restaurant's computer "bombarded" with 200 infected e-mails last month. But he has been a longtime user of Norton antivirus software, which he has programmed to check the machine every week, and an "online" tutorial he went through showed in a clear and understandable way how to protect himself against SoBig and Blaster.

"One of the things I'm always worried about is people you know who are not aware of these viruses, and they're sending you these attachments" that may or may not have operating-system landmines embedded in them, Walker said. "I've had my grandmother, my aunt, other people forward me all sorts of stuff, and you just don't know what it is."

Just not knowing what you're going to get was what ultimately led Fortin, the Scissorfight guitarist, to give up a few minutes into his hunt for worm and virus patches and leave his computer as protected -- or unprotected -- as it was before he started. "To me it doesn't look like this is going where I thought it would go, and it's talking about all these Windows updates instead of security patches," Fortin said. "I get confused looking at all this stuff."
