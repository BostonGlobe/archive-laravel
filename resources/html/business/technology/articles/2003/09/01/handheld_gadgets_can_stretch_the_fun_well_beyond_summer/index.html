AT PLAY

Handheld gadgets can stretch the fun well beyond summer

By Eddie Medina, Globe Correspondent, 9/1/2003

Summer's over, a stark fact that stings like the first cold day in August. But aside from warm, sunny days at the beach, what exactly is summer besides a really great excuse to totally goof off? And there's probably a pretty decent time-waster in your pocket right now.

You see, the summer doesn't have to really end, so long as you have a mobile device in your pocket. Simple devices like a Nextel iDen mobile phone or a Palm hand-held computer can play a variety of games.

Most phones purchased in the last two years use some form of programming language -- like Symbian or Java -- that can run small game programs, and most hand-held computers can too. Of course, we're not talking about Tomb Raider here. (At least until the Nokia N-Gage debuts; read on.) We're talking about distraction on the go, not total immersible gaming experience.

Turning these devices into, ahem, a mobile gaming platform requires very little besides a Web connection. And any Java-enabled phone (one that understands a form of the Java programming language) can play games. Nextel's iDen models, for example, can play these games, and so can personal digital assistants.

Simply visit a number of sites to download a game, connect your phone to your computer via a wire usually purchased from a manufacturer, and transfer the game to your phone using a software program provided by the manufacturer. Some sites require a small fee per download, but it's usually under $5. The games -- like Copter, Missiles, and Snake -- have two-dimensional graphics, may or may not have color, and resemble Nintendo 64 classics. Some good sites to visit include: Americas.Soft

wareMarket.com (for Nokia phones), www.iDenUpdate.com (for Nextel phones), FreePalm

Ware.com (for Palm hand-helds), Handango.com (for many platforms including the Palm OS, Symbian phones and RIM devices), PhoneGames4U.com, The

MobileGamer.com, PartyMob

.com, and PocketGamer.org or PocketGear.com (for Microsoft Pocket PC hand-held computers). Mobile gaming, however, isn't necessarily restricted to downloadable software that players interact with on a human-vs.-machine level. If you own a geographic position system, or GPS, device, the world is your game level.

Geocaching players use GPS devices to find treasure, usually a novelty or a logbook buried by somebody else for fun. The high-tech scavenger hunters head to the Web, find the location of a hidden cache, then go looking for it with nothing but a set of coordinates and maybe a little description.

There are many caches hidden around Boston: in the Arnold Arboretum, for example, or in Middlesex Fells, a large park north of the city. Just visit a website with some geocache coordinates, like Geocaching.com or NE.Geocaching.com. Malden residents Kevin and Susan Stephens expanded their interest in hiking and climbing into geocaching. The pair have pursued geocaches in St. John's Island in the Caribbean, Kansas, New Hampshire, Maine, Boston, Colorado, and Florida. For the husband and wife, geocaching is a way to discover insider locales.

"When we first started doing it, we did the ones within a 20- or 30-minute drive. . . . Then we started doing the more obscure ones when we went on vacation," said Kevin.

"One of the best ones was in St. John's," he said. "This beautiful bay called Rendezvous Bay. You follow this road down, then you follow it between two houses. The coordinates were at the edge of this walkway, and it turned out to be this edge of a beach . . . this gorgeous spot. That's why when we go on vacation, I take two or three [geocache coordinates] with me."

In October, Nokia will take mobile gaming to the next level with its N-Gage hand-held gaming system, and yes, game fans, Lara Croft and her tomb-raiding exploits will star in one of the debut games.

Rather than simply traveling with a device (like a GPS) or downloading games to a gadget that happens to be portable, the N-Gage will give players true gaming mobility. Besides 3-D animation, a telephone, an MP3 player, and a recorder, the N-Gage will allow players to compete head to head wirelessly and download new software over wireless networks.

The system uses MMC cards instead of cartridges like Nintendo's Game Boy Advance, and will launch with titles like Tony Hawk, Red Faction, and Sega's Sonic the Hedgehog.

Nokia's foray into the hand-held game hardware market marks the first of a series of challenges to Nintendo's dominance of the hand-held gaming industry. (Sony's first mobile gaming system, the PSP, will debut in a year and will use a proprietary optical media.) The competition should result in better games with mobility in mind, more options for developers, and a larger, more lucrative market for the rest of the industry. If the N-Gage and Sony's hand-held are everything they promise to be, turning phones and PDAs into gaming devices may just become so-last-summer.
