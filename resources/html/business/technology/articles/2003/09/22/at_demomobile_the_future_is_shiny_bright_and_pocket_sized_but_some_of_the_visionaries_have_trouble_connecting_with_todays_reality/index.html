At DemoMobile the future is shiny, bright and pocket-sized, but some of the visionaries have trouble connecting with today's reality

By Chris Gaither, Globe Staff, 9/22/2003

LA JOLLA, Calif. -- The talking dog stole the show. With camera phones just starting to catch on in the United States, wireless providers looking for new services to sell might want to look at Mobile Veepers, by Pulse Entertainment Inc. in San Francisco.

ADVERTISEMENT

The nifty application takes recorded speech and digital photos of people -- or pets, as Pulse executives demonstrated at the DemoMobile trade show here last week -- then blends them into a moving, talking head that can be e-mailed to cellphones

running the RealOne player. Mobile Veepers, which launches as a subscription service in Japan in November but has no US backers yet, was one of the more fascinating, if frivolous, technologies that had people buzzing at DemoMobile.

If the 1980s and 1990s were about the personal computer, this decade is about expanding mobile computing. Though far from complete, the groundwork for fast wireless networks has been laid, from WiFi to Bluetooth to next-generation cellular technologies like CDMA and GPRS. That leaves consumers and businesses trying to figure out what to do with all this connectivity -- and the communications industries scrambling to make money off it.

The 37 companies introducing products at DemoMobile have some ideas about that. They introduced new hand-held computers, pocket-sized printers, servers for sharing digital music and other files from the home PC, and software for remotely accessing corporate data.

Many of the products could fail. In an illustration of a few of the problems that consumers and businesses face, the conference was held in the lower levels of a hotel that had virtually no reception for most cellular networks, wreaking havoc for companies trying to prove how quickly their software downloaded e-mail onto mobile phones. And the patchwork of wireless technologies running in the conference hall also created such interference for some presenters that one executive started his demonstration by announcing, "In case the gods of DemoMobile don't shine upon us, our plan B is to show you how you can kill a bottle of vodka anywhere, at any time."

But many of the presenting companies overcame the technical glitches to show off their vision of using wireless networks.

Realeyes3D, another service being considered for tests by foreign carriers, lets people shoot photos of handwritten messages, then overlay that over a photo taken by a camera phone and send digital postcards to friends' phones.

Heads Up, a service by Dynamic Mobile Data, sends an automated phone call, e-mail, or text message when a repair technician is a predetermined amount of time from your home, reducing some of the stress of hearing, "We'll be there sometime between noon and 6."

Following the tradition of launching new hand-held computers at DemoMobile, as Palm Inc. and others have since it began in 1999, several companies introduced new pocket-sized hardware.

The Zodiac, by Tapwave Inc., runs Palm's operating system for personal digital assistants, but adds stereo sound, slots for memory cards, and buttons like a video-game controller to turn the gadget into a hand-held game and entertainment player. Without a retail distributor yet, Tapwave plans to sell the device, which costs $299, or $399 for more memory, through its website.

Antelope Technologies Inc. has built what it calls a Modular Computing Core -- a 3x5 inch device wielding the power of a PC. It has no screen or data entry system, but Antelope says the core, which is powered by a Transmeta Corp. processor and runs Windows XP, can hook into a docking station, a tablet-sized screen, or other frames so users can change their computer to suit their needs.

"Now you've got something very small, very compact, that can become anything you want it to become," said Antelope president Kenneth Geyer.

Although computers have gotten smaller and more mobile, most printers have not. Brother International Ltd. introduced a pocket-sized printer for receipts, tiny maps, and other documents needed on the go. It connects to phones, hand-held computers, or PCs through cables or Bluetooth, a short-range wireless technology.

Some of the hardware at the conference isn't itself mobile but contributes to the freedom of its users. Ispiri Inc., of Mountain View, Calif., attempts to solve the frustrating problem of how to retrieve digital files when you're away from your computer and makes it easier to share digital photos and documents with friends.

Its product, the Mirra, functions like a home server. It finds new or updated files on any computer on a home network and copies them onto its hard drive of either 80 gigabytes, for $399, or 120 gigabytes, for $499. Travelers can download those files from any computer with an Internet browser and decide which files to let friends download. Ispiri, too, lacks a distributor, and will sell the Mirra in mid-October on its website.

Another home device comes from a team with experience creating technology that changes the way people watch television. Anthony Wood, a creator of one of the first digital video recorders, ReplayTV, has started a new company, Roku. Its first product, available for $499 at Tweeter and Amazon.com early next month, brings digital photos, artwork, and music to high-definition TVs by connecting them wirelessly to the PC.

Hand-held computers have been available for a decade, but companies are still trying to figure out how to use more than the calendar and address-book functions to improve employee productivity.

The creators of Nantucket Nectars, Tom First and Tom Scott, started a company in Cambridge, Mass., to help salespeople on the road who are equipped with hand-helds running Microsoft Corp.'s Pocket PC operating system. Eleven Technology Inc., once called Shelflink, makes software that lets PepsiCo Inc., for example, beam instructions telling its beverage deliverers who to meet with, how many cases of soda to order, and which promotions to pitch. Other customers include Procter & Gamble Co. and Pepperidge Farm Inc.

"We're trying to help companies figure out what matters for their mobile work force," chief executive Tim Curran said in an interview at the show.

But a surge in devices also creates more headaches for corporate technology departments. AirPrism Inc., a Silicon Valley start-up, introduced software that the company says helps businesses manage their fleet of mobile gadgets, troubleshoot, and fix problems connecting those devices to networks.

The application also locks the devices remotely if they get lost. After all, with so many gadgets floating around these days -- from e-mail pagers to PDAs to cellphones -- it's easy for sensitive data to fall into the wrong hands.
