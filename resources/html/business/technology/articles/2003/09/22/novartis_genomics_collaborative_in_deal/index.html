Novartis, Genomics Collaborative in deal

By Jeffrey Krasner, Globe Staff, 9/22/2003

To help speed its drug-discovery program, giant Novartis Pharma AG of Switzerland has turned to a small, private Cambridge biotech firm with a unique collection of blood and tissue samples from 120,000 individuals.

Novartis and Genomics Collaborative Inc. will first collaborate on research involving adult-onset diabetes during the two-year agreement. The firms will also investigate up to three other unidentified diseases and have an option to extend the research partnership for an additional two years.

GCI said it will receive a payment from Novartis of $1 million $10 million, but declined to be more specific. The company could also receive additional milestone payments, depending on the success of the research.

The research collaboration is one of a growing number of deals between Novartis and local biotech firms and academic institutions since Novartis moved its worldwide research headquarters to Cambridge last year. The Novartis Institutes for BioMedical Research Inc. has undertaken an aggressive campaign to seek out local technologies and discoveries that can help it in its drug-discovery effort.

Earlier this month, for instance, ArQule Inc. of Woburn announced a deal under which ArQule would apply proprietary chemistry techniques to help the pharmaceutical firm in its efforts to develop small molecules with potential therapeutic benefit. Terms weren't disclosed.

Jeremy Levin, global head of strategic alliances for the Novartis Institutes, said the research arm is working toward many further agreements.

"Next year, you'll see a specific focus on an innovative type of deal that will build ourselves more deeply into the biotech and academic communities," Levin said. "The message is we're very interested in doing transactions in this geographic area. We're seeking partnerships."

Novartis has said it will invest $250 million and bring as many as 1,000 jobs to Cambridge as it moves its research arm into space at Technology Square and the former Necco building on Massachusetts Avenue. Over the next 10 years, the company plans to invest $4 billion in the Boston area.

GCI hopes to help Novartis with one of the central problems facing the drug industry: Despite scientific breakthroughs such as the mapping of the human genome in 2001, the rate of development of new drugs is largely stagnant. That leaves big pharmaceutical firms scrambling to fill their product pipelines with new, more effective drugs.

Meantime, the cost of drug development continues to rise. In a study released two years ago, the Tufts University Center for Study of Drug Development pegged the average cost of bringing a new prescription drug to market at $802 million. Much of that funding goes toward clinical trials, in which drug companies test their products on human patients in carefully controlled experiments. Despite the expense and the years involved, the vast majority of drugs that undergo clinical tests fail to reach approval.

GCI's approach is intended to help Novartis focus on the most promising drug candidates, thereby eliminating some of the targets that would ultimately fail in clinical trials.

Novartis will provide GCI with up to 100 genes that it believes play a role in adult onset, or Type II, diabetes. In evaluating a single gene, GCI will examine its database of DNA from thousands of patients with the disease, and a control group of DNA healthy individuals. Researchers will look for mutations within that gene. A marked difference in the frequency of mutations between the healthy patients and those with diabetes would suggest that the gene in question plays an important role in the disease. That gene would presumably become a priority for Novartis's development efforts.

GCI's genetic screening "allows you to discriminate among the many hundreds of drug targets you have," said Levin. "It's a more efficient way of experimenting."

Dr. Michael Pellini, GCI's president and chief executive, said his firm's screening process can help save time.

"The science will begin Oct. 1," he said. "It's not going to take months to recruit patients for a clinical trial. We'll have results in one year instead of three or four years."

Founded in 1998, GCI has 30 employees and has raised about $57 million in venture capital funding so far. Pellini said he expects the firm to achieve positive cash on an operating basis by the middle of next year. Earlier on, the company had sought to develop its own drugs to treat cardiovascular disease, colon cancer, and inflammatory ailments.

Given the crushing slowdown in biotech over the past two years, the firm has suspended its own drug development program and is instead concentrating on research collaborations such as the one with Novartis.

"We took a longer-term business plan and shortened it," said Pellini. "We know what we're good at.

"If we were still focused on drug discovery, we wouldn't reach break even for many more years."
