Laptops dropping in cost, weight

New Intel technology allows lighter batteries, with no loss of speed

By John P. Mello Jr., 9/8/2003

Mobility isn't the primary requirement for many notebook computer users, but for those who need to travel greater distances than from living room to home office, the arrival of the Centrino portables is an event to be celebrated. Toting these notebooks around won't break your back, and they don't shed their weight at the cost of performance.

Centrino is the name Intel has given to its technology aimed specifically at notebooks. The technology includes a speedy processor, the Pentium M; the 855 chip set; and wireless hardware called the Intel PRO/Wireless 2100 Network Connection. A true Centrino device carries all three elements of the technology. Units with just the processor and chip set usually carry the Pentium M label.

The idea underlying Centrino is to provide notebook users with more computing power while consuming less juice from a unit's battery. Intel does that with a sophisticated technology for controlling the power consumption of the processor and system bus in a computer.

A recent entry into the Centrino arena is the Gateway 450, which crams an assortment of attractive features into a package that sells for $2,000.

When designing a Centrino notebook, a hardware maker can tap-dance around the weight of the unit. Heavier batteries that provided 2 1/2 to three hours of power for older notebooks can provide five to six hours with Centrino. But extended battery life can be traded off for lighter batteries. The result is a lighter notebook with power consumption in the same range as older, heavier units.

The 450 uses a lighter battery with a time between charges of two to three hours, pretty average for a notebook. However, Gateway includes a second battery with the 450 that slips into a modular bay on the side of the unit. Installation is fuss-free. Flip two latches and the optical drive detaches from the notebook. Pull it out and slip in the second battery. That will extend your power resources, albeit reduce your optical drive capabilities.

The 450 is a slick-looking machine, silver on the outside and dark gray on the inside. Basic models sport a ravishing and spacious 15-inch LCD -- almost a full inch larger than the base units of its competitors. The display seems even larger at its native resolution of 1,400-by-1,050 pixels, and its vibrant 32-bit colors pop from the screen.

While relatively large at 10.25-by-12.75-by-1.25 inches, its rounded edges give it a sleek demeanor. Its keyboard is fully stocked. There's no skimping on the size of the tab and right shift key. Nor is there any economizing in other annoying ways. Cursor keys are configured in the traditional inverted T. Home, PgUp, PgDn and End are standalone keys, not shifted ones as can be found in some notebooks.

Several shifted functions are more conveniently located than in most laptops. Brightness controls are on cursor keys, for example, and volume controls for the unit are on the Home, PgUp and PgDn keys. There's also shifted access to the battery status for the machine as well as the familiar power icon in the Windows task tray. In addition, there are quick access buttons above the function keys for launching an e-mail program, web browser, system help, and "My Computer," a frequently consulted component of Windows.

The unit has all the requisite ports you'd expect in a notebook of this caliber. It has two USB 2.0 ports, as well as PS/2, parallel, serial, and monitor ports. The unit's FireWire port uses four pins instead of six, as are found on Apple computers. This can be annoying if you want to plug a device without a power supply into the port. In addition, it has jacks for an Ethernet connection, headphones, dial-up modem, and output to a TV, as well as a PC Card slot.

The 450 comes with a solid assortment of programs. For productivity, it has Microsoft Works, which includes a full version of MS Word for word processing. For music, it has Gateway's Rhapsody software, MS Media Player, and MusicMatch jukebox. However, you'll want to listen to music through the unit's earphones jack and not through its speakers, which sound thin and reedy. For DVD playing, it includes WinDVD4. What's more, it packages Microsoft's encyclopedia, Encarta; Roxio CD Creator 5, for burning CDs; and Quicken and MS Money, for managing finances.

If you're looking for mobility and power in a graceful Centrino notebook, the Gateway 450 will meet your needs.
