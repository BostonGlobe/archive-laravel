Microsoft to repay state nearly $1m

Judge orders fees in antitrust case to be reimbursed

By Chris Gaither, Globe Staff, 9/23/2003

A federal judge in Washington yesterday ordered Microsoft Corp. to repay Massachusetts nearly $1 million for legal fees from the state's antitrust case against the software giant.

But in a sharp criticism of Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly's office, Judge Colleen Kollar-Kotelly said the state could have received as much as $323,000 more if its attorneys had kept better records of the time they spent litigating the case.

The award of $967,014, which will be put into the state's general fund, concludes nearly a year of legal wrangling over how much Microsoft should reimburse Massachusetts, the only state left seeking tougher antitrust remedies against Microsoft.

Reilly's office had requested $2.01 million to cover lawyers' fees and expenses. Microsoft agreed to reimburse Massachusetts, but when the state submitted its bill, the software company asked Kollar-Kotelly to dismiss most of the requested fees. Microsoft, based in Redmond, Wash., argued that it should not be required to pay for antitrust claims that the state was unable to prove, or for attorney hours not properly accounted for.

The judge agreed. In a 63-page ruling issued yesterday, she awarded Massachusetts less than half the amount it had sought.

The state had made seven antitrust claims against Microsoft, spanning the markets for personal-computer operating systems, Internet browsers, e-mail programs, and office productivity software.

Judge Thomas Penfield Jackson ruled in 2000 that Microsoft was an illegal monopolist, but only in PC operating systems. A federal appeals court the following year overturned his order to break up the company, but it upheld his finding that Microsoft had used its monopoly to bully competitors.

Last November Kollar-Kotelly approved a less restrictive Justice Department settlement with Microsoft. Eight other dissenting states dropped their opposition, but Massachusetts appealed the settlement.

Accepting Microsoft's argument, Kollar-Kotelly yesterday denied all reimbursement claims not related to the state's successful claim that Microsoft had monopolized PC operating systems.

She also criticized Massachusetts for failing to keep track of expenses and attorney hours as they were incurred. She threw out $322,721 for bad bookkeeping.

The state's "haphazard and incomplete invocation of Massachusetts law required a greater expenditure of judicial resources than one would have expected, given the fact that Plaintiff is the Commonwealth of Massachusetts and represented by its Attorney General's Office," the judge wrote.

Had the attorney general's office kept track of its hours as it went along, or offered the court more detailed descriptions of the activities it sought reimbursement for, she added, "the Court's consideration of this petition would have been much less taxing and Plaintiff likely would be more satisfied with the outcome."

Assistant attorney general Glenn Kaplan said public prosecutors don't bill clients directly, so they don't keep records of their time that are as detailed as those of private litigators.

"We provided the level of documentation and type of record keeping that is required by the Massachusetts state law," he said.

Nevertheless, the state stands to receive far more money than it spent on the case in expenses and attorney salaries, Kaplan said. Federal law allowed the state to receive the prevailing fee for private antitrust attorneys in Washington -- about $400 an hour.

"We're pleased with the decision, which fully reimburses and covers all expenses, time, and outlays that Massachusetts put into prosecuting this case," Kaplan said.

The state and Microsoft are due in federal court Nov. 4 to begin oral arguments on the state's appeal of the Justice Department settlement.
