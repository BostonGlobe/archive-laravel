Verizon, unions OK tentative 5-year pact

Verizon, union leaders reach tentative 5-year pact

By Peter J. Howe, Globe Staff, 9/5/2003

After more than five weeks of intense negotiations, Verizon Communications Inc. and unions representing 78,000 of its East Coast workers said last night they had forged a tentative agreement for a new five-year contract.

The deal offers a modest wage increase of at least 10.6 percent over five years, while maintaining key job security and health insurance provisions for Verizon members of the Communications Workers of America and the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers.

Verizon workers hired before last month would continue to have a no-layoff contract clause and a health insurance plan with no weekly premiums, offset by some increases in deductibles and copayments for medical care and prescription drugs. The pact keeps a prior cap on job shifting by Verizon, addressing union concerns about wholesale relocation of work outside of higher-wage Northeastern states by the nation's largest phone firm.

"This agreement is, at its core, a triumph for trade unionism in the telecommunications industry," said IBEW international president Edwin D. Hill, claiming solid union victories on job security, health insurance, and pay and pension gains.

Verizon president Lawrence T. Babbio, who led company negotiations in Washington, said, "This landmark agreement is fair for employees and at the same time helps Verizon remain competitive in these very challenging times." Babbio said having a five-year agreement, instead of what historically have been three-year pacts for Bell System phone companies, "provides stability and certainty for both the company and our employees," staving off the threat of strikes through 2008.

Hill, Babbio, CWA president Morton Bahr and other negotiators praised the efforts of Peter J. Hurtgen, director of the Federal Mediation and Conciliation Service, who played a key role in resolving last fall's West Coast port workers' strike and who brought together Verizon and union leaders for talks starting in late July.

The prior three-year contract for about 60,000 CWA members and 18,000 IBEW members in the Maine-to-Virginia region expired Aug. 2. While members of both unions voted by overwhelming majorities to authorize a strike, they agreed to continue working without a formal contract over the last month, even as Verizon spent tens of millions of dollars to prepare managers and replacement workers to fill in if the CWA and IBEW staged a work stoppage. The tentative contract pact being worked through last night is subject to ratification by union members, which appears certain.

Hurtgen commended the willingness of Verizon and labor leaders "to look for solutions to the tough issues." Noting that a strike could have cost $16 million a day in lost wages and disrupted telecommunications services throughout the Northeast, Hurtgen said, "I think everyone was mindful of the enormous stakes [and] recognized how vital it was to reach agreement."

A key obstacle to resolving contract talks for the 78,000 East Coast technicians, operators, and customer service agents was removed earlier this week when the CWA reached a tentative contract for 51 Verizon Wireless technicians in New York City. They are the only union members among 40,000 workers at Verizon Wireless, the nation's largest cellphone carrier and a fast-growing unit where the CWA hopes to recruit more members.

Under terms of the contract, union members would get an immediate cash bonus of 3 percent of pay, or an average $1,600, to be paid next month. Guaranteed base wage increases of 2 percent in 2004 through 2008 could be increased based on cost-of-living factors, leading to an overall five-year base wage increase of at least 10.6 percent.

Verizon said it will be able to save hundreds of millions of dollars in health insurance costs through increased deductibles and copayments and new arrangements such as preferred provider organizations that will help the phone company negotiate lower health cost increases.

However, union members will continue to be offered a health plan with no weekly premium, which the CWA and IBEW consider one of their marquee gains in years of negotiations. Maintaining no-premium health insurance was a key issue driving strikes in 2000 and 1989.

Among other elements:

The last contract's cap on job-shifting from collective bargaining districts will be maintained, allowing Verizon to move no more than 0.7 percent of jobs annually from state or metropolitan bargaining zones. Verizon had sought to raise that cap to as high as 8 percent, which unions feared would lead to wholesale relocation of jobs from the former Bell Atlantic Northeastern territory to lower-wage areas served by the former GTE Inc. in Texas, Idaho, and Florida. Bell Atlantic and GTE merged in 2000 to create Verizon.

Pension benefits will rise 11 percent over the five year pact. Verizon will offer a one-time 5 percent pension increase to workers agreeing to retire in the fourth quarter of this year, which can be taken as a lump-sum bonus, as an incentive to help Verizon reduce its workforce without layoffs.

Verizon, the CWA, and IBEW will agree to hold talks every April on issues of wage hikes and job security, with a goal of keeping company operating costs in line with the telecommunications service market and competition.
