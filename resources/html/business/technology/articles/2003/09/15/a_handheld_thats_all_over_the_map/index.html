A handheld that's all over the map

Marketed for office use, iQue 3600 handheld hits the trails

By Rachel Konrad, Associated Press, 9/15/2003

DESOLATION WILDERNESS, Calif. -- Before embarking on a three-day backpacking trip deep in the mountains, through nameless boulder fields and past glistening alpine lakes, my husband and I decided to introduce a new tool into our navigational repertoire.

In addition to detailed topological maps and our wizened canine, we brought the lightweight Garmin iQue 3600.

The $589 handheld computer, which looks like any other personal digital assistant, debuted in late July.

It includes every feature of a high-end handheld -- calendar and address book, and even a voice recorder and MP3 player -- and boasts sophisticated navigational equipment typically found in luxury automobiles.

The iQue, which uses the Palm 5.2.1 operating system, relies on satellites and the Global Positioning System to determine the user's precise location -- with an error margin of as little as 30 feet.

Its cooing female voice purrs turn-by-turn instructions between addresses in your Palm address book or any other "waypoint" you select as an interim or final destination.

The iQue is the first PDA with software and hardware -- including an elegant flip-up antenna -- preinstalled to take advantage of the Pentagon's satellite tracking network.

Garmin spokesman Pete Brumbaugh said the Olathe, Kan.-based company launched the iQue with a target audience of "traveling executives" -- real estate agents, insurance salespeople, or others who pine for a device slicker than folding maps.

Then Garmin noticed that outdoor enthusiasts -- mountaineers, hikers, kayakers, and backpackers -- were snapping up iQues at electronics retailers. So outdoors stores began stocking them.

The iQue's 320-by-480-pixel color display is far more readable than monochrome displays of the relatively bare-bones GPS computers popular with orienteers. I liked the overall setup of the iQue, a big step up from my spartan Palm Zire. Using the stylus also is easier than on older Palms. Write a letter, and the screen shows the shape you've traced -- almost like you're writing with an ink pen.

But the gadget isn't flawless.

The odometer and speedometer were spot on, but the altimeter always seemed slightly off. When we drove through Sacramento on the way to the Sierra Nevada, the iQue said we were 70 feet below sea level. In fact, Sacramento is about 30 feet above sea level.

Directions -- articulated with a ladylike murmur -- were generally good. But sometimes forks and overpasses fooled the MapSource software. The iQue would say misleading things such as "turn right," when in fact we had to bear left on a diverging stretch of Interstate 80 that eventually veered right.

Several times, the iQue labored or failed to pick up the three satellite signals needed to determine our location. The iQue seemed most troubled deep in the woods when the sky was obscured by trees.

The biggest problem happened on our last day in the wilderness. The iQue mysteriously developed a black, leaf-like mark near the center of its liquid crystal display, and the top two-thirds of the display didn't work.

We're not sure why the iQue died. We kept it in its padded carrying case and we never dropped it. We returned using maps and our dog's nose, which was eager to retrace our tracks.

Something in a backpack may have dinged the display, Brumbaugh later guessed, or it got wet. He emphasized that the intended market uses the iQue in a car or office.

For professionals who have to shuttle between appointments in more civilized locations, or anyone sick of using maps, the iQue is likely to catch on. But hard-core hikers might want to stick with a dedicated GPS unit -- or a motley assortment of maps and mutts.
