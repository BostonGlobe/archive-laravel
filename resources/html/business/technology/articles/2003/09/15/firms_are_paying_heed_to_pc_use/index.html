Firms are paying heed to PC use

Companies are paying attention to PC use

By Hiawatha Bray, Globe Staff, 9/15/2003

Here's a reminder for those of us who spend way too much time at work: Unless you happen to be self-employed, that computer on your desk doesn't belong to you. Seems obvious, until you consider the ways some people use their workplace computers -- viewing movie trailers, peeking at pornography, or playing online games. Officials at Microsoft's online gaming site, Zone

.com, say that their peak traffic rolls in at around 3 p.m. Boston time, or noon on the West Coast. In other words, the time of day when most of America is supposed to be working. There's something to be said for a midday game break, but one has to draw the line somewhere. Besides, there's a lot more than wasted time at stake. Companies that ignore their employees' Internet habits could end up on the short end of a lawsuit. How about a sexual harassment suit from an employee disgusted by the habits of the porn-surfer in the next cubicle?

That's nothing compared to the prospect of a lawsuit from the big music companies. Not content with suing 12-year-old girls for illegally downloading music, the Recording Industry Association of America also tracks illegal file-swapping occurring on business networks. The association has sent many a menacing letter to companies, including the Globe's corporate parent, the New York Times Co. The message is simple and blunt: Stop your workers from file-swapping, or else.

Then there's the oft-forgotten truth that there are no free lunches. Internet bandwidth costs money. When employees stay well within the capacity of a company's Internet link, a few extra downloads don't matter. But if enough workers spend the day playing checkers and listening to Rush Limbaugh on streaming audio, the pipeline fills up. Then the business can spend thousands on additional network capacity. Or it can crack down.

And so they have, with the help of programs that can track the way workers spend their time online or lock them out of certain portions of the Internet. According to computer industry researchers at IDC Corp., businesses bought $200 million worth of this Internet management software in 2001 and $270 million in 2002.

One of the leading makers of this software, Elron Software Inc. of Burlington, was recently acquired by Dallas-based Zix Corp. Sales director Steven Irons says that most businesses already have some amount of filtering to block out Internet smut sites.

Today, "organizations are concerned with employee productivity," said Irons. "How much resources are they taking up and how much time are these activities using?" That means tracking the amount of time workers spend on irrelevant Web surfing, and preventing massive downloads of illegal, bandwidth-wasting music files.

So the forthcoming version of Elron's Web Inspector software will contain new features to block specific types of data. MP3 music files, for instance, or the data packets sent by popular instant messaging programs. And of course, the software can be configured to identify which workers are attempting to get hold of forbidden data.

"Sometimes what we're doing is a little Big Brother," Irons said.

But these intrusive tactics can bring a major payoff. Tom Pecotte, server administrator for Auto Glass Specialists Inc. of Madison, Wis., ran a one-month test of Web Inspector software and was stunned by the results. Of 1.7 million website hits logged by employees, "less than 3,000 were hits on the pages that were the reason we got the Internet in the first place." The rest was random surfing by idle workers, scattered among 84 stores in seven states.

With help from the Zix Corp. software, Pecotte slashed the company's bandwidth usage, and dramatically sped up the firm's retail data network. "Some of the programs are running 50 times faster," Pecotte said.

With its potential to save companies a lot of money and legal embarrassment, network filtering products are an easy sell. But companies aren't as aggressive about using the products as you might think. Many companies fear alienating their employees if they get too intrusive.

"There's a number of companies, more than you would think, that don't want to filter anything," said Harold Kester, chief technology officer of filtering firm Websense Inc. "Anecdotally, I would guess that somewhere around 5 percent crack down a lot." The rest, say Kester, combine total blocking of porn and other dreadfuls with occasional monitoring of network usage to detect excessive casual surfing.

That might not be as lenient as it sounds. Would you prefer to have your midday visits to a gambling site to be automatically blocked? Or would you rather be allowed to go to the site, while the company's filtering server patiently logs your daily visits?

Maybe you should spend less time at the office, and do your Web viewing at home, on a machine that isn't looking back at you.
