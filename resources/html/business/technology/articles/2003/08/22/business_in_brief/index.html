Business in brief

By Globe Staff and Wires, 8/22/2003

THE REGION

StorageNetworks urged to reconsider

A group of funds managed by Gabelli Asset Management has urged StorageNetworks Inc. to revisit its liquidation plans. The Gabelli funds group, which holds a 6.16 percent stake in Waltham-based StorageNetworks, said it's "appalled" that the company expects to incur an "excessive" $34 million in costs as part of its liquidation plan, which was approved by StorageNetworks' board July 31. In a letter dated Monday, the group requested that StorageNetworks pay a distribution to shareholders, provide the Gabelli group with representation on the liquidation board, and account for the estimates of costs involved in settling liabilities and liquidating. (Dow Jones)

Lawsuit filed over Ascential buyout offer

Mercator Software Inc., whose products link business computer programs, was sued by a shareholder who contends the company may be worth more than a $3-per-share cash offer from Ascential Software Corp. Mercator said Aug. 4 that it would be bought by Westborough-based Ascential for about $106 million. In a Delaware Chancery Court lawsuit, Mercator shareholder Anthony Kolton says the company had five potential suitors and didn't release enough information about other offers. While Wilton, Conn.-based Mercator said in a regulatory filing that adviser J.P. Morgan Securities Inc. "shopped the company around," Kolton said "that is not sufficient explanation to enable stockholders to make their own reasoned assessment" about whether $3 per share is the best price. Mercator shares closed at $2.98, up 1 cent. Officials in Mercator's corporate communications and legal departments didn't immediately return phone calls. (Bloomberg)

South Bay project gets BRA go-ahead

The Boston Redevelopment Authority gave the green light to a proposal to build a six-story Courtyard by Marriott Hotel, along with a family restaurant, in the South Bay section of Boston. The proponent of the 164-room hotel project is Jiten Hotel Management Inc. of Brockton, whose president, Nayan Patel, owns and operates the adjacent Holiday Inn Express Boston Hotel at 69 Boston St. The Courtyard project, with an estimated construction cost of $17.4 million, is expected to be completed by the second half of 2004, one of Jiten's lawyers said. (Chris Reidy)

Emerson College, Colonial Theatre link

Emerson College has signed a 20-year lease for the Colonial Theatre building on Boylston Street. Beginning Sept. 1, the lease gives Emerson an option to buy the building. An Emerson spokesman said the college "has no plans to use the theater space for any other purpose" than operating "a commercial theatrical venue" and will honor all existing arrangements, including an agreement with a unit of Clear Channel Communications Inc., which books shows at the theater. "What this means for us is that we have a new landlord, and it will be business as usual," said Ann Sheehan, a spokeswoman for Broadway in Boston/Clear Channel Entertainment. (Chris Reidy)

SEC issues subpoena to Med Diversified

The Securities and Exchange Commission issued a subpoena to Med Diversified Inc., demanding accounting documents and materials as part of an informal inquiry. The SEC launched the inquiry in August 2002 after auditor KPMG LLP notified Med Diversified of its resignation and of allegedly illegal acts by Med Diversified. In a release, the Andover healthcare company said the SEC asked for accounting and auditing documents, relationships with current and former financing sources, records of current and former directors and officers, litigation matters, records of stock option and incentive plans, news releases, personnel records, and minutes of board meetings. Med Diversified, which filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection Nov. 27, after its primary lender collapsed, noted that the letter said the inquiry shouldn't be seen as an indication of wrongdoing. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION

Mortgage rates moving higher again

Rates on 30-year mortgages rose again this week, the seventh increase in the past eight weeks, but they remain slightly below the average nationwide rates of one year ago. The average on 30-year mortgages, which dipped to 6.24 percent last week, climbed to 6.28 percent this week. Even so, the rate was slightly below this year's high of 6.34 percent, set two weeks ago. Rates on 30-year mortgages are now more than a full percentage point above their four-decade low in June. Most economists predict they will climb even higher as investors see more indications the economy is beginning a sustained rebound. Rates for 15-year fixed-rate mortgages rose to 5.60 percent this week, up from 5.58 percent last week. Rates for one-year adjustable mortgages rose to 3.84 percent, up from 3.75 percent. (AP)

Stock-fraud lawsuit settlements rise

Securities fraud lawsuits were settled for an average of $25.1 million during the first seven months of 2003, up 20 percent from an average of $19.9 million during 2002, according to a PricewaterhouseCoopers survey. Sixty settlements totaling $1.5 billion were completed in the first seven months of this year, according to the survey by the world's largest accounting firm. Though settlement values are up, the number of suits is declining. Large companies have enacted reforms in response to the Sarbanes-Oxley corporate responsibility law, passed last year. Twelve Fortune 500 companies have been named in securities litigation cases this year, according to the report. Sixty were sued in 2002. The survey projects about 190 new securities fraud cases for 2003, a 21 percent drop from the 217 in 2002. So far this year, 111 suits have been filed. (Bloomberg)

Goodyear, union agree on 3-year pact

Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co. shares rose 4.1 percent after the company and the United Steelworkers of America agreed to a tentative three-year contract, reducing the threat of a strike at the struggling company. Goodyear shares rose 26 cents to close at $6.56. After a weekend of all-night bargaining sessions and five months of negotiations, the two sides said they found common ground on job security and healthcare issues for workers, while leaving enough financial room for Goodyear to reorganize its business. The union said its members must vote on the contract before it is ratified. The contract, which expired April 16, covered 16,000 employees at 14 plants in 11 states. Goodyear reported a record $1.1 billion annual loss for fiscal 2002. It has announced a turnaround plan that includes reducing costs by $1 billion to $1.5 billion by the end of 2005 and possibly selling some of its nontire businesses. (AP)

THE WORLD

Mexico's unemployment rate jumps

Fox Mexico's unemployment rate jumped to 3.52 percent in July, the government said, the highest level in more than five years, putting new pressure on President Vicente Fox to revive the economy. The rise in the base unemployment rate was much higher than expected and is likely to trigger new calls for Fox's government to take measures to boost economic growth. The jobless rate in June was 3.17 percent, but the government said it had changed its methodology of calculating unemployment by looking at 32 cities instead of 48 previously. Some analysts said the government's measure of unemployment was unrealistic. The government said the new methodology does not substantially alter the unemployment numbers, although the rate in June would have been 3.27 percent if measured with the new formula. (Reuters)
