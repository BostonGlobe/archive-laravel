Sports fans swing for the fence

They're adopting broadband, paying extra to follow teams

By Chris Gaither, Globe Staff, 8/25/2003

SAN FRANCISCO -- When the Red Sox are battling on the East Coast, Mike Hsieh is usually in the three-bedroom apartment he shares here, programming corporate websites. But the 28-year-old Lexington native can hear the crack of the bat, the calls of the umpire, and the cheers and groans of the Fenway Park faithful.

Hsieh used to follow the Red Sox by reading play-by-play text descriptions that are posted on websites like Yahoo Sports and Boston.com, where the action is relayed in simple phrases: "strike swinging," "ground out to third," "home run," etc. But he now pays $12 a year to listen to radio broadcasts of Red Sox games streamed over the Internet. Nearly 3,000 miles from WEEI's radio towers, he often takes a break from his work to listen to the familiar voices of radiomen Joe Castiglione and Jerry Trupiano narrate particularly dramatic sequences.

"It's so much more exciting to listen to the game than to just see the stats," Hsieh said. Following the game via short, written descriptions of each play "is like reading the box scores. You can see what's happening in the game, but you don't really feel it."

By hooking diehard fans like Hsieh, sports content providers are blazing new trails toward bringing audio and video streams to the Internet mainstream. With their constant desire to get closer to the action, willingness to spend money to follow their favorite teams, and early adoption of broadband, sports fans have become a test bed for new Internet technologies and business models.

In the process, they are helping to answer some of the Internet's most vexing business problems: How do you get online broadcasts to rival TV? And will people pay for them?

The sports market was one of the early gold-rush areas of the dot-com boom. Start-ups like Quokka Sports Inc. took the lead, winning contracts to stream the 2000 Olympics and other major sporting events over the Internet. The upstarts flamed out under flawed business plans, poor video quality, and slower-than-expected adoption of broadband.

But 36 percent of US Internet users now connect from home through broadband, compared to 25 percent last year, according to research firm Nielsen/NetRatings.

For sports fans, the numbers are much higher. Of the 14.7 million unique visitors in July to ESPN.com, the leading sports site, 80 percent surfed on high-speed connections. That makes them the perfect demographic for testing better data compression technologies, higher transmission rates, subscription plans, and interactive advertising.

ESPN recently launched a technology developed in the research labs of its parent company, Walt Disney Co., that plays sports highlights and interviews in the Internet browser. By automatically downloading the clip onto users' computers before they request it, the technology, called Motion, enables a picture that is good enough that companies like Lexus and Gatorade are now paying to run their TV ads on ESPN.com. Disney liked the technology so much that it rolled out Motion to two of its other Internet properties, ABC.com and Movies.com.

"If you look at why people would want to have a fat connection, I would think sports is right up there," said ESPN executive vice president John Skipper, citing ESPN.com's core audience of 18- to 55-year-old men. "After sex, Im trying to think of what they would care more about."

Working with technology partners like RealNetworks Inc., major sporting leagues have begun to capitalize on the Internet.

More than 150,000 fans of NASCARs Winston Cup racing series pay $6.95 to $9.95 a month, or $49.95 to $64.95 per season, for TrackPass, which lets them follow the races online and eavesdrop on conversations between drivers and their pit crews. The National Hockey League and National Basketball Association sell video streams of highlights, classic games, and behind-the-scenes action. The National Football League streams its radio broadcasts online.

But Major League Baseball is among the leaders in finding ways to get Internet users to pay to follow the action. The league's Internet spinoff, MLB.com, offers statistics and pitch-by-pitch descriptions of games for free, but charges for most multimedia, said CEO Bob Bowman.

Between 150,000 and 170,000 people subscribe for online radio broadcasts. Nearly 100,000 people are paying $14.95 per month or $39.95 per season to watch live or archived games streamed over the Internet, and another 4 million people can access the games through subscription services offered by MLB.com partners, RealNetworks, and America Online, Bowman said. About 150,000 pay for MLB.com's other subscription services, which include postgame video highlights and "condensed games," which eliminate the commercials and standing-around between pitches to squeeze a three-hour game into 20 minutes.

Baseball has more tricks up its sleeve. In early September, MLB.com plans to cater to the sports memorabilia craze by creating some collectibles of its own -- digital downloads of games that users can burn (only once) onto a DVD. Bowman said he plans to start with only some of the most famous games in the league's history, then offer this year's playoff games for download.

But in the future, as data-storage costs come down, the league can make available digital copies of regular-season games. Derek Lowe's no-hitter at Fenway Park against the Tampa Bay Devil Rays last year may not be an all-time classic, but baseball is betting that some people will want to buy it. Imagine catching a foul ball hit by Manny Ramirez, then going home and downloading that game to show friends your glovework. Antipiracy software will protect the games from being traded on file-sharing programs like Kazaa or Morpheus, Bowman said.

Next year, the league plans to make its online video broadcasts more high-tech by allowing subscribers to bring up several games on the same screen simultaneously.

Already, Bowman said, "Our company streams more live events that any other media company in the world."

To make their way from the ballpark to a subscriber's computer, the games funnel through a broadcast command center in the basement of the RealNetworks headquarters in Seattle.

For years RealNetworks relied on Internet ads for revenue and gave away its media player, but it couldn't sell enough ads to support itself. So it started RealOne, a subscription-only Internet network of TV programs ranging from "Big Brother" to CNN news to Playboy. But RealNetworks has been most successful selling software to the sports leagues and game subscriptions to fans.

The company also plans to use the cost-advantage of doing business on the Internet to stream games that don't have enough followers to support TV broadcasting in the United States, like British soccer leagues, extreme sports, and cricket.

"We have the potential to make money if we get 10,000 viewers, and I'm not sure if cable is willing to go that low," said Erik Flannigan, vice president of programming for RealNetworks.

In the RealNetworks broadcast center one evening last week, as the Red Sox played the Oakland Athletics in Fenway Park, the game flickered beside CSPAN, NBC's shopping channel, harness racing, and other baseball contests on a bank of monitors. On a busy day, up to 200 events flow into the control room, where they are encoded in RealNetworks's format, then sent to computer servers across the world. RealNetworks spews 12 gigabits of data onto the Internet backbone each second, the second most traffic behind America Online, said Mark Warner, director of broadcasting for RealNetworks.

MLB.com and other sports leagues are hoping that RealNetworks's broadcast center is only the beginning of new technologies to help broaden their reach beyond the television.

If Hsieh, the New England native living in San Francisco, is any indication, sports fans may be willing to pony up for the newest ways to catch their hometown teams' games.

"Depending on how choppy it is, as long as [video quality] isn't outrageous I would probably pay," he said, when told of baseball's Internet TV service. "Red Sox fans are just so hard-core."
