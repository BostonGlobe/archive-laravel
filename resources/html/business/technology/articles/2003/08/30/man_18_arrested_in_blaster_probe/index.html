Man, 18, arrested in Blaster probe

But worm's creator remains a mystery

By Hiawatha Bray, Globe Staff, 8/30/2003

An 18-year-old Minnesota man was arrested yesterday for allegedly creating a variant of the devastating Blaster computer worm that infected thousands of computers this month.

But law enforcement agencies are still seeking the creator of the original attack program.

Security experts say Jeffrey Lee Parson's incompetence led to his arrest yesterday -- and that there's little chance of tracking down the original author of the worm, unless he makes similar blunders.

John McKay, US attorney for the western district of Washington State, said the Parson case should put Internet criminals on notice.

"With this arrest, we want to deliver a message to cyberhackers here and around the world," he said. "The Department of Justice takes these crimes very seriously."

But Richard Smith, a Brookline computer security expert, said clever worm writers would never commit the blunders that led the authorities to Parson. "As the cops like to say, `We mostly catch the dumb ones,' " Smith said. "You don't get much dumber than this."

The Blaster worm was one of several pieces of hostile computer code, or "malware," which swept around the world.

Blaster, which first appeared in mid-August, exploited a security bug in Microsoft Corp.'s Windows operating systems. The worm quickly infected hundreds of thousands of computers, causing many to malfunction. It also planted attack software on thousands of machines, programming them to launch a secondary "denial of service" attacks on customer support computers run by Microsoft.

According to a complaint filed in a federal court in Seattle, Parson modified the original Blaster worm, changing its name to one of his Internet nicknames, "teekid." He allegedly added a "back door" feature that would let him seize control of infected machines weeks or months after the worm infection. In addition, he ordered the worm to "phone home" by contacting a website. Software at the website would record the Internet address of each infected computer, providing a list of machines that could be controled.

But Parson's penchant for using his Internet nickname got him into trouble.

His modified Blaster software was programmed to contact www.t33kid.com, authorities say. Since the number 3 is frequently used by computer hobbyists as a substitute for the letter E, there was a clear connection between the Web address and the renamed teekid worm software.

Microsoft engineers dismantled the new malware, learned how it worked, found the teekid references, and informed the Secret Service, which visited www.t33kid.com and found software for producing Internet worms.

The Secret Service next ran a simple domain name search to find the owner of the website. The search led to a small Internet provider in Texas. Owner Brian Davis told the Secret Service and FBI he provided Web hosting service to teekid, and had communicated with him using a popular Internet chat room technology called Internet Relay Chat.

Davis had recorded the Internet address used by teekid. It led investigators to another website full of malware. The address also led them directly to their man, they said.

Most computer vandals first gain control of faraway computers with no apparent connection to them. They then use them to launch attacks on others, making it difficult to find the origin of the attack. But the Internet address of teekid's computer belonged to Time Warner Cable and was linked to a broadband Internet account at Parson's home. Parson had even used teekid as one of the user names on the account.

FBI and Secret Service agents searched the Parson home Aug. 19, confiscating a number of computers. According to the criminal complaint, Parson admitted creating the new version.

Parson was charged with one count of intentionally causing harm to a protected computer, which can carry a penalty of up to 10 years in prison and a fine of up to $250,000. At a hearing in St. Paul yesterday, Parson was released under home detention and barred from further use of computers.

Microsoft general counsel Brad Smith said the Blaster worm has cost Microsoft millions of dollars, but added, "The damage done to Microsoft in this instance was a small tip of the larger iceberg of damage that was done to computer users around the world." He said Microsoft needs to make its software more secure. "As a company, we're committed to do more across the board," he said.

McKay said that the Blaster investigation is continuing. He said that the Parson arrest produced some leads, but he would provide no details.

The recent spate of Internet attacks on Microsoft-based computers comes 20 months after chairman Bill Gates launched a campaign to make his company's products more secure. Microsoft officials have said the company is making progress, despite the rise of Blaster and other Internet worms.

But if preventing software attacks is difficult, catching the attackers may be even harder.

"They caught [Parson] because he basically drew a neon sign around himself and said, `It's me,' " said Mark Rasch, chief security counsel for Nebraska-based service provider Solutionary Inc. A former US attorney, Rasch in 1988 successfully prosecuted Cornell University graduate student Robert Tappan Morris, the creator of the first Internet worm. Since then, catching malware writers has gotten a lot tougher.

"There are lots of ways of creating bad code . . . that make you virtually untraceable," Rasch said.

"The number of people who are caught is woefully small, compared to the number of viruses out there," said Chris Wraight, a technology consultant at the Lynnfield antivirus firm Sophos Inc.

Wraight estimates there are about 80,000 known viruses, worms, and other malware programs. Most are relatively harmless. But others, such as the Slammer worm, have inspired global manhunts. So far, nobody has been arrested for writing Slammer, which clogged Internet computers worldwide and caused up to $1 billion in damage.

A handful of malware writers have been caught.

There was David Smith, the New Jersey man who unleashed the Melissa virus in 1999, the Israeli teens who wrote an e-mail worm called Goner in 2001, and Welshman Simon Vallor, recently sentenced to two years in prison for producing a worm called Redesi.

But these are exceptional cases, say the experts, caused by careless blunders.

The best bet, said Richard Smith, is to design computer systems that aren't easily infected by worms and other malware.

"What we need to do," he said, "is work on the infrastructure to make this stuff less popular."
