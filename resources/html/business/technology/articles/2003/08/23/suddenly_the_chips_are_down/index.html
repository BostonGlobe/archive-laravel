Suddenly, the chips are down

Vt. sees layoffs derailing recovery

By Rich Barlow, Globe Correspondent, 8/23/2003

ESSEX JUNCTION, Vt. -- The Vermont Expos are sweating through a lousy season, but aside from that, the state was having an good summer -- until this week.

Unemployment stands just above 4 percent, better than in the rest of New England and most of the nation. About 5,000 jobs have been created since the end of the recession. The good fortune even spilled over into politics: Former Governor Howard Dean vaulted from nobody to a player in the presidential race.

Then came Monday's announcement that IBM would cut 500 jobs at its Essex Junction semiconductor plant -- the third round of layoffs in as many years at Vermont's biggest private employer, which is beset by sagging sales of computer chips. "It offsets about 10 percent of our recovery," said Jeffrey Carr, vice president of Economic & Policy Resources, a consulting firm. In the nation's second-smallest state, the effects of layoffs at giant IBM reverberate quickly. An analysis by Carr's firm after IBM shed 500 jobs in 2001 estimated Vermont's six northwestern counties would lose an additional 700-plus jobs, full- and part-time, as spending by IBM and consumers slowed in response. The study pegged layoff-related tax losses to the state at $9 million. Carr fears highly trained workers will have to take lesser-paying jobs, or leave the state for tech work elsewhere. "Nine times out of 10, they take a fairly talented spouse with them," he added.With a Vermont work force after this week's layoffs of about 6,200, IBM's presence in the state is a quarter smaller than in 2001. The just-announced cuts -- to be coupled with an unpaid week's furlough for thousands more workers -- left state leaders fearful of where the bottom might be."I wouldn't use the word depressed, but I'm really concerned about it," said Chris Barbieri, president of the Vermont Chamber of Commerce. An IBM spokesman this week declined to discuss the plant's future.Governor Jim Douglas talked about the need to diversify the economy. In the past year, Vermont was one of the few states in the nation to add jobs, bolstered in part by a strong tourism season this summer and the growth of white-collar professional service jobs, such as in engineering. IBM is in Chittenden County, which is not the stereotypical Vermont of bucolic dairy farms. It is the only urban center, home to 25 percent of the state's residents, the biggest city -- Burlington -- and the highest average wage. Its economy is fattened on manufacturing's heftier-than-usual paychecks. (IBM has been in Essex Junction for 45 years.) Last year, the median income in the county was $32,139; the average pay of the workers IBM laid off was roughly double that, Carr said.

Randy St. Amand is well aware of the disparity. The 46-year-old technician, who has worked at the IBM plant since graduating from college 26 years ago, got his pink slip Monday. His twin brother, another IBM worker, also lost his job; a second brother still works at the plant. St. Amand said he has no hope of finding a job in Vermont at his IBM hourly wage of $26. His wife and his daughter, who lives at home, both work, and he'll draw severance pay.

But, he said, he'll have to sell his home in Milton. "Just my taxes and utilities on this house run me close to $800 a month, and I still have a mortgage on it," he said.

Companies that once got work from IBM are also feeling the pain. Having lunch and a beer Thursday at Murray's Tavern in Essex Junction, construction workers Marty McCuin and David Cousino recalled the boom of the late 1990s, when the South Burlington firm they work for did $10 million to $12 million a year in electrical work at the plant. "Right now, it wouldn't even make half a million a year," said McCuin.

"We're going through layoffs ourselves right now," said Cousino, "because work is very, very slow."

Adding to the gloom is the fact that there is only so much state leaders can do, given that IBM is suffering "the worst global downturn in information technology ever," Carr said.
