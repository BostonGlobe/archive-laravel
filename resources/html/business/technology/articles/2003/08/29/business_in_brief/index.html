Business in brief

By Globe Staff and Wires, 8/29/2003

THE REGION

Verizon talks on hold until Tuesday

Verizon Communications Inc. and unions representing 79,200 of its East Coast workers have agreed to recess contract negotiations until Tuesday. Disagreements over contract terms for 51 Verizon Wireless workers in New York City represented by the Communications Workers of America, the only union members at 40,000-employee Verizon Wireless, have emerged as a key obstacle to an overall settlement for the local-phone unit's technicians and call-center agents. (Peter J. Howe)

AirTran to expand service at Logan

AirTran Airways plans to expand its service from Boston to Philadelphia and Baltimore with additional flights, the Orlando, Fla.-based carrier said. Beginning Nov. 4, AirTran will add a round trip, daily flight between Logan International Airport and Philadelphia International Airport, bringing the airline's total to three daily flights on that route. AirTran also plans an additional daily round trip between Logan and Baltimore-Washington International Airport beginning Nov. 19, bringing the total to eight daily flights on that route. Boeing 717 jets will be used for the new flights. (Keith Reed)

Inverness Medical acquires Apogent unit

Inverness Medical Innovations Inc., a maker of home pregnancy tests, acquired Apogent Technologies Inc.'s medical-tests unit for $29 million in stock and cash. Waltham-based Inverness is paying Portsmouth, N.H.-based Apogent $13.4 million in cash and 692,506 shares of stock, the company said. The stock portion of the deal equals $15.6 million based on yesterday's closing price of Inverness shares. Inverness gets Applied Biotech Inc., a maker of tests to detect infectious diseases, illicit drugs, and pregnancies, the company said. Inverness said it financed the cash portion of the purchase by boosting its current senior credit facilities to $70 million from $55 million. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

Bombs explode at Chiron headquarters

Two homemade bombs exploded at Chiron Corp.'s Emeryville, Calif., headquarters, shutting down work at one of the nation's largest biotech companies in what industry experts said might have been an attack mounted by animal rights activists. Police said they had no suspects, but a biomedical industry group, Americans for Medical Progress, said the explosions could have been the work of animal rights activists because the company uses animals for research. Chiron executives have complained of being harassed by animal rights activists in recent weeks. No injuries or major property damage were reported in the explosions, which took place at 5:55 a.m. and 7:10 a.m. A third device, packed in a five-gallon plastic container, was found in front of a third building at the complex and safely detonated. (Reuters)

IBM expands recall of 15-inch monitors

IBM is expanding an earlier recall of 15-inch computer monitors after 63,000 more were found to be at risk of overheating and smoking, posing a fire hazard to consumers. The company has received seven reports of circuit boards overheating, including one case of minor property damage and another instance of minor smoke inhalation. Two of the reports were received after the original recall, which was announced in March and covered 56,000 monitors sold nationwide from June to September 1997. The expanded recall extends the manufacturing date to September 1998 and sale date to December 1998. The recalled monitors are the G51 CRT and G51t Touch Screen CRT models bearing model numbers 6541-02N, 6541-02E, 6541-02S, 6541-Q0N, 6541-Q0E and 6541-Q0S. Consumers can contact the company at 866-644-3155. (Dow Jones)

MetLife acquires Chicago's Sears Tower

MetLife Inc. acquired the Sears Tower in Chicago, buying Trizec Properties Inc.'s interest in the tallest US building for $9 million. The transaction enabled Trizec, the second-largest office real estate investment trust, to avoid assuming $766 million of mortgage debt. Trizec, seeking to reduce its $3.2 billion of debt, will continue its six-year role as building manager and leasing agent. MetLife, the second-biggest US life insurer, held the building's first mortgage, and took the title from Sears, Roebuck & Co. The current occupancy rate is 87 percent, which has been declining since the terrorist attack on Sept. 11, 2001. It was 95 percent occupied in 2001, before the terrorist attacks. Afterward, Trizec wrote down its investment in the building from $70 million, what it paid for the second mortgage position in 1997, to $23.6 million. (Bloomberg)

14th HealthSouth official pleads guilty

A former HealthSouth Corp. vice president admitted lying to auditors about the value of an investment, becoming the 14th executive to plead guilty in an accounting fraud that prosecutors say will exceed $2.5 billion. Will Hicks, 39, an ex-Wall Street analyst who managed company investments, admitted telling auditors that HealthSouth had a $13 million stake in assisted-living facilities that he knew was worthless. All who pleaded guilty are helping prosecutors investigate fired chief executive Richard Scrushy. (Bloomberg)

American, British Airways to cross-sell

American Airlines and British Airways will begin selling tickets on each other's flights on Sept. 17, American said. As part of what's called a code-sharing agreement, American will be able to sell tickets on British Airways connecting flights from London's Heathrow Airport to other destinations in Europe, Asia, Africa, and the Middle East as if they were American flights. British Airways will be able to do the same thing, connecting its passengers to US locations on American flights. The code-sharing will be phased in from Sept. 17 to Oct. 26. Code-sharing is increasingly common in the airline industry. It is a way airlines can get around barriers against operating in other countries. (AP)

Orbitz refiles plan for IPO

Orbitz, the online travel agency whose plans to go public were slowed during a recently ended government probe of its role in airline industry competition, filed to make an initial public offering of shares. The company, founded by five major US airlines, is still posting net losses, although it earned a small profit before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortization in the first six months of the year. Orbitz, the number two online travel agency after Expedia Inc., amended the registration statement it initially filed 15 months ago with the Securities and Exchange Commission. Since then, the online travel industry has grown quickly despite recent softness in the travel market. Orbitz did not specify how much money it plans to raise through the stock sale, proceeds of which it intends to use for working capital. (Reuters) . . .

Etc.

Shares of LTX Corp., a Westwood maker of test equipment for the semiconductor industry, rose $2.56, or 20.6 percent, to $14.99 after the company said its fiscal fourth-quarter loss narrowed and it forecast higher sales. The company expects revenue to rise to $37 million to $40 million in the first quarter from $30 million last year as demand for its products rises . . . Boston Biomedica Inc. said PricewaterhouseCoopers LLP resigned as its independent accountant, according to a Form 8-K filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission. The filing didn't give a reason for the resignation, which was effective last Friday. West Bridgewater-based Boston Biomedica reported no disagreements with PricewaterhouseCoopers on any matter of accounting principles or practices. (Wire services)
