Hey, check this out -- yourself

More shoppers go for the impersonal touch with self-checkout

By Mark Pothier, Globe Staff, 8/24/2003

PLYMOUTH -- More shoppers are becoming their own cashiers, but Allyson Hicks refuses to fall into line. Even if it is shorter.

ADVERTISEMENT

On a recent morning, she and her daughter, Tasha Ramos, wrestled a shopping cart full of bathroom accessories into the parking lot at the Plymouth Home Depot. They had just paid for them the old-fashioned way: at a register operated by a person.

Hicks said the store's new self-checkout lane, which allows customers to scan and bag their purchases, is too impersonal. "A little bit of human contact goes a long way," she said.

But people who need people to ring up a sale may soon be in the minority. Mike Hanrahan, an assistant manager, said customers often prefer to be on their own. A four-station self-serve "pod" activated less than a month ago already accounts for 31 percent of the store's transactions. "I couldn't believe how many people used it right off the bat," Hanrahan said.

Charlie Gibbons of Plymouth was one of several customers who said they would forgo smiles and small talk in exchange for a quicker exit. "You can wait 15 minutes" standing in line at a regular register, Gibbons said. "If it's better, I don't have problem with it."

Self-checkout, long available in supermarkets, is going from soup to nuts and bolts. Home Depot, with 2002 annual revenue of $58.6 billion, plans to eventually install the technology in all its 1,608 stores nationwide. About 600 stores have self-checkout, and another 200 are scheduled to get it by the end of the year. In addition to Plymouth, 16 of the 39 Home Depots in Massachusetts have the equipment, including stores in Avon, Dorchester, Everett, Norwood, Rockland, Salem, Waltham and Watertown. John Simley, a spokesman for the country's second-largest retailer, said self-checkout "provides a choice for customers."

Home Depot's machines accept cash, credit and debit cards, coupons, and gift certificates. A computerized voice and on-screen instructions prompt customers to pass items over an electronic scanner and place them in a bag on a scale, which detects the presence of products by weight. Hand-operated scanners are used for larger items such as lumber or ladders, and one employee monitors four stations, which cost about $25,000 each to buy and install.

At Wal-Mart, the nation's largest retailer, the company phased in self-checkout over the past two years in hundreds of the stores where it sells groceries. "We've been introducing it slowly because it's such a personal thing. Some people like it and some don't," said Thomas Williams, a Wal-Mart spokesman.

Last year in North America, the number of self-checkout lanes increased 47 percent, said Gregory Buzek, president of IHL Consulting Group in Franklin, Tenn. But some retailers continue to resist. Lowe's, a major competitor to Home Depot, does not plan to offer self-checkout anytime soon. "It is something we are certainly evaluating," said Chris Ahearn, director of public relations for the 875-store chain.

Target, the nation's fourth-largest retailer, also has shunned self-checkout. "We prefer to have human interaction," said spokeswoman Brie Heath.

Unions agree. "If they're putting in self-checkout, they're not going to be putting in as many cashiers," said Mark Govoni, political director for United Food and Commercial Workers, Local 1445, which represents about 9,000 Stop & Shop employees from Worcester County to the Boston area. Govoni said his union welcomes technological advances "as long as they don't displace the current work force."

Home Depot, which is not unionized, will not cut employees' hours or positions, said Simley, the company spokesman. He said stores are able to speed up lines and at the same time deploy more salespeople, so the number of jobs remains the same. "It doesn't have anything to do with labor management necessarily. It has everything to do with trying to dissolve bottlenecks," he said.

Peter Buzzell, an assistant manager at the Plymouth Home Depot, said some customers have "misconceptions" about the system. "They feel it takes positions away from the cashiers, but we're not," he said. "The hours that are generated from losing the cashiers go back on the sales floor." IHL says a four-unit system can save up to 150 labor hours a week.

"If it's a quick hit, this is where you want to go," Buzzell said, pointing at a touch-sensitive computer screen. "Grab a gallon of paint, a couple brushes and a roller, and boom -- you're out of the store." Some competitors disagree. At Morrison's Home and Garden, near Home Depot's Plymouth store, manager Bill Murray said companies "lose a lot" by replacing people with computers. "When we hire someone for our front counter we don't consider them cashiers," Murray said. "They're expected to learn about the products. You go to one of those self-checkouts and you get nothing."Ramos, who lives on Cape Cod, said she tried self-checkout in supermarkets and found it cumbersome. "There's always an issue," she said. "Something doesn't ring in right and it ends up taking 100 years." Ramos is "half right," Simley said. Depending on the products, Home Depot's self-scanning process itself can take longer than having a cashier tally a sale, he said, but because customers rarely have to wait in line, the total time they spend checking out is reduced by about one-third, and most self-checkout experiences last 90 seconds.

"People were terrified of caller ID and television remotes and electricity," Simley said. "Anytime you introduce a new technology they have lots of questions." But if they are left to their own devices, he said, even technology-shy consumers might one day become converts.
