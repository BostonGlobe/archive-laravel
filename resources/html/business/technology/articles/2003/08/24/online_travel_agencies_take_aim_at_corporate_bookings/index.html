Online travel agencies take aim at corporate bookings

By Suzanne Marta, The Dallas Morning News, 8/24/2003

American Express Corporate Travel -- the nation's largest corporate travel agency -- is getting some stiff competition, and it's coming from companies that made their name booking vacations.

The newest competitor is Fort Worth-based Travelocity, which launched its Travelocity Business program last week. It, like similar efforts founded in the last year by Orbitz and Expedia Inc., aims at small and midsize businesses.

Their challenge of American Express is an important opportunity for the online travel agencies.

Total managed travel -- trips booked online and through traditional travel agencies -- is expected to grow about 30 percent between now and 2008. But online corporate bookings are growing much more quickly.

Managed travel booked online grew from $2.4 billion in 1999 to $8.5 billion this year. By 2008, it's expected to nearly triple to $24 billion. And, analysts say, a good portion of those online bookings could be coming through companies like Travelocity, Expedia and Orbitz.

"It's a natural extension of their business," said Henry Harteveldt, a San Francisco analyst with Forrester Research.

Corporate customers of all sizes are going online to make their bookings in an effort to reduce transaction fees paid to travel agents. Transaction fees were a nonissue just two years ago, when travel agents earned money through supplier-based commissions. But the bulk of those --which came from airlines -- were eliminated in early 2002, meaning travel agencies had to start charging service fees.

A trip booked on one of the online agency sites costs a company about $5, vs. as much as $75 for the same itinerary booked by an agent.

"Travel managers don't want to spend more money than they have to for travel agency fees," Harteveldt said.

Traditional corporate travel agencies offer some type of online booking tool, such as the one offered by Travelocity's sister company, GetThere. But a recent Forrester Research survey found that many corporate travel managers have been dissatisfied with those online booking tools.

In the Forrester survey of 380 travel managers, only one-third said they thought technology had improved their corporate travel management. And only 17 percent said they felt their travel agency was helping them encourage employees to shift to using the online booking tool.

That dissatisfaction represents an opportunity for Travelocity and its peers, whose online business offerings look similar to their respective consumer sites.

The business travel offerings include options that offer corporations more control over their travel spending.

For example, companies can enter any special discounts or negotiated rates with key suppliers for employees to access. They can also filter out any suppliers not within the company travel policy. The sites also offer reporting tools that gather data on trips booked so companies can more closely monitor spending and buying patterns

Expedia officials say they've won several customers whose employees had been booking through the consumer site already.

"We've built our products around the end-consumer, not travel agents," said Matt Hulett, Expedia's vice president of corporate travel.

Travelocity launched its business earlier this month, about a month later than it planned and months after Orbitz and Expedia launched similar products.

Travelocity Business has a "60 second" guarantee, referring to the time it should take for a travel agent to answer the phone when customers call.

Officials say Travelocity Business has a ready-made market. An estimated 20 percent to 30 percent of bookings made on Travelocity.com already come from business travelers, Travelocity Business president Ellen Keszler said.

"It's an underserved market," she said. "We're offering the benefit of Web fares and the scale of corporate negotiated rates."

Travelocity, Orbitz and Expedia's new corporate online offerings are expected to attract contracts from small businesses, but their products will have a tougher time competing for larger clients. The real fight will occur over midsize firms, said Jared Blank, an analyst with Jupiter Research in New York.

"Midsize companies who are with a mega-agency now are trying to figure out if they really need to pay for all those services," Blank said.

But the online sites say they also plan to go after traditional customers of corporate travel management firms such as American Express. Winning those clients may prove difficult.

"When people think of online agencies, I'm not sure good customer service is what jumps to mind," Blank said.

That's why the big travel agencies are promoting their customer service. American Express, for example, launched a marketing campaign this month that emphasizes the phrase "experience matters."

The online agencies "have strong messages, but they also have a lot to learn," said John Berkley, vice president of small business travel for American Express. "We're happy to compete with them on who provides the best service. We have been able to prove over the years that we can provide value."

Berkley is quick to point out that American Express has been offering an online booking option for years -- a tool that has been growing in popularity.

About 73 percent of American Express' new small business accounts use its online services. "Two years ago, 70 percent wanted to use one of our local offices," Berkley said.

That rapid shift to the Web doesn't surprise Hulett of Expedia. Growth in online bookings has turned Expedia into the nation's fourth-largest travel agency last year, according to Travel Weekly, up from No. 8. Travelocity was seventh in 2002, and Orbitz came in No. 9.

Expedia announced its booking tool days before last year's National Business Travel Association convention, and then scrambled to put together a trade floor table and sales staff.

"We had four salespeople and a masseuse," Hulett said.

At this year's conference, held in Dallas last week, the Expedia booth was staffed by 30 salespeople.
