Challenging times despite good prospects

Firms with prospects still face challenges

By Scott Kirsner, 8/3/2003

Even tech companies with good prospects and strong momentum still face challenges, as the economic recovery takes its time getting into gear. Here's a look at three such companies.

ADVERTISEMENT

Avidyne Corp. The pilot's preflight announcement is a mite worrisome.

"I need to make you aware that this is an experimental aircraft, and it doesn't meet the certified requirements of a standard certified aircraft, according to the FAA," says Todd Handel, the chief pilot for Avidyne.

Avidyne is a Lincoln company that is steadily remaking the cockpits of small planes with its flat-panel display technology. The plane we're in today is a spiffy-looking single-engine Lancair 350 that seats four, including the pilot.

It's a breezy Friday afternoon, and all around us at Hanscom Airport, private jets are revving up for the short jaunt down to Martha's Vineyard or Nantucket. Handel starts up the engine, and then consults a weather map on one of two 10-inch color screens in the center of the dashboard.

"If we were headed up to Portland or Bangor today, we might want to wait on the ground for a while," Handel says. Red splotches on the map indicate heavy rain.

But we're just going up for a short flight above the western suburbs of Boston, where the weather is fine. Handel is in the process of testing the Avidyne display system, called FlightMax Entegra, that has only been recently installed in the Lancair. That's the reason for his unusual preflight announcement. Later in August, he'll take an FAA inspector up for a flight to get the technology approved for use in the Lancair.

Near the end of the runway, Handel revs the engine for a moment, then gets clearance for takeoff. A few seconds later, the Lancair is in the air, and the digital altimeter on the display in front of me is spooling upward, counting off feet by the tens as we rise into the sky.

Avidyne's displays are changing the way instrument panels look, in much the same way that the Macintosh's graphical user interface changed the way desktop computers looked. When Avidyne last appeared in this column, in February 2002, the question was, how many airplane makers could they win over. Since then, Avidyne's displays have been added as an option -- or standard equipment -- by manufacturers like Lancair, Diamond Aircraft, CirrusDesign, Adam Aircraft, and Safire Aircraft.

This week, they're at the annual AirVenture trade show in Oshkosh, Wis., debuting new features for the Entegra display.

The challenge for Avidyne: making a decent profit in a low-volume business. Not very many people purchase new planes each year; more people decide to upgrade their older planes with new gear. CEO Dan Schwinn won't say whether Avidyne is profitable.

Dimensional Photonics The demo room at Dimensional Photonics resembles the Sears Portrait Studio after a hostile take-over by George Lucas.

Instead of a traditional camera, at the center of the room is a device called the AFI 4000. With two triangular yellow heads mounted on a horizontal post, and a tripod on wheels for a base, it looks like a robot that didn't quite make the cut for the last "Star Wars" film. And instead of snapping a photo of a smiling toddler, the AFI 4000 has a candy-red car door in its sights.

The AFI 4000 is a new product from Southborough-based Dimensional Photonics, a start-up that traces its roots to MIT's Lincoln Lab. (Lincoln Lab is dedicated to developing advanced technologies to enhance national security.)

Priced at $250,000, its job is to make highly accurate, three-dimensional images of parts as they come off an assembly line, to check their quality and consistency by matching them against the original plans.

In 60 seconds, using lasers in one of the yellow heads and a camera in the other, the AFI 4000 "paints" the car door with a series of red vertical pinstripes, starting with wide stripes and then gradually getting narrower.

The camera discerns the shape of the object based on how the laser stripes shift and change shape when they're projected onto an uneven surface like the car door.

After the camera has snapped 12 images of the car door, the AFI 4000 (AFI stands for Accordion Fringe Interferometry, the name for this kind of measurement approach) feeds 4 million data points into a standard PC. Instantly, a three-dimensional picture of the car door materializes on the screen.

"You can compare the millionth iteration of a part to the original [computer] model of what it's supposed to look like," says Nathan Derr, the company's marketing manager. Dimensional Photonics hopes its device will replace an existing technology used for taking quality-assurance measurements.

"Our technology is mobile, so we can go where the parts are, rather than bringing the parts into a lab," says Lyle Shirley, the company's CEO. "It's faster, and it's much higher resolution."

The technology actually does owe a debt to Star Wars, but the Ronald Reagan version, not George Lucas's. Some of Shirley's research at Lincoln Labs involved using laser imaging to discern the difference between airborne ballistic missiles and decoys. That technology eventually spawned Dimensional's high-speed, high-res imaging approach.

Beta testers for the AFI 4000 include General Electric and Northrop Grumman. Dimensional has raised $4 million in funding and is now seeking more. That's one challenge ahead. Another is convincing prospective customers of the pricey new machine's value.

Groove Networks A chance meeting last August between Ray Ozzie and one of the US Navy's top doctors got Ozzie's company, Beverly-based Groove Networks, wrapped up in the rebuilding of Iraq.

At a conference in California organized by the Pentagon, Dr. Eric Rasmussen, a commander in the Navy's medical corps, expressed frustration at how hard it was to coordinate various humanitarian groups in a crisis environment, like after a war or natural disaster. Ozzie thought that Groove's collaboration software might be able to help.

On a pro bono basis, Groove employees developed several custom applications that Rasmussen thought might be useful in the aftermath of the Iraq war. When Rasmussen was sent to Kuwait City to work in the Humanitarian Operations Center set up by the US Agency for International Development, he brought his laptop and Groove's software along.

The software, designed to help large groups share information, was used to evaluate the conditions throughout the country just after major combat was over. One benefit: It doesn't require a constant connection to the Internet.

"We collected information about who was in charge of a neighborhood, who knows about the water system, how well is the infrastructure operation, which [humanitarian] groups are already active in that area," says Rasmussen. Groove exec Michael Helfrich says "50 or 60" people were using the software as the statue of Saddam fell in Baghdad, coordinating relief efforts.

For Groove, the tour of duty in Iraq supplied a nice case study about how its software could be used in intense, high-stakes situations. Although the company loaned its software to all of the users in Iraq, in July, Groove started what it calls the humanitarian systems group, headed by Helfrich, which aims to sell the software to aid groups, government agencies, and nonprofits.

"There's a demonstrated need in this segment," Helfrich says, "and we can do some good."

The challenge: Aid groups and nonprofits aren't lavish spenders. Can Groove do well financially by doing good? Stay tuned.
