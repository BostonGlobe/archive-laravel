A lesson in how not to run a trade show

By Scott Kirsner, Globe Staff, 8/18/2003

The failed negotiations to bring the Macworld trade show back to Boston could be fodder for a great Harvard Business School case study. The moral of the story for budding MBAs: get everything in writing.

ADVERTISEMENT

Last October, at an event in the North End, city officials stood alongside Charlie Greco, then the CEO of IDG World Expo, the Framingham-based organizer of Macworld, and crowed that the show would return to Boston from New York in July 2004. Greco predicted the show would bring in $40 million in revenues. At the press conference, there was even a Paul Revere impersonator who was hired to shout: "Macworld is coming! Macworld is coming!"

Unfortunately, no one at the event had a signed agreement with anyone else.

Apple quickly announced that it didn't want the show moved back to Boston. As the dispute between Apple and IDG worsened, IDG decided to broaden the focus of the show. Next year, if it happens at all, it will be called CreativePro, and geared to designers, filmmakers, and musicians. That would reduce IDG's reliance on Apple, its petulant onetime headliner. While IDG won't make an official announcement for another few weeks, odds are that CreativePro would continue to be held in New York.

How can they do that? Easy. There never was a signed contract with the Greater Boston Convention & Visitors Bureau or the Massachusetts Convention Center Authority.

So don't look for lawsuits. Last October's "Macworld is coming" deal was virtual to its core.

An IDG spokesperson says the company is still talking with Apple, hoping to secure the company's participation at next summer's CreativePro. IDG is also still doing research as to which city, Boston or New York, makes the most sense as a location. But some see New York as a foregone conclusion. One longtime Macworld exhibitor, who requested anonymity because he still works closely with IDG, says: "If I think about the creative market, I think of New York, not Boston."

In this scenario, there are losers -- and there are bigger losers. No winners. Here's my take on each party, and what's likely to happen next.

IDG Big tech conferences have been suffering through the downturn. IDG will still hold one annual Macworld, in San Francisco each January, but the company's strategy seems to be shifting toward smaller, niche-oriented events. One new show for next year: Portable Power, focusing on next-generation power sources for portable devices, like fuel cells. It will be held in San Francisco.

But if the big summer event -- Macworld/CreativePro -- fades away, IDG will need to invent a series of successful shows to make up for the revenue generated by the old Macworld, which was a 50,000-attendee show. That's the challenge facing David Korse, the CEO of IDG World Expo, who came on board as Greco's replacement in mid-June.

Apple Of all of the players, Apple makes out best.

"Apple does have some really good reason for wanting to eliminate the summer Macworld," says Adam Engst, publisher of the Macintosh newsletter TidBITS.

"What it comes down to in a lot of ways is money -- somewhere between $4 million and $10 million, [according to] my back-of-the-envelope estimate."

Second, rather than bundling all of its new product announcements into two Macworlds, "they can parcel announcements out over the course of the year," says Engst, "and each one gets a little more attention."

Apple prefers events over which it can exercise total control. Apple launched the iTunes Music Store at its own event in April, and even pushed back its developers conference by a month so that it had more time to work on Panther, its newest operating system update. Also, the company now has a network of its own retail stores, including three in the Boston area -- something that didn't exist prior to 2001.

But with no big annual event on the East Coast, Apple will have to work overtime to maintain its fan base. (Apple users more resemble fans than rational customers.) One smart move: more live events and workshops at the company's retail stores.

Charlie Greco Greco's biggest mistake was trying to strong-arm Steve Jobs, one of the pioneers of personal computing, and a man who has a reputation for making his own rules. Jobs wanted to keep Macworld in NewYork, a locale he believes offers the company better media exposure and entices Wall Street analysts who follow the company to come to the show.

Charlie Wolf, an analyst who follows Apple for Needham & Co., said that behind the scene a real battle was waged. "Steve wanted it in New York, and [Greco] wanted to move it back to Boston."

In March, after it became clear that the rift between IDG and Apple was irreparable, Greco left IDG to run the event business at Gartner, an IDG rival in Connecticut. He lasted three months in the job, before citing "cultural differences" and moving on.

Greco's now back in Boston, trying to start his own trade show consulting business. To make that successful, he'll have to distance himself from the Macworld fiasco.

Convention Center Authority In its desperation to lure a big event -- any big event -- the Convention Center Authority forgot that the essence of a deal is that both parties have some skin in the game. IDG had none, even though the authority promised free rent at the new convention center and a bouquet of other enticements.

Jim Rooney, executive director of the authority, knows that IDG may not hold a show at all next summer, whether called Macworld or CreativePro. He knows that even if it does, it may opt to hold it in New York. But Rooney is hopeful that IDG may bring one of its other shows to Boston. (Currently, only one IDG show is scheduled for Boston next year: Bio-IT World, at the Hynes in March.) It's doubtful that would happen in 2004, but maybe later.

"The trends in the technology side of the convention and trade show industry are disappointing right now," Rooney says. "We're not dismissing technology shows, but what's growing is medical, so we'll be concentrating heavily over there."

The authority launched a website this summer, www.advantage

boston.com, intended to market the Boston Convention & Exhibition Center to event planners. That's a good step, but it seems like a major infusion of sales and marketing horsepower is needed to bring shows to the sleek new facility. Right now, the first event on the calendar once the building opens, according to Rooney, is a press party associated with next July's Democratic National Convention.The Boston tech community Macworld was the one annual show held in Boston that drew techies from around the country -- and especially from Silicon Valley. It was the one event that focused on consumer-oriented technology. And it was always a source of buzz. In 1997, the last year Macworld was held in Boston, Jobs announced a $150 million investment from archrival Microsoft. Since then, Bostonians have had to travel to New York, California, and Las Vegas for a large-scale technology gathering.

The glass half-full perspective: Boston still has a steady stream of smaller-scale tech and biotech events.
