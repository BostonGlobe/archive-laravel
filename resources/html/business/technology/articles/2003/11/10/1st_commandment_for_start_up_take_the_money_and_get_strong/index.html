1st commandment for start-up: Take the money and get strong

By Scott Kirsner, 11/10/2003

Etiquette for entrepreneurs, Lessons One, Two, and Three: When someone offers you money, take it politely. If you don't need it right now, take it anyway. If you have no thought as to how you might use the money being given you, go right ahead and take it.

But those rules don't really need to be taught. Entrepreneurs, by their nature, gather money like squirrels hoard nuts. Sitting on a cache of venture capital makes them feel warm and secure. But it can also create problems: strategic overreaching, a lack of spending discipline, pressure from investors to make it big and make it snappy. Think about the child raised on a $10-a-week allowance, paid on Friday in return for a week's worth of chores, vs. the rich kid who has $1,000 wired to his Swiss bank account every Monday morning.

Rewind to the first quarter of 2000, when more venture capital was doled out than ever before. The sum in New England alone approached $3 billion. That created a lot of rich kids. Among those rolling in dough back then were local companies like StorageNetworks, Digital Broadband Communications, Bow Street Software, and Zefer. Each raised more than $100 million. Today, only Andover-based Bow Street is still alive, having cut back sharply on spending and staff.

Past results, of course, aren't a predictor of future outcomes. But companies with lots of cash on their hands make me suspicious, whether in software, hardware, telecom, or biotech. All that cash makes it hard to focus on developing a useful product and connecting with customers, and it creates incredibly high expectations. In the music world, the equivalent would be expecting a newly formed rock band to be selling out the FleetCenter after its first album, rather than gigging around the club circuit for a few years.

"There's nothing wrong with smaller levels of funding," said Robert Poor, founder and chief technologist at Ember Corp. in Boston, a maker of radio chips and software for setting up wireless networks of sensors.

"When we started Ember, this was not a market. Our first round of funding was $3 million, with performance criteria that we had to hit before we got the second tranche."

Companies sometimes complain about having to jump through hoops to get more money from their backers. But it tends to make them stronger.

Among the start-ups that raised money in the most recent quarter is Acton-based Azimuth Networks. The company is developing gear that will help makers of WiFi cards and access points test the performance of their products.

The company has moved fast, but didn't require a huge bankroll to do it. Founder Fanny Mlinarsky -- formerly an executive at Agilent, Teradyne, and Chipcom -- realized last year that most makers of WiFi products were using home-grown equipment to test their products' transmitting and receiving power. She thought they would appreciate an off-the-shelf testing platform that would deliver consistent test results.

Mlinarsky raised $5.8 million from Kodiak Venture Partners and North Bridge Venture Partners last fall to hire an engineering team and begin building what Azimuth calls its "W-Series automated wireless network test platform." The platform can simulate how a WiFi access point will perform, for example, when 1,000 business people at a convention center are all trying to check e-mail simultaneously.

Once the product was finished and almost ready to ship, Azimuth raised a second round of $7 million in July to help support sales and marketing. The company shipped its first test platform to a paying customer in late September, just about a year after it received its first venture capital.

"We have an antibubble strategy," said Azimuth chief executive Ray Cronin. "The whole thing isn't about raising money. It's about solving a real problem and developing a business model that's sustainable and profitable. We focused on getting customers before we started worrying about PR."

Building a company without big bucks isn't a new experience for Cronin, even though he previously worked for the heavily funded optical networking start-up Quantum Bridge, which raised a single round of $102 million back in 2000. Cronin, now 45, started his first company at age 28 with money from a second mortgage on his parents' house in Scituate.

"When you talk about real funding with real pressure, that's hard to beat," Cronin said. "My father used to joke, `Let me know when I should buy a tent from Sears.' "

Angels get organized

Since the spring, Boston's organized groups of angel investors have been getting together every other month at the offices of CommonAngels in Lexington. Angels are typically loose-knit collectives of successful entrepreneurs who meet occasionally to evaluate start-up companies and sometimes to write checks.

Previously, Boston's angel groups "viewed each other as colleagues, but we all operated independently," said James Geshwiler, the managing director of CommonAngels. "Each one has a different personality." The groups involved are Hub Angels, Walnut Venture Associates, CommonAngels, Angel Healthcare Investors, the eCoast Angels from the Portsmouth area, and Launchpad Venture Group.

Geshwiler doesn't think that any of the groups will merge as a result of the meetings. "We think it's important to be specialized," he said. But the groups have co-invested in the same start-ups in the past, and the regular meetings in Lexington focus primarily on how the groups can "coordinate deal flow" and work together better in the future, Geshwiler said.

Geshwiler was also involved in the Angel Summit, a meeting of angel groups from around the country that took place last week in Washington. That meeting could create a national organization, in partnership with the Ewing and Marion Kauffman Foundation, to conduct research into angel investing trends and help angel groups share knowledge.

Wait till next year

Over the past several years, start-ups have had a hard time raising money from venture capitalists. Soon, it could be venture capitalists facing a fund-raising challenge.

Todd Hixon, managing director of the $50 million Draper Fisher Jurvetson New England Fund, says that 2004 will be a key year for some of the biggest Boston venture funds. Who'll be able to attract more money from their investors, the so-called limited partners like university endowments, wealthy families, and state pension funds?

"More than half of the major funds are planning to go to their investors in 2004," Hixon said. "The limited partners will be deciding which of the top names are still really top names. Some firms will have a very rough experience."

Venture capitalists have held on to cushy offices and high salaries through the downturn. Those who can't attract new money in 2004, though, will feel a delayed hit from the recession and their underperforming tech investments of the dot-com era. That could produce more empty office space in Waltham and Wellesley.
