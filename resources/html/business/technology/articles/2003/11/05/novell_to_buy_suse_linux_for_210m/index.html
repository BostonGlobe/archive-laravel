Novell to buy SUSE Linux for $210m

Firm's commitment to software boosted

By Hiawatha Bray, Globe Staff, 11/5/2003

Computer networking company Novell Inc. will acquire Germany's SUSE Linux AG, the world's second-largest vendor of Linux operating system software. The $210 million deal cements Novell's commitment to the open source operating system, and poses a major challenge to the number one Linux company, Red Hat Inc. of Raleigh, N.C.

It's the second major Linux-related acquisition this year for Novell, which is officially headquartered in Utah but managed from offices in Cambridge. In August Novell bought Ximian Inc., a privately held Boston company that made desktop productivity software for Linux computers. In addition, Novell is migrating its popular networking and directory software products to run on Linux, and on Novell's own Netware operating system.

Novell invented personal computer networking in the 1980s, and is still widely used in the world's major businesses and government agencies. But for the past decade, the company's Netware software has steadily lost market share to Microsoft Corp.'s Windows.

With the SUSE deal, Novell now has complete control over one of the most popular versions of Linux, a formerly obscure operating system that today has about one-quarter of the market for computer servers. "With SUSE we get one of the top two Linux distributions on the planet," said Novell chief executive Jack Messman. "The SUSE merger allows Novell to offer a complete stack from the desktop to the server."

SUSE chief executive Richard Seibt said the deal would give his company access to Novell's well-established sales channels, and to customers in North America, where SUSE has had limited success thus far. "Together with Novell, we can drive the adoption of Linux much faster than we could have on our own," Seibt said.

In addition, IBM Corp., the world's largest computer firm and a major Linux booster, agreed to invest $50 million in Novell, giving IBM about 2 percent of Novell's stock. IBM spokesman Mike Darcy said the move was in keeping with similar investments IBM has made in Red Hat, SUSE and other Linux companies. "IBM is not a Linux distributor and has no interest in being in that business," said Darcy. Instead, IBM invests in Linux firms "to assure customer choice" by strengthening key suppliers of Linux software.

Dan Kusnetzky, software analyst at IDC Corp., said the deal could help Novell recapture some of the cachet it once had as a leading-edge software company, and give SUSE a far better shot at gaining share in the North American software market. "But I also think they face some challenges as a result of this move," he added. "The challenges center on creating a single unified culture." Novell's current management came aboard in 2001 after Novell bought Cambridge Technology Partners, a high-tech consulting firm. Then the company bought the Web services firm SilverStream, and now two Linux software companies. Kusnetzky said it's unclear whether so many different kinds of business cultures can coexist.

John Young, vice president of marketing at Red Hat, made a similar point. "They're a company that sells 99 percent of its software the old-fashioned way," Young said of Novell. He said Novell will find it hard to sell its proprietary Netware products and open-source Linux code. "They've got a foot in both camps," Young said. "They're trying to do something that no other company has tried -- to run a hybrid business model and succeed."
