Q&A

ThingMagic's Bernd Schoner on inventory technology

11/16/2003

On Nov. 4, when Wal-Mart Stores Inc. ordered its largest suppliers to put radio frequency identification tags on all boxes and pallets within 16 months, the retailer accelerated a movement to a new technology for managing inventory. RFID tags are integrated circuits that beam item-tracking data to transceivers. The move to the technology will benefit a number of Greater Boston consumer product suppliers and RFID vendors. One vendor is ThingMagic of Cambridge, which develops and sells tag readers. Bernd Schoner, the managing partner of ThingMagic, spoke with Globe technology writer Robert Weisman recently at the Future Forward 2003 summit in Portsmouth, N.H.

Q. What is the Wal-Mart Mandate?

A. The Wal-Mart Mandate basically demands that all of the 100 top suppliers have to be so-called EPC (electronic product code) ready by January 2005. That means they have to have RFID tags on every single case and every single pallet going into Wal-Mart.

EPC is the successor of the UPC codes - the universal product codes - embedded in bar codes. EPC is supposed to go with RFID tags.

Q. Who else is on the RFID bandwagon?

A. In terms of the mandate, the DOD (Department of Defense) followed ... with exactly the same mandate for their suppliers. In terms of other major retailers, it's very clear that everybody's extremely active and is very eager - running pilots, getting their suppliers to do similar things.

Q. Will RFID tags be universal in five years?

A. If you talk about a five-year time frame, I think they will certainly be universal in the supply chain, especially the consumer goods supply chain.

Q. What advantages will this bring to companies?

A. There's a bunch of different advantages. Avoiding shrinkage in the entire supply chain is a big goal, being able to figure out when and where a product got lost.

Optimizing processes - bar code scanning is great, but it takes a lot of time, a lot of people. Being able to track and avoiding out-of-stock (products) is a big issue that people want to deal with.

Q. Are the consumer privacy fears justified?

A. There is a big privacy debate going on.

Consumers are rightfully scared that the ability to track an object interferes with their privacy rights. But for this stage of the deployment, it's not so much an issue. We're talking about tagging pallets and cases which consumers will never see. This shouldn't affect them.

And the clear benefit is that out-of-stock is not only bad for the supplier, it's also bad for the consumer.

Q. How does ThingMagic benefit from the transition to RFID?

A. ThingMagic has, from its beginning about three years ago, been focusing on RFID readers, realizing that the devices that were available don't scale to supply chain applications. So we designed a bunch of new devices that we are now actively licensing to distributors and manufacturers.

Q. What role is the Boston area playing in the RFID rollout?

A. I think it's fair to say that, in some ways, Boston is the RFID capital of the world at this point. The Auto-ID Center (which pioneered RFID technology) was founded at MIT. Some of the big players are in Boston - some of the big end users, for example Gillette, and some of the vendor companies like us, like Oat Systems ... So for the past two or three years, the entire industry comes through Boston at least once a year, which is certainly very helpful to us.
