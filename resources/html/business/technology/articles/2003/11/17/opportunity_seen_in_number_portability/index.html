Opportunity seen in number portability

By Peter J. Howe, Globe Staff, 11/17/2003

Next Monday's arrival of a new policy letting cellphone owners keep their existing number when switching carriers has struck terror in the hearts of many wireless-carrier executives, who expect it will cost the industry hundreds of millions of dollars and set off a stampede of disgruntled consumers.

But "wireless number portability," as the Federal Communications Commission policy is formally known, is shaping up as a good business opportunity for a handful of companies, including those that make or recycle cellphones, help consumers move phone numbers from handsets, or sell customer-management software for carriers to hold down subscriber defections and operating costs.

Of the 151 million US wireless subscribers, anywhere from 10 million to 30 million are predicted to change service providers within coming months once they will be spared the hassle of getting a new phone number and having to inform scores of friends and business associates.

However, because each wireless carrier operates on a different communications system and radio frequencies, consumers making the switch will in almost every case have to buy a new phone when they sign up with a new carrier.

Christopher J. Foster, a wireless analyst with Technology Business Research in Hampton, N.H., said he thinks handset makers including Nokia, Motorola, and Samsung could see an extra 8 million to 10 million phone sales in the United States over the next year thanks to number portability. That would boost projected worldwide phone sales by 2 to 3 percent over the current industry consensus of about 400 million to 430 million phones sold.

Nokia spokesman Keith Nowak, however, said: "We haven't been able to make any definitive public statements" on how much Nokia might benefit from increased sales thanks to number portability.

"One thing that we can say is that we don't necessarily think that the effect will be as substantial as some people have suggested," Nowak said.

On the flip side of increased sales, programs that collect old phones to refurbish for sale in the Third World or donate to battered-women's shelters and other social programs are expecting a potential bonanza as subscribers switch and have to give up old phones.

Framingham-based office supply store chain Staples Inc., for example, over the last year has collected phones at its stores that are reclaimed by consumer-electronics recycler CollectiveGood.com, then resold in developing nations with profits benefiting the Sierra Club and other causes.

Last week, Staples launched an effort with the Boys & Girls Clubs of America, which runs 3,300 facilities nationally, to have 11- to 13-year-old "Torch Club" members collect phones for recycling over the next two months.

Kevin Sinclair, president of Wireless Zone, a 180-store Northeastern cellphone chain, said he expects the number of phones turned in to similar programs Wireless Zone runs will "dramatically increase" in coming weeks.

Consumers looking to hold on to long lists of contacts they have amassed over years of entering names and numbers into their cellphone may also be another opportunity. Many wireless-phone stores can transfer numbers from an old cellphone to a new phone using a cable and a computer.

But companies that sell systems letting people move numbers from phone to phone or to and from a personal computer think wireless number portability may create new demand.

"We believe this is an ideal opportunity that we should try to take advantage of," said David Wright, vice president of BVRP Software, based in Denver and France, which sells a $60 software program called Mobile PhoneTools that also comes bundled in several models of Motorola phones.

Wright said the need to move contact data from one phone to another might spur sells of the system, which can upload and download information between a phone and a computer using a cable or the infrared wireless data ports found on many PCs and phones.

Besides managing contact data, however, the product also lets people synchronize information between a phone directory and Microsoft Outlook list, download ring tones and pictures, and move digital photographs taken with a cellphone onto a computer to create a slide show-like presentation to share.

Quadstone, a 50-person Boston- and Edinburgh-based company that provides customer-analysis systems for telecommunications and financial-services companies, expects its telecom sales could double in the next year, thanks to wireless number portability, according to company president Mark Smith. The privately held company does not, however, break out actual sales figures.

Quadstone this week is launching a version of its system aimed at wireless carriers that helps them predict which of their customers may be at highest risk of switching to another carrier, so they can identify how to target customer-retention marketing. The company's clients already include T-Mobile, the sixth-largest US wireless carrier, and Verizon Communications' digital subscriber line high-speed Internet operation.

"We're expecting it to have a very big impact on our numbers," Smith said. Quadstone sells systems that analyze millions of consumers' habits and identify whether people are likely to switch because they are increasing their monthly minutes of calling, or because they initially signed up based on a onetime promotion or the availability of a certain kind of phone that competitors can now match.

Andrew Cole, a senior vice president of Boston consulting firm Adventis and director of its global wireless practice, said a wide range of "customer relationship management" companies like Quadstone will benefit from increased wireless phone switching.

"It's going to be so much more important than it's ever been for carriers to get serious about understanding their customers," Cole said. "The carriers traditionally have been focused on growth, but now they have to get serious about driving customer satisfaction."
