PERSONAL TECH

For online music, the choice is yours

By Michelle Johnson, 11/17/2003

It seems like all of a sudden somebody's turned up the volume on digital music. New online music stores and announcements of partnerships have been sprouting like weeds. A revamped Napster is back; Apple's iTunes threw open its doors to the Windows masses, and yesterday BestBuy and MusicNow took the wraps off their new "pay-per-play" service. Comcast has hooked up with RealOne's Rhapsody to offer its broadband subscribers downloadable tunes. MusicMatch, a popular "jukebox" service recently tacked on a music store. New offerings from Wal-Mart, Dell, Sony, and AOL are on tap, too. A recent Forrester Research report counted 10 online music ventures that have recently launched or are in the pipeline.

Apple got the ball rolling last April with the launch of its wildly popular iTunes store. After cutting a deal with the record labels, they began selling thousands of songs for 99 cents a track (albums, $9.99). Josh Bernoff, an analyst for Forrester, says Apple's Steve Jobs has "corralled everybody into the same spot." Meaning, we can look forward to more stores in iTune's image with songs for sale at a buck a pop and software that helps you organize, play, and transfer music to CD and portable players. Bernoff thinks that sites like BuyMusic, a "download only" store that doesn't offer such extras, won't be able to compete. The number of songs available on digital music store sites varies because they've struck different deals with the record labels. If you care about having access to the biggest number of songs, Napster's currently got the edge with 500,000 tracks. ITunes and Rhapsody are just behind with 400,000.Like musical taste, picking an online music store is a subjective thing. Take advantage of trial subscriptions and downloads to see which service is the best fit for your musical tastes. Here's a look at how they break down:

iTunes Credited with jumpstarting the online music store feeding frenzy, iTunes has gotten lots of kudos for its uncluttered look, typical Apple ease of use, and the 99-cents-per-track gimmick. (Time Magazine named iTunes the "coolest invention of 2003.")

If you want to download purchased music from iTunes to a portable player, your only option is the iPod.

The new Windows version of iTunes is identical to the Mac version. Everything you need to organize and transfer your music files to CD or the iPod is included. The program's main window is fairly clean, with playback controls on the upper left, a search box on the right, a pane along the left side that displays your library of stored files, songs you've purchased, and a link to the music store. A list of song tracks appears in a spacious window on the right.

Buttons along the bottom of the screen that do things like shuffle tracks or turn visual effects on are annoyingly cryptic, although descriptions do pop up when you hold your cursor over them. To burn a CD, you simply hit the "burn disc" button.

The music store's home page features exclusive downloads, new releases, celebrity playlists, and lists of top downloaded tracks and albums. While it's easy on the eye and easy to use, I can't say I find iTunes personally appealing. Function-wise, you can't quibble with iTunes. It simply works. Napster 2.0 The headphone-wearing kitty logo is back, sporting a new look thanks to new owner Roxio and a service formerly called "Pressplay" that's been wrapped into Napster to transform it into a legal music download site.

There's not much left of the old Napster, except for community forums where users can share playlists and yak on message boards. But there's a lot to like about the new Napster, including a cool, animated interface, 500,000 tunes to sample and fun stuff like a magazine and music videos.

Napster's main window puts the action in a large window on the left with controls for the music player, album cover art, and tracks on the right. You can either drill down through lists to find what you're looking for, use the search box or the tabbed navigation.

BuyMusic And now for something a little different, BuyMusic is strictly a download site. There's no software to download to manage your files or give you access to the music store. To buy, you just use your computer's browser. They recommend Windows Media Player to handle chores such as transferring songs to a portable player or burning a CD. That said, it may take a few more steps to figure out what to do with your files once they're downloaded, but if you're a regular user of Windows Media Player, no problem. In the nice touch department, there are video tutorials on the site's home page that step you through the basics.

Like other sites, there are restrictions on what you can do with the tracks that you buy, but here they're a little more confusing because the rules vary by restrictions set by the record label. So, for instance, one song may allow you to burn a CD three times and other allows 10 burns. Be sure to check before you hit the payment button.

Rhapsody Rhapsody, an offering from RealOne, best known for its media players, also falls in the slightly different category. You can't download music to your computer's hard drive or directly to a portable device here, however you can burn tracks onto a CD. Think "jukebox" at this site. Subscribe for $9.95 a month, and you can listen unlimited. If you want to buy tracks, you can burn them to a CD for 79 cents each. To burn a CD you simply drag and drop tracks onto a burn list window and hit the burn button.

Rhapsody's interface will seem a little familiar to users of the RealOne media player, with a control panel along the top of the screen. While nicely designed, Rhapsody's interface feels a little claustrophobic.

MusicMatch Prior to adding a download store, MusicMatch's claim to fame was as a jukebox that excelled at recommending music based on the user's taste.

Like Rhapsody, there's a nice control panel along the top of the screen, and navigation is clearly organized under labels including "Play," "Manage" and "Copy." Easy, easy, easy.
