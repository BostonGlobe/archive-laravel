Raytheon, Harris vie for $1.66b Navy pact

11/6/2003

Raytheon Co., the largest missile maker, and Harris Corp., a maker of communications equipment, will compete for a US Navy satellite communication system contract that could be worth $1.66 billion over 10 years. The two companies won separate awards to develop prototypes over the next 2 1/2 years, said a spokesman for the Navy's Space and Naval Warfare Systems Command. Raytheon's award for the first year is worth $3.1 million. Harris won $4.48 million, and additional funds will be given to both later, he said. The rival designs will compete to determine the final production award, with system delivery completed by 2014. If Lexington-based Raytheon wins, the total award could be worth as much as $1.66 billion, while Florida-based Harris would get as much as $1.47 billion if its system is selected, based on their respective bids for the project, the Navy said. (Bloomberg)

Federal prosecutors investigating IDX

Federal prosecutors in Seattle are investigating Burlington, Vt.-based IDX Systems Corp. after an employee in Washington state accused the company of making false statements on an application for a federal grant, company officials said. IDX officials said the investigation into the grant with the National Institute of Standards and Technology had been under way for several months and was nearing completion. "The government is required by statute to investigate," said an IDX spokeswoman. "We believe [the allegations] to be without merit." IDX Systems makes healthcare data systems and software for doctors and hospitals, including scheduling and billing systems. (AP)

Increased expenses widen Cubist loss

A 52 percent increase in operating expenses was the main reason Cubist Pharmaceuticals Inc. had a wider loss in the third quarter. The Lexington company had no revenue in the latest quarter, compared with $5.8 million last year. Total operating expenses rose to $30.9 million from $20.3 million, with most of the increase coming from lease-termination charges of $9.1 million related to a facility in the United Kingdom. As a result, the company's net loss widened to $34.2 million or $1.12 a share, from $17 million or 60 cents last year. (Dow Jones)

Army center, 2 contractors get $10m

The US Army Soldier Systems Center in Natick and two contractors would receive more than $10 million for research under recent defense appropriations, said US Representative Edward J. Markey of Malden and US Senator Edward M. Kennedy, both Democrats. Under bills approved by Congress but awaiting the president's signature, the funds would be used to build a $5.5 million equipment-testing facility. In addition, contractors funded under the bills would include Foster-Miller Inc. of Waltham, which would receive $3 million for combat-casualty care research, and Battelle Systems at the Natick center, which would get $2 million for research into protective clothing, the lawmakers said. (Ross Kerber)

THE NATION

Factory orders rebound in September

Orders to US factories rebounded in September, rising 0.5 percent -- another signal pointing to an economic resurgence. That marked a turnaround from the 0.3 percent dip reported for August, according to revised figures released by the Commerce Department. The decline for August turned out to be not as deep as the 0.8 percent drop the government previously reported. September's performance was slightly weaker than the 0.6 percent increase economists were forecasting. Gains were led by big-ticket goods, including cars, machinery, and furniture. In a more forward-looking report, manufacturing in October posted its highest level of activity in nearly four years, the Institute for Supply Management said. (AP)

Sources: GM plans gas-electric SUVS

General Motors Corp. is expanding its gasoline-electric hybrid lineup to include two full-size sport utility vehicles, the Chevrolet Tahoe and GMC Yukon, GM sources said. The two sources, who spoke on condition of anonymity, said the automaker plans to begin offering hybrid versions of the two SUVs in 2007. GM and other automakers have invested billions to develop more ecologically friendly vehicles to meet stricter standards for emissions and fuel economy. Hybrids draw power from two different energy sources, typically a gas or diesel engine combined with an electric motor. For now, the only versions available in the United States are small cars made by Honda Motor Co. and Toyota Motor Corp. (AP)

US agencies, Microsoft offer rewards

The FBI and Secret Service are teaming up with Microsoft Corp. to launch a new Anti-Virus Reward Program that will pay cash bounties for information leading to the arrest of virus creators. Microsoft is putting up $5 million in reward money, perhaps because most of the worst virus attacks of recent years have targeted computers that use Microsoft's Windows operating systems. The first specific bounties offered are aimed at the creators of the SoBig and Blaster computer worms. Rewards of $250,000 will be paid for information that helps catch the creators of those programs. (Hiawatha Bray)

Napster said to offer deal to Penn State

Napster, the song-swap pioneer turned online music store, is expected to disclose a deal with Pennsylvania State University to offer its newly relaunched music service for free to students, sources familiar with the matter said. Napster officials and university officials declined to comment. But Penn State issued a statement saying its president, Graham Spanier, and the president of a leading online music service would reveal a deal in Anaheim, Calif., today to provide digital music to one of the largest student bodies in the country, at no cost to students. People familiar with the matter said Napster was the partner. (Reuters)

Judge OK's Poland Spring settlement

Nestle SA, the world's largest food company, won an Illinois judge's approval of a $12 million settlement of claims that its Poland Spring water isn't naturally pure spring water from the Maine woods. Circuit Judge Michael Colwell in Kane County approved the settlement, which provides customers with $8 million in discounts and coupons over five years and requires the company to make $2.75 million in charitable donations. The settlement of the class-action lawsuit may block 11 similar lawsuits over Poland Spring. The Illinois complaint claimed that Nestle misled consumers because Poland Spring water is not naturally pure and doesn't come from deep in the Maine woods, protected sources, or even a spring. "I think it's fairly obvious that a fair settlement isn't one that gives out coupons for more pond water," said Thomas Sobol, an attorney for consumers who object to the settlement. The settlement "effectively and expeditiously moves all parties beyond the costly litigation process," Nestle said. (Bloomberg)

Senate approves more ID protections

The Senate agreed to give Americans new weapons against identity theft, including access to their credit scores, a free e-mailed copy of their credit report annually, and one-call-for-all fraud reporting. But the bill approved by the Senate 95-2 also preempts tougher state privacy laws that prevent businesses from sharing customers' financial information with other companies, consumers groups complain. Business owners say that keeps the economy working smoothly under one national set of privacy laws, rather than 50 different state rules. At issue are changes to the Fair Credit Reporting Act, which created a national credit reporting standard to make it easier for people to get credit cards, loans, and mortgages. (AP)
