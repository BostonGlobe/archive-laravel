PERSONAL TECH

Devices turn business cards digital

By Michelle Johnson, 11/3/2003

How many business cards do you have littering your desk? How many of them have you entered into your computer's address book? Yeah, right. While a lot of folks attach their contact info to the end of e-mail these days, paper business cards haven't exactly gone away. Problem is, while cards are easy to carry and hand out, there's a disconnect between them and the computers, PDAs, cellphones, and other digital contact managers that we rely on these days.

Enter the business card scanner, a device that promises to cut down on the time-consuming task of getting contact information from hard copy into software. Simply feed in a card and name, address, phone number, e-mail, etc., magically appear on your computer screen.

Well, the good news is that it does pretty much work that way. I recently test drove a couple of these diminutive scanners and they all worked pretty much as advertised. They all did a reasonable job at "OCR," or optical character recognition, meaning that they turned a scanned image of a card into text which was then dumped into contact management software.

Garbled text was minimal. A slightly bigger problem was things appearing in the wrong field, for example, a company name ending up under "job title." Still, fixing errors is easy, and on the whole, the process is painless.

While it may seem a little crazy to plunk down hard-earned cash for a gizmo just to handle business cards, if you're in a business where cards fly across meeting tables at alarming rates, a card scanner can definitely minimize some of the contact management insanity.

Power card jockeys will definitely want to look at the $249 Corex CardScan 600cx. Cambridge-based Corex has been in the business for 10 years and it shows. They've tacked some useful features onto a product that some might view as a gadget for lazy typists.

The CardScan is solidly designed, with a flip-down tray on the back to catch exiting cards. It connects to the computer using a USB cable and scans in color. While the company notes that the scanner has come down in size from previous versions, it's still bulkier than its competitors. However, bulky is relative. The CardScan is small enough fit into a briefcase if you need to take it on the road.

Corex gets a thumbs up for including a full print manual in the box instead of just on CD, as should be the case in this price range. The CD includes a useful video overview of the product as well as documentation.

The included CardScan software displays entries as an on-screen Rolodex (remember those?), and is fairly straightforward to use. You can "batch" scan cards, handy for instance if you want to add a note that they were all acquired at the same event.

The program synchronizes with popular contact management programs such as Microsoft Outlook and Lotus Notes as well as Palm and PocketPC PDAs. CardScan costs more than other card scanners on the market, but it offers more.

For instance, you get access to CardScan.net, which creates a backup of your address book online that can be accessed from a Web browser. More interestingly, the CardScan comes with AccuCard, a service that keeps your address book up-to-date by periodically sending out a message to your contacts asking them to weed out old info.

After a quick setup, I was able to sit back and watch AccuCard and the CardScan software handle incoming updates to my address book. CardScan recently announced a stand-alone version of AccuCard for Microsoft Outlook for $50 a year (trial download, corex.com). It'll be interesting to see how this product fairs against contact updaters such as Plaxo Contacts (plaxo.com) and Relationship Secure (relationshipsecure.com, lite version) that offer a similar service for free.

The Corex bundle of a time-saving scanner and time-saving software/service is worth the price of admission if there's a need to tame a lot of data.

Another winning combination for a little less ($129), NewSoft's BizCard Reader and Presto Biz Card software, are also easy to use. If there's barely room to squeeze another device onto your desk, the BizCard Reader is pocket-size and sports a snazzy metallic finish with a scan button on the unit.

The software, Presto Biz Card, sports a snazzier look and feel than the CardScan, too, with the ubiquitous Rolodex on the left side of the screen, a list view on the right, and easy to use menu buttons. I particularly liked calling up cards in "album" or page view, which helps you visually pinpoint an entry.

To edit an entry you click on it to open an edit window. Like the CardScan, Presto Biz Card displays a graphical version of the card, but only in back and white.

For simple contact management, I preferred Presto Biz Card to CardScan, but the additional functionality of the AccuCard service makes CardScan tough to turn down.

Another basic card scanner that won't break the bank, the Targus Mini USB, costs $129. It's also bundled with the Presto software and scans in black and white. Like the others, setup is quick and the Targus scanner is pocket-size.

Choosing between the Targus and the Presto BizCard Reader is a toss-up. The BizCard comes with a detailed printed manual if you care about that sort of thing, and the Targus scanner doesn't include the scan button on the unit.

Other players in this space include the Iris Business Card Reader II, which is compatible with Mac and Windows computers ($169 Windows, $149 Mac) and the PenPower WorldCard Office scanner ($160).

Finally, if you already own a "regular" scanner, note that you don't have to purchase a special card scanner just to take advantage of business card manager software. It's available separately. With the Presto software you can scan up to eight cards at once on a flat-bed scanner.
