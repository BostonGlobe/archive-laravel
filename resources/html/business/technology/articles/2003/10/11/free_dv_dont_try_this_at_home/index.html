Free DV: Don't try this at home

By John P. Mello Jr., Globe Correspondent, 10/11/2003

Free video editing programs like Apple's iMovie and Microsoft's Windows Movie Maker don't have anything to fear from Avid Free DV.

ADVERTISEMENT

Avid's offering is a pale version of its robust Express line of editing products, which range from about $1,000 to $1,695.

Professional video editors who use an Avid competitor may jump at the chance to take a free look at how Avid does things. But for the merely curious, this software is a nightmare. It isn't intuitive. It obfuscates its functions with jargon. And its interface is daunting. That's quite a checklist of negatives when you take into account there are no tutorials to explain the workings of the program, its help files are unhelpful, and there's no technical support.

Avid is giving away this product for zilch, so it certainly doesn't want to lose money choking its support lines with people too cheap to buy a proper version of the software. But it isn't going to make any friends with the poor naifs who download this software.

The easiest thing I found about it was its installation on a PC. After that, it was all downhill. When I booted the software, a splash screen popped up welcoming me to the Avid family and showing me what I was missing by not buying a $1,000 version of a program that I had yet to try. Free DV has your basic editing functions, two audio tracks, two video tracks, and 16 special effects, while the program's buff brothers support eight to 24 audio and video tracks, and over 100 effects and other goodies like unlimited video layers and third-party plug-in support.

With intuitive programs like iMovie and Movie Maker, you connect your video camera to a FireWire port on your computer, click capture, and video is transferred to the program. The software even cuts the video into clips and places them on a palette where they can be dragged to a time line and organized.

When I followed those procedures with Free DV, my video camera started running for about 30 seconds, then I received a message that the audio and video settings were incompatible. The program offered to make the proper adjustments for me, and I permitted it do so. But all the video I'd captured to that point was lost.

With audio and video properly aligned, I tried to capture more video. This time I was told that I needed to open a bin. What's a bin? I didn't know, but there was a bin window open in the program and creating a new one was easy enough to do.

Bin open, I tried to capture video yet again. I clicked the record button on Free DV and my video camera started rolling, and it appeared that the program was sucking up the video. I say appeared because while the video counter was operating on the screen, there was no display of video in the software.

After four minutes or so, I stopped the capture. But I was never able to display any video on my computer.

Annoyed and frustrated, I closed the bin window. Up popped the welcome screen. Welcome to the Avid Famiy! Enjoy your Avid editing experience! My response was not suitable for publication.
