Grand vision

Apple opens its iTunes store to millions of Windows users

By Chris Gaither, Globe Staff, 10/17/2003

SAN FRANCISCO -- Apple Computer Inc. yesterday opened its successful online music store to the millions of personal computers running Windows, as it seeks to push digital downloading of songs from a fringe interest to a mainstream way to buy music.

Apple also said it had struck a deal to sell its iTunes Music Store offerings through America Online and plans to give away 100 million free downloads through a promotion with PepsiCo Inc.

In a colorful press conference here, Apple CEO Steve Jobs said the alliances give the upstart computer company two powerful corporate partners to help encourage people who swap files for free over the Internet to begin embracing paid services.

"We're going to fight illegal downloading by competing with it," Jobs said, in a jab at the recording industry's lawsuits against 261 accused digital pirates. "We're not going to sue it, we're not going to pretend it doesn't exist."

The marketing blitz by Apple and the upcoming launch of Roxio Inc.'s Napster 2.0, a paid service for downloading and streaming songs that bears the name of the original file-sharing program used to exchange copyrighted songs for free, could provide the boost to digital distribution that the recording industry needs, analysts said.

The field of companies selling digital music downloads is growing crowded. Napster 2.0 is set to launch Oct. 29. Musicmatch Inc., BuyMusic.com, and others have already set up shop. And Dell Inc., Microsoft Corp., Yahoo Inc., Amazon.com, and others are planning or considering their own services.

Apple, based in Cupertino, Calif., said its catalog would reach 400,000 songs by the end of the month. The company also introduced features for the music store: iTunes now allows customers to send gift certificates, set a monthly download allowance for their children, and buy downloadable audio books and interviews from National Public Radio through a partnership with Audible Inc.

Apple introduced iTunes Music Store in April, but only for the 3 percent of computers running the Macintosh operating system. The service has nevertheless sold 13 million songs for 99 cents each, or as part of purchased albums, Jobs said yesterday. He set a new goal of 100 million songs sold by the service's one-year anniversary.

That would be nearly impossible without opening the service to users of Microsoft's Windows operating system. The new strategy of catering to both camps is a bold change for a company that for years has positioned itself as an alternative to Microsoft's monopoly in PC operating systems. With profit margins slim in selling songs, Apple hopes use the Music Store to sell more iPod portable music players to Windows and Macintosh users.

Jobs called iTunes for Windows something "a lot of people thought we would never add until this happened," then pointed to a sign reading, "Hell Froze Over."

To use Apple's Music Store, PC users must download the iTunes software. Jobs said the Windows version is identical to the Mac version. Yet Jobs could not resist a dig at the software giant whose operating system is supporting Apple's digital music ambitions.

"ITunes for Windows is probably the best Windows app ever written," he said, referring to a software application.

Jobs enlisted music superstars Mick Jagger, Bono, Dr. Dre, and Sarah McLachlan to help promote the new Windows version of his music service. McLachlan performed two songs, seated at a piano beneath an enormous illuminated Apple logo.

Jagger, whose band, the Rolling Stones, only recently made its music available on such for-fee music services, acknowledged the rocky start the music industry faced in dealing with the distribution of music over the Internet.

"It took so long for this format to come along that it actually makes sense," he said in a live feed from London. "What could have been a wonderful start has been a lot of bad feelings, with Napster, and people chasing each other."

Bono, of the band U2, teased Jobs, calling the meeting of Windows and iTunes "like the pope of software meeting up with the Dali Lama of integration." He called iTunes important for musicians and their fans. "This is new territory, making Apple a crossroads for all kinds of artistic endeavors," Bono said from Dublin.

By Christmas, the 25 million users of America Online can download music on the AOL Music website by clicking on an iTunes button next to each song.

"Steve Jobs and Apple untied the Gordian knot of online music," AOL chief executive Jon Miller said during the press conference.

AOL already sells subscriptions to its MusicNet service, which streams songs and allows limited downloading. AOL will still sell MusicNet subscriptions, but iTunes will become AOL's primary download service, Evan Harrison, general manager of AOL Music, said in an interview.

Apple also plans to promote iTunes through a major campaign with Pepsi. For 60 days starting Feb. 1, 2004, the beverage maker will issue 300 million bottles of Pepsi, Diet Pepsi, and Sierra Mist with yellow iTunes caps. One in three will contain directions to download one free 99-cent song from iTunes.

Such marketing arrangements are crucial for publicizing these relatively young music services, said Susan Kevorkian, a senior analyst with the research firm International Data Corp. "Consumers are only beginning to discover paid music services, and Apple is playing an important role in that discovery," she said.
