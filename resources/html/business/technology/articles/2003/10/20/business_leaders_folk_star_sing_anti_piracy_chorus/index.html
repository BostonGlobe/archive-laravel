Business Leaders, Folk Star Sing Anti-Piracy Chorus

By Chawadee Nualkhair, 10/20/2003

BANGKOK (Reuters) - As Thailand cracked down on the sale of brand-name fakes during an Asia-Pacific summit, business leaders and a folk singer urged more action on Monday to stop counterfeiters robbing them of billions of dollars a year.

Microsoft Corp and Warner Music executives took the stage with Thailand's most popular folk singer, Yuenyong Opakul, to demand a halt to piracy.

Yuenyong said efforts by Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra to stamp out intellectual piracy had not ended it Thailand.

"Piracy has slowed down the development of the music industry," he told a gathering of Pacific Rim business leaders. "I create my work and then somebody wants to get it free."

If it continued, "I cannot stay in music, I'd have to change to be an owner of a restaurant," he said.

The business leaders meeting, running in tandem with an Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) forum summit, was held in a luxury hotel just five minutes walk from a central Bangkok street usually lined with stalls selling fake goods.

But police have cleared most hawkers off the street, saying they can return next week, when the APEC meeting is over.

Microsoft Senior Vice President Craig Mundie said ownership of intellectual property had become blurred in a world where information was available cheaply via television or the Internet.

He said technological advances made it possible for counterfeiters to produce perfect copies at cut-rate prices.

TIP OF ICEBERG

"What you see happening, I believe" Mundie said, "is just the tip of an iceberg which affects the industry and the economics of global trade in a much bigger way than people fundamentally understand."

"The reality is this notion of counterfeiting in physical goods, in branded goods, is already a huge problem," he said.

Piracy could potentially cripple the $32 billion music industry, said Paul-Rene Albertini, president of Warner Music International.

"The 21st century is about defending what the human brain is creating and putting value behind it," he said.

"So we have to fight this fight. Perhaps we will lose a generation of music-buyers, that's fine, but we should not lose the rest of the battle."

Intellectual piracy remains a touchy issue in Thailand, well-known for its hundreds of sidewalk vendors selling brand-name knock-offs and DVD copies of Hollywood movies at a fifth of the cost of an original.

Forty-five-year-old Thui, selling jogging pants and T-shirts at a stall set up in Patpong, a strip well-known for its go-go girls and branded knock-offs, said police had asked her to sell Thai-made original goods during the summit.

But sales have slipped.

"Normally people like to buy brand names," said Thui, sitting alone at her stall as pedestrians streamed past. "No one wants to buy the Thai stuff."
