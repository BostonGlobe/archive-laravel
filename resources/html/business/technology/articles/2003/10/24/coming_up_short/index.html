Coming up short

Raytheon reports some positive news amid loss but questions of past problems dog defense firm

By Ross Kerber, Globe Staff, 10/24/2003

Raytheon Co. attributed disappointing third-quarter results yesterday to stumbles on a Brazilian radar pact and delays in other programs, which reminded analysts of previous shortcomings at the Lexington defense contractor.

The company reported a loss of $35 million for the three months ended Sept. 28, compared with a profit of $147 million for the same period a year ago, even though third-quarter sales rose 7 percent to $4.4 billion.

Shares fell 94 cents to close at $26.71 as the company lowered its operating profit estimates for 2004 to $1.50 to $1.60 a share from $1.60 to $1.70 a share, though it raised its sales guidance for next year.

Chief executive William H. Swanson, who moved to the top job in July, vowed deep management reviews to address the problems.

"I'm convinced that these things are all readily fixable, and we know how to fix them," Swanson told analysts in a morning conference call. "I'm upset and disappointed that we're in the situation. This is not my style, as many of you know, and it won't be the company's going forward. We will change."

With 11,000 of its 76,000 employees in Massachusetts, Raytheon is one of the state's largest employers, including about 7,500 people who work for its Integrated Defense Systems unit in Tewksbury. With other military divisions, that unit reported positive results yesterday, with quarterly operating income rising to $82 million from $70 million the year before.

Raytheon credited growth in the Navy's DD(X) destroyer program and payment for sending "roughly 44" engineers to support the Army's Patriot missile units during the Iraq war.

Those gains were more than offset by problems at the company's Network Centric Systems in McKinney, Texas, however, which led to total charges of $187 million.

Most related to 10 programs now running behind schedule. Raytheon said the programs involved air traffic control and communications systems, but would name only one, SIVAM, a $1.4 billion venture in Brazil. Work on a network to monitor environmental changes in the Amazon jungle and movements near the border began in July 1997 and was to have been completed by the middle of last year. It won't be completed until March 2004 at the soonest, Raytheon said.

Raytheon had mentioned the SIVAM problems after its second quarter but left its profit outlook unchanged. Because of the charges, operating profit fell to $21 million, or 5 cents a share, compared with a profit of $228 million, or 56 cents a share, for the same period a year ago. Analysts had expected operating income of 43 cents per share, on average, according to a Thomson survey.

Analysts had many questions about the company's performance, which Byron Callan of Merrill Lynch termed "a pretty miserable string of negative surprises" dating to previous quarters under former chief executive Daniel P. Burnham, still the company's chairman. Bear, Stearns analyst Steven Binder, said additional charges have "been the pattern for a long time out of this company."

"How would you really differentiate this review process from what the prior administration might have done? This is your first real test as CEO," Binder said to Swanson. Swanson and chief financial officer Edward S. Pliner vowed stricter management reviews; in a telephone interview Pliner said Raytheon's issues were less severe than four years ago when it struggled with acquisitions. "This company has good processes and good disciplines, and we know how to deliver on what our customers are looking for," Pliner said.

Raytheon said its results would cause a technical default on some borrowing, though it expects lenders will let it to remain in compliance. A spokesman said Colin Schottlaender will remain president of Network Centric Systems but that other personnel changes will occur.

Another problem area was the Technical Services group, in Virginia, which had an operating loss of $2 million after losing a bid to continue operating the Pentagon's Kwajalein missile range in the Pacific.

Robert Stallard, an analyst at Banc of America, who reduced the outlook on Raytheon to "sell" in September, said he doesn't expect the company's outlook to improve soon. "If Swanson had come in and this was his first month on the job, you would say the guy is clearing the decks" by issuing bad news, Stallard said. "But he's been there a while. It kind of gives me the impression that there isn't an easy fix." Stallard said.
