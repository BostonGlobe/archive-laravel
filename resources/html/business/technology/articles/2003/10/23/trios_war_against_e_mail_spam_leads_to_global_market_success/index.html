BOXBOROUGH

Trio's war against e-mail spam leads to global market success

By Nancy V. Burns, Globe Correspondent, 10/23/2003

Some of the biggest advances in the fight against spam, those unwanted e-mails that infest computers around the globe, are taking place in a home office in Boxborough.

ADVERTISEMENT

Audiotrieve -- which introduced the antispam product InBoxer in July and released the first update, InBoxer 1.1, on Tuesday -- has built up a global business almost entirely online. The company consists of three principals, each of whom works from a home office, along with independent contractors who pitch in as needed.

Chief Executive Roger Matus lives in Boxborough; Sean True, the chief technologist, lives in Natick; and Laura Strassman, vice president of product management, works from her home in Carlisle. They get together in person about once a week -- usually over lunch on Wednesdays at one of their home offices or occasionally at a restaurant. Phone calls and e-mails suffice for the rest of the week.

The three founders are speech-recognition specialists who had worked together and kept in touch for many years before deciding to create their own company. Audiotrieve was formed early this

year to create software for web searches of audio and video that are currently inaccessible to Google and other search engines. But a technical discovery came along at the right time, which resulted in an abrupt change of focus. InBoxer resulted from a project that True developed in his spare time to address his previous employer's frustrations with spam. True had been working for the permission-based direct marketer WebReply, which sends out newsletters and other items. Spam filters were interfering with legitimate messages, so True started learning about spam and created a prototype of an antispam filter for Audiotrieve.

InBoxer works by analyzing both the text of a message and the "invisible parts," including the time it was sent, how it was shipped, where it came from, and what "node" it went through. A custom filter is automatically set up for each person. That way, for example, though a doctor might want to receive messages about certain prescription drugs, such e-mail for most users would be filtered out as spam.

In a test conducted by Spamotomy, an online publication that evaluates products developed to combat junk mail, InBoxer screened out 96 percent of spam, with almost no false positives. The filtered messages are not automatically deleted, but are moved to a location where users can find them and decide whether to unblock them.

"We saw a market opportunity and turned our business on a dime," said Matus, "and decided to go after the antispam market with this product."

It took the company three months to release InBoxer, on July 7. Twelve copies were sold in the first two weeks. Since then, it has sold 1,700 copies on six continents, at $24.95 each, with about 15,000 trial copies downloaded. "We're still waiting on Antarctica," Matus said with a laugh.

InBoxer has been listed in Wired and PC magazines, and discussed in the Associated Press radio program Chip Talk this month. In Italy, InBoxer was one of two antispam products described in the Sept. 25 issue of L'Espresso (which is similar to Time magazine in the United States). The product also made the cover of the computer section of Publico, the largest daily newspaper in Portugal and received Editor's Choice and Best Buy ratings from the British magazine Personal Computer World. .

The product received the highest rating ever given by Spamotomy. "Of the dozens of antispam products geared toward Windows desktop users, Audiotrieve's InBoxer is definitely one of the best on the market," said Spamotomy's publisher and editor, Amit Asaravala. "It's easy to install and use, and it works."

The company has no retail store, and doesn't intend to have one for the foreseeable future. The product is available only through downloading off the Internet, with a 21-day free trial. There is no manufacturing, packaging, or physical product involved. While the company can accept orders over the phone, Matus said, "only two or three people have done that, because they didn't want to use the web. We also sold a CD to one customer who wanted a backup copy."

Strassman has been working for Audiotrieve while her Carlisle home has undergone construction. "Working from home is great, and not great, with contractors who are still showing up at 6 a.m.," said Strassman. There have been interruptions to her e-mail server, requiring her to dial in rather than use a broadband connection.

Still, all three said they prefer to work at home, even though the lifestyle demands certain adjustments.

Like Strassman, True had undergone a year and a half of construction surrounding his home office. Although he found the experience "horrendous to live through," he said he wouldn't have had it any other way. "If you're a technologist, there are many fewer distractions; fewer people knocking on your door, looking for office stuff. I probably get five times as much done as I ever did in an office environment, maybe more," True said.

But the convenience of working at home doesn't eliminate the need for long hours, seven days a week. Matus puts on his bathrobe and pads downstairs to his office by 7 a.m., getting online and responding to questions from Europe. "By that hour, London has been in business for five hours, the rest of the continent by six, and Australia even longer," said Matus. Fielding questions from overseas and checking sales figures from the night before usually takes the first two hours of his day.

After fixing lunch for his wife, Natalie, and chatting over breakfast before she leaves for work, Matus showers and begins tending to his customers in the United States at about 9. If the day permits, he'll exercise or run errands for a few hours in the early afternoon. He has dinner with his wife when she returns home at around 7:30. Then it's back to the office until 11 p.m., because his customers in California tend to work late.

While there's overlap among their roles, each principal has distinct responsibilities. Strassman handles product management, product and technical marketing, and promotions. Matus does whatever it takes to run the company, from serving as its spokesman to completing the insurance contract for the business. True develops and refines the product, which requires a lot of discussion with customers.

"Sean will figure out what's possible," Strassman said, "and I'm more likely to figure out why it should or shouldn't happen."

Matus is amazed that the three were able to build a global business from a virtual company, and service thousands of people. "Our first sale was from a customer in Australia. Somewhere between a quarter and a third of our revenue is coming from outside the US," he said. "It's as if the borders vanish."
