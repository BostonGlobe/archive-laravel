Second acts

Taking R & D projects and running them to market is a TIAX specialty

By Robert Weisman, Globe Staff, 10/27/2003

The sounds of crunching and scribbling echo through a room at TIAX LLC in Cambridge. Three researchers are huddled around a table, sniffing and tasting stacks of barbecued chips, then making copious notes.

"Salty, two . . . smoky, one . . . starchy cardboard, one and a half," intones taste tester Patricia A. Keane, launching into a dueling banjos-style benchmarking riff.

"Chile power, one . . . caramelized sweet, one . . . raw and toasted starch, one and a half," tester Sarah Garretson Lowry chimes in.

The team is evaluating two brands of low-fat, low-carb snack chips already on the market to develop a competing brand for an established food company. "Our goal is to produce something not significantly different in flavor and texture from transfat-containing products," said food scientist Colleen M. Zammer, principal in the TIAX food and beverage group.

They call the process "reformulation," and the goal is making healthy foods that actually taste good.

This is R&D in a soft economy. Big companies that once spent lavishly on in-house product development are now subcontracting and licensing technology, while universities are seeking help commercializing research from their labs. Sensing an opening for independent research organizations, entrepreneur Kenan E. Sahin, the TIAX founder and president, purchased the former Arthur D. Little technology business 18 months ago for $16.5 million as a base for TIAX, an acronym for "technology and innovation applications, to the power of X."

But independent research and development can be risky in an era when customers are impatient for new products. Sahin is gambling that TIAX's cross-disciplinary approach and marketing focus can capitalize on some of the trends reshaping industrial, government, and academic research. The fate of some 300 TIAX engineers and scientists and a whole new model for results-oriented research are riding on his venture by Sahin, 62, a Turkish-born MIT professor turned technologist.

Food reformulation is just one of scores of TIAX research projects underway at about 50 labs, most of them nestled into a brick-faced research honeycomb tucked away behind the MBTA's Alewife station. Researchers there are integrating visual displays into combat gear, readying decontaminant foam for chemical and biological warfare agents, and improving the crashworthiness of trains.

Across town, in Central Square, other researchers are testing air quality, energy efficiency, and stairway safety at PlaceLab, a condominium purchased for research use in a venture with the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. Across the country, at California landfills, they're converting methane into energy.

From such diverse activities, Sahin hopes to fashion an entrepreneurial second act for himself and for the Arthur D. Little labs, which long were geysers of research.

Sahin sold his first company, billing software maker Kenan Systems, to Lucent Technologies in 1999 for $1.48 billion. Now he is hoping to carve out a niche for a privately held concern that can provide custom research for larger companies and draw on expertise in a broad range of disciplines to help academic researchers bring stalled projects to market. And, of course, he would be delighted if one or more of the myriad projects under his roof blossoms into a success like Kenan Systems.

"Right now, Massachusetts companies are full of innovation," said Sahin, who has contracts or licensing agreements with about 200 commercial and government clients. "The question is, who is going to take those innovations to the market? People are cranking out all these wonderful ideas, but there are impediments to implementation."

The phrase "moving innovation into implementation" is how Sahin sums up his ambitions for TIAX. He formed the company in May 2002 to take over the technology research projects he acquired from the bankrupt A.D. Little consulting firm, which was sold off in pieces. Concluding that they lacked critical mass, Sahin shut down some Little telecommunications and information technology projects the first summer, paring 18 jobs.

But he is pressing forward with dozens of other projects he inherited. And he has launched several initiatives, some in areas like portable power and food reformulation where Little already had expertise, but Sahin saw potential for greater collaboration with industry.

Competitors have been pursuing a similar course.

"We're seeing a resurgence in corporate America wanting to partner with R&D firms," said Richard Rosen, senior vice president at Battelle in Columbus, Ohio, the largest independent research and development house and also a private company.

"One of the things that's clearly been happening over the past couple of years is that corporations have to focus on performance at the expense of the early-stage R&D they might have done in the past. When this happens over a period of time, you get a gap in the R&D pipeline."

To address this gap, big companies often acquire smaller players with key technology in their fields. But they also farm out research to firms such as Battelle; Sarnoff Corp. in Princeton, N.J.; and TIAX.

Data collected by the National Science Foundation show that research and development subcontracting have more than doubled in recent years.

"The largest corporate labs have downsized significantly, and you're seeing larger companies relying more on smaller companies for research," said Kei Koizumi, an analyst for the American Association for the Advancement of Science.

"That trend has been accelerating because of tough economic times."

But even with the windfall from technology-hungry public companies that have reined in their own research, the independent houses face the challenge of managing a diverse research portfolio for government and industrial customers. While the government outlays often generate smaller returns, the companies are increasingly oriented toward short-term financial results and can be quick to pull the plug on contracted research that is moving too slowly.

"It's a high-risk kind of business," said Robert A. Howell, professor of business administration at Dartmouth's Tuck School of Business. "You're creating all these cells of talents, and if they don't generate breakthrough developments over time, then you might have to end the program and lay people off. The customer can buy a project and set an end point where they can get out fast if the research project doesn't succeed. There's a different kind of relationship than a company would have with its in-house research department. They don't feel as guilty about ending a contractual relationship."

Sahin -- who has taught at MIT, Harvard, and the University of Massachusetts at Amherst -- is hoping that his many connections in academic circles can give TIAX an edge in striking partnerships with university labs.

MIT's president, Charles M. Vest, is chairman of a TIAX advisory board, which also includes Dennis Ausiello, physician in chief at Massachusetts General Hospital. Citing historic ties with MIT -- the school owned Arthur D. Little from the late 1930s to the early 1950s -- Sahin said that renewing that relationship is a top priority.

TIAX researchers have been working with the MIT Media Lab on the living laboratory project, and with the Harvard School of Public Health on food reformulation.

"Part of TIAX's vision is to build effective collaborations with institutions in the Greater Boston area, as well as bridges to other companies and organizations," Vest said.

"TIAX is seeking a space between academic research with moderate to long time horizons and companies in need of research-driven innovation."

Sahin, for his part, said he would like to focus the company around such collaborations. With its technological expertise in a wide variety of industries and its practical know-how in fields from intellectual property to government regulation, TIAX can be quick to identify new applications for products and push them into fresh markets, he said.

"Our strength is we not only understand innovation; we also understand the `surround technologies' that enable us to take it to the marketplace," Sahin said.

"While innovation is very important, it's only 5 percent of what needs to be done. It's really taking it to the market that's the other 95 percent."
