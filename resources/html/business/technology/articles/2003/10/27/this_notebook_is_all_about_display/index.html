PERSONAL TECH

This notebook is all about display

Toshiba P25 offers 17-inch canvas for all you'd like to do

By John P. Mello Jr., 10/27/2003

With widescreens becoming the rage on the desktop and in the living room, it should come as no surprise that notebook computer makers have begun expanding their horizons, too. Apple Computer led the widescreen charge, first with 15-inch models, then with its coveted 17-inch PowerBook. Now a new crop of laptops with elongated LCDs are arriving on the PC scene. But unlike Apple's Powerbook 17, which still has a measure of portability at 6 pounds, the Windows-powered breed of wide-body notebook, which exceed 9 pounds, won't be moving far from its initial location.

An outstanding example of this new genre of desktop replacement is the Toshiba P25 line of portables. The units, which range in price from $2,039 to $2,399, are built around a desktop rather than a mobile processor. Depending on your power requirements, the notebooks have Pentium 4 chips running at from 2.4 to 3.2 gigahertz. My review unit, the S607, had a 2.8 gigahertz P4 that had no trouble handling conventional computing tasks, as well as more taxing multimedia applications. Bolstering the luggable's processor is a muscular NVIDIA GeForce FX Go5200 128-bit graphics accelerator, which has excellent 3D performance, a big plus for those who play as hard as they work with their computers.

Toshiba didn't skimp on memory or hard drive, either. The unit comes with 512 megabytes of RAM, expandable to two gigabytes, and a 60 megabyte hard drive, although larger drives are available.

What this notebook is all about, though, is its display. Its 17-inch LCD exhibits dazzling 32-bit color with a sharp native resolution of 1,440-by-900 pixels. If you're used to portables that cramp your style, you'll have nothing to worry about with the S607. This is the wide-open spaces with plenty of room to do whatever you want to do with a computer.

The unit's keyboard benefits from its designers not having to fret over size. At 16.4 by 11.5 by 1.8 inches, the computer is heftier than some of its rivals. Its keys are ample in size; while maintaining the short stroke found on most notebook claviers, they remain comfortable to the touch. Number crunchers, though, may be irked by the unit's embedded rather than separate number pad.

In addition to the standard key set, there are three keys beside the keyboard for quick access to the Toshiba console for configuring the computer's components, the S-video port for sending display information to a television and an Internet button for jumping into cyberspace. It also has a dial on the side of the unit for manually controlling its volume, a feature I found very handy, and a switch for turning the notebook's built-in wireless networking antenna off and on.

The S607 is nicely appointed with a dark blue and black exterior and spacious metallic silver interior deck with beige keyboard inset.

Some elements of the unit's ergonomics, though, need work. The controls for the device's optical drive -- which reads, writes, and rewrites both CDs and DVDs -- are on a beveled edge at the front of the computer where their labels are difficult to see, especially in low light. What's more, the ejection button for the drive is almost flush with the drive's door. That makes it almost invisible and a real annoyance to operate.

The S607 has all the inputs and outputs you'd expect in a 21st-century notebook and a few unexpected twists. There are four USB 2.0 ports, two in the right side, two on the back; modem and LAN sockets; headphone, microphone, and line-in jacks; parallel, monitor, and infrared ports; four-pin iLink, or FireWire, port; and a PC Card slot for Type II and III cards.

But the unit also has a slot for reading SD cards, commonly used for storage on digital cameras and handheld computers, and a television tuner and a remote control that can be used in conjunction with Microsoft Windows XP Media Center, which is factory installed on the computer.

Battery life for the unit is only two hours or so, but that shouldn't deter computer shoppers looking for a desktop replacement. If you're searching for mobility, you shouldn't be eyeing at a draft horse like the S607.

An wide assortment of software is included for working with multimedia -- music, digital imaging, and video -- as well as Microsoft Works for basic office tasks.

If you're a multimedia maven and you're looking for a dream PC notebook, this Toshiba model with its panoramic display, Harman Kardon speakers, and graphics oomph will make your creative juices bubble.
