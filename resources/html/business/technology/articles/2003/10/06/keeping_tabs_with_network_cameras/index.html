Keeping tabs with network cameras

By Michelle Johnson, 10/6/2003

Whether you need to keep an eye on your small business, your house, your kid, or the cat, you have your pick of video cameras that allow you to keep tabs on them from any Web browser.

These wireless network cameras, not to be confused with less savvy "Web cams" that retail for under $100, don't need to be tethered to a PC to work. Like Web cams, they send live video over the Internet, but the resemblance stops there.

Generally priced from $250 to $700 and up, wireless network cameras can be mounted pretty much anywhere there's a power source. And they come with built-in software, such as a Web server, that allows them to operate independently, sans the clutter of network cables.

A new contender in this category is the Linksys Wireless-B Internet Video Camera, a lightweight, stylish unit that operates both wired (ethernet) and wirelessly (802.11b, a k a "Wi-Fi"). It can either be wall-mounted or sit flat in the supplied stand and looks a bit like a camera lens stuck onto the top of a modem box.

If you already have a wireless network configured, as I do in my home office, set up using the quick installation guide should be fairly fast. You simply plug in the camera, install and run the setup software, and the network should recognize the camera and start sending out video. However, I made an error entering one piece of information about my network and was briefly stopped cold.

I mention this as a caution to carefully read the instructions even if you think you're a wiz at setting up wireless devices. "Fast, easy setup" can easily become a confusing mess if you don't enter the settings to the letter.

Oddly, the quick install guide for the Linksys camera gives short shrift to how to configure it for wireless operation, referring the user to the full manual that comes on the installation CD.

Once I cleared up my boneheaded mistake with the help of Linksys tech support, the camera operated flawlessly. It transmits MPEG-4 video at 320x240 resolution and can be set to e-mail an alert and short video attachment when it detects motion.

Note that during setup it helps to have a partner who can turn the focus ring on the camera's lens while you check out the result onscreen. Otherwise, you may have to make a couple of trips back and forth to get it right.

Therein lies one drawback of this camera. It can't be adjusted remotely. Focus, pan and tilt are a strictly manual affair; other similarly priced cameras offer this feature.

Using the included software, you can view what the camera's seeing live and record video to your hard drive, live or on a preset schedule. It's a sparse, utilitarian program, but easy to use. Viewing video outside of your home network requires some additional configuration, including setting up password-protected accounts that allow you to remotely manage access to the camera.

The Linksys Wireless Internet Video Camera will allow up to four simultaneous users, requires a PC with a 550MHz process or faster, Windows 98 or higher, a cable or DSL connection, and Internet Explorer 5.5 or higher.

Linksys recently launched a companion service for the camera called SoloLink. For $19.99 a year this "Dynamic Domain Name Service" takes the hassle out of remembering a numeric IP address assigned to the camera. Instead you (and your buds, employees, or anyone else you want to have a look) enter a personalized Web address such as "mycamera.our

linksys.com." The service comes with a free 90-day trial. I found the picture quality in daylight was acceptable, but it definitely goes downhill in low-light situations. It's a plus that the unit is designed to operate both wireless and wired, however the lack of remote focus, pan and tilt is disappointing.

If that feature's important to you, Veo's Wireless Observer might fit the bill. In the same price range ($299) as the Linksys camera, the Veo Observer's design has been compared to a UFO or something that would fit comfortably into the Jetson's decor.

Like the Linksys camera, this unit can sit flat or be wall-mounted using the supplied hardware. It also needs to be connected to a PC for the initial setup. However, Veo chose to do this via a cable connected to the computer's serial port. That's odd because this is older, slower technology and some new PCs no longer come equipped with serial ports.

Unfortunately two review units supplied by the company failed to work for me until I discovered a conflict with Microsoft's ActiveSync, a program that I use to sync my PocketPC PDA, which had apparently hijacked my serial port and prevented the camera from being recognized.

The Veo has some nice features, including an LCD display that shows you the last three numbers of the IP address that's assigned to your camera on the network and a cool blue logo/power switch that lights up when the camera's on.

The Wireless Observer can record videos and take still images like its competitors, and you can toggle the size of the video screen between 640x480, 320x240, and 160x120 and it a 2X digital zoom. It requires Windows 98 or higher, Internet Explorer 5 or higher, and a working serial port for setup.

The Wireless Observer one-ups the Linksys by recording sound either through its built-in or an external microphone, but curb your enthusiasm. We're not talking high-quality audio here, but certainly good enough to let you know what's going on.

What about the picture quality? Again, it's acceptable and a brightness control helps in low-light situations.

It supports up to four cameras on the same network. If you don't need wireless connectivity, consider the ethernet-only Observer that sells for $100 less. If you spring for the optional motion sensors ($29.99 each), the Veo camera will shoot you an e-mail when it detects movement. (The camera makes a slight whirring noise when it pans, so don't think you're going to spy on someone unnoticed.)

Observer Studio, software that's bundled with the camera, includes basic Web page and movie creation programs and lets you record live video.

If you're willing to part with a few more bucks in exchange for a few more extras, Panasonic offers a line of network video cameras, ranging from $700 to $1,200 with similar features, including a DDNS service and the option to control the camera from a cellphone.

If you're planning to set up multiple cameras, D-Link's DCS 2100 ($379) is also worth a look. It records video and audio and can display up to 16 cameras in on a single screen.
