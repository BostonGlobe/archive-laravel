CEO says Raytheon Co. might consider sales of lagging units

By Bloomberg News, 12/6/2003

Raytheon Co., the world's largest missile maker, would consider the sale of business units that don't provide the desired rate of growth, chief executive William H. Swanson said.

"If you have to spend 100 percent of your time in an area that only gives 5 percent of your results, that's not very well balanced," he told reporters after dedicating Raytheon's new headquarters in Waltham. "You've got to look at it from the standpoint of `Does the effort put in give you the results coming out?' "

He did not identify specific units, and spokesman James Fetig said the company won't discuss plans for asset sales.

Analysts like SG Cowen's Cai Von Rumohr and Merrill Lynch's Byron Callan have said Raytheon may sell parts of its Network Centric Systems unit, which has been hurt by program delays.

Swanson would not directly answer questions regarding that possibility.

The unit makes radar, radios, and sensors for battlefield communications and accounted for 13 percent of third-quarter revenue. Program delays at the unit resulted in $187 million of costs that led to a loss, slashing Raytheon's overall profit by 91 percent. Management has not responded quickly enough to address the unit's problems, Callan said.

"They'd known about this for some time. You'd have thought there was a mechanism in place to prevent this. It means there's something fundamentally amiss," he said. He has a "buy" rating on Raytheon's shares.

Raytheon shares fell 43 cents to $28.30 yesterday in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. They have fallen 8 percent this year.

Network Centric Systems' third-quarter sales of $556 million were down 27 percent, from $759 million a year earlier. It had an operating loss of $138 million from profit of $63 million.

Raytheon's profit from continuing operations in the third quarter fell to $21 million, or 5 cents a share, from $228 million, or 56 cents a year earlier. The company said in October that profit for the year would fall to $1.29 to $1.31 a share, down from its earlier forecast of $1.70 to $1.80.
