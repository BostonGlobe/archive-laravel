BOSTON CAPITAL

Exuberance redux

By Steven Syre, Globe Columnist, 12/2/2003

There is no shortage of Massachusetts companies that have produced huge gains for their shareholders in this year's booming market for speculative technology stocks. Optimism, it turns out, is very hard to kill. Consider Apogee Technology Inc. of Norwood, a company that would have barely shown up on a stock market screen at this time last year. Apogee's stock has climbed 335 percent so far this year, to $23.91, giving it a modest but more visible market value of about $135 million. A 2-for-1 stock split will be distributed in two weeks.

There are no brokerage analysts recommending Apogee or even following the maker of integrated circuits for digital audio amplifiers, according to standard investment research directories. Apogee's public roster of institutional shareholders is a list of one.

You can't listen to a recording of its investment conference calls on the company website, and it's unclear if any such calls are routinely conducted. A company spokeswoman didn't call me back.

What Apogee does have is Herb Stein, the company's 74-year-old president, chief executive, and chairman with a controversial history and a gift for raising investment money.

Stein was chief executive of Organogenesis Inc. from 1987 to 1999 while the Canton company developed its artificial skin product, Appligraf. He fought a pitched battle for years with short-sellers who bet against stocks they believe are overvalued. Each side complained the other was trying to manipulate Organogenesis stock.

Stein kept the company funded by selling stock in a series of private placements to well-heeled investors. Famous former Fidelity manager Peter Lynch even bought some for his own account.

Organogenesis won regulatory approval to sell Appligraf, an accomplishment short-sellers had claimed would never happen. But it was a hollow victory for public investors, who lost when the company filed for bankruptcy protection last year.

Stein left his post in 1999 and became Apogee's chief executive in January 2001. Stein, his family, and his investment business owned nearly 20 percent of Apogee earlier this year.

"They're ahead in their technology and I think they have a very capable management team," says Hans Estin of North American Management in Boston, the company's lone institutional investor. Estin and his firm were once the largest investor in Organogenesis.

In the last year, Apogee has trumpeted a small but impressive client roster that includes Hitachi and others. Revenues are double their level of last year, and the company reports a profit for the last two quarters. This is the news driving Apogee stock higher.

Here's another way of looking at it: Revenues and cash coming in the door aren't the same thing. Apogee's accounts receivable have climbed sharply as sales have increased. That has produced "cashless earnings per share," which presumably will lead to real money when accounts are settled. But it hasn't yet.

To keep Apogee in cash, Stein has used the private placements he employed at Organogenesis. In a September deal, the company raised $1 million from an unidentified investor in the Cayman Islands by selling shares for $10 each, though the public market's closing price of the same stock ranged from $16.12 to $20.79 over the 15 days before the placement.

Apogee raised $1 million more in another private placement three weeks ago. The price per share has not been disclosed yet.

Meanwhile, any public investor can buy Apogee stock at nosebleed values, around 14 times the sales of the past year, and some will. Optimism is very hard to kill.
