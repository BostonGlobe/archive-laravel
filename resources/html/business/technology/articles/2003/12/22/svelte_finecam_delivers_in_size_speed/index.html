PERSONAL TECH

Svelte Finecam delivers in size, speed

By John P. Mello Jr., 12/22/2003

The nagging problem with digital cameras, regardless of their megapixel capacity, is shutter lag -- the time between the depressing of the shutter button and the capture of an image. With a film camera, that time frame is almost instantaneous, but with a bytebox, it can be very slow. Rather than capturing the moment, more often digital shutterbugs find themselves capturing the moment after. That problem seems less pressing to camera makers than who can pack the most megapixels into a box. But once consumers begin to penetrate the megapixel haze, you can be sure that they're going to start asking questions like "Why does it take so long for this camera to fire off a shot?" By that time, maybe camera makers will have solved the problem, or they'll start incorporating the processing system developed by NuCore Technology of Sunnyvale, Calif.

Essentially, NuCore's technology can process images at amazing speeds because it does in dedicated hardware what other camera makers do in software.

"In what takes us 20 milliseconds, an ordinary digital camera takes two seconds," observed NuCore senior vice president for marketing Gary Baum.

NuCore's technology is currently used in a svelte unit made by Kyocera called the Finecam SL300R.

Although pricey for a 3.2 megapixel camera -- it sells from $328 to $348 on the Internet -- the unit delivers a premium on two fronts: size and speed. Barely bigger than the size of a deck of playing cards at 3.9-by-2.5-by-0.6 inches and weighing a feathery 4.4 ounces, shutter lag is unnoticeable.

What's more, the camera will burst away at 3.5 frames a second until you run out of memory. And the snapshots aren't the downsized images other digicams take in burst mode but full-sized 2048-by-1536-pixel photos. To take advantage of that burst speed, though, you'll need an optional high-speed memory card. The unit will also shoot video, with or without sound, at 30 frames a second, not the lower frame rates found in other digital cameras.

The camera's size can be a boon and a bane to snapsters. Although compact enough to fit in a shirt pocket, its size makes the unit difficult to keep steady, a problem compounded when you're blasting away in continuous mode. More often than not, I found only the first shot in a series in focus, which sort of defeats the advantages of the unit's unparalleled burst speed.

Although I didn't find the camera's controls too small -- a victory in itself in a camera this compact -- I frequently found myself accidently tripping one function while trying to perform another. While opening the battery compartment on the bottom of the unit, for instance, I kept hitting the power button on the top of the unit. The same was true for trying to flip open the cover to the USB/DC-in compartment on the side of the camera.

The unit's ergonomics are also a mixed bag.

The device has a rotating lens mount, which allows the camera to maintain a flat profile without any protrusions. This rotating lens setup also lets you vary the camera angles for your shots with a minimum of contortion. However, the lens is unprotected and is easy prey for a misplaced finger.

Measuring 1.5 inches diagonally, the camera's color LCD is bright and sharp, even in direct sunlight. That's a good thing because it doesn't have an optical viewfinder. It's also a bad thing because LCDs suck up power and reduce the camera's "live time," although you can save power by turning off the display's backlighting.

Although real estate on the camera is limited, Kyocera has done an admirable job of setting up the unit's controls, which are intuitive and logically located on the unit.

The zoom control is within easy thumb reach.

A function bar with icons that light up as they're activated sits above the LCD, has its own navigation buttons and lets you quickly access set-up, display, shoot, burst, and movie functions.

Above the function bar are controls for modifying the display -- eliminating icon clutter and cutting backlight -- and for choosing from one of seven preprogrammed scene modes -- standard, sports action, portrait, night view, night portrait, macro, and landscape.

Beside the LCD is a button for entering the camera's menu structure and a circular rocker for navigating through those menus.

Photos taken with the camera had true colors indoors and out and were free of digital artifacts.

The Kyocera Finecam SL300R's size and state-of-the-art workings make it a standout in its digital camera category, but they come at a premium price.
