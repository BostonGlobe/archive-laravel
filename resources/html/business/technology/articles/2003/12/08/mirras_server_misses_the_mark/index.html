UPGRADE

Mirra's server misses the mark

By Hiawatha Bray, 12/8/2003

One of journalism's grimier pleasures is the writing of bad reviews. The chance to savage a lousy computer or wretched piece of software can provide the jaded tech writer with hours of snarky delight.

ADVERTISEMENT

But it's not all fun and games. Sometimes you're forced to trash a product you wanted to like. So it is with the Mirra Personal Server, a promising but badly flawed new device that fills a need many home computer users don't even realize they have.

When you think of servers, you may imagine racks of high-powered computers humming inside a sterile corporate data center. But millions of home users could also benefit from owning a server. After all, nearly every kind of information and entertainment is digital, or soon will be. Why not a powerful central computer, with a huge hard drive, connected to the Internet, the cable TV system, the home audio system? Such a device could record "Friends" and you could watch it on any TV or PC in the house. It could store all your MP3 music files and play them back over any household stereo. And of course, it could act as a central repository of your most important files, ranging from tax receipts to e-mails from Mom.

Because such home servers are a new concept, the early entrants aren't trying to create a Swiss-Army-knife kind of device, capable of handling everything from e-mail to high-fidelity music. Instead, they're offering more limited servers that cater to a few simple but appealing tasks.

Ucentric Systems LLC, a software company in Maynard, has targeted the market for home entertainment servers. Samsung Electronics of South Korea has built a TV set-top box with a built-in digital video recorder, driven by Ucentric software. The boxes are getting their first workout in the suburbs of Philadelphia, in a trial co-sponsored by the cable TV company Comcast Corp.

Like a TiVo recorder or a Microsoft Corp. Media Center PC, the Ucentric system can record hours of TV programs onto a large hard drive. But unlike other digital recorders, the Samsung-Ucentric box lets the user play back the shows on any TV in the house -- in effect, a networked video server. Make that video and audio. Last month Ucentric announced a key upgrade -- software that turns the set-top box into a juke box. Users will be able to copy MP3 music files onto its hard drive and play back the tunes in any room.

Ucentric CEO Michael Collette says consumers will rally around home servers when they offer something sexy and exciting, like more and better TV. By comparison, Mirra, based in Mountain View. Calif., comes across like a fussy parent urging us to eat our vegetables. Still, Brussels sprouts are good for you, and so is Mirra's pitch that people should make backup copies of their vital computer data. Nobody does it often enough, and many of us never do it at all. Backups don't matter until your hard drive dies and you lose all the digital photos of your kids, or the trip to Paris.

What's needed is a device that automates the process. Plug it into your network, turn it on, and it backs up your data, updating the backup every week or every day to keep it fresh. That's what Mirra's Personal Server is supposed to do. You can buy one with 80 gigabytes of storage for $400 at the company's website, www.mirra.com. But you might want to wait until they can get the thing to work.

We tested it on two computers, a homebuilt desktop machine running Windows XP Professional, the other a Dell laptop with Windows 2000 Professional. In both cases the results were dismal. The Mirra software simply refused to run on the Dell machine, generating fatal errors whenever it was started. The desktop computer seemed to get along well with the device at first. But it turned out that many of the files selected for backup were never copied to the Mirra. Mirra tech support suggested a network router made by Siemens was to blame, but replacing it with a Linksys didn't help.

Even when the Mirra came through, it revealed a number of troubling limitations. On a typical network, you can click on, say, a Microsoft Word file stored on a remote computer, and it opens right up. Mirra doesn't work that way. To read a backed-up document or play a music file, you must copy it onto the PC you're using. The company says they're working on a future version that'll let you run files right on the server. That'd be nice. So would a high-security feature that would let the user permanently scrub sensitive files from the server. The current Mirra software lets you "delete" files, but as everyone knows, deleted files can be resurrected. For now, anything you put on a Mirra server will stay there, possibly for a lot longer than you'd like.

Still, this is all academic. The Mirra's simply too unreliable to trust with any of your data. Mirra insists that the Globe is the first user to encounter major problems with their Personal Server. But unless you want to be the second, we suggest you leave the Mirra alone.
