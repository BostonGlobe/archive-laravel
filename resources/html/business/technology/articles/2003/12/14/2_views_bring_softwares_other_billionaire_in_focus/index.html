BOOK REVIEW

2 views bring software's 'other billionaire' in focus

By Chris Gaither, Globe Staff, 12/14/2003

In distinguishing between the two new books about Oracle Corp. chief executive Larry Ellison, the footnotes tell the story.

Matthew Symonds, who spent two years tagging along with Ellison at work and play, gave the software executive "a unique form of joint copyright" -- the ability to write uncensored footnotes to rebut or amplify any part of the text. In contrast, Karen Southwick uses footnotes to credit her sources, since Ellison wouldn't talk to her.

Neither approach is perfect. Symonds, given full access, cuddles up too closely to Ellison in "Softwar," while Southwick's tough analysis of Oracle's business is weakened by her distance from the subject, in "Everyone Else Must Fail."

But put together, if you can get through the more than 800 pages, the books create a well-rounded portrait of the brilliance, ambition, and ruthlessness that have made Ellison the longest-tenured corporate chief in high tech. The well-timed books arrive on shelves only months after Ellison launched an unsolicited bid for smaller rival PeopleSoft Inc. The hostile takeover attempt, rare in the software business, cemented Ellison's reputation as a relentless competitor and gamesman.

For three decades Ellison has run the world's second-largest software concern, the database company Oracle. The largest, of course, is Microsoft Corp., whose leader Bill Gates gives Ellison, known as the industry's "other billionaire," fits of rage and insecurity.

Gates may be richer, but Ellison seems to have more fun. He lives and plays at full-speed: racing America's Cup sailboats, building a $100 million Japanese-style mansion, befriending Nobel laureate scientists, and repeatedly recognizing sea changes about to rock his industry before his competitors.

Given the access Symonds had, we should have shared in that fun. Yet Symonds fails to drive his book with a strong storyline or insight. He spent so much time reporting that he seems reluctant to omit any mundane detail.

The book bogs down quickly. After a charming scene of Ellison blinking away tears during an overseas sales visit because his cat, Maggie, had died of cancer, Symonds spends 250 pages analyzing press conferences, technology releases, product pricing, and executive firings.

The book's first half does offer the occasional juicy tidbit. Oracle executive Jeff Henley says of a particularly difficult time, "I'll always remember calling Larry and telling him that the company was blowing up and he had to get off his . . . boat."

The patient reader is rewarded when Symonds finally delivers the promised intimate portrait. In extended quotations, Ellison tells much of his own story, disclosing his family history, his relationship with his children, his near-death in a hurricane that engulfed a sailing race, his political hobnobbing, and his obsession with anti-aging technologies. Where Symonds goes soft, Southwick, an executive editor at CNET's News.com, excels at hitting Ellison hard for his management shortcomings. Using interviews with dozens of customers, analysts, and former Oracle managers, she exposes the arrogance that drives many software buyers and Ellison underlings batty.

"A vexing dilemma for Oracle customers is Ellison's tendency to assume, once he thinks of a new product, that it exists," she writes.

The title of Southwick's book comes from a quote attributed to Genghis Khan that Ellison likes to repeat: "It is not sufficient that I succeed. Everyone else must fail." As portrayed by Southwick, he applies that ruthlessness not only to Oracle's competitors, but to employees who threaten his power. He grooms potential successors only to send them packing, often just before their stock options vest.

Without access to Ellison, however, her psychological insights are limited.

Symonds relates that Ellison's nephew, a Chicago judge, put his arm around the writer after a dinner and threatened to kill him if he weren't nice to his famous uncle. If the nephew stays awake through the first half of the book, he'll realize that Symonds has nothing to worry about. Southwick, however, should watch her back.
