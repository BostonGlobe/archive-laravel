Overstock, SmartBargains look like dot-com survivors

Though in red, sites bank on concept of online outlet mall

By Leslie Walker, Washington Post, 12/7/2003

Fifty-five venture capitalists turned down Patrick Byrne's discount shopping website for funding at the peak of dot-com investing mania. So the graduate of Stanford University and Warren Buffett's real-world school of business funded it himself.

Five years later, Overstock Inc. is a publicly traded company, pulling in nearly 7 million shoppers a month to its Internet bargain bazaar and ranking right up there with Target.com and BestBuy.com as one of the Web's top 20 e-commerce sites.

But Byrne, its maverick chief executive, won't be satisfied until Overstock.com becomes a household name on par with eBay and Amazon.com, the Internet's top shopping hangouts, each of which draws more than 30 million people a month.

"In the center of online shopping, there is this great big space waiting to be occupied," said Byrne, who considers eBay a different animal because of its auction pricing and person-to-person selling format. "That is the online outlet center with serious discounts."

Sensing opportunity, Byrne kicked off Overstock.com's first national TV campaign this fall, slashed prices on books and CDs, and invested several million dollars upgrading its rickety warehouse and computer systems. The spending spree sent the money-losing Overstock Inc. deeper into the red, but its founder told Wall Street he expects a payoff.

Luckily for consumers, Overstock faces stiff competition -- and not just from eBay, which aggressively encourages retailers and manufacturers to sell excess inventory on its sprawling site. There's also SmartBargains.com, a smaller operation specializing in closeouts and surplus goods backed by Gordon Brothers Group, one of the biggest retail store liquidators in the country.

Boston's SmartBargains is privately held and only three years old, but it expects to be profitable for the first time this year, according to CEO Carl Rosendorf.

"The category is really on the move," Rosendorf said. "We offer discounts from 40 percent to 75 and 80 percent off the same merchandise in department stores."

Both sell excess inventory, using slightly different models. SmartBargains is strictly "off price" and doesn't handle first-run goods. Salt Lake City-based Overstock sells a mix of surplus and regular items, offering books and CDs at deep discounts, for example, to attract new customers.

Another difference is that some goods on Overstock.com -- roughly half of all sales, according to Byrne -- are shipped directly from its retailing and distribution partners. SmartBargains, by contrast, takes possession of all merchandise and handles shipping itself.

That may account for why customers give SmartBargains a higher satisfaction rating on BizRate.com, a site that surveys online shoppers. While 88 percent of customers at BizRate gave SmartBargains a positive rating in the past month, only 53 percent rated their experience at Overstock as positive. Many slammed Overstock for offering poor customer support when orders went astray.

Both sites employ electronic merchandising tricks to hammer home the bargain theme, prominently displaying the original retail price, discounted selling price, and calculating the percentage difference. Overstock lets you browse "new arrivals" and "weekly specials." SmartBargains runs three-hour special sales at lunchtime, and highlights "last chance" items that are close to selling out. On Wednesday, for example, a 14-karat gold, diamond-cut Italian bracelet originally retailing for $399 was offered for $139 on SmartBargains, but by midafternoon it had sold out. Nobody bit, however, on the last pair of Gucci wood-and-leather-thong sandals for $252.

Both sites want to be seen as online versions of the traditional outlet mall, but only Overstock is investing in TV to make the point.

The company started running TV ads in October showing a woman lounging on a white sofa. "Have you discovered the secret of the Big O?" she asks, then reveals it to be "Overstock.com -- an online outlet" where shoppers "save up to 70 percent" on designer goods.

Last week Overstock said the ad campaign appeared to boost sales, which rose 156 percent over Thanksgiving weekend compared with the same period last year. That was a greater jump than occurred in Internet sales overall, which rose 25 percent Thanksgiving week compared with 2002, according to ComScore Media Metrix of Reston, Va.

Overstock reported $61 million in gross sales for the quarter ending Sept. 30. Its net loss was $3.7 million, more than analysts were expecting. Byrne blamed the widening loss partly on the cost of expanding Overstock's warehouse capacity, telephone customer support, and other systems.

He told analysts on a conference call that one reason he ramped up capacity was his belief that Overstock "choked off our growth last year" by operating close to capacity during the holiday rush. He believes the company can move into the black by the fourth quarter of next year.

For Byrne, a cancer survivor who previously ran a subsidiary of Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway and whose father ran Geico, making Overstock one of the Internet's lasting brands would be sweet.

Already, his company has helped "liquidate" 18 dead dot-coms that had been funded by the same venture capitalists who spurned Overstock.

"I would take undue satisfaction in that," Byrne said with a chuckle.
