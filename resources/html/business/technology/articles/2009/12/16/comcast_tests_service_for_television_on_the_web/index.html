TV, with the click of a mouse

Comcast tests service that will allow users to watch their favorite shows online

By Hiawatha Bray
Globe Staff / December 16, 2009

The dividing line between the Internet and television became a little thinner yesterday, as the nation’s largest cable company unveiled a nationwide test of a new service that lets subscribers watch their favorite shows online.

The offering from Comcast Corp., called Fancast Xfinity TV, demonstrates how cable companies are trying to attract and retain subscribers by integrating video entertainment services and the Internet.

Rival cable provider Verizon Communications, which operates the FiOS video service in 16 states, is testing its own TV-on-the-Internet offering. In addition, Verizon has introduced data widgets that deliver Internet news, weather, and traffic reports to the TV screen, and a feature that lets a customer remotely program a digital video recorder through a smartphone or Internet browser.

But Xfinity TV is the biggest effort yet to distribute vast amounts of TV programming over the Internet.

Comcast has about 23.8 million cable TV subscribers and 15.7 million broadband Internet customers. The Xfinity TV service is available at no additional cost to customers of both its cable television and broadband Internet service. In the next six months, the company plans to extend the service to cable subscribers who don’t also buy their Internet access from Comcast.

Xfinity TV will offer about 2,000 hours of programming from about 30 cable channels, including premium movie services like HBO and Starz.

“There really is an astounding amount of content that has not been online before,’’ said Amy Banse, president of Comcast Interactive Media.

The content will eventually include access to pornographic movies and TV shows. Comcast plans to make such programs available on Xfinity TV next year, after the company has added parental control features.

Comcast subscribers will be able to install the Fancast software on up to three personal computers, and will be able to watch cable shows on any of the registered computers. For now, the service is limited to personal computers, but Comcast plans to eventually make it accessible on other devices, such as smartphones.

The programs will depend on the cable package. For example, only those who subscribe to HBO will be able to watch that network’s shows and movies.

Xfinity TV won’t be Comcast’s only venue for online TV, if the company completes its recently announced deal to acquire entertainment company NBC Universal. The deal would give Comcast a major stake in Hulu, an Internet site that shows thousands of hours of TV shows, mostly from the NBC, CBS, and Fox broadcast networks. Hulu’s shows usually feature commercials and are available free to US users.

Banse said there would be no conflict between Hulu’s free TV offerings and Xfinity TV’s catalog of cable shows. “We see the two sites as being quite compatible and complementary,’’ she said.

Bruce Leichtman, president and principal analyst for Leichtman Research Group Inc., a media research company in Durham, N.H., said Xfinity TV is a defensive move by Comcast “to protect their base, to add value for their subscribers, and ultimately to prevent against piracy.’’

Leichtman said that illegal music swapping became popular on the Internet partly because the music industry was slow to provide legal means for listening online. He said that movie and TV producers hope to avoid the same mistake by embracing services like Xfinity TV.

But, Leichtman added, there’s still not much of an Internet audience for full-length movies and TV shows. A survey he conducted in 2008 found that 34 percent of US adults watched some kind of Internet video every week, but only 8 percent of them watched an entire TV show online. “What is being used significantly online is short-form video’’ like the brief clips found at YouTube.com, the most popular online video site, he said.

Verizon Communications spokesman Phil Santoro said Comcast is arriving late to the online party. For years, Verizon has offered ESPN 360, a sports video channel available online to Verizon Internet subscribers. Subscribers to Verizon’s FiOS video service can also get online access to New York Yankees-related programming, shows from the Disney Channel, and motion pictures from the Epix channel.

Meanwhile, about 500 Verizon customers are testing FIOS TV Online, a service similar to Xfinity TV. For now, the FIOS testers can use the service to view shows only from cable channels TBS and TNT, but if results are positive, Verizon will put a larger selection of programming online.
