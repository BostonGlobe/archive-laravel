Select the smartest phone for you

With so many competing operating systems and features, choosing a device can be daunting

By John Dyer
Globe Correspondent / February 22, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

T riders who used to crack open the newspaper on their morning commutes now surf the Web on their smartphones. Executives don't rush back to the office to write last-minute e-mails anymore; they send them via their PDAs, or personal digital assistants, a fancy name for smartphones.

Lost in an unfamiliar town? Smartphones have global positioning systems. Want to capture baby's first steps when a camera isn't handy? Smartphones take photographs.

These light, handheld devices are like electronic Swiss Army knives. They offer more doodads than most people need, making them essential tools for the technically savvy, but often intimidating for everyone else.

The Globe tested four smartphones. Each is essentially a mobile phone and computer in one, allowing users to make calls and send text messages, check e-mail, listen to music, and enjoy the other perks of browsing online - from Internet banking to finding a good local restaurant.

Each allows users to download free or inexpensive applications, a trend that's opening new markets for computer and telecommunications companies. Applications range from music files to online dictionaries to currency converters.

We asked AT&T and T-Mobile to supply us with their most up-to-date 3G or third-generation smartphones, and we tried out the $435 Blackberry Bold, the $235 LG Incite, the $200 iPhone with 8GB of memory, and the $180 T-Mobile G1.

Each used a different operating system. Blackberry has its own system, the Incite uses Windows, the iPhone is an Apple product, and the G1 sports Google's new Android.

Those prices included earphones and sometimes carrying cases, too, but were all contingent on signing two-year contracts with either AT&T or T-Mobile. Otherwise, they were costlier, if they could be purchased separately at all

BlackBerry Bold has a suggested retail price of $550 without a contract, for example, while the iPhone must be purchased with an AT&T contract.

Kelvin Peters, assistant manager at AT&T's store on Boylston Street in Boston, said customers shopping for a smartphone first need to consider which operating system they find most comfortable Windows or Apple, for example and then ask themselves how they want to interact with the device.

"Some people want to be up with the latest technology, so they want a touchscreen," said Peters. "Some people want to stay old school with a keypad and trackball, but with the latest features."

We felt T-Mobile's G1 would be the best option for first-time smartphone buyers. With a touchscreen, a keypad that slides in and out of view and a trackball that acts like a laptop computer's mouse, it combines the best of all worlds. Its Android operating system is easy to understand. And its narrow shape and soft black exterior make it a handsome accessory.

But every phone we tested had a downside, and the G1's stemmed from its links to Gmail, Google's e-mail service. We love Gmail, but others might not be able to maximize the G1's strengths, even if they can sync the phone to their other e-mail accounts.

Our choice
T-MOBILE G1
Cost: $180
Pros: The G1's touchscreen is just as responsive as the iPhone's. Its menu button works well, and its trackball work seamlessly, just like the BlackBerry's. And its sliding keypad is much easier to use than the Incite's stylus.
Cons: Google made the G1's operating system, Android, with an eye toward steering users to Gmail, the Internet search giant's free e-mail service. Users can sign up to a Gmail account, ignore it, and enjoy the phone's user-friendly features. But that would leave much of the phone's potential unfulfilled.
Final word: A great start for budding smartphone addicts.

BLACKBERRY BOLD
Cost: $435
Pros: BlackBerry created the first popular smartphone, and the Bold conforms to that tradition, with a trackball, keyboard, and buttons that move users directly to key menus. Also, like other BlackBerrys, the Bold's signals are routed through the company's servers before reaching their recipients, making them more secure than the other smartphones, which transmit over ordinary cellular towers.
Cons: Stodgy. The BlackBerry has a high-quality screen, but it's small and isn't suited for videos, gaming, or other flashy applications. Its leather backside also makes the device resemble a calculator from 1982.
Final word: The business person's best friend.

LG INCITE
Cost: $235
Pro: The Incite operates Microsoft's Windows, so most users will be familiar with how it works. It has a Start icon at the top of the phone's screen that expands into a dropdown menu, just like a personal computer. It has a Task Manager icon that tells you which programs are running and how much memory those processes are eating up, just like a PC.
Cons: The touchscreen was insensitive, even after we used a built-in tool to calibrate it. The provided stylus helped only slightly. Oddly, a little string to tie the stylus to the back of the phone was included. Smartphones are supposed to slide into one's pocket, not catch on a hanging stylus.
Final word: Take a pass unless you can't turn your back on Windows. The company declined to comment for this article.

APPLE IPHONE
Cost: $200
Pros: With 8GB of memory, the iPhone offers as much as four times the storage as the others, though, unlike the iPhone, they all have slots for memory cards. Its touchscreen is easy to figure out and responsive. Perhaps most important, iPhone users have easy access to iTunes and Apple's applications store, probably the biggest and best out there. The iPhone also comes in a $300 version with 16GB.
Cons: Non-Mac users might not get as much out of the iPhone. Its touchscreen keypad can also be a downer - anyone with large fingers is going to find themselves retyping even simple messages because it can be hard to press the right keys.
Final word: A nice machine.
