The end of the argument?

With GPS, the fight over maps or directions may be a thing of the past

Jim Thatcher, with daughter Helena, 13, and wife Ruth Weber, says their GPS, which they call “Libby,’’ has changed the dynamic of family trips. (Stephen Rose for The Boston Globe)
By Bella English
Globe Staff / August 8, 2009

A year and a half ago, Jane Tufts received a Magellan GPS navigation device as a Christmas gift from her husband, Chuck. Her sense of direction is decent, but she’d grown tired of printing out directions every time she was going someplace new and trying to follow them while driving.

Her husband had his own strategy. He’d always travel with a big book of maps. “He would pull over and look at this annoying map book,’’ says Tufts, a textbook editor who lives in Milton, “when to me it’s much easier to just stop at a gas station and ask directions.’’

Ask directions? A man? Not only wouldn’t he stop, he’d just keep driving and ask his wife to read the maps even though it would make her carsick. Their different approaches, needless to say, did not make for pleasant rides, which explains why the gift she received has been more than merely a handy tool. It’s been a marital godsend.

Portable global positioning devices have, in just a few years, gone from being nowhere to everywhere - in our cars, on our phones, and on our watches. And one way or another, the number of drivers who no longer turn to Mapquest or the crumpled up maps in their glove compartment to get them places - instead relying on global satellites deep in space to guide them - is on the rise.

Nearly 18 million personal navigation devices have been sold in the US so far this year, compared to 12 million in 2007, and predicted sales for next year are nearly 19 million, according to ABI Research, a New York firm that tracks trends in global connectivity and technology. And, according to ABI, roughly one in five automobile drivers in the US use some sort of GPS in their cars, including cellphones: 70 percent of the phones now come with the device.

With that rise has gone one of the last great arguments in the front seats of cars everywhere: She wants him to stop and ask for directions. He would sooner spend a week in a Cleveland Motel 6 with the in-laws.

“The GPS has been a family saver for us,’’ says Wendy Wanger of Newton, who gave her husband, Barry, a system for his birthday three years ago. “The stress level around giving Barry directions and/or getting lost used to make many trips difficult. He’s generally very calm but getting lost is one of his triggers.’’

As for her husband, his sense of direction is so lame that when he was single, he jokes, he lived in a one-bedroom apartment so he wouldn’t get lost. He loves the pleasant female voice on the GPS that tells him to turn left or turn right. He loves that it gives him the choice of the shortest distance or shortest time to his destination. He loves the little ding that signals an upcoming turn. Most of all, he loves getting where he’s going.

“Getting lost was always very stressful, is the polite way to say it,’’ says Barry, who has his own public relations firm.

Though the laid-back may lament the passing of the serendipitous “getting lost’’ scenario - and neurologists warn that the device is robbing us of spatial skills - for many, the GPS is the answer to their frayed nerves. With an objective, indeed a scientific, “third’’ voice giving directions, road-trip sniping between couples is headed the way of the cassette player in cars.

Jim Thatcher, a Boston psychiatrist, says the GPS can indeed change the family trip dynamic. But for his own family, it’s a change with a twist. “Libby,’’ which is what they have named their female-voiced system, gives perfectly sound directions. But Thatcher doesn’t always listen. After all, he figures, “Libby’’ doesn’t know his roads like he does.

“The time-honored tradition of the man driving and not asking for directions and getting lost has only changed slightly,’’ says Thatcher. “Now I am sure that I know a better way than ‘Libby’ so I ignore the directions and still manage to get lost. Thus the usual fight with my wife about not asking for directions can still take place, but in a slightly different form.’’

The biggest change in the car is what he calls the “computerization of the navigator half’’ of the couple, namely his wife. He used to be able to blame getting lost on her map-reading skills, but Libby has taken care of that. Their daughter, who can no longer count on being entertained by their bickering over the map, now watches videos on her laptop. “My wife calmly reads her book while I silently await Libby’s next command,’’ says Thatcher.

At Boch Toyota in Norwood, most of the higher-end cars come with a GPS that seems to do everything but scratch your back. Erico Varela, a Boch salesman, says about 35 percent of customers opt for the GPS. “Even though it’s a good option, a lot of people don’t do much traveling, just back and forth from work,’’ says Varela. He has one in his car, and it taught him a shortcut to Providence. Now, that’s the route he always takes.

And he and his girlfriend don’t argue over directions anymore.

“She is hard-headed, she always thinks her way is the best way to go,’’ says Varela. “Now we use the navigation system, which is always right.’’ There are times, he concedes, when getting lost can be fun - on the Cape, for instance, where a wrong turn might take you past a hidden swimming pond or a farm stand selling fresh corn or a beautiful sand dune. “But then when you’re finished looking at the views, you need your GPS to find your way back.’’

Julie Fallon was at Boch on a recent day to pick up her brand-new Highlander hybrid, equipped with a GPS. She’s never used one before, but is happy to have it. For her, it’s a matter of both safety and convenience. On a family vacation in Orlando, Fla., last year with her husband and three children, they got hopelessly lost at midnight, ended up in a dangerous neighborhood, got bad directions and finally, at 1 a.m. found themselves in a deserted, dark Epcot parking lot. Everyone was stressed.

“The GPS will cut down on that,’’ says Fallon, who works in corporate communications and lives in Norwell. “And who wants to start a vacation like that?’’ She sees nothing positive to come from getting lost. “It just causes stress for me or tension between me and my husband.’’

Still, there are some who believe the navigation system itself is a distraction. “Even if you’ve got the voice on, you’re still going to look at the screen and not pay attention to other drivers as much,’’ says Richard Guzman of Framingham. He and his wife, Ola , got a GPS two years ago in their Chrysler, and it has cut down on their car arguments. “Before, I’d always tell him to stop and ask for directions, and he’d always say no, no, no,’’ says Ola.

But even if the GPS has resolved one conflict, it’s started a slew of others. Sometimes one partner wants the system’s voice turned on, the other hates the voice; one partner spends too much time fiddling with the buttons while the other sits there fuming; one is a gadget geek and uses it even to drive home from work, just because. But at least, for the most part, the tension has shifted away from how to avoid getting lost.

“Now, the argument is whether to have the GPS talk to you or just look at the map on the screen,’’ says Tufts. “I like having it talk to me. I don’t like to take my eyes off the road. But Chuck doesn’t like the voice.’’ Still, she concedes, anything is better than that annoying book of maps.
