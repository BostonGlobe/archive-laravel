Austria finmin under fire for upstaging colleague

By George Jahn
Associated Press / April 4, 2012

VIENNA—Officially the tiff is over. Austrian Finance Minister Maria Fekter has apologized for upstaging Jean-Claude Juncker, her Luxembourg counterpart, underlining her contriteness with a bottle of classy cognac for the head of the eurogroup's finance ministers.

Juncker, in turn, denies reports of an "angry and bitter dispute," and Fekter's office describes the incident as passe.

But at home, the uproar caused by the outspoken Austrian government minister's decision to spill the beans on a eurozone decision to increase emergency funding for heavily indebted countries using the common currency at last week's meeting in Copenhagen is a faux pas that keeps on giving.

By Wednesday, five days after the incident, both sides tried to put the issue to rest.

Fekter is a woman "who is used to plain speaking," Juncker told Austrian state television late Tuesday, adding that tensions were over after she accepted his point of view that "one cannot constantly chat (coming) from meetings." Asked for comment Wednesday, Fekter spokesman Gregor Schuetze said: "The story is over. Period."

Austrian commentators don't see it that way however.

They suggest that Fekter's pre-empting of Juncker with her announcement Friday that EU finance ministers agreed to boost the EU emergency firewall to (EURO)800 billion ($1 trillion) -- and then discussing private details about Juncker's health -- has hurt her country, the government and the feisty former interior minister herself.

Political analyst Fritz Plasser sees Fekter, 56, as "committing two major offenses against professional norms."

"The rules of political confidentiality have been broken," Plasser told the daily Kurier, adding he is convinced that "a shadow will remain on Austria" because of the incidents -- a fear repeated by newspaper commentators agonizing that tiny Austria will have even less of a voice in future EU financial meetings than it has now because of feared future indiscretions by Fekter.

Support is hard to find. From Foreign Minister and Deputy Chancellor Michael Spindelegger downward, senior officials of Fekter's conservative People's Party who normally line up behind her have declined comment, while Hannes Swoboda, head of the Social Democrats in the EU Parliament, calls her actions "unprofessional."

Fekter herself has tried to justify her actions, saying that "my direct way of speaking is unusual on the diplomatic scene," and arguing that her attempt to brief a small group of Austrian reporters at Friday's Copenhagen meeting got out of hand when other media rushed her.

Fekter moved up from provincial politics into parliament before becoming interior minister in 2008 and then finance minister last year. An acknowledged hard worker, she defended her record in an interview earlier this week with the daily Standard.

"I came to Vienna with the moniker of 'blond, blue-eyed and from the sticks,'" she told the newspaper, alluding to opposition suggestions on her appointment to the interior portfolio that she was not up to the job. "And now I sit here as the minister of finance."

Her single-mindedness as interior minister in reducing the flow of asylum seekers -- and forcing illegals out of the country -- was criticized as heartless by her opponents. As finance minister she has been outspoken in the government's battle to reduce deficits under the EU's limit of 3 percent of the gross natural product.

But even before the most recent flap, her plainspokenness has gotten her into trouble.

She described bankers and the well-to-do as Europe's new "boogiemen" on the sidelines of a September 16 Finance Ministers' meeting and compared their perceived persecution to that of the Jews under Hitler -- a remark she then apologized for.

Her caustic comments after that same meeting about U.S. Treasury Secretary Timothy Geitner -- "I would have thought that if he explains the world to us, then he would listen to what we want to explain to the Americans" was the stuff of negative newspaper commentary for days.

Earlier, facing intense public criticism as interior minister for insisting that a Kosovo teenager be deported from Austria despite full assimilation after 9 years in the country, she declared: "I have to follow the laws, whether (her) fawn's eyes stare at me from the TV or not."
