Commerzbank to meet capital target without aid

By David McHugh
AP Business Writer / January 19, 2012

FRANKFURT, Germany—Germany's Commerzbank AG has revealed it has found a way to increase its capital cushion as required by regulators to protect against possible losses from the eurozone debt crisis -- without taking more state aid.

The bank said Thursday it would plug a euro5.3 billion ($6.8 billion) capital shortfall identified by the European Banking Authority by "relying on its own strength." The German government already owns 25 percent of the bank after a previous bailout.

Commerzbank shares shot higher on the news, trading up 13 percent at euro1.60 in afternoon trading in Frankfurt.

The bank said it would find an additional euro6.3 billion in capital padding by holding back cash from its quarterly earnings and cutting back on risky investments. The bank has until June 30 to find the additional financial padding, and had until Friday to come up with its plan for finding the money.

Commerzbank's capital needs had been a point of concern in a banking system shaken by Europe's crisis over too much government debt in some countries. The EBA is pushing banks across Europe to raise euro115 billion ($147.56 billion) in new capital by June 30 to strengthen the banking system. The crisis affects banks because they hold large amounts of government bonds, whose prices have been affected by fears of default.

European officials are trying to prevent losses from the crisis from making banks cut off loans to businesses. That could hurt the economy and deepen what is expected to be at least a mild eurozone recession.

Commerzbank said it had already found euro3.0 billion of the required capital by the end of last year by setting aside euro1.2 billion in fourth-quarter profits. It found an addition euro1.6 billion by cutting back on risky loans, securities holdings and other investments. Reducing loans and investments -- so-called risk weighted assets -- lowers the amount of capital needed to backstop against any potential losses on those assets.

It added it would find the rest by the deadline using similar methods, saying that it would also save up to euro250 million by compensating some employees in stock instead of cash, depending on how many employees participate. It also said it use funds from the sale of non-core assets such as the Frankfurt, Germany headquarters of Dresdner Bank, which Commerzbank took over in 2009, and a stake in Russia's Promsvyazbank. It said it would exercise a sell option on the Promsvyazbank stake this month.

Commerzbank officials said that they would not cut back loans in its core business banking in Germany and Poland, and particularly not to Germany's "mittelstand" -- the small to medium-sized enterprises, that are a main pillar of both the bank's business and the country's export-focused economy. "I would like to emphasize at this point, that reduction in risk assets will not come at the expense of Germany's mittelstand," chief financial officer Eric Strutz said.

Commerzbank needed state aid in the wake of the financial crisis that followed the collapse of U.S. investment bank Lehman Brothers in 2008. It has since been buffeted by Greece's financial difficulties; it wrote down euro798 million in Greek bonds in the third quarter, saying it had taken a loss of 52 percent on its holdings.

Most of the euro5.3 billion that the EBA has asked Commerzbank to raise -- euro4.8 billion -- is to cover the risk of losses on government bonds held in the bank's Eurohypo commercial real estate lender. Only euro400 million is needed to cover potential losses at Commerzbank's core banking operations, the bank said.

At Eurohypo, some 60 percent of the capital needs come from the need to cover the risk of losses on bonds issued by Italy, the recent focus of the crisis and the country with the eurozone's biggest bond market.

European banks are trying to increasing their capital in a tough environment. It is difficult to raise money from investors by issuing shares due to fears about the banking system. At the same time the EBA is pressing banks not to find the money by cutting back on loans to businesses.

Commerzbank cautioned that its plans to meet the capital requirement depended on there being no further deterioration of the economy and, in particular, no further escalation of the government debt crisis. It said that it still has other options if need be, including issuing equity capital instruments.

The bank said that it hoped to have capital buffers of 11 percent against its loans, investments and other securities holdings by June 30.

The requirement is to have 9 percent, including the financial impact of a simulated partial debt default by a government.
