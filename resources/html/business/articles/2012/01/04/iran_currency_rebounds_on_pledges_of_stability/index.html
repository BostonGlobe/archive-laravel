Iran currency rebounds on pledges of stability

By Ali Akbar Dareini
Associated Press / January 4, 2012

TEHRAN, Iran—Iran's national currency rebounded Wednesday from record lows after the central bank chief acknowledged with rare candor that the "psychological effects" of new U.S. sanctions were partly responsible for the devaluation and said the country might raise interest rates to stabilize the market.

The comments by central bank Governor Mahmoud Bahmani were a clear sign of the toll that sanctions are taking on Iran's oil-dominated economy. The U.S. and its allies are trying to ramp up pressure on Tehran over its nuclear program, which they suspect is aimed at developing a weapon, though Iran denies it.

"Fluctuations in the currency market in recent days resulted primarily from psychological factors and can't continue," Bahmani said in comments published in several newspapers Wednesday. "I say this with great certainty that sanctions don't create problems for the country's economy. While knowing this, the enemy is depending on creating psychological tensions. If we are intimidated, we will be playing into the enemy's hands," he added.

The rial rose to about 15,600 against the dollar on Wednesday. It hit an all-time low of 17,800 Monday, two days after President Barack Obama signed into law sanctions targeting Iran's central bank and oil sectors. Though the sanctions do not go into effect for six months, they are already sending ripples through the markets.

The central bank chief said Iranian officials are considering increasing interest rates on bank deposits, among other measures, to try to stabilize markets.

"The government is considering controlling the currency market and will take action soon. Dollar rates will decrease soon," he said. "Using monetary and financial instruments such as issuing bonds with high rates are on our agenda so that we can direct floating liquidity to banks," he said, adding this was an urgent priority.

Authorities ordered currency exchange shops to trade the dollar at 14,000 rials, leading to confusion and prompting many dealers to avoid transactions. Other money dealers ignored the order and sold the dollar for 15,600 rials. Imposing a 14,000 rate effectively introduced a third rate for dollar. The government rate is 11,180 rials, though it has little significance outside government circles.

The official IRNA news agency said the central bank also lowered the limit on foreign currency an Iranian passenger flying out of the country can buy to $1,000 instead of $2,000 previously. The decision reflects concerns about the flight of hard currency.

Bahmani did not say how the government could control the currency market fluctuations, but pegging the exchange rate is not seen as a likely step. Instead, the central bank could inject foreign currency or increase bank interest rates to stabilize the market.

Analysts say it is clear Iran's economy is in dire straits, after being subjected to four rounds of U.N. sanctions since 2006 and other measures by the U.S. and allies. But some experts blame government policies, not sanctions, for the devaluation.

Economic expert Mahdi Taghavi said a key reason behind the increase in the exchange rate is government's recent order to banks to reduce interest rates on deposits. While banks were offering 17.5 percent interest rate for a one-year deposit, they reduced the rate to 14 percent, prompting many to withdraw the deposits and buy gold and foreign currency instead.

"Reduction of banking interest rates prompted investors to withdraw their money from the banks," Taghavi said.

The semi-official Fars news agency reported that the central bank governor has warned he may quit if the government intervenes in shaping monetary and banking policies.

The comments show the divisions within the government over how to deal with the currency market, where the government periodically intervenes contrary to central bank demands to stay out.

Analysts say Iran is struggling to plug random holes in a dam that is structurally unsound. Years of what critics contend have been little more than one economic misstep after another by President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad, coupled with Iran's increasing economic isolation, make it difficult to reverse the negative perception of the economy.

Iran's ample reserves from oil sales offer a cushion that can be tapped to shore up the rial. But that may not be a sustainable policy or enough to offset the political and economic angst Iranians feel as a result of the pressure from the West.

"At some point, it's like shoving money out the door," said Jon Alterman, an Iran expert with the Center for Strategic and International Studies in Washington, referring to using reserves to prop up the rial.

The possibility of raising interest rates may also be little more than a short term salve.

"Iranians look at the political risk and they don't want to keep money in rials," said Alterman, adding that fears of depreciation of 30 percent or more won't be offset by interest rate hikes of 3 or 4 percent.

"There's only so much a bank can do in the face of dramatically shifting political context," he said. "There's only so much a bank can do in the face of a tornado of economic insecurity".

Iran struck a markedly different tone just a day earlier, play down on Tuesday any links between the new U.S. sanctions and the nosedive of the rial.

"For the time being, it has nothing to do with foreign policy," said Foreign Ministry spokesman Ramin Mehmanparast.

But officials are clearly nervous.

The latest U.S. sanctions would bar foreign financial institutions that do business with Iran's central bank from opening or maintaining correspondent operations in the United States. The Obama administration, however, is looking to soften the impact of the measure, fearing they could lead to a spike in global crude oil prices or pressure key allies that import Iranian oil.

One sign of the nervousness has been repeated threats to close the Strait of Hormuz -- a strategic oil route in the Persian Gulf through which one-sixth of the world's oil passes on tankers -- if the U.S. sanctions are imposed.

The comments helped drive oil prices up above $100 barrel. Iran is the world's fourth-largest oil producer, with an output of about 4 million barrels a day. It relies on oil exports for about 80 percent of its public revenues.

Associated Press reporter Tarek El-Tablawy contributed to this report from Cairo.
