In an effort to highlight a growing cultural scene in the region and draw the community into the newly redeveloped West Acton Village, local businesses and organizations will be hosting an art and music festival next month.

This will be the first edition of the Village Arts + Music Project, a collaborative effort of New Habitat Partners, which is leading redevelopment efforts in West Acton, 3Rivers Arts in Groton, and Acton Open Studios.  

“One of our goals, besides renovating the space, is to have there be a place where the community can gather,’’ said the head of New Habitat Partners, Mathias Rosenfeld. “This is an important part of making that a reality.’’

The festival will take place along Massachusetts Avenue on Sept. 9 from 12:30 to 5 p.m.

It will be picking up as the weekly Acton-Boxborough Farmers Market wraps up for the day in West Acton, where New Habitat is working to redevelop dilapidated buildings and revitalize the village.

The first phase of redevelopment was completed last year with the renovation of 537 and 541 Massachusetts Ave. The space at 541 Mass Ave. features three community units and a restaurant, and the 537 Mass. Ave. is home to office and retail tenants. In all, there are 13 new businesses in the village, Rosenfeld said.

The festival will feature a pop-up n art gallery, an artists’ market, musical performances, face painting, and free chair massages. Local businesses will be selling items such as ice cream, craft brews, and seafood. The rain date is Sept. 16.  

Participants include New Habitat Partners, the artists of 3Rivers Arts, Acton Open Studios, Indian Hill Music in Littleton, Rapscallion Ale in Bedford, Twin Seafood Market in Acton, and Reasons to Be Cheerful Ice Cream in Concord.

Julie Pampinella, director of public relations for 3Rivers, said the festival is an opportunity to showcase a growing cultural scene not only in Acton but the entire region. She said 3Rivers, formerly the Groton Center for the Arts, has been trying to reach out to the communities that border along the Nashua, Squannacook, and Nissitissit rivers. 

“There is so much cultural richness in this area and not many people think about that,’’ said Pampinella said. “We’re trying to collaborate with area towns to showcase artists and build relationships.’’

Rosenfeld said the festival has the potential to expand as his development moves forward. The second phase involves the rehabilitation of the next three properties along Massachusetts Avenue.

Rosenfeld said the development will include retail, office, and restaurant spaces, and a cultural area for performances.

Artists interested in renting space at the festival can e-maile-mail ­olivia@3riversarts.com.
