Whitman woman walking the walk for cancer

Jennifer Parker is planning to walk for the Jimmy Fund Sept. 9.
By Paul E. Kandarian
August 26, 2012

A CAUSE THAT HITS HOME: To Jennifer Parker of Whitman, walking 26.2 miles in the Boston Marathon Jimmy Fund Walk on Sept. 9 is nothing compared to surviving a disease that has hit her family especially hard.

In 1995, her brother Thomas P. Welch Jr. died of the disease, and after his death, Parker and her sister, Alice O’Neill, decided to walk the walk.

“The first year was very emotional,” said Parker, 40. “All those people were there, all touched by cancer. At the finish line, I saw the policemen and firemen waiting in uniform, clapping for us, it was just overwhelming.”

In 2003, she found a small lump under her right arm. But her son had recently been born prematurely and was so fragile that she focused on getting him healthy, and overlooked her own care, she said. Four months later, she had the lump examined and in April 2004 was diagnosed with early stage-four sarcoma. The lump had grown into a 40-pound tumor, she said.

“It was a scary time in my life, and my family’s life,” she said. “We’re a very large, tight-knit family, so everyone really came together to support me and that made all the difference.”

The tumor was removed and she underwent chemotherapy and radiation therapy. On the last week of her treatment, her sister, Alice, was diagnosed with lung cancer.

“I couldn’t believe that right as my nightmare was ending, Alice’s was just starting,” Parker said.

Then this year, their brother Kevin Welch, 50, was diagnosed with lung cancer in March and in May had his right lung removed. Both Welch and O’Neill, 54, are doing well, Parker said. And after all they’d been through, she knew it was time to walk the walk again.

“Cancer has affected four out of six of us, and so much of my family,” she said. “So I knew I had to walk again.”

The Boston Marathon Jimmy Fund Walk has supported work at the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute for 24 years. On Sept. 9, Parker will join roughly 9,000 others to walk the Boston Marathon route. The walk is expected to raise $7.5 million. Since it began in 1989, the walk has raised more than $80 million for cancer research.

This will be her third walk, and the first that she’ll attempt to complete. Her past walks were half-marathons, but she has been walking about 10 miles a day around Whitman to get ready. She said she has raised more than $1,000 for the cause thus far.

From the start of her cancer treatment, she was optimistic she’d pull through and never allowed anyone around her to speak negatively.

“I told people who’d come see me, ‘You can’t be crying and you can’t be sad,’ ” Parker said. “I wanted to have an upbeat attitude.”

She still does and now walks the race “because I’m thankful to be alive. I walk so others may be spared the horrors of this disease.”

To register for the walk, or donate in the name of Parker or any participant, visit www.jimmyfundwalk.org/2012/jparker or call 866-531-9255.
