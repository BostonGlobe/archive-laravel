Alberta minister blames US politics for pipeline

By Rob Gillies
Associated Press / February 15, 2012

TORONTO—Alberta's finance minister said Wednesday that U.S. politics trumped what should have been a no-brainer decision to approve a proposed oil pipeline from Alberta to Texas.

Last month, President Barack Obama's administration denied a permit for TransCanada's Keystone XL pipeline, but left the door open for TransCanada to apply for a new pipeline route that avoids the state of Nebraska's environmentally sensitive Sandhills region.

Obama said officials did not have enough time to review the project before a Feb. 21 deadline imposed by the Republican controlled Congress.

Ron Liepert, the finance minister of the Canadian province that has the world's third-largest reserves of oil, said it shows that Canada can no longer rely on the U.S. to be its only customer.

Liepert said in prepared remarks that the decision should have been a "no-brainer" and then reiterated that in a speech to the Economic Club of Canada.

"We all saw what happens when politics gets in the way of good economic decisions and I'm referring of course to the Keystone XL pipeline decision in the United States," Liepert said.

Liepert said Alberta's oil industry creates thousands of jobs in the U.S. and Canada. TransCanada says the Keystone XL pipeline could create as many as 20,000 jobs, a figure opponents say is inflated. A State Department report last summer said the pipeline would create up to 6,000 jobs during construction.

The decision to reject the pipeline went over badly in Canada, which relies on the U.S. for 97 percent of Canada's energy exports.

The pipeline would carry 800,000 barrels of oil a day from Alberta across six U.S. states to the Texas Gulf Coast, which has numerous refineries.

The pipeline is critical to Canada, which must have infrastructure in place to export its growing oil sands production from northern Alberta. The region has the world's third largest oil reserves with 170 billion barrels of proven reserves. Daily production of 1.5 million barrels from the oil sands is expected to increase to 3.7 million in 2025. Only Saudi Arabia and Venezuela have more reserves.

"The greatest risk to Alberta by 2020 is that we will be landlocked in bitumen. We need the ability to ship our crude to new markets," Liepert said.

Liepert said Alberta is also a strong supporter of Enbridge's Northern Gateway pipeline to the Pacific Coast which would allow oil to be shipped to Asia. Last week, Canadian Prime Minister Stephen Harper traveled to China where he discussed Alberta's vast oil reserves in a visit that was viewed as an "open warning" to the U.S which rejected Keystone XL.

Liepert dismissed U.S. protests over the Keystone expansion as "noise" coming from "environmental extremists" and celebrities.

The Keystone XL pipeline is a dicey proposition for Obama, who enjoyed strong support from both organized labor and environmentalists in his 2008 campaign for the White House.

Environmental advocates have made it clear that approval of the pipeline would dampen their enthusiasm for Obama in November. Some liberal donors even threatened to cut off funds to Obama's re-election campaign to protest the project, which opponents say would transport "dirty oil" that requires huge amounts of energy to extract and contributes to greenhouse gas emissions blamed for global warming.

By rejecting the pipeline, Obama also risks losing support from organized labor, a key part of the Democratic base, for thwarting thousands of jobs.
