More than a market

Wilson Farm employee Victoria Snow samples cold strawberry soup, one of the seasonal delicacies at the Lexington property. (Jon Chase for the Boston Globe)
By Kathleen Burge
Globe Staff / July 5, 2012

Volante Farms has been open in Needham since 1917, but this is the first year the family has sold biscuits with chocolate bacon gravy. Or scooped moose tracks ice cream. Or served a $95 four-course dinner, with wine, in one of the fields.

This summer, as the number of local farms and farm stands continues to rise, owners are trying to attract more customers by broadening their scope: In addition to perennial favorites like corn, tomatoes, and strawberries, other local farms are selling catnip, dog food, cornmeal, and goats’ milk fudge. Farms with Community Supported Agriculture, or CSA, enterprises are delivering flowers, seafood, coffee, and honey as part of their harvest shares.

In Concord, Pete Jen’s Backyard Birds and Farmyard sells eggs as well as pork, chicken, lamb, and rabbit online and at the farm store. This year, the farm is raising rare wooly pigs.

As the number of Massachusetts farms has grown — increasing 27 percent between 2002 and 2007 — so, too, has the number of farmers markets: In 2007, the state had 139 such markets. Now, there are 253. This year alone, 21 new markets opened.

“In order to be successful farming in Massachusetts, and really, the Northeast in general, farms have to diversify,” said David Webber, project coordinator for the state’s Department of Agricultural Resources. “Especially if you go into farmers markets, you have to have a full array of products. You might grow 20 varieties of tomatoes or all different types of eggplants.”

Wellesley opened one of the new farmers market this year, on Thursdays in the parking lot of the Whole Foods Market. John Spencer, one of the market’s founders, said townspeople are more interested in locally produced food than in the past, both at the new market and at the community gardens he oversees.

But Martha Sweet, operations manager for the Federation of Massachusetts Farmers Markets, says the newcomers must make sure they also have new customers. Every farmer who sells, she estimates, needs 100 customers at the market just to break even.

“What you really need to do is create new farmers market shoppers,” she said, “because some of these markets are not going to survive.”

And some have not. This year, neither Bedford nor Burlington will have weekly farmers markets.

“We weren’t getting enough customers,” said Barbara Purchia, one of the organizers of the Bedford market. “We struggled all four years.”

Last year, the Bedford market tried to attract more buyers by changing its hours, moving the market closer to noontime, and bringing in some vendors that sold lunch, Purchia said. But even then, too few customers came. This year, organizers decided to scale it back to a single day: July 22.

Purchia thinks the market had to compete with the local Whole Foods Market. And she wonders whether Monday, the day organizers chose so they wouldn’t compete with other farmers markets in the area, was a difficult day to attract shoppers.

“It was a little tiring and frustrating to work so hard and not be able to support our vendors,” Purchia said.

In Burlington, college student Rachel Dutton started the town’s market last year. But when she started a new job and had to limit her involvement this year, no one replaced her.

At the stands hosted by individual farms, customers seem to welcome both the expansion in the size and the products being offered.

Catherine Warren, 19, has been coming to Volante Farms with her mother since she was a young girl. Now that the farm stand has expanded, they are more likely to shop there than the Whole Foods that is the same distance from their Wellesley home.

“It’s obviously gotten a lot bigger,” Warren said. “I feel like they just have to compete a little bit with the other markets that we have in the area.”

Earlier this year, Wilson Farm in Lexington expanded, and held a grand opening for its new space. A few years ago, after a fire destroyed the store at Verrill Farm in Concord, the owners replaced it with a larger farm stand.

And this year, Volante Farms opened its 9,000-square-foot stand, three times as large as the old one. Last month, the farm opened an ice cream window, where employees scoop ice cream from Crescent Ridge Dairy in Sharon. The new stand, which includes a deli, has a large patio for outdoor eating.

And for the first time, the farm stand will stay open year-round. “We’re all young and crazy enough to go for the expansion idea,” said Dave Volante, 30, who runs the farm stand with his brother, Steve, 23, and sister, Terri Volante Boardman, 26.

“It’s sort of the way we had to go,” he said. “People are wanting more and more value-added products. They don’t want to make a lot of stops.”

The farm hired a chef who makes prepared food, including take-home dinners. The farm’s inaugural “Dinner in the Field,” set for July 19, is already sold out.

One recent afternoon, Tracey Matthews and her son, Erik, drove from their home in Newton to Volante Farms to buy some plants. But they had heard about the new deli, and saw the tables on the new patio, and started thinking about staying for lunch.

At Belmont Acres Farm, in the second year of its CSA, farmer Michael Chase is trying to expand the kinds of vegetables shareholders receive each week. This year, he’s growing artichokes, tomatillos, radicchio and okra on his Belmont property.

At the farm stand, too, the vegetables are changing. The farm used to grow a lot of corn, but Chase has pulled back, since corn takes up a lot of land, and the farm is small.

“So what we’re seeing now is a shift,” he said. “We have a lot of people who just came there for corn. Now we’re going to shift the market over more to people who are foodies.”

The farmers markets at rest stops on the Massachusetts Turnpike are continuing this year, with weekend sessions lined up at the Charlton eastbound and Framingham service plazas, according to Sara Lavoie, a spokeswoman for the state Department of Transportation. The state has also opened up the other service plazas along the Pike to farmers who want to sell their wares.
