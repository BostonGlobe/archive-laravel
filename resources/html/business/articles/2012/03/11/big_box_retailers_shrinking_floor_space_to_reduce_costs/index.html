Cutting corners

Big-box retailers are reducing ﬂoor space, and landlords are trying to ﬁll the gaps

The space used by this GameStop in Braintree had been part of Staples. (Debee Tlumacki for The Boston Globe)
By Jenn Abelson
Globe Staff / March 11, 2012

BRAINTREE — You can pretty much size up the state of the nation’s big-box retailers by surveying The Marketplace at Braintree: They are shrinking.

At the shopping center off Route 3, office supplier Staples has already shed about 2,500 square feet of unwanted space, leasing it to video game seller GameStop. Kmart is shaving roughly 11,000 square feet from its shell to make room for cosmetics seller Ulta Beauty. And the former Borders bookstore sits vacant in the corner of the complex, a symbol of the newretail order brought about by the Great Recession.

Similar changes are happening at shopping centers across the country. Brands such as Staples, Old Navy, and Kmart are downsizing their footprints to cut costs following a long period of tepid consumer spending and steady growth in online shopping.

Now, it is getting harder to find a single tenant big enough to fill spaces larger than 20,000 square feet that were left behind by the liquidation of major chains in recent years, including Borders, Circuit City, Linens ’n Things, and Filene’s Basement. As a result, developers and landlords are chopping up the spaces or tearing down big boxes altogether because of the shifting retail landscape.

‘‘There is a change toward being more efficient and downsizing,’’ said Andy LaGrega of The Wilder Cos., which operates 50 shopping centers, mostly in New England. He has been working on deals to slim down Old Navy stores in Dedham and Hyannis, subdivide a former Borders in Burlington, raze big boxes in Cranston, R.I., and shrink several Staples stores across the market.

‘‘We’ve had some cases where retailers have reduced square footage by 40 percent, but kept up the same total sales volume and become much more profitable,’’ LaGrega said. ‘‘If they can be as productive in less square footage, we’ll get more rent down the road. But it’s not easy.’’

Shopping center landlords and developers are loath to lose important tenants such as Old Navy and Staples after years of grappling with brands shutting stores and merchants demanding rent reductions during the recession. But accommodating retailers’ requests to give back some of their square footage can involve complicated configurations and expensive efforts to move electrical feeds, sprinklers, restrooms, loading docks, and storefront doors.

Simply slicing off the side of a building to divide it results in a long, skinny bowling-alley type space that is not desirable to most merchants. In Methuen, Old Navy wanted to keep the middle of its store and jettison excess space on both sides. But it wasn’t feasible to carve out leasable portions after trying for more than a year, LaGrega said.

‘‘You can’t just draw a line and break up the box,’’ said Tom Simmons, president of the mid-Atlantic and Northeast regions for Kimco Realty Corp., one of the country’s largest shopping center operators.

Simmons is finalizing an agreement with a national tenant to take over 7,000 square feet in Shrewsbury that Staples relinquished about six months ago, and he is working on several other local deals to subdivide or lease out unwanted space from big boxes.

‘‘They’ve had to get creative to squeeze and get prototypes to get into the downsized box,’’ Simmons said of Staples. ‘‘But retailers have been sensitive to tweak their stores to ensure we have something left that we can actually lease.’’

Framingham-based Staples, which reported flat sales in 2011 at North American stores open at least a year, has decided to reduce the size of select suburban locations to 15,000 square feet — some were as large as 24,000 square feet, according to Carrie McElwee, a Staples spokeswoman.

‘‘We are more efficient and better able to meet the needs of our customers with smaller buildings. In fact, in many cases our sales have increased as our customers love the new, more open layout, which highlights our new technology offerings, our Copy & Print Center and our EasyTech services center,’’ McElwee said. ‘‘Our associates can provide great service in a smaller building, and we save on rent and utilities.’’

But it is a delicate balancing act, and a sensitive subject for retailers who, if they are willing to discuss the topic, typically call it ‘‘resizing’’ or ‘‘rightsizing’’ rather than downsizing.

‘‘Retailers are very reluctant to talk about downsizing their stores,’’ said Robert F. Sheehan, vice president of research at Key-Point Partners, a Burlington commercial real estate services firm. ‘‘They think it projects a negative image to say, ‘I don’t need as much space as I use to need.’ ’’

Old Navy, which is run by Gap Inc., would not comment on efforts to reduce the sizes of specific sites in New England. The company reported sales dropped 3 percent in 2011 at stores in North America open at least a year, a key measure of a retailer’s health. Gap Brands said it planned to reduce square footage for existing company-operated stores by about 1 percent by the end of the fiscal year.

‘‘In remodeling the stores, we also wanted to make sure the stores are more productive — downsizing and improving the energy, store flow, through a better experience helped accomplish this,’’ said Liz Nunan, an Old Navy spokeswoman.

Sears Holdings Corp. has embarked on a sweeping effort to control its real estate costs. The Illinois department store chain, which also operates Kmart, plans to close up to 120 locations, sell 11 sites to mall operator General Growth Properties, spin off its smaller hardware and outlet stores, and condense some of the remaining big-box stores.

The Kmart in Braintree is scaling back its automotive and seasonal departments and lopping off 11,000 square feet to lease to Ulta Beauty.

‘‘This is an initiative for us to try and devise buildings to attract subtenants at Sears and Kmart stores,’’ said Gary Stevens, the Northeast regional vice president of leasing and development for Sears. ‘‘It’s a store-by-store analysis. We look at the performance of each individual store and assess the desirability of subleasing space to a tenant.’’

Retail analysts say the smaller-is-better thinking is likely to become the norm as more consumers do their shopping online. Many retailers have cut inventory in stores to focus on bestselling and most profitable items — and invested more into building online business.

The NPD Group, a market research firm in Port Washington, NY, recently reported that a significant number of consumers research products by visiting stores, but then make the actual purchases online.

The practice is known as ‘‘showrooming,’’ said Perry James, NPD’s president of home and office supplies. The company’s survey found between 15 and 20 percent of consumers showroomed categories such as stand mixers, electric knives, and sewing machines last year. Other categories — including power tools, hairsetters, and robotic vacuums — are now beginning to become part of the trend.

‘‘If 10 or 15 percent of a retailer’s business that they used to do in the physical store is being done online, they don’t need as much space,’’ said Sheehan. ‘‘There is kind of a new world order to retail and everyone is trying to figure it out.’’
