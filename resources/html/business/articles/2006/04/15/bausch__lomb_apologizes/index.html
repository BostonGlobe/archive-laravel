Bausch & Lomb apologizes

Ads seek to blunt negative publicity

By Bloomberg News  |  April 15, 2006

Bausch & Lomb Inc. ran ads yesterday urging consumers to use other contact lens cleaners made by the company, such as ReNu MultiPlus, rather than one withdrawn after being linked to an infection that can cause blindness.

The company late Thursday said it was withdrawing ReNu with MoistureLoc in the United States and offering refunds. A full-page color advertisement in the USA Today newspaper also said the product had not been proven as a cause of the infection, only that there was a ''disproportionate association" between it and ''a small number of events."

''I am sorry for any inconvenience or anxiety this situation has caused," chief executive Ron Zarrella wrote in a section of the ad designed to look like a letter.

The ad campaign, set to run in 11 US markets, may blunt criticism from marketing specialists and a medical ethicist who said this week the 153-year-old optical products maker wasn't doing enough to inform the 30 million US wearers of contact lenses. Bausch & Lomb stopped shipping the cleaner Monday when US health officials said it was investigating 109 reports of a rare fungal eye infection linked to the product's use.

On Thursday, the Rochester, N.Y.-based company asked that drugstores and supermarkets remove the solution from shelves, an action many, including Wal-Mart Stores, Walgreen Co., Albertson's Inc., and CVS Corp., had already taken.

It's ''a little bit to early to tell" what the financial impact of the withdrawal will be, said company spokeswoman Meg Graham. ''It depends on how long this lasts." The company was to have met yesterday to ''flesh out" a communications strategy beyond the current campaign, Graham said, declining to say how much the ads will cost.

Shares of Bausch & Lomb had fallen for 15 straight days through Thursday, losing 32 percent, or $1.18 billion of market value.

Analysts at Banc of America Securities LLC yesterday cut their forecast for the stock to $40 from $49 and said the company wasn't properly managing the situation.

Arthur Caplan, director of the bioethics center at the University of Pennsylvania, criticized the company's initial failure to recall the cleanser.
