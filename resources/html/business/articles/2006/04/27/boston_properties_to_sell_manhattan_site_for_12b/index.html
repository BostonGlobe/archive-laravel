BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Boston Properties to sell Manhattan site for $1.2b

April 27, 2006

THE REGION
Boston Properties Inc. agreed to sell 280 Park Ave. in midtown Manhattan for about $1.2 billion, to take advantage of rising property prices. Boston Properties did not identify the buyer of the 1.18 million-square-foot complex, which includes a 30-story building and a 43-story tower, said Michael Walsh, senior vice president of finance. The Boston-based real estate investment trust last month said it was in talks to sell two Manhattan properties. It hasn't yet found a buyer for the other property, the 37-story 5 Times Square. (Bloomberg)

Ex-Merrill global strategist opens closed-end fund
Charles Clough, former chief global investment strategist for Merrill Lynch & Co., plans to raise $900 million in an initial public offering of a closed-end mutual fund. Clough Global Opportunities fund expects to sell 45 million shares at $20 each, Boston-based Clough Capital Partners LP said. The fund, which will trade on the American Stock Exchange, is Clough's third US-based closed-end offering. The fund will invest in stocks and bonds, including corporate and sovereign debt outside the United States. (Bloomberg)

N.E. at greater risk this summer for power trouble
New England's failure to build electricity generating plants will put the region at a higher-than-usual risk of resorting to precautions this summer to keep its power grid from overloading, an organization that oversees the grid said. Compared with New York and parts of eastern Canada, New England faces a greater likelihood of measures to ease the load, including temporary voltage reductions and supply interruptions to certain industrial customers, the Northeast Power Coordinating Council said. The council's annual summer forecast highlighted the tight electricity supply in New England, and cited the Boston area and Southwestern Connecticut as problem spots, because of local transmission bottlenecks and thin capacity. (AP)

THE NATION
Shareholders back change for election of directors
Bank of America Corp. shareholders, rejecting management's recommendation, approved a measure calling for the number two US bank's directors to be elected by majority vote. Separately, Bank of America said it may spend up to $12 billion over 18 months to buy back up to 200 million shares. Shares closed up 62 cents at $47.71 after hitting a record high of $47.78. At the annual meeting near its Charlotte, N.C., headquarters, a 56 percent majority of shareholders said the board should amend the company's certificate of incorporation or bylaws to require a majority vote. (Reuters)

Comair loses a ruling on flight attendants deal
A US Bankruptcy Court judge denied Delta Air Lines subsidiary Comair's request to reject its contract with its 970 flight attendants so it can impose pay cuts the regional carrier says it needs to stay in business. Judge Adlai Hardin in New York said requirements of the bankruptcy law that would allow the rejection of a collective bargaining agreement were not met. Comair and the International Brotherhood of Teamsters, which represents the flight attendants, must return to the bargaining table. (AP)

Diesel passenger vehicles gain favor with consumers
The number of diesel cars, trucks, and sport utilities on US roads rose 80 percent between 2000 and 2005 as more buyers chose fuel-efficient diesels as an option, according to data released by a diesel advocacy group. There were 543,777 new diesel vehicles registered in 2005, compared to 301,471 five years earlier, according to data compiled by the auto information firm R.L. Polk & Co. and released by the Diesel Technology Forum. (AP)

Ace Ltd. settles bid-rigging suit with 3 states for $80m
Insurance company Ace Ltd. settled a bid-rigging investigation by New York, Illinois, and Connecticut for $80 million in restitution and penalties, New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer said. Ace, a Bermuda holding company, and its subsidiaries were accused of bid rigging and other improper transactions in so-called finite insurance and reinsurance contracts. Reinsurance is taken out by insurance companies to protect against risk they assume when they write traditional insurance polices, such as those for homeowners and business owners. Finite-risk insurance is a type of reinsurance. Ace agreed to reform its practices and apologized, Spitzer said. A company spokesman declined to comment. (AP)
