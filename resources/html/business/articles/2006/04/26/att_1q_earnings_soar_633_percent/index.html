AT&T 1Q earnings soar 63.3 percent

The AT&T, Inc. logo is seen on on of the company's buildings in San Antonio, Tuesday, April 25, 2006. AT&T said its earnings rose 63.3 percent in the first quarter. AT&T Inc. says its earnings rose 63.3 percent in the first quarter, the first period it reported combined results after SBC Communications Inc.'s acquisition of AT&T Corp., which closed late last year. (AP Photo/Eric Gay)

By Peter Svensson, AP Technology Writer  |  April 26, 2006

NEW YORK --AT&T Inc., formerly SBC Communications Inc., Tuesday said its earnings rose 63.3 percent in the first quarter mostly because of its acquisition of AT&T Corp.

It was the first full quarterly report since SBC bought AT&T Corp. in November and adopted its brand to become AT&T Inc.

Net income was $1.445 billion, or 37 cents a share, for the January-March period, up from SBC's earnings of $885 million, or 27 cents a share, in the same period last year.

Revenue came to $15.8 billion, up 55 percent from SBC's $10.2 billion the previous year. Counting AT&T Corp.'s results last year, revenue declined 5 percent. Revenue figures exclude sales from Cingular Wireless, AT&T Inc.'s joint venture with BellSouth Corp.

Excluding the costs of SBC's acquisition of AT&T Corp. and Cingular's 2004 acquisition of AT&T Wireless, earnings were 52 cents a share, beating the average estimate of analysts polled by Thomson Financial by 3 cents.

AT&T's shares rose 7 cents to close at $25.60 on the New York Stock Exchange. The shares are up 4.5 percent so far this year.

UBS analyst John Hodulik said margins were overall stronger than expected, and gave him increasing confidence in his annual earnings estimate of $2 a share.

As expected, results were strong at Cingular, which reported its first-quarter earnings last week along with Atlanta-based BellSouth Corp., which owns 40 percent of the venture. San Antonio-based AT&T owns the rest.

Cingular, already the country's largest cell-phone carrier, ended the quarter with 55.8 million subscribers, up 5.5 million from a year ago, and contributed $287 million to AT&T's income. Its revenue of $9 billion was not counted in AT&T's revenue.

Revenue in the traditional wired phone business was $14.7 billion, down 5.5 percent from SBC's and AT&T's combined results a year ago. Contributing to the decline was the continued free fall of the former AT&T Corp.'s consumer long-distance business. Even before the merger, AT&T stopped marketing the service due to dwindling margins and competition from regional phone companies like SBC.

Revenue from large business accounts fell 6.9 percent due to competition, but the company said the rate of decline was slowing.

The number of phone lines served also declined, to 49 million phone from 52 million, due to competition from cell phones, cable and Internet calling. However, this was offset by added revenue from data services. AT&T added 511,000 broadband digital subscriber lines, for a total of 7.4 million.

AT&T's chief financial officer, Rick Lindner, said on a conference call that the company plans to double the top speed available to its DSL subscribers, launching a service with download speeds of up to 6 megabits per second this week.

The service will cost $29.99 per month for the first 12 months. Starting Monday, it will also be available for online ordering at a slightly lower price -- $27.99 per month for the first year. After 12 months, the price rises to $39.99 for both ordering options.

Service at that speed has previously been available to AT&T's business customers. BellSouth introduced 6 Mbps service in November.

AT&T Chief Executive Ed Whitacre said the integration of AT&T Corp. and SBC was going according to plan. The front-line sales force has been combined, and plans for integrating the networks are in place. Whitacre still expects the merger to save $600 million to $800 million this year.

The company job cuts, partly due to the merger, are running ahead of schedule, with 3,400 eliminated so far this year. Executives have noted before that the number is comparable to the normal attrition rate at the company, which had 186,560 employees at the end of the quarter.

Even as the former SBC is digesting its acquisition of AT&T Corp., it has announced another mega-merger. In March, AT&T announced a deal to buy BellSouth for $67 billion. The deal was initially expected to close by March 2007, but the companies said last week that they now expect the deal to close by the end of this year. A major reason for the deal is that it would give AT&T full control of growth engine Cingular.

Among U.S. phone companies, AT&T is second in revenue only to New York-based Verizon Communications Inc., which unlike AT&T counts its wireless joint venture in its sales. AT&T has had slightly more access lines than Verizon, which reports its first-quarter results next week. A merged BellSouth and AT&T would be far larger than Verizon.
