SMALL BIZ

Establishing goals is the first key to success

January 15, 2006

Debbie Gilster doesn't make new year's resolutions.

She finds setting intentions less intimidating for the small business clients of her consulting practice, the Center for Growth & Productivity in Laguna Niguel, Calif.

''I'm a big proponent of planning, but many small-business people are fearful to put goals in writing: It seems inflexible," Gilster explains. ''If they just write down what they intend to do for the year, it [seems] more changeable."

''New year" and resolution go togeter like e-mail and spam. But several small-business experts, who are small-business owners themselves, recognize the importance of setting goals -- or intentions -- and January is as good a time as any to start.

''I recommend that my clients get away from the office hustle and bustle so they can concentrate," Gilster says. ''You can go anywhere you can think: the beach, library, mountains."

The effort must be intentional, says Raul Medrano of R.J. Medrano & Associates, a Santa Ana diversity consultant to major corporations. Without a formal program and commitment, owners and top executives are unlikely to define goals that are worthy to do or devote the resources to achieving them.

Furthermore, the owner might need to do research before starting, adds Euiwon Chough of Chough & Associates in Anaheim. ''They need to know what makes their business unique. They need to understand their market and how their customers are doing. And they should prepare their financial resources to have a cushion" for achieving the goals.

Information isn't enough, Chough says. ''You have to have the attitude that you can achieve the goals you set. They can't just be wishful thinking."

The owner needs to take a broad, long-term view toward setting goals, says corporate trainer Judy Sherkow of Training Dynamics in Corona. ''Americans tend to have a short-term focus. In training, for example, they need to look at their human capital and identify the kinds of skills they need [employees to have] to get the company where they want it to be."

Huntington Beach consultant and business coach Susan Bock, of Susan Bock Solutions, adds one more essential to start the process of goal-setting: a clear understanding of the company's values and ethics.

She walks her clients through three steps to set goals: reflection, contemplation, and commitment.

''The first step is to look over the last year," she says. ''What have they learned? What are they thrilled about? What didn't get done?" Contemplation is the time to set goals the business owner can believe in. Then commitment involves identifying the steps to achieve them.

Gilster offers another approach: Type up lists of what you want to have and do and be, and refine them over time. Eventually, the lists will help the business owner define a mission statement for the company, Gilster says.

''It takes several years to get the process down. Trying to write a mission statement to start can be overwhelming."

But keep it realistic, advises George Blanc of GEES and Associates, a Newport Beach small-business advisor. "The first thing is that goals must be measurable and realistic." The goal shouldn't be merely to grow revenues, but to grow them a specific amount, say 5 percent, and specify how that growth will be achieved, Blanc explains. ''Will they have a sales campaign? Will they hire more sales staff? Will they do it themselves and if so, how?" Most small-business owners are too close to see the needs of their companies, Blanc says.

''Sometimes they need another person to look at their business," he says. ''I have one client, the owner of a sheet metal plant, who is spending so much time training employees that he has no time to market the business. Profits are down and he doesn't know what to do."

A disinterested party could easily advise that the dual goals of improving employee skills and finding more customers requires outside help.

Defining measurements for either goal first will improve the chances for success, Blanc says.

Goals are just the beginning, all these advisers agree.

Leaders must be committed to achieving the set goals, Medrano says, and then they must train employees to view the goals as opportunities for the company, not nuisances to be circumvented.

Sherkow agrees. ''You have to have commitment from the top down and follow through to make sure people do the work. Sometimes they forget the progress they make and fall back to old ways of doing things. That's human nature to go back to their comfort zone."

Such backsliding is why business owners need accountability partners, Gilster says. "It can be a business coach, some small-business group, a spouse, your partners, or business advisers.

''I'm a firm believer that we can do this ourselves, but some people need to pay for it to value it."

Jan Norman is a columnist for The Orange County Register.

More than just wishful thinking

Here's how to approach goal-setting to achieve results:

  • Reflect on the past year's accomplishments and failures.

  • Do your research: Identify company uniqueness and prepare financial resources.

  • Be realistic. Don't set goals that are too big.

  • If needed, get a disinterested outsider to look at the business.

  • Align each goal with your business values and ethics.

  • Set goals you believe in. Worthy goals that you don't really want to achieve won't be met.

  • Believe you can accomplish the goal you set.

  • For every goal, assign necessary tasks to achieve it that are measurable and set dates by which they will be accomplished.

  • Get commitment from management and employees for each goal.

  • Train employees how to facilitate and achieve identified goals.

  • Find partners to hold you accountable for achieving identified goals.
