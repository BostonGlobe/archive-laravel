EARNINGS ROUNDUP

Profit drops 41% at N.Y. Times Co.

January 25, 2006
YESTERDAY
Close $28.12
Change +$0.81
52-WEEK
High $40.40
Low $26.09

The New York Times Co. said fourth-quarter profit declined 41 percent on costs to eliminate jobs.

Net income fell to $64.8 million, or 45 cents a share, from $110.2 million, or 75 cents a share, the publisher of The New York Times and The Boston Globe said. Sales rose 3 percent to $931 million.

Excluding the one-time costs, profit was 64 cents a share, beating the 62 cents expected by Lauren Rich Fine, a newspaper analyst at Merrill Lynch & Co.

Times Co. may be a harbinger for the rest of the industry. Dow Jones & Co. will report this week, followed by Gannett Co. and Tribune Co.

All face defections from advertisers to other media such as cable television and the Internet.

Knight Ridder Inc. put itself up for sale last year after three quarters without sales growth.

Separately, Times Co. said Yasmin Namini has been named senior vice president of circulation for The New York Times newspaper effective March 20. Namini, who is currently chief customer officer of Times Co.'s New England Media Group, has also served as senior vice president of consumer marketing for the New England group, senior vice president of circulation at the Globe, and vice president of circulation marketing at the Times. (Bloomberg)

EMC Corp. said its fourth-quarter profit dropped by 54 percent as the data storage company reported hefty charges from tax expenses, job cut severance costs, and an acquisition.

But EMC issued a 2006 sales forecast that exceeded analysts' expectations.

Hopkinton-based EMC said its net income for the October-December period was $148.3 million, or 6 cents per share, compared with a profit of $320.5 million, or 13 cents per share, in the fourth quarter of 2004. Revenue rose 15 percent, to $2.71 billion from $2.36 billion a year earlier.

Excluding one-time charges, last quarter's profit was $409 million, or 17 cents per share, matching the consensus forecast of analysts. Analysts forecast $2.69 billion in revenue, just below the $2.71 billion EMC reported.

Citing expectations for continued growth in corporate spending on information technology, EMC forecasts 2006 revenue of $11.1 billion to $11.3 billion, up 15 to 17 percent from 2005. Analysts had expected 2006 revenue of $10.95 billion. (AP)

Waters Corp. reported fourth-quarter earnings above the Wall Street consensus estimate, citing in part the strength of its Asian businesses.

Milford-based Waters said fourth-quarter earnings were $198.29 million, or 71 cents a share, up from $194.01 million, or 58 cents a share a year-ago.

Analysts were expecting 64 cents a share.

Revenue for the quarter grew 2.5 percent to $332.3 million from $324.2 million. Wall Street anticipated revenue of $326.77 million. (Dow Jones)

Johnson & Johnson said fourth-quarter earnings rose on high-profit medical devices. Revenue dropped for the first time in 22 years.

Revenue unexpectedly fell 1.1 percent to $12.6 billion on lower drug sales, the New Brunswick, N.J., company said. Net income jumped 79 percent to $2.18 billion, or 73 cents a share, from $1.21 billion, or 41 cents, a year earlier.

J&J, which was facing a midnight deadline to answer a higher offer for Guidant Corp. from Boston Scientific Corp., said revenue from medical devices rose 3.7 percent.

Excluding certain costs, fourth-quarter profit was 73 cents a share. That matched the estimate of analysts. (Bloomberg)

Strong momentum at its US restaurants and improved sales in Europe helped McDonald's Corp.'s fourth-quarter income rise 53 percent.

Net income for the last three months of 2005 was $608 million, or 48 cents per share, up from $398 million, or 31 cents per share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 4 percent to $5.23 billion from $5.01 billion.

The fourth-quarter numbers all were foretold by the company in guidance a week earlier, but Wall Street continued to endorse the performance of the Oak Brook, Ill., company, sending its shares to a 5 1/2-year high. (AP)
