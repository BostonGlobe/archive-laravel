Despite auto-rate cut, insurers offering deals

Safe-driver reductions drying up, but group discounts are plentiful

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  January 18, 2006

Massachusetts automobile insurers are continuing to offer voluntary discounts to drivers this year, despite regulatory approval of an 8.7 percent reduction in the average statewide premium.

Discount plans filed yesterday with the Massachusetts Division of Insurance indicate most of the state's auto insurers plan to offer a variety of voluntary group discounts to employees of various companies and members of associations, but special discounts for good drivers continue to dry up.

Two companies, Amica Mutual Insurance and Electric Insurance, last year offered discounts to the state's best drivers, but this year only Amica is offering the same type of rate relief and has targeted the benefit at a smaller group of drivers.

Amica last year offered a 5 percent discount to the state's best drivers and an additional 5 percent to drivers who insured multiple cars with the company. This year, the company is offering a combined 10 percent discount, but only to good drivers who also insure more than one car with the company.

Robert Suglia, Amica's general counsel, said the insurer had to narrow the reach of its good-driver discount in part because state regulators here set all auto insurance rates.

''If we had a more competitive rating environment, we might be able to do more for other drivers," he said.

Christopher Goetcheus, a spokesman for the Division of Insurance, which is pushing legislation filed by Governor Mitt Romney that would allow insurers to set their own rates, said the decrease in good driver discounts is another sign of a troubled auto insurance system.

''We've hit a new low in terms of consumer choice in this marketplace," Goetcheus said.

Automobile insurers offered much bigger voluntary discounts in the late 1990s, largely to steal customers away from competitors. As regulators have reduced average premiums over the years, the discounts, particularly those targeted at good drivers, have diminished.

But so-called group discounts have remained plentiful, although some have been reduced. Commerce Insurance of Webster is continuing to offer a 5 percent discount to members of the American Automobile Association of Southern New England, which anyone can join by paying the $48 annual membership fee.

Plymouth Rock Assurance Corp. is also continuing to offer discounts of 10 percent to members of the Conservation Law Foundation, the Massachusetts Audubon Society, and the Appalachian Mountain Club, which charge individual membership fees ranging from $30 to $50.

Plymouth Rock is also offering a 10 percent discount to anyone with a life insurance policy from Savings Bank Life Insurance who pays an extra $5 to join a special health and safety group set up by the company.

Most insurers continue to offer discounts to employees at certain companies and members of a number of associations. For example, Metropolitan Property & Casualty is offering a 12 percent discount to employees at Raytheon, Premier Insurance is cutting rates 10 percent for employees at Boston University, and Safety Insurance is offering a 5 percent discount to members of the Boston College Alumni Association.

Overall, Goetcheus said, the number of group discounts appears to be holding steady compared to last year, despite the steep cut in rates approved by state regulators this year. Goetcheus said 1,981 group discounts were approved last year and companies are on pace to offer a similar number this year.

Stephen D'Amato, a consultant to the Center for Insurance Research in Cambridge and an opponent of the Romney Administration's auto insurance bill, said he doubts all good drivers would benefit more under a competitive rate-setting system.

Under the governor's bill, he said, insurers would be able to look beyond driving history to other factors, including the driver's occupation, credit history, and marital status.

''Some drivers would do better under a competitive system, but it's not going to be beneficial to safe drivers across the board," he said.

Senator Andrea Nuciforo Jr., cochairman of the legislative committee considering changes in the state's auto insurance system, said the continuation of voluntary discounts is a good sign for drivers.

''It tells me that there continues to be competitive options for those consumers that want to shop around," he said.

Nuciforo, a Pittsfield Democrat, is not in favor of the sweeping changes sought by the Romney administration, but he does favor taking steps to cut costs for companies, reduce fraud, and change the way high-risk drivers are apportioned.

Nuciforo's reluctance to endorse competitive rate setting has made him the target of an ad campaign by the Massachusetts Insurance Federation, which consists of companies pushing for more control of the rate-setting process.
