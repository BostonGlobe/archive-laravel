Scandal's temblors shut Tokyo exchange early

By Washington Post  |  January 19, 2006

TOKYO -- A stampede of sellers forced an early close of the world's second-biggest stock exchange yesterday, as investors panicked over a mounting financial scandal that could thin the gains made during Japan's economic comeback.

The alarm spread after a series of government raids began Monday on the offices of Takafumi Horie, 33, the billionaire president of Internet and corporate investment firm Livedoor Co. and whose golden touch may have been the product of Enron-like cooked books. The Tokyo Stock Exchange suspended all trading 20 minutes before its scheduled close, after its computers were overwhelmed by the volume. After shedding 2.84 percent on Tuesday, the Nikkei index fell 2.94 percent more to 15,341.18, making it the biggest two-day loss in 5 1/2 years. In three days, the exchange has shed roughly $300 billion in shareholder value. Livedoor alone has lost $1.8 billion in market capitalization.

The Nikkei index rose slightly at the opening today, adding 55.42 points, or 0.26 percent, to 15,396.60 in early trading.

It has thrown an unexpected bump into the path of Japan's economic recovery and comes after the Nikkei had climbed Friday to a five-year high; it gained a whopping 40 percent last year. But with fears mounting that Horie and Livedoor and its related companies illegally drove up stock prices and inflated company earnings, investors dumped what had been red-hot Japanese Internet, information-technology, and start-up stocks.

Some analysts feared a domino effect of falling stocks if investigators find that other sizzling Japanese investment companies and start-ups had been engaging in similar activities.

''Livedoor triggered this," said Shinichi Ichikawa, equity strategist with Credit Swisse in Tokyo. ''Just like the Enron case in the US, where one accounting scandal triggered a major upheaval, the situation will be worrisome here if what was going on at Livedoor was going on in other companies."

The closure was the first time since World War II that the exchange had to close because of overwhelming volume. A wave of shock hit the Japanese public as investigators carted off computers, files, and documents from the offices of Horie, who rose to celebrity status in 2004 by trading in the traditional Japanese business suit for $300 Louis Vuitton T-shirts and designer denim. He ushered US-style corporate aggression into Japan, staging hostile takeovers and using unorthodox business practices that many saw as a much-needed shift away from the staid norms that had left Japan wallowing in recession for more than a decade.

In the process, Livedoor drew new players into Japan's stock market -- particularly small investors and day traders, both relatively new concepts in Japan.

Takafumi Horie
Accepted to study literature at Japan's most prestigious university, but dropped out and started Livedoor, his Internet portal, at age 23 with a $52,000 loan. ... Worth $87 million by age 30. ... Achieved growth largely through acquisitions and stock swaps. ... A hero to younger countrymen, is considered a bane of Japan's power structure for his brashness, exemplified by the title of one of his books on entrepreneurism: ''Earning Money is Everything: From Zero to 10 Billion Yen, My Way." ... Recruited by Junichiro Koizumi's Liberal Democratic Party to run on his pro-reform platform. Koizumi became prime minister; Horie gave a good showing but ended up losing. ... Nicknamed ''Horiemon," a pun on the character Doreamon, a chubby blue cat with magical powers ... Indulges in gourmet food but also works out at celebrity gym ... Drives a Ferrari, owns a jet ... 33 years old, formerly married.
