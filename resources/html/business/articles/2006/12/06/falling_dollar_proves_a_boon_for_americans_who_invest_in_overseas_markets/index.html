JEFF BROWN

Falling dollar proves a boon for Americans who invest in overseas markets

By Jeff Brown, The Philadelphia Inquirer  |  December 6, 2006

You probably know that the dollar is plunging. That will make it expensive to travel overseas for the holidays or to buy something made in a foreign land.

But I prefer the bright side -- the way the falling dollar is driving up the value of foreign investments owned by Americans.

Just imagine if you'd invested in a basket of blue-chip European stocks last December. Those stocks, represented by the Dow Jones STOXX 50 index, have had a so-so run, returning only about 7 percent since the start of the year.

But that assumes you'd bought those stocks with euros, the European currency. If you'd started with dollars, you would have made about 20 percent because the dollar, compared with the euro, is worth less today than it was a year ago.

Back then, a dollar bought 0.85 euros. Today it gets only 0.75 euros. Looked at the other way, a euro could be exchanged for $1.17 a year ago; today it would be converted to $1.33.

When an American invests in a foreign stock, or a mutual fund with foreign stocks, it works like this: Your dollars are converted into one or more foreign currencies, which are then spent on the foreign shares. When you sell, the shares generate foreign currency, which is then converted into dollars.

If it works out perfectly, the dollar is high when you buy -- your money converts to lots of euros, or yen, or whatever, so you can buy lots of stock. Then the dollar is low when you sell, so that the foreign currency you get for your shares converts to many, many dollars.

For Americans, many foreign investments have benefited from this perfect scenario. The falling dollar boosts this year's returns on big French stocks to 25 percent, versus 11.5 percent without the exchange rate taken into account. Spanish stocks are up an impressive 28 percent, but an American investor would have made nearly 44 percent because of the dropping dollar.

Americans' returns for many Latin American and Far East stocks also have benefited from the dollar's fall, though generally not as much.

The easiest way for Americans to buy foreign stocks is through mutual funds. While the average fund holding US stocks is up about 11.5 percent this year, funds with European stocks have returned 29 percent, while Latin American funds are up nearly 35 percent and Chinese stock funds are up nearly 48 percent.

Many experts advise Americans to keep 20 percent to 40 percent of their stock portfolios in foreign stocks or funds. That helps diversify, or spread, your risk around the world. If your US stocks do poorly, perhaps the foreign ones will do well. Also, many foreign markets are thought to have more room for growth than the United States does, offering better prospects.

But you face risks overseas that you don't have at home. Most other countries have looser corporate accounting requirements and don't require companies to disclose as much financial information. Conflict-of-interest rules are often less stringent. Many foreign economies are shaky. Politics are more turbulent.

And, of course, changes in currency exchange rates can hurt as well as help. If the dollar gets stronger after you invest overseas, your holdings could lose value even if those foreign stocks do well.

Now that the dollar has already fallen so far, and so many foreign stocks have soared, is it too late to invest overseas?

This might not be the best time to put a huge sum into foreign stocks or funds all at once. But it does make sense to try to reach one of those targets -- 20 percent to 40 percent of your stock portfolio, depending on your stomach for risk -- in a series of monthly or quarterly investments over the next few years.

Currency fluctuations are impossible to predict, so it's best to assume the dollar's ups and downs will even out over time rather than to try to time the currency markets.

To start, I'd look first at the funds designed to mimic the performance of the Morgan Stanley EAFE Index, which represents stocks in Europe, Australasia, and the Far East. These index-style funds have very low fees and will provide very broad diversification. Go to morningstar.com and research the ticker symbols EFA, VDMIX, FSIIX, and ETINX. Find others by keying those symbols into the site's Similar Funds tool.
