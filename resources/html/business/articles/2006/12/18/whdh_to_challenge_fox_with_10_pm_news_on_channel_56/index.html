WHDH to challenge Fox with 10 p.m. news on channel 56

By Robert Gavin, Globe Staff  |  December 18, 2006

WHDH-TV Channel 7 launches a 10 p.m. edition of its top-rated 11 o'clock news Tuesday night, but faces a tough battle to repeat its success in the earlier time slot.

With the completion of its parent company's $114 million takeover of WLVI-TV Channel 56, WHDH will produce an hourlong newscast to air on Channel 56, replacing WLVI's 10 o'clock news. Dubbed "7News at 10 p.m. on CW 56," WHDH will challenge the Fox network affiliate, WFXT-TV Channel 25, which has long dominated the 10-to-11 p.m. time slot for news viewers.

WHDH is an NBC affiliate. WLVI is an affiliate of the CW network, created by the recent merger of the WB and UPN networks.

The 10 p.m. slot now will feature two high-energy news programs that attract audiences with aggressive reporting, high story counts, sophisticated graphics, and generous doses of local crime and dramatic satellite footage. The formula has propelled both to the top of the ratings in their respective late-night spots.

WFXT, known as Fox 25, launched its 10 o'clock news in 1996, and quickly vaulted past the longer-established WLVI in the ratings. In 2002, WBZ-TV Channel 4 tried a 10 o'clock newscast on WSBK Channel 38, owned by its parent, CBS Inc., but pulled the plug three years later.

"We literally went from having no viewers to No. 1," said Gregg Kelley, WFXT's general manager, "and that speaks to the value of our newscast."

But officials of WHDH and its parent, Sunbeam Television Corp. of Miami, believe their "7News" brand is strong enough to attract viewers to the 10 o'clock program, without hurting the 11 p.m. product. Different audiences watch the newscasts, they said: one group that doesn't stay up past 11, the other that does.

"Fundamentally, we are expanding our local news franchise," said Ed Ansin, owner of privately held Sunbeam. "We're creating a new product, but it's the same franchise."

Sunbeam agreed this fall to buy WLVI from its longtime owner, Tribune Co. of Chicago, which put several TV properties up for sale. The Federal Communications Commission approved the transfer of the broadcasting license last month.

With the acquisition, Sunbeam creates another television duopoly, in which one company owns two broadcast stations in the same market. In Boston, it joins CBS, the owner of channels 4 and 38, and Hearst-Argyle Television Inc., which owns ABC-affiliates WCVB-TV Channel 5 and WMUR-TV Channel 9 in Manchester, N.H. (Manchester is considered part of the Boston market).

The growth of duopolies reflects market forces buffeting traditional media and driving consolidation. Viewership and advertising revenue are shrinking as competition from cable, satellite, video games, and the Internet fractionalize audiences. The number of people watching the 11 p.m. news in Boston, for example, has fallen by more than one-third since 1994, according to Nielsen Media Research.

At its most basic, the WLVI deal gives Sunbeam revenue from two stations at the cost of operating one. Sunbeam consolidated operations at WHDH's downtown studio, where programming from the two networks is received and distributed to the respective stations, and added just 30 employees, with about half going to expanded news programming.

Meanwhile, Sunbeam has laid off more than 100 employees from WLVI and put the station's Dorchester building up for sale.

"When you can run two stations from one, it becomes very efficient economically," Ansin said. "That was one of the keys."

The other: the chance to produce a 10 p.m. news program.

Local news, primarily supported by local advertisers, is becoming increasingly important to the stations as national advertisers spread spending among outlets. A few years ago, national advertising accounted for about 60 percent of local station revenue; now it makes up just 45 percent, said Michael Carson, the general manager of Sunbeam's Boston stations.

Consequently, stations depend more than ever on locally generated revenue, of which local news advertising makes up the biggest share, about 40 percent, according to Carson. Local ads on network programs, in comparison, account for 30 percent of revenue.

The added newscast becomes a new source of revenue generated at a slight increase in costs, Carson said. The hourlong format will give the 10 p.m. news a slightly different pace, introducing entertainment and style features, and a slightly different look in its graphics. Frances Rivera will anchor both news programs, sharing duties with Matt Lorch at 10, then Randy Price at 11.

But it will be produced in Channel 7 studios with essentially the same staff. For this reason, said Jim Thistle, journalism professor at Boston University, Sunbeam won't have to top the ratings to make the 10 p.m. news profitable.

WFXT, industry specialists noted, has advantages, including a well-established broadcast and higher-rated network programming leading into the 10 p.m. slot. "To get people to switch the channel at 10 o'clock is going to be tough," said Paul Niwa, a journalism professor at Emerson College.

Still, insisted Carson, Sunbeam's goal is to win the ratings, which would mean higher ad rates and more money. It plans to promote the 10 o'clock news on WHDH, and spend "significantly" on advertising to get viewers to switch.

"When you're sharing a pie," Carson said, "you want the biggest piece."
