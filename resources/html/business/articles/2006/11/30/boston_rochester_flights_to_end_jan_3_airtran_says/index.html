BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Boston-Rochester flights to end Jan. 3, AirTran says

November 30, 2006

THE REGION
AirTran Airways, which began offering Boston-Rochester, N.Y., nonstops in July, will phase them out Jan. 3, a spokeswoman said, after failing to make much headway against US Airways, the dominant carrier on the route. AirTran has recently averaged just 5 percent of the business on the 343-mile route, compared to 84 percent for US Airways, according to FareCompare.com, a travel website that analyzes airline ticket records submitted to the US Department of Transportation. (Peter J. Howe)

Waltham man sentenced on insider-trading charges
A hedge fund manager was sentenced in federal court in Boston on insider-trading charges, the offices of US Attorney Michael J. Sullivan and the FBI said. Michael K.C. Tom, 37, of Waltham, was sentenced by US District Judge Reginald C. Lindsay to three years of probation, with the first six months to be served in a community confinement facility, federal officials said. The charges arose from Tom's trading of Citizens Bank common stock and options based on a tip from a Citizens employee about the then-pending merger with Charter One Financial, officials said. (Chris Reidy)

FDA schedules meeting for Hemopure decision
Biopure Corp., of Cambridge, and the Navy will make their case to regulators next month for using injured civilians to test Hemopure, a cow-derived blood substitute, that may eventually save lives on the battlefield. Medical and scientific advisers to the Food and Drug Administration will meet Dec. 14 to consider whether the product should be tried on people who may be too seriously hurt to give permission, the agency said in a notice being published today . The FDA canceled a closed-door session in July after a consumer advocate sued to make it public. (Bloomberg)

Tower operator reports profits after restatements
American Tower Corp. said it posted profits in the second and third quarters, swinging from losses in the previous year. The broadcast and communications tower operator also filed amended 2005 and first-quarter 2006 reports with the Securities and Exchange Commission, after a review of its historical stock-option granting practices. In its SEC filing, the Boston company said that for the third quarter, ended Sept. 30, it earned $3.47 million, or a penny a share, compared with a loss of $22.1 million, or 7 cents a share, in the same period a year earlier. Total operating revenue for the third quarter rose to $333 million, from about $265 million in the year-before period. (Dow Jones/AP)

Morgan Stanley sells 13 properties for $537.9m
Morgan Stanley's real estate unit said it sold 13 properties acquired in the purchase of Glenborough Realty Trust Inc., an office real estate investment trust, to Normandy Real Estate Partners, for $537.9 million. The properties, totaling about 2.4 million square feet, are located in Boston, New Jersey, and the Washington area, Morgan Stanley said. The cash acquisition of Glenborough, valued at about $1.9 billion, including $792 million worth of assumed debt, was completed yesterday, said Morgan Stanley. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Labor Dept. excludes brokers from OT pay
Brokerages need not pay overtime to stock brokers, the Department of Labor said in an opinion that could help firms defend against possible employee lawsuits. Brokers typically earn more than the legal threshold of $455 a week in salary as well as other compensation in commissions, fees, and bonuses, according to the nonbinding opinion. In addition, brokers generally are advisory and administrative employees rather than salesmen, according to the eight-page opinion. (Bloomberg)

Ex-official gets three years in prison for tax fraud
Raymond S. Stevenson, the former head of Tyco International Ltd.'s tax department, was sentenced to three years in prison for filing a tax return that failed to report more than $170 million in company income. US District Judge Donald Middlebrooks in Miami also ordered Stevenson to pay a $100,000 fine after he pleaded guilty to a single charge of filing a false corporate income tax return. The prison sentence was the maximum Stevenson could get under the law, Middlebrooks said. (Bloomberg)
