By STEPHEN MAJORS , Associated Press  |  November 17, 2006

ORLANDO, Fla. (AP) -- Odorless, tasteless and colorless. Despite that trifecta, vodka has maintained a popular following, partly because of the martini -- a sexy drink in an elegant glass reflecting status and sophistication. It mixes well with fruit juices, too.

Oh, but how vodka is changing.

The urge to look good drinking it is not.

Flavored vodkas have exploded onto bar shelves, and the young and old, women and men, college kids and yuppies, are drinking fruity, chocolate and coffee-flavored varieties. In a society defined by Starbucks customization and status, vodka and martini lovers are seeking the flavors that are perfectly, undoubtedly "me."

"The world is going after lifestyle," said Dave van de Velde, president of Orlando-based Luctor International. Luctor imports Van Gogh Vodka, a small but steadily growing "super-premium" brand. "How do I want to live? How can I express who I am?"

According to Adams Beverage Group, whose "Liquor Handbook" tracks spirits sales, Van Gogh Vodka is only the 11th best-selling imported vodka. In an import business where most are making money, Van Gogh is staking its business on its 16 flavors -- more than any other producer. With virtually no advertising, its plain vodka can't compete with the standard fare produced by Grey Goose, for example.

Absolut is the best-selling imported vodka, with Grey Goose coming in second. Smirnoff is the best-selling vodka overall, selling about twice as much as Absolut, but is not imported or considered a premium brand by industry analysts.

Plain vodka still dominates the U.S. vodka market, comprising 90 percent of sales in a market where 46 million cases were sold in 2005. But industry analysts say that remaining 10 percent -- the territory of the flavors and premium brands selling at $20 a bottle and up -- is driving vodka's future. Van Gogh sells for $25 to $29 a bottle on average.

"It's like the new thing," said Tania Bernard, a bartender at Orlando's trendy Peacock Room.

No one knows this better than van de Velde.

The same consumers who will tell a waiter the meticulous details of how they want their food prepared are schizophrenic, he said.

They will order a Grey Goose or Chivas Regal based on status, but don't really want vodka or whiskey. And even if dieting, they will order a high-end plain vodka mixed with a cheap, high-calorie, sugary liqueur without knowing what they are really consuming, van de Velde laments.

He boasts of Van Gogh's naturally infused flavors, which have the kick of apple or raspberry without the glut of sugar. Van Gogh said its flavored sales have increased 38 percent year-to-date over last year, and in September and October of this year, sales are up 70 percent over last year. And the heavy-drinking holiday season is approaching.

Van Gogh sold 28,000 cases in 2000 and rose to 125,000 in 2005, according to Adams Beverage Group.

After one year, Double Espresso Double Caffeine is now Van Gogh's sales leader, the company said. Younger consumers are turning to the flavor as an alternative to Red Bull and vodka, said Stuart Sutherland, general sales manager for National Distributing Company in Deerfield Beach, a Van Gogh distributor.

Van Gogh, however, has to compete where it has a chance -- cutting edge flavors such as pomegranate and espresso that attract consumer attention from the bottom up. The biggest vodka players spend on advertising what Van Gogh pulls in revenue.

Absolut, owned by the Swedish V&S Group, sold 4.6 million cases in 2005. It comes in eight different flavors.

Grey Goose Vodka, whose traditional vodka drives over 90 percent of its market share, sold over 2 million cases in 2005.

"A great many players in the vodka category are using flavors to basically sustain their volume going forward," said Monsell Darville, vice president group marketing director at Grey Goose, owned by Bacardi Ltd. "Flavors tend to mask some issues in a number of ways. Our single most important priority is our base brand. The last thing we ever want to do is take our eye off the ball."

From mojito mint to mango, will there ever be too many choices?

"You would think that with all these flavors and all these brands it's going to reach a saturation point," said Eric Schmidt, research director for Adams Beverage Group.

With so many brands and so many flavors competing for a limited amount of shelf space at bars, it can be a headache for bar operators.

Brian Cofman, general manager at martinibar in South Miami, is downright irritated.

"I think it's almost criminal the amount of flavored vodkas that have come out," Cofman said. "It makes it very difficult as an operator because all of the brands want to get placement for all of their products. You get handstrung sometimes by the 72 flavors that come in."

But Van Gogh and others press on. The latest flavor? Pomegranate. Thank Oprah, who is on a pomegranate kick.

Van de Velde said he tries to look at what people will be drinking two years from now to give him enough time to develop the flavor, get it approved, and get it on the market. Judging the tastes of the consumer is a tough task.

"Success in the vodka marketplace almost has nothing to do with the quality of the spirit," said Jack Robertiello, the Web editor for Adams Beverage Group. "It has to do with whimsical consumer behavior and response to marketing."
