BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Conley Terminal to add 4th container ship line

November 29, 2006

THE REGION
CMA CGM, the world's third-biggest container ship company, will begin service to the Conley Terminal in South Boston every other Thursday starting Dec. 7, the Massachusetts Port Authority said. The move will increase total container-shipping business to Boston's port by 4 to 5 percent annually and will also offer the first direct-shipping service between Boston and two European ports, Rotterdam and Liverpool. Boston's port now gets Mediterranean Shipping Co. vessels twice weekly from Europe and China Ocean Shipping Co. vessels weekly from China, along with a weekly Boston-New York Columbia Coastal Transport barge that moves containers to and from ships in New York Harbor. (Peter J. Howe)

Herald employees approve wage-freeze contract
After first rejecting a proposal three weeks ago, advertising, circulation, and other business employees at the Boston Herald approved a two-year contract extension that calls for a wage freeze, which was already accepted by editorial employees. The 61-to-46 vote will help Herald publisher Pat Purcell close budget gaps, but Purcell has warned that job reductions may still loom. The vote follows Purcell's disclosure that Ken Chandler is leaving the Herald's top editorial job and will be replaced by managing editor Kevin Convey. (Christopher Rowland)

Huawei stake in venture to be acquired for $882m
3Com Corp., a Marlborough maker of computer-networking equipment, agreed to buy Huawei Technologies Co.'s 49 percent stake in the profitable Chinese venture for $882 million to gain full control of the business. Huawei-3Com Co. is a three-year-old joint venture that makes routers and network switches and sells equipment made by 3Com in Asia. The purchase values Huawei-3Com at $1.8 billion, said 3Com, whose latest offer was accepted Monday. 3Com's bid trumped an effort by private equity firms Bain Capital LLC and Silver Lake Partners. (Bloomberg)

Eaton Vance raises $2.6b in IPO of closed-end fund
Eaton Vance Corp. said investors put $2.6 billion into the initial public offering of its Tax-Managed Diversified Equity Income Fund as it begins trading this morning, the largest amount ever raised in the launch of a closed-end fund. Eaton Vance, the Boston investment firm, said it issued 131 million shares at $20 each on the New York Stock Exchange. Eaton Vance said it will take expenses of about $17 million in the first quarter of 2007 in connection with the offering. (Ross Kerber)

Comcast begins offering wireless phone service
Comcast Corp. has become the first US cable operator to take on telecommunications providers in the cellphone market, having begun marketing the service to consumers in Boston. The largest US cable television operator launched the service in Boston and Portland, Ore., this month after a yearlong trial, but is only promoting its "mobile access" service with TV commercials and direct-mail drops in the Boston metropolitan area. (Reuters)

THE NATION
Bank of America passes Citigroup in market value
Bank of America Corp., whose shares have climbed 18 percent this year, passed Citigroup Inc. for the first time to become the biggest US bank by market value. Shares of Bank of America rose 35 cents to $54.27, lifting the company's market capitalization to $243.7 billion. Citigroup's market value fell to $243.5 billion as its shares shed 0.7 percent. (Bloomberg)

Revised study says more oil, gas now unavailable
About half the oil and more than a quarter of the natural gas beneath 99 million acres of federal land is off-limits to drilling, the Bush administration says in a report that the industry sought to highlight environmental and other hurdles to development. Just 3 percent of the oil and 13 percent of the gas under federal land is accessible under standard lease terms that require only basic protections for the environment and cultural resources, according to the survey. The revised inventory, released by the Bureau of Land Management, is starkly different from a study three years ago that covered 59 million acres in the Rocky Mountains and estimated that more than 80 percent of oil and gas was accessible, although in some cases subject to restrictions. Energy companies disliked the earlier inventory because, they argued, it suggested more oil and gas was accessible, even when restrictions made drilling difficult or impossible. They lobbied Congress to force a change in the way the inventory is figured. (AP)
