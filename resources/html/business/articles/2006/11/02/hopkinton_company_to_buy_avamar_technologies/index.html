BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Hopkinton company to buy Avamar Technologies

November 2, 2006

THE REGION
EMC Corp., the world's biggest maker of computers that store and manage business records, agreed to buy Avamar Technologies Inc. for $165 million to add data-protection software. Closely held Avamar's software helps companies make copies of their records on disk drives instead of increasingly costly magnetic tape, Hopkinton-based EMC said. (Bloomberg)

N.E. power grid operator OK's closing electric plant
New England electric grid managers have told the owners of a South Boston power plant they are free to close it down when they want. The move could lead to the L Street property, officially known as the New Boston 1 power plant, being redeveloped. Independent System Operator New England, the Holyoke organization that runs the six-state power grid, had been ordering the 350-megawatt plant to keep operating to maintain reliable electric supplies for Boston. Plant owner Exelon Corp. has made no comment about its plans for the site. (Peter J. Howe)

THE NATION
Bank of America offers combined account for rich
Bank of America Corp., which last month introduced free stock trading, is now offering an account it said will let wealthier individuals access a wide range of investments in a single account. Portfolio Strategies-Managed, will combine mutual fund "wrap" accounts with separately managed stocks, bonds, and other investments chosen by a variety of portfolio managers to help investors diversify their holdings while minimizing taxes. The account offers more than 70 portfolios, with investment minimums starting at $250,000, through the bank's brokerage unit. (Reuters)

Chief executive's cellphone records accessed online
Hewlett-Packard Co. obtained the cellphone records of chief executive Mark Hurd as part of its investigation into leaks to the media. Hurd said he wasn't aware investigators had reviewed call information for his company-issued cellphone until after the probe was complete, according to a letter Hewlett-Packard said it sent to a US House committee investigating the company. Hurd reiterated that he knew investigators were collecting phone records to trace the source of boardroom leaks and that he was told the information was obtained over the Internet. (Bloomberg)

Ex-general counsel set to plead guilty in probe
Comverse Technology Inc.'s former general counsel, William Sorin, will plead guilty in the stock-options backdating prosecution that has ensnared his onetime boss, fugitive Jacob Alexander, court papers say. The docket in the case said a "guilty plea" is set for Sorin today before US District Judge Nicholas Garaufis in Brooklyn. Sorin is the second Comverse executive to admit guilt in the case. He may become a witness against Alexander, now in Namibia fighting extradition. David Kreinberg, the company's former finance chief, pleaded guilty last month and implicated Alexander, the former chief executive of the biggest maker of voice mail software. (Bloomberg)

To attract more viewers, Yahoo starts food website
With online competition for eyeballs intensifying, Web portal Yahoo Inc. is launching a site devoted to food that will feature videos and other content from celebrity chefs such as Rachael Ray and Martha Stewart. Yahoo Food is set to launch today, the first major offering from the company's Lifestyles group. Financial arrangements with content providers were not disclosed. (Reuters)

Intel, Texas Instruments face oversupply of chips
Chip makers Intel Corp. and Texas Instruments Inc. head into the holiday shopping season with record inventories that may lead to profit-trimming price cuts. Intel, whose semiconductors power more than 70 percent of all personal computers, had $4.48 billion in stockpiles on Sept. 30, up 59 percent from a year earlier. Texas Instruments, maker of processors for more than half the world's mobile telephones, had $1.49 billion in chips, up 28 percent. (Bloomberg)
