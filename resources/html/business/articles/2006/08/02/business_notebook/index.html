Business notebook

August 2, 2006

Sorry: Boeing chairman and chief executive James McNerney at a Senate hearing apologized for a series of scandals that forced out two of his predecessors and led the company to pay a record $615 million settlement to the Justice Department.

No more tax: AT&T Inc., Verizon Communications Inc., and BellSouth Corp. stopped collecting a 3 percent US tax on some phone services after a government repeal of the levy. Customers no longer have to pay the tax for long-distance calls and services sold in package deals, said the three companies. The IRS plans to refund about $13 billion to customers who have paid the tax for the past three years.

$200 million more: General Motors Corp. said its second-quarter net loss was $200 million more than first reported because of a charge tied to the sale of its finance unit and warned that the transaction may be delayed. The loss rose to $3.4 billion from $3.2 billion.

Plugged in: Power grid operators in the Northeast said the supply of electricity was sufficient to meet the record demand. Despite setting fresh consumption records, grid operators said they did not expect to have to institute rolling blackouts, aimed at preventing uncontrolled outages, due to any lack of generating capacity or problems with transmission lines.

Sweets and fruits: McDonald's Corp. plans to sell cinnamon rolls in limited markets and introduce fruit snacks to expand sales during slow periods of the day. Pricing for the items was not disclosed.

Deal: IBM said it will sell five new servers run by Advanced Micro Devices Inc.'s Opteron processors. IBM's decision is a win for AMD as it seeks to take market share from Intel Corp.

Deadline: Apple Computer Inc. met the deadline to respond to Scandinavian regulatory claims that the company is violating laws by making its market-leading iPod the only compatible portable player for iTunes downloads. Apple's response was not immediately made public.

Lawsuit: Former Cendant Corp. lawyer Lisa Biase claims in a sex-discrimination lawsuit that the company ignored her complaints about unfair treatment of women and then fired her.

Wipeout: Mercury Interactive Corp. has erased another $5.2 million of its previously reported profit, continuing the business software maker's effort to repair the damage from a stock-options scandal before it's sold to Hewlett Packard Co. for $4.5 billion. Mercury has wiped out more than $530 million in earnings dating back to the early 1990s.

(Globe wire services)
