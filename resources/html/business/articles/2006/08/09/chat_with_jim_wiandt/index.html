Chat with Jim Wiandt

Transcript from August 7, 2006

August 9, 2006

Jim_Wiandt: Hi everyone. It's Jim Wiandt - and I'm here today to chat with you about exchange traded funds (ETFs) for the next hour. We've certainly got plenty we can talk about, with all of the recent developments in the ETF industry.

Bob: Hi Jim, what are ETFs exactly? How do they compare to mutual funds?
Jim_Wiandt: Sorry - I didn't see all of your questions here. I'll try to work my way through them now. Check out Lynn Asinof's story in the Sunday Globe - she did a great job summarizing what ETF's are and how they work. ETFs are just like mutual funds, except for one major difference...they can be traded like stocks. That's it essentially - ETFs are mutual funds that can be traded like stocks...notable differences because of structure include lower fees, generally better tax efficiency, and of course the ability to trade in real time.

mm34: Are ETFs the right strategy for a first-time investor?
Jim_Wiandt: That's a good question, and it depends on your circumstances. I would say if you plan to invest small amounts frequently, you may actually do better with a low cost mutual fund. However, if you plan to invest an amount of say $5,000 or more in a lump sum, you could indeed do better with ETFs, depending on the expense ratio, you brokerage fees etc. ETFs can also be a great option for investors who may not have no cost access to good funds, but can directly buy stocks through a brokerage account.

Mark_in_Maine: Jim - What do you think about the new ETNs from iShares?
Jim_Wiandt: I think they reflect the great diversity of new structures we've seen to meet investor needs. I also think that their return is going to look very much like that of, say the new Goldman Sachs Commodity Index iShare. The differences are that the ETF (or an ETF like the DB Commodity ETF) has slight potential tracking risk, but actually owns the basket of futures/ fixed income securities. The ETN has no tracking error (Barclays pay the return period)but has issuer risk (however slight). If Barclays were to have problems, that would be in the mix with the notes. But the net result of the commodities products is that they're VERY similar (right down to their 75 basis point fee level). The most important thing to look at is the index, and if that's where putting you where you need to be in terms of your investment area. I have every confidence in the note structure, particularly for a big reputable operator like Barclays.

money_no_pro: could ETFs be a smart investment for the begining investor with small to moderate sums to invest?
Jim_Wiandt: It depends how small you're talking about and what you're looking to do. I throw out the $5000 number as a very broad benchmark to give people something to think about. It depends on the fee differential, and of course what index you want to be investing in. For example, if you're putting in $1,000 and are paying a $15 brokerage fee in (and out), even just on the in, that's 1.5% you're paying not including how you could get hit on the bid/ask spread. So a .5 or .15% fee differential the ETF may have, plus a nominal tax efficiency benefit could take a long time to make up, if you can ever make it up (your fee setback is compounded over time). So at that level, you're likely better off w/ a regular low cost index mutual fund (all ETFs are index funds more or less, still. But if you're looking for the ability to get in and out in real time, or targeting an investment area you can only access with ETFs, even at smaller levels, the ETF may make sense. But again, I'd say if you've got $5,000 or more to put in in lump sums, start looking at ETFs and comparing expense ratios.

Mark_in_Maine: Jim - Do you think the new iShares ETNs will attract a lot of assets?
Jim_Wiandt: Well, Mark - I don't know. But I suspect their cause has not been helped by the fact that their most appealing area - the GSCI mentioned above, got approved for a regular iShare less than a month after the ETN came out. And of those two structures, for myself, I'd favor the regular iShare.

aw: Hello Jim, I have about 70K invested in 7 different mutual funds. My average expense ratio across the whole portfolio is 1.13. Only one fund is based on an index...would I do better switching all moneies to similar ETFs?
Jim_Wiandt: My first response is "Probably" But - it REALLY depends where you are in a number of areas. Is all that money in taxable accounts? If so, and given the recent market run, you might have to really suck it up and eat some capital gains to make that switch...and generally the longer you can hold off on capital gains distributions, the better. If you're non-taxable, or don't have big gains to deal with, I think that either moving to broad ETFs, or broad, cheap index funds, is likely to be the most healthy thing you could do for your portfolio long-term. I'd need to look at what you've got to get a better feel...but I'm a real indexer and by and large to me 1% plus is just money out of my account and into the manager's.
Jim_Wiandt: OK - sorry to pile all of my answers in there at once - I'd have enjoyed getting some back and forth going on. You've got 5 more minutes to post any more questions you may have...until 12:15 pm.
Jim_Wiandt: Thanks everyone for your questions. If you have any further questions for me, feel free to send them to my offline at jwiandt@indexuniverse.com and I'll be happy to answer all of them personally. jw
