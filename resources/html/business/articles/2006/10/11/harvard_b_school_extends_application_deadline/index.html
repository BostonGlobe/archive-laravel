BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Harvard B-School extends application deadline

October 11, 2006

THE REGION
Harvard Business School yesterday extended an application deadline for 24 hours due to an "electrical event" that took down the school's website for much of the day. Deirdre Leopold , the school's director of admissions, said the new deadline for first-round applicants for the class starting next September was reset for 5 p.m. Thursday. While applicants apply through a third-party site that was unaffected, Leopold said admissions officials determined that a one-day delay was justified. ``Our website was inaccessible, and that's not a good thing for people who were trying to consult it at the last minute," she said. The site was back up yesterday afternoon. (Robert Weisman)

Office supplier says profit next year may climb 20%
Staples Inc., the world's largest office supplies retailer, said per-share profit next year may increase up to 20 percent, exceeding analysts' estimates, as it adds corporate customers and expands copying services. Growth of 20 percent for the year through January 2008 would equal $1.51 a share, more than the $1.47 average estimate of analysts surveyed by Thomson Financial. Framingham-based Staples said it expects this year's earnings to be in line with the average analyst estimate, which is $1.26 a share, according to Thomson. (Bloomberg)

Smith to resign this year as Mass. Eye and Ear chief
F. Curtis Smith is stepping down from his job as president of the Massachusetts Eye and Ear Infirmary, a job he has held for 14 years, the hospital said. Smith came to the specialty hospital in 1990 as chief operating officer and was promoted two years later. His resignation will be effective at the end of the year, and the hospital is launching a search for a replacement. Smith was credited with turning around the hospital's finances after two years of losses in the late 1980s. Its endowment grew from $30 million to $83 million under his watch. (Christopher Rowland)

Marlborough firm aims to fully own China venture
3Com Corp., a maker of computer networking equipment, wants to become the full owner of Huawei-3Com Co. to help the Chinese company expand outside its home market, chairman Eric Benhamou said. The company plans to link its joint venture with Huawei Technologies Co. to 3Com's global sales and distribution network, Benhamou said in an interview in Barcelona. 3Com aims to reach a pact to buy the remaining stake owned by Huawei by the end of November, Benhamou said. 3Com formed the venture with Huawei in November 2003 to enter China. (Bloomberg)

Community hospitals join trial angioplasty program
The cardiology program at Caritas Norwood Hospital has been selected by the state Department of Public Health to participate in a clinical trial to determine whether community hospitals should be allowed to routinely provide elective angioplasties to cardiac patients. Angioplasty is a procedure using a balloon to widen clogged coronary arteries. In Massachusetts, elective angioplasties have been restricted to teaching hospitals. Other hospitals participating include Brockton Hospital , Lowell General Hospital and Saints Memorial Medical Center in Lowell , Melrose-Wakefield Hospital in Melrose, Metro West Medical Center in Framingham, and South Shore Hospital in Weymouth . (Christopher Rowland)

THE NATION
Google to make package of free software available
Google Inc. is making its word processing and spreadsheet programs available for free to all comers on its website, marking the Internet search leader's latest effort to provide an alternative to Microsoft Corp.'s dominant software applications. The Mountain View, Calif-based company's software package, expected to be available today, will be called Google Docs & Spreadsheets. (AP)

Microsoft offers patches to plug holes in software
Microsoft Corp. released six patches to fix software flaws that carry its highest threat rating, including three for defects that attackers have already tried to exploit. The company said all six could allow an attacker to obtain some access to other people's computers. The Redmond, Wash., software maker also released four other patches to fix vulnerabilities that the company deemed less severe. Customers can download all the patches for free on Microsoft's security website. (AP)
