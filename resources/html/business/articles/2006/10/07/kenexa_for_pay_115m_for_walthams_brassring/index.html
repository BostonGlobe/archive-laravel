BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Kenexa for pay $115m for Waltham's BrassRing

October 7, 2006

THE REGION

Kenexa Corp., a human resources software and service provider, said it will pay $115 million to acquire closely held BrassRing Inc. of Waltham to reach more clients around the world. Kenexa also expects to meet the high end of its third-quarter earnings forecast of 21 to 22 cents a share and exceed its revenue expectations of $26 million, the Wayne, Pa.-based company said. BrassRing, which has about $10 million in net cash, lists Time Warner Inc., Ikea, and ConAgra Foods Inc. among its 180 clients. The deal will allow Kenexa to expand into global markets through BrassRing's offices in seven countries. The acquisition of BrassRing will be financed with cash and loans, Kenexa said. The purchase is expected to close in the fourth quarter. (Bloomberg)

Genzyme files extension in hostile bid for AnorMed
Genzyme Corp. yesterday filed a two-week extension to its hostile bid for Canadian drug company AnorMed Inc. Genzyme's $380 million offer for the company, disclosed in late August, was set to expire tomorrow but will now expire Oct. 23. Although Genzyme's price has already been trumped by a $515 million offer from Cambridge-based Millennium Pharmaceuticals Inc., Genzyme, also based in Cambridge, is considering whether to make a higher bid. Millennium's offer for AnorMed is open until Nov. 10. (Stephen Heuser)

FleetBoston to pay Enron $20m to settle claims
Enron Corp. said FleetBoston Financial Corp., Fleet National Bank, and other affiliates agreed to pay $20 million to settle their portion of a lawsuit filed against several banks accused of failing to prevent the energy company's collapse. FleetBoston, bought by Charlotte, N.C.-based Bank of America Corp. in 2004, will pay Enron $10.4 million to settle the so-called ``MegaClaims" lawsuit and $9.35 million to settle an avoidance action to recover preferential transfers in connection with Enron's commercial paper litigation. FleetBoston did not admit liability or wrongdoing as part of the settlement. The settlement must be approved by a US bankruptcy court in New York. (AP)

Firms: Tysabri eased need for steroids in MS patients
Biogen Idec Inc. and Elan Corp.'s Tysabri lowered the need for steroids in multiple sclerosis patients and cut the number of hospital visits, the companies said. Data found a 69 percent relative reduction in the annual rate of relapses requiring steroid use, and a 65 percent relative reduction in the rate of hospitalizations, the companies said in a statement. The comparisons, released at a medical conference in Chicago, were made with patients using a placebo. (Bloomberg)

Raytheon in $112.9m pact for air-to-air missiles
Raytheon Co. signed a $112.9 million contract to produce and supply medium-range, air-to-air missiles to the Air Force, Navy, and Army. The defense contractor said the latest agreement is a supplement to a contract signed in February and provides more funds for AIM-120C7 missiles. The two contracts have a combined value of $168 million. (Dow Jones)

Sovereign shares surge on report CEO may leave
Sovereign Bancorp Inc. shares surged 12 percent, the biggest advance in the Standard & Poor's 500 index, after the Wall Street Journal said the bank may dismiss chief executive Jay Sidhu next week. The stock climbed $2.50 to $24.10 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. Seven of the bank's 12 directors plan to meet Oct. 10 to debate Sidhu's fate and whether to revamp the board's leadership because of Sovereign's share performance, the Journal said. In July, Sovereign reported its first quarterly loss in more than five years after writing off unprofitable investments. Sovereign spokesman Ed Shultz declined to comment. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

US jobless rate dips to 4.6% in September
The unemployment rate last month dipped to 4.6 percent from 4.7 percent in August even though the number of new jobs was disappointing, a sign the economy is slowing but in no danger of stalling as Election Day nears. Employers added 51,000 jobs to the nation's payrolls, the fewest new hires in 11 months -- a tally well short of the 120,000 positions private analysts predicted. Job gains for both July and August turned out to be stronger than previously estimated, however, and that blunted some of the sting. (AP)
