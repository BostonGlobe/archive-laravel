Plotting a course of continuity

Hired in period of upheaval, Harvard business dean looks to build existing programs

By Robert Weisman, Globe Staff  |  October 12, 2006

Call him Mr. Continuity.

Harvard University is still recovering from its president's resignation last winter. Harvard Law School is scrapping an approach to first-year studies dating back over a century. And elite business school rivals like Stanford and Yale are overhauling their curriculums.

By contrast, Jay O. Light , who was named the ninth dean of Harvard Business School in April, is the model of stability. Light sees his priorities as building up the school's programs in globalization and health care -- programs launched by previous deans with whom he worked closely -- while strengthening ties with the rest of Harvard.

``It's a continuation of what we know we really ought to do given the changes going on in the world," Light, 65, said in an interview, noting that business is becoming ever more international and health care has emerged as a fast-growing sector. ``And to some extent, it's driven by what's happening around us in the institution as a whole."

To some, the appointment of Light is viewed as a sign that Harvard is reluctant to make dramatic changes at its venerable business school while the university is searching for a new president and coping with the aftermath of Lawrence H. Summers's departure in June following clashes with the faculty. Before Summers said in February that he'd be stepping down, Light, who is uncomfortable in the spotlight, hadn't been viewed as a candidate. There was speculation Summers would name an Indian-born professor as business school dean, a move that would have underscored the school's increasingly global profile.

``In this world of chaos, they decided to hand over the reins to a person who is thoroughly Harvard Business School," said Arlington consultant Laurence O. McKinney , a 1969 graduate of the business school and class columnist for its alumni bulletin. ``Jay Light is able; he's affable; he's collegial; and he's the last of the breed, you might say, of people who were brought up in the system. Harvard Business School is already the big frog in the puddle, and they are staying the course."

Light, the consummate Harvard insider and a long time associate of Harvard's interim president Derek Bok , said he isn't content to rest on the business school's laurels. He sees his mission as forging ahead with the school's current programs and pushing their boundaries, partially with the aid of nearly $600 million the business school raised in a multiyear capital campaign concluded this year.

A task force he formed to consider expanding the school's global business initiative is set to present its recommendations later this fall. But while some faculty members favor opening international campuses, as other business schools have done, Light leans toward more joint programs with overseas partners and short study trips and symposiums at the half dozen research centers Harvard has set up over the past decade to write case studies in business hubs like Hong Kong, Paris, and Mumbai. Recalling that Harvard opened and closed an executive education branch in Switzerland in the 1970s, Light said one lesson learned was ``when the faculty left for extended periods to go overseas, they became disengaged from the process here."

For the health care initiative, Light's goal is to hire more faculty members with industry experience, broaden an MD-MBA program launched with Harvard Medical School last year, and collaborate with other arms of the university, like the life sciences campus that is planned for a site next door to the business school in Boston's Allston neighborhood. The school also is developing a sciences-based business program focused on managing innovation not only in medicine but also in fields like microprocessors and energy.

``We want to keep it deeply rooted in what's practiced all over the world," Light said. ``What are the things that practitioners are worried about this year? We don't want to teach yesterday's theories."

Light, a one-time analyst at California's Jet Propulsion Laboratory who joined the Harvard faculty 36 years ago, conceded he wasn't seeking the permanent job when he accepted the position of acting dean in August 2005. His predecessor, Kim B. Clark , had completed a 10-year tenure and accepted a job at Brigham Young University in Rexburg, Idaho. ``I enjoyed it more than I thought I would," Light said of his time as acting dean. ``And I think the institution came to see the needs they had as ones I could do a pretty good job at."

Critics question whether Light's embrace of continuity is the right strategy at a time of sweeping change in both business and higher education. Earlier this year, the Stanford Graduate School of Business unveiled a new MBA curriculum designed around individual students. At the Yale School of Management, new dean Joel M. Podolny , a former Harvard business professor, has redesigned the curriculum with a multi disciplinary approach focused on organizational roles and innovation. Even Harvard Law School is refocusing its first-year curriculum on complex problem-solving and international law.

``Harvard Business School is in the center of things, but innovation doesn't happen at the center," said management consultant Thornton A. May , who teaches executive education at Ohio State University and the University of California in Los Angeles. ``This is a time of experimentation in the business world and any business school strategy, to be successful, has to be relevant to that environment."

Light, however, insisted Harvard is responding to sweeping changes in business with the initiatives he's expanding in globalization and health care, along with entrepreneurship. And he's unapologetic about being at the center of things. As the university expands its footprint on the Boston side of the Charles River, he noted, the business school increasingly will be the center of the Harvard campus. That will help it collaborate with the university's medical and life sciences programs.

As to his own tenure, he is reluctant to predict how long he might stay at the helm of Harvard Business School. ``Who knows?" he said. ``I serve at the pleasure of whoever the [Harvard] president is. Right now, that would be Derek this year. And who knows next year. And when they tell me it's time to go, it'll be time to go."
