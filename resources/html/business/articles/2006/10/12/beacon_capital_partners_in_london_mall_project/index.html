BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Beacon Capital Partners in London mall project

October 12, 2006

THE REGION
Land Securities Group PLC and Beacon Capital Partners LLC plan to build the first purpose-built shopping mall in London's main financial district, which is budgeted to cost about $928 million. Land Securities, Europe's biggest property company, chose a unit of Boston's Beacon Capital as its venture partner after gaining planning permission for the project in January. Almost half of the building at One New Change, near St. Paul's Cathedral, will be used as retail space while the rest will be offices, the United Kingdom-based company said yesterday in a statement. (Bloomberg)

Morgan Stanley lures trio from Merrill Lynch
Morgan Stanley has lured away three brokers from Merrill Lynch & Co. in Boston. Brothers Jonathan and Matthew Greer, and Bruce Ledoux on Friday joined Morgan Stanley's Wellesley branch, which has around 35 financial advisers. The trio together generated more than $2.3 million in commissions and fees over the past year and held around $207 million in assets, said a Morgan Stanley spokeswoman. A Merrill spokeswoman confirmed their exit but declined to comment on their production and asset figures. (Dow Jones)

Maine & Maritimes cuts workforce by 4 percent
Maine & Maritimes Corp. said it eliminated 4 percent of its workforce under a staff restructuring plan. The pretax restructuring cost is estimated at $326,000, the company said in a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. All charges will be taken in the third quarter of 2006, the Presque Isle, Maine, company said. The SEC filing didn't say how many workers were let go. The company had 219 employees in 2005, according to its annual report filed with the SEC last month. Maine & Maritimes is the parent Maine Public Service Co., an electric and transmission utility that serves 36,000 customers in Northern Maine. (Dow Jones)

Deal for drug's rights to lift Oscient in 3d quarter
Waltham's Oscient Pharmaceuticals Corp. expects to report that third-quarter revenue climbed to slightly more than $11 million from $5.94 million a year ago, helped by the August acquisition of the US rights to cardiovascular drug Antara. Oscient plans to report quarterly financial results on Nov. 9. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION

Biotech firm can market treatment for lung cancer
Genentech won US approval to market its Avastin colon cancer drug as a treatment for lung cancer, an added use that may revive sales growth for the biotechnology company's second-biggest product. The Food and Drug Administration cleared Avastin to treat the most common form of lung cancer in combination with chemotherapy, the South San Francisco, Calif., company said in a statement. Genentech, the world's second-largest biotechnology company, first received initial approval of Avastin two years ago. The clearance may help spur further growth for Avastin after third-quarter US sales fell short of analysts' estimates. (Bloomberg)

Ex-chief executive Skilling seeks reversal of verdict
Former Enron chief executive Jeffrey Skilling has asked a federal judge to overturn his guilty verdict based on an August decision that reversed other Enron-related convictions against Merrill Lynch employees. Skilling, 52, was found guilty in May of fraud, insider trading, conspiracy, and lying to auditors for actions leading to his abrupt resignation in August 2001. Skilling is asking US District Judge Sim Lake to dismiss those counts or grant a new trial in light of the federal Appeals Court ruling. Skilling faces sentencing on Oct. 23. (AP)

Banned fund trader agrees to settlement with SEC
Steve Markovitz, who was barred from the securities industry in 2003 for after-hours trades in mutual funds, agreed to pay $170,000 in penalties for starting an investment fund despite the ban. Markovitz, 43, will also pay a $400,000 fine tied to the original late trading case, the SEC said in settlement papers filed in US District Court in Manhattan. The New York resident neither admitted nor denied the accusations under the accords, the regulator said. Markovitz pleaded guilty to a criminal charge in October 2003 for profiting from improper after-hours mutual fund trades while working at hedge fund group Millenium Partners. He agreed to leave the industry in a partial settlement with the SEC at the time. (Bloomberg)
