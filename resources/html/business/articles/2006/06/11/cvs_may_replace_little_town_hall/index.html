CVS may replace `Little Town Hall'

By Kathy McCabe  |  June 11, 2006

N & J Handcut Doughnuts on Route 62 in Middleton is known as ``Little Town Hall," where locals hash out town politics and gossip, town workers stop by for morning coffee, and residents gather at the counter for a doughnut or a $1.99 egg and cheese on an English muffin.

But soon the little red shop could be razed to make way for a new CVS drug store. Lisa Curran, third-generation owner, has decided to sell the popular shop started 55 years ago by her grandfather, Nick Pellicelli, and later run by her father, Jack Pellicelli. (They are the N & J in the shop's name.)

For years, Curran has been approached to sell by developers looking to snap up land along Route 62 in an area familiarly called either Howe's Station or Four Corners .

``They usually choked when I told them my price," said Curran, 42, who declined to disclose the sale price. ``But this time, they didn't choke."

On June 22, the town's Zoning Board of Appeals will consider a plan submitted by Maple Street CVS Nominee Trust to tear down the shop, plus two adjacent single-family houses that sit on just over an acre of land.

The meeting will start at 7:30 p.m. at Fuller Meadow Elementary School.

A Colonial-style CVS store, made of wood and brick, would replace them. The almost 13,000-square-foot store would have a drive-up window for prescription pick up and drop-off, entrances from both Maple and East streets, plus antique -style lighting and new landscaping along Maple Street, according to the application filed at Town Hall.

The plan cleared a first hurdle last week, when the Board of Selectmen voted, 3-to-1, to recommend the project to the Zoning and Planning boards.

Curran said she hopes the project gets swift approval, due to the added tax revenue.

``I think the town will get a lot more money out of this property than they do now," she said.

Curran said she is tired of running the shop, where she has worked since she was 10 years old. N & J opens at 4:45 a.m. each day and closes at 1:45 p.m., six days a week.

In addition to coffee and doughnuts, the shop sells breakfast and lunch, often advertising its specials on a sandwich board out on Route 62.

Although business is good, competition has slowly crept into N & J's backyard. A Dunkin' Donuts opened a few years back, and Farmer Brown's, a gourmet market that has a soup and salad bar, is next door.

A steady decline in doughnut sales prompted Curran to stop making them in 1992 and buy them from an out-of-town baker.

``When my grandfather started here, it was the only place in town that sold coffee and doughnuts," said Curran, who took over the business 20 years ago after the death of her father.

``Now you can get coffee everywhere, even at the gas station across the street."

Curran plans to keep the shop open while town boards review the CVS plan. But she will close in August, as she always has, for vacation.

And if the CVS doesn't get approved? Well, Curran said she has an idea for Little Town Hall that could shake up things at the real Town Hall.

``I'm thinking about still closing, and opening an adult video and bookstore here," said Curran, who insists the idea is not a ploy to pressure town boards to approve the CVS plan.

``Sex sells. Doughnuts don't," she said.

Nazarenes buy Catholic church

The former St. Alphonsus Catholic Church on Route 62 on the Beverly-Danvers line has been sold for $1.3 million to Beverly Church of the Nazarene, according to a deed recently filed at the Registry of Deeds in Salem.

The Archdiocese of Boston, which closed the church in 2004 as part of its downsizing, also sold the former rectory on Route 62 for $325,000 to Niall and Patricia McManus , property records show.

The single-family house is down the street from the church, on the Beverly side of Route 62.

Kim Richardson, pastor of Beverly Church of the Nazarene , could not be reached for comment.

The Beverly Church of the Nazarene on Cabot Street was founded in 1908. The former St. Alphonsus will be the Nazarene church's third location. It also leases space in the Brown School in Peabody, where it runs a satellite location.

Store collecting for food pantry

Flukes & Finds & Friends, an antique shop in Newburyport, will celebrate its fifth year in business at 70 State St. by donating 5 percent of this weekend's sales to Community Service of Newburyport, a nonprofit that runs a food pantry.

The antique shop, operated by seven dealers who share space, also is collecting canned goods and other nonperishables through the end of the month, when they will be donated to Community Service.
