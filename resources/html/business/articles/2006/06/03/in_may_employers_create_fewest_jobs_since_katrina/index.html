In May, employers create fewest jobs since Katrina

Economists speculate that the Fed may halt raising interest rates

By Nell Henderson, The Washington Post  |  June 3, 2006

WASHINGTON -- The economy generated fewer jobs in May than at any time since Hurricane Katrina last fall, the government reported yesterday, leading economists to speculate that the Federal Reserve might finally quit raising interest rates.

The nation's jobless rate slipped to 4.6 percent in May from 4.7 percent in April, as more workers found jobs than started seeking them.

But employers added just 75,000 jobs to their payrolls last month, the weakest gain since October, when hiring nearly halted following a series of destructive hurricanes.

The report followed others this week that showed the economy is losing some momentum because of rising interest rates and high energy prices. The housing market softened, auto sales dropped, and manufacturing activity dipped last month.

``With evidence that the economy is slowing, the employment data suggest firms have grown more cautious of taking on additional workers," wrote economists at PNC Financial Services Group Inc., in an analysis for clients.

Stocks fell after the report fanned investor worries about slower economic growth.

The Dow Jones industrial average declined 12.41 points to 11,247.87. The Standard & Poor's 500 index edged up 2.51 points to 1,288.22. The Nasdaq Composite index inched down 0.45 of a point to close at 2,219.41.

Several analysts said the weak job and wage figures should ease inflation concerns, which might prompt the Federal Reserve to leave short-term interest rates unchanged at its next meeting June 28-29, after two years of steady increases. But others said the Fed's decision will hinge more on inflation figures to be released this month.

The Labor report showed no sign that the low unemployment rate is pushing wages up enough to fuel inflation.

Average hourly wages for most workers rose just one cent, to $16.62, following a bigger 10-cent gain in April. Moreover, because they worked slightly fewer hours last month, average weekly wages fell $1.32 to $561.76.

May marked the third consecutive month of slowing job growth, according to Labor Department figures. The employers that trimmed jobs last month included automakers, retailers, and temporary help agencies.

Declining employment in gambling and other forms of recreation may reflect high gasoline prices, said Ray Stone, of Stone & McCarthy Research Associates.

The job losses were outnumbered by small gains in other industries, including education, healthcare, professional, and business services, Labor reported.

Construction employment was flat in May, the department said, reinforcing other reports that construction spending, mortgage applications, and home sales have fallen in recent months.

The construction industry had been a big source of job growth during the housing boom, adding 300,000 jobs in each of the last two years.

Before the job report was released, futures markets reflected traders' expectations that the Fed would probably raise its benchmark short-term interest rate at the upcoming meeting to restrain inflation. After the release, traders' estimated a 50-50 chance of either another increase or no change in the rate.

Even if the Fed takes a breather, that would not necessarily mean it was finished raising interest rates, chairman Ben Bernanke recently told Congress. A pause would give Fed officials a chance to gather more information about how much the economy is slowing, and whether inflation is rising temporarily or more persistently.

Many economists forecast the economy will grow at around a 2.5 percent annual rate this spring, less than half as fast as the rapid 5.3 percent pace in the first three months of the year.

Fed officials want and expect the economy to slow to dampen inflation pressures. Their challenge is to raise interest rates enough to squelch inflation pressures, and to quell inflation fears in financial markets, without triggering a more serious economic slump.

The difficulty is that interest rate increases take effect over many months, and inflation typically peaks some time after the economy starts slowing -- making it hard to know when to stop.

As Bernanke told Congress this year, the Fed can make two types of errors: raising interest rates too high or stopping too soon. ``The slowdown is in place," wrote John Silvia, chief economist at Wachovia Corp. Economics Group. ``How much and how rapid remains an issue."
