Sun might have burned a hole in Essex County tourism

By Kathy McCabe  |  June 4, 2006

Sunny skies over Memorial Day weekend, the traditional start of summer, ironically may have put a damper on tourism in Essex County.

The National Park Service Visitor Center in Salem, a key starting point for touring the region, had 1,153 fewer visitors from last Friday to Monday compared with Memorial Day weekend in 2005. The center logged 4,072 visitors this year, compared with 5,225 last year.

``I think because of all the rain we had before the holiday, many people opted to visit more beaches, have picnics and just do more outdoor recreation in general," said Peter LaChapelle , the national park's chief of visitor services. ``I think there was real pent-up demand."

But the good weather didn't seem to deter foreign tourists, a lucrative segment that has slowly returned since the Sept. 11, 2001, terrorist attacks. European and Australian visitors have been spotted around more frequently in Salem, often inquiring about visits to Gloucester, Newburyport, and other tourist destinations.

``We're trying to stimulate tourism up and down Essex County," LaChapelle said.

``Foreign visitors play a large part in that, because they generally stay here longer" than domestic travelers.

Unemployed get aid due to floods
Unemployment assistance is available for individuals in Essex, Middlesex, and Suffolk counties left high and dry without a job from last month's flooding.

Residents and business owners have until June 29 to file for the benefits from the state Department of Workforce Development. They are available because the three counties were declared disaster areas by the federal government.

Jane C. Edmonds , the state department's director, said the assistance will ``provide a much-needed helping hand" to those hurt by the floods following the storm, from May 13 to 16, that dumped over 10 inches of rain on parts of the region.

People who lost work because of floods are eligible for up to 26 weeks of benefits. They include: people who can't reach their job, because travel is still restricted in the area; those who were to start a job, but could not because of flooding; people who now are the breadwinner, because the head of a household died in the flood; and people who can't work due to injury from the floods.

The benefits are not available to people who would not ordinarily qualify for unemployment benefits, such as people who are self-employed.

For more information, call 617-626-6800.

Honoree gives prize to YMCA
When David McKechnie of Gloucester won a $5,000 charitable prize for being named ``Investment Professional of the Year" by ValMark Securities, the YMCA of the North Shore ended up in the winner's circle.

McKechnie, managing partner of Beauport Financial Services , matched the prize in donating $10,000 to jump-start the fund-raising efforts for the Y's annual gala. McKechnie and Jon Gray , a gallery owner from Beverly Farms, are cochairmen of the gala, to be held in November. Proceeds will benefit low-income youths and families.

``I've always felt very close to the Y," said McKechnie, 44, a father of two and long an active member of the Cape Ann YMCA. ``It's really a place that builds body and spirit. I think it really does change the lives of families."

McKechnie hopes his donation encourages other local businesses to support the gala, which will have a James Bond theme. The Nov. 18 event at the Radisson Ferncroft is a major fund-raiser for the Y's annual charitable campaign. The Y serves 11 area communities, including Beverly, Gloucester, and Haverhill.

``I don't think people really understand how much the Y gives to families," said McKechnie, who also sits on the Y board of directors. ``The need to provide financial help for them to afford the Y is growing, not shrinking."

McKechnie knows a thing or two about numbers. He started Beauport Financial Services 20 years ago. The independent firm now manages $150 million in assets, much of it placed through ValMark, which is based in Ohio.

Grocery donation goes to the birds
If you buy a chunk of cheese, whole wheat pasta, or any other healthy stuff at Whole Foods in Swampscott on Wednesday, you will also be helping out the Massachusetts Audubon Society. The supermarket is donating 5 percent of all purchases that day to MassAudubon, which has sanctuaries in Ipswich, Wenham, Marblehead, Nahant, and Newburyport.
