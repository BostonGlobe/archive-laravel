Local advertising team tackles Super Bowl spot

Arnold Worldwide works seven months on high-stakes investment for Mobile ESPN

By Jenn Abelson, Globe Staff  |  February 5, 2006

Roger Baldacci has a coveted seat at tonight's Super Bowl XL in Detroit, but come the second quarter, he's making a mad dash to the nearest television.

That's when Baldacci, creative director at advertising firm Arnold Worldwide in Boston, will try to watch his agency's contribution to a Super Bowl rite: the commercials.

''We're playing on the biggest field," Baldacci said of the spot Arnold made for client Mobile ESPN, a new cellular phone service. ''We've got all eyes on us. Not just our peers, but the millions of people watching the game."

Everyone can see what it takes for football teams to make it to the Super Bowl, but few know better than Arnold and other select advertising firms what it takes from start to finish to pull off a Super Bowl commercial. It's the agency's most ambitious effort ever, spanning seven months from the first brainstorming session to the final edit last Sunday night. An ESPN commercial typically takes about three months, but its Super Bowl ad involved coordinating two dozen athletes filmed live over four days in Los Angeles. Just to get the players meant a month of combing through schedules for more than 100 athletes and getting approvals from various leagues, coaches, and agents.

For Mobile ESPN and every Super Bowl advertiser, it's a high-stakes investment -- the average cost for a 30-second spot is about $2.5 million this year. It's the most-watched television event, with an estimated 90 million viewers -- and at least one-third of them tune in just for the commercials, according to Bernice Kanner, author of the book ''The Super Bowl of Advertising: How the Commercials Won the Game."

Successful Super Bowl spots can define careers of ad executives and agencies, but flops can be devastating: Just for Feet, a shoe company, was ostracized for airing a spot that was seen as racist when it featured a Kenyan runner drugged by hunters. The company sued its ad firm, and trade publication Adweek later rated the 1999 commercial the second worst in the past decade.

''There's a big fear factor -- can the commercial live up to the hype?" said Tara Campbell, a vice president at New York media-planning firm MPG.

The jockeying for Super Bowl slots begins months, if not years, in advance. There will be about 50 ads during this year's game, from perennials like Anheuser-Busch to newcomers such as Dove skin care. Boston shaving giant Gillette will have two spots debuting its new Fusion razor.

For ESPN, the road to the Super Bowl began last summer when executive John Skipper decided that Mobile ESPN, which is part of ESPN, deserved a splashy launch. And what better venue than the Super Bowl to unveil a new cellular phone that allows people to access scores, game highlights, and sports news.

ESPN executives wanted to go big with a 60-second commercial. They wanted to go high definition -- which looks better but takes longer to edit and costs more money. And they wanted to go early in the game -- in case a lopsided score turned off viewers. But by the summer, all first-quarter ads were locked up. So they settled for the second quarter, and Arnold Worldwide went to work.

First the agency held brainstorming sessions, dividing 10 people into five teams and generating hundreds of ideas involving everything from animals to cartoons. Then, top Arnold executives whittled the list to about seven ideas and pitched them to Mobile ESPN.

The winning concept, titled ''Sports Heaven," features an average sports fan who flips open a Mobile ESPN phone and is instantly immersed in a heaven in which the streets are filled with race cars, Olympic gymnasts flipping on the sidewalks, and the world's strongest man pulling a bus. Arnold's storyboard featured numerous athletes to be filmed live -- a spot so chock full of detail that people would TiVo it to watch the commercial over and over.

At first ESPN's Skipper thought the ad would be impossible to film and was ready to nix it. But Arnold insisted that if given the green light and the budget, the agency could pull it off. ''We pledged high stakes," Skipper said. ''We went for a very difficult spot."

Arnold needed a broad range of athletes, from Bassmaster champion Skeet Reese to David Wells of the Red Sox, but players who had endorsement contracts with other phone carriers were out. ESPN knew it couldn't ask superstars such as golfer Tiger Woods to share the stage with other athletes. New York Knicks point guard Stephon Marbury could do the commercial only if his team won the game the night before the shoot -- otherwise he had a mandatory practice.

Then, there were the props: a Zamboni, Monster Truck, motorcycles, a marching band, among others. Arnold pored through hundreds of songs to find music that gave the feeling of heaven.

In early November, Arnold's creative team flew to Los Angeles to pick the ''average sports fan" actor and check out the location for the shoot. The site, Grand Hope Park, was mostly what Arnold wanted, but with flaws. The pond for the fishing scene was a nonworking fountain. A digital artist would have to fill in water later.

Somehow, the filming went off without a hitch. Marbury won his basketball game, the weather cooperated, and the athletes got along. Sure, there were minor issues: professional bowler Chris Barnes's ball grazed the nose of actor P.J. Palmer. Pro golfer Mike Weir's ball almost hit the camera, so he swung at air instead.

In late November, four hours of filming was chopped into a rough 60-second spot. The footage, shot on 35-millimeter film, was then transferred to high-definition broadcast videotape. More than a dozen people at Brickyard VFX, a production company with offices in Boston and Santa Monica, Calif., edited the piece for two months. The trickiest fix: digitally altering the uniform of center fielder Juan Pierre, who was traded in December to the Chicago Cubs from the Florida Marlins.

During the last stretch two weeks ago, Brickyard digital artist Geoff McAuliffe worked nearly nonstop (and unshowered) on the final scene, adding pools and tennis courts to rooftops and a blimp to the sky. Around 8 p.m. Sunday, the editing was done. Arnold sent the ad on Tuesday to ABC, which is broadcasting the Super Bowl. The network must approve the commercial -- which it did -- and now all that is left is to wait for the second quarter.
