BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Burlington company files patent-infringement suit

February 18, 2006

Nuance Communications, a Burlington maker of speech recognition software, accused Tellme Networks of infringing two US patents in a lawsuit filed in US District Court in Wilmington, Del. Nuance alleges that Tellme is wrongly using Nuance's inventions in applications for call centers and directory assistance. A spokeswoman for Mountain View, Calif.-based Tellme said the company doesn't comment on pending litigation. (Bloomberg)

Waters to pay $6.1m to settle with Agilent

Waters Corp., a Milford maker of instruments used by chemical researchers, said it reached a settlement with Agilent Technologies Deutschland GmbH to resolve their patent suit. Waters agreed to make a one-time payment of $6.1 million. In turn, Agilent will withdraw claims of past patent infringment. Waters is revising its fourth-quarter and full-year 2005 results, which it previously reported on Jan. 24, to account for the settlement. The revision includes a $3.1 million provision for damages and estimated remaining costs. (AP)

THE NATION
Inflation moderates after drop in gasoline prices

Prices at the wholesale level rose in January at just half the pace of December as a big drop in gasoline prices helped offset higher costs for new cars and other items. But the biggest one-month rise in core inflation in a year raised concerns that last year's surge in energy costs was beginning to spill over to other areas. The Labor Department reported its producer price index, which measures inflation pressures before they reach the consumer, rose 0.3 percent in January, down from a 0.6-percent jump in December with the change reflecting flat energy prices last month after a big surge in December. However, core inflation, excluding volatile energy and food costs, rose 0.4 percent in January, the biggest rise since a 0.6-percent jump in January 2005. (AP)

Time Warner, Icahn bury hatchet about company

Time Warner and billionaire Carl Icahn said they reached an accord, signaling an end to their battle over the future of the world's largest media company. Time Warner said its board had agreed to repurchase as much as $20 billion in stock and would consider electing two new directors, seeking Icahn's advice in the process. Time Warner also said it would implement $1 billion in cost savings and continue to review a report commissioned by Icahn and prepared by Lazard Ltd, which investigates ways to overhaul the company. In return, Icahn Partners will not contest the company's slate of directors, Time Warner said. (Reuters)

RadioShack earnings dive, 400-700 stores will close

Electronics retailer RadioShack said fourth-quarter earnings dropped 62 percent and disclosed plans to close 400 to 700 stores and two distribution centers. Shares fell $1.67, or 8.1 percent, to $19.08. The company said it could not project the number of job cuts until it identifies all the affected stores. RadioShack said the stores it plans to close are company-operated. There are nearly 7,000 RadioShack stores operated by the company or dealers, and more than 700 wireless kiosks. Earnings were $49.5 million, or 36 cents per share, in the three months ended Dec. 31, down from $130.9 million, or 81 cents per share, a year earlier. Revenue grew 5 percent to $1.67 billion from $1.59 billion. (AP)

Chicago planning citywide wireless Internet service

Chicago is launching an effort to offer wireless broadband, city officials said. Chicago has hundreds of Wi-Fi hotspots in places like coffee shops, bookstores, and libraries, where anyone can connect to the Web. Hoping to extend that wireless blanket to all 228 square miles, the city plans to ask technology companies this spring to submit proposals for the project. (AP)
