BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Borland to buy Segue Software for $100m cash

February 9, 2006

Borland Software, which makes tools used to build software, is buying Segue Software for about $100 million in cash. Borland will pay $8.67 a share for the Lexington company, a 25-percent premium to Segue's closing price Monday. Borland expectes the deal to close in the second quarter. (Bloomberg)

Nuance Communications to acquire Dictaphone

Speech recognition company Nuance Communications of Burlington will buy Dictaphone of Stratford, Conn., for $357 million in a bid to become the leading supplier of medical transcription technology. Nuance is a major supplier of software that enables computers to understand human speech. Dictaphone already licenses Nuance software for use in its medical transcription systems. (Hiawatha Bray)

FDA warns Biogen about label claims for Zevalin

The Food and Drug Administration warned Biogen Idec about the wording in brochures promoting Zevalin, a drug used to treat some forms of non-Hodgkin's lymphoma, according to a document made public by the FDA. The agency said in a letter dated Dec. 15 that the brochure, distributed last June, raised ''significant public health and safety concerns because it suggests that Zevalin is safer and more effective than has been demonstrated." The agency said the brochure failed to point out risks that could include potentially fatal reactions. In October, Cambridge-based Biogen Idec revised the warning label for Zevalin after patients developed serious side effects and an undisclosed number died. (Jeffrey Krasner)

Boston company settles pricing dispute for $27m

Cabot Corp., the world's largest producer of carbon black, agreed to pay $27 million to Australia's Sons of Gwalia Ltd. to settle an 18-month-old dispute over tantalum-ore pricing. Under a new three-year contract, Cabot will pay about $13 million more annually than under the prior agreement and get ''significantly" less tantalum from Sons of Gwalia, Boston-based Cabot said. A Cabot spokeswoman declined to provide specific prices or quantities. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Morningstar says SEC ends action on publishing error

Morningstar, the mutual-fund and stock research company, said the Securities and Exchange Commission has ended an investigation related to erroneous data published by the company in 2004. The SEC launched its probe in May 2004 after Morningstar published incorrect total return data for the Rock Canyon Top Flight Fund during a short period earlier that year. Its enforcement division had told Morningstar it intended to recommend the agency take legal action for an alleged violation of US securities laws. Morningstar said it has been informed that the SEC's enforcement division would not pursue its formal investigation and that the matter is now closed. (AP)

Spitzer subpoenas 9 radio companies in payola probe

New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer said he has subpoenaed nine of the largest US radio conglomerates in his payola investigation of major artists and songs that he says got air time because of payoffs by recording companies. ''A lot of the major songs have been implicated in this and it showed how pervasive the payola infrastructure had become," Spitzer said. ''Major artists, major songs were sent up the charts through improper payments to buy spins on the air that translated into sales." The companies that have received subpoenas control thousands of stations nationwide, including Clear Channel Communications, Infinity (which now operates as CBS Radio), Citadel Broadcasting, Cox Radio, Cumulus Broadcasting, Pamal Broadcasting, Entercom Communications, Emmis Communications, and ABC, according to court records. (AP)

US, EU both claim win in biotech trade dispute

The United States and the European Union are again at loggerheads over trade issues, with each claiming a World Trade Organization report favored its position on EU curbs on imports of genetically modified foods. The preliminary ruling concluded the EU had an effective ban on biotech foods between June 1999 and August 2003, when the panel was established, and had therefore acted inconsistently with its WTO obligations. But the EU countered that its current regulations on biotech products were vindicated by the report, saying there was never a European moratorium on the imports of genetically altered crops from the United States or elsewhere. (AP)
