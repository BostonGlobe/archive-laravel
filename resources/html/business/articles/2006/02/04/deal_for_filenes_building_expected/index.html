Deal for Filene's building expected

Investors see space for residences, retail

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr. and Jenn Abelson, Globe Staff  |  February 4, 2006

A pair of New York investors expects to sign a deal to buy the historic Filene's building in Downtown Crossing within a week, one of the partners said yesterday. The buyers would add residences and, possibly, a hotel to the existing retail space, which could be renovated for a chain such as Target.

''We should be wrapped up next week," said Alex Adjmi, president of A&H Acquisitions LLC, who along with partner Robert Cayre has apparently beaten a small group of bidders for the block-sized complex, which was put on the market by Federated Department Stores Inc. late last year.

James M. Koury, an executive with the real estate firm Spaulding & Slye who has done business with the pair but is not involved directly in the Filene's deal, estimated that they are paying about $130 million for the property.

Adjmi would not say how much he bid for the building. He cautioned that the deal is not done, and others in the real estate community emphasized that there are uncertainties around any deal involving so much money and such a high-profile piece of real estate. A Federated spokesman said the company doesn't comment on reports of sale or lease negotiations.

The prospective buyers have not yet met with City Hall, where Mayor Thomas M. Menino has taken an active interest in the future of the property because it will have a significant influence on the direction of downtown retail development.

Adjmi and Cayre, vice president of Aurora Capital, last year purchased the former Abercrombie & Fitch building near Faneuil Hall, and are close to signing a lease with another retailer for three or four floors of that building, which currently sits empty.

''What they're saying is they like Boston," Koury said. ''They're saying they have confidence in Boston."

The Filene's building, at the intersections of Washington, Summer, and Franklin streets at the heart of the downtown retail district, was built in 1912 and is listed on the National Register of Historic Places. It sits on top of an MBTA stop that brings in throngs of people every day. The 656,000-square-foot complex, which has been expanded over the years, houses Filene's, now in its last months of operations, and Filene's Basement, which is run by a different company and holds a long lease to remain.

Adjmi declined to say whether he plans to redevelop the building or tear down part of the block and add one or more towers.

''We're not at that stage yet," he said. ''We've got to meet with the city and the mayor's office. We've got to see what the right thing to do with this property is."

The possibility of a deal was first reported in the Boston Business Journal.

''We have no comment," said Susan Elsbree, a Boston Redevelopment Authority spokeswoman.

Menino has said he would like to see a Target store in downtown Boston. Yesterday, Target spokeswoman Aimee Sands said, ''We are very interested in downtown Boston. But at this time I can't confirm any specific sites."

Federated, of Cincinnati, is selling the flagship Filene's store in Downtown Crossing this year as part of its $17 billion acquisition of Filene's parent, May Department Stores Co. Federated, which also operates the Macy's chain, plans to continue to operate the Macy's store across Summer Street.

Federated is converting many well-known regional chains, including Filene's, into Macy's this year to increase Macy's prominence as a national department store.

Two Boston developers who placed bids on the Filene's building said yesterday they had not heard anything from Federated.

''I don't know what they're doing. I'm completely in the dark," said Robert Epstein, chief executive of Abbey Group, which redeveloped the historic Sears building in the Fenway into Landmark Center.

Epstein said he offered a fixed price for the Filene's building and land -- he wouldn't say how much -- and a higher price contingent on winning permits to redevelop part of the site that is not historic and add a taller building.

John B. Hynes III, president of Gale International of New York, which successfully developed One Lincoln Street, now State Street Financial Center, agreed that expanding the site makes sense.

Hynes, who also aggressively went after the Filene's building and called it ''an awesome site," said it makes sense for a mix of uses but is only really valuable if the newer buildings, on the Franklin Street side, are knocked down and replaced with something taller.

The prospective buyers' plans met with support from some neighbors yesterday. ''To have a combination -- with residential and retail and possibly a hotel -- are all welcome additions to the neighborhood," said Mary Ann Ponti, a Downtown Crossing resident and volunteer on the Downtown Crossing Association.

''Condos would be gorgeous on the upper floors, and it would bring the neighborhood up a little," she said. ''And getting a Target wouldn't be a bad idea for a neighborhood that is becoming more and more residential."

Hynes, too, said Target makes sense. ''The intelligent play is to reposition the whole block into some first-class retail," said Hynes. ''There's a place for Target for sure. You're not going to get Tiffany's there. Downtown Crossing is not Newbury Street."

Federated is closing sites in shopping centers where both a Macy's and Filene's exist. A close-out sale at the downtown Filene's will end in March or April, the company said.

Winning city permits to tear down some of the newer, less historic portions of the block and building a new tower for any use would be an uncertain, time-consuming, and expensive process.

Hynes estimated it would cost $10 million to get through the design and planning and permitting process to the point where a new building could be constructed -- and there are no guarantees.

But Koury said Adjmi and Cayre are serious investors. ''They're active buyers out there," he said. ''They've made offers on other product. They're creative."
