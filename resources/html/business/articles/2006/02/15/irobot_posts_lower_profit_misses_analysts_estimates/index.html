BUSINESS IN BRIEF

IRobot posts lower profit, misses analysts' estimates

February 15, 2006

Robot maker iRobot posted lower net income for the fourth quarter, missing Wall Street estimates and sending shares down 9 percent in after hours trading. The Burlington firm, whose mobile robots are used to disarm bombs, vacuum floors, and as toys, said its net income totaled $12,000, or a break-even per share, compared with $214,000, or a penny per share, a year ago. Sales rose 24 percent to $46.5 million from last year's $37.5 million. Analysts, on average, expected earnings of 4 cents per share on sales of $49.1 million, according to a poll by Thomson Financial. (AP)

Analyst says Gillette buy helping P&G bottom line

Procter & Gamble's purchase of Gillette is helping counter slowing growth in the health and beauty units, JPMorgan Securities analyst John Faucher said. Gillette's blades and razors business grew at a faster pace in the second quarter than P&G's health and beauty businesses and may continue to outperform, Faucher wrote. Faucher maintained his ''neutral" rating on P&G shares. The $57 billion Gillette acquisition helped P&G report a 29 percent gain in second-quarter earnings, the biggest profit increase in more than a year. It also gave P&G dominance in the $5-billion-a-year global market for razors, where Gillette has a 72 percent market share, as P&G faced slowing growth in the United States. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Carmaker to spend $545m to upgrade 5 Mich. plants

General Motors said it will invest more than $545 million to upgrade five Michigan assembly and component plants, even as it slashes jobs and closes plants. GM said it will spend $163 million to retool its truck assembly plant in Pontiac where it will build redesigned Chevrolet Silverado and GMC Sierra pickups. The world's largest automaker will also invest $138 million in its Lansing Grand River assembly plant, $152 million in a transmission plant in Ypsilanti, $60 million in its Romulus engine facility, and $32 million in its metal stamping plant in Pontiac. GM plans to hire 280 people at the Pontiac truck assembly plant, all of whom are expected to be laid-off workers or employees from other plants. (Reuters)

Movie industry sues eBay sellers over DVDs

The Motion Picture Association of America, the trade group representing the seven largest US movie studios, sued seven people who the group says sold counterfeit DVDs over eBay's auction site. The lawsuits were filed in federal court in Los Angeles, the industry group said. Movie studios lost about $3.5 billion last year from counterfeit sales, the industry group said. Including illegal downloads over the Internet, the cost to the industry was about $5.4 billion last year, the group said, citing a Smith Barney study. (Bloomberg)

Merck witness testifies in retrial of 1st Vioxx case

Merck & Co. opened its defense in the retrial of the first federal Vioxx lawsuit with testimony about the history and workings of the once-popular painkiller. Dr. Briggs Morrison, who was on the ''project team" coordinating Vioxx research, told jurors the drug and similar medicines reduce an enzyme called prostacyclin which helps keep blood from clotting. The five men and three women will eventually have to decide whether Vioxx caused the blood clot that killed Richard ''Dickie" Irvin in May 2001 -- and, if that answer is yes, whether Merck hid the drug's dangers. (AP)

Retailer's CEO's resume said to have discrepancies

RadioShack directors said they stand behind their chief executive despite a published report that he didn't earn two college degrees listed on his resume. A biography of David J. Edmondson given to reporters and posted on the company website claimed he earned degrees in theology and psychology from Pacific Coast Baptist College in California. But the registrar of the school, which moved to Oklahoma in 1998 and renamed itself Heartland Baptist Bible College, told the Fort Worth Star-Telegram that records showed Edmondson completed only two semesters and that the school never offered degrees in psychology. (AP)

Verizon Communications sues 3 Fla. telemarketers

Verizon Communications sued three Florida telemarketers, claiming they made almost 600,000 illegal unsolicited calls to cellphone customers. The complaints, filed in state Superior Court in Somerset County, N.J., said the Florida companies, by using recorded messages and automated dialing, violated state and federal law. The suits were filed by Verizon Wireless, a unit of the second-largest US telephone company. (Bloomberg)
