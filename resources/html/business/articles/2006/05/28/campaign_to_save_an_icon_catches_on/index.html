Campaign to save an icon catches on

Oil firm replacing its `76' logo balls

By Scott Gold, Los Angeles Times  |  May 28, 2006

LOS ANGELES -- In January, Kim Cooper was driving through the Lincoln Heights neighborhood when her local gas station caught her eye.

The station's familiar ``76" insignia, its stocky blue numbers splashed against an orange ball, had been replaced with a new sign. The new 76 was printed on red -- ``a queasy color," she recalled with a grimace, ``like liver."

Cooper, the publisher of Scram -- a ``journal of unpopular culture" -- does not typically concern herself with megacorporations like ConocoPhillips, owner of the Union 76 brand.

But the 76 logo, she decided, isn't just an ad. Not anymore. The orange balls that have rotated above gas stations for 45 years are a piece of roadside Americana, and in Southern California they are iconic, not much different than palm trees or the Hollywood sign. They no longer belong to a boardroom, Cooper decided, but to the public.

``I felt that this shouldn't pass unnoticed," she said.

Cooper launched a website -- www.savethe76ball.com -- dedicated to the 76 balls' preservation.

At first glance, it seemed a little frivolous. In a city of transit and transients, is this what preservationists are left with: fighting to save relics of urban design known in the subculture of petroliana as ``meatballs"?

It hasn't taken long, however, for the campaign to catch on. Support has poured in, not just from drivers, but from prominent voices in architecture and design, a board member of the Los Angeles Conservancy, even the 79-year-old man who designed the balls to mark the 1962 World's Fair in Seattle.

Houston-based ConocoPhillips, which has been quietly replacing the balls with modern-looking signs for at least six months, declined to respond to detailed questions about its decision. In a written statement, a spokeswoman said the balls were being replaced -- and the logo's color changed to red -- to give a ``common image" to the company's 76, Phillips 66, and Conoco gas stations.

``We appreciate motorists' loyalty," the statement said. ``Though our look is a little different, the quality of our products and our commitment to our customers remains the same."

To Cooper, 39, it sounds like corporate hooey, ``the complete rejection of the good will that this brand has built."

Many of the 1,600 postings to Cooper's online petition suggest that she is not alone. ``Please don't destroy my childhood memories," one reads. Another asks: ``Why does everything we love in life go away?"

While the testimonials trickle in, the balls continue to fall each week in Los Angeles neighborhoods. At Dodger Stadium, where Union Oil had a fruitful sponsorship from the start of construction: Vin Scully once responded to home runs by announcing that Union 76 would be making a donation in a player's name, and during some games, the orange ball beyond center field made it seem as if two suns were setting.

In 1961, Ray Pedersen was a 34-year-old art director for the advertising firm of Young & Rubicam in Los Angeles. Union Oil Co., the venerable California firm founded in 1890, asked Y&R to design a sign that would rise next to a cable-supported ``sky train" at the Seattle World's Fair.

Pedersen began fiddling with the advertising schematic Union Oil was already using -- the blocky numbers, the orange-and-blue motif .

``I thought: `We've got to do something really hot -- a big ball, lit from the inside,"' he said.

By the time he had found someone who could mold plastic into a ball that would reach 12 feet in diameter, he had spent an estimated $50,000, he said. A Y&R manager called in a rage.

Pedersen said he was nearly fired -- but Union Oil loved it.

They became an oddball expression of unity. Union Oil created tiny versions that could be affixed to car antennas, and distributed millions.

``The 76 sign is part of a tremendous history," said Alan Hess, an Irvine architect and author of 10 books on 20th-century architectural history. ``The 76 sign was colorful, it was shapely, and it was delightful. It was also functional; your tank is getting low, you see it far down the street and you knew exactly where you were going to get gas."

Pedersen, among others, can't understand why ConocoPhillips would abandon an image as powerful as the 76 balls.

``It's their nickel," he said. ``But this was a franchise. It just doesn't make any sense."
