BUSINESS IN BRIEF

No fine levied in SEC suit over Hemopure report

March 14, 2006

THE REGION
Biopure Corp. said it reached agreement with the Securities and Exchange Commission to settle allegations that the company misled investors about its Hemopure blood substitute. No payments would be required under the proposed settlement of a complaint that the SEC filed last year, the Cambridge company said. The SEC had sued Biopure saying the company hid a negative report from investors as it sought to raise money in 2003. The settlement still needs to win final approval from the SEC and the US District Court in Boston, where the case is pending. The company filed for approval of Hemopure in 2002, and the Food and Drug Administration said it would need more information before approval. (Bloomberg)

State Street chief gets $5m bonus for last year
Financial services company State Street Corp. awarded chairman and chief executive Ronald E. Logue a bonus of $5 million for 2005, up from $2.8 million in 2004. According to a proxy filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission, the 2005 bonus included a cash award of $3.3 million and a deferred stock award of $1.7 million. Logue's compensation for 2005 also included a $1 million salary and 292,000 stock options. In addition, the compensation committee approved a cash payment of about $2.2 million to Logue for the 2004 to 2005 performance period. Last week, the Boston-based company said its board approved a $2 million supplemental award for Logue. (Dow Jones/AP)

Staples gives $1.33m bonus for year to CEO
Staples Inc. said it approved the payment of a $1.33 million bonus to chairman and chief executive Ronald L. Sargent for the year ended Jan. 28. For the previous fiscal year, Sargent received a $1.73 million bonus, according to a company proxy. The Framingham office products company said in a document filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission that it also raised Sargent's salary by $40,000, or 3.9 percent, to $1.07 million. Staples also said Sargent is eligible for a maximum bonus equal to 2.5 times his salary for the current fiscal year. (Dow Jones)

4th-quarter profit falls 20% at Sonus Networks
Sonus Networks Inc., a Chelmsford maker of software used in Internet voice communications, said fourth-quarter profit fell 20 percent despite revenue growth, as sales and marketing costs increased, but still managed to beat Wall Street expectations. Net income shrank to $5 million from $6.3 million a year ago. Per-share earnings were flat at 2 cents, based on a decrease in the number of shares outstanding. Revenue grew 27 percent to $57.3 million, from $45.1 million in the same quarter in 2004. Operating expenses increased at the same rate year-over-year to $33.5 million, including a 45 percent jump in sales and marketing costs. (AP)

Mercury Computer to cut 80 jobs to reduce costs
Mercury Computer Systems Inc., a Chelmsford maker of digital signal processing systems for the defense and medical imaging markets, said it was cutting 80 jobs -- 8 percent of its workforce -- to lower costs. The company said the move would save $7 million. The reduction is expected to result in total cash expenditures of about $2 million, payable over the next three quarters, with the charge accrued in the fiscal quarter ending March 31. (AP)

THE NATION
Discount carrier raises fares, others follow suit
Southwest Airlines Co. raised its air fares by up to $10 for a one-way ticket, seeking to offset $600 million in estimated additional fuel costs this year, a spokesman said. Other big airlines, including Continental Airlines, Northwest Airlines, US Air, Delta Air Lines, American Airlines, and United Airlines followed Southwest's lead. It was the second fare boost this year for Southwest, which has less protection from contracts that protect it from higher fuel costs. (Reuters)

Guidant warns doctors on low-voltage defibrillators
Guidant Corp., which is being acquired for $27 billion by Boston Scientific Corp., cautioned doctors to check the voltage on certain implantable defibrillators after the company received several reports of defective devices. The company issued the warning for Contak Renewal 3 RF and Contak Renewal 4 RF models that shock the heart back to a normal rhythm. Guidant said it had received 39 reports from doctors saying defibrillators that had been tested before being implanted had a lower-than-expected battery voltage. None of the 39 devices has been put into patients. (AP)
