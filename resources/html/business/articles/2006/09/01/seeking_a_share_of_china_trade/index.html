Seeking a share of China trade

Smaller law firms filling niche in helping midsize companies profit in Asia

By Sacha Pfeiffer, Globe Staff  |  September 1, 2006

Meet Transcontinental Sailing, also known as Timothy B. Bancroft, a partner at the Boston law firm Goulston & Storrs.

``Transcontinental Sailing" is the English translation of Bancroft's Mandarin name, which he has adopted -- and printed on his business cards -- as Goulston prepares to aggressively market itself to midsized companies in New England and beyond interested in doing business in China.

To promote itself as a regional ``gateway to China," the firm is also giving green tea to its clients and chopsticks to its staff, creating ads that use colors linked with good fortune in China (like red), and offering in-house training in Chinese culture and protocol, such as the polite way to exchange business cards (both hands extended, card facing up).

With more than a billion potential consumers and its status as a global manufacturing center, China has long been on the radar of large American companies lured by its profit potential -- and large American law firms have followed closely behind to provide critical legal services. But as China continues its shift from a state-run to a market economy, lowering barriers to entry for overseas companies and embracing Western economics, it has widening appeal to smaller US companies and investors, too.

That has smaller law firms, like 185- lawyer Goulston & Storrs and 130-lawyer Burns & Levinson, jockeying to help midsized corporate clients navigate China's challenging regulatory system as they outsource manufacturing, expand retail operations, invest in Chinese startups, and attract Chinese investors to US firms.

``A growing number of companies in the Northeast and East Coast and elsewhere are coming to us and saying, ``We want to move into China,' " said Goulston partner Daniel R. Avery, ``and given the exploding consumer power in China and the government's increasingly flexible legal requirements in terms of encouraging foreign investment, the ability from a cost perspective for middle-market companies to enter China has changed dramatically."

In working to raise the profile of its China practice, Goulston is training its staff and lawyers in ``guanxi," the Mandarin word for the mutually beneficial relationships critical to success in China, and other nuances of building lasting connections with Chinese business partners. The training will address proper seating at business meetings (the most important players go in the middle), what to wear when meeting Chinese clients (formal dress is expected), and how to behave at business dinners (drinking alone is considered rude, and frequent toasts are expected).

``The critical need to be able to bridge the cultural gap is actually more challenging than the need to bridge the legal and regulatory gaps," Avery said.

Indeed, China -- with its evolving legal and judicial systems, complicated regulatory environment, emphasis on personal relationships when inking deals, and legacy of government corruption -- can be a tricky place to do business.

As a result, ``it's crucial, if you're a company that wants to do business in China, that you have competent counsel," said Paula Murphy, director of the Massachusetts Export Center, a government agency that helps businesses in the state export products or services overseas. Last year, the center created the Greater China Business Council of New England, a networking group for companies exploring Chinese business opportunities. Tellingly, executives from some of the state's best-known companies serve on the council's board of directors, including Analog Devices Inc., Genzyme Corp., Teradyne Inc., New Balance Athletic Shoe Inc., and Spalding.

Many multinational law firms with a Boston presence, including Wilmer Cutler Pickering Hale and Dorr, Holland & Knight, and DLA Piper Rudnick Gray Cary, have offices in China and serve major American companies with Chinese business interests. But many smaller companies with China aspirations are unable or unwilling to pay the rates charged by such megafirms, or prefer to work with midsized firms that offer more contact with partners.

``There is a large group of medium-sized companies in the US who have business in China who are not well-served by large law firms," said Boston lawyer Michael J. Meagher, who speaks Mandarin and recently established East Asia Law and Government Strategies, which does cross-border work in North America, Europe, and Asia. ``So there's plenty of business out there" for smaller firms, said Meagher, formerly with Burns & Levinson and, briefly, Goulston, ``but it's a big commitment in terms of the culture, the language, and the law, and it's a big learning curve."

Goulston, which plans to open an office in Shanghai or Beijing by year's end, sees potential for a wide range of legal work in China, from handling commercial transactions and public offerings, to brokering real estate deals and licensing agreements .

The firm's in-house training on Chinese culture has occasionally forced its lawyers to rethink long-held habits, such as Americans' tendencies to clean their plates.

``In our culture, it's pretty clear if you don't finish everything that your host is probably going to wonder what the problem is," Bancroft said, ``whereas if you eat everything in China they feel like they should keep feeding you because they think they didn't give you enough."

With four people who speak or are studying Mandarin, the firm is trolling for new clients hoping to crack the China market and following existing clients with work in China; earlier this year, for example, Goulston represented an investment company involved in opening China's first Saks Fifth Avenue stores.

Bancroft is not the only Goulston lawyer with a new Mandarin name; Goulston associate Lucia Lian chose Chinese names for each of the 10 lawyers in Goulston's China practice group. In Bancroft's case, he was dubbed Transcontinental Sailing because he enjoys international legal work and ``he likes sailing," Lian said.

The firm, too, now has a Chinese name: Gu Shi Den. Taken together, the three characters -- which translate roughly as ``take care," ``worldwide," and ``peak," respectively -- mean ``taking care of our worldwide clients at our peak capacity," according to Lian.

``So it sounds like `Goulston,' " she said, ``and it also has a very good meaning."
