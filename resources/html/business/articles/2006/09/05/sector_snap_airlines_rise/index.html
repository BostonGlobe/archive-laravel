Sector Snap: Airlines rise

September 5, 2006

NEW YORK --Airline shares rose in Tuesday afternoon trading, as easing oil prices overcame some analysts' concern over August traffic results from some of the industry's biggest players.

The Amex Airline Index more than 1 percent, with seven of 10 component stocks marking gains. Earlier in the session, the index reached its highest point since Aug. 8.

Helping all shares was the price of oil dropping 60 cents to $68.59 on the New York Mercantile Exchange. Falling crude prices help trim airlines' fuel budgets, as well as encourage more leisure and business flying.

Based on new assumptions for fuel prices and revenue, Lehman Brothers analyst Gary Chase lowered his earnings estimates for much of the industry in this year's second half, while boosting his 2007 estimates. He sees US Airways Group Inc., for example, earning $5.76 per share in 2006, down from previous projections of $6.41. Wall Street overall is looking for $6.80, according to an analysts poll by Thomson Financial.

For 2007, Chase upped his profit per share projection to $5.70 from $5.50, compared with Wall Street's overall expectation of $7.19.

But he singled out Continental Airline Inc.'s August traffic figures, saying he was slightly disappointed.

The carrier said its unit revenue grew by between 6.5 percent and 7.5 percent versus August 2005. Elevated security concerns following a foiled trans-Atlantic terrorist bombing plot shaved off about 1.5 percentage points, Continental said.

That was slightly higher than the half point that Merrill Lynch analyst Michael Linenberg said he expected. He maintained his "Neutral" rating on the carrier.

American Airlines, meanwhile, reported a 1.4 percent decline in August traffic on a 1.9 percent capacity decrease. Those and Continental's numbers pushed Morgan Stanley analyst William J. Greene to say his previous earnings estimates for the pair "were simply too optimistic."

He cut his 2006 profit projection for American Airlines' parent AMR Corp. to $1.20 per share from $1.67, compared with Wall Street's overall view of $1.92.

For Continental, he trimmed his 2006 profit outlook to $2.90 from $3.02, still below Wall Street's overall view of $3.24.

The earnings revisions, though, had little dampening effect on AMR's and Continental's shares.

AMR rose 64 cents, or 3.1 percent, to $21.04, while Continental gained 82 cents, or 3.3 percent, at $25.82, both on the New York Stock Exchange.

Alaska Air Group Inc. picked up 16 cents at $37.91, and Southwest Airlines Co. added 6 cents to $17.28, also on the NYSE.

On the Nasdaq, Frontier Airlines Holdings Inc. climbed 21 cents, or 3 percent, to $7.15, and JetBlue Airways Corp. picked up 16 cents to $10.40.

Regional carrier SkyWest Inc. rose 9 cents to $24.25 on the Nasdaq.

Among the index's decliners, AirTran Holdings Inc. fell 2 cents to $11.08, and regional carrier ExpressJet Holdings Inc. slipped 3 cents to $6.95, both on the NYSE.

Mesa Air Group Inc. lost 4 cents to $7.83 on the Nasdaq.

Outside the index, US Airways rose $1.25, or 3 percent, to $43 on the NYSE, while United Airlines parent UAL Corp. gained 23 cents to $25.06 on the Nasdaq.
