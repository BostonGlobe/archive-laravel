Economic wreckage in Lebanon to outlast bombs

It could be years before recovery takes hold again

By Associated Press  |  July 27, 2006

BEIRUT -- Israel's bombardment has dealt a devastating blow to Lebanon's fragile economy, just as it was recovering from a yearslong slump.

A profitable tourist season was lost, investors fled, much commerce has stopped, construction is at a standstill -- costing at an estimated $70 million a day.

Economists say it may take up to $5 billion and years to rebuild shattered infrastructure and other damage.

``This is a crippling war, one that is obviously disastrous for the economy," Economy and Trade Minister Sami Haddad said yesterday.

When Hezbollah kidnapped two Israeli soldiers July 12 -- triggering a massive Israeli offensive that sent tourists and foreigners packing and displaced more than half a million Lebanese -- Lebanon's economy had just started to rebound from a recession that was compounded by the assassination of former Prime Minister Rafik Hariri in February 2005.

Within hours, the tiny Mediterranean country was transformed from a land filled with tourists and businessmen into a war zone packed with foreign correspondents and refugees. Israeli warplanes pounded bridges, roads, power plants, ports, and grain silos, as well as TV and cellphone transmission towers.

Israel also bombed all three runways at Beirut International Airport, forcing its closure, and imposed a blockade on the country's main seaports. Foreign passport holders fled overland to Syria or were evacuated by boat to Cyprus and Turkey.

Authorities say more than 400 people have been killed, the majority of them civilians.

``We're talking about enormous losses here. The negative effects will be felt for years," Lebanese economist Louis Hobeika said, undercutting Lebanon's nascent image as an investment.

Even if there is a cease-fire today, he said, ``it will be at least two years before the economy can get back into its regular cycle again."

Many shops and businesses, including major factories in eastern Lebanon that were bombed, have temporarily closed as many employees stayed home. Restaurants are either closed or deserted. The Beirut stock exchange closed for several days after the start of the bombings. Shares in Solidere, a company founded by Hariri to rebuild downtown , fell about 15 percent.

Even garbage collectors, many of them from India and Sri Lanka, fled the country, leaving heaps of trash on the streets -- a scene reminiscent of the 1975-90 civil war.

But while the economic and human effects of the war are severe, experts believe the impact on the country's financial sector seems limited.

Despite a severe shortage in dollars, the Lebanese pound has not fallen as sharply against the US dollar as it did after the assassination of Hariri, according to a JP Morgan report quoted Tuesday by Zawya Dow Jones. The report said that the overall solid financial system, expectations of significant reconstruction assistance, and investor awareness of Lebanese ability to handle crisis situations would help limit the downside risk to the economy in the longer term.

Lebanon, already saddled with debt of about $38 billion, or 180 percent of GDP, will have to rely on foreign donations to rebuild.

European and Arab foreign ministers meeting in Rome yesterday agreed to call for a donors' conference for Lebanon's infrastructure. On Tuesday, Saudi Arabia's King Abdullah disclosed donations to Lebanon totaling $1.5 billion -- $500 million for reconstruction and $1 billion to be deposited in Lebanon's central bank to support the economy. The European Commission said it was committing $25.3 million in financial assistance to Lebanon.

But most agree it will be very difficult to lure back investors to Lebanon unless a serious and lasting solution is reached.

``We cannot rebuild the country if we are under constant threat of attack by the most formidable army in the region," Haddad said.

Economist Charbel Nahhas warned of other problems, such as the large number of people who have lost businesses and the expected bankruptcies.

Without a lasting peace, one of the biggest challenges facing Lebanon will be the loss of human capital, Lebanon's main asset, Haddad said. Many Lebanese who fled during the civil war had been returning and starting businesses; now there's big concern they will leave again and take others with them.

``People are increasingly thinking of leaving Lebanon," he said, ``and those immigrants who were considering a return to the country will ask themselves if they really should."
