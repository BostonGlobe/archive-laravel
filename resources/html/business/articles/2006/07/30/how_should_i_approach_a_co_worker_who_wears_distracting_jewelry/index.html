ETIQUETTE AT WORK

How should I approach a co-worker who wears distracting jewelry?

By Peter Post  |  July 30, 2006

Q: I work in a fairly quiet office. A woman who sits near me, however, wears an anklet with bells on it every single day to work.

She's up and down the cubicle aisles constantly, and it sounds like a cat is running by all day long. You can even hear her three rows over. The nonstop jingling makes it very hard to concentrate. How do I approach her about this issue?
S.M., Marlborough

A: When asked how they'd prefer to be clued in about personal issues, workers invariably say they'd rather be told by a friend. If Ms. Ankle Bracelet has a good friend in the office, ask the friend if she or he has noticed the problem and would be willing to speak with her about it.

Otherwise, you'll have to do it yourself.

Arrange to talk with her in private, and explain to her that one of the things you appreciate about your particular office cubicle environment is that it's so quiet, but that lately you've been noticing her ankle bells have become distracting. Then ask her nicely if she'd mind not wearing them at work.

If she complies, be sure to follow up with a warm thank you a few days later, to let her know how much you appreciate her cooperation in ``keeping the peace."

If she refuses, you'll have to decide whether to drop the matter or go to a supervisor about it. In making your decision, remember that it's always wise to pick your office battles carefully. Is the jingling noise something that bothers only you, or do others find it distracting, as well? If it's also bothering others, then you have more of a case.

Q: Our company is hosting a picnic for all of our employees, and I'm on the picnic-planning committee. A considerable number of door prizes have been donated for the event.

The chairman of the planning committee feels it's perfectly acceptable for him and the rest of the committee members to submit our names for the door prize drawing.

I feel this is in poor taste. Since we planned the event and asked for the donations, it seems to me that committee members should be disqualified from winning. Who is correct?

A.J.,
Milwaukee

A: While it isn't considered cricket for the host to win the door prize at an event he or she is hosting, in this case it's the company that's the host -- not the committee. For this reason, all employees, including management, should be free to participate in all events, including a drawing for door prizes. (You wouldn't ask the dinner committee not to eat the dinner, would you?)
