Human service aides get raise

By Kathy McCabe  |  July 30, 2006

Human service workers on the low end of the wage scale will receive an estimated 3.2 percent pay raise after state lawmakers voted to restore to $28 million a salary reserve fund slashed in half by Governor Mitt Romney.

The House and Senate voted unanimously to restore the funding, which Romney had cut to $14 million, saying the state could not afford the full pot. The vote was closely watched by human service providers, who lobbied for the pay raise, mostly to help those earning $11 or less per hour, such as home health aides, youth workers, rape counselors , and others.

``They save lives," said Michael Ripple, operations director at the Massachusetts Council of Human Service Providers , a 280-member organization. ``But they aren't paid well . . . For all of our members, their number one priority is to see that their staff is well paid."

Low pay is mostly to blame for high turnover among workers at human service providers. North Shore ARC in Danvers, which provides services to the mentally disabled, can't fill jobs. The agency struggles with a 30 percent turnover rate among its 450 workers, an official said.

``Turnover is a tremendous problem," said Jerry McCarthy, executive director at North Shore ARC.

``We have vacancies for a long time before we can get anyone to fill them. We simply can't find people to hire for the jobs because of the low pay."

Employees sometimes must work double or triple shifts. Overtime costs have ballooned $1.5 million over the last three years. The agency also has to recruit workers from Jamaica, the Czech Republic, Ireland , and other foreign countries .

North Shore ARC -- formerly known as North Shore Association for Retarded Citizens -- made the pay raise a top priority.

The agency hosted a legislative breakfast and spent additional time speaking with legislators about the need for a raise, McCarthy said.

``We talked with them on several occasions about how important this was for our workforce," said McCarthy, who sits on the board of the statewide human service council. ``We are just very grateful that they listened."

The raise will be available to workers earning $40,000 or less. Most are employed by nonprofit s working on contract for the state department s of mental health services and public health and other agencies.

The council estimates 3,000 workers statewide will qualify for the raises. Of that number, about 13,000 are the lowest-paid, earning $11 or less per hour, Ripple said.

Flood recovery aid deadline extended

Businesses, homeowners, and renters have been given more time to apply for low-cost loans and grants to help them recover from the May floods. The deadline was extended from July 24 until Aug. 7 by the US Small Business Administration and the Federal Emergency Management Agency.

Businesses that sustained lost revenues due to the floods will still have until Feb. 26 to apply for financial assistance. Staff from the SBA will continue to staff disaster recover centers in Haverhill, Melrose, Peabody, and Revere until the deadline passes.

FEMA staff are no longer on site, however. People seeking help from FEMA should call 800-621-FEMA . The SBA can be reached at 1-800-659-2955 or apply on line at www.sba.gov/disaster.

Little bank could, and will, branch out

Pentucket Bank of Haverhill touts itself as ``The Little Bank That Could," with a train logo. The $425 million-asset bank has chugged along against bigger rivals to garner a 39 percent market share, more than any other bank in its hometown, according to the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.

Now its next stop is southern New Hampshire. Pentucket plans to open new branches in Hampstead and Salem, N.H., markets rich with potential, particularly for small-business and commercial real estate loans. ``There are tremendous growth opportunities there," said Kendall C. Smith , chief executive officer at Pentucket. ``We're not looking to make loans to the Wal-Marts and Targets of the world."

But Pentucket is hardly the only local bank eyeing greener pastures in the Granite State. Merrimac Savings Bank opened a branch in Newton, N.H. East Boston Savings Bank of Peabody owns 40 percent of Hampshire First Bank, a start-up bank in Manchester.

A wave of bank mergers in the early '90s left southern New Hampshire dominated by mega-banks like TD Banknorth and Sovereign. But new housing growth and demand for small-business loans prompted Bay State banks to expand there.

Pentucket now wants a piece of the pie. The bank estimates it will cost $3.5 million to build its two New Hampshire branches. The Salem branch will be a two-story office on North Broadway. The Hampstead office will be in a strip mall on Route 111. Both will have drive-up windows and ATMs, Smith said. The Hampstead branch should be ready to open by October.
