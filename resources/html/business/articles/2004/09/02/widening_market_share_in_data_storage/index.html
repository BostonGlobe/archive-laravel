BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Widening market share in data storage

September 2, 2004

EMC Corp. widened its lead as the world's top seller of data storage computers over Hewlett-Packard Co. and International Business Machines Corp. in the second quarter, researcher Gartner Inc. said. In the second quarter, EMC's sales of the machines that organize and retrieve data rose to 23.1 percent of the worldwide market, or $745.6 million, from 20.6 percent a year earlier, Gartner said. HP's share slipped to 16.5 percent, and IBM boosted its share to 13.2 percent. EMC expanded in a market that generated $3.23 billion in sales last quarter by winning small- and medium-sized business customers with new machines that cost as little as $5,000. Two years ago EMC's cheapest computer cost $350,000. HP's third-quarter profit missed the company's forecast partly because of declining storage computer sales. Worldwide second-quarter sales of storage systems rose 5.1 percent, according to the report. Hopkinton-based EMC is also the world's biggest seller of software that runs data computers. (Bloomberg)

Ex-Triumph Capital official barred for life

Triumph Capital Group Inc.'s former general counsel, Charles Spadoni, agreed to be barred from the investment industry in a civil proceeding with securities regulators. Spadoni, 55, was convicted last year of federal racketeering, bribery, mail fraud, and obstruction of justice charges in connection with a 1998 investment by Connecticut's state pension fund. The Securities and Exchange Commission alleged Spadoni helped provide consulting contracts worth $2 million to close associates of then-Connecticut Treasurer Paul Silvester to secure more pension investments for Boston-based Triumph. Spadoni neither admitted nor denied the allegations in his agreement with the SEC. (Bloomberg)

Fund tied to interest rates to be offered

Putnam Investments, the Boston investment unit of Marsh & McLennan Cos., opened Putnam Floating Rate Income fund, its first designed to benefit from rising interest rates. Putnam already oversees about $750 million in floating-rate investments in other portfolios, Steve Peacher, chief investment officer of the company's core high-yield bond group, said. The interest levels in floating-rate loans adjust regularly when benchmark rates change. In another development, Putnam was fired by an English pension plan because of poor performance. Cumbria County Council, the municipality that oversees the northwest region adjoining Scotland, is looking for a replacement for Putnam to oversee $180 million of stocks. Putnam managed the money for three years. (Bloomberg)

Minn. company to buy Lawyers Weekly

Dolan Media Co. of Minneapolis said it is acquiring 14 newspapers from Lawyers Weekly Inc., which publishes Massachusetts Lawyers Weekly, Rhode Island Lawyers Weekly, New England In-House, which is sold to corporate attorneys, and other legal publications. Terms of the deal were not disclosed. The newspapers involved have combined circulation of nearly 93,000 and are read by 15 percent of the US legal profession, Dolan Media said. Lawyers Weekly began in the early 1970s with the launch of Massachusetts Lawyers Weekly, and Dolan Media said Boston will continue to be home base for the legal publications. ''This is an excellent opportunity to expand the newspapers' editorial mission with the additional resources of a larger company," said Susan Hall, chairman of Lawyers Weekly Inc. (Kimberly Blanton)

NStar wants to buy out power contract

NStar, the largest utility company in Massachusetts, said it will save $30 million by buying out a contract for wholesale power purchases from a partnership. The agreement is the fourth reached by Boston-based NStar in the past three months, with savings totaling $115 million, according to company filings at the Massachusetts Department of Telecommunications and Energy. The utility is asking the agency to recover the undisclosed buyout costs from customers, according to the filings. The savings estimates include the cost of the buyouts. The latest buyout agreement is for a contract signed in 1989 and running through 2017 for supplies from a power plant in Dartmouth owned by Northern Star Generation LLC, a joint venture of American International Group and the Ontario Teachers' Pension Plan of Toronto. (Bloomberg)

Starrett settles federal investigation

L.S. Starrett Co. of Athol said it reached a settlement with the Department of Justice that will end the government's previously disclosed investigation of Starrett's CMM unit in Mount Airy, N.C. Under terms of the settlement, Starrett said it will pay the government $500,000 which will be recorded in the company's fourth fiscal quarter which ended June 26. Starrett said it cooperated with the government throughout the investigation and agreed to settle the matter to avoid the expense of protracted litigation. In 2002, the government began examining allegations made by a former independent contractor that the CMM division defrauded customers and the government with sales of its measuring machines and software. Douglas A. Starrett, president and chief executive, said the settlement was in the best interests of the company as protracted litigation would have cost too much. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION
Oracle shareholder suit reinstated

A federal Appeals Court reinstated a shareholder suit that claims Oracle Corp., the world's third-largest software maker, improperly booked about $228 million in sales in 2000 to conceal slowing demand. The 9th US Circuit Court of Appeals in San Francisco reinstated the suit, which had been dismissed three times by a trial court judge. The suit also claims that Oracle executives, including chief executive Larry Ellison, sold stock before revealing the company wouldn't meet financial targets. (Bloomberg)

THE WORLD
Data released on drug trials

GlaxoSmithKline PLC has taken a first step toward fulfilling a promise to disclose the results of every drug trial it sponsors by posting summaries on the Internet. The pharmaceutical company said it had posted summaries of dozens of clinical trials concerning the diabetes medication rosiglitazone in its new online registry. Glaxo sells the drug under the brand name Avandia. Results of other drug trials will be added to the registry as summaries are compiled, the company said. The first of the postings comes just days after Glaxo agreed to pay $2.5 million and release data on the safety and effectiveness of its drugs to settle a lawsuit by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer. Spitzer accused the company of fraudulently withholding some results of studies that had examined the safety of prescribing the antidepressant medication Paxil to children. (AP)

Nashua's SkillSoft PLC, an Internet-based training and software company, reported net income for its 2005 second quarter of $1.4 million, or a penny a share, compared with a loss of $53.2 million, or 53 cents a share, for the year-earlier quarter. Revenue totaled $50.6 million, up from $45.1 million a year earlier. . . . Toy retailer KB Toys Inc. of Pittsfield is asking the bankruptcy court overseeing its Chapter 11 case to approve a compromise with Walt Disney Co. that will avoid a protracted court fight. According to court papers submitted in Wilmington, Del., KB will pay $365,776 to Disney and will stop sales of Disney-themed dolls. In exchange, Disney agrees not to sue KB for infringing on the entertainment giant's intellectual property. (Globe wire services)
