Bank to move a key unit to Boston

Vow comes amid intense criticism of recent job cuts

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  September 2, 2004

Bank of America Corp. chief executive Kenneth D. Lewis, under pressure to explain recent job cuts made following the bank's $48 billion acquisition of FleetBoston Financial Corp., yesterday said he will move one of the bank's four main divisions to Boston.

The move will offset the loss of some high-paying executive jobs eliminated in the deal, as Bank of America brings business-line presidents, marketing, and other support staff to the region.

Lewis declined to name the division, but it is likely to be the bank's wealth-management arm, which currently has its top executive in Boston but several of its key business lines in New York.

Wealth management includes the mutual fund lines of both banks, Bank of America's Nations Funds and Fleet's Columbia Funds, as well as its private bank for the wealthy and its ''premier banking" for the near-affluent.

Lewis said the decision developed recently, and he would have announced it in its entirety, except that since that it is vacation season he has not been able to reach all the top executives to inform them about it.

Lewis and several other executives also said for the first time yesterday that the bank has cut 700 jobs in Massachusetts since acquiring Fleet in April, including last month's layoffs in Fleet branches.

The bank had declined for months to discuss how many jobs it would cut in each state, but it faced mounting pressure from lawmakers in recent weeks, and from the state's banking commissioner, who had demanded more detailed data.

''When you see what was here and what we're going to do, there's no way this won't be better for Boston," Lewis said during an interview with Globe editors and reporters yesterday.

The bank has come in for fierce criticism in recent weeks since it moved responsibility for two units away from Boston and laid off about 800 branch workers across the Northeast.

Lewis said the bank did not intend to mislead when it said at the time of the merger that it would have six units based in Boston, even though most of them consisted largely of the top executives running the units, with most of the operations elsewhere.

''What I hope you'll see, and what I think you'll see, is that we've made a recommitment to Boston that hopefully is better than what we pledged in the first place," Lewis said.

The bank has also drawn fire from state and local officials over the layoffs of the branch workers. Before the merger became final, Lewis said ''customer-facing" employees would not lose their jobs. Yesterday, he said he made those statements because he knew no Fleet branches would close because of the merger.

However, Lewis said he did not anticipate his comments would apply to the changes the bank would make as it converted Fleet branches to its own way of doing business.

The bank has said it would hire about 600 workers for the branches in the Northeast, mostly part-timers, which will help to offset the layoffs. The bank will pay most of those workers full benefits, executives said.

The job cuts and division shifts by the bank have elicited a firestorm of criticism from public officials. State Treasurer Timothy P. Cahill threatened to withdraw $120 million worth of state business from Bank of America. He joined with Governor Mitt Romney and US Representative Barney Frank, Democrat of Newton, in demanding a public accounting of job gains and losses.

Frank, who met with Lewis and other bank executives yesterday, said lawmakers had not realized what the bank meant when it said it would headquarter a division in Boston.

''They meant that the chief guy was here, and his secretary was here, and nothing else," he said.

If Lewis decides to bring the wealth management unit here, he would have to uproot executives in Bank of America's private bank for the wealthy and its brokerage, which are run from New York City. The bank also has mutual fund managers in places like Chicago, Charlotte, and St. Louis.

Boston makes sense as a home for the bank's wealth management arm because of its position as home to many of the nation's dominant mutual funds and the businesses that support them, said Bernard Horn, president of Polaris Capital Management in Boston. ''It's not surprising," he said. ''The ability to hire and retain good people is better in the Boston area."

The other three main divisions, in addition to wealth management, are consumer, large corporate, and commercial.

Lewis reaffirmed other commitments the bank has made: to maintain the same number of employees, about 17,900, that Fleet had in New England, and to increase Fleet's level of charitable giving.

Frank and US Representative Michael Capuano, Democrat of Somerville, said they were happier after meeting with bank executives, but not completely satisfied. ''As Mr. Lewis goes back to North Carolina, we'd like a little more flow and a little less ebb from Massachusetts," Capuano said.

The Boston City Council also called for a hearing into the merger, and several councilors said the city should reconsider its relationship with Bank of America if the Charlotte bank does not keep its commitments to New England.

''It's very simple," said Councilor Jerry McDermott, who represents Allston-Brighton. ''They need to explain their recent actions and right their wrongs."
