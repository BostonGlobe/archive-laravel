Scramble to skirt foreclosure

Seeking to avoid red tape,expense, lenders developnew strategies to dealwith delinquent loans

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr., Globe Staff  |  September 2, 2004

Commercial property owners all over the Boston area may be defaulting on loans to their empty buildings, but lenders are avoiding foreclosures.

ADVERTISEMENT

In a trend that defies conventional real estate patterns, banks and other financiers are cutting deals -- from quickly selling loans at a discount to softening the terms -- to avoid the expense and red tape of foreclosures. And in some cases, the original owners or developers are regaining control of the real estate.

''There's beginning to be a whole new round of delinquencies," said George J. Fantini Jr., chairman of Fantini & Gorga, a real estate advisory firm in Boston. And lenders, he said, ''have adopted entirely new strategies."

Foreclosures, so common in the crash of the early 1990s, aren't unheard of today. But they are rapidly being preempted by a range of less onerous alternatives.

''The lenders would rather work it out, as opposed to taking it back on the books and be in it for the long haul," said Marci Griffith Loeber, executive director of Cushman & Wakefield of Massachusetts Inc., a real estate services firm.

When South Boston land owner Frank H. McCourt Jr. recently ran into trouble on his 51 Sleeper St. building, his lender, a syndicate of investors, put the note on the market. Hunneman Capital Group, which is brokering the $22 million loan, is expected to select a winning bidder in the next few days, and someone is likely to grab the eight-story building with good potential for residential use for $15 million to $19 million -- a big discount. McCourt, meanwhile, could have a hand in the property's redevelopment.

Ted Saracen, president of Newton-based Saracen Properties LLC, and his father, Dom, developed a large suburban portfolio in the 1980s, including Stony Brook Office Park in Waltham. They sold the group to Wellsford/Whitehall Group LLC, in 1998 and over the next few years watched their old portfolio go from 98 percent occupancy down to 35 percent today, Saracen said.

Wellsford/Whitehall defaulted on the $64 million note and the loan's manager, GMAC, negotiated with Saracen and a new partner, Rockpoint Group LLC, to buy the property back. Rather than go through a messy foreclosure and market the property, GMAC will get a new owner that has extensive experience with it.

''There's nothing like being motivated and having your fate in your own hands," Saracen said.

Other recent transactions that did not involve foreclosure:

International Place Developer Donald J. Chiofaro's lender, Teachers Insurance and Annuity Association of America, sold his $650 million note -- which was not in default -- before Chiofaro could bring in new capital. Tishman Speyer Properties of New York bought the loan for the bargain of just under $600 million earlier this year and is trying to leverage control of the two towers. But Chiofaro is digging in his heels, filing for bankruptcy protection to prevent a takeover.

Developer Dean F. Stratouly last week regained control of his nearly empty Wayland Business Center, which only last year he handed back to lender Credit Suisse First Boston after Polaroid, a major tenant, filed for bankruptcy. Stratouly bought the property in 1997 and put $55 million into it but couldn't find enough tenants. The bank took back the keys and just sold it back to Stratouly for a fraction of the $50 million remaining loan, though he would not say how much he paid.

Prudential Investments is selling its $36 million note on Totten Pond Office Center in Waltham, owned by Crow Holdings, and expects to find a buyer within a month.

Some lending institutions may lose some money in deals like these, but they're finding it's better than foreclosing and being stuck indefinitely with empty buildings.

''The new strategy is you take your lumps all at once and be rid of the problem," said Fantini.

Real estate and financial specialists say their world has changed dramatically in just 10 years. So-called distressed properties -- like so many that sit on Route 128 and Interstate 495 -- are much more marketable. So are the loans supporting them.

The new landscape also includes the so-called securitized loan market. No longer does a bank hold a loan for 30 years, nor is a loan held even by one entity. Instead, loans are sold off like stocks or bonds to multiple investors.

Also, the Internet has provided an expanded marketplace and lubricated the previously cumbersome process of selling loans to the point where there is almost a perpetual auction of property -- with anyone around the globe who has a computer (and money, of course) able to bid.

Kingsley Greenland is chief executive and president of a company founded only four years ago -- not to sell land and buildings but rather the loans behind them. The Debt Exchange Inc., known as Debtx, sells mostly loans on commercial properties -- 2,000 last year in North America and Europe.

''The Internet was key," said Greenland. ''You could never reach a critical mass of buyers. You had a guy with a Rolodex with seven names in it. Now we have a vibrant marketplace."
