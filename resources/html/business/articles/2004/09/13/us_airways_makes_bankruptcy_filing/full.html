US Airways makes bankruptcy filing

Negotiations fail with pilots union

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  September 13, 2004

US Airways Group Inc., the nation's seventh-largest airline, filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection yesterday for the second time in two years, after last-ditch negotiations with pilots failed to win the airline the $295 million in pay and benefit cuts it had been seeking.

The filing came almost three years to the date after the terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, which plunged the domestic aviation industry into a tailspin from which it has yet to recover. Though passenger counts approach levels reached before Sept. 11, high jet-fuel prices and intense competition from low-cost carriers have crippled older carriers such as US Airways, Delta Air Lines Inc., and UAL Corp., parent company of United Airlines.

US Airways now must convince its creditors, passengers, and employees that it can survive another bankruptcy. Its chairman, David Bronner, warned several weeks ago that the carrier would probably end up closing down and selling off its assets if it sought bankruptcy protection a second time.

But US Airways' president and chief executive, Bruce Lakefield, subsequently backtracked from those comments, and yesterday he again sought to assure customers that the airline faced no immediate danger of shutting down.

"We have devoted the last six months to building and implementing a transformation plan that leverages our strengths and allows us to compete successfully," Lakefield said in a statement released shortly after the bankruptcy filing. "We have made the difficult but necessary decision to complete this process with the help of the court."

The airline said it plans to continue flying its normal schedule and paying wages and benefits during its reorganization. It also said it plans to ask the US Bankruptcy Court to allow it to honor its commitments to its frequent-flier program members and the holders of its Dividend Miles credit cards.

Yesterday some US Airways passengers at Logan International Airport in Boston said they had already taken steps to protect themselves in the event the carrier folds. US Airways is the third-largest airline at Logan, carrying nearly 16 percent of all passengers.

Thomas Friberg, a physician from Pittsburgh, said he took out an insurance policy on his nearly 500,000 US Airways frequent-flier miles last year, while the carrier was under its first court-supervised reorganization. The policy will reimburse him up to $7,500 in cash, or an equivalent number of tickets on another airline, if US Airways is liquidated and no other carrier honors its miles.

"I'm certainly concerned because I live in Pittsburgh, and I fly internationally a lot," Friberg said.

US Airways is the dominant carrier at Pittsburgh International Airport. But since emerging from its first bankruptcy filing in March 2003, it has cut costs and ended Pittsburgh's "hub" status, cutting more than 100 daily flights from its Pittsburgh schedule already.

The airline said it hopes that the Chapter 11 filing will give it the breathing room it needs to complete a costly restructuring at the same time that it climbs from under a mountain of debt. US Airways listed $8.8 billion in assets and $8.7 billion in liabilities in its filing in the US Bankruptcy Court in Alexandria, Va.

The first hearing on its reorganization was scheduled for 10:30 a.m. today.

The airline faced several financial hurdles this month that led to its bankruptcy filing. It owed a $110 million pension payment and faced the expiration of interim finance agreements with three aircraft makers -- General Electric, Bombardier, and Embraer --at the end of this month.

It also said yesterday that it faced potential violations of the terms of its $900 million, government-backed loan. It plans to use a portion of that cash for working capital while under Chapter 11. The airline still owes the federal government $718 million Yesterday's filing suspends those obligations until the court determines how much of the debt US Airways will repay and when.

At the same time, the airline is seeking $800 million annually in concessions from unions, but it has been unable to win over the pilots union, and other employee groups have said they would not negotiate concessions without a pilots' deal. A group of pilots from Pennsylvania, who control more than half their union's votes, refused last week to allow the full union to vote on a proposal from the airline. That called for 20 percent pay cuts and a 50 percent cut in retirement plan contributions from the airline.

The airline sent pilot representatives an amended proposal over the weekend, but no deal was reached. A spokesman for US Airways' pilots union said last week that many of the pilots were "dumbfounded" at the Pennsylvania pilots' actions and are nervous about what the bankruptcy filing will ultimately mean for them.

"Historically, bankruptcy judges have been very sympathetic to the needs of a company operating under bankruptcy," said Jack Stephan, a spokesman for the pilots union. "They won't take lightly the fact that all of labor here has had the opportunity to work things out on their own."

US Airways stock closed at $1.46 a share on Friday, down substantially from its 52-week high of $12.90, set in October.
