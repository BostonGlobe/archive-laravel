'Town center' planned at old air station

September 24, 2004

THE REGION

'Town center' planned at old air station

ADVERTISEMENT

LNR Property Corp. aims to build five neighborhoods connected by a ''town center" at the 1,400-acre former South Weymouth Naval Air Station. The 12-year, phased development (parts of the facility are in South Weymouth, Abington, and Rockland) would create 2,855 residential units and 1.3 million square feet of commercial space. Three-quarters of the site would be recreational and open space. The village center would be near public transit and would yield an estimated $11 million a year in taxes. A final plan will be presented early next year. LNR was a partner in the team that this week lost its bid to develop Fan Pier on the South Boston Waterfront. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

Banknorth urged to go on auction block

Private Capital Management LP, the largest shareholder of Banknorth Group Inc., wants the bank to seek a buyer at auction. In a Securities and Exchange Commission filing, the fund said it is concerned about Banknorth's plan to sell control to Toronto-Dominion Bank. Naples, Fla.-based Private Capital has an 8.8 percent stake in Banknorth, of Portland, Maine. Private Capital ''has significant concerns about the proposed transaction, including the up-front deal price to be paid to current shareholders," the filing said. Toronto-Dominion agreed last month to buy 51 percent of Banknorth for about $3.1 billion, its first foray into US consumer banking. (Bloomberg)

TJX Cos. makes its online debut

Framingham-based TJX Cos., which has over 1,750 clothing and household-goods stores, began selling products online for the first time, with websites for T.J. Maxx and HomeGoods. The sites, said spokeswoman Sherry Lang, ''offer what we sell in stores, and we'll ensure there's a rapid turnover of goods and products to give people a treasure-hunt experience." She was unable to provide details about the number of customers TJX expects to shop online or the sales outlook. (Bloomberg)

Sovereign's New England CEO to resign

John P. Hamill, chief executive of Sovereign Bank's New England division, will resign in January but continue as nonexecutive chairman of the board. He will be replaced by Joseph P. Campanelli, Sovereign's president and chief operating officer. Hamill, 64, was president of Fleet National Bank from 1992 to 2000; before that, he was Shawmut Bank's president. Sovereign said he will remain ''closely involved" in hiring, attracting new business, and acquisitions. (Sasha Talcott)

Five Star to buy housing company

Five Star Quality Care Inc. of Newton, which owns and operates housing developments for senior citizens, agreed to buy closely held LTA Holdings Inc. for $208 million to become less dependent on government-funded business. The company will then sell 35 of the 47 developments it acquires to Senior Housing Properties Trust for $165 million and lease them back, Five Star said. It plans to finance the rest with cash, long-term mortgage debt, and a lease. Five Star gets about 40 percent of its revenue from Medicaid and Medicare. The acquisition will boost the number of developments Five Star operates to 148 from about 100. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

Stewart's post-prison salary: $900,000

Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia renewed an employment contract for five years with its soon-to-be jailed founder, but she will not be paid during her five-month incarceration, according to a government filing. Stewart, who parlayed a catering business into a media and retail empire, reports to prison Oct. 8 for lying about a stock trade. When she gets out, she will earn $900,000 a year, not including bonuses of up to $1.35 million; be chauffeured to work; and get an annual $100,000 expense account and a new title: chief editorial and media director. Her old title was ''founding editorial director." She has resigned as chairwoman, chief executive, and chief creative officer. (Reuters)

Ex-official pleads guilty to misdemeanor

A former senior vice president of Qwest Communications International Inc., Thomas Hall, pleaded guilty to a misdemeanor count of falsifying documents. Prosecutors dropped three counts of wire fraud and one count of securities fraud. Hall faces up to a year in prison and a fine of up to $100,000. He was accused of participating in a scheme to inflate revenue by $34 million. A jury deadlocked on a broader set of fraud charges against him in April after a seven-week trial. Hall and three other former executives were charged with fraud and conspiracy. Two, John Walker and Bryan Treadway, were acquitted. Grant Graham pleaded guilty to being an accessory to wire fraud with reckless indifference. He has not been sentenced. The investigations ultimately led the company to erase $2.5 billion in revenue and prompted chief executive Joseph Nacchio to quit in 2002. (AP)

Lehman said ready to settle for $220m

Lehman Brothers Holdings Inc. is close to a deal to pay about $220 million to settle a class-action lawsuit alleging it and other big brokerage firms schemed with Enron Corp. executives to mislead shareholders, The Wall Street Journal said. Lehman would pay investors who had billions of dollars in losses as a result of Enron's collapse amid an accounting scandal in 2001, the Journal said, citing a person close to the matter. Lehman said ''there is no assurance that a settlement will be achieved on terms acceptable to the company." In July, Bank of America Corp. agreed to pay $69 million to settle similar allegations but denied wrongdoing. (Dow Jones/AP)

Onetime $20b firm on verge of closing

Commerce One Inc., an Internet software maker valued at $20 billion at the peak of dot-com mania, is poised to go out of business as a pauper. The San Francisco company delivered the news in a Securities and Exchange Commission filing that warned shareholders shouldn't expect to receive anything. No specific timetable was provided. Down to its last $700,000, the company said that expenses continue to outstrip revenue and that additional financing has not been found. ''We anticipate that we will eventually wind down our business and may file for bankruptcy," Commerce One said. The company did not immediately respond to requests for comment yesterday. Commerce One has lost $3.7 billion since its 1994 inception and eliminated 3,600 jobs in the past four years. Less than five years ago, Commerce One was lionized as a software pioneer that planned to create online exchanges to revolutionize buying and selling. Its stock, above $1,300 in early 2000, plunged 38 cents yesterday to 23 cents. (AP)

THE WORLD

R&D center planned in China

Network equipment maker Cisco Systems Inc. said it plans to spend $32 million to open a research and development center in Shanghai by April. The San Jose, Calif., company expects to hire about 100 people at the center. Cisco's plan is a response to the ''sustained growth" of network service providers in China, and to competition from China's own network-gear maker, Huawei Technologies Co. (AP)
