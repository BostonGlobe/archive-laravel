BUSINESS IN BRIEF

PolyMedica names Ryan president, CEO

September 28, 2004

PolyMedica Corp. named Patrick Ryan president and chief executive, replacing Samuel Shanaman, who remains chairman. PolyMedica said Ryan, 46, joined the company from Physician’s Dialysis Center of Hermitage Pa., a specialty healthcare company, where he served as chief executive and chairman. PolyMedica also appointed Ryan to its board. In addition, PolyMedica named the president of its Liberty Healthcare Group unit, Stephen Farrell, 39, to the additional post of chief operating officer at the parent company. Meanwhile, the provider of health care supplies to people with diabetes said it expects to take a fiscal second-quarter charge due to damage from Hurricane Jeanne at its facilities in Port St. Lucie, Fla. PolyMedica estimated charges at $5 million, or 12 cents a share, for the second quarter ending in September related to the hurricane damage. (Dow Jones)

Progress Software to buy Calif. company

Progress Software Corp. agreed to acquire Persistence Software Inc. for $5.70 a share, or about $16 million in cash. Upon the closing of the deal, Persistence, which provides distributed data access and caching software, will become part of ObjectStore, an operating company of Bedford-based Progress Software that provides products for real-time data management. The offer represents a premium of 52 percent, based on San Mateo, Calif.-based Persistence Software’s closing price Friday of $3.74. Progress Software said the acquisition is another step in accelerating the company’s real-time data services offering. Progress Software anticipates first-year revenue related to the acquisition to be between $8 million and $9 million. (Dow Jones)

Concord Communications options offer

Concord Communications Inc. is offering employees cash in exchange for their ‘‘underwater’’ stock options, according to a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission. Concord Communications, whose shares closed at $9.55, is giving employees a chance to cash in options that have strike prices of $25 or more. Some options have strike prices as high as $62, the filing said. The Marlborough developer of Internet-based performance analysis and reporting services is offering as much as $4.07 for an underwater option, depending on its strike price. Concord Communications is offering to buy options with strike prices above $60 for $2.76 each, while those with strike prices between $25 and $30 will fetch $4.07 each. The top six executives at Concord Communications hold about 53 percent of the roughly 970,000 options that are eligible to be swapped for cash, according to the SEC filing. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION
3d company testing drug-coated stents

Abbott Laboratories, a maker of diagnostic tests and cardiovascular drugs, said will begin a US study this year pitting its drug-coated heart stent against Boston Scientific Corp.’s Taxus device. Abbott plans to test the stent, which keeps heart arteries open after they’ve been cleared, in 1,670 patients at more than 70 locations in North America, the company said. Details of the trial, to be called ZoMaxx II, were outlined at the Transcatheter Cardiovascular Therapeutics meeting in Washington, D.C. The start of the trial follows similar testing against Taxus that Abbott began this month in 400 patients outside the United States. The company is racing Guidant Corp. and Medtronic Inc. to be third to the market for drug-coated heart stents, which analysts estimate will have sales of $5 billion next year. Only Johnson & Johnson and Boston Scientific sell drug-coated versions. (Bloomberg)

Martha Stewart assigned inmate number

Martha Stewart still does not know where she will do her prison time for lying about a stock sale, but she’s already been designated federal inmate No. 55170-054. The celebrity homemaker, who must begin serving her five-month prison sentence by Oct. 8, was assigned the number by federal marshals. The number now appears under Stewart’s name on the US Bureau of Prisons website. She is also listed as ‘‘in transit,’’ but the designation simply means she has a reporting date, prisons spokesman Dan Dunne said. She has asked to be assigned to the minimum-security federal prison in Danbury, Conn., to make it easier for her 90-year-old mother to visit. (AP)

Bank names chief financial officer

Citigroup Inc. named Sallie Krawcheck chief financial officer, extending her reach across the world’s largest financial services company and bolstering her image as Wall Street’s most powerful woman. Krawcheck, 39, widely credited with turning around Citigroup’s Smith Barney unit, will become the bank’s CFO and head of strategy effective Nov. 5. She replaces Todd Thomson, who will take her job as head of Smith Barney, the company’s global private client wealth management and equity research unit. The swap comes amid a series of missteps by Citigroup international units, leading regulators to shut down the bank’s Japanese private banking operation. The new post marks the latest step in Krawcheck’s rapid rise on Citigroup’s corporate ladder. She was appointed head of Citigroup’s retail brokerage operations two years ago. (Reuters)

Software company curbs ex-CEO’s perks

Computer Associates International Inc. said it has revoked home security and office support benefits to former chief executive Sanjay Kumar, who was indicted last week on charges related to a $2 billion accounting scandal at the company. The software maker, which last week agreed to pay $225 million to shareholders to avoid federal criminal charges, said in July it would provide Kumar with phone and network support and home security services at an annual cost of $9,000, off-premises office space, one assistant, and medical insurance. CA revoked all those benefits, except for 20 years of medical insurance for Kumar and his family subject to his death or his obtaining insurance from a new employer, according to a letter sent to Kumar’s attorney last week. The letter did not say if Kumar’s sizable past compensation during restated periods will be recalled. (Reuters)

Bank of America subsidiary hires four

Banc of America Securities hired four technology investment bankers as part of chairman Carter McClelland’s plan to win more underwriting assignments and mergers advisory business by increasing staff. Stephan Month, 45, Sean Rogers, 39, and Georg Schloendorff, 40, joined from Citigroup Inc., the bank said, while Jeffrey Liu, 38, formerly worked at Deutsche Bank Alex. Brown. The Bank of America unit has boosted its roster of bankers by 20 percent to about 600 since early last year. McClelland began hiring more than two years ago when Wall Street rivals such as Merrill Lynch were cutting staff as markets declined. His investment may now be paying off — the firm has jumped to third from sixth among underwriters of both US equity-linked securities and high-yield bonds this year from last year, according to data compiled by Bloomberg. (Bloomberg)

SEC might penalize Wachovia unit

Wachovia Corp., the fifth-largest US bank, said the Securities and Exchange Commission may recommend enforcement action against its Wachovia Securities brokerage unit for alleged improper mutual fund trading. The SEC notified Wachovia on Sept. 17 of its decision, the bank said in a filing with the SEC. The case relates to an arrangement between an unnamed ex-Evergreen Investment Management Co. officer and an individual broker who made trades on behalf of a client in the Evergreen Mid Cap Growth fund between December 2000 and April 2003 in excess of limits set in the fund prospectus. (Bloomberg)
