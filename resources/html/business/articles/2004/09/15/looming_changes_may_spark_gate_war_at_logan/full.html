Looming changes may spark gate war at Logan

Rivals eye space amid upheaval in industry, planned Delta move

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  September 15, 2004

The already cutthroat competition for passengers and market share at Logan International Airport is likely to get even more heated over the next several months.

ADVERTISEMENT

Three of the top airlines at Logan are grappling with financial problems, and up to 19 gates at the crowded airport will become available when struggling Delta Air Lines moves into its new terminal next spring. The combination of weakened competition and new room to expand will likely embolden the slew of low-cost airlines that have been challenging older carriers for market share in recent years, specialists said.

"JetBlue and AirTran have new airplanes coming on, and they will obviously be looking for places to use them," said Dan Kasper, managing director and head of the aviation practice at LECG LLC, a Cambridge consulting firm.

Logan has always been a crowded airport where gates -- the places where passengers wait to board their planes and where jets park while passengers board and deplane -- are a valuable commodity. In loftier times, established carriers like American Airlines, United Airlines, and Delta controlled most of Logan's gates, effectively keeping low-cost competitors out.

In 2001, the Massachusetts Port Authority rebuffed JetBlue Airways' efforts to start service at Logan, telling JetBlue the airport had no room for it. But since the terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, forced some airlines to cut back service, carriers like JetBlue and Independence Air found room to sneak in.

Still, most low-cost airlines at Logan have little, if any, room to grow. AirTran flies out of one gate in Terminal D, a dank space near Delta's baggage claim area. Most of JetBlue's flights are domestic, but its two gates are in Terminal E, the airport's international departures area. Low-cost airlines ATA and America West Airlines share a gate in Terminal B and Independence Air leases one from US Airways.

Delta has 12 gates at Logan, 10 in Terminal C, and two in Terminal B. It plans to occupy 18 gates at its new terminal, a spokeswoman said. That would leave seven slots for small, commuter planes in the new terminal, and 12 other gates unaccounted for at Logan six months from now.

For its part, Massport officials are mum about which carriers might take up Delta's old space or any leftover space in the new terminal.

"Negotiations are ongoing to determine what airlines will go into Terminal C," after Delta moves, said Tom Kinton, director of aviation at Massport. "The same type of talks are ongoing to determine what airlines other than Delta will go into Terminal A when it officially opens."

But the low-cost airlines are hungry for expansion space, and several will have brand new planes next year to help with any expansions they may be planning.

JetBlue officials said they hope to expand from two gates to three in Terminal C, and the airline will get the first of 200 new 100-seat jets next year.

Airline spokesman Todd Burke wouldn't comment on specific expansion plans, but said "obviously, we can operate quite a few flights a day out of one gate."

AirTran will get 22 new Boeing 737 jets between now and the end of next year.

Kevin Healy, AirTran's vice president of planning and sales, said where the carrier expands its service and by how much depends on what happens to its competitors between now and the new terminal's opening. Two carriers, US Airways and United, are currently in bankruptcy and either may have been liquidated or emerged as a stronger airline by then. And Delta is currently trying to avoid bankruptcy by cutting costs.

Any number of other airlines could expand or decrease their Boston service, Healy said.

AirTran's plans "depend a lot upon what else happens there. There's so many variations of how things may occur," he said.

Specialists who watch the Boston market, though, said AirTran would likely start beefing up its service to Philadelphia if US Airways shuts down. AirTran currently has four daily flights from Logan.

In addition to that, said Terry Trippler, a consumer advocate for aviation website Sidestep.com, other low-cost carriers such as Frontier Airlines are likely candidates for new service at Logan.

Frontier already operates in Pittsburgh, where US Airways has been pulling back, and could use new gates at Boston to feed connecting flights to the West Coast or cities like St. Louis, Kansas City or Denver, Trippler said.

"Frontier might be the airline that looks best to locate in Boston that's not already there," he said.

Frontier used to fly from Boston to Denver, but pulled out of Logan in 2002. Spokesman Joe Hodas said the airline will get 23 new jets by 2008, but won't make any new service decisions until at least next March.

Still "Boston continues to be one of the markets that we evaluate on a regular basis," he said, adding that Detroit and Boston are the only among the top 25 busiest cities for air travel nationally that Frontier doesn't currently serve.
