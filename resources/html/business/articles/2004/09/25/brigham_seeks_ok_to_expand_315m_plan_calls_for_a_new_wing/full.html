Brigham seeks OK to expand

$315m plan calls for a new wing

By Liz Kowalczyk, Globe Staff  |  September 25, 2004

Brigham and Women's Hospital, one of the region's medical powerhouses and one of its most crowded, is proposing its largest expansion in two decades, including a new 13-floor wing connected to the main campus by tunnels and a bridge.

The Brigham submitted its plan this summer to the state Department of Public Health, which along with hospital boards and the city must approve the project, and met with community groups as recently as last weekend to address neighbors' concerns about traffic and congestion. The new wing, which would house 16 operating rooms, 30 new patient beds, 120 relocated beds, and outpatient services, would be located at 70 Francis St., across the street from the hospital's main entrance at 75 Francis St.

"We're very serious about this, and we're working very hard, in hopes that we can get the external and internal approvals we need," said Dr. Gary Gottlieb, Brigham's chief executive.

Brigham executives said patients and staff don't have enough space in the existing hospital; patient rooms are smaller than state guidelines, and most are double-occupancy, lack privacy, and cannot accommodate the latest technology. All new rooms would be private. Only six of the hospital's 40 operating rooms are large enough for the latest high-tech equipment such as robots that assist in heart surgery, and surgeons and patients must queue up for those rooms. "It's like a family that outgrew its house," said Dr. Michael Zinner, head of surgery.

The Brigham project, which executives estimate would cost $315 million, is part of the biggest building boom to hit the Longwood Medical and Academic Area in more than a decade. Like the Brigham, many hospitals in the compact neighborhood are overflowing with patients, clinical staff, and researchers, and don't have room to squeeze in more services, beds, and labs.

Last fall, Children's Hospital opened a new 12-story research facility and now is building an 11-story intensive care and surgery wing to be completed next summer; Children's and Joslin Diabetes Center are planning another nine-story research building and a 150-unit residential complex; Harvard Medical School opened a new research facility last fall with labs for 800 scientists; and Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center is selling land to a developer that will build two additional research centers. The area's colleges also are expanding.

The Longwood area, which is sandwiched between the town of Brookline to the west and Boston's Fenway and Mission Hill neighborhoods to the north and east, already contains 14.8 million square feet of building space, employs 37,000 people, and accommodates more than 2 million patients annually. Potential development over the next decade could increase space by 30 percent, according to the Medical Academic and Scientific Community Organization, a coalition of colleges and hospitals.

"This entire area, if you look up Huntington Avenue, the colleges, the dormitories, the hospitals, they're all building," said Robert Healy, president of the board of Roxbury Tenants of Harvard Association, a nonprofit group founded by residents in the late 1960s to protect their interests. "At the same time, we want to keep our residents here and keep them happy. We don't want to get swallowed up by all the buildings."

Healy complimented the Brigham's approach to the project, saying hospital executives have met with neighbors regularly about their plans. The Boston Redevelopment Authority, which must approve the project, also is concerned about development in the area and is requiring hospitals that expand to provide either more housing or green space. The Brigham plans to hide parking on the corner of Francis Street and Huntington Avenue underground and create an "elegant park," said Mark Maloney, BRA director.

"We have been concerned for a long while about rapid expansion of the Longwood Medical Area," he said. "We see the impact on traffic and quality of life in the community. Because of that, we're placing strict guidelines on expansion."

Gottlieb said that the hospital has tried to respond to the concerns of its neighbors. To mitigate objections to the new wing's height, three of the 13 stories would be located underground. The site is now a vacant lot, used for parking and some temporary offices in trailers. Healy said that the hospital is working on a plan to build an entrance for underground parking near the edge of the hospital's property at Huntington Avenue, so additional cars will not have to travel down Francis Street. He said residents are hoping to get the Brigham to agree to hire a specific percentage of residents to staff the new wing from the surrounding Mission Hill neighborhood.

The Brigham board and the board of Partners Health Care, the hospital's parent organization, also must approve the project. He said it may not go forward if the hospital has to make significant changes to the proposal.

Gottlieb said the hospital must expand in order to accommodate the latest technology to treat sicker patients. At the same time, the number of patients has grown. Surgeons logged 13.5 million minutes of surgery last year, nearly double the number in 2001, while the number of diagnostic radiology procedures -- 3.4 million last year -- grew nearly 40 percent.

Zinner said the hospital has one operating room now that allows surgeons to do high-tech imaging while they operate. In the new wing, he hopes to have several of these rooms, which, for example, allow neurosurgeons to see very clearly the edges of brain tumors as they operate.

The hospital has more than 690 beds. As 120 medical/surgical and intensive care unit beds are moved to the new building, the remainder of rooms and beds in the existing hospital would be renovated. Executives hope to complete the entire project by 2008.
