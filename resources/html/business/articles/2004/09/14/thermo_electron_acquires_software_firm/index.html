Thermo Electron acquires software firm

September 14, 2004

Thermo Electron Corp., the biggest maker of analytical instruments for medical labs, has bought laboratory software maker InnaPhase Corp. for about $65 million. InnaPhase, of Philadelphia, has about 100 employees and expects revenue of $26 million this year, Waltham-based Thermo Electron said. Thermo had about 10,800 employees and $2.1 billion in sales in 2003. It sold an optics unit to semiconductor equipment company Newport Corp. in June and bought U.S. Counseling Services Inc. in April to help healthcare and pharmaceutical companies manage equipment. Thermo Electron, the biggest provider of laboratory information management systems, will get closely held InnaPhase's Watson product, used by biotechnology companies. (Bloomberg)

Bank of America rebrands Fleet website

Bank of America Corp. added its own logo and changed the colors of FleetBoston Financial Corp.'s banking website, HomeLink, to red, white, and blue over the weekend, the most visible change yet that New England's Fleet customers have seen of the banks' $48 billion merger. The website, renamed Bank of America HomeLink, retains its format, and customers keep the same ID numbers and PINs. A spokeswoman said the company had no reports of problems during the switch. Customers eventually will be moved to Bank of America's website. (Sasha Talcott)

Grid operator, utilities sign pact

Holyoke-based ISO New England Inc., which oversees a $4.5 billion-a-year power market, has reached an agreement with its members that's expected to clear the way for federal approval of a regional power market this year. The pact would give ISO New England full authority to manage a six-state regional transmission grid. It currently runs the grid under a service agreement with utilities, generators, power marketers, and large electric customers. As a federally approved grid operator, ISO New England will be able to require generators and transmission owners to take actions in a crisis or take steps to improve long-term reliability. Regulators have encouraged utilities to form regional organizations to promote competition and enhance reliability. (Bloomberg)

Pimco market-timing suit settled

Three companies will pay $50 million to settle charges they defrauded investors in Pimco Funds by not disclosing improper market-timing trades. The Securities and Exchange Commission will drop a suit filed in May against PA Fund Management LLC, PEA Capital LLC, and PA Distributors LLC. The firms consented to cease-and-desist orders and censures, and promised to undertake mutual fund governance reforms. The settlement is to be distributed to shareholders whose funds were affected. The SEC suit followed a suit by state regulators settled in June for $18 million. The suits alleged investors were cheated when hedge fund operator Canary Capital Partners LLC was allowed to market-time trades worth over $4 billion. Canary settled for $40 million. (AP)

N.C. bank aims to cut up to $1b in costs

Wachovia Corp.'s chief executive, Kennedy Thompson, said a plan to trim $600 million to $1 billion in expenses at the fifth-biggest US bank will be in place by year's end. He called it ''very doable for us in the next three years" and said the Charlotte, N.C., company is ''in the middle of formulating all our plans, and we are taking that to the board in the fourth quarter." Thompson said the cuts will be in addition to the $255 million in expenses Wachovia will eliminate after it completes its $14.3 billion takeover of SouthTrust Corp. in the fourth quarter, and savings from its purchase of Prudential Financial Inc.'s brokerage. (Bloomberg)

FDA approves first implantable lens

The first implantable lens for nearsightedness was approved yesterday -- an alternative to glasses, contact lenses, or Lasik surgery. Already in use in Europe, the lens is manufactured by Ophtec USA Inc., of Boca Raton, Fla. An implant will cost $3,000 to $4,000 per eye, and won't usually be covered by insurance. After three years, 92 percent of 662 patients who had implants had 20/40 or better vision, and 44 percent had 20/20 or better, the FDA said, citing Ophtec research. The implant was tested in people with moderate to severe nearsightedness. (AP)

Federated ends its regional store names

Federated Department Stores Inc. will change the names of all 184 of its regional department stores to Macy's, dropping the Rich's, Burdines, Lazarus, Bon-Marche, and Goldsmith's brands. Cincinnati-based Federated will then operate stores exclusively under the Macy's and Bloomingdale's names. The goal is to expand Macy's throughout the United States, enabling Federated to coordinate marketing and purchasing. Federated has 239 Macy's stores, excluding those in the regional chains, and 36 Bloomingdale's locations. (Bloomberg)

US, German drug makers team up

Schering-Plough Corp. of New Jersey and German chemical and drug maker Bayer AG have formed an alliance under which Schering-Plough will take over US marketing of Bayer's primary care medicines, and Bayer HealthCare will build up a new US-based cancer medicine unit and refocus its US organization on high-profit specialty and biotech products through a business to be based in West Haven, Conn. The partnership could give Bayer a bigger presence in the world's biggest drug market, while offering Schering-Plough badly needed new products and a potentially key marketing partner in Europe and Japan. Schering will take over US marketing of Cipro and Avelox, both broad-spectrum antibiotics, plus the blood pressure drug Adalat and the impotence treatment Levitra. Bayer will promote Schering-Plough cancer drugs in Europe. Schering has lost money as revenue plunged due to competition or loss of patent protection. (AP)

Quattrone raps judge's comments

Frank Quattrone, who got 18 months in prison for obstructing justice, accused the judge of ''callousness" for revealing his 15-year-old daughter's emotional disorder and asked a federal appeals court in New York to seal letters from family and friends seeking leniency. US District Judge Richard Owen released the letters Thursday, a day after he sentenced the former chief technology banker for Credit Suisse First Boston, convicted of obstructing probes into how CSFB allocated shares in initial public stock offerings. His sentence was longer than the 10 to 16 months in federal guidelines; Owen said the banker lied at trial. (Bloomberg)
