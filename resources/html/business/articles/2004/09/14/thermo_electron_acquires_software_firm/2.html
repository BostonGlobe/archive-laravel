FDA approves first implantable lens

The first implantable lens for nearsightedness was approved yesterday -- an alternative to glasses, contact lenses, or Lasik surgery. Already in use in Europe, the lens is manufactured by Ophtec USA Inc., of Boca Raton, Fla. An implant will cost $3,000 to $4,000 per eye, and won't usually be covered by insurance. After three years, 92 percent of 662 patients who had implants had 20/40 or better vision, and 44 percent had 20/20 or better, the FDA said, citing Ophtec research. The implant was tested in people with moderate to severe nearsightedness. (AP)

Federated ends its regional store names

Federated Department Stores Inc. will change the names of all 184 of its regional department stores to Macy's, dropping the Rich's, Burdines, Lazarus, Bon-Marche, and Goldsmith's brands. Cincinnati-based Federated will then operate stores exclusively under the Macy's and Bloomingdale's names. The goal is to expand Macy's throughout the United States, enabling Federated to coordinate marketing and purchasing. Federated has 239 Macy's stores, excluding those in the regional chains, and 36 Bloomingdale's locations. (Bloomberg)

US, German drug makers team up

Schering-Plough Corp. of New Jersey and German chemical and drug maker Bayer AG have formed an alliance under which Schering-Plough will take over US marketing of Bayer's primary care medicines, and Bayer HealthCare will build up a new US-based cancer medicine unit and refocus its US organization on high-profit specialty and biotech products through a business to be based in West Haven, Conn. The partnership could give Bayer a bigger presence in the world's biggest drug market, while offering Schering-Plough badly needed new products and a potentially key marketing partner in Europe and Japan. Schering will take over US marketing of Cipro and Avelox, both broad-spectrum antibiotics, plus the blood pressure drug Adalat and the impotence treatment Levitra. Bayer will promote Schering-Plough cancer drugs in Europe. Schering has lost money as revenue plunged due to competition or loss of patent protection. (AP)

Quattrone raps judge's comments

Frank Quattrone, who got 18 months in prison for obstructing justice, accused the judge of ''callousness" for revealing his 15-year-old daughter's emotional disorder and asked a federal appeals court in New York to seal letters from family and friends seeking leniency. US District Judge Richard Owen released the letters Thursday, a day after he sentenced the former chief technology banker for Credit Suisse First Boston, convicted of obstructing probes into how CSFB allocated shares in initial public stock offerings. His sentence was longer than the 10 to 16 months in federal guidelines; Owen said the banker lied at trial. (Bloomberg)
