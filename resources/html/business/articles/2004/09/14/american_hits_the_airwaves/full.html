American hits the airwaves

Revitalized carrier launches ads to grab travelers as rivals struggle

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  September 14, 2004

With its major competitors mired in a struggle for financial survival, American Airlines yesterday kicked off a multimillion-dollar advertising campaign aimed at grabbing market share and capitalizing on its own turnaround.

''It's further evidence that we're moving forward and that we're not stuck in all our industry's problems," said Steve Schlachter, American's director of worldwide advertising.

The campaign's launch comes a day after US Airways Group Inc., the seventh-largest airline in the country, made its second Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing in two years. American, the world's largest carrier, itself flirted with bankruptcy last spring, but escaped it after dramatic negotiations with employees and creditors and a sweeping restructuring.

''We think it's never been more timely to say that we have a connection" with passengers, Schlachter said.

American won't confirm exactly how much it is spending on its new campaign. Published reports estimate that the carrier will spend as much as 75 percent of its estimated $60 million advertising budget over the next year on the campaign.

Spending that much is a big move for a carrier that just a year ago was talking about a potential Chapter 11 filing of its own.

The airline industry was thrown into financial disarray by the terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, and by March 2003 American was negotiating with its employees over $1.8 billion in annual labor cost cuts it said it needed to survive. If it hadn't won the concessions it wanted by last March 31, American would file for bankruptcy, the company had warned.

Instead, through tense, around-the-clock negotiations, the company was able to avoid Chapter 11.

''Certainly, by the summer we were operating as a leaner airline, and that allowed us to avoid bankruptcy," said Dan Garton, American's executive vice president of marketing.

Still, while the airline is in a good position compared to other so-called legacy airlines like United Airlines and US Airways, they all face stiff challenges from low-cost airlines like JetBlue Airways and AirTran Airways.

About half of American's last-minute agreements with its employees were in pay cuts, with the rest coming through benefit cuts and increased productivity from workers.

The airline cut its workforce by 15,200 people between the end of 2002 and the end of last year. American's creditors agreed to restructure its debt, giving the carrier another $200 million in annual savings.

The airline cut another $2 billion a year in expenses by trimming management staff, and by shifting much of its reservations and ticket sales away from agents and toward self-serve kiosks and its website.

It ''de-peaked" flights at its hubs, meaning it spread its flights out more evenly throughout the day as opposed to packing departures in tightly at two or three daily ''peak hours." That can save an airline millions because it doesn't have to spend money to bring on extra workers.

The airline also reduced the number of different types of planes it flies, from 30 before its restructuring to its current six, a streamlining that trimmed employee-training and aircraft-maintenance costs.

''It was difficult from a financial standpoint and difficult from an emotional standpoint," American's Garton said. ''But when we look at where Delta and US Airways and United are today, but we're glad that we're a year and a half past that."

Some employees, though, are still bitter about the painstaking cuts they had to endure to keep the airline afloat.

''I think we're far better off solvent than in bankruptcy, but some people are still very angry about what took place," said Denis Breslin, a Los Angeles-based American pilot and national communications chairman for the Allied Pilots Association, American's pilots union.

Although American has changed, the industry looks much the same then as it does now. Both US Airways and UAL Corp., the parent of United Airlines, were under bankruptcy protection in March 2002. United has never emerged.

Delta Air Lines said last week that it will cut 6,000 to 7,000 jobs over the next 18 months under a plan to reduce costs by $5 billion a year before 2006. It also warned that it could face a bankruptcy filling soon.

With its competitors still grappling with financial problems and attempting to introduce cost-cutting plans similar to American's, the timing of the marketing campaign could not have been better said Dan Kasper, director of the aviation practice at LECG LLC, a Cambridge consulting firm.

''Three of your significant competitors are in the news and not in a way that's going to convince people to fly them," Kasper said. ''American's made a lot of changes. They think they've improved the product and it's not a bad time to let people know that they're still there."

With the tagline ''We know why you fly," American's new advertising makes emotional and practical appeals to the reasons why business and leisure travelers take to the skies. It is also seeking to grab market share at some airports where troubled rivals have a major presence.

Since May American has increased flights from Logan to Dallas, Seattle, San Jose, Calif., and Santo Domingo, Dominican Republic, and started service between Boston and Manchester, England. American is the biggest carrier at Logan.

American also plans to beef up service 10 percent from Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport by next summer, while Delta is slashing most of its service there.

American's new commercials will run in English on local TV in New York, Dallas, Miami, and Chicago, and on seven cable networks. Print ads in English will run in 25 national magazines, while banner ads will appear on a host of popular news and travel websites, including Forbes.com and Citysearch.com. Spanish-language commercials and newspaper ads will run in Boston, New York, Chicago, Miami, and Washington, D.C.

Despite the new campaign, though, Kasper said American isn't out of the woods yet. The airline still needs to trim its operating expenses more to make it competitive with the wave of low-cost airlines like AirTran, JetBlue, and Southwest Airlines, he said.

''If the bear is Southwest, I don't think American can outrun the bear," Kasper said. ''But it can outrun the other legacy carriers, so it will live to fight another day."
