Mutual fund votes show limits to change

By Beth Healy, Globe Staff  |  September 5, 2004

When corporate America came looking for raises last year, Boston's largest mutual funds routinely turned them down, voting in hundreds of cases against lucrative stock and options packages for senior executives, according to proxy-vote data released last week for the first time.

But the revolt stopped there. Across all issues, the mutual funds voted with management 86 percent of the time, according to a Boston Globe analysis of 32,000 votes cast by 15 major funds at three firms. The five biggest funds at Fidelity Investments, Putnam Investments, and MFS Investment Management, which manage a combined $268 billion in stocks, voted overwhelmingly against proposals by shareholders to further rein in executive pay, as well as measures related to employee benefits, social issues, and the environment.

"It's really not in their DNA to vote against management on these things," said Peter D. Kinder, president of KLD Research & Analytics Inc., a Boston firm that provides research for socially oriented investment funds. "It was very interesting that they were willing to go as far as they did."

Proxies are documents that publicly traded companies send to their stockholders every year ahead of an annual shareholders' meeting. Many individual investors toss aside these papers, full of dense, tiny print and legal jargon. But proxies provide a brief, democratic opportunity for investors to weigh in on such matters as executive pay plans, boards of directors, mergers, and pension plans. The nation's mutual funds vote 22 percent of all the proxies each year. But until now, mutual fund shareholders had no idea how the funds that manage their money were casting those votes.

Some powerful industry players, such as Fidelity and Vanguard Group, staunchly opposed the vote disclosure, complaining that it would be costly and a distraction from the business of making money for investors. But the Securities and Exchange Commission decided funds had a responsibility to let customers see the votes, after the sting of corporate frauds led by Enron Corp. The regulator ordered funds to start reporting, every Aug. 31, how they voted on the stocks they held in the preceding year.

The new data reveal that fund groups took a dim view of any requests by company management that the funds believed could hurt the stock. For example, Fidelity, Putnam, and MFS all rejected the 2004 stock incentive plan at Hewlett-Packard Co. -- a plan that would make 180 million shares, or 6 percent of the computer company's stock outstanding, available to be issued for executive incentives.

Hewlett-Packard argued that it needed to be able to offer competitive pay to attract talent. The fund groups opposed the plan because big new releases of stock tend to dilute the value of the shares.

Nonetheless, management won, with a 69 percent approval. But the funds sent a strong message with their votes, which in the past would have been anonymous. For many years, large corporations have been accustomed to rubber-stamp approvals of such pay deals.

All three mutual fund firms said they have long opposed excessive compensation plans. But there is speculation among some investors that funds voted more aggressively in the latest proxy season, knowing that their votes would be public for the first time.

"We have no standard by which to judge this. We don't know what they did last year," Kinder said. He is encouraged that fund groups voted against management at all, but noted, "They've got a long way to go."

MFS, the third-biggest fund based in Boston and number 10 in the country, which manages $74 billion of mutual funds, voted with management 92 percent of the time last year, much more than the other two firms in the Globe's analysis. Fidelity, the nation's largest fund group, with $630 billion in stock and bond funds, voted with management 84 percent of the time; Putnam, with $113 billion in funds, said yes to management 81 percent of the time.

In published proxy-voting guidelines, Fidelity said it votes with management on routine matters, such as reappointing auditors, and against any shareholder proposals that won't help the stock's return or the company's profits.

"We are, at our core, active portfolio managers, who invest in companies in which we view the business prospects as highly attractive," said Fidelity spokesman Vincent Loporchio. "So we would expect to vote in favor of management's proposals most of the time. When we believe a company is not being well-run, we have the option of selling our shares."

There are exceptions, however. When Fidelity voted on incentive plans, and stock and option plans last year, it voted "no" half the time, Loporchio said. Generally, Fidelity is against big exit packages for executives who sell their companies, as well as "poison pills," which are measures designed to ward off a takeover. Acquisitions often boost a stock.

Fidelity also said it will generally vote for a shareholder proposal to require directors to be elected each year, rather than every three years, or staggered election periods that make it hard to unseat many directors at once.

Putnam has racked up many of its "No" votes against the election of corporate directors and full boards. The company said it made the "independence" of directors the center of its voting policy last year. An independent director does not work for the company or make any money from it. If a board meets the independence standard, Putnam said, it's likely to go along with management on other issues.

But, said Deborah F. Kuenstner, chief investment officer of the value fund group at Putnam and chairman of the firm's proxy committee, "We have turned down a lot of votes on options plans and incentive compensation plans" when such plans appeared to be dipping too deeply into the shareholders' pockets. At those companies, Putnam tended to punish certain directors, or the entire board, for allowing the compensation, she said.

Putnam, like Fidelity, MFS, and most mainstream fund groups, typically rejects shareholder proposals on social or political issues, saying those are business decisions, not board-level matters. Putnam, in its guidelines, said such proposals often are not in the best economic interests of the company.

MFS executives declined to comment on their high level of voting with management. But in its voting guidelines, the firm states that it is likely to vote mainly against measures that could hurt the stock price, including excessive pay programs.

On one hot-button issue last year, the Boston mutual funds were consistently on management's side. They voted against treating stock options as an expense in virtually every case, which sometimes put them on the losing side of the vote. At Hewlett-Packard, for example, 57 percent of shareholders voted in favor of expensing, against management's advice. In a regulatory filing, the company said the vote was nonbinding, but the board would "consider the recommendation."

None of the three local fund firms addresses option expensing in its posted proxy-voting guidelines.
