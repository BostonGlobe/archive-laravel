It’s so not hip to be Union Square

September 5, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Local politicians have long promised to revitalize the district. But pub owners say for now, it needs resuscitation.

ADVERTISEMENT

Last year, Kelly ran for Ward 3 alderman, focusing his platform on bringing private investment to the square, and pushing City Hall to improve landscaping, sidewalks, and benches. He lost to Thomas F. Taylor, who has held the post since 1986 and is opposed to issuing 2 a.m. liquor licenses.

A study commissioned last year by Dorothy Kelly Gay, who was mayor at the time, blamed heavy traffic and limited parking for the square's malaise. But Kelly Gay lost reelection, and the issue has been sidelined while Mayor Joseph A. Curtatone focuses on redeveloping Assembly Square.

The plantings Kelly Gay added last year to the square's traffic island have withered and died.

''It hasn't been a concentrated effort," Taylor said. ''I'm not sure some businesses are going to survive."

Taylor met last week with Curtatone, and he said he will push plans to reduce congestion and increase parking.

He said that he has been discouraged by progress so far, after failing to persuade Kelly Gay to convert the former Bow Street police station to office space instead of condominiums. ''It's very frustrating," he said.

Curtatone, who lives in nearby Prospect Hill, says former mayors have commissioned endless studies to improve Union Square, one of only two central business districts in Somerville.

He has pledged to act quickly to encourage investment, by simplifying city permits and redeveloping city-owned land, perhaps including the Somerville Community Access Television building, a central structure opposite The Independent.

''There was never any action or strategy put in place," he said. ''We want to make Union Square a destination, not just a place to drive through to get to Boston." Still, Curtatone will first publish yet another document on Union Square, an ''action plan" his staff is already researching.

The afternoon lunch crowd at the square's restaurants, meanwhile, remains nearly nonexistent. Tir Na Nog, for example, fills perhaps five tables in an afternoon.

Now state legislators, including Senator Jarrett T. Barrios, are promising a Green Line MBTA stop in Union Square, saying it would bring about a second coming of Davis Square. But all agree it is at least 10 years off, too late for eat, which was shuttered while straining to hear that rumbling train.

''We've been here eight years; they've been talking about the T for eight years," O'Toole said. ''I'll believe it when I pay my $1.25."
