BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Firm to supply 'dirty bomb' detectors

September 17, 2004

THE REGION

Firm to supply 'dirty bomb' detectors

American Science & Engineering Inc. received a $23.2 million order from the government for a mobile screening system that can detect nuclear ''dirty" bombs in places like vehicles. The Billerica company said it's the largest order it has received for its 24 Z Backscatter Van X-ray screening system. The government will use the mobile ZBV system, which is built into a van, for counterterrorism applications. With this order, 57 Backscatter Vans have been sold. The order includes installation, operator training, and a one-year warranty. (Dow Jones)

At Stake sold to security software maker

Symantec Corp., the world's largest maker of computer security software, is expanding is acquiring privately held At Stake Inc., of Cambridge. Financial details were not released, but the deal should be final next month. At Stake was founded in 2000 by former members of Lopht Heavy Industries, a group of Boston hackers who publicized security breakdowns at major companies. Some of the network-cracking tools Lopht developed are used by At Stake to help network administrators shore up their defenses. (Hiawatha Bray)

Bank plans $3m in grants, sponsorships

Bank of America Corp. plans about $3 million in grants and sponsorships in the Boston area this year and will be active in the community as it merges with FleetBoston Financial Corp., its executives told the Boston City Council. The bank has faced criticism for moving two business lines out of Boston and cutting 800 positions across the Northeast, while adding back about 600 jobs with benefits, mostly for part-timers. Councilors held a hearing to assess the moves; one said the bank's response ''alleviated a lot of our fears." The bank's Northeast president, Anne Finucane, called Boston a ''hugely important" market. (Sasha Talcott)

Progress Software's profit up 16 percent

Progress Software Corp. said its profit jumped 16 percent in the latest quarter, ended Aug. 31, as it continued to see increasing demand for real-time data, connectivity, and integration. The Bedford company supplies application servers, databases, and other products. Progress reported net income of $8.5 million, or 22 cents a share, up from the prior year's earnings of $7.3 million, or 19 cents. The latest results include after-tax charges for amortization of acquired intangibles of $1.2 million. Excluding the charges, per-share earnings increased to 25 cents from 20 cents. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call had forecast, on average, earnings of 23 cents a share, excluding items. Third-quarter revenue rose 15 percent to $89.3 million. (Dow Jones/AP)

MKS Instruments lowers sales forecast

MKS Instruments Inc., of Wilmington, expects lower third-quarter sales than forecast, due to lower-than-anticipated orders. The chip equipment supplier now expects sales to range from $135 million to $140 million, 8 to 11 percent below second-quarter sales of $151.6 million. Previously, the company had projected sequentially flat revenue. In the year-ago third-quarter, MKS had $81.6 million in sales. (Dow Jones)

Switch maker narrows its loss

Marlborough's 3Com Corp., whose switches run corporate data networks, said its first-quarter net loss narrowed to $35.5 million, or 9 cents a share, vs. $106 million, or 29 cents, a year earlier. Sales were unchanged at $162.3 million from $161.9 million. The quarter ended Aug. 27. The company expects second-quarter sales of $170 million to $180 million. Chief executive Bruce Claflin has been relying on job cuts to reduce expenses and end 18 quarters of losses. Excluding certain costs or gains, the company was expected to have a loss of 8 cents, the average estimate of 11 analysts surveyed by Thomson Financial. (Bloomberg)

Kodak gets help developing sensor chips

The IBM plant in Essex Junction, Vt., will work with Kodak to develop and produce sensor chips for consumer products such as digital cameras and cellphones, IBM said. IBM will manufacture these image chips for Kodak in Essex Junction. Image sensors act as the ''eye" of a digital camera by converting light into electric charges to begin the capture process. The deal will not lead to any new jobs at the plant. (AP)

THE NATION

United's cost-cut goal grows by $500m

United Airlines said it needs to cut about $500 million more in costs than the $655 million outlined two weeks ago, but it has not decided where. The disclosure came in a Bankruptcy Court filing. ''We haven't been advised of any new cost-cutting programs by United Airlines," said Joseph Tiberi, spokesman for the International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers. A judge warned the airline last month to mend relationships with unions, who say major decisions -- such as stopping pension fund contributions -- are being made without their input. United says it must slash another $1.15 billion -- nearly 8 percent of 2003 operating expenses -- even if it ends pension plans. United's head count is down 40 percent from 2001, to 62,000. (Chicago Tribune)

Index suggests chip equipment stalling

Orders at North American companies that sell equipment used to make semiconductors more than doubled in August from a year earlier: The book-to-bill ratio was 1.00, indicating the companies got $100 in orders for every $100 in sales, San Jose, Calif.-based Semiconductor Equipment & Materials International said. That's down from an adjusted 1.04 in July. The ratio, which has declined for four months, shows chip-equipment growth may be stalling. (Bloomberg)

AOL won't back Microsoft spam-fighter

America Online Inc. joins the list of companies and organizations shunning a spam-fighting plan from Microsoft Corp. AOL cited ''tepid support" for Microsoft's Sender ID technology, which seeks to cut down on junk e-mail by making it difficult for senders to forge e-mail headers and addresses. AOL ''has been especially concerned at the lack of acceptance for Sender ID among the free and open-source online community." Some, like the Apache Software Foundation, object to Microsoft's plans to prohibit software developers from licensing the Sender ID technology to others, even though the initial license would be free. (AP)

Manulife buys 37-floor Chicago tower

Manulife Financial Corp., the world's fifth-largest life insurer, bought a 37-floor office building in downtown Chicago for $222 million. The seller was a partnership of the California Public Employees' Retirement System and Hines, an international real-estate firm, Toronto-based Manulife said. The building was completed in January 2003. Manulife has more than 5.3 million square feet of space in the Chicago area. Manulife is John Hancock Financial Services' parent. (Bloomberg)
