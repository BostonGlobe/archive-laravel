Beyond the bag

September 26, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- With brands like Tropicana, Gatorade and Dasani, Coke and Pepsi own nearly 75 percent of the noncarbonated drink market. Coke posted $22 billion in revenues last year and Pepsi tallied $27 billion. Sales of Coke's Minute Maid refrigerated orange juice were $544 million last year, according to Chicago market research firm Information Resources Inc. Sales of Pepsi's refrigerated Tropicana Pure Premium Orange Juice totaled $1.4 billion.

By comparison, Ocean Spray's total revenues were $1.4 billion in its last fiscal year ended in August. Sales of its cranberry drinks totaled about $375 million, according to IRI.

Those are the numbers that lead beverage industry consultants like Pirko and academics like Tufts University professor and former Ocean Spray board member Jim Tillotson to believe Ocean Spray lacks the scale to survive on its own.

Grocery chains such as Stop & Shop and Shaw's would rather deal with big companies that can offer an array of products than with smaller outfits with limited offerings. Ocean Spray also lacks the distribution network to get its products into convenience stores, in vending machines, and cafeterias, where Pepsi and Coke do booming businesses selling single cans or bottles.

''You can't take a kid from Little League and expect him to play with the Yankees," Tillotson said. ''It's the same idea."

Papadellis respectfully disagrees. Ocean Spray is the number one brand on the juice aisle. Its cranberries can be found in everything from granola bars to blueberry muffins. (Dried cranberry husks can be infused with blueberry flavoring.) It was the first juice maker to blend two juices with its introduction of CranApple in 1964. It introduced the first light juice to the market a decade ago -- sales of its Light line are now growing at 40 percent a year.

And if there's any doubt America can eat any more cranberries, Papadellis just points to Ocean Spray's line of White Cranberry juice introduced in 2001. In the past two years, the company has grown the line into a $100 million business, broadening the brand's appeal among families with young children. (The company said parents preferred light-colored juices because they leave fewer stains.)

And over the past three months, consumption of Ocean Spray cranberry and grapefruit juices rose 12 percent compared to a 1.5 percent drop for bottled juices overall. On Friday, Ocean Spray said it bought Northland Cranberries Inc.'s processing plant and inventory for $28 million in cash to bolster its manufacturing and ingredient business.

''We're doing it," Papadellis said. ''Are we finished? No. But it's not as if we're still at the starting line."

Papadellis set a turnaround strategy in motion in June 2003 when he took the helm at Ocean Spray. He examined every detail of the business, looking for opportunities to free up money for marketing, step up product innovation and improve manufacturing and distribution.

He consolidated management teams for the cranberry, grapefruit and international businesses into one team. Instead of buying advertising on an as-needed basis, the new team decided to buy airtime upfront, giving Ocean Spray 25 percent more purchasing power and locking in better time slots.

It trimmed $92 million in costs from the supply chain, including several million dollars by reducing the thickness of the corrugate cases used to protect bottles during shipping -- the corrugate was apparently so thick that a person could stand safely on an empty case without crushing it.

Ocean Spray also took a closer look at product packaging. For example, the company knew from consumer research that products stored in the refrigerator got used 2.5 times faster than those stored in the pantry so they began working on a 64-ounce bottle that could fit on refrigerator door shelves. The result was a rectangular bottle that was also easier to grip and more efficient to ship than the old round bottles.

As for finding partners, Papadellis pinpointed three areas in particular: the single-serve market, which includes convenience stores, vending machines and any other outlet selling drinks in single cans or bottles; the fountain-drink market at cafeterias, restaurant and fast-food chains; and international markets.

In Japan, for example, Ocean Spray recently struck a deal with beer and beverage company Sapporo Beverage Co. Ltd. to produce, market and distribute its products.

It has a similar deal with beverage company Gerber Foods Soft Drinks Ltd. in the United Kingdom, where Ocean Spray is now the number one juice brand. By creating a network of partnerships, Papadellis hopes to patch together the scale Ocean Spray needs to survive against bigger rivals.

''We can tap into the scale other companies already have," he said.
