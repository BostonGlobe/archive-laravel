BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Bond fees lowered to woo customers

September 21, 2004

THE REGION

Bond fees lowered to woo customers

Fidelity Investments, seeking more bond business, is cutting fees as much as 75 percent and providing free information, usually reserved for professional investors, on 5,000 investment grade bonds. In July, the firm expanded independent stock research and introduced low-cost commissions for online stock trades, challenging the biggest discount broker, Charles Schwab Corp. Last month, it capped five index funds' expenses, undercutting Vanguard Group's pricing. Investors will be charged for trades based only on the bond type, eliminating factors like date of maturation. Fidelity will charge as little as $1 per bond for trades in US Treasuries; as much as $5 for mortgage-backed securities. (Bloomberg)

Governor raps plan for gas terminal

Governor Mitt Romney urged regulators not to approve plans for a Fall River liquefied natural gas terminal, saying Weaver's Cove Energy LLC's proposal fails to properly evaluate terrorism threats and environmental concerns. ''There is simply no way that it makes sense to site an LNG facility in this location in the post-9/11 world," he wrote to the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission. More studies are needed, he said. He also questioned plans to excavate 2.5 million cubic yards of sediment from the Taunton River, which could churn up contamination. Weaver's Cove says LNG can be handled safely and the project would provide 70 jobs and $3 million in annual tax revenue. (Peter J. Howe)

Verdict against Viacell thrown out

A federal judge in Delaware threw out a $7 million patent infringement verdict against Boston-based ViaCell Inc. and three other companies that store umbilical cord blood for stem cell research, a setback for plaintiff PharmaStem Therapeutics Inc. of Wayne, Pa. The judge found no infringement of one patent and ordered a new trial on a second. ''This means any individual, corporation or institution providing services to assist a family in preserving the cord blood does not infringe PharmaStem's patents," said ViaCell's chief executive, Marc Beer. (Bloomberg)

Teradyne cuts its profit forecast

Teradyne Inc., a Boston maker of semiconductor test equipment, lowered its profit forecast, blaming an industrywide slowdown. It forecast earnings for the quarter ended Oct. 3 of between 21 and 26 cents per share, below its previous forecast of between 39 and 46 cents. Teradyne now sees quarterly revenue of $450 million to $470 million, below its prior forecast of $530 million to $560 million. (Reuters)

Conn., buyout firm settle suit for $15m

Forstmann Little & Co., a New York buyout firm, settled a lawsuit with the Connecticut pension fund for $15 million, state officials said. Connecticut's $20 billion pension fund sued to seek the return of money lost in investments in two telecommunications companies, McLeodUSA Inc. and XO Communications Inc. Forstmann Little also agreed to release to Connecticut a $1.2 million distribution from an investment it had withheld to cover legal expenses. Connecticut estimated its losses at $120 million but settled because a jury, while finding Forstmann breached its contract, awarded no damages. (Bloomberg)

Banks faulted on lack of aid in Spanish

Many Massachusetts banks lack Spanish speakers to help customers, even though Latinos represent the state's fastest-growing minority group, according to state Senator Jarrett T. Barrios, a Cambridge Democrat. He and his staff surveyed 27 banks; 74 percent had no materials in Spanish or Spanish-speaking tellers. Their report said many Latinos avoid banks and use check-cashing stores, which often charge high fees. The reasons may include lack of Spanish speakers at banks, a mistrust of financial institutions, and strict identification requirements for opening an account. ''The challenge . . . is to overcome the cultural barriers against formal lending by offering services and financial products which Latinos want," Barrios said at a news conference. (Sasha Talcott)

THE NATION

Eisner says he'll stay at Disney till '06

Walt Disney Co.'s chief executive, Michael Eisner, plans to quit the board when his contract expires in 2006, but won't leave Disney before then, as some have demanded. He made his comments in the Oct. 4 issue of Fortune magazine, which released the article yesterday. Two weeks ago, Eisner said he'll retire in two years, and that he backs Disney president Robert Iger as his successor. That did not satisfy two ex-board members. Roy E. Disney and Stanley Gold have said they will nominate alternate directors next year if Eisner is not gone within six months. (AP)

Lawsuit aims to block generic drug

Novartis AG, Europe's fourth-largest drug company, has sued in New Jersey to stop Teva Pharmaceutical Industries Ltd., of Israel, from selling a generic version of the hypertension drug Lotrel. Teva is seeking Food and Drug Administration approval for the generic, which combines the Novartis drug Lotensin and Pfizer Inc.'s Norvasc. Novartis says it has patent protection through December 2017. Lotrel is the sixth-biggest drug for Novartis, of Basel, Switzerland. Novartis's complaint prevents the FDA from granting final approval to Teva for 30 months or until the suit ends. (Bloomberg)

IBM, Boeing to team up on defense jobs

International Business Machines Corp. and Boeing Co. agreed to work together for 10 years to compete for military and intelligence agency contracts. Boeing will contribute its experience building defense communications systems; IBM will provide its microprocessor technology, software, and skills linking computers into networks. Boeing's rank as the number two US defense contractor is based on $17.3 billion of Pentagon contracts awarded in fiscal 2003. Lockheed Martin Corp. is at the top of that list. IBM is 50th, with $469 million. (Bloomberg)

Internet ad revenue up sharply

US Internet ad revenue jumped 40 percent in the first half of this year, driven largely by the growing popularity of keyword ads tied to search results, to $4.6 billion, compared with $3.3 billion for the same period in 2003, according to PricewaterhouseCoopers. Search made up 40 percent of the ad revenue in the second quarter, compared with 29 percent in the year-earlier period. Ad revenue from e-mail marketing dropped 29 percent in the second quarter, to $47 million. (AP)

Acambis lowers its sales forecast

Acambis PLC cut its 2004 sales forecast after the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention said it won't order the remaining half of a 54 million-dose order for Acambis's ACAM2000 smallpox vaccine. The order was worth about $50 million, the English company said. It now expects 2004 revenue of $151.7 million to $160.7 million, not $241 million to $249.9 million. Acambis had a three-year order with Baxter International to supply vaccine for the US antiterrorism program. Second-quarter profit increased 93 percent on a $9 million payment from Baxter to end a pact involving Acambis's Canton facility. (Bloomberg)
