BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Stent maker's rating is raised

September 8, 2004

Standard & Poor's raised its outlook on Boston Scientific Corp. to positive from stable, citing its recovery of market share for its drug-coated Taxus cardiac stent after recalls that began in July. The debt rating agency said the upgrade reflects the Natick company's "surprisingly rapid recovery" of market share for Taxus, and its diverse product line. Last week, Boston Scientific said its share of the drug-coated stent market was at 70 percent in late August, about where it stood before the recall, and up from a low of 55 percent just after the recall began because of a manufacturing flaw. (Ross Kerber)

Mellon to acquire Providence Group

Mellon Financial Corp. agreed to buy Providence Group Investment Advisory Co. to build a wealth management business in Rhode Island. Terms weren't disclosed. The purchase will add about $750 million to the more than $75 billion overseen by Pittsburgh-based Mellon's wealth management group, the company said. Closely held Providence is the seventh acquisition in four years for the Mellon group. Providence Group, founded in 1976, caters to those with a minimum of $1 million to invest. Co-managing directors H. James Field Jr. and Scott Laurans will remain at the firm, along with eight other employees. Mellon plans to expand staff in Rhode Island and move two Mellon executives, Don Quattrucci and Matt Littlefield, to Providence from Boston. (Bloomberg)

Managers buying out Eastern Mountain

Eastern Mountain Sports said a management team and investors are buying the Peterborough, N.H., company from American Retail Group. The buyout is led by Will Manzer, president and chief executive, and is backed by the investment firm J.H. Whitney & Co., of Stamford, Conn. Terms were not disclosed. "With our new partners, we will continue to strengthen our unique retail model of serving outdoor athletes and enthusiasts," Manzer said. American Retail Group is owned by the Brenninkmeyer family of Belgium, which has $7 billion in retail worldwide, including 635 US apparel stores. Eastern Mountain was founded by two rock climbers in Wellesley 37 years ago. They wanted a wider selection of climbing equipment, so they opened a store near Boston. EMS has 80 stores in 16 states. (AP)

3 National Energy power plants to be sold

Dominion Resources Inc., owner of Virginia's largest utility, agreed to pay $656 million to buy three power plants from National Energy & Gas Transmission, a bankrupt unit of PG&E Corp.: the 1,599-megawatt Brayton Point station in Somerset, 745-megawatt Salem Harbor plant, and 495-megawatt Manchester Street station in Providence. The purchase, which requires Bankruptcy Court approval, includes about $120 million for inventory and capital spending prior to closing. "These new units increase Dominion's generation portfolio by 10 percent and improve our competitive position," said Thomas Capps, Dominion's chief executive. The plants will be offered at auction in November for bidders willing to top Dominion's offer by at least $30 million. The state is seeking to have the Salem plant retrofitted with air pollution control equipment. (Bloomberg)

Suit accuses Thermo of patent violation

Applera Corp. and MDS Inc. sued Thermo Electron Corp. of Waltham, accusing the research instrument maker of infringing on a patent for spectrometers used to analyze chemicals. The suit, filed in Wilmington, Del., accuses the company of using technology covered by a US patent licensed to a joint venture of Applera's Applied Biosystems unit and MDS, Canada's largest medical services company. Thermo Electron declined to comment. (Bloomberg)

Business confidence index slips a bit

Overall business confidence in Massachusetts dipped slightly in August, but the outlook remains positive, Associated Industries of Massachusetts said. Its index slipped to 61.6 from 63.3 in July, which was the best reading in nearly four years, and was substantially higher than a year earlier, when at 50.3 it barely broke into positive territory. Above 50 indicates companies are optimistic; such confidence is considered a prerequisite to investment and hiring. Analysts attributed the August dip to the recent slowing of the economy, lingering uncertainty, and high costs, particularly for health insurance. The sub-index that measures perceptions of the state economy rose, however, to 58.9 from 58.8 in July and 45.3 in August 2003. (Robert Gavin)

Drugstore chain cuts earnings forecast

Rite Aid Corp., the third-largest US drugstore chain, cut its annual sales and earnings forecasts because of increased competition from mail order pharmacies. Its shares fell 19 percent. Net income for the year ended Feb. 26 will rise to as much as $149.5 million, or 22 cents a share, less than a previous forecast of as much as $167 million, the Camp Hill, Pa., company said. Sales will increase to as much as $17 billion, not $17.3 billion. Rite Aid said more customers are required by their health plans to order drugs by mail. Rite Aid has no mail order business. Net income was 11 cents in fiscal 2004 on sales of $16.6 billion. (Bloomberg)

Automaker to produce a new Acura SUV

Honda Motor Co. will expand its lineup of sport utility vehicles with a new model from the Acura luxury division, to be made in Ohio starting in 2006. It will be smaller and cost less than Acura's MDX SUV. Honda does not plan to add jobs. Among the top six automakers in the United States, Honda is the only one without a pickup, but there are no plans to launch a full-fledged pickup. For the first eight months of 2004, Honda's US vehicle sales were roughly flat from a year earlier. Toyota Motor Corp. was up 9 percent for the same period; Nissan, 23 percent. (AP)

Dealers say GM ready to fatten rebates

General Motors Corp., hurt by weaker sales for the past three months, today will raise cash rebates by $500 to $1,000 on most 2005 models, dealers said. The automaker is also extending a program offering "bonus cash" in addition to the rebates of between $500 and $1,500 on most 2004 and 2005 models financed through General Motors Acceptance Corp. A GM spokeswoman declined to comment. Last week, GM cut its targeted fourth-quarter vehicle production in North America by nearly 8 percent from year-ago levels, due to high inventories of unsold vehicles. (Reuters)

Halliburton might not rebid on Iraq work

Halliburton Co. said it may decide not to submit new bids for work in Iraq if the military divides up the work. A spokesman confirmed the Army is considering which parts of a multibillion-dollar logistics contract awarded to Halliburton to feed, house, and operate services for US troops could be competitively bid. "I'm not sure we're going to rebid if it's hacked into too many pieces in Iraq. If we do choose to rebid, we're going to jack the margins up significantly," said Halliburton chief executive Dave Lesar. Last month, Pentagon auditors urged the Army to withhold 15 percent of payments to Halliburton for overcharges on more than $4 billion in logistical work. Halliburton denies any wrongdoing. (Reuters)

Etc.

Biogen Idec Inc. of Cambridge and Sunesis Pharmaceuticals Inc. will collaborate to develop small-molecule cancer therapeutics targeting kinases, enzymes that play a major role in cancer. The companies will apply Tethering, Sunesis' fragment-based drug discovery technology, in an effort to generate novel small molecule leads that inhibit kinases.
