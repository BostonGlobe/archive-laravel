Lucent union fights for jobs, benefits

By Kathy McCabe  |  September 19, 2004

Union workers at Lucent Technologies in North Andover will rally Tuesday to try to save health care benefits for retirees and protest job cuts at the telecommunications company, once one of the largest private employers in the Merrimack Valley.

ADVERTISEMENT

Lucent retirees, who number in the thousands locally, have been asked to join the picket line. Health care benefits for retirees and jobs at the plant are at risk as negotiations for a new contract start next month, a union leader said.

''These are both very serious issues," said Gary Nilsson, president of the Communications Workers of America, Local 1365, the union representing 300 workers at the plant. ''Lucent is claiming they can't maintain retiree health benefits because they don't have the revenues. They don't have the revenues because they are outsourcing our jobs."

The rally is planned for 2:30 p.m. at the company's Route 125 plant.

Lucent, based in Cherry Hill, N.J., is the country's largest telephone equipment maker. The North Andover plant manufactures transmission equipment. But the telecom bust in recent years took its toll on Lucent, prompting the company to retool and slash costs. In North Andover, the cuts were dramatic, particularly on hourly workers. Local 1365, the plant's largest union, has 300 members today, compared with 3,000 just three years ago, the union said.

Job security and pension benefits will be major issues when Lucent starts national contract negotiations next month in Washington, to replace a contract due to expire Oct. 31. In an early round of negotations in June, health care benefits for retirees were discussed, but no agreement was reached, the union and company said.

''Retiree health care is a critical issue for us," said Mary Ward, a spokeswoman for Lucent in New Jersey. ''Health care costs have just increased significantly for us. . . . We've been very open and frank about our health care costs, both with the union and management."

Health care for retired hourly workers and managers cost Lucent $800 million last year, almost 10 percent of the company's $8.5 billion in revenues, Ward said.

''This is clearly an issue that has to be dealt with. It's a challenging issue, but it points to the reality of what Lucent is. We are a far smaller company than we once were. We can't afford to shoulder the costs on our own anymore," she said.

Retirees now pay no premium for their health care benefits, but do have copayments for doctor's visits, prescription drugs, and other related costs, Ward said. She declined to discuss what or how the company plans to change the coverage, saying only, ''We will be negotiating in good faith . . . on all issues."

Nilsson said changes proposed in June called for retirees to pay $550 per month, on average, for health care benefits. With some retired union workers collecting minimum pension checks of just $450 per each month, the increase would pose a hardship for many, he said.

''They'd be in the hole $100 before they even buy a loaf of bread or a prescription drug," Nilsson said. ''We told them in June, and we will tell them again next month, 'This is outrageous.' "

Saucony opens 'think tank' unit

Saucony Inc., the sneaker company in Peabody, surprised Wall Street for 11 straight quarters, by meeting or beating stock analysts' expectations for profits and sales.

Now Saucony hopes to keep the momentum going, by never running out of ideas for new athletic shoes or clothing. The new John Hyde Fisher Product Creation Center is Saucony's think tank. It's where shoe and apparel designers will develop new product and marketing ideas for the $44 million company.

Saucony, based at the Centennial Industrial Park, held a ''tape breaking" ceremony at the sleek brick, wood, and steel center last week. A panel of employees decided to name the wing for John H. Fisher, company chairman and grandson of Saucony founder A.R. Hyde.

''We've had good solid growth in the last few years," Fisher said in an interview. ''We expect that to continue. . . . We've told [Wall Street] that we'll double our business by 2008 and we will meet that."

The new wing is Saucony's first major expansion since it moved to Peabody from Cambridge 19 years ago. It includes enough space to increase employment 45 percent in Peabody. Saucony now has 137 employees who design and market running and walking shoes, and a clothing line sold under the Hind brand at Centennial Park. Saucony manufactures in China, bringing total employment to 325.

While other shoe manufacturers, including Nike, battled charges of labor exploitation in Asia, where labor costs are lower, Saucony has never had any problems with foreign labor practices, Fisher said. Saucony moved production there in the mid-1990s, after manufacturing for years in Bangor, Maine.

''We were the last one to go. We held out as long as we could, but we just couldn't [justify the cost] anymore. . . . We maintain the highest standards in China. We follow the same worker rights in China that are mandated in the US," Fisher said.

Saucony's plan for growth will start in Peabody. The new production center, which cost almost $3 million, is actually old space. About 16,000 square feet of storage and office space was renovated into a wing for shoe designers and a marketing team to think up new ways for Saucony to compete with rivals like Asics and Adidas.

At least one fan thinks Saucony will keep pace. Olympian Barbara Lindquist, who competed in the women's triathlon last month in Athens, is sponsored by Saucony. Lindquist, ranked as the world's top woman triathlete heading into the Olympics, trains every day in a pair of Saucony.

''The shoes are a really great fit for me," said Lindquist, 35, who did not medal in Athens. ''Saucony gives me great support."
