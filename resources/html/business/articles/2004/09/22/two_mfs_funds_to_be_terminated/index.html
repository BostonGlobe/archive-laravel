BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Two MFS funds to be terminated

September 22, 2004

The board overseeing MFS Institutional Emerging Equities Fund and MFS Institutional Mid Cap Growth Fund has voted to terminate the funds about Oct. 28, according to a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. It did not say why. Mid Cap Growth had about $17.8 million in net assets June 30; Emerging Equities had $56 million. Massachusetts Financial Services Co. is the funds' investment manager. (Dow Jones)

Investor confidence index declines

State Street Corp.'s September investor confidence index fell 1.3 points to 85.3 from August's revised figure of 86.5, on concern about stock prices. The index has fallen in seven of nine months and is down 22 percent from December. "The appetite for risk-taking remains subdued," Harvard University professor Ken Froot said. Froot developed the index along with State Street Associates, a unit of the Boston-based corporation. Interest rate increases and a "US election without a clear national favorite" worry investors, Froot said. (Bloomberg)

Denial-of-care cases vs. Magellan decline

Fewer Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Massachusetts members are claiming that they were denied care by Magellan Health Services, which oversees mental health treatment for 1.1 million subscribers. The number of appeals of Connecticut-based Magellan's decisions to deny hospital care fell to 11 in the first six months of 2004, vs. 153 for all of 2003, 106 in 2002, and seven in 2001. Blue Cross says Magellan has been approving longer stays since it held talks with hospitals. From January to June, consumers in a variety of health plans filed 162 complaints over all types of care with the state Department of Public Health; in 30 cases, the state ordered health plans to pay for care. (Liz Kowalczyk)

Fund-raising starts for R.I. biotech center

The Rhode Island Economic Policy Council and the Rhode Island Technology Council are raising money for a campaign to support a proposed $50 million biotechnology center at the University of Rhode Island. Voters will be asked to approve borrowing for the project. The two groups say the center would make the state more competitive, The Providence Journal reported. Brown University is privately funding a $118 million expansion of its life-sciences and biomedical labs, though, which could lead some to question state support for URI's project. (AP)

Lottery equipment firm's profit up 9.5%

Gtech Holdings Corp., West Greenwich, R.I., the biggest seller of computerized lottery systems, said second-quarter profit rose 9.5 percent to $53.1 million, or 40 cents a share, from $48.5 million, or 37 cents, a year earlier. Sales were boosted by an order for 5,000 hand-held lottery terminals from Organizacion Nacional de Ciegos Espanoles in Spain. Gtech also renewed a contract in Mexico. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Lucent cuts more retiree health benefits

For the second time in a year, Lucent Technologies Inc. is cutting retiree benefits. The New Jersey company, which lost billions during the telecom slump, will no longer provide free health insurance for dependents of management workers who retired on or after March 1, 1990, at a salary of $65,000 or more. Last September, Lucent made identical cuts for managers who retired during the same period but had a base salary of at least $87,000. The latest cut affects 7,400 dependents. The prior cutback affected about 9,000 dependents. Management retirees' costs for dental coverage also are being increased. The latest changes should save about $16 million annually, Lucent said. (AP)

Airline, dispatchers agree on wage cuts

US Airways Group Inc., which sought bankruptcy protection last week, has agreed with the Transport Workers Union on a contract for 151 dispatchers that saves the company $4.5 million due to lower wages, said Don Wright, president of the union's Local 545. US Airways plans to cut $800 million in labor costs. The agreement, not yet ratified by employees or the US Bankruptcy Court, "was painful but necessary," Wright said. US Airways is still negotiating with pilots, flight attendants, and other workers. (Bloomberg)

House backs bill to target ID theft

The US House approved a bill that would increase jail time for identity thieves and other fraudulent Web users. It would not directly outlaw the use of fraudulent registration information but would increase by up to seven years the prison terms of those convicted of felonies. Investigators frequently find that suspects have filled out Web-site registrations with false information -- providing "555-555-5555" as a phone number, for example. Ten percent of the Internet's 30 million domain names may be registered under false identities, according to a 2003 study. The bill also calls for fines and jail time for those who use legitimate-looking labels to trick consumers into buying illegally copied music, software, and other copyrighted materials. The bill goes to the Senate. (Reuters)

Firm to pay $2m to settle SEC allegations

TD Waterhouse Investor Services will pay $2 million to settle allegations it made undisclosed payments to three investment adviser firms to lure their clients' business, the Securities and Exchange Commission said. The SEC also settled with two of the investment advisers and their principals for alleged failure to disclose the payments so as to avoid conflicts of interest. Kiely Financial Services of Greenville, N.C., and Joseph Kiely agreed to pay a $100,000 civil fine and to return money they were said to have received from TD Waterhouse, plus interest, an amount totaling $54,256. Rudney Associates of San Ramon, Calif., and Eric Rudney will pay a $40,000 civil fine and return $22,331. SEC action against the third investment adviser, Brandt, Kelly & Simmons of Southfield, Mich., and Kenneth Brandt is pending. (AP)

Moderate holiday retail growth forecast

The National Retail Federation is to release its forecast today for "moderate" growth this holiday season. The group predicts an increase of 4.5 percent over 2003, to $219.9 billion in retail sales in November and December. Last year, holiday sales grew 5.1 percent. During the 2002 holidays, sales rose 1.2 percent. (Naomi Aoki)

THE WORLD
Japan, US at odds over soy sauce

Japan has a bitter taste in its mouth over the United States' insistence that all soy sauces are equal. The dispute involves setting an international standard for making soy sauce. In 1998, Japan asked the Codex Alimentarius Commission, which has "harmonized" food standards since the 1960s, to set a standard like Japan's. The Japanese want to differentiate traditional soy sauce, fermented from soybeans, and American knockoffs that use soybean extract or other protein, flavor enhancers, and artificial coloring. "It's national pride," said Ellen Matten, of the US Codex Office. The US position is that labeling should be left to each country. Bruce Silverglade, of the Center for Science in the Public Interest, asked, "What can be more absurd than the US telling Japan that soy sauce doesn't need to be made from soybeans?" (Washington Post)

Spanish bank to buy Texas institution

Spain's second-largest bank, Banco Bilbao Vizcaya Argentaria, agreed to buy Texas-based Laredo National Bancshares for $850 million, its largest-ever US purchase. It will give the bank a presence at one of the busiest Mexican border crossings. Laredo has $3.4 billion in assets. "It's a clear business opportunity for us," said Vitalino Nafria, BBVA's head of Latin America. The deal comes on the heels of BBVA's $375 million purchase of Mexican mortgage bank Hipotecaria Nacional. (Reuters)
