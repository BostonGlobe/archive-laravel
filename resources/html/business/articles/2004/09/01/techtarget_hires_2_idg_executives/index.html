BUSINESS IN BRIEF

TechTarget hires 2 IDG executives

September 1, 2004

The rivalry between TechTarget Inc., an upstart Needham technology publisher, and the giant International Data Group intensified as TechTarget hired away two key IDG executives. Maryfran Johnson, longtime editor of the IDG publication Computerworld, and Joseph Levy, who headed the company's CXO Media group, are joining TechTarget to launch publications. ''Right now we're not revealing what we're working on," said TechTarget founder and chief executive Greg Strakosch, but he said his company is researching possible new print and Internet publications, as well as events like trade shows. TechTarget raised $70 million in venture capital this year, and plans to spend the money on launching media products, as well as acquisitions and expansion into overseas markets. (Hiawatha Bray)

Defense contractor wins Navy pact

Raytheon Co., the world's largest missile maker, received a US Navy order worth as much as $210 million for radar warning systems used on aircraft in Iraq. Raytheon will supply 30 of the ALR-67(V)3 radar warning systems, used on Navy F/A-18 Super Hornet jets launched from aircraft carriers, under an initial $43.3 million contract, the Waltham-based company said. Another 140 systems may be supplied under four annual contract options worth $166.3 million. Raytheon began initial production of the warning systems in 1998, and full production in 1999. Deliveries under the new contract will begin in October 2005 and will be completed by September 2006. (Bloomberg)

Firms join forces on drug research

Biocon Ltd., India's biggest biotechnology company, said its unit Syngene International has signed a research agreement with Novartis Institutes for Biomedical Research Inc. to develop drugs. Biocon and Cambridge-based Novartis Institutes signed the agreement last month, the Indian biotechnology company said. Biocon is among Indian drug makers allying with overseas companies to either jointly develop drugs or help them in the early stages of research program, allowing them to build relationships with overseas partners and boost research capabilities. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Analyst wary on chip maker's sales

Intel Corp.'s third-quarter sales may fall short of the highest estimates as desktop personal-computer orders slow and inventory builds, three analysts said. Intel, the world's biggest chip maker, will probably narrow its sales forecast when the company updates investors tomorrow, Morgan Stanley analyst Mark Edelstone wrote to clients. He cut his prediction to $8.7 billion, from $8.8 billion. The remarks heightened concern that higher interest rates and oil prices are hampering computer sales and limiting chip orders. Intel ended the second quarter with record inventory and last week chopped prices by as much as 35 percent to stoke demand. Those efforts may not be enough to win consumer business in coming months, Edelstone said. Fourth-quarter demand ''may be even more disappointing," said Edelstone, a chip analyst who ranked second last year in an Institutional Investor magazine survey. (Bloomberg)

Investigators point to Hollinger's ex-CEO

Conrad Black, the former chief executive of Hollinger International Inc., conspired with associates to systematically loot the newspaper publishing company of more than $400 million -- nearly all of its profits from 1997 through 2003 --an internal investigation has found. The report, which was filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission, was prepared by a special committee of Hollinger's board which was formed last year to examine concerns from shareholders about payments made to Black and others. Black has since been forced out as chief and chairman of Hollinger International, the parent company of the Chicago Sun-Times and The Jerusalem Post, following initial findings from the committee that he and others improperly received millions in fees and payments that should have gone to the company. He remains the company's controlling shareholder. (AP)

SEC bans 3 former Invesco executives

Three former Invesco Funds Group Inc. executives were barred from the mutual fund industry and agreed to pay $340,000 in fines to resolve regulators' allegations that they helped favored clients make about $58 billion in improper trades. Timothy Miller, former chief investment officer, Thomas Kolbe, former national sales director, and Michael Legoski, a former assistant vice president in sales, settled with the Securities and Exchange Commission without admitting or denying the allegations, the commission said. The SEC and New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer sued Invesco, a unit of London-based Amvescap PLC, in December. Spitzer claimed Invesco executives engaged in a ''massive mutual fund scheme" by allowing investors, including the Canary Capital Partners LLC hedge fund, to make short-term trades, a practice known as market timing. (Bloomberg)

Ex-Freddie Mac CEO won't lose his $60m

A federal judge has ordered that $60 million in compensation cannot be withheld from an ousted chief executive of Freddie Mac, in a setback to federal regulators seeking to strip him of the money for his alleged role in the company's accounting scandal. US District Judge Richard Leon ruled that the federal agency that oversees Freddie Mac exceeded its authority when it ordered the mortgage giant in December to withhold severance benefits and stock awards that Leland Brendsel received after being forced out by the company's board in June 2003. The Office of Federal Housing Enterprise Oversight also has been seeking to remove $750,000 in severance benefits from former chief financial officer Vaughn Clarke, who left the government-sponsored company, along with Brendsel and former president David Glenn. In addition, the agency has asked for restitution of $3.8 million from Brendsel and $537,000 from Clarke as well as civil fines of $5.8 million and $2.6 million from Brendsel and Clarke, respectively. (AP)

Former Enron official pleads guilty

Former Enron Corp. executive Kevin Hannon pleaded guilty to fraud and conspiracy, admitting that he helped senior executives misrepresent the value of the company's broadband services division. Hannon agreed to work with prosecutors who are trying to convict other former Enron executives, including ex-chief executive Jeffrey Skilling and former chairman Kenneth Lay, following the collapse of the energy trader. Hannon said he helped unnamed executives present misleading information about the broadband unit in a 2001 conference in which Lay and Skilling participated. Hannon entered a guilty plea to one count of conspiracy to commit securities and wire fraud before US District Court Judge Vanessa Gilmore in Houston. He agreed to pay a $250,000 fine and relinquish his claim to $8 million in deferred compensation from the company. (Bloomberg)

THE WORLD
National Grid to sell half its gas network

Britain's National Grid Transco agreed to sell half its gas distribution assets for $10.4 billion, raising money to return to shareholders and invest in growing US markets. The deal further opens up Britain's gas network, first sold off by the government in 1986. National Grid said it had sold four networks to buyers, including Britain's Scottish & Southern Energy and United Utilities, as well as Australia's Macquarie Bank and Cheung Kong Infrastructure Holdings -- a unit of Hong Kong businessman Li Ka-shing. But National Grid, which plans to return $3.61 billion to shareholders, will remain Britain's biggest gas distributor, with 11 million business and domestic customers. (Reuters)
