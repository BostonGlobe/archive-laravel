It's looking up for Beverly biotech firm

By Kathy McCabe  |  September 12, 2004

So what's a dog's life like, anyhow?

ADVERTISEMENT

Scientists at Agencourt Bioscience Corp. in Beverly know. The biotech company helped to sequence the genetic coding that makes a dog a dog. Last month, the data was added to several public databases, making it available to biomedical and veterinary researchers around the world.

The project is just one reason Governor Mitt Romney, Beverly Mayor William Scanlon, and local business leaders applauded Agencourt last week during a ribbon-cutting ceremony at the company's new 30,000-square-foot headquarters at the Cummings Center. Agencourt, a privately owned company, is the first tenant in the center's new six-story office building overlooking Shoe Pond.

Agencourt produces DNA data for a mix of government, academic, and commercial entities. It also makes nucleic acid purification products. Customers include the National Cancer Institute, Harvard Medical School, Genzyme, and Millennium Pharmaceuticals.

The private company was started four years ago at the Cummings Center by three brothers, Brian, Brendan, and Kevin McKernan. It now has 84 employees, but plans to have 100 by the end of the year, and revenues of $30 million by June 2005, a company spokesman said.

Agencourt's growth is a sign that biotech is alive and well in the Bay State, Romney said. "This is the right place if you want to be in the life sciences arena," Romney told over 100 people gathered for a ribbon-cutting and tour of Agencourt's new facility. "I want to continue to make sure we are the right place . . . where [biotech firms] continue to grow and thrive."

He cited investment tax credits as a key tool to help biotech firms grow. Over the next 10 years, biotech employment is expected to grow to 100,000 employees, from 30,000 today, Romney said.

Romney, a former venture capitalist, noted that many fast-growing companies often fail because they lose sight of their market. He praised Agencourt for identifying a niche, and sticking to its business plan. "This is a company that has focused, focused, focused on the most important opportunities. Now it has the luxury of casting about . . . and focusing on those with the greatest future potential."

Agencourt's expansion is driven in part by a $27.2 million grant it received last year from the National Human Genome Research Institute, part of the National Institutes of Health. It was one of five DNA sequencing centers, and the only commercial entity, selected nationally. Research on genetic coding for the dog was conducted as part of that grant. The company is also working on NIH products to study cancer growth.

Georgetown bank has a growth plan

Georgetown Savings Bank is taking steps to go public. Corporators at the bank will meet Tuesday to consider a plan to form a mutual holding company and sell a minority interest in the bank to shareholders. Trustees unanimously approved the plan in June.

Georgetown Savings plans to offer up to 49.9 percent of its common stock, through the holding company, to depositors, employees, and eventually the general public. An appraiser is now deciding the value of the stock to be issued, which must be approved by state banking regulators.

As part of its plan, the $124 million-asset bank also plans to convert from a state to a federally chartered institution. The federal Office of Thrift Supervision is reviewing the application. The change would give Georgetown Savings more flexibility to expand into new markets, particularly out of state.

Bob Balletto, the bank's chief executive, declined to discuss specifics of the public offering, citing regulatory restrictions. But he said the bank, which has grown an average of 10 percent in recent years, plans to expand its tiny franchise in the southern Merrimack Valley.

"We see a lot of opportunity in the marketplace," Balletto said. "We're trying to put our brand of banking out there. There has been a lot of consolidation in the industry. We'd like to continue to expand our market."

Georgetown Savings, founded in 1868, now operates in a small universe. The bank's new brick headquarters, which opened last year, anchors the town center. It's only other location is a branch on Route 1 in Rowley. Most other customers hail from Haverhill and surrounding towns, Balletto said.

Fashion designer tasting success

You wouldn't expect to find an up-and-coming fashion designer whose line is sold in trendy boutiques in California, New York, and Canada in an apartment complex off Route 495 in Haverhill.

But that's where Melissa Alexis, 27, spends her days, working in cramped quarters out of the one-bedroom apartment she shares with her husband. Out of this home-office, Alexis has had so much success with her clothing line, La Cuitique, that she has hired seamstresses. The name is a combination of the words "cuisine" and "boutique."

Alexis moved her business from New York to Haverhill last fall. She said she hopes to eventually open a store with her designs on one side and food on the other.

"Like a one-stop shopping type of venue," she said.

Up until now, she has done all the sewing herself and has had small orders. But there is a greater demand now for her fashions, which include skirts, tops with large floral pins, and strapless dresses. As of now, she sells to three boutiques.

The last store that placed an order asked for 15 bags and six T-shirts and wanted them in two weeks.
