INDUSTRY FALLOUT

Navigating the troubled skies

With 3 major airlines in crisis, Logan is looking to avoid losses

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  September 12, 2004

As financial troubles roil three major airlines that carry nearly half of passengers at Logan International Airport, travelers aren't the only ones worried.

Logan executives are trying to avoid potential losses if valuable gate leases with US Airways, Delta Air Lines and United Airlines wind up disputed in bankruptcy court. United has been under Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection for almost two years, and the other two carriers have acknowledged that they face financial hurdles, which could land them in Chapter 11 soon.

The Massachusetts Port Authority, which owns Logan, already is requiring at least one airline to pay a security deposit on its landing fees and gate rent in advance, said Tom Kinton, the airport's director of aviation. He would not disclose which airline that was.

''We'll work with the airlines," Kinton said. But if an airline does not pay its bills, costs will rise for all the other carriers at the airport, he said.

Security deposits are among a few steps Massport takes to protect against losses in the event an ailing airline files for bankruptcy. It also limits gate leases to five years before a carrier can re-lease, and it reserves the right to take back gates from airlines that cut service too much.

But if leases do wind up being rejected by the airlines in bankruptcy court, Massport could quickly re-lease them to other carriers that want to grow at Logan, Kinton said.

''Should one carrier fail, we have a presence of many carriers that could expand their operations into the failed carrier's gates," he said. ''That's more business to be had for them."

Low-cost airlines such as JetBlue Airways and AirTran Airways would be the most likely to take over those slots, said Dan Kasper, managing director and head of transportation at LECG LLC, a Cambridge consulting firm. Both those carriers have cash, new planes on order, and a hunger to expand beyond their current digs at Logan, he said.

''You really start by looking at the carriers that are in a financial position to expand," Kasper said. ''You're looking at probably the AirTrans, JetBlues, and Southwests, but I think it's unlikely Southwest would come." Southwest tends to avoid crowded urban airports, preferring instead to fly out of secondary facilities where costs can be kept low.

Those airlines would likely want to take over US Airways' or Delta's New York shuttle routes, instantly grabbing a presence at crowded LaGuardia Airport, Kasper said. Otherwise, they could pick and choose among the other routes that an airline abandoned.

''What I suspect is that they're all sitting back and analyzing the traffic," Kasper said. ''It's a fairly big plate of potential items for low-cost airlines that aren't huge to begin with."

The airlines' predicament is the result of what specialists say is the most difficult financial crisis in aviation since at least 1992, when three other airlines -- Trans World Airlines, Continental Airlines, and America West Airlines -- were under bankruptcy protection at the same time.

''This is worse because that was short term," said Darryl Jenkins, a professor at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University in Daytona Beach, Fla. All three airlines under Chapter 11 then emerged relatively quickly and with not much structural damage to their operations, he said. Trans World was ultimately bought by American Airlines in 2001.

This time around, the carriers that go into bankruptcy will look drastically different if and when they emerge.

''Everybody's operating under the assumption that there are long-term changes taking place in the industry," Jenkins said. ''I see Chapter 11 for these guys as inevitable, and not a bad thing. Right about now the only way you have to innovate is Chapter 11."

The problem for airlines -- and by extension their employees and passengers -- is a mix of high costs, leaner competitors, and a string of bad luck. US Airways, Delta, and United all rank in the top four for operating expenses among domestic airlines, measured in cost per seat-mile.

Cost per seat-mile measures the average amount an airline spends to fly one seat for one mile on a domestic trip. All three carriers spent more than 11 cents per seat-mile in the fourth quarter of 2003, the most recent data available from the US Bureau of Transportation Statistics.

Low-cost carrier JetBlue Airways, by comparison, spent only 6.1 cents per seat-mile in the same quarter.

The terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, plunged the airlines into financial disarray. Nearly every domestic carrier, save for the already profitable low-cost airlines, have been trying to restructure and cut costs since.

But the SARS scare, record jet fuel costs, and mass flight cancellations from the recent hurricanes in Florida, have wiped out the hopes that the airlines would turn things around quickly.

In a recent report on the financial health of the airlines, Merrill Lynch analysts predicted that the industry would lose $700 million before taxes in the third quarter.

If workers are forced to swallow any more harsh pay and benefit cuts -- last week Delta said it was cutting 7,000 jobs and trimming benefits to avoid bankruptcy -- things might get worse because employee morale would likely suffer, Jenkins said, ultimately affecting customer service.

''The passengers are going to feel this," he said.

As for the immediate effects on passengers at Logan, it would likely take the liquidation of a carrier to bring about large-scale problems there, said Terry Trippler, a consumer advocate with the aviation website Sidestep.com.

Though the airport could pass any losses from a Chapter 11 carrier on to other airlines, Trippler believes Logan might suffer if it does so. A stronger airline could simply choose to shift flights to nearby Manchester Airport or T.F. Green Airport in Warwick, R.I.

''I think Logan right now is worried about keeping everybody happy at the airport," he said.

In the meantime, the three troubled airlines are trying to strengthen themselves at Logan.

US Airways, Logan's third-largest carrier, is already refocusing its service from Logan on more profitable trips to leisure destinations in Florida and the Caribbean. The airline runs an hourly shuttle service to Washington, D.C., and New York on weekdays, as well as connecting flights to other cities through its hubs in Philadelphia and Charlotte.

Delta also operates hourly shuttles to New York and Washington, as well as service to 21 other cities from Logan. It is the second-largest carrier at the airport, behind American Airlines. United, the fourth-largest carrier at Logan, offers fewer flights and carries fewer passengers than the other two, but focuses its service on cross-country flights to such cities as Denver and Los Angeles.

If any of the airlines were liquidated, their service would go away, but Jenkins said competitors would likely move in quickly. Because Logan is not a hub airport that is dominated by a single carrier, several airlines are watching out for another's misfortunes and would clamor to take over their gate space, he said.

Kinton said that because of the slim availability of gates at the airport, a potential Chapter 11 filing by any airline is worrisome. Like labor contracts, airport gate leases are assets that can be held in limbo by a bankruptcy proceeding, which is why Massport has put financial safeguards in place.

''You don't want to be exposed, which means you don't let any carrier go a long time without paying their bills," Kinton said. ''When you go to Chapter 11, it's a lot more difficult to collect."
