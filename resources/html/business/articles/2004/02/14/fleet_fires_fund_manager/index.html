BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Fleet fires fund manager

February 14, 2004

FleetBoston Financial Corp. said it fired a mutual fund manager for making frequent trades in his 401(k) retirement account. Spokesman Charles Salmans said the manager, Bill Garrison, was dismissed Nov. 11, not long after Fleet's Columbia funds unit said it discovered the trading in his fund, the $400 million Columbia Small Company Equity fund, and others. In a letter to investors dated Jan. 30 and posted to its website, Columbia Management Group said information about the trades, which were "relatively small," was given to regulators. (Reuters)

Possible strike at KeySpan

KeySpan Corp. said delivery of gas and electricity will be unimpeded should contract talks with 2,600 union workers have failed before a deadline of midnight last night. Managers are trained and ready to step in should union workers strike, said a spokeswoman for New York-based KeySpan. The firm sells gas in New York and Boston, and generates about 25 percent of New York City's electricity. Union negotiators would not say whether a strike is imminent. (Bloomberg)

Putnam gives out bonuses

In an attempt to raise morale, Putnam Investments, the first mutual fund firm sued for alleged fraud in state and federal investigations of improper trading, paid annual bonuses to employees yesterday, about a month earlier than usual. The Boston-based company also will begin giving entire bonuses to senior employees each year instead of deferring some of the compensation over five years. (Bloomberg)

Firm to turn over records

An Ohio company accused of selling individuals' financial data without their consent has been ordered to turn over its records on Massachusetts customers to state Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly. Suffolk County Superior Court Judge Elizabeth M. Fahey issued a preliminary injunction against ICU Inc. of Berea, Ohio, prohibiting the company from selling any personal information related to Massachusetts residents and requiring it to turn over all its business records related to state residents. ICU, which runs a website that offers the ability to track individuals' private telephone, business, and telephone records for a fee, is being sued by Reilly's office for allegedly violating the Massachusetts Consumer Protection Act and other state and federal credit-reporting and privacy laws. (Keith Reed)
