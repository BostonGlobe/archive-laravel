Floating fixer-upper

A rusty tugboat awaits a salty buyer to make it a rockin' home

By Chris Reidy, Globe Staff  |  February 22, 2004

Looking for a splashy address with great skyline views and a low price tag? Then break out the Top-Siders and sailor suits, because Doug Della Porta is selling a tugboat he says will make a great dockside home. His asking price? $60,000. To a landlubber, the tug's outsides look a bit rusty. But to Della Porta, this boat's a floating fixer-upper. With plenty of TLC -- and a big tab at Home Depot -- the right person, he said, could gut the interior and turn it into one of the coolest living spaces in Boston, a houseboat with style and attitude and maybe a sunken living room where a 1,700 horsepower engine now sits.

Such a makeover would come at a price, as restaurateur David Rockwood can attest. He bought a tug to live on a few years ago after learning the rent would go way up on his Cambridge apartment. Transforming a tug into posh digs, he said, would cost "well in excess of $100,000."

And did anyone mention marina fees? At Boston Harbor Shipyard & Marina in East Boston, where nearly 20 boats, including Rockwood's, double as year-round residences, it would cost about $14,000 a year to dock a tug that's 105 feet long, manager Pat Gately figured. (That fee includes parking and water. Utilities, though, would be extra.)

Such expenses need to be kept in perspective. With the median sales price for a Boston condo now north of $300,000, paying $60,000 for a tug and another $200,000 on renovations doesn't seem quite so outlandish.

"If you're a young guy" who enjoys a long-term remodeling project, "this could be great," Rockwood said. Living on a tug is like "having a second job," he said, but there's a payoff: "I probably have the best deal in the city. My view is the Boston skyline."

As president of Eastern Towboat Corp. in East Boston, the 52-year-old Della Porta has been taken with tugboats for more than 30 years. With a flotilla of five, his company takes jobs towing barges to New York and in and around Boston Harbor.

In 1997, Della Porta bought the New Jersey, the tug now up for sale, for a price he won't disclose. Its engines needed a major overhaul, and the plan was for a long-term refurbishing. In 2001, though, he was able to buy another tug, one with engines in better condition. With one tug too many, and a non-operational tug at that, he decided the New Jersey needed a new home and a new purpose.

Another man might look to sell it for spare parts, but Della Porta would rather not break up a boat he thinks still has many years of life in it. Its construction and quality materials strike him as a marvel of old-fashioned craftsmanship.

"To scrap it, that's a terrible end," he said. "I couldn't do it. I love the boat too much."

With scrapping off the table, an idea came to Mike Terrill, Della Porta's nephew and Eastern Towboat's operational manager. Several friends had seen the New Jersey, and their reaction had been, "Wow, what a great place this could be to live."

So Della Porta began to explore a sale to someone willing to reincarnate the tug as a houseboat. In all likelihood, that would mean removing the big diesel engine, which could make a great media room with a skylight. The remodeled tug would rely either on a small engine for traveling short distances or on a sister tug to tow it to a marina. Della Porta started taking out newspaper ads last month. He has had about 15 responses. Della Porta talks up the New Jersey's history. Built in 1953, it's a railroad tug, meaning it has a high wheelhouse to allow the pilot to see over the barges of boxcars it once towed from Jersey switchyards to Manhattan and the Bronx. People responding to the ad are surprised by the size, he said. They expect to see "a little wooden tugboat," not a 400-ton behemoth of brass and bronze and Bethlehem steel. The New Jersey, Della Porta said, was built to last a hundred years; alas, its working life was cut short by the proliferation of large trucks.A word of caution to those who find the idea of tugboat life intriguing: The New Jersey is no impulse buy. "You'd want to make sure you had space in a marina before you bought the boat," Gately said.People who live on houseboats generally qualify for the same tax breaks as someone who takes out a mortgage on a conventional home, said Jerry Solomon, who leads workshops on tax issues for the Massachusetts Association of Realtors.In most cases, marina fees would not be tax deductible, he said, and lenders would probably require a houseboat owner to carry more insurance than a typical home owner. "What if your boat got loose during a hurricane and caused a lot of damage?" he said. Despite all the extra work, living on the water is well worth it, tugboat dweller Rockwood said. He and his wife, he said, "wouldn't live anywhere else." Della Porta hopes to find someone with a similar temperament -- and sea legs -- to buy the New Jersey.

"It's a beautiful thing that could give someone so much," he said. "It would be nice to see someone enjoying it every day."
