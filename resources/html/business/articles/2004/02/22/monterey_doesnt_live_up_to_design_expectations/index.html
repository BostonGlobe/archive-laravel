Monterey doesn't live up to design expectations

By Craig Fitzgerald, Globe Correspondent  |  February 22, 2004

Igot pregnant at the 2003 New England International Auto Show. A Ford ergonomics engineer fitted me with what Ford engineers call an "Empathy Belly," which is designed to give male and nonpregnant female engineers a sense of what it's

like to use a vehicle while expecting a child. Donning the weighted vest-like apparatus isn't just a matter of putting your arms in the holes, either. First, I was asked to expel as much air as I could from my lungs, while a rib belt was fastened tightly around my chest. The vest contains two seven-pound lead weights that represent a baby's limbs.

Then they attached a six-pound pouch to the underside of the belly that pressed against my bladder when the suit was finally tightened. That third cup of coffee wasn't such a great idea after all.

The 2004 Mercury Monterey minivan was ergonomically designed by engineers wearing these very suits. Bending over, squeezing into third rows, and operating folding seats with the Empathy Belly provides a unique look into how pregnant women use minivans every day.

But when the Monterey arrived in my driveway a few weeks ago, it had been some months since I'd climbed in and out of it with the Empathy Belly on. How would I remember what I'd learned? Luckily for me, my wife is six months pregnant and was willing to experiment.

The Monterey is the Mercury version of the Ford Freestar, which is the replacement for the Windstar line. In Mercury's press materials, it claims that the Monterey is Mercury's "first long-wheelbase" minivan. Don't get too excited. It's the same size as everyone else's minivan. It's just significantly bigger than the old Villager van.

The Premier trim is the highest of three trim packages available on the Monterey, with Convenience and Luxury trims being the lower two. All Monterey trim levels feature the same 4.2-liter V-6 with 201-horsepower and 263 foot-pounds of torque. That second number is pretty impressive and represents the highest torque figure in any minivan. But it's unfortunately sopped up by a curb weight of more than 4,300 pounds.

The Premier includes a load of standard equipment from the Convenience and Luxury trims (such as heated leather seats, power-adjustable pedals, dual zone climate control, and power sliding doors) and then ups the ante with cooled seats, perforated leather, and the AdvanceTrac stability and traction control system.

Here's the thing with traction control: It's wonderful, but you might as well save the money if you're not going to invest in a set of snow tires. In three inches of snow, the Monterey's AdvanceTrac system limited power to the front wheels to the point that I sat halfway up my driveway at a dead stop. The 16-inch tires were quiet on the road, but are for summer use only.

Seats all around are about as comfortable as a minivan can get. I had my wife Lisa get in and out of the first two rows and they worked as advertised. Second- and third-row seats are easy to get in and out of, thanks to one-handed operation of the fold-and-tumble feature on the second row.

I also removed all of the seats to pick up a 1978 Vespa Rally 200 motorscooter from a friend's house in Natick. The third row was a snap. With a pull of two straps it simply folds flat into the load floor, though it does leave a little hump right at the door opening.

The second row wasn't so easy. The dual captain's chairs fold forward easy enough, but the mechanism that latches them to the floor is stubborn. It took me a good 15 minutes with the owner's manual to determine it just needed a bit of persuasion to get the seats out.

Once out, the seats are heavy and awkward -- way too heavy for a pregnant woman or an elderly person to move. So what's the point of being removable? A lighter seat with better carry points is definitely the order of the day.

Tying the scooter down was a bit of a challenge, since there are no tie-down points in the load floor. The seat mounting points can be utilized, but there

are no points to hook to in the back of the van. With that notable exception, Lisa had no trouble operating any of the Monterey's features. But at $35,525, the Monterey doesn't really do anything that any other minivan already does exceptionally well.
