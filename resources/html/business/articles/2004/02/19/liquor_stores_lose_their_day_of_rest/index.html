Liquor stores lose their day of rest

Critics say costs dilute added sales

By Matt McDonald, Globe Correspondent  |  February 19, 2004

On Sunday, Natick resident Garrett Tunison decided to make baked cod for dinner for his girlfriend, who was sick. The recipe for the topping called for sherry, which he didn't have.

So he went to Natick Wine & Spirits off Route 135 to buy some.

As simple as that seems, it would not have been possible just a few weeks ago, before Natick selectmen, acting under a new state law, allowed liquor stores in the town to open on Sundays.

''I think it's great," said Tunison, 31, with a bottle of sherry tucked under one arm.

But the new Sunday opening law, which went into effect in January and was intended to help boost the state economy, is dividing liquor store owners across Boston's western suburbs. Some welcome the change, some say they feel forced to adapt, and some are simply reluctant to give up their Sunday break.

''My feeling is, on Saturday nights, when the store closes, I can relax," said Pauline Mullen, who is in the process of selling Boylston Wine & Liquor on Shrewsbury Street (though not because of the new law).

While the new law does not require liquor stores to open on Sunday, many store owners say it might as well, since many of their competitors have chosen to open.

''I hate it," said Maria Monteiro, who owns Stephen's Liquor Mart on Main Street in Waltham. ''All the stores around here are open and I don't want to lose all of my customers, so I have to stay open."

But Jeff Brown, owner of All Star Liquor Mart of Newton, said the change in the law is necessary to reflect people's evolving shopping habits.

''I thought it was a long time coming," Brown said. ''Is there any retail business in Massachusetts that isn't open on Sunday? It was totally ridiculous."

The Sunday liquor store ban was among the last of the so-called Blue Laws, which reach back to Colonial times and once restricted almost all commerce on Sunday.

Legislators had chipped away at the liquor store law in recent years, allowing package stores to open on Sundays between Thanksgiving and Christmas, and allowing stores in towns within 10 miles of the state line to open on Sundays year-round, so they could compete with liquor stores in neighboring states.

Those chinks in logic irked some, such as Brad Brasher, 36, of Natick, who is happy that he was able to shop at Natick Wine & Spirits this past Sunday. ''I think it's a good thing, personally. Basically, I just think it was hypocritical that the stores were closed on Sundays for 11 months of the year and then open one month," Brasher said. ''It just seems like a law that didn't need to exist."

For many consumers, like Jill Kamphaus, 25, of Natick, who was used to planning ahead on Saturdays, the ability to buy liquor on Sunday increases convenience. ''It just makes things a little easier," she said.

But many who operate small stand-alone liquor-only stores say opening on Sundays might actually cost them money, since they don't expect customers will drink more alcohol, and utilities and wages will add to their costs.

While Bob Anspach, manager of Framingham Liquors on Marble Street, said Super Bowl Sunday was good for business, he predicts that in the long run his store will do roughly the same six days' worth of business over seven days. ''We complemented what was our normal Saturday business. It's as simple as that. We didn't do more, we didn't do less," Anspach said.

Since the new law went into effect, most towns and cities west of Boston, at the request of store owners, have allowed stores to open on Sundays. But the policies are all over the clock. According to state law, cities and towns can allow liquor stores to open from noon to 11 p.m. on Sundays, or until 11:30 p.m. when followed by a legal holiday on Monday.

But local government officials can set further restrictions. Some towns, such as Berlin, Medway, and Sudbury, are setting closing times at 6 p.m. on Sunday.

It's 8 p.m. in Waltham, Natick, and Marlborough; while 9 p.m. is the witching hour for spirits in Wayland. Other towns are letting store owners decide and then writing the closing time into their permit.

Local officials are setting closing times they think work for local package stores and fit with the community.

In Sudbury, for instance, the Board of Selectmen chose the 6 p.m. closing time after considering requests from the three stores in town that sell alcohol for 5 p.m., 6 p.m., and 9 p.m., respectively.

Selectmen tried to balance the needs of the license-holders with the type of town Sudbury is, said Kirsten Roopenian, who chairs the board.

''We're not an open-late community. We're a quiet suburban community," Roopenian said.

Legislators are still tinkering. Currently, liquor stores can open on Sunday only if a city or town expressly allows it.

By spring, a new law will likely allow liquor stores to open on Sunday as a matter of right unless the local government expressly forbids it, said a spokesman for state Representative Daniel E. Bosley, a North Adams Democrat and House chairman of the Legislature's Joint Committee on Government Regulations.

The law should also allow store owners to decide on hours, said Frank Anzalotti, executive director of Massachusetts Package Stores Association Inc., which represents about 650 independent liquor stores statewide and is headquartered in West Springfield.

The association took no position on Sunday openings before the law because of disagreement among members -- a survey about three years ago found about 65 percent against Sunday openings and about 35 percent in favor.

''I think it's the nature of the store, the product line it sells, the clientele it has, and where it's located that really determines the desires of the store owner and the profitability of the store," Anzalotti said.

Liquor stores near supermarkets, for instance, are likely to pick up business on Sundays, since Sunday is generally the busiest shopping day for groceries. This past Sunday, Natick Wine & Spirits had steady foot traffic, including many shoppers from the nearby grocery store, Roche Bros., which does not sell liquor.

''We're getting extra business from Sundays. Business has been very good," said Peter Turlo, who owns Natick Wine & Spirits. ''Everybody's looking for that one-stop shopping."

Convenience stores that carry alcohol are also likely to prosper, since most tended to stay open on Sundays even before the new law. ''Business sales have shot up," said Richard Aronovitz, president of Lincoln Liquors, which operates convenience stores that sell alcohol in Milford and Westborough. ''We know that it will be dramatic and have a very positive effect on our yearlong bottom-line numbers."
