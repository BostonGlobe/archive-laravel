BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Moody's raises CareGroup debt rating

February 6, 2004

CareGroup HealthCare System, a group of loosely affiliated hospitals and doctors led by Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, got a boost this week when Moody's Investors Service increased the rating on their bonds to investment grade. Moody's had downgraded the network's rating to "junk" status in August 2001 when the system was losing millions of dollars a year. However, saying the organization posted another "year of steady improvement," Moody's raised the rating on $604.7 million in outstanding debt to the lowest level of investment grade. In the fiscal year that ended Sept. 30, 2003, CareGroup whittled its operating deficit to $17.4 million, Moody's reported. (Liz Kowalczyk)

Amtrak to offer AT&T Wireless Internet

Amtrak and AT&T Wireless Services Inc. will deploy WiFi high-speed Internet access at the Route 128 railroad station in Westwood and five other Northeast Corridor train stations in early summer. Access will be available for $10 a day or through AT&T WiFi plans that include unlimited monthly usage for $70. The five other Amtrak stations where "wireless fidelity" service will be offered are Providence, Pennsylvania Station in New York, 30th Street Station in Philadelphia, Wilmington, Del., and Baltimore Penn Station. An Amtrak spokeswoman said service is not being rolled out immediately at Back Bay and South Stations in Boston because of "real estate issues" being negotiated with the Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority, which shares the stations with Amtrak. (Peter J. Howe)

Ex-chairman of Newton firm charged

The former chairman of a Newton real estate company has been indicted by a federal grand jury for allegedly misappropriating approximately $9 million in client funds, US Attorney Michael Sullivan said. Daniel E. Carpenter, 48, of Simsbury, Conn., was charged with mail and wire fraud in a 19-count indictment. Carpenter was the chairman of Benistar Property Exchange Trust Co., a now-defunct corporation based in Newton, and its parent corporation, Benistar Ltd., based in Simsbury. The indictment charges Carpenter with misappropriating client funds that had been given to Benistar Property to hold in escrow, using the money to make high-risk trades in the options market. (AP)

THE NATION

Assurant surges in 1st day of trading

In the first billion-dollar IPO of the year, stock in insurer Assurant Inc. debuted on the New York Stock Exchange, rising more than 12 percent above its initial public offering price. Shares of the US insurance arm of Dutch-Belgian financial services group Fortis closed their first day of trading at $24.70 -- up $2.70 or 12.27 percent above the IPO price of $22 set Wednesday. The stock was the most active on the NYSE, with more than 23 million shares changing hands. Fortis Insurance NV, a unit of Fortis, was offering 80 million common shares in the spin-off. It hoped to raise up to $1.76 billion. If the underwriters, led by Morgan Stanley, exercise the option to buy another 12 million shares to cover overallotments, the IPO could raise more than $2 billion in proceeds. Credit Suisse First Boston and Merrill Lynch will assist. Assurant will not receive any proceeds from the offering and Fortis will retain a 45 percent stake in the company after the IPO. (Reuters)

Morgan Stanley suffers setback in suit

Morgan Stanley, the world's second-biggest securities firm by capital, must provide internal documents that it claimed were privileged to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission in a federal bias suit against the bank. US District Judge Richard M. Berman in New York affirmed a magistrate's rejection of a bank argument that a "self-critical analysis privilege" barred EEOC access to documents made during a review of "diversity issues" by an outside consultant. Berman's opinion said in his review of US Magistrate Judge Ronald L. Ellis's ruling that the bank's grounds for withholding the six documents were insufficient "in the absence of `information about individual persons.' " The EEOC sued the bank in 2001 on behalf of about 100 female employees who claimed they were treated unfairly in pay and promotions. (Bloomberg)

Tyco judge rebuffs bid for dismissal

Kozlowski Former Tyco International Ltd. chief executive L. Dennis Kozlowski lost his bid for dismissal of criminal charges that he took unauthorized loans and bonuses and defrauded Tyco shareholders. State Supreme Court Justice Michael Obus ruled in New York that prosecutors, who wrapped up their case Tuesday against Kozlowski and former chief financial officer Mark Swartz, have presented enough evidence to let jurors decide whether the two men stole $170 million in unauthorized bonuses and loans and defrauded Tyco shareholders of another $430 million. The ruling forces Kozlowski and Swartz to begin presenting their defense to nearly all of the original charges on Monday, after four months of trial. If convicted, the two former executives might get as many as 30 years in prison. (Bloomberg)

Tower Records considering bankruptcy

Tower Records, the pioneering record retailer that invented the music megastore, is likely to file for bankruptcy in the next week, a source close to the matter said. The bankruptcy could clear the way for a potential buyer, the source said, speaking on the condition of anonymity. MTS Inc., the privately held parent of the chain, declined to comment on the possible Chapter 11 filing. Such a filing would be nearly a year after MTS decided to sell Tower because it could not pay off $5.2 million in debt. (AP)

THE WORLD

CIBC replaces chief executive

Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce, under investigation by US regulators for financing illegal mutual fund trades, said David Kassie resigned as its top investment banker. Shares fell C$1.45, or 2.2 percent, to C$64.80, their biggest drop in more than eight months. Kassie, former chief executive of CIBC World Markets, left to pursue "other interests," a Canadian Imperial spokesman said. Gerry McCaughey, 47, vice chairman of asset management, will replace Kassie, Canada's number three bank said. Kassie's departure comes two days after Paul Flynn, a former managing director in New York, was arrested and charged by US authorities with helping finance illegal mutual fund trades. (Bloomberg)
