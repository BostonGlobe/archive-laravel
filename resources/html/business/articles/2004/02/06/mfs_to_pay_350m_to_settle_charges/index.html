MFS to pay $350m to settle charges

Two executives barred for 3 years from top posts in mutual fund industry

By Andrew Caffrey and Jeffrey Krasner, Globe Staff  |  February 6, 2004

MFS Investment Management yesterday agreed to pay $350 million to settle fraud charges with regulators, and its two top executives were dealt among the most severe penalties yet in the short history of the market-timing scandal ripping through the mutual fund industry.

John W. Ballen and Kevin J. Parke are barred from holding top-level positions in the mutual fund industry for three years as punishment for allegedly allowing investors to rapidly trade in and out of 11 company mutual funds, despite policies that prohibited market-timing trades.

Securities attorneys said the suspensions taint the reputations of two rising talents in the industry, especially 44-year-old Ballen, who rose from star stock-picker to become chief executive in 2002. Yet these lawyers said the settlement is also carefully crafted to allow the pair to one day return to money management.

Regulators said MFS, the third-largest fund firm in Boston, steered timers to certain funds from 1999 to October 2003, even though a senior company employee warned about the damages from such trading in a June 2002 company presentation that included a chart titled, "Market Timing Wheel of Terror."

Regulators said the profits reaped by the market timers cost MFS's long-term investors $175 million, which the company will repay shareholders as part of the settlement with the Securities and Exchange Commission. MFS also will pay another $50 million in penalties, while Ballen and Parke will pay around $300,000 each. In May 2003, market timers had $2 billion invested in MFS funds.

Separately, MFS agreed to cut its fund-management fees by $25 million annually over five years to settle with New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer. The settlement includes $1 million to New Hampshire to pay for investor education. The state also won a concession that MFS disclose the settlement terms to its clients. The MFS investigation was conducted by the SEC, Spitzer, and the New Hampshire Bureau of Securities Regulation.

The SEC said Ballen approved an internal policy that allowed excessive trading in 11 MFS funds, and that he and Parke, the president and chief investment officer, implemented the policy inside MFS.

"We think the sanctions show the message that anyone in the mutual fund industry involved in fraud will be severely punished," said Peter H. Bresnan, acting administrator of the SEC's Boston office, who led the investigation.

In a statement yesterday, MFS chairman Jeffrey L. Shames acknowledged that while it faces "many challenges ahead, the entire company is focused on shoring up the trust that fund investors and clients have long placed in MFS and delivering on the high standards that all of our constituencies expect of the company."

MFS parent Sun Life Financial Inc. said that chief fixed income officer Robert J. Manning will replace both Ballen and Parke.

MFS, Ballen, and Parke consented to the regulators' orders without admitting or denying the findings. Attorneys for Ballen and Parke did not return phone calls seeking comment. MFS spokesman John Reilly said their future employment with the company has not been determined.

The settlement "demonstrates to long-term investors that unethical and illegal selling practices will not be tolerated," said Mark Connolly, New Hampshire's director of securities regulation.

MFS had argued that the 11 funds were so large that investors could not be hurt by market timing. But the SEC yesterday released internal documents that showed Ballen and Parke and other MFS employees had evidence of direct harm caused by such trading.

In 2002 and 2003 portfolio managers and other employees repeatedly complained about market timers adversely affecting the funds, including Ballen himself at one point. Once, the manager of the flagship Massachusetts Investors Trust fund was forced to draw on the fund's line of credit because of the timers' trading. Yet regulators said MFS and Ballen and Parke did not act to protect the funds.

Regulators said MFS reaped millions in management fees from the timers, while Ballen received $57,737 in additional bonus money over four years, and Parke $58,853 as a result of the extra business from timers -- money they will now have to give back.

Ballen cannot have any role in MFS's many operations for nine months, and Parke for six months. Additionally the pair are barred for three years from serving as an officer or director of any money management operation. They are also restricted from other duties during that period; for example, they are barred from involvement in the sales of MFS funds.

Richard M. Phillips, a securities attorney for Kirkpatrick & Lockhart in San Francisco, said the suspensions "effectively severely impairs their ability to serve in any meaningful capacity in the investment management business for three years."

But the settlement does allow them to return to the money management business as, for example, a portfolio manager, or to manage others involved in investment decisions. They could also go off to work in the less regulated world of hedge funds, attorneys said.

The executives' lawyers "have been very careful about containing the damage," said Julie Allecta, an attorney for Paul, Hastings, Janofsky & Walker in San Francisco. "If these guys want to stay in the money management business, there's room for them to do this. MFS would be crazy to bring them back, but there's a lot of room for them to maneuver here."

Additionally, in a swipe at the independent trustees of MFS's mutual funds, the settlement with Spitzer requires the trustees to hire a "senior executive" to ensure the funds pay reasonable management fees -- one of the functions of fund boards. In a statement, the trustees said previous fee negotiations "were done at arm's length with a view toward serving the best interests of shareholders."
