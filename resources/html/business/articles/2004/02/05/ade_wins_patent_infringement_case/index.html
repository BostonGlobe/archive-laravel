BUSINESS IN BRIEF

ADE wins patent infringement case

February 5, 2004

KLA-Tencor Corp., the world's largest semiconductor inspection equipment maker, lost a bid for $8.4 million in damages from competitor ADE Corp. when a jury decided that one of its patents is invalid. The federal court jury in Wilmington, Del., concluded KLA's invention, patented in 2001, wasn't new and was incompletely described in patent documents. ADE lawyer Juanita Brooks told jurors KLA was trying "to stop ADE from selling its own product." KLA and ADE have been getting new orders as an increase in consumer spending for digital cameras, DVD players, and other electronics helped boost the semiconductor industry after three years of lagging sales. The verdict was revealed after the close of regular market trading. (Bloomberg)

Two International Place signs Choate Hall

The law firm Choate, Hall & Stewart has signed a letter of intent to lease 156,000 square feet of space on nine floors of Two International Place in Boston's Financial District. A move is planned from Exchange Place, where the firm occupies 126,000 square feet and was one of the first tenants after that tower, at 53 State St., opened in 1984. The pending lease is welcome news for Donald J. Chiofaro, manager and part-owner of International Place, whose two towers total 1.8 million square feet, after recently reporting a 20 percent vacancy rate. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

Brand president resigns from Reebok

Reebok International Ltd., the number two US athletic shoe maker, said Reebok brand president Martin Coles resigned for personal reasons. Chief operating officer Jay Margolis has assumed Coles's duties, Canton's Reebok said. Coles, 48, oversaw the expansion of Reebok's Rbk line, part of the company's efforts to attract younger shoppers, the biggest purchasers of sneakers. US sales of Reebok-branded shoes rose 17 percent in the fourth quarter. (Bloomberg)

Redevelopment proposals sought

A request for redevelopment proposals was issued for the 2.9-acre former Massachusetts Mental Health Center location in the Longwood Medical and Academic Area. The state Division of Capital Asset Management is seeking a private master developer to rehab or replace the 91-year-old clinical, training, and research complex and to develop a portion of it for compatible private use. The center was relocated temporarily late last year to the Shattuck Hospital in Boston. Proposals are due May 21. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

SEC sues N.Y. man over Fleet trades

The Securities and Exchange Commission sued a New York man who it said used inside information to buy FleetBoston Financial Corp. stock before Bank of America Corp. disclosed a plan to buy the company. The suit, filed in New York federal court, claims that Kenneth Martin, while working for an unidentified consulting firm, in October purchased 6,200 shares of FleetBoston before Bank of America said it would buy the bank. Previously, he had bought 1,000 shares of FleetBoston's predecessor, BankBoston Corp., in 1999 after learning Fleet Financial Corp. intended to acquire BankBoston, the SEC said. Jeffrey Plotkin, Martin's attorney, said his client apologizes for his conduct and that the SEC agreed to settle the lawsuit. He declined to comment on what the settlement was or Martin's former employer. Martin's employer had been hired as a consultant on the banks' employee benefit programs. Martin gained $47,266 in the trades, the SEC said. Martin couldn't be reached to comment. SEC senior trial attorney Dorothy Heyl declined to comment. (Bloomberg)

Sebago shoes factory to be shut down

Production of Sebago shoes is ending in Maine and the famous brand will join the likes of G.H. Bass, Dexter, and Cole Haan in making shoes at factories outside the United States. Sebago, which made shoes at a Westbrook, Maine, plant, was purchased last year by Wolverine World Wide, the Michigan company whose major brands include Hush Puppy and Merrell, officials said. Sebago employed about 360 workers in Maine two years ago but the number had dwindled to 75 in Westbrook currently. Wolverine World Wide, which completed its purchase of Sebago last November, said Monday it will close the plant in April. Company officials could not be reached to comment. (AP)

THE NATION

Factory orders rose 1.1% in December

America's factories saw orders rebound in December, rising by a strong 1.1 percent, a fresh sign that the national economy's recovery was in full stride as it headed into the new year. The over-the-month increase reported by the Commerce Department came after orders placed with factories dropped by 0.9 percent in November. The latest snapshot of manufacturing activity was better than economists were expecting. They were forecasting an 0.3 percent rise in orders for December. Much of the strength in December reflected stronger demand for "nondurable" goods, such as food products and apparel. For all of 2003, orders to US factories rose by 3.9 percent -- the best showing since 2000, when the economy was still enjoying a record expansion -- and a big improvement from the 1.9 percent decline registered in 2002. (AP)

IBM to manage 21 Sprint call centers

IBM will take over much of Sprint Corp.'s customer services operations, Sprint said. The five-year agreement calls for IBM to manage 21 Sprint call centers already owned and operated by outside contractors, as well as a Sprint-owned call center in Nashville. The IBM deal also calls for the computer company to sell its customers Sprint phone and data services, and for Sprint to pay nearly $100 million to deploy an IBM technology that enables customers to transfer desktop computer applications to a mobile device. No other financial terms of the agreement were disclosed. (AP)

NYSE to mull increased electronic trades

The New York Stock Exchange's board of directors will hear tentative plans today to increase the amount of electronic trading at the exchange, an effort to mollify critics who believe orders are executed too slowly, according to sources close to the effort. The board will discuss a variety of proposals to increase the use of its electronic trading capabilities, which to date have accounted for only 6 percent of all trades on the NYSE, sources said on condition of anonymity. The current system also caps the number of shares that can be traded electronically at 1,099 per order, and each electronic order is subject to a 30-second waiting period. The new preliminary plans, championed by chief executive John Thain, include doing away with the 30-second rule and removing or expanding the cap on order volume. (AP)

THE WORLD

Brazil central bank denies resignation

Meirelles Brazil's Central Bank denied that bank president Henrique Meirelles had resigned, knocking down a report by a local news agency. The report cited market rumors that Meirelles had resigned over disagreements over monetary policy. The bank has faced criticism after it decided last month to hold interest rates unchanged after seven straight months of cuts. This week Finance Minister Antonio Palocci sought to quash market speculation there is a rift within the government over economic policy. He also said rates should continue falling despite the bank's decision last month. A former executive at FleetBoston Financial Corp., Meirelles was tapped by President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva to head the Central Bank in January 2003. Meirelles' credentials as a former executive at a US bank have helped reassure financial markets (Reuters)
