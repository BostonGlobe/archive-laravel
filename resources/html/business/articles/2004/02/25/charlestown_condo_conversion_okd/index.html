BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Charlestown condo conversion OK'd

February 25, 2004

To help revitalize a piece of the Charlestown Navy Yard, the Boston Redevelopment Authority's board approved the sale of Constitution Quarters to developer Carlyle CQ Boston LP, which plans to convert a building with 367 rental units into condominiums. With a soft rental market and low mortgage rates available to buyers, many rental buildings are being rehabilitated as condos. (Chris Reidy)

Sunroom contractor's assets frozen

A Suffolk Superior Court judge has frozen the assets of a North Andover contractor accused of defrauding customers out of hundreds of thousands of dollars, according to Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly. Judge Janet L. Sanders issued the preliminary injunction on Monday against contractor James A. Brien and his company, American Sunroom Co. Inc., at Reilly's request, Reilly's office said. Reilly filed a lawsuit on Feb. 9 against the company that stems from complaints from customers who claimed they paid the company deposits of $15,000 to $28,000 to install sunrooms the company never built, Reilly's office said. A message left for the company's phone listing was not immediately returned. A recording on its voice mail stated "we hope to work through the issues at hand very quickly." (AP)

Sonus, Motorola sign marketing pact

Sonus Networks Inc., of Chelmsford, signed a deal with Motorola Inc. to sell Sonus equipment to wireless carriers. Motorola will incorporate Sonus's media gateway device with a Motorola switching system used to offer wireless voice and data services carried in Internet protocol format. Sonus gave no revenue estimate. Motorola reported fourth-quarter sales of $1.4 billion for its unit that sells to telecom carriers. Sonus shares, hammered since the company said on Jan. 20 that it was delaying reporting 2003 earnings because of accounting problems, closed at $5.37, down 1.5 percent. (Peter J. Howe)

Suit blocking Fleet merger dismissed

A federal judge has dismissed a lawsuit brought by Native Hawaiians against the Federal Reserve Board seeking to halt the merger of Bank of America Corp. and FleetBoston Financial Corp., saying he lacked jurisdiction to decide the case. The plaintiffs had wanted the judge to require the board to block the merger on the grounds that Bank of America failed to comply with commitments it had made to provide mortgage financing for Native Hawaiians. Commitment to community is one factor the Fed considers in approving mergers. US District Judge David Alan Ezra criticized Bank of America for failing to meet its obligations, and said the board should consider those concerns. (AP)

THE NATION

Older workers' special treatment OK'd

The US Supreme Court ruled 6-3 yesterday that younger workers can't sue employers when older colleagues get preferential treatment. The justices said the law that protects older employees from age discrimination doesn't apply in reverse. Groups such as the AARP, the largest advocacy group for people 50 and older, praised the decision. "If the court had decided the other way, it would have ignored that older workers face very significant problems in this country and always have," said Laurie McCann, an AARP lawyer. The court ruled in a case brought by about 200 General Dynamics Corp. workers in their 40s who claimed they suffered a type of reverse discrimination because they were too young to get benefits being offered to older colleagues. They sued when the company changed its retirement benefits in 1997. Until then, longtime employees could retire with full health benefits. Under a new union contract, only those 50 or older could receive full health benefits after retirement. (AP)

IBM restricts use of stock options

Executives at IBM Corp. have a new string attached to their stock options: They can cash out only if the company's shares rise more than 10 percent. IBM executives said the decision makes the top 300 executives more accountable. An option's "strike" price -- the price at which stock can be bought at a certain point in the future -- will be set 10 percent above the market price on the day the options are granted. For IBM executives to still get market-priced options, they must spend part of their cash bonus on buying IBM stock at the same price, and hold it for at least three years. That part of the plan will begin next year. The new rules do not apply to the rest of the 85,000 IBM employees who get options or restricted stock. (AP)

Federated's quarterly earnings up 35%

Federated Department Stores Inc. reported a 35 percent increase in fourth-quarter earnings, helped by strong holiday sales. The company, whose stores include Macy's and Bloomingdale's, earned $460 million, or $2.50 per share, in the quarter ended Jan. 31, compared with $341 million, or $1.78 per share, a year earlier. Excluding one-time items, Federated earned $2.29 per share, 3 cents higher than the $2.26 estimate of analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call. The company had a $38 million reduction in deferred income tax liabilities in the fourth quarter. Sales were virtually unchanged at $5.05 billion, vs. $5.02 billion a year earlier. (AP)

Home Depot says profit rose 39%

The Home Depot said a nearly 39 percent jump in fourth-quarter profit is proof its strategy to pump billions into retraining employees, improving customer service, and renovating stores is working. The earnings beat Wall Street expectations and grew faster than the quarterly profit reported a day earlier by its chief rival, Lowe's Cos. Home Depot earned $951 million, or 42 cents a share, for the three months ended Feb. 1, compared to $686 million, or 30 cents, a year earlier. Analysts surveyed by Thomson were expecting 39 cents a share. Home Depot's sales were $15.13 billion, compared to $13.21 billion in the year-ago period. (AP)

Senator aims to block nomination

US Senator Byron Dorgan said he will slow Mark McClellan's nomination to head the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services because the Food and Drug Administration chief has hurt Americans' access to low-cost Canadian drugs. Under McClellan, the FDA has been involved in a "relentless campaign to defeat" drug importation, the North Dakota Democrat said. The objection of a single senator often is enough to prevent action on a nomination because it signals that leaders may have to spend time fighting a filibuster. President Bush, who nominated McClellan, is counting on him in an election year to help guide the biggest expansion of Medicare in 40 years. (Bloomberg)
