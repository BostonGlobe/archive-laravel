Key to boardroom remains elusive

Women still underrepresented at Mass., US firms

By Kimberly Blanton, Globe Staff  |  February 25, 2004

Women in Boston business face a stubborn reality: Corporate boards are still men's clubs.

Women held just 9 percent of board seats at the 100 largest public companies in Massachusetts in 2003, according to a recent report. Half of those companies have no women directors at all, including a shoe company, a fabric manufacturer, and a laundry-service company.

Those least surprised by the low figure are women at the top.

"There hasn't been much progress since 2001," when a similar survey was done, said Toni Wolfman, partner with the Boston law firm Foley Hoag, who is active in The Boston Club, which advocates for women executives and sponsored the report. The 2003 study, discussed yesterday at a Greater Boston Chamber of Commerce event, ranked companies by revenues; the 2001 study surveyed 100 companies on the basis of a composite of financial measures.

There has been little progress between 1997 and today, a period of economic boom and bust and post-Enron turmoil in the nation's boardrooms. In 1997, a similar survey showed, women held just 8 percent of Massachusetts board seats.

Passage of the Sarbanes Oxley Act of 2002 was expected to force companies to cast a wider net and seat more women on boards. The act required corporations to bring on more board members without ties to the company they oversee and with appropriate expertise.

Boston is not unique. Nationwide, women hold 13.6 percent of all Fortune 500 seats. Patricia Flynn, a Bentley College management professor who conducted The Boston Club study, said that data show women occupy 12 to 13 percent of board seats of Fortune 500 companies in or near Atlanta, Chicago, and Boston.

"They're all bad," Flynn said yesterday prior to speaking before the chamber. Flynn noted that women are half of the US labor force and make 80 percent of purchasing decisions.

Women have difficulty gaining board seats, observers say, because they do not have numerous contacts among directors and chief executives, who are predominantly men. Also, few women are top executives at big companies, making them less visible. To a lesser extent, they leave careers to take care of their children, narrowing the field of candidates.

Boston, with its large technology industry, may have "fewer women with the technical background" required for tech firms, said Kristine Langdon, director for Spencer Stuart, a Chicago-based executive search firm that this month placed a woman, C. Kim Goodwin, on Akamai Technologies's board. Goodwin is managing director of State Street Research & Management Co.

Women on boards, though rare, are selected for the same reason men are: They know someone. High-level contacts were key to election to boards, said three prominent women yesterday.

Christine Cournoyer, who sits on the boards at Stride Rite Corp. and GTech Corp.; Sally Crawford, who is on the board at Cytyc Corp., Exact Sciences Inc., and Chittenden Corp.; and Gail Deegan, who is on the board at EMC Corp. and TJX Cos., are all highly accomplished. But their experience shows it was difficult, even for them, to gain access.

The good news is that once women are named to a board or two, other board appointments come easily, Deegan said, adding that search firms "find you."

Deegan's first appointment came while she was chief financial officer of Eastern Gas & Fuel and made regular presentations to its board. She met Eastern director Hal Miller, then Houghton Mifflin Co.'s CEO. He learned she had been an elementary school teacher and invited her to join the publishing company's board.

Cournoyer and Crawford obtained board seats through women's networks. Cournoyer was recommended to Stride Rite by The Boston Club, which compiles names of women candidates.

Crawford said Nancy Leaming, Tufts Health Plan's current chief executive -- still rising then through Tufts's ranks -- referred her to Cytyc Corp. Crawford has extensive experience in healthcare. Cytyc, which makes Pap smear kits to test for cervical cancer, had no women on its board and wanted one, Crawford said.

"It was one personal connection" that led to the post, she said.
