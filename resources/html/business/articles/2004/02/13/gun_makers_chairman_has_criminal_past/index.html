BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Gun maker's chairman has criminal past

February 13, 2004

The new board chairman of Smith & Wesson Holding Corp., the parent company of the famous gun maker, has offered to resign after reports surfaced about his criminal past. James Joseph Minder, 74, spent time behind bars during the 1950s and 1960s for a string of armed robberies and an attempted prison escape in Michigan. Other Smith & Wesson board members were unaware of Minder's criminal record until he called and told most of them. "I offered my resignation . . . and to a man they refused it," Minder said. "As long as it's at their pleasure, I'll stay. Quite frankly, I've done nothing wrong." Asked why he didn't disclose his past to Smith & Wesson earlier, Minder said "nobody asked the question, so I guess I never answered it." Minder said he hasn't owned a firearm for decades and is prohibited from owning any because of his criminal history. He was elected chairman last month, when Smith & Wesson said it would close the offices of its parent company in Scottsdale, Ariz., and transfer all operations to its main plant in Springfield, Mass., where the handgun maker was founded 150 years ago. (AP)

THE NATION

Microsoft source code put on Internet

Microsoft Corp. said portions of its Windows source code -- the tightly guarded blueprints of its dominant operating system -- had been leaked over the Internet. Microsoft spokesman Tom Pilla said some incomplete portions of the Windows 2000 and Windows NT4 source code had been "illegally made available on the Internet." Access to the source code could allow hackers to exploit the operating system and attack machines running Windows, which is used on hundreds of millions of computers. The company was made aware of the leak yesterday and is investigating, Pilla said. He did not know how much of the code had been leaked, how many people may have gained access to it, and could not immediately pinpoint the source of the leak. The company has contacted law enforcement authorities, he said, adding there was no indication the leak was a result of a breach of Microsoft's corporate network. (AP)

Electric industry vows better compliance

A national electricity council promised to do more to make sure utilities obey rules designed to prevent blackouts like the one that cut power to 50 million people last year. Investigators have discovered that utilities ignored several voluntary safeguards in the months before the blackout hit the Northeast last August. The North American Electric Reliability Council -- whose members include companies that transfer power from generators to customers -- agreed to collect and review all reported violations. Power companies must report any violations within a month, and the council will set a deadline for correcting the problem. (AP)

McDonald's signs agreement with Yao

Yao McDonald's Corp. signed pro basketball player Yao Ming to a multiyear endorsement contract, a month after the world's largest restaurant chain ended its relationship with Los Angeles Lakers All-Star Kobe Bryant. The 7-foot-5 Yao, who plays for the National Basketball Association's Houston Rockets, was named a starter in fan balloting in the All-Star Game on Sunday for the second straight year. Yao will appear in commercials, make personal appearances, and be a part of the company's Olympic sponsorship, McDonald's said. Financial terms of the contract weren't disclosed. McDonald's joins several other companies in signing Yao as an endorser who can appeal to people in the United States and Asia. He also has agreements with Reebok International Ltd., PepsiCo Inc., Visa International Inc., and Apple Corp. (Bloomberg)

United's discount carrier takes off

United Airlines' discount carrier Ted took its first flight from Las Vegas to Denver before dawn, launching a key component of United's strategy to emerge from bankruptcy this summer. United parent UAL Corp. hopes Ted will be competitive with low-cost carriers that are rapidly snatching customers from big airlines. United, the nation's number two airline, has pitched Ted as laid-back fun, with free overhead programming -- Tedevision -- that includes music videos and comedies, and free music on Tedtunes. (AP)

PC sales seen rising 13% in 1st quarter

Personal-computer sales will rise 13 percent in the first quarter on higher demand for portable devices by consumers, market researcher Gartner Inc. said. Shipments will increase by 14 percent for all of 2004, with gains of at least 10 percent every quarter as businesses replace PCs bought in 2000, especially in the United States, Stamford, Conn.-based Gartner said. Lower prices and improved wireless products are attracting consumers, Gartner analyst George Shiffler said. Shipments grew 11 percent in 2003, up from a 2.7 percent increase in 2002 and a 4.6 percent decline in 2001, according to Gartner. (Bloomberg)

Lawsuit against CBOT finally dismissed

The Chicago Board of Trade, the second-biggest US futures market, said a lawsuit accusing it of fixing soybean prices and seeking payments of about $50 million was dismissed 15 years after it was filed. Soybean farmers sued the exchange in 1989, claiming it manipulated prices on the July soybean contract, violating antitrust law. The US Appeals Court for the Seventh Circuit in Chicago this week affirmed a lower court decision to dismiss the suit, saying that rather than restraining trade, the CBOT had facilitated it, according to a letter from chairman Charles Carey to members. (Bloomberg)

Financier agrees to cut Revlon debt

Financier Ronald Perelman agreed to spend as much as $775 million to buy shares in Revlon Inc., partly through a debt-for-equity exchange, to cut the money-losing cosmetic maker's debt in half. Fidelity Management & Research Co. will also exchange about $155 million in debt for equity, Revlon said. The moves are expected to reduce debt by about $930 million by 2006. Perelman owns 83 percent of Revlon through his MacAndrews & Forbes holding company. (AP)

THE WORLD

Hostile bid for Aventis clears first hurdle

French drug maker Sanofi-Synthelabo SA said its $60.2 billion hostile bid for bigger rival Aventis SA will open Tuesday. Sanofi said stock market authorities have given their stamp of approval to the bid's "background information," which is a legal filing providing details about the offer. The French stock market watchdog, Autorite des Marches Financiers, or AMF, later confirmed it has cleared the document. The AMF has said it would set a closing date for the bid at a later stage, depending on how long it takes antitrust authorities to review the bid. Under French stock market regulations, Aventis has until Monday to appeal the AMF decision to clear Sanofi to launch the offer. Aventis has rejected the bid, saying it is not in the best interests of its shareholders. (AP)

Etc.

Shares of Fisher Scientific International Inc. rose $5.91, 11.8 percent, to $56.20, after the Hampton, N.H., supplier of lab equipment raised earnings estimates for this year and next. Fisher will earn $2.75 a share to $2.87 in 2004 and $3.30 to $3.50 in 2005, the company said . . . Defense contractor Raytheon Co. said it would terminate a "poison pill" plan that helps protect it against potential takeover bids. The plan is triggered when any shareholder acquires 15 percent of company stock. If that happens, shareholders other than the potential acquirer are allowed to buy stock at half price and increase their relative voting power. Raytheon said the plan would expire March 1. (Globe wire services)
