JOB DOC

Build support, repackage skills on way to success

By Elaine Varelas  |  February 29, 2004

Need advice about managing your career or your workplace? The Job Doc can help. Our specialists can answer your questions on topics ranging from career transitions to management issues. E-mail queries to jobdoc@globe.com, or send letters to Job Doc, c/o the Boston Globe, P.O. Box 55819, Boston MA 02205-5819. Letters may be edited for clarity and length.

Q. What is the best way for me to highlight my talents, eagerness to learn a new trade, and use my current experience as a senior administrative assistant to catapult me out of the clerical role? I left college six years ago due to financial reasons and because I felt I needed to gain "real-world" experience. I've since continued with my education while working full time and expect to graduate within the next year. Though I've achieved many successes and have quickly advanced to my current role, I'm finding myself typecast as a "clerical" when I apply for other positions or enlist the help of an agency. Also cited is a lack of relevant "experience." I would ideally like to move into a career that would allow me to work independently and be creative, with more advancement potential. Any suggestions for future success?

A. Congratulations on returning to school to complete your degree. Working full time and going to school should show your employer that you have the skills they are looking for, including goal orientation, perseverance, and the ability to juggle multiple demands. Your advancement in your roles and responsibilities also demonstrates that others have recognized your skills and potential. Though you didn't tell me in which area you chose to pursue your degree, or which industry you are in, there are a few general guidelines I would suggest.

Let's start with your current job. Your timing is great. As you approach any career or personal milestone -- like achieving a degree -- it becomes easier to explain to an employer why a transition should happen. Is there a manager or human resources representative at your company who you can talk to about your career goals and your frustrations on remaining in an administrative role? Perhaps they can help advocate for you.

By building internal support, you and your advocates will be better able to identify appropriate professional positions within your organization where a transfer may be possible. Are there colleagues whose jobs you covet? Do you have the skills to do those jobs? Can one of your supporters advocate for you to get interviewed when there is an opening for one of these roles?

Looking for "stretch" assignments is also a way to build experience and show others your willingness to take challenges. These assignments need to be situations where you can be resourceful, recognized for your talent, and exposed to colleagues who can serve as additional internal advocates. This may be difficult to do in the department or area where you are the "go to" clerical person, so a change in work groups may be necessary to give you the opportunity to start fresh in a new role with a new image.

If these tactics don't work at your company, begin repackaging your skills and education on your resume and work with the career development staff at your university. Be sure to network with other part-time students, alumni, and the faculty, advises Elaine Goldman, associate director of career services at Simmons College. "Most of these students are also working, and may know of available entry-level professional positions," she said. "Faculty and alumni are usually eager to support both graduating students and hiring organizations."

With your background, working with recruiters may be difficult, as they will be motivated to present you as an administrative person. To maximize your opportunities with search firms, focus on those that specialize in your areas of education and interest, with entry-level positions to fill. And though I dislike saying it as much as you will dislike hearing it -- you may need to take a slight cut in pay. While pursuing a longer-term opportunity may feel like starting over, changing direction now rather than waiting any longer will get you started on your new path.

Opportunity to re-evaluate

Q. I'm in my midfifties and recently unemployed after being fired for the first time in my career for performance reasons (unjustly I feel). How do I explain my departure to a potential employer, and the fact that I don't want them to talk to my previous boss? Also, I'm not sure I want another management position. How do I explain the fact that I would take a lesser position without arousing suspicions that I would leave when I found a higher one? And lastly, having worked in the same field all my working life, how could I pursue something in my true loves of music or sports?

A. The concerns you state are not uncommon, and many people find themselves in very similar situations.

When an opportunity presents itself to us to re-evaluate our careers, we often fantasize about our options, which is a wonderful way to begin the process. It's your time to re-evaluate your career, your aspirations, and what the world of work means to you. Looking at your career dreams regardless of your age, releases lots of energy and excitement about options and related areas. Sports and music are your dreams, but turning into A-Rod or Rod Stewart are most likely not a reality. That doesn't mean that your passions shouldn't be pursued, though.

When thinking of your areas of interest, try to identify related fields, positions, or supporting industries you'd enjoy and where the skills you've developed over your career would prove valuable. I personally know people who love cars and now work at automobile dealerships and consult to the automotive industry, and baseball fanatics who work for sports equipment distributors. If these aren't possibilities, will having sports or music in your life as an avocation be enough? Consider playing in an adult recreation league or coaching a kids' team; look into becoming a DJ or playing in a band.

Whether you decide to return to your earlier career path or pursue a new one, you will be faced with the reference issue, and evaluating which level in an organization is right for you. There are many reasons that fall into the performance related category of separation. If you cannot work out a mutually agreeable "public statement" with your former supervisor, look for another manager, internal customer or colleague who can speak to your skills and contribution to the organization.

Good interviewers will ask you what your manager would say even if they don't plan on calling, so be prepared. Your response might go something like this, "After XX number of years, my manager was not happy with my performance. I attempted to rectify the situation, and though my internal customers and colleagues were happy with my performance and the project work I did for them, my manager and I were not able to see eye to eye." At this point, you may offer your other company contacts as references.

This presentation, with no anger and good collegial references, should satisfy a hiring manager. Whether you search for a management position, or an individual role, your focus needs to remain on the skills you use and the contribution you make. You can actually use a step back to focus on strengthening and/or learning skills that will aid in working with colleagues in a more comfortable role than manager. By not overly focusing on potential titles or compensation, you may also help demonstrate your flexibility.

Take the time to plan ahead

Q. With the future of my husband's company and his job in question these days, I am planning to sharpen some skills and think about what I might like to do with my next career, should he be out of work for a while. I wondered if you have any suggestions on computer skills I should know or areas that might be interesting for a woman of my years to start into. It feels very strange to think about starting a new career at 45 instead of 25. I don't even know if it's worth putting the old job experience on a resume, since it was so many years ago.

A. Planning ahead remains a great skill, even when the causes for planning may not be what you'd hoped for. Your question is one more and more women are asking as they consider returning to work -- whatever the circumstance.

As you look at your next steps, have you outlined what is most important to you and your family in regard to your career? Is it cash compensation? Health benefits? Flexible hours? Working close to home? Personal and career fulfillment? Is this a short- or long-term plan?

You get the picture. As much as I'd like to say you can have it all, it's more realistic to start by prioritizing what you want and/or need, and target careers and companies based on that information. There are opportunities out there, and typically there are trade-offs. If you get healthcare benefits, your hourly rate is low. If you are on commission, you may not get benefits. You may work close to home, and get benefits but have standard hours. This initial prioritization stage can often save frustration later in the process.

Another area to explore is your passion. Are you a gardener? A shopaholic? A gourmet cook? An exercise fanatic? Use the network you have built over the years to find opportunities at the local nursery, boutique, bakery or fitness center.Are there small businesses you can offer to partner with? For example, if you are an experienced seamstress, you could give classes at a fabric or crafts store, or partner with a clothing store to provide alterations. Many women have returned to work building off one of their personal areas of interest by either starting a business, partnering with others with similar interests, or joining a company whose focus aligns with their own. Try to find a way to couple your areas of interest with paid work.

You also asked about what to include on your resume and what computer skills you should learn. Your resume will need to include your past work history, and certainly any related volunteer or community activities with which you've been involved. Don't forget to include school support activities and charitable fund-raising projects.

The computer skills you may want to learn depend on the type of jobs that you are pursuing. Most office positions require a working knowledge of Microsoft programs including Word, Excel, and Outlook. Other specialized fields such as graphic or Web designers use more specialized platforms like Quark and Pagemaker. Many community colleges, centers for adult education, and even computer stores, offer courses on different types of software applications.

Sharpening existing skills, and learning new ones, will be worthwhile. They can be added to your resume as a technical skill and also help you to feel more confident in what you have offer an employer.

Elaine Varelas has over 20 years of career development experience and is currently managing partner of business development at Keystone Partners, a career management firm headquartered in Boston. Reach her at evarelas@keystonepartners.com.
