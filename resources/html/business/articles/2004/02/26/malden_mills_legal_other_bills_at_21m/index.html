BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Malden Mills legal, other bills at $21m

February 26, 2004

Lawyers and restructuring experts who say they saved Malden Mills Industries Inc. from liquidation want about $21 million for their work. Bankruptcy rules require Malden Mills to pay expenses related to its reorganization, which took control from chairman Aaron Feuerstein and granted a majority stake to creditors led by General Electric Co.'s finance arm. "It's shocking," Feuerstein said of the cost. The bankruptcy judge reviewing the bill, however, has a reputation for slashing fees and expenses. Judge Joel Rosenthal's order may come by the end of March. The bill covers two years of work by more than a dozen firms and equals about 6 percent of Malden Mills's sales in that period. (Reuters)

Hasbro wins a higher credit rating

Hasbro Inc., the world's second-biggest toy maker, had its credit rating raised to investment grade by Fitch Ratings after the company lowered debt and increased profit and sales. The increase to BBB- from BB+ affects about $700 million in debt. Hasbro's focus on its classic brands such as Transformers and G.I. Joe -- instead of on hit items like Pokemon -- has paid off, Fitch said. Sales rose 11 percent last year; traditional toy sales increased 23 percent. Hasbro also trimmed costs and pared debt by $369 million. Hasbro, of Pawtucket, R.I., is rated Ba3 by Moody's Investors Service and BB+ by Standard & Poor's. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

Panel raps predatory lending rule

The House Financial Services Committee yesterday voiced concern over new rules that bar states from enforcing predatory lending laws against national banks. The panel voted 34-28 in favor of an amendment questioning whether the federal Office of the Comptroller of the Currency can effectively handle the added responsibility. A day earlier, New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer said the rule are designed to bar state officials from enforcing consumer protection laws the Bush administration doesn't like. Predatory lenders convince people with loans, often minorities or poor people with home mortgages, to take on new debt at excessive costs. The comptroller's office argues the rule, which went into effect last month, still allows states to police financial institutions that aren't national banks. (AP)

ITT Tech campuses searched

Federal agents yesterday searched the headquarters of ITT Technical Institutes and about 10 of its campuses in Indiana, Texas, Virginia, Florida, Louisiana, Nevada, California, and Oregon. Shares of its parent, ITT Educational Services Inc., plunged 33 percent. ITT said the investigation involves grand jury subpoenas of records concerning student placement, retention, graduation, attendance, recruitment, grades, graduates' salaries, and transfers of credits to other colleges. About 68 percent of ITT's 2003 revenue of nearly $523 million came indirectly from federal education aid programs, according to a company filing. (AP)

Pimco defends Canary's trades

In full-page newspaper ads, Pimco executives expressed regret for allowing Canary Capital Management LLC, a major hedge fund client, special trading privileges, but said such activity did not hurt average investors. Executives said the mutual fund trading "was infrequent, did not come close to violating the prospectuses, and harmed no shareholders in the fund." Chief executive Bill Thompson said that "if any investor in our funds was disadvantaged . . . then we want to give assurance we will make it up -- in full." New Jersey's attorney general sued Pimco and parent Allianz Dresdner Asset Management of America last week, charging they defrauded investors by allowing Canary and others to make impropper trades worth $4 billion; in return, Canary invested in funds that generated substantial fees, the suit contends. (AP)

Plan for independent chairmen rapped

A prominent mutual fund official opposed a plan to require fund boards to have independent chairmen, saying it won't prevent abuses. The Securities and Exchange Commission plan "is certainly no silver bullet," James Riepe, vice chairman of T. Rowe Price Group, told the Senate Banking Committee. It's weighing legislation to overhaul the $7 trillion fund industry. Riepe said independent directors cannot and should not be responsible for the day-to-day management of a fund's activities. Experts say that if the SEC plan is adopted, the boards of 80 percent of US mutual funds would have to replace their chairmen. (AP)

Frankel accomplice pleads guilty

Mississippi businessman Marvin Thurston Little, 70, pleaded guilty to federal charges of conspiracy and money laundering related to financier Martin Frankel's theft of insurance company funds. Frankel hired Little, a politically connected real estate developer, to bribe and deceive insurance regulators about the ownership of two Mississippi insurance companies that Frankel owned secretly because he had been barred from securities trading. He stole assets from these companies, and from others in Arkansas, Oklahoma, Missouri, and Tennessee, to finance a lavish lifestyle in Greenwich, Conn. Frankel agreed to pay Little $5 million, plus $30,000 a month, to help him deceive Mississippi regulators. Altogether, Frankel wired Little more than $6 million from a Swiss bank account. Little is scheduled to be sentenced Sept. 24. He faces up to 20 years in prison and must make $6.46 million in restitution. (AP)

THE WORLD

The two Apples open court battle

Apple Computer and the Beatles' record company, Apple Corps, went to court in Britain yesterday over who can use the Apple name now that the computer company has entered the Internet music business. The companies reached a deal in 1991 after a fight over the trademark. But the record company says the computer company broke the agreement by using the Apple name to market its new iTunes Internet music service. Apple Computer asked a court to rule that the battle should occur in California courts. Its lawyer said the 1991 agreement allows Apple Computer to use the name for data transmission, including music. (Reuters)

Another arrest made in Parmalat case

Italian police made another arrest in the Parmalat fraud case, apprehending Romano Bernardoni, a former chairman of a travel company that was part of the business empire of the family of Calisto Tanzi, the jailed Parmalat founder. In Milan, US Securities and Exchange Commission investigators met with Italian prosecutors probing the scandal at the dairy giant. Bernardoni, is accused of false accounting and violation of bankruptcy laws. Tanzi has admitted to diverting up to 500 million euros ($640 million) from Parmalat. Twenty people have been arrested. Parma prosecutor Vito Zincani told Dow Jones Newswires that US and Parma authorities would swap documents in hopes of shedding light on the company. In December, the SEC sued Parmalat, accusing it of "one of the largest and most brazen corporate financial frauds in history." (AP)

EU's Microsoft hearing said to be delayed

European regulators have delayed final hearings in the antitrust case against Microsoft Corp. as settlement talks continue, sources said. The hearings will be March 15 and 22 in Brussels, the sources said. A European Commission spokeswoman declined to comment. A draft ruling finds Microsoft abused its dominant position with Windows to gain market share in markets for media players and servers. (AP)
