THE ADVISER

Funds' defensive positions likely adequate

By Kenneth Hooker  |  February 8, 2004

Q. My problem is preservation of capital. I am sole trustee of a family trust, with two sons who will become co-trustees upon my demise. I am nearly 93 years old, own no real estate, have no auto, nor anything else to list in a will. My wife died in 1998 and I live with an unmarried son. I have excellent medical coverage, including prescriptions. My income and expenses match exactly, at $2,400 monthly. The trust has these assets: $203,000 in Vanguard GNMA fund, $18,000 in Vanguard Intermediate-Term Corporate Bond fund, $73,000 in Vanguard Short-Term Corporate Bond fund, $68,000 in Vanguard Short-Term US Treasury fund, and $3,000 in Vanguard REIT Index fund. There is also $95,000 in CDs and $20,000 in DRIP accounts, balances that are about to be transferred to Vanguard. All of the bond funds are subject to interest-rate risk, with increases likely in the yields and with declines likely in the principal values. I am considering redeeming set amounts at regular intervals and laddering the proceeds in CDs, seeking to avoid loss in principal without losing some income during the period. What do you suggest? L.B., Lexington

A. Yes, it's true that your bond fund positions face the prospect of higher interest rates in the future, with accompanying declines in principal value. But it's also true that most of these funds are pretty well insulated against the principal erosion that usually comes from rising interest rates. This is because the managers of three of these funds have cut their average effective portfolio durations to very low levels. The figures are 2.09 years for Vanguard GNMA, 2.20 years for Vanguard Short-Term Corporate Bond fund, and 2.18 years for Vanguard Short-Term US Treasury fund, according to Morningstar Mutual Funds. It seems to me that all of these funds have taken sufficient defensive measures that you should be willing to ride out the likely storm with them.

The other two Vanguard holdings are a different story. Vanguard Intermediate-Term Corporate Bond fund has a portfolio with a 4.58-year average effective duration. I think that signals more risk than you should be willing to assume. And while the Vanguard REIT fund invests almost entirely in stocks, these stocks are sensitive to interest rates.

Although these two positions are the smallest in your Vanguard portfolio, I suggest that you sell both and put the proceeds in laddered CDs as you suggest. I also suggest that you follow the laddered CD strategy in dealing with the money you are considering moving from the DRIP accounts rather than adding the money to any of your remaining bond fund accounts. Altogether, these moves would give you $41,900 to use in establishing the laddered CDs.

However, I don't suggest that you achieve the ladder by moving money into the CDs over preestablished intervals of time. Nor do I suggest that you build a very long ladder. Figures at www.bankrate.com show the nation's best rate for a one-year CD is 2.20 percent, and that you can get 2.65 percent on a two-year CD.

I suggest you split the $41,900 evenly between those two instruments, planning to replace each maturing CD with a 2-year CD. If interest rates have stabilized or even seem poised to fall, you might consider longer-term CDs.

Help is sought for disabled son

Q. My son is 40 years old and disabled. He receives $564 a month from Social Security Disability, plus about $120 in food stamps. He also gets subsidies for heat, electricity, and transportation. His father has left $30,000 for him, with the money in my name. It is now in a money market account, and I send him a small amount each month, representing the interest earned, keeping the principal intact. How do you advise that I invest this trust money for him? I would like a safe investment with monthly income. R.R., Canton

A. This is a tough season to be setting up fixed-income accounts. The typical instruments of choice -- bonds -- are dangerous, with widely anticipated increases in interest rates likely to bring the value of bond investments down.

For that reason, I have been suggesting that most fixed-income investors more or less "park" their money on the sidelines, anticipating they won't want to be considering bonds for at least the next 24 to 30 months. But parking money doesn't mean settling for the miserable rates now being paid on money market accounts.

You could invest in a 30-month CD, where you could get as much as 3.06 percent, citing the highest rate available as this is written. That rate is offered by First Trade Union Bank in Boston (1-800-242-0272). The interest on these accounts is compounded monthly; if you were to leave the funds in the account for a full year, the compounding would bring the annual return up to 3.1 percent.

If you withdrew the interest each month, it would work out to $76.50, probably more than triple what you are now sending.

Stick with IRAs over annuities

Q. I am 61 years old and plan to start drawing Social Security payments at age 62. I am retired from the Navy and will receive retirement pay of $1,129 monthly for the rest of my life. My wife is still working in the civil service and will receive a monthly pension of $2,000 when she retires next year. I currently have my money invested with Thrivent Financial for Lutherans, with $21,943 in a joint account with my wife in AAL Capital Growth fund, Class A shares. My IRAs are $118,507 in AAL Balanced Fund, Class A shares; $1,752 in AAL Capital Growth fund, Class B shares; $33,803 in AAL Capital Growth fund, Class A shares; $34,279 in AAL Equity Income fund, Class A shares; and $1,699 in AAL Aggressive Growth fund, Class A shares. In addition, my wife has an $18,000 annuity with Thrivent and I have a $5,000 annuity there. I have $65,029 in a credit union savings account, and will soon have a $45,000 inheritance coming to me. My adviser at Thrivent suggests I move my IRA accounts to a no-load variable annuity account, which would give me more investment options, better diversification, and would protect principal in case of my death. It would also have a guaranteed 5% increase in value each year. Accounts he suggested are 15 percent US Large-Cap Growth Stocks, 19 percent US Large-Cap Value Stocks, 3 percent US Mid-Cap Stocks, 6 percent US Small-Cap Stocks, 15 percent International Stocks, 14 percent High-Yield Bonds, 4 percent Intermediate-Long-Term Bonds, 4 percent Short-Intermediate-Term Bonds, and 10 percent cash. What do you suggest?

D.S., San Diego

A. To begin with, the suggested allocations for the variable annuity account come up only to 90 percent, but my suspicion is that it doesn't make much difference.

It appears to me that the adviser simply took your current allocation of assets, calculated that it is roughly 68 percent stocks, and then designed a variable annuity portfolio that also has 68 percent stocks. Yes, there is broader diversification in the variable annuity proposal, but my question is whether that additional diversification is significant. Bear in mind that the funds you now own have portfolios of as many as 298 different stocks (in the Balanced Fund), and the four funds you now own all have different objectives.

The big appeal of the variable annuity account, it would seem to me, is the guarantee of 5 percent annual return. But this guarantee doesn't come free; variable annuity accounts are much like a portfolio of mutual funds, with an element of insurance added.

This element of insurance allows the annuities to qualify as tax-deferred investments. It is for that reason that many people buy them, figuring that the cost of the insurance required to establish these as annuities is a fair price for the benefits of tax deferral.

However, you already have achieved tax deferral by dint of having this money in IRA accounts. In the past, I have analyzed some of the Vanguard variable annuity accounts, and determined that the level of insurance offered -- about the minimum that could be applied for the accounts to be qualified as annuities -- tended to reduce investment returns by about one half of one percent when compared to similarly invested mutual funds.

My suspicion is that, with a more robust level of insurance being presented in the Thrivent annuity accounts, the costs there would be higher. On the Thrivent website, they list the total mortality and expense charges for a balanced variable annuity account as 1.55 percent. In contrast, a balanced annuity account with Vanguard involves a total of .63 percent in all forms of expenses. Now, that is a somewhat unfair comparison, since there is no question that the Thrivent annuities offer significantly higher insurance protection.

But I would ask two questions. One is whether, given your substantial income and potential income from pensions and Social Security, you need that much insurance.

The second, again predicated on your income from other sources, is whether you need to sacrifice the likely higher returns from your current portfolio for a likely lower return -- with insurance as a fillip -- that would probably come with the annuity.

If it were my choice, I'd stick with the IRAs. You didn't ask, but I would also take a closer look at AAL Aggressive Growth fund, which has had a pretty dismal history. Even though it's one of your smaller accounts, I suggest you consider merging that position into your AAL Capital Growth holdings.
