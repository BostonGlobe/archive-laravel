Air waves

To lure more passengers, JetBlue and Song go heavy on in-flight entertainment

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  February 8, 2004

EN ROUTE TO ORLANDO -- At an altitude of 34,000 feet, Courtney Heether of Marshfield discovered a use for her knowledge of music trivia.

As question after question popped up every few seconds on the small, interactive green touch-screen in front of her, Heether piled up the points as the timer in the lower right-hand corner of the screen ticked down to zero.

Up on artists as varied as P. Diddy and the Bee Gees, Heether dealt her husband, David Chaunt -- and every other player on the plane -- a solid thrashing, racking up the top score of 6,200 points out of a possible 10,000. None too pleased with the beating, Chaunt threw in the towel.

"She ranked one through the whole trip and it was killing me," he said, after their Song flight landed in Orlando. "I played one game and she crushed me."

Chaunt spent the rest of the flight engaged in something he knew a lot better: watching "SportsCenter" on ESPN.

The next evening, sisters Deb Cafarella of Revere and Tricia Sulick of Wilmington used the televisions at their seats on their JetBlue Airways flight back to Boston from Orlando to distract them from the turbulence caused by a massive winter storm over the mid-Atlantic states.

Cafarella hunched down in her middle seat in the plane's last row, tightening the blue headphones she was given by a flight attendant so she could get a better listen to the news on CNN. Being able to flip channels instead of grabbing onto her seat every time the plane dipped or heaved was a big relief, she said. "The TV is really a great help," Cafarella said. "It gets your mind off of it. You can flop stations, you can find something comedic just to distract your attention from the turbulence."

The battle for the airways has turned into a battle of the airwaves. After decades of service sameness in the skies -- peanuts for short flights, overcooked, underwhelming meals and a six-month-old movie that might or might not be visible several rows away on the long ones -- the times are changing. Passengers still can't make calls from their own cellphones, but two upstart airlines are intent on packing their planes with enough entertainment options to fill a small Circuit City store.

Song, a unit of Atlanta-based Delta Air Lines, started Boston service last May, promising low fares and friendly service to sunny destinations, mostly in Florida. JetBlue followed suit in January, with service from Logan International Airport to California, Denver, and many of the same Florida routes that Song was hoping to dominate.

The airlines are locked in a game of one-upmanship as they try to establish themselves as the low-fare carrier of choice in New England. JetBlue offers digital satellite television at every seat, while Song is installing its own seatback entertainment system on all its planes. Both plan to offer pay-per-view, on-demand movies at every seat this year.

Each lauds itself for having cheap fares and superior customer service, including flight attendants and pilots who smile, joke, and banter with the passengers.

The hype has enticed Boston passengers to find out whether it's all a marketing ploy or a high-flying reality. The result has been travelers delighted by the in-flight amenities, but perplexed by the difficulty of finding those low fares to the few destinations those airlines fly.

"This is our first experience on JetBlue and I would definitely fly it again," said Sulick, who, when turbulence was not too heavy, used her seatback TV as a reading light for the romance novel she brought along.

Both women flew Song on a trip to Tampa in November, and said they enjoyed its pastel leather seats and the airport kiosk that let them check in quickly for their flight. Song didn't yet have TVs on board, so the two read during the trip, and Cafarella took a bagel and coffee from Dunkin' Donuts on board.

JetBlue had no check-in kiosk, giving them the impression they were in for a rough trip on an airline that skimped on even the basics. In the end, though, both found they liked JetBlue more than Song.

"I was pretty surprised at how good this is," Sulick said.

The sisters paid $79 each for their tickets to Orlando, and $114 apiece for the trip back. JetBlue only sells its tickets in one-way segments. For that price, they were able to enjoy the televisions that distracted them from the bumpy ride. They even fell asleep at one point, missing out on the selection of free snacks -- Terra Blue Chips, Frito Lay Munchie Mix, Karen's Biscotti, Stouffer's Animal Crackers, and Carvin's Oatmeal Chocolate Chip Cookies -- that flight attendants offered them.

"As soon as we woke up, they came over to us and offered us some food. We didn't even have to ask," Cafarella said. "That was above and beyond."

Meanwhile, Heether, her husband, and their daughter were also getting the in-seat entertainment for free, but their flight and the food on board came at a higher cost. The family paid $275 each, roundtrip, for their tickets.

The couple said they appreciated the broad food selection on Song, which included a lunch menu of a turkey sandwich, a Mediterranean vegetable, and toasted lavosh vegan sandwich, an oregano-scented organic chicken and baby spinach salad, each for $8, and the $4 fruit and cheese snacks the family ended up sharing.

While mom's hubris over her success in music trivia kept dad quiet, daughter Madeline was soothed by "The Grim Adventures of Billy & Mandy" on Cartoon Network, though she occasionally stopped to watch her DVD of the Disney movie "Dinosaur" on the portable player her parents brought along.

David Chaunt called the onboard TVs a godsend because they kept his otherwise rowdy 3-year-old quiet.

"A normal flight with her is just miserable," he said. "A kid that age is not going to want to sit in a seat for four hours and not want to run up and down the aisle, or go to the bathroom."

Song, however, wasn't the couple's first choice. Heether said they had actually wanted to fly JetBlue, which would have been cheaper, and which her parents took for the trip to Orlando earlier the same morning.

JetBlue, though, didn't have enough Boston flights yet to make it convenient for them to fly the airline. After leaving Florida, the couple was headed for a quick stop in Baltimore before coming back to Boston -- a flight they could get from Orlando on Song's parent, Delta, but a market in which JetBlue has no service at all.

"The one thing I know about JetBlue was that they didn't have as many direct flights," Heether said.
