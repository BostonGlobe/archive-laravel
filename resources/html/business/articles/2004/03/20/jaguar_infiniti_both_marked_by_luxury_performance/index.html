AUTOMOBILIA

Jaguar, Infiniti both marked by luxury, performance

By Royal Ford, Globe Staff  |  March 20, 2004

Call me veXed.

As a so-called eXpert in tracking automotive trends, I am perpleXed over one such trend and vow to get to the cruX of the matter.

The eXtant trigger for this eXclamation? The virtual eXplosion of the letter X - in upper and lower case -- in the naming of cars and trucks that has swept the automotive landscape. By my last count, there were more than 40 such vehicles in showrooms, split equally among both sorts of transportation -- and 40-plus does not include sub-models such as the BMW 325xi.

But what does the X/x designate?

It depends on what you're buying.

In some cases, X marks the sport. That is, it designates performance enhancement for those with the need for speed. For others, it means good handling in bad conditions.

And in some instances, it offers the promise of each: solid high-performance handling coupled with get-to-the-ski-chalet winter capabilities or, even in some cases, all that plus true off-road capability.

Of course, not all use the X to demark their spots. Subaru and Audi are pioneers in all-wheel drive. Mercedes-Benz has followed, vowing that AWD will be a performance/safety demand for some consumers much as the antilock brake system has become. Many others are following this path.

But back to today's head-to-head test of X cars: 2004 models of the Jaguar X-Type and the Infiniti G35x.

Both promise good bad-weather travel, interior luxury, and sportiness.

Who delivers? Each, in its own way.

So here's the Jag, a company improving under Ford Motor Co. ownership, up against stiff competition, the Infiniti, whose rear-wheel drive coupe and sedan were last year's car of the year winner in several polls. Picking a favorite -- as is often the case in buying a car -- can depend on point of view.

Let's say, at the outset, that I'd recommend the Jag to the real estate agent who wants interior luxury, a softer ride, and a low-RPM burble that hints at aggressiveness. The G35x? That's for the lone wolf, tracking to a hi-tech job.

At sub-$40,000 prices, each will be a happy driver.

The G35x is powered by a 260-horsepower, 3.5-liter, V-6 engine. Its steering is tight and responsive, with telling stiffness in the suspension that does not cross the line into jarring. As with any car that has power going to its front wheels, it does exhibit hints of understeer in aggressive cornering. Yet, the adage used by race drivers -- go in slow, come out fast -- can be clung to in this car.

That is because its front-to-rear torque bias is 25/75, meaning you can still use the gas to "steer" the car -- up to a point. So go in slow, sense the understeer, and judiciously get on the gas and give a gentle countersteer at the wheel. I found I could even make the rear snap out just a bit, which is cool in an AWD car. With understeer gone, exit from apex fast, as proscribed.

Of course, as with all good AWD systems these days, torque will transfer front and rear as needed when slippage is detected. That is a good thing in snow, and, in the G35x, it is accentuated by a Snow Mode option that fixes the split at 50/50 and makes the drive-by-wire throttle less sensitive.

The X-Type, on the other hand, was not so much a handful in understeer situations as it was a "keep your hands off me" situation. Going into corners, it is common to turn in too sharply. Understeer (plowing) occurs. You must let off the gas and let the stability-control system fix the problem, which it does with automatic braking. You've come in fast. You'll go out slow.

But don't get me wrong. This is not a bad thing. It is fixing a common error that leads to trouble for many drivers.

The Jag is a softer ride, a less power-ful ride, yet a more elegant ride.

I found it to be a bit too gentle on the highway, rolling in lane changes, with a tendency to porpoise in heavy braking or acceleration.

The trade-off? A smooth, supple ride for those looking to cruise in splendor and comfort.

Its 3.0-liter V-6 produces 231 horsepower and it comes with either a five-speed manual transmission (as tested) or a five-speed automatic. The manual was a bit sloppy and soft, not up to snuff when you consider the other clicking trannies (manuals and autos with manual option) against which it must run.

Its torque is split 40/60 front to rear in normal driving, so you lose some of the performance hints that a stronger rear-wheel bias offers.

As far as interiors, the Jag wins hands down. Quality leathers, bird's-eye maple, textured dash, a preponderance of storage bins, and an air of open space in what is really not a very large car all speak to elegance and comfort.

The G35x had touches of plastic I'd rather see disappear, and its interior, while nice, will not compare with the insides of the Audis, Mercedes, Saabs, and other AWD sedans with which it will have to compete.

Of course, you may have so much fun driving this car that you won't notice.

How do you choose if AWD and sedan luxury is your preference?

Shop these two against a BMW 325xi, a range of Mercedes products, and Audis, and then ask who you are and what you want.
