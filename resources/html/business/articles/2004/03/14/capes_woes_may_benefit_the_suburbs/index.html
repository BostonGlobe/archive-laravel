Cape's woes may benefit the suburbs

Insurers' exodus could reduce prices inland

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  March 14, 2004

There may be a silver lining in the exodus of home insurers from Cape Cod, the islands, and other coastal areas -- lower insurance prices for suburbanites.

The retrenchment on the Cape, caused by the soaring cost of reinsurance for catastrophic wind storms, is forcing many homeowners to scramble to find coverage, often at higher prices and with wind damage deductibles of up to 5 percent of the policy.

But insurance industry insiders say the capital being diverted from the Cape and islands will have to be channeled somewhere else. Companies pulling back on the Cape are going to have to expand their business elsewhere, presumably by lowering prices and taking customers away from other firms.

"A price war in suburbia or even in urban communities is not at all out of the realm of possibility," said Francis O'Brien, vice president and regional manager at the Property Casualty Insurers of America. "We're in a very unpredictable cycle right now. In this environment, a lot of the old assumptions are going to be turned on their head."

Thomas Callahan, executive director of the Massachusetts Affordable Housing Alliance, said a price war in other parts of the state is a real possibility.

"Presumably, companies aren't looking to shrink their market share, so they may look to expand their business in other areas," Callahan said.

He said he would encourage companies to focus on urban areas because that's where he sees the biggest potential for growth. In the suburbs, he said, companies would have to steal market share from competitors; in urban areas the chief competition would be the state's home insurer of last resort, the Massachusetts Fair Plan.

The Andover Group is facing perhaps the largest capital shift. The company, which sells insurance through two subsidiaries, Cambridge Mutual Fire and Merrimack Mutual Fire, recently announced that it was pulling out of Cape Cod and the islands entirely.

Starting this month, Andover Group began notifying its 14,000 customers on the Cape and islands that it planned to not renew their policies. The company expects to be off the Cape entirely by early next year.

"They're going to have some surplus capital that they're going to have to redeploy elsewhere. Where they're going to redeploy it, I couldn't tell you. I don't think they know yet," said O'Brien, who has become a de facto spokesman for the Andover Group, one of his trade group's member companies.

If the past is any guide, it's a good bet the Andover Group will focus on the suburbs rather than urban areas.

According to a recent study by the state Division of Insurance, the Andover Group had a disproportionately low share of its business in urban areas. The Andover Group is the largest home insurer in the state, with 8.3 percent of the overall market, but it ranks 20th in the top 25 insurers in terms of business in urban areas, according to the study.

"They have shown no signs to date that they would choose an urban area as a place to grow," Callahan said. "But we never give up on companies. To some extent it's an educational process. Some companies have outdated assumptions about what doing business in Dorchester is like."

DOR's long armThe Massachusetts Revenue Department is trying to enlist the state's insurance companies as tax collectors.

Legislation filed by the Romney administration would require insurers, before cutting a check for any claim exceeding $500, to determine whether the claimant owes back taxes and whether a portion of the check should be remitted to the state.

Insurance companies are currently required to check whether claimants owe child support payments. The Revenue Department says it wants to extend the concept to income and other taxes. The agency estimates it would snare an extra $3 million to $4 million a year with the provision in place.

But insurers don't want to be placed in the position of saying to one of their customers, "Here's your claim check for $1,000, minus the $500 in taxes you owe the state."

"We believe this amounts to a potential unconstitutional delegation of state power -- the power to collect taxes -- to a private party," said Francis O'Brien, spokesman for the Property Casualty Insurers of America.

Hershey bar shrinking The giant-size Hershey chocolate bar is going through a metamorphosis that may be hard to spot.

The standard giant bar used to be 8 ounces, but some time ago it shrunk to 7 ounces with no apparent shrinkage in the retail price. Occasionally, an 8-ounce bar would reappear as a special promotion, marketed as "14 percent free -- an 8 oz. bar at the 7 oz. price."

In January, Hershey discontinued the 8-ounce bar. Officials say the 6-ounce bar has now replaced the 7-ounce bar as the standard giant size. My downsizing spies say the retail price still hasn't changed significantly, and predict the 7-ounce bar will now assume the role previously played by the 8-ounce bonus bar, the focus of the occasional promotion offering an extra ounce at no extra charge.

In essence, Hershey has downsized its giant bar from 8 to 6 ounces with no significant reduction in the retail price. A Hershey spokeswoman said the company doesn't set retail prices.

Edgar Dworsky, editor of Consumerworld.org and a chocolate fan, said the amazing thing about all three Hershey bar sizes is that they all look identical. He said the only thing that changed was the thickness, and that's hard to detect.

Phil Salmon of Boxborough said Hershey's downsizing has turned him off and made him think about switching to another candy bar. "It was another sneaky price increase, and I am sure the Hershey people hoped no one would notice," he said.
