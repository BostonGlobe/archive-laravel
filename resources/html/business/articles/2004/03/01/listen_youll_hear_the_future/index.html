Listen, you'll hear the future

Radio stations, receivers get in tune with digital age

By Peter J. Howe, Globe Staff  |  March 1, 2004

It's been 101 years since Guglielmo Marconi made this country's first trans-Atlantic radio broadcast from South Wellfleet on Cape Cod, but radio continues to be a vibrant focus of technological innovation.

After years of development, in recent months three Boston stations have begun broadcasting digital versions of their signals that can offer CD-quality music as well as streaming text information such as song titles and news and weather headlines to display screens on special digital receivers. Another 10 area stations plan to offer digital -- sometimes called ''high-definition radio" -- this year, joining nearly 300 stations nationwide that have licensed the technology.

Japanese radio maker Kenwood in mid-January rolled out the first digital radio receiver, a $350 unit that includes a display screen similar to the ''crawler" at the bottom of cable television news channels. JVC and Panasonic are expected to introduce digital car radios in the next 30 to 60 days. The sets would all pick up conventional broadcasts when digital versions are unavailable.

Meanwhile, satellite radio services XM and Sirius are booming, nearing 2 million subscribers nationally. XM on April 1 plans to add a continuously updated traffic information channel covering 85 miles of highways around Greater Boston, deriving travel times from solar-powered roadside vehicle sensors.

Competition from commercial-free satellite radio services that cost $10 to $13 a month is clearly a major driver in the growth of digital radio, which gives conventional commercial stations a way to offer sound quality comparable to satellite, even if they are the same ad-laden conventional formats. National Public Radio is developing ''Tomorrow Radio" plans that would let stations such as WBUR-FM and WGBH-FM use digital technology to offer a second full channel, which could include rebroadcasts of news and talk shows during hours music plays on the main station.

''This is like where personal computers were in 1982: We have a core technology that is on the cusp of a revolution" as profound as FM radio in the 1940s and color TV in the 1960s, said Robert Struble, chief executive officer of iBiquity Digital Corp., a Columbia, Md., company backed by major radio conglomerates and equipment makers that has developed the new standard US digital radio system. With dozens of new cars now offered with satellite radio built in -- General Motors expects to produce its 1 millionth satellite radio car this month -- Struble said he expects digital radio to become a feature in new cars by the 2006 model year, and potentially as soon as late this year.

International Data Corp. in Framingham predicts that total sales of digital radio sets, including home, car, and portable units, will grow from 197,000 units this year to 4.95 million in 2007, representing a $1.25 billion annual business. In a December survey of consumers, IDC found that 11.3 percent were likely to buy subscription satellite radio in the coming 12 months, and 18.8 percent were interested in free digital radio that would cost $150 to $300 for the receiver.

In contrast to the multimillion dollar upgrades and new airwave spectrum licenses required for television stations to add digital and high-definition broadcasts, and the $4 billion spent by XM and Sirius to launch service, radio stations typically need to spend only $50,000 to $100,000 to add a digital signal. It is broadcast within their existing frequency, with the digital 0s and 1s that create CD-like sound and wireless data essentially sprinkled among the standard analog radio waves.

John C. Donlevie, executive vice president and general counsel of iBiquity backer Entercom Communications Corp., which added digital broadcasts at WQSX (93.7 FM) late last year and plans to upgrade WAAF (107.3 FM) later this year, said, ''The question is going to be what services do listeners want out of their radio? You could put news, weather, traffic, commercials -- you could put anything you want in that bandwidth."

The digital radio technology lets stations add streams of content to their signals that are comparable to what a computer with a dial-up modem can download from the Internet. For AM, it adds about 36 kilobits per second of capacity, which is typically used mainly or solely to upgrade the sound quality to FM levels. But for FM, it can add up to 96 kilobits per second. With some of that capacity used to improve the signal, it can leave 50 kilobits per second or more for data streams, conceivably including rudimentary video or images -- although few radio executives see demand for turning car radios into a new visual distraction for drivers.

''All we're doing right now is focusing on the sound," said Ted Jordan, general manager of WBZ (1030 AM), which began broadcasting a digital signal late last fall. The Federal Communications Commission has approved digital AM broadcasts only during daytime hours but is likely to authorize 24-hour digital AM broadcasts this year once issues of nighttime signal interference risk are worked out.

Other station owners, however, are holding off. Mario Mazza, vice president of programming for Charles River Broadcasting in Waltham, which runs WCRB (102.5 FM) said, ''We're sort of waiting on digital right now. It's not on the front burner. There aren't a lot of digital receivers out there, and the conversion costs are significant. But it's certainly something that we are interested in. The questions are: Is it worth it? Will listeners perceive enough value, enough difference to make it worthwhile?"

Jed Kolko, a Forrester Research analyst, said he can envision systems similar to the TiVo television recording boxes showing up in digital radios. They would enable listeners to search by name through the hundreds of songs played by multiple radio stations over the course of a day and play them back, or fast forward and rewind a talk or music show or sports broadcast. ''With digital, content becomes searchable. This brings it to a whole new level," Kolko said.

An early example of this feature is available through PoGo Products Inc.'s Radio YourWay device, which costs $150 to $200 based on memory and can be programmed to record as much as 18 hours worth of over-the-air radio programs for playback later.

Struble said for stations moving to digital, ''something analogous to the crawl that you see on the bottom of a cable news channel is probably the first thing you'll see right out of the box. A lot of people think traffic will be the killer application," with stations streaming a continuous report on backups and accidents.

XM clearly agrees, with plans to launch 24-hour traffic channels in 15 cities today and Boston April 1 as a supplement to its $10-a-month, 100-channel lineup that has nearly 1.4 million subscribers nationally. The Boston traffic information will come from roadside traffic-flow sensors along Routes 3, 24, 128, 93, and soon the Massachusetts Turnpike Boston extension operated by Mobility Technologies of Wayne, Pa.

''You're not going to listen to this for 30 minutes straight, but you'll be able to check any hour of the day or night for continuously updated, very geographically specific traffic reports," said XM spokesman Chance Patterson.
