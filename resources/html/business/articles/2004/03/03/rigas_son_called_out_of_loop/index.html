Rigas son called out of loop

Defense says he had no role in accounting fraud

By Bloomberg News  |  March 3, 2004

The eldest son of Adelphia Communications Corp. founder John Rigas played no role in looting the company, lying about operations, or directing a massive financial fraud, his lawyer told jurors yesterday.

Michael Rigas, 50, former vice president of operations, had nothing to do with accounting at Adelphia, the nation's fifth-largest cable television provider, defense attorney Andrew Levander said in his opening statements in federal court in New York.

A prosecutor in his opening on Monday said Michael Rigas, his brother, Timothy, 47, and their 79-year-old father stole cash advances, fabricated revenue, and hid billions of dollars in debt before Adelphia sought bankruptcy protection in June 2002.

"The government will not produce any credible or direct testimony that documents that Michael Rigas intentionally defrauded anybody," Levander said. "They will not prove beyond a reasonable doubt that Michael Rigas was either a liar or greedy."

The Rigases and a fourth executive, Michael Mulcahey, 46, are accused of securities fraud, wire fraud, and bank fraud, and conspiring to commit those crimes.

Levander said Michael Rigas worked hard to integrate customers from six cable companies that Adelphia bought in the late 1990s, boosting its subscriber base to almost 6 million from 2 million. Michael Rigas, who "doesn't have an evil bone in his body," was mocked in e-mails as "clueless" and "harmless" by prosecution witnesses, Levander said.

"Ask yourself whether Michael Rigas would have been charged if his last name was Smith, Jones, or anything but Rigas," Levander said.

Assistant US attorney Richard Owens told jurors Monday that the Rigases used Adelphia as a "private piggy bank" by spending $13 million in company money on a golf course, $700,000 for a golf club membership for Timothy Rigas, and taking personal trips on company jets.

Levander said Michael Rigas was a "terrible" golfer who didn't like to play, and that while his contract called for Adelphia to pay his country club fees, he paid them himself.

Michael Rigas, who lives with his parents, "took great pains" to separate his business and personal affairs, Levander said. While Rigas was not the "smartest or savviest guy," he's not a criminal, the lawyer said.

Mark Mahoney, an attorney for Mulcahey, the company's former director of internal reporting, used his opening statement to emphasize that prosecutors haven't alleged that his client took anything beyond his salary from Adelphia.

Mahoney also stressed that many of the fraud charges turn on how the company applied complicated accounting principles.

"Your decision in many respects is going to turn on very fine points of accounting," Mahoney said. "I've got to tell you, a lot of it is beyond me."

If convicted of defrauding creditors and investors, the defendants face dozens of years in prison and forfeiture of $2.5 billion.

Prosecutors called their first witness, Christopher Pelto, 40, an investor from Endicott, N.Y., who bought 5,000 shares of Adelphia stock on Oct. 12, 2001, and eventually bought 38,400 shares. Assistant US Attorney Christopher Clark questioned him about a series of Adelphia financial statements that prosecutors say omitted critical information about company debt.

Clark asked, for instance, about a filing in November 2001 that reported a private company owned by the Rigases bought Class B stock in a transaction that would repay $259.9 million in Adelphia bank debt. Prosecutors claim the Rigases paid no cash and that they entered a series of "crooked" stock deals.

"It appeared to me that John Rigas and members of his family were making an investment," Pelto testified.

Pelto said he didn't know that Adelphia paid $26 million for timber rights on land owned by John Rigas adjacent to a farm he owned in Potter County, Pa. Prosecutors say the timber rights purchase was another example of Rigas looting that the company didn't disclose to investors.

"For the life of me, I have no idea why a cable company would need to purchase timber rights," Pelto said on cross-examination. "It makes no sense to me."
