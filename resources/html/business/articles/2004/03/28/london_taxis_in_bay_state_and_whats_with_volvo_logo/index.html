London taxis in Bay State? And what's with Volvo logo?

By Royal Ford, Globe Staff  |  March 28, 2004

Will the batteries on hybrids run out? A bit of London on the streets of Concord? A ''death cage" battle as entertainment? What's the scoop on all-wheel drive? And is the Volvo symbol a guy thing?

You ask, we answer.

Q. I've wondered about hybrid cars. Has anyone done a study on how long their batteries will last? I'm concerned that after the eight-year warranty runs out, I'd be left with a useless used car.

A. Toyota's studies show the batteries will easily last 180,000 miles and my assumption is that given the eight- and 10-year warranties offered, they expect them to last well beyond that. Further, eight years from now, the costs of the battery pack -- now more than $5,000 -- will have fallen dramatically as more and more hybrids hit the market.

Q. Perhaps you can help solve a mystery. My wife and I spotted a bright yellow cab that looks like a miniature London taxi near Concord. It looked like it might be a KIA. What was it?

A. What you saw was a Sunshine Taxi, which is no KIA, but is in fact a London Taxi, with big headroom, spacious legroom, built in England for ''for hire" jobs. It is imported by London Taxis North America Inc. of Sudbury. I can see how the grille and adornment LTI (for London Taxis International) might make you think KIA. Sunshine Taxi is based in Hudson with a hub in Concord. The London Taxis need to be booked in advance -- for airport transportation, weddings, graduations, proms, and other ceremonial events. Asked where they will transport patrons, Laura Elshiemer, who along with her husband, Thomas, owns the company, said: ''We go everywhere." These taxis are also wonderfully equipped to accommodate disabled passengers.

Q. I saw your piece on driving the Porsche SUV in the Yukon. You wrote about a ''death cage" battle between a coyote and a lynx. I wasn't too happy to even hear that they do that.

A. It was a noneuphemistic description of a true battle in the wild. I wrote about it to illustrate the wildness of the land where we were driving. ''Death cage" match has been used to describe, not literally, events from sports to politics. Your question reminds me of the reader who wrote, when I mentioned ''moose crash testing" done by Saab and Volvo, that we should not be running cars into moose on purpose. Of course that does not happen. And heaven forbid that I mention child safety seat testing, lest someone think toddlers are strapped into their seats and, well, you get the picture.

Q. I saw an Infiniti ad on television for its all-wheel drive sedan, the G35x. In the commercial, the company says that while all-wheel drive is good in bad weather, it's not ideal on dry roads. Is this an accurate statement?

A. ''I don't know what they're talking about," says Sandy Stevens, race driver and driving instructor. Old-fashioned four-wheel drive, with its purely mechanical links, can be problematic on dry pavement because, in turns, inner and outer wheels travel different distances at different rates and this puts huge strain on the system. Modern AWD, however, uses a viscous coupler -- basically fluid, typically silicon -- that compensates for spin or wheel rotation. In fact, AWD can offer so much of an edge, even on dry pavement, that Audi race cars with it were ruled to have an ''unfair advantage" and banned from certain circuits.

Q. I have had several Volkswagens over the years and they have been very reliable. I now own a VW diesel and was told by my dealer that if I use an engine block heater my warranty is void. Is this true?

A. If you drive diesel in New England, you need an engine block heater for those cold-morning starts. Tony Fouladpour, VW spokesman, tells me that no, your warranty will not be voided. There may have been a misunderstanding because VW has ceased installing its own block heaters because of a problematic installation process (the next generation of diesels will bring it back). If you install an after-market heater, your warranty is voided only if VW can show that the heater caused whatever malfunction occurred. Otherwise, the warranty is in effect.

Q. Regarding the Volvo designed by a team of all women. When can I look forward to buying one?

A. In a word: never. You may see certain features of that car incorporated in Volvos of the future. I liked the notion of putting the shifter back on the steering column to free up flat floor space between the front seats. But a gull-wing Volvo? Not going to happen. That is because concept cars such as this one are often designed in a scattershot fashion, with many options that, while intriguing or beneficial, would be too costly to produce, or too unrealistic. The gull-wing doors, for instance, would not only be expensive, but impractical to operate in tight parking areas, such as the local mall.

Q. Did you notice the irony when Volvo introduced the car designed by a team of women that Volvo's logo is a phallic male-gender sign?

A. That one got by me, so I did a little research. Sure, the circle with the arrow emerging from it can be seen that way. But one man's phallic sign is another's chemical symbol for iron ore, which is what the Volvo logo represents. ''Rolling strength" is Volvo's interpretation of the symbol.

Q. What is your best guess as to when BMW will offer a third row of seating in its X5, an SUV?

A. Never. David Buchko, BMW spokesman, said that ''nothing we make is big enough to make that third row of seating safe." Further, he added, ''I don't know that we'd ever make a vehicle that big." An interesting stance that makes me want to look into third-row safety.

Q. I saw your story on the Cadillac XLR and I thought it looked like a Corvette with Cadillac cosmetics. Am I right?

A. Good job of peeking beneath the skin. The two-seat sports car does, indeed, have Corvette roots -- though not on the outside. It basically sits atop the same platform: hydroformed rails, a wonderful magnetic suspension system that hardens and softens itself depending on driver demand, and stability-control systems. But it has its own engine and transmission.
