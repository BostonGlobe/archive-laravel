Q&A CMGI’s George McMillan on the dot-com comeback

March 28, 2004

When George McMillan arrived at CMGI Inc. in 2001, there wasn’t much helium left in the Internet balloon, and CMGI’s portfolio of technology ventures, once valued in the billions, was dragging the Boston ﬁrm toward ruin. First as chief ﬁ- nancial ofﬁcer and later as chief executive, Mc- Millan launched a quietly ruthless campaign to strip away the Internet ﬂotsam. Today, CMGI has enough money in the bank to buy local rival Modus Media Inc. Globe staff reporter Hiawatha Bray asks McMillan how he brought CMGI back from the brink and what’s next for the company and the sector.

Q. What were your ﬁrst moves?

A. Job one in that window of time was to get the business in control ﬁnancially, clean up the balance sheet, triage cash use.

Q. Why did you take apart CMGI’s conglomeration of technology ﬁrms?

A. When I became CEO, my focus was different —can this company be saved?

For example, we had some businesses we ﬁgured we probably didn’t want to keep but probably weren’t sellable at that period of time. In every case, given where CMGI was, you have to be very clear on what you want to do. In some cases we had to say, ‘We like the strategy, but if you can’t implement it quickly, you’re gone.’ In many cases we sold them, or we shut them down.

Q. Meanwhile, you had the SalesLink business that packs and ships computers and other electronic components, and was bringing in lots of money. How did you leverage it?

A. The challenge they had in 2002, as we got to know them better, was, ‘How do we ﬁnd additional opportunites offshore?’ That’s why we bought Ilogistix [a California ﬁrm] out of bankruptcy. That gave us good customers but just as importantly it gave us supply chain operations in locations outside the United States.

Q. What does Modus Media bring to the table?

A. What we needed next was a combination of sales and presence in China, which was the next geographical migration of our customers. The combination of SalesLink and Modus gave us an unparalleled global footprint.

Q. But it’s also put you into a very controversial business. Some would say you’re helping US electronics ﬁrms move jobs out of the country.

A. They’re not following us offshore. They’re already there, or going there.

Q. But if the manufacturing jobs and CMGI’s supply chain work is all overseas, what’s left for US workers?

A. The further away that manufacturing gets from the market where they’re consumed, the more opportunity there is for us to create jobs doing all the other things that need to be close to the customer.

Q. Such as?

A. Order management, fulﬁllment, customer retention, customer management and returns. We’re going to learn to take advantage of that migration to create new services, new jobs, new industries.

Q. Is the technology-sector recovery ﬁ- nally at hand?

A. I remain prudently cautious. We have seen some business and service lines bouncing back. But I don’t think we have seen, in the developed world, an acrossthe- board surge. The US has been spotty, Europe has been intermittently OK, Asia has been good. Most of our business planning has been predicated on a conservative and gradual recovery.

Q. Venture capital investments in tech companies made CMGI a hot property. How’s that business going?

A. It is alive and well and living in Charlestown. There were three or four funds that spanned a period of the mid- ’90s to 2000. There were still almost two dozen investments in @Ventures 3 and @Ventures 4 [two of the funds]. We made a corporate decision that we should continue to fund those investments. I think that on balance, that has proved to be a wise decision.

Q. So you think these companies could still pay off ?

A. There are still many nice investments in the online commerce area. @Ventures owns a considerable investment in myfamily. com. It’s the dominant genealogical service. It’s a wonderful business model that has scaled up beautifully.

We own a piece of Alibris.com, which is probably the best-known rare book market maker on the Web. We have investments in Object Video. It is software that analyzes remote video from things like the US border or military installations.

Q. Could these be the companies that ignite the next CMGI venture boom?

A. One never knows. We’re really now an operating company, not an investment house. The venture capital operation, if it does an effective job, the sort of blended return of the whole, should be very attractive to our shareholders.
