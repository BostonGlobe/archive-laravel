Judge rules against Genzyme request

March 18, 2004

THE REGION

A US District Court judge in New York Tuesday denied Genzyme Corp.'s motion to dismiss a shareholder complaint against the Cambridge biotech firm and its directors. The complaint alleges Genzyme schemed to buy back the shares of its Genzyme Biosurgery division tracking stock at an artificially low price. Genzyme claims it followed the precise procedure spelled out in its corporate charter when it exchanged two tracking stocks for Genzyme shares last year. The judge's ruling allows the lawsuit to move forward, though no court dates have yet been set. (Jeffrey Krasner)

Maven Networks gets $10m in funding

Maven Networks Inc., a Cambridge start-up that sells a system for companies to offer marketing videos over broadband Internet connections, has raised a $10 million second round of venture funding from Accel Partners of Silicon Valley and other investors. The round, being disclosed today, brings the 30-person company's total raised to over $15 million. Maven sells a package of software and services that have been used by 20th Century Fox, Virgin Records, and other companies to promote products through high-resolution videos delivered to broadband Net subscribers. (Peter J. Howe)

Summit Partners raises $465m fund

Summit Partners, a private equity and venture capital firm which has an office in Boston, raised a $465 million subordinated debt fund that will provide debt financing for private companies. With the additional funds, Summit now has nearly $2.6 billion available for investment, managing partner Joseph Trustey said. Investors in the latest fund include public and private pension funds, university endowments, and corporations. A subordinated debt ranks lower in repayment than senior debt. (Bloomberg)

Funds with outsider chairmen said to lag

Fidelity Investments said a study found funds led by independent chairman didn't perform as well as those headed by people affiliated with the money management firms. In a study of fund firms with at least $10 billion of assets, commissioned by Fidelity, 43 fund groups with management chairmen did better over the past three, five, and 10 years than did 14 fund groups headed by outsiders, according to a Fidelity letter filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission. Fidelity is seeking to head off an SEC proposal requiring people who aren't tied to fund managers to head fund boards. That would force Fidelity chairman Edward C. Johnson III to step aside as chairman of the company's fund boards. According to the study, management-led funds outperformed 68 percent of similarly run funds during the past three years when taking into account the size of each fund, compared with 55 percent at funds headed by independent chairmen. In the past 10 years, management-led funds beat 71 percent of peers, compared with 57 percent of independent funds. (Bloomberg)

Amtrak, train makers settle dispute

Amtrak and the makers of its high-speed trains said they had settled all legal disputes. Amtrak and the consortium of Bombardier of North America and Alstom Ltd. of France sued each other in federal court over problems with the Acela trains that run between Boston and Washington. Bombardier said Amtrak imposed costly new design requirements. Amtrak blamed Bombardier for "extraordinary delays and pervasive failures." The parties will drop all claims relating to the program and the manufacturers will be entitled to receive as much as $42.5 million against approximately $70 million that Amtrak withheld. (AP)

14 states want WorldCom auditor fired

KPMG should be disqualified as bankrupt WorldCom Inc.'s auditor and return $146 million in fees, 14 states that claim WorldCom owes them unpaid taxes said in a court filing. The states, led by Massachusetts, want KPMG fired for designing a strategy that helped WorldCom, the second-biggest US long-distance phone company, avoid hundreds of millions of dollars in taxes between 1998 and 2001. Former US Attorney General Richard Thornburgh said in a January report that KPMG's strategy for minimizing WorldCom's state taxes was flawed. WorldCom subsidiaries paid more than $20 billion in royalties for services to a separate WorldCom unit, according to Thornburgh's report, which was commissioned by the bankruptcy court. The subsidiaries deducted the royalty expenses for state tax purposes and the parent company was lightly taxed on them, the report said. US Bankruptcy Judge Arthur Gonzalez will consider the matter at a hearing April 13. KPMG denied the allegations. (Bloomberg)

Manager details many Adelphia abuses

Adelphia Communications Corp. paid $3,000 in "beauty" costs for founder John Rigas and $700,000 for his son, Timothy, to join an exclusive golf club in South Carolina, a company employee told federal jurors. Accounts payable manager Linda Pekarski said Adelphia made payments on Rigas expenses for golf, mortgages, educational donations, bedroom slippers, and personal credit cards. Prosecutors accuse John Rigas, 79, and two of his sons, Michael, 50, and Timothy, 47, of looting billions from Adelphia, the number five cable television operator, and engaging in accounting fraud. (Bloomberg)

Stewart asks friends to vouch for her

Martha Stewart, to be sentenced June 17 for obstructing a federal investigation, has asked friends in a letter to vouch for "my character, my work ethic, my integrity, and my probity" with the judge in charge of her case, a person familiar with the matter said. Stewart asked friends in a March 12 one-page form letter to write to US District Judge Miriam Goldman Cedarbaum, who presided over Stewart's trial in Manhattan and who will sentence her. "Many people have inquired as to whether they can help by writing to Judge Cedarbaum about my sentence," Stewart wrote. "I am advised by my lawyers that it is appropriate to do so, and that they believe Judge Cedarbaum will conscientiously read everything sent to her." Stewart faces a sentence of 10 to 16 months. (Bloomberg)

EU 'on track' to make Microsoft ruling

The European Union insisted it was "on track" to conclude its antitrust case against Microsoft Corp. next week, even as the world's biggest software company scrambled to reach a deal to avert sanctions for allegedly abusing its Windows monopoly. Microsoft chief executive Steve Ballmer and general counsel Brad Smith were in Brussels negotiating with the EU antitrust office, sources familiar with the case said, after arriving Tuesday for a surprise face-to-face session with EU competition commissioner Mario Monti. No progress toward a settlement was made public but both sides indicated talks were intensifying. (AP)
