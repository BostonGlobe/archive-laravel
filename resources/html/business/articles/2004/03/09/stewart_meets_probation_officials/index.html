Stewart meets with probation officer

Her firm's stock falls 8 percent

By Associated Press  |  March 9, 2004

NEW YORK -- Martha Stewart met for about an hour yesterday with the federal probation officials who will recommend the amount of her fine and the length of her prison sentence for lying about a stock sale, then she emerged from the courthouse to thank her supporters.

Stock in Stewart's company slid another 8 percent and the board of Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia was to gather to discuss life without Stewart.

The probation meeting is the first step toward sentencing. Although lawyers did not comment on what took place at the meeting, newly convicted defendants typically give basic information about themselves.

Stewart, who carried a Martha Stewart Living umbrella in the heavy rain, was convicted Friday along with stockbroker Peter Bacanovic. Both are expected to get 10 to 16 months in prison when they are sentenced June 17.

Probation officials will also recommend a fine for Stewart. The law allows for up to $1 million for the four counts on which she was convicted -- conspiracy, obstructing justice, and two counts of making false statements.

"I want to thank my readers, my viewers, and the Internet users," Stewart said as she stepped into a sport utility vehicle outside. "I just want to thank everyone for their support."

Stewart, 62, and Bacanovic, 41, were found guilty of lying to investigators about why Stewart sold 3,928 shares of ImClone Systems stock Dec. 27, 2001. The day after Stewart sold, the government said it would not review ImClone's application for approval of a cancer drug, Erbitux. The drug was eventually approved -- during Stewart's trial.

Stewart told investigators in 2002 that she had no memory of being tipped that ImClone chief executive Sam Waksal was trying to sell his shares. Her attorney, Robert Morvillo, later admitted in court that Stewart was tipped.

As Stewart left the courthouse Friday after the verdict, the Daily News asked her to comment on the fairness of the trial. "The unfairness of the trial, that's the right comment," she said.

The board of directors of Martha Stewart Living was to meet yesterday to discuss her fate, according to a source close to the company who spoke on condition of anonymity.

Stewart stepped down as CEO and chairman of the board in June after being indicted but remains as chief creative officer and a member of the board.

With her conviction, the government will likely press to have Stewart removed from the board, but the big question is how involved in the company she will be. Her name, now tainted by the conviction, is stamped on products from TV shows to magazines and merchandise.

Stewart stepped down from the board of cosmetics giant Revlon Inc. yesterday, , a Revlon spokeswoman confirmed. Stewart had served on Revlon's board since 1996.

Stewart's syndicated television show, "Martha Stewart Living," was taken off the air yesterday on Viacom-owned CBS and UPN stations. There was no immediate word about the show's future from King World, the show's syndicator, but the Viacom-owned stations in major media markets were considered its most important customers, said Bill Carroll, a specialist in syndication for Katz Television. King World spokesman Arthur Sandow did not immediately return a call for comment.

Shares of Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia Inc. fell 96 cents to close at $9.90 yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, adding to a nearly 23 percent tumble on Friday after the verdicts.

The stock had traded at about $19 a share before the ImClone investigation. Stewart owns about 61 percent, or 30 million shares, of her company.
