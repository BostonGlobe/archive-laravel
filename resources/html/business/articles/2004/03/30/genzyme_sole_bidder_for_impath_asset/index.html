BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Genzyme sole bidder for Impath asset

March 30, 2004

THE REGION

Genzyme Corp., which makes the Cerezyme treatment for a rare genetic disease, has submitted the only bid for Impath Inc.'s cancer-testing business, Impath's lawyer said. Without a competing bidder, Impath canceled today's auction, said George A. Davis, Impath's bankruptcy lawyer. Impath will ask US Bankruptcy Judge Prudence Carter Beatty to approve the $215 million sale to Genzyme tomorrow, Davis said. Impath will use the sale proceeds to fully repay its creditors under a reorganization plan. Impath filed for bankruptcy in September after the Securities and Exchange Commission opened a formal investigation into its accounting. The sale is scheduled to close by the end of April. (Bloomberg)

Constitution Quarters sells for $90.5m

Constitution Quarters, a historic five-building apartment property in Charlestown, has been sold to Carlyle Realty Partners, and the 367 units will be converted to condominiums, the company said. The waterfront residences, with studio, one- and two-bedroom units, plus some luxury homes on two or three floors, were purchased from Building 42 Associates, a company controlled by Martin Oliner of New York, for $90.5 million. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

Oxigene shares leap 15% on test results

Oxigene Inc., a developer of cancer treatments, said its experimental Oxi4503 drug helped stop cancer in mice. The Waltham company's shares rose $1.28, or 15.5 percent, to $9.54. The medicine increased the effects of standard treatments for breast, ovarian, and other cancers in the rodent research, Oxigene said. The results suggest the drug shuts off blood flow to tumors, starving them of nourishment needed to grow, Oxigene said. Findings from studies at universities in Sweden and Florida will be released this week at the American Association for Cancer Research meeting in Orlando, Fla. (Bloomberg)

Raytheon wins $116m US bomb order

Raytheon Co., the world's largest missile maker, said it was awarded the maximum available share of three contracts for Paveway II laser-guided bombs and components for the US military. The awards consist of two contracts for the Air Force and Navy worth a combined $116 million, or 70 percent of procurements of the bombs in fiscal 2004, the Waltham defense contractor said. A third contract is for guidance units for the Air Force. A Raytheon spokesman declined to give the value of that order. Production will be done primarily at Raytheon facilities in Tucson and in Sherman, Texas. (Bloomberg)

SkillSoft to pay $30.5m to settle lawsuits

SkillSoft PLC, a training software maker, agreed to pay $30.5 million to settle shareholder lawsuits accusing current and former managers of violating securities laws. Investors sued SkillSoft in 2002 after the company said it would restate profits and sales at SmartForce PLC, which merged with SkillSoft that year, because revenue from sales was recognized too soon. SkillSoft's insurers will pay a portion of the settlement, the company said. The restatement sparked a Securities and Exchange Commission investigation of Nashua-based Skillsoft. The company, which had estimated net income would be 20-22 cents a share for the fiscal year ending in January 2005, will update its estimates to take the settlement into account when it discloses earnings April 30. Tom McDonald, chief financial officer, didn't return a call. (Bloomberg)

Kraft Foods acquires Veryfine Products

Kraft Foods Inc. said it had acquired Veryfine Products Inc. of Littleton, a private maker of juices and other drinks, for an undisclosed amount. Veryfine posted sales of over $150 million in 2003, led by Fruit2O, the number one flavored water in the United States, according to Kraft. Kraft, the country's largest food company, sells juice under brands like Capri Sun and Kool-Aid Jammers. Kraft is taking over Veryfine's two factories in Berlin, N.H., and Littleton, along with a distribution center in Ayer. (Dow Jones/AP)

ExxonMobil penalty cut by 70% to $3.5b

An Alabama circuit court judge reduced an $11.8 billion punitive damages award against ExxonMobil Corp. to $3.5 billion in a lawsuit filed by the state, seeking unpaid royalties on offshore natural gas leases. The decision comes two weeks after Judge Tracy McCooey declined to order a new trial over the verdict, which was awarded by a Montgomery state court jury on Nov. 14. Two weeks ago, she declined to override the verdict and rule in ExxonMobil's favor. ExxonMobil said it would appeal McCooey's decision and the underlying $63 million actual damages verdict. (Reuters)

Slaves' descendants file $1b lawsuit

Descendants of slaves filed a $1 billion lawsuit against US and British corporations, accusing them of profiting by committing genocide against their ancestors. Lawyers for the eight plaintiffs said the complaint -- unlike past suits seeking reparations for slavery -- was the first to use DNA to link the plaintiffs to Africans who suffered atrocities during the slave trade. The suit, filed in federal court in Manhattan, accuses Lloyd's of London, FleetBoston Financial Corp., and R.J. Reynolds of ''aiding and abetting the commission of genocide" by allegedly financing and insuring ships that delivered slaves to tobacco plantations in the United States. The defendants ''have destroyed our national and ethnic identity," one of the plaintiffs, Deadria Farmer-Paellmann, said. DNA testing has made a ''direct connection" between Farmer-Paellmann and the Mende tribe in Sierra Leone, whose people ''were kidnapped, tortured, and shipped in chains to the United States," the suit said. (AP)

The Liberty All-Star Equity Fund, Variable Series, a $45 million variable-annuity fund, will be shut down because of slow asset growth. The board of trustees voted to terminate the fund, which will be liquidated April 23, according to a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. Investors may transfer their holdings to other accounts immediately, the filing said . . . Maxwell Shoe Co. advised shareholders to reject a $297 million hostile takeover bid by Jones Apparel Group Inc. as too little to warrant discussions. Jones's $20-a-share cash tender offer ''significantly undervalues the strength and diversity" of Maxwell's business, Hyde Park-based Maxwell said. Jones made the bid last week after the Maxwell board rejected an earlier offer from Jones with the same terms . . . Nixon Peabody LLC, one of Boston's largest law firms, with 150 attorneys in the local office, moved into new headquarters at 100 Summer St. The firm previously was at 101 Federal St. Both buildings are owned by Equity Office Properties Trust . . . Starent Networks Inc., a privately held Tewksbury wireless data network equipment maker, said it closed a sale to Verizon Wireless's Puerto Rico unit to offer its 300,000 subscribers high-speed wireless Internet access, push-to-talk communications, and other services. Terms were not disclosed . . . Westborough-based American Superconductor Corp., which develops equipment to improve electricity transmission, and General Electric Co. sold a power-compensation system to the Long Island Power Authority. The $1.7 million system will be installed in Long Island's East Hampton electrical substation to reduce the risk of power failures during times of high demand . . . Ember Corp. , a three-year-old Boston start-up that makes low-power wireless sensor networks, said it has bought a package of radio technology and intellectual property for $5 million from Cambridge Consultants Ltd. of the United Kingdom.
