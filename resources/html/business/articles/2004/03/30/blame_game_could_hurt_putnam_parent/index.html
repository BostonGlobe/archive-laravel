Blame game could hurt Putnam parent

Attacks on ex-CEO may raise questions on firm's oversight

By Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  March 30, 2004

Marsh & McLennan Cos. Inc. says Lawrence J. Lasser is to blame for the mess at its Putnam Investments subsidiary.

But is Marsh & McLennan to blame for Lasser?

Marsh & McLennan is using a scorched-earth approach to attack Lasser's handling of the mutual-fund trading scandal while he was chief executive of Putnam so it won't have to pay him tens of millions in deferred compensation and benefits.

But business specialists said Marsh & McLennan's harsh characterizations of Lasser as a rogue executive may boomerang on the parent firm, because they raise questions about Marsh's oversight of the man it allowed to run Putnam for 18 years as one of the highest paid executives in the mutual fund industry. Lasser also was a member of Marsh & McLennan's board of directors, meaning he wasn't a distant figure off on his own in Boston who didn't have much contact with the parent company in New York.

Samuel L. Hayes III, professor emeritus at Harvard Business School, said Marsh & McLennan is essentially claiming ''all it was, was a passive investor in Putnam, with no management oversight of this huge and very important contributor to Marsh McLennan's bottom line. That strains credibility. No parent company would leave such a huge subsidiary to the whims of any subsidiary CEO."

The arbitration proceeding is confidential, but Marsh & McLennan's words against Lasser were liberally excerpted by the Securities and Exchange Commission in a publicly filed legal brief it prepared this month for its ongoing fraud case against Putnam. The Marsh & McLennan claim provides a rare peak behind corporate curtains to the hardball tactics used in an ugly business negotiation.

In Marsh & McLennan's view, Lasser's management style, greed, and fear of losing a lucrative, high-profile position caused him and Putnam to keep quiet about improper trading by senior investment managers.

For example, in Marsh & McLennan's words, Lasser had ''absolute control" over the company, which he would lose if word of the employee trading got out; the ''management processes and structures" he used to run Putnam ''permitted" improper trading by employees ''to occur and go undetected"; Lasser was ''motivated" to ''stick his head in the sand" to protect his compensation, which Marsh & McLennan helpfully pointed out in its filing amounts ''to more than $100 million over the last five years."

To Hayes and other business specialists, Marsh & McLennan itself has to answer for Lasser's ''absolute control," faulty management processes, and huge paychecks. Executives at ''Marsh are in a very difficult position. Either they look like they were not providing sufficient oversight, or they were providing sufficient oversight and missed" the bad stuff at Putnam, said Howard Schneider, president of Practical Perspectives, a Boxford consulting firm to investment management companies.

Marsh & McLennan spokeswoman Barbara Perlmutter declined to comment, as did a spokesman for Lasser. However, in separate letters he wrote late last year, Lasser at one point said he was ''wholly unaware" of the employee trading, and at another said he ''knew there was a problem" but did not ''ask for details" and assumed his underlings would take of it.

Marsh & McLennan's statements about Lasser's handling -- or nonhandling -- of the employee trading problems in 2000 are at variance with what Putnam said was the situation back then.

To Marsh & McLennan, and others involved in the litigation, Lasser's main failing was not disclosing to the trustees and to corporate headquarters in New York that prominent fund managers had been caught making excessive trades in funds they supervised. But in its arbitration claim, Marsh & McLennan also cited ''the significant delay in properly responding to the market timing trades" as one example of Lasser's ''failure to perform his duties."

Yet in a March 19 filing to the SEC's most recent legal brief, Putnam said, ''When the former employees' trading was recognized as inappropriate, they were told to stop. And, for the most part, that is what they did."

Prior to the trading debacle, Marsh & McLennan benefited enormously from Putnam's success, for which Lasser presumably deserves some credit. Putnam was one of the hottest investment firms of the bull market years of the 1990s, and delivered tremendous profits to the parent company in New York. It accounted for 40 to 50 percent of Marsh & McLennan's pretax earnings and was the primary driver of the growth in the parent company's stock during this period, according to analysts who follow the company. In exchange, Marsh & McLennan officials rewarded Lasser with the enormous compensation packages they now say he so desperately was trying to protect -- and which Marsh & McLennan is also trying in the arbitration case to prevent him from collecting.

''Because he was a money maker, the rainmaker," Marsh & McLennan ''allowed him to continue," said Tamar Frankel, a law professor at Boston University who specializes in regulation of money management companies. The big paychecks, she added, also signaled to Lasser that Marsh & McLennan approved of what he was doing. ''Having paid him so much gives the message, 'I don't care how you make it, just give me' the profits," Frankel said.

In recent years Putnam has not been such a great money maker, as its go-go investment style set it up for a huge fall once the bull market bubble burst in 2000. As so many of its once high-flying funds tanked, Putnam experienced among the largest withdrawals by investors of any company in the industry. And with it, Putnam's importance to Marsh & McLennan's bottom line diminished. It's now about 20 percent of pretax earnings.

Marsh & McLennan kept Lasser on through the dark years of the bear market period, even though he had long passed the informal limit inside Putnam that senior executives serve no more than 10 years in their posts. But his handling of the crisis as it surfaced inside the company last fall -- a deliberative, analytical approach that bordered on paralysis, according to other Putnam executives and officials involved in the process -- may have convinced Marsh & McLennan that Lasser had stayed too long, and made it easier to get rid of him.

To be sure, some of the tough talk from Marsh & McLennan is posturing a party takes in a bitter negotiation over money to ''try to beat down the settlement" amount, in Hayes's words. But Marsh & McLennan's words may also be motivated by legal events outside the narrow issues of the arbitration proceeding. Marsh & McLennan is also a defendant alongside Putnam in some of the 70-odd lawsuits investors and class-action lawyers have filed over the market-timing trades.

Schneider, the investment management consultant, said Marsh & McLennan executives have no choice but to fight Lasser. ''They need to make it clear to the stockholders that they are not tolerant of what they perceive as inappropriate leadership behavior. Further, if they do not fight it, they are perceived as condoning if not encouraging this behavior. They also want it to appear that there is someone at Putnam to blame," he added. ''Otherwise, questions would rightly point to why Marsh Mac leadership was so hands off and failed to detect" the failings at Putnam.
