Cash only, please

Boston cabbies buck trend, say no to plastic

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  March 31, 2004

Fly to Chicago, and chances are you can pay the cabbie with your credit card for the ride from O'Hare International to the city. Go to Dallas or Los Angeles, and it's a certainty that your Visa or American Express will be welcome in every cab. The same will be true in New York City by late next year.

But in Boston -- where the tourism industry is struggling to recover from three years of economic doldrums and where 35,000 credit-card toting political conventioneers will gather in July -- the chances that you can use anything other than greenbacks are slim.

Most Boston cabbies accept only cash, even as cities around the country mandate that taxi drivers accept credit and debit cards. And some Boston drivers with the capability to process card payments dislike the practice so much that they discourage it.

''It's better if I get cash," Boston cab driver Yvon Celestin said yesterday. He will accept a credit or debit card payment only if the passenger tells him up front that he or she doesn't have cash.

''If you don't have cash, I'm not going to lose the job," he said. ''If you just get in the cab and you don't tell me you have a card, when you get there, you're going to have to give me cash."

Many cities don't want cabbies to have that option.

Yesterday, the New York City Taxi & Limousine Commission voted to mandate that all 12,187 cabs in the city have the capability to accept credit card payments by Nov. 1, 2005. Other cities, including Dallas, Chicago and Los Angeles, have similar regulations on the books. And paying the cabbie with a credit card is common in many other cities around the globe, from Singapore to Helsinki to Brussels.

But not in Boston.

''I don't think it's a handful of these operations" that take credit or debit cards, said Mark Cohen, director of licensing for the Boston Police Department, which regulates taxicabs. ''You just don't see that many."

Some cabbies would like to keep it that way. While swiping a card instead of digging for cash might seem more convenient for some passengers, there are clear disincentives for cab drivers. A wireless device that accepts credit-card payments and receives instant approvals or rejections can cost $800 to $1,500, some cabbies said.

On top of that, drivers generally have to pay a percentage of each card payment to the processing company, in addition to an 8 percent fee that the taxi garages collect -- a fee that does not apply to cash transactions. For some, that's just too much.

''From a company standpoint, we like the charges," said George Summers, treasurer of the Independent Taxi Operators Association, which dispatches about 380 cabs in Boston. ''Obviously, we get a benefit, but it's an 8 percent charge against what the drivers make."

Some passengers, including those who live in Boston and those who visit from cities where cash is not the only option, said they hope more drivers will find a way to get around the expense. Credit cards are much more convenient than cash, especially on business trips, when collecting receipts is necessary, said Michael Maloney of Somerville.

Maloney, who was hailing a cab to his home from Logan International Airport yesterday, said he finds that convenience in other cities. ''It's mostly my card for cabs, but not in Boston," he said. ''It's easier to expense if you have a receipt from a credit card than if you use cash."

Jill Hearn, an Alabama resident who traveled to Boston yesterday, said she was surprised that of all the cities where she can pay her cab fares with a credit card, Boston and New York are not among them.

''In New York, very few cabs in the city and from the airport take them," she said. ''In Dallas, I didn't have any problem, and in Orlando I had no problem."

Cohen, however, said there is no push to make Boston drivers accept plastic.

''I can't even remember the last time I've seen this come across my desk, either from individuals or from the hospitality industry in 17 years," he said.

New York's new credit-card requirement came with some concessions to cabbies, including an across-the-board fare increase and new fees for working at night and for taking trips to and from the John F. Kennedy, LaGuardia, and Newark international airports.

''It's a small number of cabs that have it at present," said Alan Fromburg, a spokesman for the New York City Taxi & Limousine Commission.

Fromburg noted that the measure was designed to keep cabbies safe by allowing them to carry less cash, making them less attractive targets for robbers.

''Its one of the things that we had to explore from a regulatory standpoint, to make it convenient for the consumer," he said.

At the beginning of the decade, several US cities began to require card-processing equipment in cabs --following the lead of many European cities, said Alfred LaGasse, executive vice president of the Taxicab, Limousine & Paratransit Association in Kensington, Md.

Initially, cabbies in those cities resisted the change, because the early portable equipment did not allow them to get an instant approval for a credit card charge. Instead, they would have to make an impression of the card on a carbon paper receipt, then turn the slips in at their garages to get the card payments approved at the end of the day.

''In the past, you couldn't get instant verification, so you'd have to take the card on good faith, then at the end of the day, if it was invalid, you had to take the loss," Lagasse said.

Since then, the technology has improved.

Boston cabbie Fred Meister carries a device that is essentially a Blackberry wireless unit with a credit-card slot on the bottom. If a passenger wants to pay with credit, Meister swipes the card, then waits about five seconds for an approval. Then he aims the device at a small printer, which receives the data wirelessly and prints a receipt.

Meister said the average credit card charge is $30, and fares to and from the airport and hotels are more likely to be paid with plastic. Last week, he took in $263 in credit card receipts.

''Most drivers don't like it. Most prefer to deal in cash," he said, despite that it is generally safer for cabbies not to carry a lot of cash.

''I would say the most savvy drivers would not want to go back to driving a cab without a credit card machine," he said. ''They know that it means they can make more money."
