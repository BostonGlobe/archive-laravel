More automakers seek star treatment for vehicles

By Royal Ford, Globe Columnist  |  March 21, 2004

These are not automobile accidents.

Cadillac CTS sports sedans and EXT sport utility vehicles snarl and slide across the screen in the movie ''The Matrix Reloaded."

Miami sometimes seems overrun with Hummer H2 SUVs, piloted by crime scene specialists, in the television show ''CSI: Miami."

And the fourth version of Sony's wildly popular PlayStation game, Gran Turismo, to be released this fall, will offer enthusiasts the chance to drive the super-expensive Ford GT high-performance sports car and the latest, affordable Ford Mustang.

The cars are making these calculated appearances in movies, video games, and television shows because of a critical and newly expansive change in how auto companies sell their vehicles.

Product placement is at the forefront of sweeping changes in automobile advertising strategy, and, say industry spokesmen, it is being fueled by an explosion of electronic entertainment options.

In some cases the car companies are paying millions of dollars, if not directly for placement then later for ''back end" costs that include promotion of film and product combined.

Television advertising, once covered with buys on the Big Three networks, is now a far more diverse field with the spread of cable channels. Gone are the days when a limited buy urging viewers to ''See the USA in Your Chevrolet" blanketed the electronic landscape.

Further, the advent of digital recording technologies such as TiVo, which offers viewers the option of avoiding commercials, have forced automakers to look for less intrusive -- and inescapable ways -- of showcasing their products.

''We are feeling a big fear about new technology as it relates to the 30-second commercial," said Steve Tihanyi, the general director for marketing alliances and regional operations for General Motors.

The appearance of cars that become virtual characters in movies is not, of course, a new concept.

James Bond, though he drove other cars, will forever be at the wheel of an Aston Martin in movie lore. Yet back when he first gripped a steering wheel, it was the car that gave panache to the character.

Today, the vehicles are placed by their builders in hopes that the aura of the character who drives them, or the show in which they appear, add to their image. Minis romped through the recent film ''The Italian Job." Lara Croft, played by Angelina Jolie, drove a Jeep Wrangler Rubicon in ''Tomb Raider: The Cradle of Life." And Ice Cube will tool about in an upcoming road film -- ''Are We There Yet?" -- in a Lincoln Navigator.

But does product placement pay for the automakers?

Tihanyi of GM said that, while his company's test results on placement are proprietary, they do show ''significant lifts in image and consideration" of the vehicles that are displayed. ''It works, we know it works," he said.

The companies will not disclose what they spend on product placement, but BusinessWeek has placed the number at $100 million for Toyota and $35 million for Aston Martin, though Aston Martin says that if that much was spent, it had to come from its parent, Ford Motor Co.

But the greatest evidence of the trend to broadening alternative promotions can be found in video gaming.

Ryan Bowling, a public relations specialist for Sony, makers of the PlayStation Gran Turismo racing series (30 million sold in its first three versions; Gran Turismo4 is on the way), recalls that, ''In the middle and late '90s, we were knocking on everybody's door" asking for permission to use their cars in the video game.

It was a common licensing deal then and remains one today, he said, but now, ''a lot of car companies are approaching us," looking to have their cars tear up the video screen.

The video game appeal is so compelling, in fact, that Volvo has produced a traditional television ad featuring a virtual version of its hip new S40, aimed at young buyers, as it rockets around in mockup of the Xbox game RalliSport Challenge 2.

Positioning cars as ''characters" in film, on television, and in video games, Musson said, ''is less in your face."

Jan Valentic, vice president of global marketing for Ford said that, in fact, a key question asked when taking a vehicle onscreen is: ''How do I embed my product in a sensitive way?"

Ford has been among the most aggressive of the automakers in its search for new ways to present its cars and trucks.

It sponsored, ad-free, the premieres of the first two years of the popular Fox network show ''24" -- except for the three-minute mini-movie that preceded each show featuring ''24" star Keifer Sutherland driving Fords that would later appear in the show.

Some companies, such as Mercedes-Benz, prefer to stay independent, scouring television and movie scripts for just the right spot.

The company, said Claire Curran, lifestyle marketing manager for Mercedes and Maybach, prefers to ''maintain our ability to work with any studio at any given time."

The appearance of the new E-Class sedan in ''Men in Black 2," she said, was the result of careful consideration and negotiation. Whatever the approach, control over who drives what and how the cars are used is carefully considered: You likely do not want terrorists using your Honda Odyssey minivan to transport explosives.

''We actually look at scripts" in an effort to find proper automotive roles, said Curran.

And placement can get quite specific to character, said Bob Hadler, vice president of Hadler Public Relations in California, which handles Chrysler products. Nothing beats the hero riding off into the sunset in your car.

Consider the syndicated television show ''Walker, Texas Ranger," said Hadler.

The martial arts-schooled good guy, played by Chuck Norris, always gets his man, drives a Dodge Ram, and, Hadler said, ''You really can't do a whole lot better than that."
