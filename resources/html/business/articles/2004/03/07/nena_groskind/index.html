Nena Groskind

March 7, 2004

Q. When my wife and I purchased a home, the seller failed to disclose its defects, and the home inspector failed to detect and report them to us. The repairs have cost us $20,000. An attorney has sent demand letters to both the seller and the inspector, but neither one has responded. Now we have to decide whether to pursue the matter in court and spend more money in an effort to recover what we’ve lost. What do you suggest? S.T. Gloucester

A. The question in any law suit is whether the damages and the likelihood of recovering them justify a court battle. A $20,000 bill is certainly worth ﬁghting over, if you have a reasonable chance of prevailing in court. Your attorney is in the best position to advise you on that point. Not knowing who said, or didn’t say, what, I can’t comment on the speciﬁcs. But I can point out that in Massachusetts, state law does not require sellers to disclose anything to prospective buyers. Sellers can’t lie, but if you didn’t ask speciﬁcally about a problem, your seller was not required to disclose it. And in order to recover anything from that source, you will have to prove that the information provided was so misleading it amounted to fraud. Your chances of recovering from the home inspector may be better, if the defects involved conditions a competent inspection should reasonably have identiﬁed. You’d probably have a good argument if the inspector ignored a sagging roof or water stains on the ceiling; defective pipes hidden behind a wall would be another matter.

Q. I own a two-family house. Until six months ago, I occupied the duplex portion and rented the ﬁrst ﬂoor unit. then moved to the ﬁrst ﬂoor and rented the duplex to a tenant, who now wants to purchase it as a condominium. My proﬁt after paying off the mortgage will be about $60,000. Will I be able to exclude that gain even though I am still living in a portion of the house? P.C. Boston

A. The gain isn’t the amount you pocket after paying off the mortgage; it’s the difference between your ‘‘basis’’ in the property, which is essentially the price you paid for it and the selling price. If you paid $60,000 for the property and sold the entire structure for $200,000, your gain would be $140,000 adjusted up or down for capital improvements and selling expenses. Your situation is more complicated because you’re selling a portion of the property. Let’s say the duplex represents two-thirds of the property and your tenant is paying $120,000 for it. Your gain would be $80,000, that’s $120,000 minus $40,000 (two-thirds of the $60,000 staring basis).

A married couple ﬁling jointly can deduct up to $500,000 of the gain on the sale of a primary residence and an individual up to $250,000, if they have owned and occupied it for two of the ﬁve years preceding the sale. If you meet that ownership and occupancy test, you can exclude the gain, regardless of where you are living now.
