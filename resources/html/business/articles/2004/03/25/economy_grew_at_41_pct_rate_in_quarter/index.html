Corporate Profits Rose in 2003

By Jonathan Nicholson, 3/25/2004

WASHINGTON (Reuters) - Corporate profits posted a second straight double-digit annual gain in 2003, the Commerce Department said on Thursday, but a lackluster job market suggests companies remain hesitant to hire.

The report also worried some in the inflation-wary bond market with signs of nascent price pressures.

The Commerce Department said after-tax corporate profits rose 7.6 percent in the fourth quarter, a slowdown from the 10.1 percent gain in the third quarter. For all of 2003, the department said after-tax profits were up a hefty 19.2 percent. That was down, however, from 2002's 24.6 percent increase.

The department also said a price index for personal spending outside of food and energy was revised upward, to a 1.2 percent annual rate from a previously reported 0.7 percent pace in the fourth quarter. It is the Federal Reserve's favored measure of inflation, and the unexpected revision could make it harder to justify keeping interest rates at current 46-year lows.

The data was contained in the third reading on fourth-quarter 2003 U.S. economic growth, which showed gross domestic product rose at a 4.1 percent annual rate, the same as reported last month and half the pace of the third quarter.

It was the first time since 1996 and 1997 that U.S. companies have enjoyed back-to-back double-digit annual profits, the department said.

In a separate report, the Labor Department said new claims for jobless benefits rose by 1,000 to 339,000 in the week ended March 20. That was slightly above economists' projections.

Market reaction was mixed. Stock markets were higher by the end of trading, with the Dow Jones industrial average up 1.70 percent. But bond traders focused on the price index and bid prices lower.

PROFITS EQUAL JOBS?

Economists were impressed by the uptrend in corporate bottom lines. Higher profits usually lead to increased spending on new plants and equipment, and eventually, jobs.

Bush administration officials keep predicting job gains will follow as the economy grows. On Wednesday, Treasury Secretary John Snow said he was "mighty optimistic" about the economy. "When businesses see their order books fill up, when the cash register is jingling, businesses want to expand, and they expand and they hire people," he told Fox Television.

In one good sign for job-seekers, the Conference Board said its index of help-wanted ads in U.S. newspapers rose in February, to 40 from 39 in January. The gauge tracks ad volume in 51 newspapers across the country.

But Jared Bernstein, senior economist with the liberal-leaning Economic Policy Institute, said the GDP report shows the income generated by companies -- profits and worker compensation -- becoming more uneven. The share going to profits hit 21.9 percent in the fourth quarter, he said, matching a high seen 1997.

"It's very difficult to sustain a robust recovery .... without more compensation to the labor market," Bernstein said.

Labor market weakness has led the Federal Reserve to keep short-term interest rates at 1.0 percent, the lowest since 1958. Last week, the Fed's monetary policy panel said: "Although job losses have slowed, new hiring has lagged."

LOOMING PRICE PRESSURES?

The sizable revision to the price index excluding food and energy raised eyebrows on Wall Street.

Stephen Stanley, chief economist with RBS Greenwich Capital, said in a research note: "This is noteworthy because it means that inflation is no longer too low for the Fed's liking."

In another report, the National Association of Realtors said sales of existing homes in February rose 2.0 percent to a seasonally adjusted 6.12 million annual rate, close to analysts' expectations.
