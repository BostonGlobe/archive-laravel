BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Liberty Mutual profit jumped 67% in '03

March 24, 2004

Liberty Mutual Group, the fifth-largest US commercial insurer, said profit rose 67 percent last year as it raised prices and had a gain on the sale of its Canadian health insurance business. Net income climbed to $851.3 million from $508.4 million in 2002, Boston-based Liberty said. Revenue rose 16 percent to $16.6 billion. Liberty, owned by its policyholders, raised rates on workers' compensation, liability, and commercial car policies and increased profit even as it added $331 million to reserves for asbestos claims. The additional reserves prompted Standard & Poor's in October to revise its outlook on the company's debt, to stable from negative. Premiums rose 17 percent to $13.96 billion, investment income rose 11 percent to $1.76 billion, and realized investment gains, including $81.9 million from the Canadian sale to John Hancock Financial Services Inc., climbed to $372.8 million from $274 million. (Bloomberg)

Akamai operations chief to leave in April

Akamai Technologies Inc., which makes software to speed the delivery of data over the Internet, said chief operating officer Michael Ruffolo will leave the company April 9 and won't be replaced. Members of Akamai's senior management, including chairman and chief executive George Conrades, president Paul Sagan, and vice president of global sales and services Robert Hughes, will assume Ruffolo's responsibilities, Akamai said. Ruffolo joined Akamai in August 2001 as executive vice president and was named chief operating officer in November. He will stay active on the boards of technology companies and focus on investing in new companies and provide consulting services for business needing restructuring advice, Akamai said. Shares of Cambridge-based Akamai fell $1.43, or 10.1 percent, to $12.68. (Bloomberg)

Biogen Idec, partner seek OK for MS drug

Biogen Idec Corp., the third-largest biotechnology company, and partner Elan Corp. will ask European regulators this year to approve their Antegren multiple sclerosis treatment. The European Agency for the Evaluation of Medicinal Products agreed to review the medicine based on data from the first 12 months of two studies that continue for another year, the companies said. Biogen and Elan last month said they planned to seek US approval of Antegren by mid-2004, a year earlier than analysts had expected. Cambridge-based Biogen is counting on Antegren to make up for slowing growth of older products, such as its top-selling Avonex MS treatment, and Ireland-based Elan needs a successful product after selling $1.8 billion in assets to pay down debt. (Bloomberg)

Brazilian official may have helped GTech

Brazil's government said it has ''strong evidence" that Waldomiro Diniz, a former aide to cabinet chief Jose Dirceu, may have helped lottery equipment company GTech Holdings Corp. extend contracts in that country. The government, in a report of an internal inquiry into corruption allegations, said it has evidence Diniz discussed matters that weren't related to his official functions with West Greenwich, R.I.-based GTech, the world's biggest supplier of online lottery systems. President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva fired Diniz last month, after Epoca magazine published allegations that the aide had accepted campaign donations on behalf of Lula's Workers' Party from an illegal gambling business in 2002. A week later Epoca reported that Diniz allegedly used his position in the government to help GTech. A GTech spokesman in Brazil, who asked not to be identified, said the company hasn't received the report and will only comment after that. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Bank of America names two to top posts

Bank of America Corp., the third-largest US bank, named a chief financial officer and head of investment banking as it prepares to complete its $47 billion purchase of FleetBoston Financial Corp. next month. Marc Oken, 57, was named CFO to replace James Hance, 59, who will remain as vice chairman. Alvaro G. de Molina, 46, is taking over for investment banking chief Edward Brown, 55, who the company said is retiring. Bank of America chief executive Kenneth Lewis, 56, is creating the number two US bank by assets with the merger, setting the stage to rival Citigroup Inc. and J.P. Morgan Chase & Co. He's also trying to reassure investors amid regulatory probes into the company's mutual fund trading, stock research, and financing of Italian food company Parmalat Finanziaria SpA. Oken will oversee the corporate treasurer's office and lead the merger transition. (Bloomberg)

Goldman Sachs earnings rocket 95%

Goldman Sachs Group Inc., the third-largest securities firm, said fiscal first-quarter earnings almost doubled to a record on gains from trading stocks, bonds, and commodities. Net income increased 95 percent to $1.29 billion, or $2.50 a share, from $662 million, or $1.29, a year earlier, the company said. Chief executive Henry Paulson controlled expenses and exploited price swings that fueled a 47 percent rise in trading revenue. The earnings cap the most profitable quarter ever for Goldman, Morgan Stanley, Lehman Brother Holdings Inc., and Bear Stearns Cos., which had combined net income of $3.55 billion. Goldman's net revenue in the quarter ended Feb. 29 rose 42 percent to a record $5.93 billion. That was led by a 9 percent gain in fixed income, currency, and commodities trading, which amounted to $2.1 billion. Equities trading almost tripled to $946 million. (Bloomberg)

Wal-Mart launches online music store

Wal-Mart Stores Inc. officially launched its online music store with an expanded roster of artists, and kept the price at the same 88 cents per song that it offered during a three-month test. The store, which sells digital downloads for 11 percent less than major competitors, expanded its catalog of artists by 50 percent, including exclusive songs from Jessica Simpson, 3 Doors Down, Shania Twain, and others, the retailer said. Wal-Mart is the dominant force in US retailing, but it was relatively late to the dot-com world and has been adding online services in hopes of boosting its Web presence. A Wal-Mart spokeswoman declined to comment on whether the service was profitable, or on how many songs had been downloaded, but said demand ''far exceeded" company forecasts. (Reuters)

Penthouse buys Internet billing company

Penthouse International Inc., which publishes the adult magazine by the same name, said it acquired Internet Billing Co. from InterCept Inc. for $23.5 million to help streamline customer-service operations. The purchase of InterCept's iBill includes $20 million in assumed debt, Penthouse said. Customers use iBill to market and sell their products over the Internet. The service also processes online payments and tracks sales transactions. Penthouse said its majority shareholder and an unnamed insurance company funded the purchase. It wasn't more specific. (Bloomberg)

Options-expensing rule kicks in next year

Intel Corp., Yahoo Inc., and other US companies that have resisted making stock options an expense will have to do so starting next year under a rule the Financial Accounting Standards Board will issue this month. FASB, which sets US rules under authority of the Securities and Exchange Commission, will publish the rule as soon as this week, said a FASB spokeswoman. Expensing would reduce the average per-share profit among Standard & Poor's 500 companies next year by 3 percent, according to a report by Bear Stearns Cos. analyst Patricia McConnell. Federal Reserve chairman Alan Greenspan and other advocates say omitting option costs lets companies overstate profit, encourages executives to inflate results, and obscures a transfer of wealth from holders to executives and employees. (Bloomberg)
