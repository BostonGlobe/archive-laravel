Putnam: Lasser ignored problems

Firm says ex-CEO ousted 'with cause'

By Andrew Caffrey, Associated Press  |  March 24, 2004

Putnam Investments accused its former chief executive, Lawrence J. Lasser, of making a ‘‘conscious decision to stick his head in the sand’’ about improper trading by top employees in 2000 because the disclosures could undermine the ‘‘absolute control’’ he exerted over the ﬁrm, the company said in an arbitration proceeding against Lasser.

The 18-year chief executive also was terminated ‘‘with cause’’ in October for not dealing swiftly with the employee trading scandal that engulfed the venerable ﬁrm, Putnam added, leading clients to pull some $54 billion from Putnam in subsequent weeks.

‘‘Lasser’s failure to perform his duties has resulted in substantial and mounting damage to Putnam’’ and its parent, Marsh & McLennan Cos., according to the arbitration ﬁling. '‘Because of the signiﬁcant delay in properly responding to the market-timing trades, the failure to make adequate disclosures regarding those trades, and his failure to manage Putnam properly,’’ the company said it faces ‘‘a wave of litigation’’ and withdrawals by clients that ‘‘has resulted in millions of dollars in lost fee income.’’

While the arbitration proceedings are conﬁdential, excerpts of Putnam’s and Marsh & McLennan’s argument against Lasser are contained in a legal brief the US Securities and Exchange Commission recently ﬁled in its ongoing fraud proceeding against Putnam. The company has so far agreed to a partial settlement of charges that it failed to prevent six investment managers from repeatedly trading company funds, and for not telling the funds’ trustees about the trades.

Putnam and Marsh & McLennan are using their unusually harsh assessments of Putnam’s long-time chief executive to argue Lasser isn’t owed tens of millions of dollars he is thought to be seeking.

And the excerpts show the companies are trying to use the less ﬂattering aspects of Lasser’s reputation — as a hard-charging, mercurial executive obsessed with protecting his and Putnam’s position — against him, even thoughfor years Marsh & McLennan raked in handsome proﬁts from Putnam as a result of Lasser’s driving management style.

‘‘Lasser was motivated by a desire to protect and maintain his position as president and CEO of Putnam, and to avoid `rocking the boat’ or doing anything that might reﬂect unfavorably on his stewardship or affect his compensation, which amounted to more than $100 million over the last ﬁve years,’’ the companies said in the arbitration ﬁling.

Lasser hasn’t disclosed how much he is seeking from Marsh & McLennan, although some compensation specialists who have reviewed his complicated contract have estimated he could be due $46 million to $89 million, depending on variables such as the value of stock options.

Yesterday, Lasser declined to respond to the accusations levied at him by Putnam and Marsh & McLennan. Instead a spokesman for him issued a statement that said, ‘‘Mr. Lasser has responded in the arbitration proceeding, which he initiated against Putnam and Marsh & McLennan in January 2004. It is his belief that the appropriate forum for resolution of his claims is in the arbitration proceedings.’’

But in other documents Lasser has taken a harder line against his former employer, and also said several different things about what he knew — or didn’t know — about the trading, and when. For example, in a Dec. 18, 2003, letter to trustees chairman John A. Hill, Lasser said that once he left Putnam at the end of last year, he would ‘‘no longer allow myself to be made a scapegoat.’’ Also in that letter, Lasser asserted that he was ‘‘wholly unaware’’ of the trading activity of the employees at the time it became an issue in 2000. ‘‘I knew nothing about market timing by any Putnam employee until September/October 2003,’’ Lasser wrote.

But in a Nov. 5, 2003 letter to George Putnam, the former chairman of the funds and son of the company’s founder, Lasser wrote, ‘‘I knew there was a problem and had conﬁdence in the people who were responsible for dealing with it. I didn’t ask for details.’’ Also Lasser had scheduled a discussion on ‘‘market timers’’ for a meeting of top company executives in June 1999, according to the excerpt of Putnam and Marsh & McLennan’s arbitration ﬁling.

‘‘Nevertheless, when it came time to respond to evidence of market timing by Putnam employees, Lasser made a conscious decision to stick his head in the sand,’’ the companies said. A Marsh & McLennan spokeswoman declined to comment on the arbitration ﬁling.

While Lasser had helped build Putnam into an investment powerhouse, he also developed a reputation as an overbearing boss who, in addition to ruling Putnam with an iron hand, successfully kept executives at Marsh & McLennan at arm’s length.

Putnam and Marsh & McLennan appear to be playing on this lack of sympathy for Lasser — that he wouldn’t have a reservoir of goodwill to rely on among, for example, his colleagues on the board of trustees of Putnam’s mutual funds.

‘‘Lasser knew that disclosure of the employee market timing trades would reﬂect poorly on him, and raise questions about his leadership and the management processes and structures he had employed to run Putnam — structures and processes which permitted the employee market timing transactions to occur and go undetected,’’ the SEC quotes Putnam and Marsh & McLennan as saying.

‘‘In turn, Lasser knew that if the market timing trades were revealed to the Putnam Trustees or to anyone at [Marsh], he could be forced to surrender the absolute control with which he had managed Putnam for years.’’

Since his dismissal Lasser and Marsh & McLennan have been ﬁghting over how much he is owed. Marsh & McLennan has previously questioned whether Lasser should get anything, when in January the company disclosed the arbitration proceeding ‘‘with respect to the amount of money, if any, owed to Mr. Lasser in connection with his termination from Putnam.’’

Indeed, Lasser would not get certain beneﬁts if he was booted out, according to the terms of his employment contract. For example, Lasser would not receive a ‘‘deferred special payment,’’ valued at $16.7 million based on 300,000 shares of Marsh & McLennan’s stock on Feb. 15, 2001, if he is terminated ‘‘for cause,’’ or if he ‘‘terminates his own employment’’ for reasons other than ‘‘good cause.’’ ‘‘When you’re talking about that much money at stake, things can get nasty,’’ said Alex Thomson, a managing director at executive search ﬁrm Russell Reynold Associates.

He said it was widely perceived in the ﬁnancial industry ‘‘that Marsh & McLennan allowed Larry to run his own show, and as long as the revenues were coming in, they seemed satisﬁed with that. But when things go awry, and you’re talking about that much money at stake, the tide turns. So I’m not surprised’’ the companies would ‘‘use these words’’ about Lasser.
