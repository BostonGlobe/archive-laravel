Stewart's conviction could hurt Kmart

By Associated Press  |  March 10, 2004

DETROIT -- Kmart Holding Corp., on the mend after a trip to bankruptcy court and struggling to lure consumers back to its stores, could see the shine on its marquee brand dim now that Martha Stewart faces prison for lying about a stock sale.

The brand -- which analysts call a key driver of store traffic -- is one of the few things the retailer has going for it as it struggles to compete with Wal-Mart Stores Inc. and Target Corp. Kmart called its exclusive rights to Martha Stewart Everyday housewares and other products key to its recovery since emerging from Chapter 11 last year.

"Other than Martha Stewart and her daughter, nobody was more upset Friday afternoon than executives at Kmart," said Seth Siegel, cofounder of The Beanstalk Group, a trademark licensing agency.

Since Stewart's legal woes began, her company's branded merchandise, such as the Martha Stewart Everyday line of sheets, towels, and other products, has continued to do well.

But Burt Flickinger, managing partner at consulting firm Strategic Resource Group in New York, says as much as 60 percent of the brand's shopper base could be lost because of Stewart's conviction.

"They won't want to support what is perceived to be personal or professional corporate crime," he said.

Their defection will cost Kmart $1 billion in sales in one full year, including lost sales from other merchandise shoppers buy after they have been lured to Kmart by Martha Stewart merchandise, Flickinger said.

Kmart has been silent on the future of its relationship with Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia since Stewart was found guilty of lying to government investigators. The retailer said only that it was "saddened" by the conviction.

Still, analysts said Kmart is unlikely to abandon Stewart. Sales of her products at Kmart were $1.5 billion in 2002 -- about 5 percent of Kmart's total sales.

"The brand Martha Stewart was probably more favorably looked at than Kmart itself," said Gary Ruffing, a retail consultant with BBK Ltd. and a former Kmart executive. "It would be very tough for them to walk away."

Kmart, which first teamed up with Stewart's company in 1987, entered into a seven-year licensing agreement with it in June 2001.
