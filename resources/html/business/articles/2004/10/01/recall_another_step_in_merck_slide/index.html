Recall another step in Merck slide

Latest stumble pummels stock, to hurt earnings

By Christopher Rowland, Globe Staff  |  October 1, 2004

Merck & Co.'s decision to pull its widely prescribed drug Vioxx off the shelves yesterday because of a risk of stroke and heart attacks marked a new low for a pharmaceutical company that has suffered a steady decline in both market position and prestige over the last decade.

The drug for osteoarthritis symptoms and acute pain earned the company $2.5 billion in 2003 and was among Merck's biggest-selling drugs, with sales pushed by a $100 million-a-year television and print advertising campaign that gave it a high profile in American homes.

Wall Street investors reacting to the crisis yesterday morning almost immediately stripped $26 billion away from Merck's market value. The company's stock plummeted 26.8 percent, from $45.07 to $33, by the close of trading, pulling down the Dow Jones Industrial Average by 55.97, or 0.6 percent, to 10,080.27.

Merck said the disclosure would also mean losses on earnings per share of 50 or 60 cents for the rest of the year.

Although its global sales in 2003 gave it a ranking as the world's third-largest drug company, behind Pfizer Inc. and GlaxosmithKline, Merck has been criticized for having a weak discovery pipeline. CEO and president Raymond Gilmartin, scheduled to retire in 2006, has tried to turn that perception around, including the opening of a research center with 300 scientists and 450 support staff in Boston next week. But those efforts have yet to sway Wall Street skeptics.

"They've been in effect weakening themselves over the past decade. They have been losing market share and losing relative stock market value," said Sam Isaly, manager of Eaton Vance Worldwide Health Sciences fund. "This exacerbates the situation."

Isaly said his fund did not hold any Merck shares, in part because of earlier studies that Vioxx had potentially toxic effects on the cardiovascular system.

With yesterday's news, Merck's market capitalization dropped to $73 billion, near that of Amgen Inc., a California biotech company without mass-market drugs like Vioxx or Merck's biggest seller, the cholesterol-fighter Zocor.

That represents a long slide from Merck's position in the 1980s, when it introduced 10 new drugs to the market and commanded a leading position in the world's pharmaceutical industry. The company, with headquarters in Whitehouse Station, N.J., has had a US presence for more than 100 years.

Now, as it works through the logistics of withdrawing millions of pills from the market, Merck said it is bracing for a flood of patient and stockholder lawsuits. Jim Hall, president of the life sciences group at Wood MacKenzie in Boston, a global consulting firm, said doctors have probably written 100 million Vioxx prescriptions nationwide, creating wide exposure to potential lawsuits. The volume of litigation will exceed that of the diet drug fen-phen, he predicted, which was withdrawn by maker American Home Products in 1997.

"They've got a huge problem going. There's got to be a potential pool of 100,000 patients out there that could turn around and sue," said Hall. "It's not only heart attacks and strokes, it's problems like blood clots. That's a pretty wide area of problems as a result of taking the drug."

An early estimate is that it could cost Merck more than $10 billion to $20 billion related to litigation, Hall said. Merck said it plans to fight the court actions.

Lawsuits already filed allege Merck made misleading claims in advertising for Vioxx, playing up product benefits while failing to inform consumers of increased health risks.

The company pulled the drug yesterday, it said, after receiving results from a long-term clinical trial for Vioxx's potential effect on colon cancer that showed an increase in risk for serious cardiovascular problems.

But there were earlier signs of trouble. After Vioxx won Food and Drug Administration market approval in 1999, a 2000 clinical study showed an increase in heart-related risks. In 2001, the FDA issued a warning letter to Merck, saying it had failed to include information about that trial in promotional materials. Then, two years after the 2000 trial results, the FDA required a change to the Vioxx label to reflect the elevated risk.

Despite the early indications of risk, Merck continued to say its drug was safe and marketed Vioxx aggressively as it fought for share against Pfizer's Celebrex. From January to July this year, Merck spent $49.5 million on its Vioxx direct-to-consumer ad campaign, according to TNS Media Intelligence/CMR.

"Certainly it's an ethics issue. This is an example of why direct-to-consumer advertising of prescription drugs that have major potential side effects is just a very bad idea," said George Annas, a professor of health law at Boston University School of Public Health. "In theory it is educational. But it doesn't educate, it just misleads."

As recently as a month ago, Merck's president of research, Peter S. Kim, a former Massachusetts Institute of Technology scientist, strongly defended Vioxx after a critical study released by the health insurance company Kaiser Permanente. "The robust clinical trial data available support the safety of Vioxx," Kim said in a company press release.

Merck spokeswoman Janet Skidmore said yesterday that Kim did not have access to the new data when he issued that statement. The company and the FDA said Merck brought the latest clinical information to the agency's attention on Monday after being alerted to the problem last week.

"The data only came to us a week ago," said Skidmore.

Harvard Medical School physician Dr. John Abramson, who recently published a book critical of the drug industry, with a chapter about Vioxx, said Merck failed to provide adequate health-risk information to consumers and doctors. He called it a systemic problem.

"The information that docs rely on to make decisions has to be unbiased, and the government needs to play a role in that," he said. "Drug companies cannot do it themselves. It's like a pro football team trying to referee its own game."

But Dr. John Jenkins, chief of the office of new drugs at the FDA, said the agency requires in consumer advertisements that risk to be disclosed. Moreover, he said the FDA took into account a good safety profile on gastrointestinal effects of Vioxx when it weighed the early data showing the potential for heart problems. Critics who say the agency didn't recognize the severity of problems fast enough, he said, are engaging in "Monday-morning quarterbacking."
