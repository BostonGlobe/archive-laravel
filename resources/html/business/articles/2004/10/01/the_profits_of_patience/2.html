The profits of patience

October 1, 2004

Not all of Karp's deals have worked out.

A plan for a three-way merger of Karp's properties with those of Richard Jacobs of Cleveland and Retail Property Trust fell apart in 1997, and a partnership with Robert Kraft to own Foxboro Stadium ended after Kraft decided to buy the New England Patriots. A plan to revitalize a faltering downtown Worcester retail center as Worcester Common Fashion Outlets brought no enduring success.

But Karp's wins well outnumber his losses. New England Development built more retail space in the go-go 1980s than any other firm in the nation, according to industry rankings. And in 1999 he sold 14 of his regional malls to Simon Property Group for $1.7 billion.

He is developing The Pinehills in Plymouth and Wisconsin Place, the latter a 1.1 million-square-foot retail, office, and residential community in Chevy Chase, Md. Karp is working on a 900,000-square-foot multiple-use project near the Atrium. And -- 13 years after the CambridgeSide Galleria opened -- a hotel last year became the project's final piece.

Karp is a partner in developing a new Westin hotel at D and Summer streets in South Boston that will become the headquarters inn of the new Boston Convention & Exhibition Center.

"The guy does more deals in a week than most people do in a lifetime," Rob Griffin, president of Cushman & Wakefield of Massachusetts Inc., said one day last week. "He sold his mall business and reinvented himself when a lot of people thought he was going to ride off into the sunset."

But something will have to be built soon on Fan Pier, which has been used as a parking lot for years.

"I don't think that land can sit idle for long because of the amount of money people paid for it," said Kevin C. Phelan, executive vice president of Meredith & Grew Inc./Oncor. "The cost of money runs every day, 7/24."

John E. Drew, developer of Fan Pier's neighbor, the World Trade Center, has plans to build a shopping center on 12 acres of Massachusetts Port Authority land near Anthony's. But even so, Phelan says don't rule out the possibility of Karp doing what Karp does best on his land. And that's building even more shopping space in that area. "I think retail has much more life than people give it," said Phelan.

Fan Pier is big -- but Karp says it's not the most important project he's involved in.

"Children's Hospital -- that's where I spend most of my time," said Karp, who is heading a capital campaign that he says will exceed its $250 million goal.

Karp is involved with several real estate projects the hospital is undertaking, as well as being chairman of its board. Last week Children's officials agreed to buy the former Waltham Hospital for $53 million, saying they've run out of room at their Longwood Avenue campus.

"He's as passionate about Children's Hospital as he as about anything else he does," said Griffin. "On the Waltham deal, he had his whole staff working on it."

Karp this year bought the Jared Coffin House, a historic inn on Nantucket, adding it to the Wauwinet and other holdings there.

He says he has no plans to quit looking for new deals.

"We were looking," Karp said on the phone one evening this week, "at something today."
