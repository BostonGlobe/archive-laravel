Sign off, then sign in

Banks' changing names boost a Cape business

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  October 29, 2004

SOUTH YARMOUTH -- G. Michael Caggiano Jr. lies awake at night thinking about bank signs. He ponders them during breakfast, while brushing his teeth, and "constantly" during the day, he says.

He and his fellow sign makers at Plymouth Sign Co. have been working overtime for weeks, cutting boards and painting letters by hand to meet the demands of the bank merger boom sweeping New England. When banks need new signs for their branches anywhere on Cape Cod, they frequently turn to Caggiano, who owns Plymouth Sign with his father, George M. Caggiano. And these days, there are a lot of banks needing new signs.

"We love bank mergers," Michael Caggiano said. "It's great for the economy down here."

"We want more of them," his father agreed.

In the last year, there have been at least eight mergers involving banks in the Massachusetts market, including the biggest of them all, Bank of America's $48 billion acquisition of FleetBoston Financial Corp. Banks have been providing steady work for sign makers for a decade, as once-familiar names such as Shawmut, BayBanks, and BankBoston disappeared from the landscape.

On the Cape, "first Banknorth came in, then Rockland Trust bought out Falmouth Bank, then Sovereign Bank took over Compass Bank and Bank of America took over Fleet," Michael Caggiano said. "We just finish one of the bank conversions, and then we start on another."

And there may be more to come: Plymouth Sign hopes for work from Eastern Bank, which recently said it would merge with Plymouth Savings Bank, and another round of business from Banknorth Group, which plans to change its name to TD Banknorth after its acquisition by Canada's TD Bank Financial Group.

Now, Plymouth Sign's revenue is soaring, and it has added five workers to its staff in the last year, swelling its ranks to 20. It also has gone on an acquisition streak of its own, gobbling up two smaller sign makers on the Cape. Bank signs now comprise more than a third of the company's business.

Other bank sign makers also are reaping the rewards of mergers. Batten Bros. Inc., a Wakefield sign maker, said its bank sign business has grown more than 15 percent over the last several years. Philadelphia Sign Co., one of the larger national sign companies, has added 14 workers at its local office in Littleton in the last three years, thanks in large part to business from Citizens Financial Group.

At Plymouth Sign, the shop itself is a veritable shrine to the history of Massachusetts banking. The Caggianos keep a photo album on the counter stuffed with photos of their bank jobs over the years, more than 25 in all. In one office, a green BankBoston sign hangs on the same wall as a calendar featuring Pamela Anderson in a bikini, with equal prominence given to both. In file folders nearby, the sign makers have plans for dozens of new Bank of America signs, which the shop will help put on Fleet branches in December.

Out back in a patch of dead leaves is what the Caggianos call the "old sign graveyard": a pile of signs from defunct banks around the state. They have an old Bank of Boston sign, blue with a gold eagle. From around the same time, there is an old Fleet Bank sign with the characteristic sailboat logo, which went out of style after the bank acquired BankBoston. There is also a blue Norwest Mortgage sign, a Hospital Trust sign, and a white NBIS sign, missing the I, for New Bedford Institution for Savings.

Occasionally, an antique dealer will visit, looking for a deal. Other times, nostalgic bank executives will call and ask for mementos. But mostly the signs just sit there. Michael Caggiano insists that he is "in the process" of cleaning up. His father sounds more inclined to leave the bank signs alone. "It's almost nostalgic," George Caggiano said. "It's hard to throw this stuff away."

George Caggiano joined his brother in the sign business in 1958, the year they moved the shop from Plymouth to Yarmouth. He married his high school sweetheart, had three children, and plans to pass the business on to his son, Michael. The Caggianos' cousin, Butch, came to work in the shop more than 30 years ago, and he has stayed ever since.

The company's first bank deal: First National Bank of Yarmouth, which he says over the years turned into First National Bank of Boston, then Bank of Boston, then BankBoston, then Fleet. Soon, it will be Bank of America. The Caggianos think they have created every new sign for that one Yarmouth branch since the 1960s.

"Those were the days when you could walk into the bank and talk to the president and tell him what you needed," George Caggiano said. "It doesn't work that way anymore."

That was before the Cape exploded in wealth, before hordes of rich executives clamored to buy summer homes near the shore. Close on their heels came large and acquisitive banks, eager to do business with the newly rich. George and Michael Caggiano live a couple towns over from some senior executives at Bank of America, though they have never met them.

Still, Bank of America, Fleet, and most major banks in the state have been good to them, turning to Plymouth Sign time and time again to replace signs from Plymouth to Provincetown. The bigger banks tend to rely on large companies to manufacture the signs, but they farm out the installation work to dozens of local sign makers. One reason: the locals often have a town-by-town knowledge of sign code regulations, which differ wildly across the state.

On the Cape these days, most towns allow only carved or painted signs, nothing with interior lights. And there are towns that are even pickier. In Chatham, local officials recently barred Sovereign Bank from using its trademark red on its signs; the bank had to settle for burgundy instead.

The busiest time in a bank sign maker's life comes the weekend of a bank's conversion. The banks generally do all their switching over a single weekend, requiring signmakers to work 14- to 16-hour days to get it done. Sometimes, depending on the bank's preference, some of the work can be done in advance: The sign makers can replace the signs in the weeks leading up to the formal switch, then put a canvas covering with the other bank's name back over it.

Though the sign makers are happy to be busy now, they grow contemplative when they think about the future of the bank sign business, when banks run out of other banks to buy. "This isn't going to go on forever and ever," George Caggiano said. "But if it does, that's OK."
