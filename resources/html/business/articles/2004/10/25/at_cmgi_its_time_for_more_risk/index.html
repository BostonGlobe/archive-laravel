At CMGI, it's time for more risk

By Scott Kirsner  |  October 25, 2004

CMGI is planning to re-up its commitment to venture capital investing by dedicating $50 million to the company's @ventures arm.

To quote from ''This is Spinal Tap," that ageless cinematic masterpiece: ''It's such a fine line between clever and stupid."

CMGI, once a sprawling Internet conglomerate based in Andover, has spent the post-bubble years selling off businesses and shrinking as its stock price has dropped from triple digits to just over a buck. The company, which once operated online auction sites, Web advertising companies, stock talk bulletin boards, and digital entertainment portals, is now primarily in the business of distributing printed literature. That's right: shipping brochures to bank branches.

The company's new chief executive, Joe Lawler, comes out of the printing industry. So I was amazed to hear earlier this month from Marc Poirier, one of the few remaining venture capitalists at CMGI, that the company was planning to dedicate $50 million of its $270 million cash war chest to making investments in a new crop of start-ups.

The company, now headquartered in Waltham, won't talk. A spokesman told me they're ''not prepared to discuss what's happening with @ventures," the VC division that made early investments in companies like Lycos, GeoCities, eGroups, and Half.com. And when I tried to wring more details from Poirier, he said that the renewed commitment to venture capital was part of a larger strategy at CMGI that Lawler, who started back in August, wasn't yet ready to lay out.

But the @ventures website indicates that the group is planning to invest in some new areas, such as clean energy technologies and ''technology applied to physical security."

In the past, they'd been focused predominantly on e-commerce and digital media. @ventures has just two active partners these days: Peter Mills, a longtime business associate of CMGI chairman David Wetherell who works on the West Coast, and Poirier, a former Ernst & Young accountant who still works in Andover.

David Wetherell ''had some pretty unique Internet visions that ended up panning out pretty well," says Dan Nova, an early @ventures employee who now works at Highland Capital Partners in Lexington. (Wetherell was closely involved with most of the early @ventures picks.) ''The question I would have is, what is their unique expertise in areas like renewable energy?"

''The only possible explanation is that they see the venture business as a money-making opportunity," says Bo Peabody, a founder of Village Ventures in Williamstown. ''But I would be surprised if CMGI's institutional holders would be excited about that."

Clever or stupid? Too early to tell, at least until @ventures starts making some new investments from its fresh stash.

But it'd be especially intriguing if Wetherell again had a hand in identifying promising start-ups or green-lighting investments -- a real possibility, according to former CMGI president David Andonian. And it's certainly a daring decision for a company that barely survived the Internet bust.

Digital Play-Doh
SolidWorks is a Concord company that makes serious modeling software for the engineers who design things like handheld GPS devices, bikes, telephones, and medical devices. So it's surprising that the company's newest product is a software package for kids called Cosmic Blobs.

''You always want to try new things, and be able to nurture technologies," says Scott Harris, a SolidWorks cofounder who has been shepherding the Blobs project along for several years. Still, getting into the kids' software business is an odd strategy for a company whose 325,000 customers so far are predominantly holders of advanced engineering degrees. (SolidWorks is owned by the French company Dassault Systemes S.A.)

But the company wanted to pursue a radically simplified approach to designing and animating 3D objects. The traditional approach involves creating a series of specific geometric forms and attaching them to one another -- think of trying to build something out of wooden blocks, after being forced to specify the exact size and shape of each block. Cosmic Blobs is more like playing with digital Play-Doh, or, as the company describes it, ''digital modeling goo." You simply push, pull, and stretch the stuff to create the object you want.

''Movies like 'Shrek' and TV shows like 'Jimmy Neutron' have exposed the mass market to this idea of 3D graphics and models," says Harris, who designed the software with researcher Ilya Baran. Harris thinks that creative 7- to 14-year-olds will want to create their own Shreks and Jimmy Neutrons. The software, priced at $39.95, will be available for download next week, at www.cosmicblobs.com.

This is one of those rare forays by a Boston-area company into the world of consumer software, which makes SolidWorks' latest move worth tracking.

Storyteller moves south
Is Brookline writer Evan Schwartz becoming Boswell to Priceline inventor Jay Walker's Johnson?

Schwartz wrote about Walker's Stamford, Conn.-based patent factory in his recent book ''Juice: The Creative Fuel That Drives World-Class Inventors." (You may remember Walker's idea of creating a corps of volunteers who'd monitor security cameras via the Web, to prevent domestic terrorism. He dubbed it USHomeGuard, but it hasn't yet taken flight.)

''Walker is a high-energy thinker who reads voraciously, watches no television, and seems to love nothing more than to hyperfocus on someone else's problems," Schwartz writes in ''Juice."

This month, Schwartz started a full-time job at Walker Digital in Connecticut, as the firm's ''chief storyteller," working alongside the noted high-energy thinker and voracious reader.

What's a chief storyteller? ''If a product has twelve benefits, what benefit do you focus on? What's the lead?" Schwartz says, describing his new gig. ''How do you tell the story of that benefit?" He'll work with Walker on product development and business development. ''After writing about invention and how it worked over the past two years," Schwartz adds, he wanted to try his hand.

Green gathering
Woody Tasch sometimes describes himself as the only venture capitalist who uses a composting toilet. He's also the host of the annual Investors' Circle conference in Cambridge, which focuses on investing that seeks to deliver positive social and environmental -- not just financial -- returns.

The dual themes this year are renewable energy and sustainable food. Speakers include Gary Hirschberg, president of Stonyfield Farm; Tom Chappell, chief executive of Tom's of Maine; and Mike McConnell, chairman of Niman Ranch, purveyors of tasty high-end meat products. Linked to the conference this year is a broadcast of the syndicated radio show ''etown" from Harvard's Sanders Theatre. The conference runs through tomorrow; more information at www.investorscircle.net.
