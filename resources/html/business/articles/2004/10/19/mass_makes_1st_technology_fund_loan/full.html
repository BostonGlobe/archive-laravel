Mass. makes 1st technology fund loan

October 19, 2004

Massachusetts awarded the first loan from its newly created $25 million emerging technology fund to help Acusphere, a Watertown-based biotech company, renovate a Tewksbury building as a manufacturing facility. The granting of the $2 million loan brought Senate President Robert Travaglini, a Boston Democrat, back together with former House Speaker Thomas Finneran, who recently took a job as president of the Massachusetts Biotechnology Council. Finneran helped establish the emerging technology fund last year as part of an economic development package designed to bring more jobs and companies to Massachusetts. The fund targets companies transitioning from research to manufacturing and offers incentives to keep those manufacturing jobs in Massachusetts. Acusphere develops new drugs and new formulations of existing drugs using microparticle technology. (AP)

Defense company gets 5-year extension

Raytheon Co., the fifth-largest US defense company, received a five-year extension of its contract to support the National Science Foundation's US Antarctic Program worth as much as $546 million. The extension brings the total value of the contract awarded in April 2000 to $1.17 billion and extends the agreement from April 1, 2005, to March 31, 2010, the Waltham-based company said. Raytheon said it provides science, operations, and maintenance support, including all labor, materials, facilities, and equipment. The work is performed in Antarctica, South America, New Zealand, and the United States. (Bloomberg)

Shares tumble after quarterly forecast

Shares of Cognex Corp. fell almost 8 percent in aftermarket trading after the computer technology company gave a fourth-quarter outlook that falls short of Wall Street expectations. Cognex shares fell $1.54, or 6.2 percent, to $23.20 in the extended session, after closing the regular day up 20 cents. Despite positive third-quarter earnings, the company's bookings were down 22 percent, mostly due to softening results from its original equipment manufacturer customers in the semiconductor and electronics industries. As a result, Cognex said its fourth-quarter results will likely be 10 to 14 cents per share on revenue of $42 million to $46 million. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call expect fourth-quarter earnings of 24 cents per share on revenue of $56.1 million. For the three months ended Oct. 3, the company reported earnings of $11.7 million, or 25 cents per share, more than double the $5.1 million, or 11 cents per share, in the year-ago period. The results are based on revenue of $55.4 million, up 43 percent from $38.7 million last year. (AP)

Cytyc stock gains on contract award

Cytyc Corp. shares rose 6.2 percent after the company said it obtained a contract to provide its imaging system for cervical cancer tests to the nation's second-largest clinical laboratory company. Terms of the contract weren't disclosed. Boxborough-based Cytyc's only products are the ThinPrep System and ThinPrep Imaging System. ThinPrep System is widely used for cervical cancer screening. Shares closed up $1.64 to $27.89. (Dow Jones)

Vertex reports on heart drug study

Cambridge-based Vertex Pharmaceuticals Inc. said phase 2a clinical trials showed that its VX-702 oral p38 MAP kinase inhibitor for heart patients greatly reduced levels of the inflammatory biomarker linked with higher rates of heart attacks, strokes, and chest pain. The study involved 45 angina patients. Patients were treated with VX-702 once daily for a five-day period just before and following the intervention. (AP)

Texas company to close Boston plant

Texas-based Vignette Corp., which makes Internet software, said it will cut about 120 jobs worldwide and consolidate software development activities in Boston and San Francisco into existing facilities to cut costs. The affected jobs constitute 13 percent of the workforce, based on a total of 925 employees at the end of the second quarter, according to a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. Vignette hasn't determined what the costs will be for employee severance and real estate restructuring, its latest filing said. The cuts began Oct. 15. (Bloomberg)

$3.5b charge to be taken in 3d quarter

Telecommunications firm MCI Inc. said it will take a $3.5 billion charge in the third quarter to impair property, equipment, and intangible assets related to its consumer phone business. The company said its decision stems from recent regulatory actions that have affected its consumer business, as well as an increasinly competitive industry environment. The move comes as other telecoms such as Sprint Corp. and AT&T Corp. have begun winding down their consumer long-distance businesses after a federal court ruled that regional phone carriers do not have to sell network space to third-party operators at a discount. MCI expects the impairment charge will reduce depreciation and amortization expense by about $200 million in the second half of 2004, which will boost earnings for the period. (AP)

Home builder confidence hits high

US home builder optimism grew in October to its strongest level in 2004 on low mortgage rates and heavy traffic of potential buyers, beating analyst expectations, an industry report said. The National Association of Home Builders said its housing index, a measure of builder sentiment about sales and buyer traffic, was at 72 in October from September's downwardly revised figure of 67. An index reading above 50 indicates more builders consider sales conditions good rather than poor. Analysts polled by Reuters on average had expected that the group's index would be flat at 68, September's original reading. The latest reading of the National Association of Home Builders/Wells Fargo Housing Market index, considered a barometer for housing activity, equaled the level set in the same year-earlier month. (Reuters)

Martha Stewart Living stock rises

Shares of Martha Stewart's company rose after Kmart Holding Corp. hired a chief executive known for his branding savvy. Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia Inc. closed up $1.11, or 6.7 percent, at $17.66. Kmart, which has a license to sell Martha Stewart Everyday home, garden, kitchen, and decorating products, is among the few businesses that has held up well for MSLO, despite the stock-trading scandal involving its founder. Kmart appointed Aylwin Lewis, former chief of multibranding at Yum Brands Inc., to the post of CEO. He replaced Julian C. Day, who led Kmart out of bankruptcy last year. (Dow Jones/AP)

Music, radio groups reach $1.7b deal

Music publishers and US commercial radio station owners said they had agreed to a new $1.7 billion deal to cover licensing rights for music played over radio airwaves or via radio station websites. The American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers, representing over 190,000 members, and the Radio Music License Committee, representing most of the nearly 12,000 US commercial radio stations, said it was the largest single licensing deal in the history of American radio. The settlement, which was approved by US District Court Judge William Conner in New York on Oct. 15, provides stations with the right to perform ASCAP music over the air and as part of a simultaneous stream on radio websites, the parties said. ASCAP's repertory, the largest in the world, contains more than 7.5 million copyrighted musical works, in addition to works in the repertories of over 60 affiliated foreign performing rights organizations. (Reuters)
