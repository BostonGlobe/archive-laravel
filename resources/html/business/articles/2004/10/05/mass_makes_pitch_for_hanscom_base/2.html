Mass. makes pitch for Hanscom base

October 5, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- NStar says revised deal to save $191m

ADVERTISEMENT

NStar said it will save $191 million from a renegotiated contract for electricity purchases from a wholesale power plant. The company has been seeking for a year to cut costs from above-market power contracts and began concluding renegotiated agreements in the past three months. The most recent agreement is the fifth and largest signed so far, according to filings at the Massachusetts Department of Telecommunications and Energy. The four earlier agreements, including contract buyouts, represent a total savings of $148 million, according to company requests for transaction approvals filed with the agency. Savings will be passed on to NStar's 1.4 million customers in Boston and Eastern Massachusetts, the filings say. (Bloomberg)

US Airways managers face layoffs, cuts

US Airways said it would cut $45 million a year in pay and benefits for roughly 3,700 management employees, a move the airline hopes will persuade its union employees to collectively accept $950 million in annual cost cuts. An airline source who briefed reporters on condition of anonymity said the planned cuts will include hundreds of layoffs and shed at least $45 million off the $201 million collective payroll for its management workers. In addition, all managers will face cuts in their retirement plan. The source acknowledged that proportionately, management workers are facing less severe cuts than the airline is seeking from its union workers. (AP)

High-speed Internet access plan offered

Facing tough industry competition for customers, Verizon Communications Inc. has begun offering high-speed Internet access for $30 a month, a reduction of $5, if subscribers agree to sign a yearlong contract, even if they do not get phone service. Previously, Verizon charged $35 for its service that offers Net downloads of up to 1.5 megabits per second, discounted to $30 only if people buy a phone plan with their broadband digital subscriber line. New customers making a one-year commitment will also get a free ''home gateway" router device that normally costs $100 and allows up to four computers to share the DSL connection. Verizon is also cutting by $5, to $40, its rate for a faster DSL service -- 3 megabits for downloads, 768 kilobits per second for uploads -- for subscribers willing to sign a one-year contract or combine DSL with phone service. (Peter J. Howe)

Airlines must honor failed rivals' tickets

The Senate voted to extend a law forcing carriers including American Airlines to honor tickets of failed rivals for a fee of no more than $25 each way. Congress enacted the measure after the Sept. 11 attacks in 2001 to give consumers confidence to buy tickets without fear of losing their money. It's slated to expire Nov. 19, and would continue for another year under an amendment approved by the Senate. Travel groups say the extension would give passengers the assurance needed to buy tickets for the coming holiday season, given the financial problems faced by many carriers. (Bloomberg)

Closing arguments in ink expert case

The ink expert accused of lying while testifying for the government at the Martha Stewart trial chose his words carefully on the stand and ''couldn't possibly have been mistaken," a prosecutor said. A defense lawyer, however, insisted the ink expert, Larry Stewart of the Secret Service, never would have risked his successful career to lie. In closing arguments in Larry Stewart's perjury trial, prosecutor David Esseks told jurors that lies told by the expert were capable of influencing the jury in the Stewart trial. Larry Stewart is accused of exaggerating the role he played in testing the ink on a stock worksheet that was placed in evidence at the trial, and of lying when he said he was familiar with a proposal for a book on ink analysis. (AP)

Jury says Sun infringed Kodak patents

A federal jury found that Sun Microsystems Inc.'s Java programming language infringed three patents owned by Eastman Kodak Co., the world's biggest photography company. The jury in Rochester, N.Y., found in favor of Kodak on Friday after a three-week trial. Kodak acquired the three software patents in 1997 when it bought Wang Laboratories Inc.'s software business. Kodak is seeking $1.06 billion in licensing fees and damages. That's about half of the operating profit Sun expected to earn in sales from servers running Java from 1998 to 2001, Kodak said in a court filing. Sun said in court documents that Kodak is entitled to a license fee of no more than $25 million. (Bloomberg)
