A thriving survivor

Ex-Fleet executive's star shines bright as he tackles locating his unit in Boston, producing growth for bank

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  October 5, 2004

Saturday mornings often find Brian T. Moynihan multitasking. The president of Bank of America Corp.'s wealth and investment management division stashes the household garbage in the back of the family minivan and drives it to the dump, while his children chatter in the back seat. As he drives, Moynihan talks with colleagues on his cellphone about complicated business transactions, performing many of the calculations in his head.

"There are three kids hollering, cans of trash banging, and he'd be asking me legal questions," said Duncan Johnson, a lawyer who collaborated with Moynihan on Fleet Bank's acquisitions during the 1980s and 1990s.

Moynihan, one of the most powerful FleetBoston Financial Corp. executives to land at Bank of America during the banks' merger, rose to even more prominence recently when the Charlotte, N.C., bank said it would move about 100 executives from his division to Boston. The decision signaled Bank of America's commitment to New England, as well as its commitment to 44-year-old Moynihan, a lawyer known for his lightning-quick patterns of speech -- colleagues frequently ask him to slow down -- and his marathon workdays, with some conference calls scheduled for Sunday nights from 9 to 11.

Bank of America's wealth management move leaves Moynihan as a top Fleet survivor in the Charlotte bank's highest level of management. Ultimately, Moynihan may turn out to be the only Fleet survivor at that level: Of the five Fleet executives originally slated for top roles at Bank of America, two, Eugene M. McQuade and Bradford Warner, have resigned.

Some analysts speculate that two others, Bank of America chairman Chad Gifford and vice chairman H. Jay Sarles, may not last long. Gifford has said he will remain in the chairman's role for two years, while Sarles works primarily on issues pertaining to the $48 billion Fleet merger, leaving open the question of what will happen in the coming months.

Another top Fleet executive, Anne Finucane, remains as Bank of America's president for the Northeast.

Moynihan now faces one of his biggest and most high-profile leadership challenges yet. He spent the first several months after the merger announcement weathering mutual-fund scandals at both Bank of America and Fleet, which culminated when the banks paid a $675 million fine.

Now, Moynihan must deliver the kind of rapid growth that Bank of America expects and pare down the banks' mishmash of mutual fund offerings, all while persuading 100 executives to uproot their families and move to a strange city for the sake of building a business.

Bank of America's wealth management division includes mutual funds, the private bank for wealthy clients, "premier banking" for the near-affluent, and its brokerage business.

"These are hard businesses right now, but they are easy to do at Bank of America," Moynihan said, adding that the transition to Bank of America has been "easy."

Bank of America executives sound just as high on Moynihan. "He was an immediate partner. He's a superb individual," said Alvaro G. de Molina, president of Bank of America Global Corporate and Investment Banking.

Moynihan displays an endless fascination with the minutiae of the business, including the tiniest details of a balance sheet, the funds' distribution network, and changes in the regulatory climate.

"There's no rule of thumb for like funds," he'll say when talking about how to merge the two banks' mutual funds, his eyes bright with interest, leaning forward and gesturing broadly. He talks about everything with that level of intensity, from the job he once had working on a sewer system in Ohio to a school in Haiti that he has funded with thousands of dollars of his own money.

Still, it remains unclear precisely why Moynihan emerged on top, while colleagues fell. One plus: Wealth management, though important to Bank of America's future, has not historically been a core area of strength for the bank, which may leave the North Carolina executives more open to outsiders.

Moynihan grew up in Ohio as the sixth of eight children and came east to attend Brown University. He later joined the law firm Edwards & Angell and ended up working on mergers for Fleet, a client of the firm.

Outside the office, rugby served as Moynihan's outlet, and he frequently would show up to work bruised. But Moynihan also arrived for work early, stayed late, and grew adept at the financial calculations that make or break deals. Fleet's former CEO, Terrence Murray, eventually recruited him to work for the bank.

"He is smart; he works very hard; and he makes connections," said Finucane, who worked with Moynihan at Fleet. "He'll coalition-build."

In the go-go days, as Fleet swallowed banks across New England, Moynihan lived down the street in Wellesley from Sarles, the former top Fleet executive who now is a vice chairman for Bank of America. Like clockwork, Sarles would walk his golden retriever, Otis, at about 10 p.m. Moynihan would wait for him to walk by his house, then join him. As they circled the block, the two would talk over the nuances of the acquisitions Fleet had made, as well as think big about the other banks it planned to buy.

"When you're in the middle of a deal, the clock doesn't stop," Moynihan said. "One of the attributes of being one of eight kids is you can multitask."

His subordinates say all the working from home is optional. They also like that Moynihan does not just dictate orders; he asks for their advice on strategic planning.

"It's part of what makes it fun for me," said Lauren Mogensen a Bank of America lawyer who once missed an exit while driving with her mother to the fabric store because she was too busy talking to Moynihan on her cellphone.

These days, Moynihan spends much of his time on the road, including a trip to Charlotte once a week.

When he is home, Moynihan does a chunk of his work after his children are in bed. At the office, he tacks up the children's artwork across the room from Fleet mementoes and a rugby ball. The title on Moynihan's plaque reads "Director of Donuts," a moniker he earned from his daughter while eating snacks after Catholic Mass on Sundays.

Moynihan said keeping his family life visible helps establish a rapport with people at work. The artwork also serves as a reminder: "My family goes through a lot to allow me to do what I do," he said.
