CONSUMER BEAT

Tight oil supplies hit dealers in region

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  October 31, 2004

As if cold weather and record-high prices weren't enough, New England heating oil customers now have to worry about whether their dealer can survive through the winter.

This new concern moved to the forefront this month when the parent company of the region's largest heating oil dealer, Petro, warned that customers were leaving in droves, earnings were plummeting, and bankruptcy protection was a possibility.

Star Gas Partners Limited Partnership of Stamford, Conn., said its Petro heating oil division, the biggest heating oil dealer in Massachusetts and the country with more than 500,000 customers, would have a substantial decline in earnings for the fiscal year that ended Sept. 30 and was likely to see earnings fall even further in the coming fiscal year. As a result, the company said, Petro would not be able to meet its loan obligations.

Star Gas blamed the problems at Petro on the combination of ''an inability to pass on the full impact of record heating oil prices to customers" and unusually high customer attrition. Star Gas partnership units dropped $17.28, or 80 percent, on Oct. 18 and have recovered only slightly since. The units closed at $6.99 a share on Friday on the New York Stock Exchange.

Company officials did not return repeated phone calls last week, but analysts and competitors said Petro's problems are not unique. The firm has struggled with customer service, but its biggest problem has been the tight oil market. Political instability in Iraq and strong demand in China, India, and recovering Western economies are pushing up oil prices and wreaking havoc on heating oil dealers.

''They're looking at the same constraints and challenges that any oil company is looking at going into this winter," said Ken Williams, president of Scott-Williams Inc., a heating oil dealer in Quincy. ''It's sizing up to be a very difficult year for everyone."

Most of the companies selling heating oil in the Northeast are relatively small, privately owned mom-and-pop operations that have no obligation to report financial problems. Star Gas, by contrast, is one of two publicly traded heating oil companies serving the Northeast. The other company, Heating Oil Partners of Toronto, which serves Massachusetts under the Alliance name, cut the payout to its investors this month because of rising costs and uncertainties in the heating oil market.

The unprecedented increase in heating oil prices has pounded Petro and other heating oil dealers on several fronts. Like every other dealer, Petro borrows money to buy oil, then sells it to customers and waits for payment. When prices spiral upward, as they have the last six months, heating oil dealers get stretched two ways. Their borrowing costs soar as the price of oil rises, and the flow of cash coming in the door slows down as customers delay payments as long as possible.

Competition also tends to retard retail price increases. Star Gas said it was paying $1.55 a gallon for heating oil on Oct. 15, up 25 percent from a month earlier, and nearly 80 percent higher than a year ago. By contrast, the average retail price of heating oil in Massachusetts has risen a bit more slowly. It hit $2.07 a gallon last week, according to a survey by the state Division of Energy Resources. That's up 25 percent in the last two months and 57 percent over the last year.

Petro also has continued offering customers price caps and fixed-price contracts, despite the risks involved. A Globe survey in late August indicated Petro was offering new customers a fixed price of $1.65 a gallon for the winter or a cap price of $1.75 a gallon. This month it was offering existing customers a price cap of $1.99 a gallon.

A price cap sets a ceiling on the price the homeowner pays for oil while allowing the consumer to take advantage of lower prices if the cost of oil falls.

Richard Ambury, Star Gas's vice president and treasurer, said price hedging problems associated with caps and fixed-price contracts were not the cause of the company's woes, telling Dow Jones News Service on Oct. 20 that it was ''competitive pressure more than anything else. . . . I would suspect that many companies are in the same situation."

Michael Ferrante, president of the Massachusetts Oil Heat Council, said a survey of his members indicated far fewer companies were offering their customers some form of price protection this winter.

Heating oil dealers say the risks are just too great. Those wanting to offer a cap or a fixed-price contract to customers have to buy the oil and then purchase what is basically insurance to protect them in case market prices exceed the cap or fixed price. With the market so uncertain this year, the cost of that insurance went way up, to as much as 14 cents a gallon.

On top of the higher costs, dealers also have to gamble that they will be able to sell their price-protected oil and not get stuck with it if price-sensitive consumers find a better deal elsewhere.

Officials at the Massachusetts Energy Consumers Alliance, a heating oil consumer cooperative in Jamaica Plain, say Petro has been offering price protection contracts at below-market prices.

''If they did not cover those gallons by buying futures a while ago when oil prices were much lower than they are today, they have taken a huge risk, betting that prices would eventually stop rising," said Phil Lindsay, the alliance's oil program director, in a statement.

Petro, which has grown by gobbling up smaller heating oil dealers, has tried to cut costs the last few years by consolidating operations, but that move appears to have backfired, at least in the short run.

According to Star Gas financial documents, Petro now dispatches service technicians from two locations rather than 27 local offices. Oil deliveries are handled by 11 regional offices rather than 27. All customer inquiries are routed through a single call center in Canada.

But the centralization of services has not gone over well with customers. Star Gas said in July that its heating oil and propane customer base had dropped 4 percent, after adjusting for acquisitions, from a year earlier. This month Star Gas said Petro had lost even more customers over the last three months and was expecting more attrition in the coming year.

''Net customer losses are a result of various factors, including price, credit, and service," Star Gas said.

Judith Rogers said Petro recently showed up two hours late for a service call at her home in Brockton. She said she could get very little information about the whereabouts of the serviceman from customer service representatives in Canada and was amazed to learn that the service dispatching center was located somewhere in New York State.
