More turbulence ahead

US Airways workers could face a 23% pay cut -- or much worse

By Kimberly Blanton, Globe Staff  |  October 7, 2004

Kim and Brian Ursino haven't yet earned enough seniority at US Airways Group to get some major holidays off, forcing an unusual tradition on their family: the three-hour Christmas.

ADVERTISEMENT

Dylan, 4, and Daniel, 10, wait to open their gifts until mom comes home from her 3:30-11:30 a.m. shift at the US Airways ticket counter at Logan International Airport. Midafternoon, dad dashes out the door to start his US Airways shift at Logan.

Sacrifices the Ursinos have made during their 32 years combined as US Airways employees seemed a fair trade for good pay and job security. Not anymore. Reliant on the airline for both incomes, they are now facing financial double jeopardy.

The airline filed last month for protection in US Bankruptcy Court for a second time. The first filing came in the aftermath of the terrorist attacks of 2001, dropping the Ursinos' joint, pretax income $5,500 per year, to the $78,000 they live on today. In hearings that begin today at Bankruptcy Court in Alexandria, Va., the airline will ask the judge to slash unionized employees' pay an additional 23 percent. That would mean the Ursinos would have to live on a combined income of $60,000.

''The thought of it just makes the hairs on the back of my neck stand up," said Kim Ursino, 36. Things could deteriorate further if, as US Airways warned in court documents filed last month, it cannot reorganize and is forced to cease operations in 2005, potentially resulting in job losses for roughly 30,000 employees.

''How are you going to make the payments?" asked Brian Ursino, 41. ''There's no income."

American workers represented by unions earn, on average, 15 to 20 percent more in pay and benefits than do nonunion workers, according to the Economic Policy Institute in Washington, which studies workers' living standards. But that benefit is eroding. Unions representing US Airways workers have been virtually powerless to stop an onslaught of layoffs and demands for concessions by an old-line carrier buckling under the competitive pressure of JetBlue and other newer, discount carriers.

''As unionization has declined, the ability of unions to support higher pay is lessened," said Lawrence Mishel, the institute's president. ''When nonunion workers see their wages eroded, it's not in the paper," he said. ''But over the last 25 years, working people have taken it on the chin, period. Union and nonunion."

In today's hearing, US Airways is seeking emergency relief in the form of a 23 percent pay cut imposed on workers. The court has the right to override contracts negotiated by unions on behalf of the airline's employees. A decision may not be made immediately. The company is also asking for court permission to defer a $110 million payment to its employee pension funds.

''The company is struggling to survive in a low-fare environment when its costs are greater than that of its competitors," said US Airways spokesman David Castelveter. Workers, he said, have much at stake in this, too: ''preservation of the company and their jobs."

Pilots may have averted imposed pay cuts. The Air Line Pilots Association and the airline over the weekend hammered out a new five-year contract, which the union said would result in $300 million in cost savings for the airline next year and $1.85 billion over the contract's life. If pilots vote Oct. 21 to ratify the contract, emergency pay cuts would not apply to them.

The airline's other major unions -- International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers, which represents mechanics and fleet-service crews; Communications Workers of America, which represents passenger ticket and reservation agents; and the Association of Flight Attendants-CWA -- are also talking with US Airways, though no new agreements had been reached late yesterday.

Landing a job on a US Airways crew was once the ticket for working-class kids graduating high school who liked the overnight shifts and free flights to exotic places on their days off. Asked how they felt about the current turmoil, several US Airways workers interviewed by the Globe this week, most of whom are now approaching middle age or retirement, said simply, ''depressing."

Unions say too much is being asked of members. ''As costs increase to operate the business, whether it's fuel costs or taxes, the airlines aren't passing the costs on to the consumer, so they keep returning to employees," said Joseph Tiberi, spokesman for the machinists union.

With incomes falling, US Airways workers at Logan's Terminal B said they take as much overtime as the airline will give them.

''I shouldn't have to spend 16 hours a day here to make ends meet," said Louis Fabello, 48, who loads and unloads baggage and freight in the US Airways fleet service. Overtime isn't really an option for Brian Ursino, who cleans jet interiors for quick turnaround at the gates. Extra hours aren't regularly offered in his department, he said.

The Ursinos' stress revolves around an increasingly uncertain future. They are bound to a $2,000 monthly mortgage payment for their split-level home in Middleton, which Kim Ursino calls ''my 'Leave it to Beaver' neighborhood." In addition to cutting hourly pay, the airline increased workers' health-plan premiums and no longer provides an annual $180 allowance to dry clean uniforms. And the unions have raised dues, Brian Ursino said.

Benefits, a key attraction of airline employment, are eroding. Brian Ursino has lost a week's vacation and a couple of holidays. The company is seeking to reduce its contributions to Kim Ursino's 401(k) retirement plan and eliminate its partial match of her employee contribution. (US Airways recently said it would cut nonunion employees' pay by $40 million and reduce contributions and end matching for the 401(k)s of managers.) There is the prospect the airline may terminate Brian Ursino's traditional pension plan and turn it over to the federal government's pension agency. The Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp. usually does not pay the full retirement benefit promised by the employer.

Kim Ursino wants to send her sons to college. ''That's going to be a worry," she said. To prepare for retirement, she puts the maximum allowed -- 16 percent of her salary -- into her 401(k). ''We're going to be working for the rest of our lives. There's no point in our lives we can say 'Let's retire and live off our pensions.' We're never going to get to that point -- ever," she said. US Airways ''is what I dedicated my life for, and I feel like they're just taking it and sticking it to me."

For now, the couple await their fate in US Bankruptcy Court. Brian Ursino is reluctantly considering an offer for another unionized job, outside the airline industry, which offers lower pay but greater security. A 23 percent cut in pay would be a compelling reason to leave US Airways after 14 years.

During the airline's first round in bankruptcy court, the unions granted $1.2 billion in concessions, only to see their employer return for more. There are no guarantees this time either for beleaguered employees, said CWA spokeswoman Candice Johnson. She said her members want reassurances the airline is finding other sources of cost savings, in addition to cutting workers' pay.

''What's unclear is, is the company going to do this emergency cut and come back for more?" she said. ''We think they will."
