The family that controls Cox Communications Inc., the third-largest US cable-television operator, will take the company private after raising its offer to buy out shareholders to $8.5 billion. The company's board approved the takeover after parent company Cox Enterprises Inc. boosted the price to $34.75 a share from an initial proposal of $32 in August, Cox said. Cox Enterprises currently owns 62 percent of the cable company. Cox chairman James Cox Kennedy said the parent firm, controlled by his billionaire mother and aunt, is taking Cox private to boost its investment in the cable-TV industry. The initial offer valued the purchase at about $7.9 billion, including fees and expenses. (Bloomberg)

Executive resigns at Bank of America

Jon Sandelman, one of Bank of America Corp.'s top securities executives, is resigning, the second major departure to occur in the Wall Street business unit this year. The bank said Sandelman will start his own hedge fund. He wasn't available to comment. He will be succeeded by Mark Werner, a J.P. Morgan Chase & Co. veteran of 22 years. Sandelman's exit comes after the decision in March by Edward Brown to leave. He ran the corporate and investment banking division. (Dow Jones)

Judge delays Ebbers trial until January

A federal judge delayed the trial of former WorldCom chief executive Bernard Ebbers until January, granting his lawyers more time to prepare their defense. The trial, among the most closely watched cases in the recent spate of white-collar prosecutions, was set to begin Nov. 9. US District Judge Barbara Jones said it may have been an error for lawyers for Ebbers to agree to the November trial date in the first place, but said she would not penalize him for it. Ebbers is accused of orchestrating the $11 billion accounting fraud at WorldCom Inc., a telecommunications giant that collapsed in 2002 in the most massive bankruptcy in US history. The judge denied a defense request to move the trial from New York to Mississippi, where Ebbers lives. (AP)

Quattrone to remain free during appeal

Former investment banking star Frank Quattrone can hold off on reporting to a California prison camp until his request to have his obstruction of justice conviction overturned is heard, a federal appeals court ruled. The US Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit acted after a 10-minute hearing in Manhattan, just nine days before Quattrone, 49, was slated to report to the federal camp in Atwater, Calif., as prisoner number 55004-054. Quattrone, the former head of Credit Suisse First Boston's technology banking group, was convicted in May of obstructing a federal investigation by sending a December 2000 e-mail in which he encouraged subordinates to ''clean up those files." His legal team argues that biased and mistaken rulings by the trial judge irrevocably tainted the conviction. (Washington Post)

Ex-PeopleSoft CEO gets $3.2m severance

Craig Conway, the former chief executive of PeopleSoft Inc., will get at least $3.2 million in severance, the company disclosed. The software company, which is the target of a long-running takeover attempt by Oracle Corp., said it agreed to make the lump-sum payment, which corresponds to the value of Conway's base salary plus his target bonus for two years. Conway, an outspoken opponent of Oracle's bid, was ousted by PeopleSoft's board of directors on Oct. 1. He will also receive cash for some restricted stock that would have vested in the next two years. His unvested stock options will vest immediately. (Dow Jones/AP)

EU, Coca-Cola settle sales dispute

The Coca-Cola Co. has agreed to change sales practices that helped it win roughly half of the soft drink market in Europe as part of the settlement of a long-running antitrust investigation by the European Union. Coke's biggest rival, PepsiCo Inc., applauded the deal, which was aimed at creating more competition in the $21 billion European soft drink market. EU Competition Commissioner Mario Monti said that commitments presented personally by Coca-Cola chief executive Neville Isdell were ''sufficient for a settlement decision, which will close a five-year probe." The changes include an end to exclusivity arrangements with stores or restaurants, and allowing rival drinks into Coke-branded coolers. The deal allows Coke to avoid a fine. (AP)
