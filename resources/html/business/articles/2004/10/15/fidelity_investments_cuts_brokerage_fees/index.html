BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Fidelity Investments cuts brokerage fees

October 15, 2004

THE REGION

Fidelity Investments, the world's largest mutual fund manager, said it reduced the cost of online stock trades by one-third for entry-level brokerage customers, keeping pace with Charles Schwab Corp. Fidelity lowered commissions for ''bronze-level" clients by $10 to $19.95 a trade, the Boston-based company said. The bronze level accounts for two-thirds of Fidelity's customers, spokesman Adam Banker said. The company has cut customer costs to win market share from rivals. This year, it lowered fees on online trades to challenge E-Trade Financial Corp., Ameritrade Holding Corp., and Schwab, the biggest discount broker. Schwab said on Oct. 4 that it would trim trading fees to $19.95 from $29.95. In August, Fidelity reduced investment management fees on five index-tracking funds, undercutting Vanguard Group's pricing. In September, the firm slashed bond-trading fees by as much as 75 percent. (Bloomberg)

Wakefield company acquires processor

Implant Sciences Corp., of Wakefield, agreed to acquire semiconductor-wafer processing company Core Systems Inc. for $2 million in cash and 311,000 common shares valued at $3.25 million. Implant Sciences said the business combination is expected to double its total revenue in fiscal 2005 and should be accretive to earnings. Implant Sciences reported a loss of $4 million on revenue of $8.6 million for its fiscal year ended June 30, including $1 million in revenue from its semiconductor business. The company estimates privately held Sunnyvale, Calif.-based Core Systems' revenue for the prior 12 months at $5 million. Implant Sciences said the acquisition, expected to close in the next several days, will enhance its growth in the explosives-detection business. The company said the combination will form the second-largest wafer-implant processing company in the world. (Dow Jones)

Diomed suit alleges infringement

Diomed Holdings Inc.'s Dio Inc. unit filed a lawsuit against New Star Lasers Inc., which does business as Cooltouch Inc., for alleged infringement of a patent covering the endovascular laser treatment of varicose veins. The lawsuit was filed in US District Court in Massachusetts. Andover-based Diomed, a developer and marketer of minimally invasive medical technologies, said it acquired exclusive rights to the patent from the five inventors of the procedure in September 2003. A Cooltouch spokeswoman was not immediately able to comment. The company is located in Roseville, Calif. A Diomed spokeswoman said the company doesn't comment on litigation. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION

Pilots union agrees to concessions

Pilots for Northwest Airlines Corp. said their negotiators have agreed to $265 million in concessions. As part of the tentative agreement, Northwest managers agreed to $35 million in concessions of their own. Union leaders still must vote on whether to recommend the two-year proposal to pilots. Northwest has been seeking labor concessions worth $950 million a year, and the pilots were the first group to negotiate. The airline has lost $2.1 billion on operations since the beginning of 2001. Shares rose 67 cents, or 8.8 percent, to close at $8.25. Air Line Pilots Association spokesman Will Holman said union leaders were reviewing the plan at a meeting in Hollywood, Fla. They could vote on it any time through Sunday, he said. If approved, it would be sent to pilots for a vote. The union has said the airline wanted to hurry the process along because of the pending renegotiation of the $975 million revolving credit agreement. The agreement becomes a short-term liability and could trigger consequences in other credit agreements if not renegotiated at least a year in advance, the union has said. (AP)

Buyout report drives ATA Holdings stock

Shares of ATA Holdings Corp., parent of discount airline ATA, rose as much as 22 percent after The Wall Street Journal reported a rival, America West, is in talks to buy all or part of the struggling low-cost carrier. The report, citing unidentified sources familiar with the talks between ATA and America West Holdings Corp., said ATA also has held preliminary talks with AirTran Holdings Inc. Neither company would comment. Shares closed at $2.85, up 21 cents, or 8 percent. ATA has been racing to cut costs, but some analysts say it faces a liquidity crisis that could push it into bankruptcy by early next year. It said in August that it may consider selling some assets and that barring changes to its aircraft leases, it does not expect to have enough cash to meet those obligations in the first quarter of 2005. (Reuters)

Intel cancels plans for Pentium 4 chip

In the latest change to its product lineup, Intel Corp. canceled plans to release a 4-gigahertz computer microprocessor, saying it would rely on approaches besides faster clock speed to improve the performance of chips. The world's largest semiconductor company had originally said it would release the fast Pentium 4 by year-end. Then in July, it pushed back the release to early next year. Intel engineers are studying adding additional processing engines to a single chip and improving the efficiency in how the chips interact with the rest of the system. Intel still could produce a processor that runs at 4 GHz or faster, but it won't be part of the current Pentium 4 model. (AP)

FCC acts to help broadband deployment

Federal regulators approved rules aimed at making high-speed Internet service available to more Americans. The plan lets the major regional phone companies build fiber-optic networks to within 500 feet of a customer's home without requiring the companies to share their networks with competitors. Currently, the former Baby Bell companies do not have to lease their networks for fiber that is installed directly to the home. BellSouth requested the change so it could reach more homes at once, rather than having to lay down fiber to each household. Three members of the Federal Communications Commission approved the plan in whole, while a fourth objected to others. Commissioner Michael Copps, a Democrat, voted against the rules. He said loosening the rules keeps smaller players from getting into the market. (AP)

Bank acknowledges SEC investigation

SunTrust Banks Inc., the number seven US bank, said the Securities and Exchange Commission started an informal inquiry into its disclosure four days ago that the bank would restate profit for this year's first two quarters. SunTrust said it ''intends to cooperate and will provide information and documents to the SEC on a voluntary basis." A SunTrust spokesman declined to comment. An informal inquiry may precede a formal probe, which can lead to enforcement action. SunTrust chief executive Phillip Humann on Sunday said the bank will restate results for the first and second quarters because the company mistakenly inflated its reserve for loan losses. (Bloomberg)

SEC chairman rips reform opponents

Too many business interests are clinging to a failed status quo and resisting necessary governance reforms, the government's top securities regulator said. William Donaldson, chairman of the Securities and Exchange Commission, made the remarks in a speech in New York to the Conference Board, an influential business executives' group and research organization. Public mistrust of Wall Street and corporate America remains high following the wave of business scandals, though doubts have been eased somewhat by SEC enforcement actions against hundreds of companies and by corporate improvements, he said. (AP)
