Small drugstores seek advantage in prepackaged pills

October 4, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- At Brooks Pharmacy, a large New England chain drugstore, Daniel Haron, the senior vice president for pharmacy, said Brooks pharmacists can work with individual customers to help them use pill organizers that are sold in the store. But as far as prepackaged trays, Haron said there is not enough room for the labeling that Brooks uses on every prescription.

Independent drugstores in the Boston area already have larger client lists. At the Community Pharmacy in Brighton, owner Bhuren Patel says he has more than 1,000 customers who get the prepackaged trays, most of them in assisted-living settings.

''Assisted-living facilities in Massachusetts have almost tripled over the last five years. You almost have a captive audience," said Patel. ''They have three choices. They can do the medicine on their own. Or if they are not capable of doing that, a family member can pour the medication into a planner. Or they get a prepackaged service like the ones we provide."

Although the system does not provide for direct interaction between pharmacists and patients, a nurse who makes deliveries is available to answer questions, and patients can follow up with a phone consultation with a pharmacist, Patel said. Patel does not charge a fee. In New Bedford, Brisson said there is a $12 monthly fee for the service for patients who are not residents of assistant-living centers.

At Sullivan's Pharmacy in Roslindale, owner Gregory Laham set up his own system using seven-day pill organizers that are delivered weekly to about 600 clients around Boston. The pharmacy charges a $20 fee for the service, which Laham said is better than Medicine-on-Time because it provides weekly interaction with the client and it does not require an elderly person to rip off a label covering a tray. Laham said he has eight people working on the service.

''It's not a money-maker. It's a service," he said. ''You've got to set yourself apart from the chain stores."
