What's your TLA IQ?

By Scott Kirsner  |  October 4, 2004

Acronyms are the secret language of technology and biotech. If you have to ask what they mean, you're not an insider. There are even acronyms on top of acronyms: someone who peppers his speech with TLAs is fond of using ''three-letter acronyms."

ADVERTISEMENT

Speaking in letters rather than words can be an efficient shorthand among people in the same field. But more often, it's a way to flash your credentials. ''You show your inclusion in a group, and your mastery of a topic," says Sam Birger, a linguist who runs Nomenon, a Cambridge naming and identity firm. ''But to a certain degree, you're trying to lord it over the other guy when you force him to ask, 'What does the X in XML stand for?' " (That's a trick question. It's eXtensible Markup Language, a coding scheme used for digital publishing.)

Some aren't afraid to ask what an acronym means. ''I'm 48 years old, and I've been in the computer business since 1983," says Sam Whitmore, whose Beverly company, Sam Whitmore's Media Survey, sometimes goes by the acronym SWMS. ''If I haven't heard an acronym, I'm pretty confident that it's an obscure one."

But David Weinberger, a marketing consultant who publishes a blog and e-mail newsletter called JOHO (Journal of the Hyperlinked Organization), is in the opposite camp. ''I don't ask if I don't know," he writes via e-mail. ''I google 'em!"Birger says that the more exclusive a community is, the more acronyms its members deploy. And as older technologies become obsolete, or the general public becomes familiar with them (think CD-ROM or CAT scan), new acronyms must be minted to preserve that exclusivity.

How conversant are you with the current crop of acronyms used in the tech and biotech industries? See how many you recognize -- without peeking at the answer key.

When companies that sell software for PLM, CRM, DRM, BPM, ERM, ECM, or ERP start thinking about international expansion, they often open an overseas office dedicated to serving EMEA.

Whether you've got DSL or not, your e-mail is probably sent via SMTP, even if its UCE offers you discounts on prescription drugs imported from Bulgaria.

Later this month, Boston will be host to a conference about VoIP, featuring speakers like FCC chairman Michael Powell and Senator John Sununu of New Hampshire. (Birger points out that many techies try to turn VoIP into a word, pronouncing it as ''voyp" instead of ''v-o-i-p," but that it doesn't seem to be catching on. Instead, they sound like Brooklyn-ites trying to say ''verb.")

Biotech companies like Alnylam Pharmaceuticals in Cambridge and Araios, Inc. in Worcester are excited about the potential of RNAi, which uses siRNA to destroy mRNA. This new approach could stop genetic diseases like ALS and AMD in their tracks.

VCs, trying to keep their LPs happy, often demand PPS in return for investing in a start-up. Then, at BOD meetings, the VCs ask the start-up's CEO what she is doing to help customers increase their ROI. It's bad news if the VC responds by ROTFL.

Start-ups like to create acronyms to prove that their technology is unique and can't be described by an existing acronym. That way, they can describe themselves as ''the world's leading provider of AOM solutions," as the Waltham company Archivas did in a recent news release. IMNSHO, that's just confusing.

In the pharma world, companies sometimes use HTS to identify promising candidates for new drugs. When they're ready to begin clinical trials, they file an IND. When they're done with trials and hope to get an approval from the FDA, they prepare an NDA.

Big companies like Hopinkinton-based EMC Corp. glom onto new acronyms and repeat them incessantly in ads and speeches as a way of planting a flag and establishing their dominance over a new idea.

EMC's chief executive, Joe Tucci, has been hawking ILM lately as a way of reducing the cost of managing information as it changes in value to an organization. But yikes! EMC competitors like HP use the acronym, too, and StorageTek describes itself as ''the leader" in ILM solutions.

If you're old enough to remember DEC's VAX machines, you probably also know how to program in COBOL. (Aside from what it's supposed to stand for, COBOL, an ancient computer dialect, can also mean ''Completely Obsolete Business Oriented Language.")

Big companies like Microsoft and Oracle often try to stir up FUD in the marketplace by insinuating that technologies being pushed by competitors aren't mature or secure enough -- or that they are about to render their competitors' technologies irrelevant by offering the exact same features real soon.

When an employee calls the corporate help desk with a stupid question, help desk employees usually would like to instruct them to RTM. When the questioner is exceptionally belligerent, a help desk employee might recommend that he RTFM.

Most laptops sold today have a slot for a PCMCIA card, which comes in handy if you'd like to add WiFi access, extra memory, or maybe a GPS system.

But aside from its original meaning, technology wags also point out that PCMCIA can also stand for ''People Can't Memorize Computer Industry Acronyms."

TTFN!

The acronym decoder

There are two websites for help decoding acronyms: www.hightechbuzzwords.com and www.acronymfinder.com. Below are the acronyms used in this column.

PLM: Product lifecycle management software.

CRM: Customer relationship management, or e-marketing software

DRM: Digital rights management software, to prevent piracy.

BPM: Business process management, from companies like Pegasystems

ERM: Employee relationship management, or human resources software

ECM: Enterprise content management, or software to organize documents

ERP: Enterprise resource planning, or supply chain software

EMEA: Europe, the Middle East, Asia

DSL: Digital subscriber line, high-speed Internet service

SMTP: Simple mail transfer protocol, to route e-mail around the Internet

UCE: Unsolicited commercial e-mail (spam)

VoIP: Voice over Internet Protocol, which enables phone calls over the Net

FCC: Federal Communications Commission

RNAi: RNA interference, an attempt to ''silence" the genes that cause diseases.

RNA: Ribonucleic acid.

siRNA: small interfering RNA, a double-stranded RNA molecule

mRNA: messenger RNA, which carries the signals that kick-off a disease process

ALS: Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, also known as Lou Gehrig's disease

AMD: Age-related macular degeneration, an eye disease

VC: venture capitalist

LP: limited partner, the deep-pocketed investors and organizations that give money to venture capital firms

PPS: participating preferred stock, a type of stock that makes it less likely that a VC will lose money

BOD: board of directors

ROI: Return-on-investment

ROTFL: Rolling on the floor laughing

AOM: Archive object management, which involves organizing large data troves

IMNSHO: In my not so humble opinion, often used in instant messaging conversations

HTS: High-throughput screening, a way to review large numbers of drug candidates

IND: Investigational new drug application

FDA: Food and Drug Administration

NDA: New drug application; in tech, non-disclosure agreement

EMC: Named for founders Richard Egan, Roger Marino, and ''C," who has never been identified

ILM: Information lifecycle management

HP: Formerly Hewlett-Packard Co., like EMC named for its two founders, Bill Hewlett and David Packard.

DEC: the late Digital Equipment Corp.

VAX: Virtual Address Extension, a line of mincomputers made by DEC

COBOL: COmmon Business Oriented Language, a widely used programming languages of the 1960s and 1970s

FUD: Fear, uncertainty, and doubt

RTM: Read the manual

RTFM: Read the ''bleeping" manual

PCMCIA: Personal Computer Memory Card International Association, the trade association that developed the standard for the cards

WiFi: Wireless fidelity, a high-speed wireless networking protocol

GPS: Global positioning system

TTFN: Ta-ta for now
