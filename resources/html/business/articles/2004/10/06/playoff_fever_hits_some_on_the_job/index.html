Playoff fever hits some on the job

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  October 6, 2004

Executives around Boston ended business meetings with a handshake and a cheer of ''Go Sox" yesterday, turned office televisions from CNN to baseball, and left the office early to catch the team's first playoff game against the Anaheim Angels, which the Sox won 9-3. The weekday afternoon matchup, which started around 4 p.m., sparked workplace debates across Massachusetts, as some companies gave in to Red Sox mania, while others insisted that everything was business as usual.

City Councilor John M. Tobin Jr. sailed out the door of City Hall by midafternoon yesterday, taking all but one staff member with him.

''I consider it an extension of the work day," said Tobin, who could hardly concentrate during a round of meetings on Boston's school assignment plan because ''visions of world championships danced in my head. Rooting for the Red Sox is not a vacation. It's work. It's like having another job."

At Sovereign Bank, executive Thomas B. Kennedy dashed out the door yesterday afternoon so he could catch the playoffs sitting at home in the same ''ratty" beige chair that has survived Red Sox games dating back dozens of years.

''I just put my feet up on a little footrest, and I don't move. I stay right there," said Kennedy, who arrived for work at 7 a.m. to make sure he could leave early.

Other companies also made it easy for workers to catch the Red Sox. An executive at Raytheon Co. yesterday turned the game on his office television and walked around to update his colleagues on the score.

In Springfield, an executive at Massachusetts Mutual Life Insurance Co. bounced back and forth between his office e-mail and the Red Sox scores on Yahoo once the game started. The executive, communications director Mark Cybulski, spent the day chatting near his cubicle with Sox-obsessed colleagues, predicting scores for the game. (He guessed a 7-2 Red Sox win, though another colleague hotly insisted that the score would be 5-3.)

''I did check the score after the first pitch and saw that Johnny Damon had popped up and Bellhorn was at bat," Cybulski said. ''Then I checked my work e-mail. Then I checked back 10 minutes later and saw David Ortiz had singled home Manny."

Though many companies did not officially encourage workers to leave early yesterday, many did not exactly frown on the practice, either.

''We operate on flex time," said Kathy Shepard, senior corporate communications manager for New Balance Athletic Shoe Inc., who said many employees could tailor their schedules around the game.

But executives at some companies weren't completely absorbed by the game during the work day yesterday. At Eastern Bank, when an executive walked through the company's hallways yesterday, he found several of his colleagues huddled around the television set in the break room -- watching presidential campaign highlights.

''What can I say?" spokesman Joe Bartolotta said. ''We're a bank."

Reebok International Ltd. of Canton found some of its executives more occupied with the gala in West Hollywood, Calif., last night to celebrate the grand opening of its Rbk store, which aims to cater to a celebrity crowd and give them an opportunity to wear fashionable footwear and apparel.

Even companies watching the Red Sox insisted that productivity yesterday remained high.

''People are working away," said James Fetig, a spokesman for Raytheon, which had turned some televisions from CNN to the Sox. ''It looks normal. Everyone is working hard."

Still, some said they do not expect to get too much done if the Red Sox keep winning.

''When the Red Sox are in the playoffs, it's workplace paralysis," said Tobin, the city councilor.

Other companies split their loyalties. Tweeter Home Entertainment Group, a chain of stores that sells TVs and audio equipment, turned the televisions in its stores to the Red Sox game, as well as the televisions in its Canton corporate headquarters. And closer to New York City, some of Tweeter's stores planned to turn on the Yankee's game last night, said spokeswoman Kate MacKinnon.

''Not all our colleagues in Connecticut are as loyal to the Red Sox as they ought to be," she said. ''We try to be tolerant."
