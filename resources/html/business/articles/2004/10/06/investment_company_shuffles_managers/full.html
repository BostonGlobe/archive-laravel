Investment company shuffles managers

October 6, 2004

Fidelity Investments said it appointed Scott W. Dell'Orfano executive vice president and national sales manager of Fidelity Registered Investment Advisor Group. The group is one of the largest providers of custody and brokerage services to independent registered investment advisers. Separately, Fidelity said Penelope A. Dobkin was named portfolio manager of Fidelity Advisor Diversified International fund, succeeding William C. Bower, who will continue to manage the Fidelity Diversified International fund. William Kennedy will run the Fidelity International Discovery fund, succeeding Dobkin. Dale O. Nicholls will oversee the Fidelity Pacific Basin fund, succeeding Kennedy. Samuel J. Wald was named portfolio manager of Fidelity Advisor Real Estate Portfolio, succeeding Steven J. Buller, who will continue to run Fidelity Real Estate Investment Portfolio, Fidelity International Real Estate fund, and VIP Real Estate Portfolio. Fidelity also said it named Harlan P. Carere portfolio manager of Fidelity Select Biotechnology fund and Fidelity Advisor Biotechnology fund. Carere succeeds Andraz Razen, who left the firm. (Bloomberg)

Viisage acquires N.H. firm in $34.2m deal

Billerica-based Viisage, a developer of biometrics and other technologies that police and government use to identify people, said it has acquired a Bedford, N.H.-based company that develops similar products. Viisage said it acquired all outstanding shares of stock in Imaging Automation for $5 million in cash and 3.9 million newly issued shares of Viisage stock, based on a $6.74 average price of Viisage stock over a 20-day period before the deal's completion. Viisage also agreed to assume $2.9 million in debt. Imaging Automation recorded $6 million in revenue for the fiscal year ended Sept. 30, double its revenue the previous year, Viisage said. (AP)

Lin TV trims third-quarter forecast

Lin TV Corp. lowered its third-quarter revenue outlook as a result of lower-than-expected political and automotive advertising spending in its markets, as well as the effect of recent hurricanes on its Puerto Rico stations. The Providence-based television-broadcasting company said it now expects net revenue growth in the high single digits for the period ended Sept. 30, as compared with net revenue from continuing operations of $84.3 million for the third quarter of 2003. Lin TV had previously expected third-quarter revenue growth in the mid teens, compared with the year-earlier period. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call estimate, on average, revenue of $96.6 million. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION
Latest version of MSN TV introduced

Microsoft Corp. introduced a new version of its Internet TV service, seeking to broaden its appeal beyond a relatively small base of older, computer-phobic users. The set-top hardware it uses, the MSN TV Internet and Media Player, is a strange hybrid for an increasingly networked world. Essentially a media receiver, the player lets people surf the Web, display digital photos, watch downloaded video clips, and send e-mail -- all while using a television set as a monitor. The new version, MSN TV 2, adds a port for people with high-speed Internet connections and home networks. Made by Thomson under the RCA brand, it has more processing power than its predecessor but contains no hard drive. The box has 128 megabytes of RAM and 64 MB of flash memory. It sells for $199.95 plus a subscription fee, depending on whether it is used with broadband Internet access or dial-up. (AP)

Kodak to cut 870 jobs at European plants

Eastman Kodak Co. said it will cut 870 jobs at three of its manufacturing facilities in Europe as part of the company's shift from traditional film production to digital photography. The layoffs at the three plants are part of the 12,000 to 15,000 job cuts the photography giant disclosed this year as part of its strategy to accelerate the transition from film-related products to digital imaging. Kodak said it will eliminate 600 jobs at two sites in England and will cut 270 jobs in France. (AP)

Bank to spend $600m on investment unit

Bank of America Corp.'s corporate and investment bank plans to spend more than $600 million adding traders, sales people, and bankers to expand its US and international businesses, according to an internal memo. ''We'll use this significant investment to transform our business," wrote Al de Molina, president of the unit, which includes Banc of America Securities LLC. The firm will focus on fixed-income and equity sales and trading, as well as expanding in Europe and Asia, according to the memo. Bank of America chief executive Kenneth Lewis signed off on the plan last month and the money will flow into the division over the next year or more, said spokesman Tim Gilles. (Bloomberg)

House passes measure to curb spyware

The House of Representatives approved a measure designed to curb spyware computer programs, which install themselves and monitor users' Web surfing without their knowledge. The bill, passed 399-1, directs the Federal Trade Commission to fine violators as much as $3 million for actions such as changing a Web browser's start page without the user's knowledge or collecting data on users' keystrokes. The measure goes to the Senate, which is considering different legislation on spyware. The House measure, whose provisions would expire at the end of 2009, lets companies install programs that collect personal information if users accept an on-screen agreement. Congress is trying to crack down on spyware and related adware programs present in as many as 90 percent of Internet-connected computers, according to Web-access provider EarthLink Inc. (Bloomberg)

THE WORLD
2 former Parmalat auditors indicted

A judge ordered two former auditors at dairy giant Parmalat to stand trial for false accounting and market-rigging, handing down the first indictments in the massive fraud scandal that stripped many Italians of their savings. At the hearing, consumer groups also filed for compensation on behalf of thousands of small investors. The fast-track hearing in Milan marked the start of the legal process to assign blame for the demise of Parmalat, long held up as a standard of innovative, family-run Italian business before it crashed in December. Judge Cesare Tacconi indicted Maurizio Bianchi and Lorenzo Penca, who both worked at auditing firm Grant Thornton's former Italian office. Penca, the former head of the company's Italian branch, and Bianchi, his partner, were arrested Dec. 31 and released. Prosecutors accuse them of having falsely certified Parmalat's balance sheets and of having proposed ''fictitious operations" needed to carry out Parmalat's fraud. (AP)
