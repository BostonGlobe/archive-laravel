Safety fears cut vaccine for flu

Priority urged for the aged, frail

By Stephen Smith, Globe Staff  |  October 6, 2004

The British government, citing safety concerns, shuttered a drug plant yesterday that had been expected to produce nearly half of all US influenza shots this year, sending American health authorities scrambling to ensure that those in greatest danger from the flu -- the elderly and frail -- can get remaining vaccine supplies.

Top federal health authorities, expressing surprise at the scope of the British action yesterday morning, convened emergency meetings to address the sudden shortage, and by the middle of the afternoon had issued sweeping new recommendations about who should be given priority access to the 54 million vaccines now expected to be available for the coming flu season.

In Massachusetts, the state's commissioner of public health, Christine C. Ferguson, said she was considering recalling vaccines already parceled out to community health centers and private doctors to make sure they reach the patients who need them the most: people 65 and older, children between the ages of 6 and 23 months, patients with chronic medical conditions, and pregnant women.

"This is very disappointing news that creates a serious challenge to our vaccine supply for the upcoming flu season," said Tommy G. Thompson, US secretary of Health and Human Services, who urged younger, healthier people to forgo vaccines. "This is serious, but we are on top of it and we are taking all of the necessary precautions in safety to make sure that we will be able to stretch this vaccine as much as possible and get it to the people in need."

Amid campaigns by public-health agencies to encourage widespread vaccination, flu shots in recent years have become a fixture on the health care landscape, with clinics held in drugstores and workplaces.

Chiron Corp. was expected to provide 46 million to 48 million doses of its vaccine, called Fluvirin, but in August the company alerted US and British regulators that it would be late delivering the shots because of manufacturing concerns. Still, company executives had assured health authorities that they anticipated those problems would be resolved and that vaccine would be flowing to the United States by mid-autumn.

Instead, the Emeryville, Calif., company received notification at 6 a.m. yesterday from Britain's Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Agency that the production license of a plant in Liverpool was being revoked because of concerns over contamination.

"We profoundly -- and I cannot overemphasize how profoundly -- we profoundly regret that we will not be able to meet public health needs this influenza season," Chiron president Howard Pien said during a sober press conference yesterday afternoon. "I want to say to the American public that our testing program identified the issue before a single vial of the flu vaccine was released to the US market."

The remainder of the US supply of flu shots is manufactured by Aventis Pasteur, the vaccine arm of a French drug maker. That company has reported no production problems and has begun shipping doses.

Thompson said that he was dispatching a team of investigators from the Food and Drug Administration to confer with British inspectors and visit the Chiron plant, and a top FDA official, Dr. Jesse Goodman, would not rule out trying to determine whether any of the vaccine was safe to use.

The chief of the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases, Dr. Anthony Fauci, said vaccine specialists will review whether it would be feasible to dilute some doses of Aventis vaccine, preserving its efficacy while increasing the total number of shots available.

While federal authorities said repeatedly that they do not view the loss of so many flu shots as an emergency, the director of the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, Dr. Julie Gerberding, acknowledged that her agency had activated its emergency communication center, which, she said, is done "at times of public-health crisis."

The nation's supply of flu vaccine is controlled by both public and private buyers, and Massachusetts disease specialists speculated that federal authorities will move to distribute remaining lots of government-controlled vaccine so that states have relatively equal access to the Aventis product.

"Today, I'm here to say that we are going to do everything possible to make sure that we redistribute vaccine so that people who are at highest risk can receive it," said Gerberding. "We do not want people to rush out today to look for vaccine."

The news that almost half of the nation's vaccine stock would not be available sent shudders throughout state and local health agencies across the nation, which had planned aggressive immunization campaigns amid fears that the world could be beset with a flu pandemic similar to the outbreak of illness that caused hundreds of thousands of deaths in the United States in 1918 and 1919. The spread of avian flu from birds to humans in Asia in recent weeks has elevated anxieties, with concerns that the bird flu could merge with a human strain to create a particularly lethal virus.

Infectious disease specialists meeting in Boston last week predicted that a modern-day flu epidemic could result in more than 200,000 deaths and inundate hospitals with nearly three-quarters of a million severely ill patients. During a typical flu season, the virus kills 36,000 people in the United States, mostly the elderly.

"It's very concerning to lose 50 percent of flu vaccine because the public-health community makes every effort to encourage vulnerable populations to have access to vaccine," said John Auerbach, executive director of the Boston Public Health Commission.

The Massachusetts Department of Public Health, which has an unusually expansive government-funded vaccination initiative, had ordered 462,000 shots from Chiron -- 70 percent of the total the state had intended to provide -- because the state got a better price from Chiron. Now, it will have to make do with 168,000 doses from Aventis Pasteur.

The state has already shipped out about 41,000 of the Aventis doses to clinics and doctors, said Ferguson, the public health commissioner, who said she believes the state has enough vaccine in public and private hands to cover those at highest risk of death from flu.

"We are absolutely looking at every means that we have at our disposal to make sure that the high-risk populations are targeted with the vaccine that we have available," Ferguson said. "That may include taking back some of what's already been distributed. Everybody is scrambling to figure out a national policy as well as a state policy in terms of distribution and where the available vaccine goes."

The first hint that the nation's supply of flu vaccine could be significantly compromised emerged six weeks ago, when Chiron alerted US and British regulators that it had detected contamination in a limited number of batches.

Pien, Chiron's CEO, described the problem yesterday as a "sterility issue", possibly attributable to human error that resulted in a flaw in one of the dozen steps that lead to production of flu vaccine. FDA officials said that a bacteria called serratia had apparently contaminated some vaccine.

Infectious disease specialists predicted yesterday that the latest crisis will spur action both to increase production using the traditional methods and to hunt for new, more efficient ways to make the shots.
