Profiteering seen with flu vaccine

Pharmacists say some firms ask a tenfold increase

By Stephen Smith, Globe Staff  |  October 14, 2004

Amid an unprecedented shortage of flu vaccine, pharmacists at several Massachusetts hospitals say that suppliers have offered to sell them shots in recent days for as much as $90 a dose, more than 10 times the normal price.

Across the nation, pharmacists are reporting efforts by some medical supply companies to profiteer after last week's announcement that nearly half of the US flu vaccine supply would not be available this year because of contamination at a British plant.

A survey of health-system pharmacists found that more than half had been contacted by vendors selling vaccine at drastically inflated prices.

Bob Moura, director of pharmacy services at Quincy Medical Center, said he received such an offer Friday afternoon from a company in Fort Lauderdale, Fla. The asking price for a 10-shot vial of the vaccine: $900. Earlier in the year, Quincy Medical Center had contracted to buy vaccine for $68 a vial from Chiron, the company whose production line was closed last week by British health authorities.

''It's outrageous, it's obscene," said Moura, who refused to pay the quoted price. ''This is a huge public health crisis that somebody with no morals at all is trying to capitalize on."

Meanwhile, Massachusetts public health authorities issued an order yesterday requiring healthcare providers to limit flu shots to people considered at high risk of death from influenza, such as the elderly, young children, and patients with chronic illnesses. In a typical year, 36,000 Americans, including 800 to 1,000 Massachusetts residents, die of the flu, the government estimates.

The state Department of Public Health said violators could face fines of up to $200 and six months in prison, though officials conceded they would not actively look for violators. It's the first time in history the state has taken such action regarding a vaccine.

Christine C. Ferguson, the state's public health commissioner, said she decided to impose the rule because she also wanted to help physicians, who are being inundated with calls from anxious patients demanding shots.

Doctors agreed yesterday that it will aid them in dissuading healthy patients from seeking shots.

''It's difficult for the people answering the phone and for the doctors to say no to patients, because we're in a business where we like to say yes," said Dr. William Corbett, vice president of community practice for UMass Memorial Medical Group and a Shrewsbury internist

The state also mandated that medical practices and hospitals report how many flu shots they have purchased. The Department of Public Health intends to have an online reporting system operating within one to two weeks, said Dr. Susan Lett, medical director of the state's vaccination program.

The monitoring initiative is designed to keep track of how much vaccine is in the state and to determine if shortages are more severe in Massachusetts than elsewhere.

The state has also established a hot line for patients seeking information about flu vaccine. The number is 866-627-7968.

Only 55 million doses of vaccine are expected to be available for the upcoming influenza season, far fewer than the 90 million needed to vaccinate all adults and children in the high-risk category, which also includes healthcare workers who have direct contact with patients.

Smaller drug vendors, which buy products from large wholesalers, responded by drastically escalating prices on their remaining stocks of vaccine, according to an online survey conducted by the American Society of Health-System Pharmacists.

That survey, which yielded 677 responses, found that 55 percent of the pharmacists had been offered flu vaccine at inflated prices and that 20 percent had been solicited to buy vaccine at $800 or more a vial. And 16 percent of the pharmacists who had been offered the inflated price said they would pay it.

''We're not dealing with automobiles and typical consumer goods," said Kasey Thompson, director of patient safety at the society, which has about 33,000 members who work mainly in hospitals. ''We're dealing with a pharmaceutical that saves lives every year and deals with the most vulnerable individuals: the elderly and children."

The company that contacted Moura at Quincy Medical Center is Meds-Stat. Moura received a fax from the firm trumpeting that it had Fluzone, the vaccine made by Aventis Pasteur, the lone remaining US producer of shots. When Moura called the company, a salesman quoted a price of $900 a vial.

In a statement issued through a law firm, Meds-Stat executives denied wrongdoing.

''We are confident that we have done nothing wrong and intend to vigorously defend our actions," the statement said.

Representatives of the US Food and Drug Administration and the CDC said their agencies have no authority over pricing. Instead, CDC spokesman Llelwyn Grant said the agency is working with the National Association of Attorneys General to determine if state penalties can be levied.

The president of STAT Pharmaceuticals in San Diego acknowledged that his company sold vaccine last week for $150 to $295 a vial, when last month it had sold vaccine for about $120 a vial.

The firm's sales force, company president Gene Alley said, was deluged with calls from doctors and hospitals

He said that he did not regard the higher prices as profiteering and that he had lost a total of $100,000 in 2001 and 2002, when vaccine sales fell substantially short of projections.
