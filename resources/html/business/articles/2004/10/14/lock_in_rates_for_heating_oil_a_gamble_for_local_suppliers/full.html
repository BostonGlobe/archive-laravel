Lock-in rates for heating oil a gamble for local suppliers

By Joyce Pellino Crane, Globe Correspondent  |  October 14, 2004

LAWRENCE -- The 6,000 letters offering to lock in prices for a season's worth of heating oil were mailed a couple of weeks ago to customers of Haffner's Service Station Inc. But even though the prices being charged are about a third higher than last year, the contracts are a crapshoot for Peggy Willett.

Last year, Willett, the office manager at Haffner's, bought 420,000 gallons of heating oil at about $1.08 a gallon for her prepaying customers. This year, Lawrence-based Haffner's is offering a lock-in rate for the 20 percent of its participating customers at almost $1.44, and the 36-cent-a-gallon increase is one of the lowest in the region.

But whether the company has calculated a price that will yield a profit remains to be seen.

"The market creeps up so fast some days, we're not making any profit at all," Willett said. "It's a very volatile market."

Oil traders aren't just on the floors of commodities exchanges in New York or London. In the offices of heating oil companies around New England, employees are constantly scanning computer screens linked to global markets, looking to take advantage of small shifts in oil prices.

Local companies say they have to be price conscious because of intense competition. Fred Fuller Oil Co. in Hudson, N.H., for example, keeps tabs on the prices and services offered by about 20 companies in southern New Hampshire and the Merrimack Valley, said owner Fred Fuller.

In addition, the practice of offering customers prepayment plans has become a tradition, and heating oil companies have to purchase oil at prices low enough to make a profit on the plans.

The result is a search to find the lowest prices on the so-called spot markets, which provide for immediate delivery, and protect against price surges by locking in prices on futures markets. Willett holds futures contracts for the next few months, under which she will buy oil at a specified price at a specified date in the future. She declined to provide details of her contracts.

Last Friday, Willett was keeping her eye on fuel oil prices through a service that runs a continuous stream of information on a blue screen in her office. Willett said she watches for dips in the day-to-day prices and buys on the spot market from one of eight or nine suppliers.

"I watch it every minute, and if I see the price go down, then I go and I call them up," she said.

"I'm buying all the time. I buy every day," said Willett. "I don't buy for the whole winter. I never would do that." Instead, she initially buys enough oil to cover prepayment agreements, then covers the rest of the winter's supply through purchases on the spot and futures markets in the fall and winter.

Willett said she began buying oil for this winter in April and May, when prices were lower.

"You start buying as the market goes down, and you take the prices of everything you bought and average it out," said Willett. "You figure out what you need to cover for expenses for the winter and price it for the customer."

Friday afternoon, the wholesale price of fuel oil was $1.39 a gallon on the spot market, said Willett -- too high to make a purchase, especially because the price excluded whatever profit the supplier tacks on, which she declined to divulge.

Many heating oil companies, like Haffner's, offer customers the option of paying ahead of time for all the oil they'll need over the winter, in return for a guaranteed price per gallon. Customers who do not pay in advance pay a higher cash price for having their tanks topped off. Haffner's cash price last Friday was just under $1.55 per gallon, said Willett.

Representatives from several fuel companies said they could not offer the lock-in rates this season because of the market's volatility.

Although the deadline to prepay set by most area oil companies has passed, Haffner's program is open until Oct. 28. For customers worried that the price of fuel oil will drop after they commit to the lock-in rate, the company offers downside protection for an additional 8 cents per gallon. Willett said few customers are spending the extra money, because they don't expect oil prices to decline this winter.

It's a tough call, she acknowledged, because there's no rhyme or reason for the fluctuations in the wholesale prices from one minute to the next.

The price of a barrel of crude oil, which is refined to produce heating oil, has soared this year to about $53 a barrel from about $33 in February. The rise reflects a burgeoning global demand for the product, said Vahan Janjigian, vice president of New York-based Forbes Investors Advisory Institute.

"The real problem with the rise in oil prices is that the world is using just about everything it's producing, and it's producing everything it can," he said. "So as a result, there's virtually no slack."

Janjigian said India and China are significantly boosting the demand for oil as their industries use more oil to manufacture products and wealthy citizens buy more cars. He added that Hurricane Ivan, which damaged oil rigs and seaports in the Gulf of Mexico, has cut US oil production, normally 1.7 million gallons a day, by about one-fourth.

Other factors contributing to the soaring oil prices, Janjigian said, is unrest in the oil-producing nations of Nigeria and Venezuela. War-torn Iraq, capable of producing 4 million barrels of oil a day, is turning out an average of 2 million barrels. The country is second in the world only to Saudi Arabia in oil reserves.

It all adds up to an extraordinary year for small-business owners like Fred Hilton, whose Lawrence-based Hilton Oil Co. has just two delivery trucks. Hilton said he could not afford to offer a $1.10-a-gallon prepayment program to his customers this year.

"You have customers who expect you to offer that again this year, and it's just impossible," he said. "You're kind of getting to the point where it's all driven by the market and traders and how they influence the price."

Other companies, such as Merrimack Valley Oil Co. of North Andover and Commonwealth Oil Co. of North Reading, are in the same boat.

But several companies reached by The Globe said they have offered regular customers the lock-in option, typically at around $1.70 per gallon. Some, like Erickson Fuel Co. of Medford, offered a prepayment plan, but company representatives would not divulge the price.

Despite the heavy demand, oil analysts say there appears to be no danger of fuel shortages like those experienced in the 1970s. Last month, the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries announced it would boost production by 1 million barrels a day to stabilize the market.

All the oil companies reached last week said they would deliver oil to anyone's home on any given day, but the customer would pay the cash price set on the date of delivery. An informal survey showed that cash prices last Thursday and Friday ranged from Haffner's low of about $1.55 per gallon to Erickson's high of $1.95.

For Willett, the uncertainty of the future continues to prey on her mind.

"We had one year when the prices came down and customers who prepaid were paying higher" than the market price, she said. "That's the gamble you take. If you can't see the future, you can't know."
