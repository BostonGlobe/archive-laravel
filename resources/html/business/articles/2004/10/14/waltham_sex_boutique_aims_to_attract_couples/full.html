Waltham sex boutique aims to attract couples

By Joshua Myerov, Globe Correspondent  |  October 14, 2004

On a recent Wednesday night at the Lifestyles shop on Moody Street, owner Gary Stone gestured toward a couple in their 40s who were perusing the store's lengthy wall of sex toys. They were dressed smartly, as if they had just dined at one of Waltham's fancier downtown restaurants.

''You see that couple right there?" Stone asked. ''That's now become our clientele."

Stone, who runs the store with his wife, Naomi, always intended Lifestyles to be more than a Combat Zone-type sex shop. When he opened the shop 11 years ago with his younger brother, Michael, he teamed up with AIDS awareness organizations to promote safe sex and raise money for disease prevention efforts.

Back then, Lifestyles was primarily a condom shop. Today, it is a sex superstore with X-rated videos, sex toys, sex books, fetish wear, and gifts, all displayed in a brightly lighted 6,000-square-foot space.

''We're trying to create a couples-friendly store," said Stone. His efforts are raising the store's profile, generating both positive and negative reactions.

Since Naomi Stone got involved in the business three years ago and took over the day-to-day operations last year, she has accelerated the couples-friendliness emphasis.

''I've added a softer women's touch," she said, citing a new line of gift baskets, a greater emphasis on novelties, and the hiring of female staff. Lifestyles has drawn comparisons to Grand Opening in Brookline, a couples-focused sex boutique that opened the same year as Lifestyles in a much smaller space.

Ten years ago, she said, the vast majority of Lifestyles' customers were heterosexual men from age 18 -- the legal minimum -- to 50, who shopped alone. Now, she estimates, about half of the customers are women or couples.

While the Stones -- who have three children -- say they will continue reaching out to heterosexual women and couples, they are also trying to attract people with alternative tastes.

Recently, they reopened an anteroom with their most alternative movie titles (transgender, fetish, bondage) and hired staff for their familiarity with unconventional lifestyles. And three weeks ago, they kicked off an instructional campaign, ''A Sex-Positive Education Series."

Workshop topics in the six-week program include bisexuality, bondage, integrating sexuality and spirituality, and erotic art. Last night's session was titled ''Tantra and Sacred Sexuality."

To Gary Stone, who graduated with a business degree from Bridgewater State College, selling sex-related products fits ''the perfect business model," because there's always a demand.

Stone sees himself ''catering to the needs" of the community: offering people a place to explore their sexuality and diverse sexual interests. He sees Americans growing more open and permissive about sex.

''I'm not going to say it's a good thing or a bad thing," Stone said. ''It's a thing that's happening, and somebody's got to steer it in the right direction."

But some people are uncomfortable with Gary Stone in the driver's seat.

When Lifestyles opened in July 1993, then-Mayor William Stanley, city councilors, and religious groups joined in protest. They argued that Lifestyles, then the third sex shop on Moody Street, threatened to turn the area into a Combat Zone, attracting an undesirable crowd and impeding efforts to revitalize downtown.

At the time, Stone commented that the publicity, no matter what its tenor, only boosted his bottom line. Now, as Lifestyles raises its profile with the sexuality workshops, it is again drawing fire, at least from one city official.

''Do you think for one minute that they're doing this series to educate people?" said Councilor Sally Collura in an interview. Collura complained to the Daily News Tribune after it ran an unpaid listing announcing the workshop. ''They're doing this to move product off the shelf," she said. ''If they want a sex-positive education series, they should be telling you what not to do."

The kickoff workshop last month featured a panel of five activists representing a range of alternative lifestyles.

Vivienne Kramer, chairwoman of the National Coalition for Sexual Freedom, lamented that laws throughout the country fail to make a distinction between assault and the make-believe assault of consensual bondage and sado-masochism.

''Everything that we consider fun is illegal," Kramer said.

Sarah Thompson, president of the Tiffany Club, a Waltham-based transgender and transvestite advocacy group, said she knew at age 4 that she was a girl trapped in a boy's body. Until her sex change operation in 2001, Thompson said she paid a steep price for trying to act male.

She said she married twice, fathered four children, tried to kill herself four times, turned to alcohol along the way, and during one period took a cocktail of drugs that her doctor promised would ''cure" her.

''So that's life as a transsexual," she concluded.

Another panelist, erotic writer Cecilia Tan, said in an e-mail that Lifestyles is promoting a healthy brand of sexuality.

''Your typical adult store courts a guilt-ridden customer who sneaks out to buy his smut and keeps his porn consumption a secret," said Tan, a spokeswoman for the New England Leather Alliance. ''By having sexual lifestyles seminars, Lifestyles is 'out,' encouraging people to be exploring arousal and consuming adult products as a positive, open, nonsecretive thing."

Seminar organizer Linda Marks, a writer and founder of the Boston Area Sexuality and Spirituality Network, said the workshops take ''the sexuality industry out of pornography and put it in education and health."

Not that Lifestyles is turning into an educational institution. Its inventory also includes shelves lined with videos such as the ''Girls Gone Wild," ''Dirty Debutantes," and ''Taboo" series.

Andrew Rapp, another panelist, who is editor of Bay Windows, a Boston-area gay newspaper, called Lifestyles ''a pocket of liberty" in an otherwise sexually closeted society.

At an alternative lifestyle newspaper in North Carolina where he worked, Rapp said, 75 percent of the distribution was through adult bookstores. ''Nobody else would carry it," he said.

Lifestyles has always been a family business. Gary, now 36, and Naomi, 31, met when she was a waitress in a restaurant above Lifestyles. At the time, Gary was working in MovieLand, a general-interest video store that his father, Eliot, owned. When Gary became general manager, he realized that the X-rated movies were the only videos making money; he decided to close MovieLand and open Lifestyles.

Eliot Stone still owns the building, which also houses the Asian Grill restaurant and a Haitian church. ''We don't bother them, and they don't bother us," Gary Stone said.

For years, Naomi Stone was too busy raising their children -- now 9, 7, and 4 -- to participate in the business. Then, after her third child was born, she began clerking at the store, gradually taking more control to the point now where her husband rarely stops in.

The couple say they are discreet about the business when it comes to their children.

''I'm not ashamed of what I do, but I'm not going to talk to my 9-year-old about it," Gary Stone said.

His wife, who says she has been dismayed to see sex toys on display at a gift store in a local mall, also makes it a point to shield the children.

''They know we have a company in Waltham called Lifestyles and they know that it's an adult store," but they don't know what it sells, she said.
