BURLINGTON

Planning Board approves Lahey expansion

More beds may cut ER diversions

By Alexander Reid, Globe Staff  |  October 14, 2004

Lahey Clinic has received the approvals it needs for the $135 million expansion of its Burlington campus and is planning to start construction by December.

The Planning Board voted unanimously to approve the project last Thursday. Lahey's attorney, Mark Vaughan, said the hospital would like to begin construction late this year. ''We still have to file plans in order to get a building permit," said Vaughan. ''It's been a long process in which we've answered every concern raised by the Planning Board."

Vaughan also said construction would proceed in phases over the next six years. The first would involve building expansion in the rear portion of the campus close to Route 128.

In gaining approval for the project, the hospital agreed to comply with several conditions imposed by the board. They include an agreement to review traffic flow at intersections and roads near the hospital 18 months after the entire project is completed and to install additional traffic signals along Burlington Mall Road.

Patient demand for specialized medical services in cardiology and cancer treatment prompted this expansion, said hospital officials. The project will add 80 new hospital beds and increase the facility's parking capacity from 2,384 spaces to 3,120 with a parking deck and a four-story parking garage. In addition, the expansion includes plans for four new operating rooms, a new center for cancer treatment and research, and an expanded heart and vascular clinic.

The hospital filed its plans with the Planning Board in May.

At Thursday's session, some Burlington residents criticized the board for not approving the project more quickly, but board members said the five months it took them to consider the proposal was not unusually long.

In previous sessions before the board, critics of the project have argued that Lahey was expanding to accommodate patients who needed care for cardiology or cancer treatment while abandoning other types of health services used by many people who live in Burlington and surrounding areas.

One of the critics, Maureen Ryan, said in an e-mail after the Planning Board decision that she was hoping to see a ''strong commitment" from Lahey to providing needed services to the community. She also said she hoped selectmen would negotiate a strong agreement with the hospital for more funds in lieu of the local taxes Lahey does not pay because it is a tax-exempt institution.

Ryan and the other critics questioned Lahey's decision to transfer ownership of its pediatric practice to Harvard Vanguard Medical Associates on July 1. Harvard Vanguard has an office at 20 Wall St., near the hospital.

Lahey has said the transfer would not lessen the service available for families who had previously obtained pediatric care at the hospital. Officials have said Harvard Vanguard provides obstetrical care, which Lahey did not, and is focused on primary care for children and pregnant women. Only a small fraction of Lahey's patients were children, officials said, and the move was designed to allow the hospital to focus more on adult medicine.

Ryan and the other critics have also pointed to the high number of diversions of patients from Lahey's emergency room as an example of how the hospital was neglecting its responsibilities to the community.

Statistics from the state Department of Public Health indicate that Lahey had the highest number of emergency room diversions in the state from July 2002 to November 2003.

At last Thursday's meeting, Planning Board member Ernest E. Covino Jr. asked Vaughan and Lahey senior vice president Jeff Doran whether the expansion would help reduce the number of emergencies turned away by the hospital.

Vaughan said the additional 80 beds in the hospital's surgical unit would help free up space in the emergency room. In addition, the hospital would create holding bays in existing areas for emergency room patients if Lahey's diversion rate did not go down after the expansion.

''The high diversion rate is a result of the high number of hospital closures in the vicinity of Lahey, " said Vaughan in an interview after the Planning Board meeting. He also said the hospital would monitor its emergency room. ''We think the new beds and other strategies, like the new holding bays, will help with the problem."

Covino said he was still concerned about the diversions but was satisfied with the hospital's response.

''I'm not an adversary," Covino said after the meeting. ''I'm sure the hospital is as concerned about the diversions as we are. I just wanted to make sure they are going to be just as committed when this expansion is over. We [town officials] want to work with them on this."

Jayne Hyde, chairwoman of the Planning Board, said she also was concerned about the emergency room diversions but speculated the expansion would help the problem.

''There's no easy formula for reducing the diversions, but we're hoping the increase in beds will help," said Hyde. ''Lahey has been a good neighbor in the community."

Ryan said that she believes ''Lahey is a valuable asset to the town," but she questioned whether the hospital was ''interested in being the community hospital that they have promised several times in the past to be."

''I am still disappointed with their recent actions to drop pediatrics and remain deeply concerned with the high diversion rate from the ER," Ryan said.

Lahey Clinic is on a 40-acre campus bounded by Burlington Mall Road, South Bedford Street, and Route 128. The site contains about 890,000 square feet of hospital space. The hospital has 257 beds and parking for almost 2,400 vehicles.

The hospital needed four special permits from the Planning Board and approval of its site plan to move forward.

Burlington town planner Anthony Fields said the four special permits pertained to storage of hazardous materials, storage of fuels needed to run emergency generators, construction of a parking garage, and the hospital expansion itself.

Fields said the hospital must now submit architectural plans to the building department.

''They will file a package, which will contain structural layout, plumbing, heating and venting systems, and lighting," he said, pointing out that the hospital will pay the town about $1.5 million in building fees for the project. ''It's a major project."
