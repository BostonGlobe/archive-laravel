Software giant releases security updates

ADVERTISEMENT

Microsoft Corp. released 10 security fixes for various products, including seven that fix flaws the company said pose the highest threat to users. The new patches are for some versions of Microsoft's Windows operating system and server software, its Internet Explorer Web browser, and its Excel spreadsheet program. The seven critical flaws could allow an attacker to take control of a user's computer, while three other fixes deemed "important" -- the second-highest rating -- pose less of a threat. The software giant also rereleased a patch it put out last month because of problems with the fix and with the company's massive new security update for the latest version of Windows, which is called Service Pack 2. The patch aimed to fix a flaw that could allow an attacker to infiltrate a computer if the user viewed a specially crafted image in the popular JPEG format. (AP)

SEC bars ex-Prudential trader for 5 years

US securities regulators barred former Prudential Securities Inc. broker Joseph T. Falcone from the industry for at least five years for allegedly trading on information he obtained via advance copies of Business Week. The Securities and Exchange Commission and US prosecutors accused Falcone and other brokers of paying a foreman at a magazine distribution facility $3,000 to fax them advance copies of Business Week's "Inside Wall Street" column in 1995 and 1996. The SEC claimed the brokers made more than $190,000 in profits by trading on the nonpublic information. Falcone was convicted of 13 counts of securities fraud in 1999; a federal appeals court upheld his conviction in 2001. The SEC also permanently barred Larry F. Smath, a broker at now-defunct Renaissance Financial Securities Corp., from the industry for his alleged role in the scheme. (Bloomberg)

Contenders emerge for 2 Ahold chains

Buyout firms Apollo Management and Cerberus Capital Management have emerged as leading contenders to buy two US supermarket chains from Ahold NV in a deal that could be valued at up to $1 billion, sources close to the auction said. The Dutch retailing giant said in February it was selling the two chains, Bi-Lo and Bruno's, in a move to reduce debt and focus on its other US food chains, which include Giant Food Stores and Quincy-based Stop & Shop Supermarket Cos. Ahold said it appointed Chicago investment bank William Blair & Co. to sell the businesses and expects to clinch a deal by year-end. Apollo and Cerberus, both multibillion-dollar funds, are known for buying, upgrading, and selling distressed assets. (Reuters)

PayPal problems continue for 5th day

Despite furious repair efforts, intermittent service outages at the online payment service PayPal continued for the fifth consecutive day, affecting payments, log-ins, and account creations. The technical glitch plaguing the eBay Inc.-owned company has affected an untold number of users, making it hard for some online sellers to complete deals, according to the eBay website. Since some people are able to log in while others are not, eBay spokeswoman Amanda Pires said the total number of affected users remains unclear. The payment service has more than 50 million user accounts worldwide. It processed almost 78 million transactions worth $4.4 billion in payments during the second quarter, a 53 percent increase from the same period a year earlier. (AP)

SEC seen seeking figures from papers

Regulators are seeking information about circulation figures from more than a half dozen major newspaper companies as part of an inquiry triggered by disclosures of inflated sales at other chains, The New York Times reported in today's editions. The request for documents by the Securities and Exchange Commission over the past two months involved Dow Jones, Gannett, Knight Ridder, McClatchy, The New York Times Co., and The Washington Post Co., sources told the Times. Regulators are interested in any potential inflation of these figures because the numbers are viewed as a measure of the relative health of publicly held newspaper companies, and they are also used to set advertising rates. Representatives from the newspaper companies and the SEC declined to comment. In recent months, Newsday, Hoy, The Dallas Morning News, and the Chicago Sun-Times have admitted to overstating circulation figures by tens of thousands of copies each day.
