Under Wiley, The Partnership expanded to include leadership programs to help minorities crack the unwritten rules that can hinder advancement. In all, 1,300 fellows -- about 160 per year -- have participated, up from 34 in 1991. Of those fellows, 84 percent are still in the area. And, according to a recent benchmarking study by the group, 70 percent feel The Partnership was instrumental in their decision to stay.

One is David Thomas, the H. Naylor Fitzhugh Professor of Business Administration at the Harvard Business School. Thomas joined the faculty in 1990. A decade later the University of Pennsylvania offered him a lucrative position at the Wharton School, where he worked prior to moving to Boston. Thomas declined the offer.

''We had lived in Philadelphia before, and we had thought of it as a much more welcoming city for African-American professionals and middle-class families," said Thomas, vice chairman of The Partnership and one of its fellows in 1991 and '92. ''I also realized that The Partnership had helped me build a set of connections that, 10 years later, continue to prove valuable in both my professional and my personal life."

Nevertheless, the boards of Boston's top companies and institutions are still mostly male and mostly white, something that concerns Wiley. In a recent report, her organization found 2.5 percent of all board directors are African-American, 1.4 percent are Asian, and fewer than 1 percent are Latino. Native Americans are not represented at all.

''We want to see more people of color moving onto boards," she said. She added that African-Americans have made great strides in landing professional, managerial, and executive positions in financial services but are hardly represented in biotechnology or technology, growth areas for Greater Boston.

One reason: Fewer African-Americans and Latinos are studying technology or the sciences. A study by Johns Hopkins University in Baltimore found blacks, Latinos, and Native Americans represent nearly 25 percent of the total US population but only 6 percent of the nation's engineers. In addition, undergraduates who chose technical fields often dropped out. In 2001, for example, the retention rate for whites in engineering was 62 percent and just 38 percent for minorities, according to the study.

Wiley also said Boston still retains some of its reputation as a hostile place for minorities, causing some to back away even though it offers more professional opportunities. She sees that as a challenge for The Partnership and is hoping a new program for college students will bring more minorities into the management pipeline.

Hubert Jones, dean emeritus of the Boston University School of Social Work and founder of a precursor to The Partnership, believes that as more minorities become involved in city politics, the situation will change.

''The Partnership is trying to get corporate Boston to do better, but no one should expect one organization like The Partnership to be a solution to a massive challenge," Jones said. ''It is part of a strategy, an important strategy and, right now, the only strategy that is operative. But that will change."

Despite such challenges, The Partnership's fellows say it has made a difference in their professional lives. Current fellow Caroline Wallace, 40, is a senior manager of sales marketing and services at Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Massachusetts. Like most fellows, she was selected to participate in the organization by the company. ''The focus is on showing you how to be savvy about managing your own destiny," she said.
