BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Dorchester groundbreaking set for today

October 28, 2004

A groundbreaking is set today for a waterside apartment complex called The Peninsula at Columbia Point in Dorchester. Plans call for 335 rental apartments, 46 of which will be reserved for affordable housing. The first part of the project should be ready for occupancy in the second half of 2005. The $45 million project is being developed by Corcoran Jennison Cos., which was a codeveloper of the nearby Harbor Point apartment complex and which also owns Bayside Expo & Executive Conference Center. Peninsula rents are expected to range from 10 to 25 percent above rents at Harbor Point. At Harbor Point, one-bedroom apartments rent from $1,050 to $1,300 and two-bedrooms from $1,300 to $1,600. (Chris Reidy)

Waltham company, Dell in agreement

Linux software maker Novell Inc. of Waltham has reached an agreement with Dell Inc. to supply Novell's SUSE Linux operating system for use on Dell's PowerEdge server computers. The leading supplier of Linux software, Red Hat Inc., already has a similar arrangement with Dell. Novell's Linux will cost between $175 and $269. By comparison, Red Hat charges $349 for its server Linux software. The deal further bolsters Novell's efforts to move away from dependence on its NetWare network operating system, and reposition itself as a Linux company. (Hiawatha Bray)

Cambridge company forms joint venture

Zebra Technologies and ThingMagic LLC formed a partnership that pairs a player in retail bar-code labels with a start-up developing radio-tagged product code technology to replace bar codes. Illinois-based Zebra said it is licensing ThingMagic technology to read radio frequency identification tags. The RFID tags are used to track products from the factory all the way to the checkout stand. ThingMagic, a four-year-old company based in Cambridge, is licensing its Mercury4e reader, which is designed to read a tag regardless of what communication setting it uses -- a feature that recognizes the multiple settings the industry now uses as it moves toward standardization. ThingMagic's reader will be used with a line of Zebra RFID label printers and encoders, the companies said. Terms of the partnership were not disclosed. (AP)

Equity fund seeking to raise $750m

Eaton Vance Corp., a Boston-based money manager of more than $94 billion, began offering shares in a closed-end equity fund that is expected to raise $750 million. Eaton Vance Enhanced Equity Income fund invests in midsize and large company stocks and has an option-writing capability to moderate market volatility, the company said. The stock investments will be made by Eaton Vance while Boston-based Rampart Investment Management Co. will oversee the options strategy, Eaton Vance said. The fund, which began trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange under the symbol "EOI," is issuing 37.5 million shares at $20 each, Eaton Vance said. The proceeds exclude the underwriters' overallotment. The underwriters were led by UBS Investment Bank, Merrill Lynch & Co., and Wachovia Securities. (Bloomberg)

Lawsuits mount vs. insurance broker

The insurance broker accused by the New York attorney general of bid rigging and price fixing faces another threat: lawsuits filed by angry customers and the company's own shareholders, who are upset the company's stock has plummeted. Civil lawsuits against Marsh & McLennan Companies Inc., some of which seek class-action status, take numerous forms. At least seven federal lawsuits have been filed, and a California attorney has filed a lawsuit in state court. Potential litigants number in the tens of thousands, one lawyer said. Spitzer filed a civil suit against Marsh & McLennan, the nation's largest insurance broker, on Oct. 14, alleging that fraudulent practices resulted in corporate buyers of property and casualty insurance paying more than was necessary. He also found instances of bid rigging and price fixing. A spokesman for Marsh & McLennan declined to comment, saying the firm does not discuss litigation. Marsh & McLennan is the corporate parent of Boston-based Putnam Investments. (AP)

Ovitz testifies he begged to keep job

Michael Ovitz, the former president of Walt Disney Co., testified that he fought and begged to stay in his job until his good friend and boss Michael Eisner pushed him out. At a trial over the board's decision to pay Ovitz a $140 million severance package -- which shareholders want returned to the company -- Ovitz told the court he had wanted to remain in his job despite months of turmoil. "I wasn't going to leave there as a loser," he said during his second day of testimony at Delaware's Court of Chancery business court. But shareholders say rather than firing Ovitz, Eisner and the board allowed him to walk away with the multimillion-dollar severance. They charge that Ovitz, who along with the board and Eisner is named as a defendant, could have been fired for cause because of his lavish spending and poor performance. (Reuters)

Sales of handheld PDAs continue decline

Shipments of personal digital assistants declined for the third straight quarter as large vendors continued ceding the depressed market to smaller electronics makers, the research firm IDC said. Worldwide shipments of handheld PDAs lacking telephone capabilities decreased to 2.1 million units in the third quarter ending Sept. 30. The total represents an 8.7 percent drop compared with the same quarter a year ago and a 4.6 percent decline from this year's second quarter, Framingham-based IDC said. Shipments decreased 2 percent in the second quarter and nearly 12 percent in the first quarter. Shipments were off a total 18 percent in 2003. (AP)

DreamWorks unit raises $812m in IPO

DreamWorks Animation SKG Inc., the creator of the "Shrek" and "Shark Tale" animated films, raised $812 million in an initial public offering to pay debt and to fund more movie-making. DreamWorks Animation and its shareholders sold 29 million class A shares at $28 each. That compares with a price range of $23 to $25 a share indicated in a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. By selling now, DreamWorks Animation chief executive Jeffrey Katzenberg capitalizes on a year of box-office successes. DreamWorks Animation's market value is $2.96 billion based at the IPO price of $28 each. The company's shares will trade on the New York Stock Exchange under the symbol "DWA." DreamWorks Animation plans to use $175.5 million of the IPO's proceeds to fund its business and the remaining money to pay back $405 million of debt. (Bloomberg)

Enron-Merrill Lynch case nears end

Four former Merrill Lynch and Co. executives and two former mid-level Enron executives conspired to push through a sham sale of barges in 1999 because they didn't think they'd get caught, a prosecutor said. In closing arguments for the first criminal trial to emerge from Enron's 2001 collapse, prosecutor Kathryn Ruemmler told jurors they have seen "how one of the most powerful investment banks in the world helped Enron Corp. cook its books." The defendants are each accused of helping push through Enron's year-end 1999 sale of interest in the barges so the energy company could book a $12 million pretax profit critical to meeting earnings targets. The government says the deal was a loan because Enron promised to resell or buy back the barges at a premium within six months, meaning Merrill wouldn't risk losing its $7 million investment. Prosecutors say Merrill participated in hopes of gaining investment banking business from the energy company. (AP)
