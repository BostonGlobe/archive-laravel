Microsoft Corp. said its MSN instant messaging service, with 187 million users, was unavailable to some customers from Saturday to yesterday because of network maintenance. Microsoft did not immediately identify the source of the problem, other than to note in an e-mailed statement that it followed work on the system over the weekend. Virgin Electronics unveils portable player

The consumer electronics arm of the Virgin Group is introducing a 5-gigabyte hard-disk portable music player, bringing a powerful brand name in music to that increasingly crowded product space. Virgin Electronics hopes its Virgin Player, smaller than a deck of cards, will rise as a lead competitor to Apple Computer Inc.'s iPod players. Apple dominates the portable player market, which is also filled with choices from Rio Audio, Sony Corp., Samsung Electronics, and Creative Labs Inc., among others. The Virgin Player has 20 percent more storage capacity than the iPod Mini. It is slightly larger but is a half-ounce lighter at 3.1 ounces. Virgin says it has eight hours of continuous playback time on its rechargeable lithium ion battery -- the same as the iPod Mini. Unlike the iPod, the Virgin Player includes an FM tuner. (AP)

UAW, Daimler-Chrysler unit reach accord

United Auto Workers and an automotive supplier have reached an agreement that covers current hourly workers and possibly those at yet-to-open factories, the first multiplant pact negotiated by the union in 20 years. The UAW and Daimler-Chrysler's Axle Alliance Co. revealed the deal after the two sides negotiated a four-year pact in February, covering workers at Axle Alliance's plant in Redford, Mich., and struck the national agreement two weeks ago. For now, Axle Alliance runs only the plant in Redford with about 70 hourly employees and is beginning work at another in Kings Mountain, N.C., but expansions are under way. The new agreement would cover hourly workers at any additional facilities if a majority of them choose UAW representation. (AP)

XM Satellite starts online radio service

XM Satellite Radio Holdings Inc. has launched a premium Internet music service, entering the company into the online music battleground by broadcasting its commercial-free stations to subscribers through the Internet. The service, XM Radio Online, pulls XM slightly ahead of its main competitor, Sirius Satellite Radio Inc., which in January began allowing subscribers to listen to its stations online for free but did not offer an Internet-only option. Current XM satellite subscribers would pay $3.99 per month for XM Radio Online, while listeners who want only Internet-based service must pay $7.99 each month for access to 68 stations, the company said. (AP)

Delta shares fall as negotiations continue

Shares of Delta Air Lines Inc. fell 5.5 percent on the fear among some analysts that the struggling carrier will be forced into bankruptcy if it doesn't get significant wage concessions from its pilots in the next three weeks. Shares of Delta fell 18 cents, to $3.08. Representatives for the nation's third-largest airline and its pilots union said the two sides continue to negotiate, though they would not say if a deal is possible soon. Meanwhile, 99 Delta pilots retired in September, effective Oct. 1, the union said. In a research note published yesterday, Morgan Stanley airline analyst William Greene advised clients to avoid Delta's shares. The firm estimated Delta will lose $14 a share this year, $3.90 a share in the third quarter alone. (AP)

Software glitch disrupts PayPal service

Repairs were under way at PayPal as the widely used online payment service struggled to get customers back on track after several days of intermittent outages. Users of the eBay Inc.-owned company began experiencing glitches Friday after a monthly upgrade of PayPal's software systems, said eBay spokeswoman Amanda Pires. The problems seemed to subside over the weekend but then recurred yesterday, she said. It is unclear how many of PayPal's users were affected. Pires said some users were able to access PayPal. (AP)
