BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Mobile phone customers get connected

October 27, 2004

Cingular Wireless marked the completion of its $41 billion takeover of AT&T Wireless Services Inc. by immediately expanding unlimited mobile-to-mobile calling between the two networks. Subscribers of the original Cingular and AT&T Wireless who have had plans that gave them unlimited calls to fellow subscribers now also get free unlimited calls to subscribers of the other carrier. Cingular is now the biggest US cellphone company with 46 million customers. Within 30 days, AT&T stores and bills will get the Cingular brand, Cingular Eastern region president Steve Sitton said. Cingular's trademark ''rollover" plan that lets people carry forward unused calling minutes will not be extended to current AT&T customers unless they switch to a new plan. Cingular would not say how many of the combined company's 68,000 employees will be laid off, but said job reductions will not occur before Christmas. (Peter J. Howe)

American Airlines to cut Boston flights

American Airlines is trying to cut costs by canceling nearly 15 percent of its daily mainline flights out of Boston's Logan International Airport, ending service entirely to Fort Lauderdale, Fla.; San Jose, Calif.; and Seattle. Service is being reduced by a flight a day to Los Angeles, San Francisco, and West Palm Beach, Fla. In all, seven of the airline's 48 long-haul flights out of Boston are being eliminated as part of a cost-reducing initiative to take 15 planes out of service. The reductions will be phased in between January and June. American also operates 68 daily American Eagle flights out of Logan. American reported a $214 million loss in the third quarter ended Sept. 30. (Bruce Mohl)

Affiliated Managers names president

Sean M. Healey will become president of Affiliated Managers Group Inc. on Jan. 1, the Beverly investment firm said. Healey, 43, is the husband of Massachusetts Lieutenant Governor Kerry Healey. He succeeds company founder William J. Nutt, who will remain chairman. (Beth Healy)

Toy maker plans to close more stores

KB Toys Inc., the toy store chain which sought Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection in January, said it will close another 148 to 238 stores. The latest round of cuts will leave the Pittsfield chain of mall-based toy stores with less than half of the 1,231 stores it had at the beginning of the year. The 80-year-old chain has already closed 427 stores over the past eight months and cut 3,400 jobs. ''These store closings represent a significant element of KB Toys plan of reorganization and will not hinder our holiday operations," said president and chief executive Michael L. Glazer. He said he expected the store closings to be completed in January, marking the last stage in the reorganization. Because of uncertainty over the number of stores that will be closed, a spokesman said he could offer no estimate on the number of employees who will lose their jobs. (AP)

Thermo Electron quarterly profit doubles

Thermo Electron Corp., the biggest maker of analytical instruments for medical laboratories, said third-quarter net income more than doubled from a year earlier on the sale of some assets and a 21 percent increase in revenue. Net income rose to $106.5 million, or 65 cents a share, in the period ended Oct. 2, from $48.5 million, or 29 cents, a year earlier. Sales rose 21 percent to $542 million from $449 million, Thermo Electron said. The Waltham company said this quarter's results include a gain of 39 cents a share related to the sale of its optical instruments business for $300 million. Chief executive Marijn Dekkers expects the company to earn 36 to 38 cents in the fourth quarter and changed the 2004 profit forecast from continuing operations to $1.23 to $1.25 a share, excluding charges, gains, and other items, from a forecast of $1.20 to $1.25. (Bloomberg)

Boston Properties earnings rise 20%

Boston Properties Inc., a real estate investment trust whose main markets are Boston, Manhattan, Washington, and San Francisco, said third-quarter earnings rose 20 percent because of increased revenue from rents. Net income advanced to $68.5 million, or 62 cents a share, from $57 million, or 57 cents, a year earlier, the company said. Revenue rose 9.7 percent to $359.7 million, and base rent revenue increased 9 percent. Funds from operations, a measure of cash flow used by real estate investment trusts, rose 21 percent to $119.9 million, or $1.07 a share, from $99.1 million, or 98 cents, a year earlier. (Bloomberg)

Natick company plans share buyback

Boston Scientific Corp., the world's biggest maker of heart stents, said it will buy back as much as $1.75 billion of its common stock. The company will purchase up to 50 million shares at market prices, spokesman Paul Donovan said. The Natick medical device maker will fund the share purchases with available cash and credit facilities. The buyback will allow Boston Scientific to purchase as much as 5.9 percent of the outstanding shares as of Sept. 30. Boston Scientific has been buying shares as its stock price fell 4.8 percent this year. The company said last week that third-quarter profit more than doubled because of sales of its Taxus drug-coated stent used to prop open cleared arteries. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
SEC orders hedge funds to register

Hedge fund advisers will have to register with the Securities and Exchange Commission and submit to examinations under a new rule approved by the market policing agency. In a setback for the booming, nearly $1 trillion hedge fund industry, the SEC ordered that basic information must be submitted by the managers of the free-wheeling capital pools that cater primarily to the rich and institutions. The rule will take effect in February 2006. Funds with less than $25 million under management would generally be exempt. SEC chairman William Donaldson had pushed hard for the rule, defying the Bush administration and Federal Reserve Board chairman Alan Greenspan. Donaldson argued for the crackdown as hedge funds targeted less affluent investors and became implicated in mutual fund trading scandals. (Reuters)

ATA Airlines files for Chapter 11

ATA Airlines, the nation's 10th-largest carrier, filed for bankruptcy protection and sold off airport slots and other assets to AirTran Airways Inc. for $87.6 million, to become the latest US airline tripped up by rising fuel costs and fare wars. Orlando, Fla.-based AirTran Holdings Inc. will assume ATA's flight operations, gate leases and routes at Chicago Midway Airport and arrival and departure slots at New York's LaGuardia Airport and Ronald Reagan Washington National Airport. The deal is subject to approvals by the bankruptcy court and other entities and is expected to take effect by early next year, ATA officials said. (AP)

THE WORLD
EU clears Oracle bid for PeopleSoft

European Union antitrust regulators cleared Oracle Corp.'s hostile $7.7 billion bid for rival business software maker PeopleSoft Inc., removing one of the last major hurdles in the contested takeover fight. No conditions were placed on the decision, which came after a yearlong investigation halted several times while also under scrutiny in the United States. ''There is an absence of sufficient evidence of competitive harm," the European Commission said in a statement. The commission, which initially opposed the deal, changed course a few weeks after the US Department of Justice dropped its challenge to the combination of the business software makers. (AP)
