CONSUMER BEAT

Supermarket promo is for real, more or less

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  December 19, 2004

Local supermarket chains are telling the truth, but not the whole truth, with their 10 for $10 deals.

The promotions, first launched in this area by Shaw's Supermarkets and then picked up by many other chains, invite customers to mix and match a select group of products and pay just $10 for 10 of them.

What the circulars and in-store ads don't say is that a shopper doesn't have to buy 10 items to qualify for the deal. In other words, it's really a 1 for $1 deal, but the supermarkets don't mention that because they want you to buy more items and spend more money.

''It's not deceptive really because they're stating a truth. They really are selling 10 items for $10," said Kathleen Seiders, an associate professor of marketing at Boston College. ''But most people probably think that's a requirement for the promotion."

Officials at Shaw's and other supermarkets say they are not misleading their customers, but they acknowledge their 10 for $10 promotions are specifically designed to persuade customers to buy more products. They've effectively taken the popular dollar-store concept and kicked it up a few notches.

John DeJesus, president and chief executive of the Foodmaster chain, which is running its own 10 for $10 program, said there are only two ways to increase sales: Either bring in more customers or boost the average sales per customer. He said the 10 for $10 promotions are boosting the average sale per customer dramatically.

''Are we pushing the envelope at 10 for $10?" he asked. ''Maybe a little bit, but the 10 for 10 has a nice sound to it."

In an unscientific survey at a Shaw's store in Braintree, the Globe did its own 10 for $10 survey and found that 9 out of 10 shoppers were confused by the promotion. They either thought they had to buy 10 items to take advantage of the deal or weren't sure what was required. The 10th shopper said she had checked with store officials on an earlier visit to make sure she could just buy one item for $1.

Shaw's listed close to 20 items in its circular that were included in the 10 for $10 sale. They ranged from a three-pack of Pop-Secret microwave popcorn (savings of 99 cents on each pack) to a six-pack of Kool-Aid Bursts (savings of 59 cents each) to a 5-pound bag of Gold Medal flour (savings of $1.19 per bag). Shoppers had to have a Shaw's card to qualify for the sale prices.

Rick Tonetti of Braintree said he hadn't taken advantage of the 10 for $10 promotion yet and wasn't sure of the rules. Shown the store circular, he said: ''I'd assume it means you have to buy 10."

Alice Lamb of Quincy said it was good to learn she could buy one of the items and pay just $1. ''I thought you had to buy 10," she said.

Albertsons Inc., the parent company of Shaw's, tested the 10 for $10 concept for a year before rolling it out to all of its 2,300 stores late this summer. Larry Johnston, the chairman and chief executive of Albertsons, said in an interview in October that the dollar-store concept was becoming too big to ignore.

There are about 16,000 dollar stores in the United States, and companies like Dollar General, Family Dollar, and Dollar Tree are expanding rapidly, according to company statements. Several major retailers, including Kroger Co. and Target Corp., have jumped on the bandwagon with dollar aisles or sections.

Albertsons gave the concept a slight twist, offering a series of items for a dollar while promoting the notion of buying them in groups of 10.

''Customers really like it," said Terry Donilon, a spokesman for Shaw's. ''Everything we see is positive. In a very competitive industry, we are simply giving our customers an added reason to shop with us where they can enjoy real savings."

Officials at other supermarkets liked what they saw and quickly copied Shaw's.

''Everybody's picking it up because it's inducing people to buy more," said Charles Carroll, deputy director of the Massachusetts Division of Standards, which enforces many of the state's pricing regulations.

Under state law, Carroll said, unless a store specifies otherwise, consumers are entitled to buy grouped sale items in any quantity they want and pay a proportional amount.

For example, consumers can buy one or 50 items under a 10 for $10 promotion and pay $1 per item. Similarly, they would pay $2 for a single item under a 2 for $4 promotion. Carroll said the only time a consumer would pay more for a single item is if the retailer specifically states in its advertising and store displays that the price is higher when buying a single item.

Carroll said his office has received no complaints about the 10 for $10 promotions. Supermarket officials said they have received no complaints, either.

Edgar Dworsky, the editor ofConsumer world.org, said he thinks the 10 for $10 promotions are obnoxious.

''The best deceptions are the ones where the consumer doesn't know he's been taken, and this may be one of those cases," Dworsky said. ''The consumer who doesn't know better will end up buying more goods than he really needs when all he really needs to do is plunk down $1 for one item."

Other phone options

Readers of last week's column offered their own phone suggestions for people who don't make enough calls to qualify for a flat-rate, all-you-can-talk service.

Prepaid recommendations included Easycall.net, which offers service at 2.4 cents a minute, BigRedWire.com at 2.7 cents a minute, and Pingo.com, which specializes in international service but charges 3.2 cents a minute for US calls.

Other readers suggested regular long-distance services like GTC Telecom (2.9 cents a minute at www.gtctelecom.com) and Affordable Voice Communications (3.5 cents at Avcinc.com).

For details, check the company websites closely.
