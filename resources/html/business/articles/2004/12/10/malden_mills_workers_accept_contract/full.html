Malden Mills workers accept contract

December 10, 2004

A union representing about 700 textile workers at Malden Mills Industries Inc. voted to accept a contract offer, averting a possible strike. Paul Coorey, president of the Union of Needletrades, Industrial and Textile Employees Union, Local 311, said workers voted 301-136 to accept a three-year pact that included annual wage increases of 2 percent, 2 percent, and 3 percent. But the accord also raised workers' healthcare premiums. "We think we did very well, considering the times and what's happening in the textile industry," Coorey said. The Lawrence firm's chief executive, Michael Spillane, said workers "recognized that the company made a fair offer." (Ross Kerber)

Mass. companies bullish on hiring

A Massachusetts job vacancy survey shows recent improvement in hiring prospects, with the largest number of vacancies in healthcare, manufacturing, and professional and technical services. Findings released from the semiannual survey show a 2.0 percent job vacancy rate statewide during the second quarter, up from 1.8 percent during the comparable period a year earlier. The results, which took several months to compile, list job vacancy rates ranging from a high of 3.1 percent in Berkshire County to 1.8 percent in Southeastern Massachusetts, the Department of Workforce Development's Division of Unemployment Assistance said. About 35 percent of the companies responding to the survey indicated they expected to hire more workers during the following six months, compared with 6 percent that intended to cut jobs. Companies in the Boston area offered the most confident hiring projections of all regional employers, with 38 percent expecting to boost hiring. Confidence levels were higher in all seven survey regions. (AP)

Demolition permit to be issued for Gaiety

The Boston Inspectional Services Department will today issue a demolition permit for the defunct Gaiety Theatre, at 659-665 Washington St., said Susan F. Rice, assistant commissioner for plans and zoning. Kensington Investment Co., owner of the nearly 100-year-old building, seeks to tear down the structure and replace it with an apartment building, but is opposed by some preservationists. Kensington has major approvals from the Boston Redevelopment Authority, but the development is still being challenged in court. Demolition could begin on Dec. 17 at the earliest. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

Project planned for 1304-1330 Boylston

Samuels & Associates, a development company that broke ground this fall on a $200 million, 576-unit residential and retail project called Trilogy, near Fenway Park, told City Hall it plans another big development in the area, at 1304-1330 Boylston St. The second project would have a total of 340,000 square feet on a little more than an acre, with residential, office, clinical, retail, and other uses, plus underground parking. The developer offered no details. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

Ex-Testa Hurwitz lawyers staying in Hub

Seven partners at the Boston law firm of Testa, Hurwitz & Thibeault LLP will join the Boston office of Proskauer Rose LLP, said Daniel Finkelman, one of the departing lawyers. The lawyers are part of a group of more than 20 partners that have quit Testa Hurwitz this year. The firm this week said 10 partners were leaving. Three will join another Boston firm, Bingham McCutchen. "We have no comment on either the group of people who are going or their destination," Stephen Barrett, Testa Hurwitz's chief marketing officer, said. "To my knowledge, our firm has not been officially notified of where they're going." New York-based Proskauer Rose's Boston office was opened in March by a former Testa Hurwitz lawyer, Steven Bauer. (Bloomberg)

Blackouts unlikely in N.E. this winter

The operator of New England's power grid said changes in market rules will allow it to avoid threats of rolling blackouts that it made last winter, when extreme cold shut plants and limited fuel supplies. During a Jan. 14-16 cold snap, real-time prices approached $1,000 a megawatt-hour, about 20 times average, and an unspecified number of plants shut down to sell their gas supplies, which were trading at record highs. The changes include adjusting bidding deadlines so that power plant owners are informed of needs for the next day and have time to secure fuel. The grid is also eliminating generator requests for economic shutdowns during extreme weather. (Bloomberg)

EU makes decision in trademark case

Gillette Co. of Boston, the world's largest razor maker, can't stop a Finnish company from using its Sensor trademark on replacement blade packages, an aide at Europe's highest court said. "Use of another's trademark is necessary to indicate the purpose of the product when this usage constitutes the only way to give consumers complete information on the possible uses of the product," said Antonio Tizzano, an advocate general at the European Court of Justice. At stake is whether companies can restrict rivals' use of their trademarks when they're competing on replacement parts. The full court, which follows the advocate general's advice about 85 percent of the time, is likely to rule next year. (Bloomberg)

Internet DVD-rental service offered in UK

Amazon.com Inc. made its anticipated entry into renting DVDs over the Internet, launching a service in the United Kingdom that the world's largest retailer says will charge up to 30 percent less than its competitors. The company said it didn't have plans for a similar US service at this time. Amazon said its UK service will start at about $15.50 a month. Customers also will get 10 percent off DVD purchases. The lowest-price service will let customers keep two DVDs at a time, and rent a total of four DVDs per month. For about $19.30 per month, customers can keep up to three DVDs at a time, and rent six per month. (AP)

FDA issues heart warning for Bextra

The government is warning of potential heart problems associated with use of the painkiller Bextra in people who have recently had bypass surgery. The Food and Drug Administration said it is adding the warning to the labels of Pfizer Inc.'s Bextra, and strengthening label warnings to say there is a possibility of severe skin reactions. The FDA said it was adding the heart warning because "results from a new study of more than 1,500 patients who had just had cardiac surgery show that patients treated with Bextra for pain were more likely to have heart and blood clotting problems than other patients who did not receive any drug." (AP)
