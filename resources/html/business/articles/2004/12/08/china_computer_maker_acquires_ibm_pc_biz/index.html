China computer maker acquires IBM PC biz

By Stephanie Hoo, Associated Press Writer  |  December 8, 2004

BEIJING -- China's biggest computer maker, Lenovo Group, said Wednesday it has acquired a majority stake in IBM Corp.'s personal computer business in a deal valued at US$1.75 billion (euro1.32 billion) -- one of the biggest Chinese overseas acquisitions ever.

The acquisition will likely quadruple Lenovo's sales, making it the third-largest PC company in the world, the companies said.

Like other major Chinese manufacturers hoping to expand overseas, Lenovo is leveraging a well-known foreign brand. Lenovo Chairman Liu Chuanzhi said that Lenovo, formerly known as Legend, will be entitled to freely use IBM's brand name in five years' time.

"This acquisition will allow Chinese industry to make significant inroads on its path to globalization," Liu said.

Lenovo is taking over IBM's desktop PC business, including research, development and manufacturing for US$1.25 billion (euro946 million) in cash and shares, while IBM retains a 18.9 percent stake in the company, Liu told reporters.

Including liabilities Lenovo agreed to take on as part of the deal, the total value for IBM is US$1.75 billion (euro1.32 billion).

The companies appear to have structured the deal to ensure IBM, despite its relatively small stake, retains a say in the PC business.

Lenovo Group's worldwide PC business will move its headquarters to New York and its chief executive will be Stephen M. Ward Jr., an IBM senior vice president and general manager of IBM's Personal Systems Group. Lenovo's current president and chief executive officer, Yang Yuanqing, will be chairman of the PC business.

However, the deal shifts International Business Machines Corp. to a peripheral role in a corner of the technology industry it pioneered.

Once a key player in popularizing the personal computer, IBM is now focusing on consulting, outsourcing and software, analysts say. The sale to Lenovo is expected to cut manufacturing costs and breathe new life into its PC business, which now accounts for a small portion of its total sales and profits.

"The IBM brand will gain great recognition in China, the world's fastest growing economy and the world's fastest growing market for PCs," said John Joyce, IBM senior vice president and group executive of IBM Global Services.

Lenovo is China's biggest computer maker, claiming a 27 percent market share, and is also the biggest in Asia. Its shares are traded in Hong Kong.

Both IBM and Lenovo have seen profit margins shrink amid aggressive competition.

IBM sold 6.8 million PCs in the first nine months of 2004 for a 5 percent market share, according to Gartner Inc., a U.S. provider of research and analysis on the information technology industry.

That compares with 16.4 percent for Dell Inc. and 13.9 percent for Hewlett-Packard Inc., which makes both the HP and Compaq brands.

Beijing-based Lenovo was ranked fifth in sales worldwide.

Lenovo had expanded into cell phone manufacturing and information technology services, with lackluster results. It now says it is focusing on its core computer business again.

The announcement Wednesday followed reports that a deal was imminent. Trading of Lenovo on the Hong Kong bourse was suspended Monday pending announcement of the deal.

IBM shares fell US$1.57 to close at US$96.10 Tuesday on the New York Stock Exchange.

IBM executives sought to reassure jittery investors, customers and employees, emphasizing the company's focus on continuity.

About 10,000 IBM employees will move to Lenovo, joining the Chinese company's current staff of about 9,000, the companies said. Less than a quarter of those 10,000 are based in the United States and 40 percent already work in China.

The deal was expected to have "minimal impact" on employment and pay in both companies.

IBM, based in Armonk, New York, has nearly 320,000 employees.

"For employees, this represents an opportunity to join a vibrant and growing company and for investors this agreement represents an opportunity to reap the rewards of a growing company in a growing market," IBM senior vice president Joyce said.

Lenovo was founded in 1984 by a group of scholars at the government-backed Chinese Academy of Sciences in Beijing. The group first worked out of a small cottage.

First set up to distribute computers and related products made by IBM and other foreign companies, by 1990 the group was selling its own brand PCs.

The group listed its shares on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange in 1994.
