BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Malden Mills workers vote on offer

December 8, 2004

About 700 textile workers will begin voting on a revised contract offer as the new owners of Malden Mills Inc. seek to avoid a threatened strike. Voting will run through tomorrow, with results expected tomorrow night, union spokesman Warren Pepicelli said. Pepicelli and Malden Mills spokesman James Harter declined to comment on what changes the company made to its offer. The key sticking points are the size of proposed wage increases and growing employee healthcare costs. Union members voted 231 to 198 last week to reject a previous offer and authorize a strike. Workers at the company's plants in Hudson, N.H., Lawrence, and Methuen have been working under an extension of a three-year contract that expired Nov. 30. The company's earlier offer included pay increases of 2 percent in each of the first two years and 3 percent in the final year. The Lawrence company also had proposed steep increases in employee contributions for health insurance. (AP)

Manulife to exceed cost-savings target

Manulife Financial Corp., Canada's largest insurer, said it will exceed its cost-savings target from its $13.9 billion takeover of John Hancock Financial Services. Toronto-based Manulife will reduce costs by $325 million through the end of next year from the purchase of Boston's John Hancock, the insurer said. Manulife said last year that it expected to save $255 million each year starting in 2006 from the acquisition, which closed in April. Bruce Gordon, Manulife's senior executive vice president and general manager for Canada, said about 40 percent of the cost savings will come from Canada, where John Hancock operated under the Maritime Life brand. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Merck board names Vioxx review panel

Drug maker Merck & Co. said its board appointed a committee to review the company's actions prior to the voluntary withdrawal of its painkiller Vioxx. Millions of Americans had used Vioxx before Merck withdrew the drug on Sept. 30 after a study showed that long-term use of the medicine doubled heart attack and stroke risk. Merck, based in Whitehouse Station, N.J., said the committee also will act for the board in responding to shareholder litigation related to the drug's withdrawal and advise it of any action that should be taken. William Bowen, president of the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation and chairman of the Merck board's corporate governance committee, will head the special committee. (Reuters)

Stent seen effective on smaller arteries

Johnson & Johnson's Cypher, a drug-coated stent used to hold open cleared heart arteries, is effective for small, sensitive vessels, a study in the Dec. 8 Journal of the American Medical Association found. Devices like Cypher and Boston Scientific Corp.'s Taxus are the new standard for treating most clogged heart arteries, because of their ability to prevent them from narrowing again after treatment, called restenosis. Clearing smaller arteries has been a problem, however, since complications and restenosis rates typically rise as vessel size declines. The Italian study, funded primarily by the academic centers where it was conducted, show Cypher's benefits carry over to the smallest vessels. (Bloomberg)

Toyota to offer satellite radio rivals

Toyota Motor Corp. will offer XM Satellite Radio as a factory-installed option in Toyota and Lexus vehicles beginning in 2006, but customers also will be able to order rival Sirius Satellite Radio at Toyota dealerships starting next year. Sirius shares rose sharply, as both companies' stocks hit 52-week highs. XM, the market leader with roughly 2.5 million subscribers to Sirius' 800,000-plus, already is available as a dealer-installed option on 10 Toyota and Lexus models, and that number will grow in 2005. Sirius said its equipment will be available as a dealer-installed option on nine Toyota and Lexus models beginning in February. Sirius Satellite Radio Inc.'s shares soared 92 cents to close at $9.01 on the Nasdaq. Shares of XM Satellite Radio Holdings Inc. gained 68 cents to finish at $38.41 on the Nasdaq. (AP)

Hooters Air sets Pittsburgh service

Hooters Air, a discount carrier associated with the restaurant chain, will begin flights out of Pittsburgh International Airport early next year, airport officials said. Hooters will begin offering one daily nonstop flight, four days a week, between Pittsburgh and Myrtle Beach, S.C., on Feb. 10. Based in Myrtle Beach, Hooters Air started service to Atlanta in March 2003 and now serves Newark; Baltimore; Gary, Ind.; Columbus, Ohio; and Nassau, Bahamas. (AP)

PC firm eyes second European plant

Dell Inc. is looking to open its second European production plant, the company's chairman said, and a source familiar with the plan said it was considering a location in France. The move by the world's largest personal computer company to locate production in France appears partly designed to shore up its business against rival Hewlett-Packard in one of the few big markets where it outpaces Dell in PC sales. Michael Dell, the company's chairman and founder, told a news conference in San Francisco that Dell was looking to expand beyond its manufacturing plant in Limerick, Ireland, to feed European demand that is growing around 30 percent a year. He did not specify in which country the plant would be located. A company spokesman declined to comment. However, an industry source said the location under consideration was in France. No further details were available. (Reuters)

Airlines would honor failed rivals' tickets

American Airlines and other US carriers would have to accept failed rivals' tickets for $25 or less each way under a compromise bill to reorganize intelligence agencies that Congress plans to pass before adjourning this week. The House and Senate had enacted the measure two months after the Sept. 11, 2001, attacks, giving consumers confidence to buy tickets without fear of losing money as airlines posted widening losses. The law shields credit card companies from passengers charging back tickets defunct airlines can't honor. The requirement expired Nov. 19. Legislation adopting some recommendations of the Sept. 11 commission revives the law through Nov. 19, 2005. (Bloomberg)

THE WORLD
French fine company, ex-chief Messier

French regulators fined Vivendi Universal SA and its former boss, Jean-Marie Messier, nearly $1.4 million each for deceiving investors with a litany of inaccurate financial communications issued over a two-year period. The Financial Market Authority's ruling, posted on its website, cleared Messier of insider trading but found that he consistently misstated cash flow in 2001 and 2002 and used false accounting to hide the true level of the media conglomerate's debt. Messier, Vivendi's former chief executive and chairman, was forced out in 2002 after an acquisition spree that took the company close to bankruptcy and racked up $47 billion in debt. Messier's lawyer said his client would appeal the fine. Vivendi said it was studying the decision and keeping its legal options open. (AP)
