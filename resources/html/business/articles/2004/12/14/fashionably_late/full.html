Fashioning a new strategy

After 34 years in business, the owner of JasmineSola plans a major expansion

By Linda Tucci, Globe Correspondent  |  December 14, 2004

What do women want?

Luciano Manganella, the owner of the trendy Boston women's boutique JasmineSola, has a pretty good idea. And now after 34 years in business, he's plotting a major expansion.

The eight-unit chain has plans to open 12 stores in the next 18 months in New England. Manganella also is in talks with former Saks Fifth Avenue chairman and chief executive Philip Miller at Tri-Artisan Partners, a New York merchant bank, to explore a venture capital deal to allow JasmineSola to open another 50 stores nationwide in five years.

The hip boutique, which targets teens and twentysomethings with its oh-so-cool selection of apparel, shoes, and accessories, is filling a niche in a crowded retail landscape. Unlike other specialty chains like Ann Taylor, Gap, and Talbots, which promote their own brands, JasmineSola dishes up an ever-changing lineup of approximately 200 labels, from Juicy Couture to Miss Sixty, all under one roof.

"The young people today, they don't want to dress only in the Gap or in just Express," said Manganella. "They have graduated to a new level, and we are right there with open arms."

The results bear that out. Manganella says the privately held chain will generate about $20 million in revenue this year. Profit for the first nine months of the year is up fourfold over previous years. A five-year plan projects sales of $120 million to $140 million.

And according to mall operators doing business with Manganella, JasmineSola outperforms many national competitors, racking up an average $800 in sales per square foot. Sales at its high-traffic stores at Boston's Prudential Center mall and the Mall at Chestnut Hill in Newton have topped $1,400 a square foot. And the flavor is anything but vanilla, said Jeremy Sclar, developer of the Derby Street Shoppes in Hingham, where JasmineSola will open early next year.

"Luciano is a differentiator," said Sclar. "If he has his way, he'll be in lots of places, but today he is unique, and he has a very loyal customer base."

JasmineSola strikes retail gold because it has "broad appeal, so that the 21-year-old and the 45-year-old can find something that will add a little pizazz," said Miller, the former Saks executive.

Over the weekend, the Harvard Square store attracted David Spencer, 38, from Weston, who was Christmas shopping for his wife Julie, 36. "It's an annual trip. I try to find clothes that are a little bit more unique, and there's a lot of fun stuff," he said, clutching a miniskirt marked down to $29 and looking for jeans to go with a $128 top by Custo Barcelona.

Meanwhile, Julie Lane, a 19-year-old Harvard sophomore, was looking for a gift for a friend. Lane said she spends a sizable chunk of her summer earnings on designer jeans.

"There's a lot of cute things when you get dressed up, very current, very trendy," Lane said.

Whether a hot local merchant can maintain its up-the-minute fashion sense and manage its inventory as it expands nationally will be a challenge, said John Macht, a retail consultant in Boston. "Fashion changes. The key is staying nimble," said Macht. "And you can't be in 50 stores at once. He'll have to have a first-rate management team out in the field."

The 58-year-old Manganella is hardly a novice in the fashion business. The native of Salerno, Italy, opened his first store in Cambridge in 1970 on Boylston Street (now JFK Boulevard) in a 300-square-foot space. Unable to afford name brands, he filled the tiny store with his custom-made designs.

In the beginning, the store was simply called Jasmine after "the hippie-era, flower children," says Manganella. The peripatetic merchant split his time between Boston and New York, starting a manufacturing company called Gruppo Americano and a shoe business, known as Sola, which in Italian means "the only" one. The distinctive name came naturally, after sales people kept answering the phone "Jasmine Sola."

The chain's current concept was really driven by jeans, says Manganella's wife and business partner, Stacey, 41, who cultivates and scouts out the brands. "When denim became a fashion item, you needed tops, jackets to go with those jeans," she said.

The courtship by exclusive malls is a fairly recent affair for the business.

"In the beginning, the malls only cared about the Gap, Banana Republic, The Limited, Talbots," said Manganella. "So at some point, no matter where you go, Texas, Illinois, every mall looks the same, like stamps. The uniqueness we have, has become a tremendous value."

Over the next six to 12 months, that uniqueness will become more accessible, with stores slated to open in Hingham, Lexington, Natick, and Connecticut. The chain's stores at the Prudential and Newton malls are expanding, and its Harvard Square location is adding a store that will focus on casual wear with a preppy appeal, featuring brands such as Lacoste and Le Tigre.

The company is also testing out a new format. Over the weekend, it opened a discount facility, JasmineSola Warehouse Store, on Commonwealth Avenue in Boston.

Outside of New England, the chain is looking at locations from New York to Florida, and also in California, but Manganella says no market is off-limits. The stores can be successful in a variety of venues, said Steve Ferris, a principal with Boston Strategic Retail Advisors hired recently by the Manganellas to craft a real estate plan.

But no matter where stores open, Manganella not only will have to delegate responsibility but also give up some control as venture capital money chases him, said Ferris.

Manganella agrees on one point. He is happy to let the deep pockets decide where to put the new stores, he says.

"But remember, I didn't look for them. They looked for me. I don't take orders. I am in control when it comes to the artistic aspect of the company," he said. "I started this business with no money, but I feel I am one of the top small specialty chains in the country."
