Sox make off-season pitch

Owners mull attractions to draw fans to Fenway -- even in winter

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr., Globe Staff  |  December 16, 2004

Red Sox executives are considering creating a hall of fame or museum at Fenway Park to help keep the fever going, and cash flowing, all year long.

Aggressive about increasing revenues as well as improving the fan's ballpark experience, the owners of the world champions have already opened up two adjacent buildings, adding concessions behind center field near Gate B and planning a couple of restaurants at Brookline Avenue and Lansdowne Street.

They have added more seats for Red Sox fans and are working to build a festive, pizza-filled atmosphere on the perimeter of Fenway Park.

But a memorabilia-packed exhibit complex, perhaps combined with a room commemorating Red Sox stars, could increase the appeal of beloved Fenway and help achieve the goal of expanding the public's use of the ballpark beyond home games.

"We may have a museum or souvenirs -- we want to use them to enliven the street front all year," Janet Marie Smith, the Red Sox's vice president for planning and development, said at a breakfast meeting with real estate executives yesterday. She stressed that a museum was just one of several ideas being considered.

With more seats, wider public concourses, and new varieties of food added since the Red Sox's new owners took over in 2002, visitors are already arriving earlier and lingering longer, Smith said. As part of an effort to enhance the aesthetic experience surrounding the park, Red Sox owners installed a statue of former Sox slugger Ted Williams, near Gate B.

A museum could appeal to Boston area residents as well as to out-of-towners. "Year-round uses -- that's something we're looking to do all around the ballpark," said Smith. "It's already a sports and entertainment district. We're looking to burnish and make more of it."

John W. Henry and other Red Sox executives have not said "never" to a new ballpark. But since they took over, they have embarked on a determined effort to increase the capacity of 82-year-old Fenway Park, the smallest facility in Major League Baseball. (The New York Times Co., parent company of the Globe, owns 17 percent of the Red Sox.)

"Our purpose has been to give Fenway Park long-term life," Smith told the annual meeting of the National Association of Industrial and Office Properties' Massachusetts chapter, which was meeting at the Boston Harbor Hotel. "We don't pretend to have a horizon that is 20 years out yet."

But the front office is thinking more about real estate, and how a park as old as Fenway can fit into today's world. "It was built pre-radio, and before there were calamari and fried chicken concessions," Smith said.

Renovated in 1934, the park legally accommodates 36,298. Owners are asking the City of Boston for a 10 percent increase, to 39,928, though some of that would be standing-room. Their goal is to have between 37,000 and 38,000 seats, Smith said.

Seats that have been added over the past couple of years, atop the Green Monster left-field wall and elsewhere, have only replaced standing-room space, with the total never exceeding the current 36,298 cap.

As many as 45,000 fans jammed into Fenway Park in the 1940s, Smith said, but that was before the team's ownership and administration, as well as room for support for increased concessions operations, grew dramatically and absorbed space.

Fenway's lights are frequently on when the team isn't playing -- even out of season. That's because some rooms at the facility, particularly the .406 Club behind home plate, are used often for parties, weddings, and meetings.

A likely location for a team museum would be a garage now known as the laundry building, located in back of the center-field wall. Space around that 45,000-square-foot building, formerly used for dumpsters and storage, has been opened up, with concession stands and tables added to the area. Red Sox president Larry Lucchino has pushed for more of what he calls "milling-around room," Smith said.

The upper floors of the garage building may be turned into function rooms, more space for parties, and gatherings that might enhance the bottom line.

Construction is underway already -- at the other side of the park, at Brookline Avenue and Lansdowne Street -- at another adjacent building owned by Henry and his partners, including Thomas C. Werner. Faux brick is being removed from the actual brick of the so-called Jeano building, where a restaurant or two are planned, probably in time for next season.

One change the team isn't planning: seat size. Smith was asked by a husky man in the audience yesterday about Fenway's narrow seats. "It's the hardest question we face," she said. "We don't have a plan for that, but we hear it over and over again."
