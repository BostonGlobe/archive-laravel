Tension, fear after mall shooting

Violence outside Galleria stirs debate in Cambridge

By Suzanne Smalley and Michael Levenson, Globe Staff and Globe Correspondent  |  December 16, 2004

CAMBRIDGE -- In a quiet entryway of the CambridgeSide Galleria, 75-year-old Mary Dellolio stood gazing out the window, Sears bags in hand, waiting for a ride from a friend. Dellolio said she feels safe in the mall during the daytime but would never venture there after dark, though she lives only two blocks away.

''In the nighttime, there's more young people here than there is during the daytime," Dellolio said. ''I don't come here at night."

For many holiday shoppers in the mall yesterday, the shooting the night before of a 19-year-old from Boston just outside the east entrance had underscored a fact of life at the Galleria: Many are comfortable strolling amid the 120-plus stores during the day, but shun the mall at night, when teenagers arrive en masse.

''I left work early today to come Christmas shopping, and I told them all I have to get here early before the hoodlums arrive," said Kathy Mitchell, 57, outside the Cheesecake Factory. ''I'm one of those optimists who think it happened last night so it won't happen again today."

Unlike some urban malls, the CambridgeSide Galleria does not have a curfew for teenagers or require them to be accompanied by adults.

The mall, which bills itself as a ''hip urban center," does not plan to start such policies now, said Issie Shait, the mall's vice president and general manager.

Shait said the mall adds security guards during the holiday season, but believes mandating chaperones or a curfew would do nothing to stop youth violence.

''Both the Cambridge police and mall security were there less than a minute after it happened," Shait said of the shooting Tuesday night. ''This wasn't a random act. It was individuals from Boston who happened to bump into each other here."

Mall managers also do not want to treat customers unfairly based on their age or race, Shait said.

''We can't keep people from coming to the mall because we don't like how they look," he said. ''This is a place where the public is welcome to come as long as they don't create a problem."

There have been two other shootings in the past eight years at or near the mall: A 20-year-old man from Roslindale was shot and killed on Christmas Eve 1999 in the parking garage, and a 19-year-old man from Somerville was shot at least three times just outside the mall in 1996.

The shooting Tuesday began about 7 p.m. when two teenagers argued by a canal outside the mall and then started shoving each other, witnesses and police said. One of the men took out a gun and fired several shots, at least one of which hit the other in the stomach, police and witnesses said.

The victim, whose name police have not released, was listed in stable condition, police said. For security reasons, they would not identify the hospital.

Police had not made any arrests by last night, but said they are looking for five suspects. The shooter and victim knew each other, police said.

''Police presence was right there," Cambridge police Commissioner Ronnie Watson said yesterday. ''I don't think we can stop it when you have young people inclined to become involved in violent confrontations. You can have police officers right next to them and that's not going to stop it."

Some malls, including the nation's largest, the Mall of America in Minnesota, restrict access to teenagers to bolster security and make other shoppers feel safer. Mall of America bars children under 18 on weekends after 6 p.m. unless a parent or guardian accompanies them.

Watson said he believes such curfews are unfair. ''We're giving young people a bad label if we blame them all for acts of a few," Watson said. ''There's a lot of young people that go to the Galleria mall that abide by the law, shop, and eat at the food court."

Some teens say they endure close scrutiny while shopping at the Galleria. Yesterday, Jean Fleurissaint, 18, of Randolph said he and three friends were followed from store to store for about 15 minutes by one officer, then two more.

''It was like, was this really necessary, that they were following us?" Fleurissaint said. ''It kind of made me mad. It was messed up, because we're black. I know they've got to do their job, but they don't have to do it like that."

Norman Bates, a mall security specialist who has worked with the Galleria, said mall guards use cameras to track and disperse teens who are not shopping.

''What they'll do is when they see groups of youths hanging around and apparently not shopping or eating they will deal with them aggressively," Bates said. ''But if you're talking about two or three kids together, who don't appear to be doing anything wrong, they're not going to do anything about that."

Many shoppers interviewed yesterday said there is little authorities can do to clamp down on youth violence at the Galleria.

''These kinds of isolated incidents between young people can happen any place, any time," said Jim Thomas, 56.

In Perfumania, employee Yelitza Gonzalez, 17, said she had seen shoplifting at the mall, but never violence. ''I just think it was an act of stupidity," Gonzalez said.

US Representative Michael Capuano, who was in the mall on his lunch break, said crime in the Galleria is a fact of urban life.

''Bad things can happen everywhere," he said. ''And this part of the world is no exception."

Cambridge Mayor Michael Sullivan, wrapping gifts as part of a fund-raiser for Rosie's Place, said he does not believe increased police presence is necessary.

''I think the good work of everyone responding to this incident helped ensure that nothing escalated," Sullivan said.

Bill Gumes, 75, shopping for Red Sox caps for his family, said he was willing to endure some level of uneasiness to complete his holiday shopping.

''All you can do is hope something doesn't happen to you and go on your merry way," he said.
