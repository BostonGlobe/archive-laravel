For retailers, it's go time

Scramble on to keep hot items in supply and sell off the duds

By Naomi Aoki, Globe Staff  |  December 24, 2004

Larry Mansdorf, a buyer for Newbury Comics Inc., realized as soon as he logged on to his computer that the just-released "Napoleon Dynamite" DVD was going to be the sleeper hit of the holiday season.

Just three hours after going on sale Tuesday, 263 of the 1,050 copies the New England chain had in stock were gone. Mansdorf jumped on the phone and ordered as many copies as he could find. He ordered 510 copies from one supplier, 500 from another, then 400, then 350, then another 350, and finally another 845.

By the close of business Tuesday, more than half its stores were sold out, and the retailer had only 100 copies of the DVD left in stock. In fact, the DVD sold out at stores nationwide. Sitting in his office amidst boxes and stacks of CDs and DVDs, Mansdorf was exhausted from frantically calling suppliers all day, tracking sales, and directing shipments to the chain's 25 stores.

"There's no good buying mistake," the retail veteran said with an air of resignation. "They're all disasters."

In this last week of the holiday shopping season, as people search for last-minute presents, retailers scramble to keep hot items in stock and clear their shelves of the duds. Though every retailer plans promotions and sales throughout the holiday season to lure shoppers, this is the week that separates the good from the ugly.

As a rule of thumb, if a store is slashing prices across the board and offering discounts of 70 percent or more, it's a sign that its holiday sales lacked luster. On the flip side, a store offering smaller discounts and fewer of them is enjoying a merry holiday.

Judging by the sale signs cluttering storefronts this week, most analysts say the season will disappoint.

Five large "Last Minute Sale" signs hang from the ceiling at the front of the KB Toys store at the CambridgeSide Galleria. Payless ShoeSource is offering as much as 70 percent off and advertising its lowest prices of the year. At Banana Republic, a retailer that avoided discounts last holiday season, women's coats are marked down as much as 50 percent, $28 tank tops are selling for $9.99, and $58 Merino stretch sweaters for $39.99.

"If sales of luxury goods and consumer electronics weren't so good, this season would be a total bust," said C. Britt Beemer, chairman and founder of America's Research Group.

Amid the sea of last-minute sale signs, however, a few individual retailers, including Newbury Comics, are doing well. While other retailers are out of "Napoleon Dynamite," Mansdorf managed to secure enough copies of the DVD to restock the chain's stores by 2 p.m. Wednesday and last into next week. There are no sale signs in its store windows, no clearance tables full of cheap tchotchkes. Newly released CDs and DVDs are marked with pink stickers indicating sale prices to compete with retail giants like Wal-Mart Stores Inc. and Best Buy Inc., which undercut prices on new releases to lure shoppers.

Here and there, slow-moving merchandise has been marked down. A Wonder Woman calendar is selling for $8.99, down from $12.99. SpongeBob SquarePants and Care Bear dolls were moved to the top shelf, making room for more popular characters like Ugly Dolls on easier-to-reach shelves. But for Newbury Comics, a privately held chain with $80 million in yearly sales, the big challenge this past week has been to keep merchandise in stock.

"A lot of stores have the same merchandise," said John Macht, founder of the retail consulting firm Macht Group in Chestnut Hill. "But Newbury Comics carries things you can't find everywhere else. There's always room for creative retailers."

Because many of its customers are young, Newbury Comics stores aren't typically bustling with holiday shoppers until the second week in December. So instead of a Thanksgiving day circular, the chain issued its holiday circular in early December. The idea was to attract shoppers with a weeklong, 20-percent-off DVD sale. Once there, they'd see the wide selection of DVDs, the assortment of Red Sox merchandise, and gifts that run the gamut from zany to demented.

Even if they didn't buy anything during the sale, it would spur them to start thinking about the people on their Christmas lists and to put Newbury Comics at the top of their mind.

"People don't try new things when they're panicking that last week before Christmas," said Mike Dreese, cofounder and chief executive of the comic book store turned music and DVD seller.

At least for this holiday season, when most shoppers seem to be waiting until the last minute, the strategy has been working. While most of the retail industry was forecasting sales growth of 4 to 5 percent, Newbury Comics went into the season expecting sales to remain relatively flat. In the age of downloadable music and iPods, sales of CDs are under pressure. Though the chain's sales in October were strong, they were weak in November.

But December is shaping up to be a record month for the chain. Its sales are on track to be up as much as 17 percent, compared to last December. As a regional chain, it was in a better position than national retailers to anticipate the demand for all things Red Sox. Its buyers stocked up on everything from Red Sox caps to Red Sox World Series Monopoly games to Red Sox Bobble Heads and Bobble Mates.

It has now sold more than 14,000 "Faith Rewarded" DVDs chronicling the Red Sox's World Series Championship season, 5,000 Red Sox Christmas tree ornaments, 1,500 Red Sox Merry Merry Merry Frickin' Christmas CDs that feature a song lambasting the Yankees, and 2,600 talking bottle openers. (The bottle openers, which play authentic game calls from Red Sox radio announcer Jerry Trupiano, sold out Thursday night.)

Johnna Dehlinger, 41, and six of her family members came from Camden, Maine, in search of distinctive Red Sox merchandise. She'd been to the Newbury Street Newbury Comics store and remembered they had Red Sox paraphernalia. She found a T-shirt with silhouettes of Manny Ramirez, Pedro Martinez, and Johnny Damon, bearing the slogan "Must be the Hair." She also set her sights on a Fenway Park sign that she said was perfect for a younger brother.

"We've been searching like crazy and not been able to find anything," she said. "I even went to the official Red Sox store at Copley, and they'd been cleaned out completely."
