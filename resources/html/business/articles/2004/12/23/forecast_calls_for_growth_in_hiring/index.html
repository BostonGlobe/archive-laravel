Forecast calls for growth in hiring

By Joyce Pellino Crane, Globe Correspondent  |  December 23, 2004

The employment outlook is brightening in the Merrimack Valley, according to a recent survey and local officials, though they caution that this spurt in hiring largely compensates for the job losses in recent years.

In a survey conducted in October by Manpower Inc. and reported last week, 54 percent of the companies said they planned to expand their payrolls in the first quarter of 2005, compared with 25 percent of those surveyed in August. None of the companies in the Merrimack Valley, which includes Greater Lowell and stretches to the North Shore, expected to reduce their payrolls in the latest survey, according to Matt Penny, a spokesman for Milwaukee-based Manpower, a job-placement and consulting agency.

In the Burlington-Waltham area, which includes Billerica, Westford, and Concord, 33 percent of employers planned to increase their staffs, which is about equal to the statewide figure of 32 percent.

"I think that the Merrimack Valley has more room for improvement. I think we've been hit hard," said Penny. "Since there were so many layoffs since [the Sept. 11, 2001, terrorist attacks], particularly in the Merrimack Valley, there was more room for improvement."

The most significant layoffs in the Merrimack Valley were at Lucent Technologies Inc., said Steven Quimby, director of planning and policy research for the Lawrence-based Merrimack Valley Workforce Investment Board. Earlier this decade, Lucent, one of the region's major employers, rapidly shrank to about 500 workers in the region from 10,000, he said.

"When one big anchor company like that experiences layoffs, it's going to have a ripple effect on all manufacturers and service-providing companies that serve both the workers and the company," said Quimby.

Quimby added that the Manpower survey is a credible forecaster of a developing trend. "It's designed to give you a general idea of direction rather than magnitude," he said.

Manpower regional director Mark Spengler said that "pent-up" demand may be fueling future prospects. "Maybe someone landed a big contract, or someone has new business that makes them need to increase staff in order to address their needs," he said.

But Robert Halpin, president of the Merrimack Valley Economic Development Council, thinks the demand may run deeper. Halpin, whose work includes recruiting businesses to the Merrimack Valley, said he has been hearing murmurs of higher staffing needs since the summer.

"I was hearing . . . inventories were down and orders were up, and companies would probably need more people," he said. "I'm surprised it's taken this long."

These findings corroborate data compiled last spring by the state Division of Career Services, which showed increased hiring expectations for the state's Northeast region, which includes the Merrimack Valley.

"I think the economy is doing better," said Elliot Winer, chief economist for the state agency. "We're seeing, across the board, much higher expectations than from the last survey."

Unemployment in the state was at 2.5 percent in August 2000, then it started to rise slowly. At the end of 2001, it stood at 4.7 percent, but it continued going up, reaching as high as 5.9 percent at times during the next two years, Winer said. The current unemployment rate in Massachusetts is 4.3 percent.

The Division of Career Services conducts periodic business hiring surveys for seven regions across Massachusetts. Last spring's survey showed that 35 percent of 4,800 companies across the state planned to increase their staffs, and only 6 percent anticipated reductions. Hiring expectations are weighted, said Winer, to adjust for larger and smaller companies.

Amid the optimism, the Manpower survey showed that even in the Merrimack Valley, employers are facing uncertainty. Twenty-nine percent of employers in the region said they didn't know whether they would expand or reduce their payrolls in the first quarter. That percentage was at least four times higher than in any other region.

"Predicting what the economy is going to do in any given quarter is challenging," said Quimby. Employers are "confident in that the economy is growing and that's going to mean good things for their business. But they may not be quite sure that now is the right time to hire additional workers."

Quimby said he and his investment board colleagues have identified three emerging industries for the Merrimack Valley: specialty foods, trucking and warehousing for Internet-related businesses, and medical instruments and supplies. Another four industries that are growing, though not as rapidly, are health care, construction, wireless and landline communciations, and manufacturing.

One company showing strength is the Andover-based endoscopy operations of Smith & Nephew Inc., a unit of the British company Smith & Nephew PLC. The company, whose products treat patients with minimally invasive techniques, expects to add about 90 positions next year in five US locations, about 25 in the first quarter, said Joseph Metzger, director of corporate communications.

Another bright spot is in the biomedical sector. The Andover-based Wyeth BioPharma Genetics Institute, a unit of New Jersey-based Wyeth, for example, has had consistent job growth over the past two years, said Halpin, while Straumann Holding AG of Switzerland will be moving its dental-implant operations from Waltham to Andover sometime next year.

Quimby, who tracks labor force numbers quarterly, is confident that despite large numbers of people who are unemployed, underemployed, or no longer seeking work, the tide has turned in the employment market.

"It's certainly much better than it was two years ago," he said. "There was a period of time when the unemployment rate was shrinking, but it was entirely because people were leaving the labor force. Now we're seeing the labor force go back up, and a gradual decline in the unemployment rate."
