Company alters retirement plan

December 30, 2004

Boston Scientific said in a filing it changed its retirement plan to enable key employees to exercise stock options for up to three years after they leave the company, compared to 90 days before the change. The company modified the terms of stock options awarded prior to May 2001 so employees could take advantage of the longer period. The company estimates the change will result in a charge of $80 to $100 million during the fourth quarter. The company also said it would move $1 billion in earnings from its foreign subsidiariaries to the United States to take advantage of a tax break passed by Congress that lowers the effective tax rate to 5.25 percent from 35 percent on such capital repatriations. Taking advantage of the tax break is estimated to save the company about $250 million, according to an analysis by Standard & Poor's, the bond rating agency. (Jeffrey Krasner)

N.Y. trust buys 2 Foxborough properties

Lexington Corporate Properties Trust, which leases office, industrial, and retail real estate to businesses, said it bought two properties in Foxborough, for $48.3 million. The Cocasset Facility, consisting of three office and light assembly buildings, was purchased for $22.8 million, New York- based Lexington said. Lexington paid $25.5 million for the two office buildings comprising the Bristol Park Facility. Both properties are leased to Invensys Systems Inc., Lexington said. (Bloomberg)

Nike gives CEO $9.1m in restricted stock

Nike Inc. gave its new president and chief executive William D. Perez 100,000 shares of restricted stock worth about $9.1 million, according to a regulatory filing. Perez was appointed, effective Tuesday, to succeed founder Philip H. Knight. Perez was also awarded options to acquire 200,000 more class B common shares at an exercise price of $90.85 each. The restrictions on the restricted shares expire over three years. The stock was granted Tuesday, when shares closed at $91.49. The grant of stock options becomes exercisable in three equal annual installments beginning in one year. If Perez is terminated "without cause" or if he leaves the company for "good reason," any unvested options will vest immediately, according to the filing. (Dow Jones/AP)

Sallie Mae goes private -- 3 years early

Student loan company Sallie Mae became a private company, severing its ties with the federal government. Officials signed the final documents at the Treasury Department -- ahead of a congressionally set deadline of 2008. Congress set up Sallie Mae in 1972 to help make sure there was ample financing for educational loans. In its former role as a "government-sponsored enterprise," it had benefits such as exemptions from state and local taxes. But it also was limited in the kinds of businesses it could enter, Treasury said. Through the process of privatization, Sallie Mae over the years has diversified its business beyond student loans into related areas, including debt collection and consumer finance, the company said. (AP)

$5b of bonds sold to boost capitalization

Fannie Mae, the biggest provider of money for the US mortgage industry, sold $5 billion of preferred stock after its regulator said it broke accounting rules and is "significantly undercapitalized." The company issued $2.5 billion that can be converted to common stock and $2.5 billion with floating rates that can't be converted, according to Lehman Brothers Inc., which handled the sale. It is the biggest sale of preferred stock by a company this year, according to data compiled by Bloomberg. Fannie Mae said this month it would have to restate earnings from 2001 through mid-2004 by up to $9 billion after the Office of Federal Housing Enterprise Oversight and the Securities and Exchange Commission determined it made bookkeeping errors. Fannie Mae in September agreed to build a 30 percent capital surplus after federal housing found it broke rules in accounting for hedges on its more than $900 billion portfolio of mortgages and mortgage assets, used improper "cookie jar" reserves, and improperly deferred expenses. (Bloomberg)

AT&T, Sprint eye mobile service deal

AT&T Corp. could reveal details as early as next week about how it plans to offer mobile phone service through Sprint Corp.'s cellular network, The New York Times reported today, citing executives close to the companies. Sprint had said this year that it would work with AT&T to offer cellphone service to AT&T's business customers. AT&T plans to start marketing Sprint's cellular service as its own next year, after it regains the rights to the AT&T Wireless brand from Cingular Wireless, the Times said. Cingular acquired AT&T Wireless in October. Also yesterday, Time Warner Inc.'s cable television unit confirmed it is in talks to offer wireless service with Sprint Corp. to fend off competition from satellite-TV and telephone companies that sell package deals. (Globe wire services)

SEC allows proxy to ignore proposal

Reversing its decision, the Securities and Exchange Commission gave Walt Disney Co. permission to withhold from its proxy a proposal to let shareholders directly nominate board members. The SEC staff said Tuesday it would not recommend any enforcement action if Disney excludes the proposal from its 2005 proxy, which is expected to be printed this week. The nonbinding proposal would have allowed individual or groups of shareholders that own more than 5 percent of Disney's shares for more than two years to directly nominate candidates for the board. Given the size of Disney's board, the proposal would have meant that qualifying shareholders could nominate as many as two candidates. The proposal was submitted in October by the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees' pension plan and several state pension funds. (AP)

Continental to purchase 10 Boeing jets

Continental Airlines Inc. said it would become the first major US carrier to buy Boeing Co.'s new 7E7 airplane, with an initial order of 10 jets. Gordon Bethune, the carrier's outgoing chairman and chief executive, said the new airplanes would be "an important part of our international growth strategy." Boeing said the deal would be worth about $1.3 billion at list prices, although airlines typically negotiate steep discounts. The airplanes are to be delivered beginning in 2009. Continental is the second US airline to say it would buy Boeing's new plane. Primaris Airlines Inc., a little-known carrier which plans to offer low-cost business class service, said in October it would buy 20 of the new planes. That carrier's 7E7s are to be delivered between 2010 and 2013. (AP)

Canadian cattle cleared for import

More than 19 months after a mad cow scare closed US borders to Canadian cattle, the United States said it will allow limited imports beginning in March. The new policy will permit imports of cattle younger than 30 months and certain other animals and products from Canada, which the Agriculture Department said has effective measures to prevent and detect bovine spongiform encephalopathy, or mad cow disease. The department said the ruling, which will take effect March 7, came after determining Canada is a "minimal-risk region," the first country recognized as such. The cattle imports will be subject to certain restrictions, including ensuring they are slaughtered by the age of 30 months, which scientists say is too young to contract mad cow disease. The cattle must also be transported in sealed containers to a feedlot or slaughter, and they are not allowed to move to more than one US feedlot. Live cattle shipments from Canada were banned in May 2003 after a case of mad cow disease was discovered in the province of Alberta. (AP)
