Pfizer Inc. asked US regulators to approve the intravenous form of its Bextra painkiller, a drug linked to an increased risk of heart attacks in some patients when taken in pill form. The application for parecoxib was filed with the Food and Drug Administration in mid-December, Susan Bro, a Pfizer spokeswoman, said. She didn't give a specific date. The FDA usually aims to complete its review of applications within 10 months. The FDA doesn't comment on applications under review. Last week, the agency urged doctors to limit prescriptions of Bextra and Celebrex before the February meeting. Bextra is one of five drugs FDA reviewer David Graham identified as unsafe at a Nov. 18 US Senate hearing. (Bloomberg)

Crude oil prices retreat 19 per barrel

Crude oil futures fell as forecasts for warmer winter weather in the Northern Hemisphere eased supply fears, helping to roll back part of a $2-a-barrel spike a day earlier. Benchmark light, sweet crude for February delivery slipped 19 cents to settle at $43.45 on the New York Mercantile Exchange. Heating oil for January delivery settled at $1.2297 per gallon on the New York Mercantile Exchange, down 4.77 cents from Wednesday. On Nymex, unleaded gasoline for January delivery rose 0.44 cent to $1.0887 per gallon, while February natural gasoline fell 25 cents to $6.149 per 1,000 cubic feet. In London, Brent crude futures for February rose $1.20 to $40.37 per barrel on the International Petroleum Exchange. (AP)

Oracle fires 4 PeopleSoft executives

Oracle Corp. fired PeopleSoft Corp.'s copresident and chief financial officer Kevin Parker and three other top executives, a day after assuming control of the rival software maker in a $10.3 billion takeover. Oracle terminated W. Phillip Wilmington and Nanci Caldwell, chief marketing officer and James Shaughnessy, general counsel, effective yesterday, according to a regulatory filing. Oracle named its top officials, copresidents Charles Phillips Jr. and Safra Catz, and Harry You, chief financial officer, to the PeopleSoft posts. (Bloomberg)

Company fires auditor Ernst & Young

Best Buy Co. Inc. said it is dropping Ernst & Young as its auditor -- more fallout from the company's May disclosure that a director took money from the accounting firm while serving on Best Buy's auditing committee. Best Buy said Ernst & Young would still audit its books through the close of its fiscal year on Feb. 26, according to a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission. The company said it has not picked a new auditor. Director Mark C. Thompson resigned on May 4, the same day he disclosed a personal service agreement with Ernst & Young, one of the nation's largest accounting firms. The firm paid Thompson $377,500 between December 2002 and April 2004, Best Buy said in May. Thompson was a member of Best Buy's auditing committee from March 2000 until August 2003. (AP)

Bush signs bill for federal pay raises

President Bush spelled out in greater detail the pay raise that takes effect Jan. 1 for all US government employees. Congress passed the pay raises this year, but Bush was required to sign an executive order detailing the pay hikes before year-end. The president's annual salary of $400,000 is not affected by the legislation. The cost-of-living raise lifts salaries for members of the House and Senate from $158,000 this year to $162,100 in 2005. The measure provides civil servants with 3.5 percent raises -- the same that military personnel will receive next year. Under a complicated formula, that translates to 2.5 percent for members of Congress. The 2.5 percent pay hike also applies to the vice president -- who is president of the Senate -- congressional leaders, and Supreme Court justices. (AP)

Samsung sets aside $100m for probe

Samsung Electronics Co. of South Korea is setting aside $100 million to cover the cost, including possible fines or court settlements, of a US investigation into alleged price-fixing in the market for computer memory chips. Samsung, the leading maker of memory chips, has previously declined to set aside any money, even though the probe has been going on for more than two years. It was unclear what prompted Samsung executives to change their minds. A spokesman declined to comment beyond announcing the move. (Dow Jones/AP)
