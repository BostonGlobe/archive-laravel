Support builds for minimum wage hike

State lawmakers weigh a link to inflation rate

By Scott S. Greenberger, Globe Staff  |  December 9, 2004

Union leaders and advocates for the poor are uniting behind an effort to raise the state's $6.75-an-hour minimum wage for the first time since 2001 and to tie future increases to the inflation rate, a move that would make Massachusetts only the fourth state in the nation to establish that link.

The Legislature last approved an increase in 1999, voting to raise the hourly minimum wage from $5.25 to $6 starting in 2000 and from $6 to $6.75 in 2001. Bills pending in both the House and the Senate would raise the hourly minimum wage to $7.50 in 2006 and $8.25 in 2007 and connect future increases to annual inflation increases.

Backers point to the Bay State's rapidly rising cost of living as a sign that the time is right for another push for a minimum wage increase.

''It's time that we begin this discussion again, to talk about what's happening with the minimum-wage workers. These are people who are doing the right thing, working, trying to make a living," said Senator Marc R. Pacheco, a Taunton Democrat who is sponsoring the bill's Senate version. ''We should try to do everything we can to make work pay."

Like their counterparts in some other states, policy makers on Beacon Hill are also exasperated by the US government's failure to raise the federal minimum wage, which has been $5.15 since 1997. Massachusetts, which enacted the nation's first minimum-wage law in 1912, is one of 13 states that have set a higher standard than the federal government. Under the 1999 law, the Bay State must exceed the federal rate by at least 10 cents.

Some cities, such as Boston, have set even higher ''living wages" for contractors that do business with them. In Boston that wage rose to $11.29 an hour last July.

''There's a sense that low-wage workers have continued to fall behind in America and that one thing we can do here in Massachusetts that can directly help those people is to raise minimum wages," said Noah Berger, executive director of the Massachusetts Budget and Policy Center, a liberal think tank. ''I've gotten the sense that a lot more people are interested in it now."

Berger's group estimates that as many as 404,000 people, or as much as 13.8 percent of the workforce, would see a raise if the minimum wage were increased. Most potential beneficiaries are older than 20, more than half are women, and many are poor, the center said.

Governor Mitt Romney, a probusiness Republican, has backed indexing the minimum wage since 2002. Even with his endorsement, which could make indexing more palatable for business backers in the Legislature, supporters will face strong opposition from the state's leading business groups, who argue that raising the minimum wage would lead employers to eliminate low-paying jobs rather pay the new, higher wage.

''There are better ways to help people at the low end of the economic spectrum. All raising the minimum wage does is raise that first rung on the ladder. What it does is make jobs less available, and economic opportunity less available, to the people who really need it," said Bill Vernon, state director of the National Federation of Independent Business.

Brian Gilmore of Associated Industries of Massachusetts said tying the rate to inflation would be especially unwise ''in times of economic sluggishness" because ''it would discourage employers from making work opportunities available for young people and people who want to work part time." He argued that a minimum wage increase now would lead some businesses to cut jobs or not hire new workers.

Gilmore said a recent report by two Duke University economics professors concluded that ''an increase in the minimum wage must result in a decreased possibility of an individual finding a job."

Under the measure to be considered on Beacon Hill, workers would get minimum-wage increases in 2006 and 2007, and future increases would be tied to the annual inflation rate, which is used, for example, to determine increases in Social Security and other benefits. The most common measure of inflation is the consumer price index set by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, an arm of the federal government.

Supporters argue that indexing the minimum wage would help shield workers from inflation, which eats away at their wages -- especially in the Bay State, with its rapidly rising cost of living. Opponents argue that indexing the minimum wage would make increases automatic, without a more sophisticated review of the market and labor supply.

Some Democrats are uneasy about locking in automatic increases tied to inflation. Representative Michael J. Rodrigues of Westport, the House chair of the Commerce and Labor Committee, said such as system would be overly simplistic.

Senate President Robert E. Travaglini and House Speaker Salvatore F. DiMasi, known as allies of organized labor, declined yesterday to take a position on the minimum-wage bills. But Romney, who has often been at odds with labor during his two years in office, is squarely in the unions' camp on the issue of indexing the minimum wage.

''I do not believe that indexing the minimum wage will cost us jobs," he declared during his campaign for governor in 2002. ''I believe it will help us to retain jobs." Romney spokeswoman Shawn Feddeman said that he plans to file his own minimum-wage bill in the coming months.
