Retailers' accuracy high on toy pricing

December 9, 2004

Massachusetts retailers are doing a good job of charging the correct prices for toys, according to a survey released by state regulators that found an accuracy rate of 99.5 percent at eight retailers. The state Division of Standards checked 6,493 toys at 65 stores and found 6,304 of them scanned at the correct price and 189 at an incorrect price. Of the 189 that scanned incorrectly, 35 were overcharges, the biggest being a $20 overcharge for a toy advertised at $19.99 at the Toys "R" Us in Bellingham. Wal-Mart stores in Hanover, North Adams, and North Dartmouth and a Kohl's store in Stoughton were issued fines for failing to meet the state's price accuracy requirement of 98 percent. Another $3,600 in fines were issued for overcharges at Wal-Mart, Kohl's, Toys "R" Us, and Target stores. BJ's Warehouse, Costco, and Disney stores had no errors. (Bruce Mohl)

Biopure posts smaller loss in 4th quarter

Biopure Corp. narrowed its fiscal fourth-quarter loss on slightly higher revenue and cuts in research and development expenses. The Cambridge company, which makes blood products for medical applications out of bovine blood cells, anticipates burning through its cash by January, and said it needs $19 million to get through 2005. Given its financial predicament, the company expects its auditor, Ernst & Young, to issue a going-concern warning with its fiscal 2004 filing. Biopure posted a loss of $9.4 million, or 16 cents a share, for the quarter ended Oct. 31, narrower than the loss of $13 million, or 30 cents a share, a year earlier. For the latest period, Biopure booked an inventory write-down of $2.2 million due to uncertainties about the dates of initiating Hemopure sales in South Africa. Fourth-quarter revenue rose 54 percent to $1.7 million, from $1.2 million for the prior year's period. The latest tally includes a reimbursement of $1.4 million from the Army related to trauma studies using Hemopure. (Dow Jones)

Bayside Expo, Mass. Horticultural in deal

The Massachusetts Horticultural Society has signed a five-year contract with Bayside Expo Center in Dorchester, guaranteeing that the organization's New England Spring Flower Show will stay at the convention hall. Officials on both sides said they reworked the contract so that the society would no longer have to pay the private convention hall fees up front, while at the same time giving Bayside guarantees that it won't lose money. Specific financial details were not disclosed. The center, which is owned by developers Corcoran-Jennison Cos., has been the subject of rumors that its owners may close the facility, potentially to make way for a housing development. (Keith Reed)

Charles River Labs to end joint venture

Charles River Laboratories International Inc. plans to close its Charles River Proteomic Services joint venture, leading to a fourth-quarter charge of 2 to 3 cents a share. The Wilmington drug company said the venture, which it established with Proteome Systems Ltd. in 2002, hadn't met its strategic goal. The venture was intended to provide protein-related research services on a fee-for-service basis. Charles River said that eliminating the business will help boost its profits for 2005. (Dow Jones)

T.H. Lee Partners to buy stake in insurer

Private equity firms Thomas H. Lee Partners and Texas Pacific Group have agreed to buy 25 percent of Fidelity National Financial Inc., the nation's biggest title insurer, for $500 million. The insurer said its Fidelity National Information Services unit is entering into $2.8 billion of senior secured credit facilities, for which some commitments have been received. The proceeds will be used to recapitalize the unit and to provide a special cash dividend to Fidelity National shareholders. Fidelity National said the transaction will be completed in the first quarter of 2005. (Bloomberg)

R.I. company lays off 90 employees

Hasbro Inc. has laid off about 125 workers, mostly in Rhode Island, as the number two US toy maker concentrates on new toy technologies. The cuts are in the toys, games, international, and corporate divisions. They allow "added flexibility and resources to more aggressively pursue the fastest growing part of our business while meeting the changing needs of our customers and consumers," spokesman Wayne Charness said. The laid-off workers' expertise was "not in those newer areas," he added. About 90 people lost their jobs in Pawtucket, R.I., where the company is based. The cuts amount to 6.9 percent of Hasbro's 1,300 workers in Rhode Island and 1.8 percent of the company's 6,900 employees worldwide. (AP)

European insurer's unit hires State Street

State Street Corp., the world's largest service provider for institutional investors, will oversee administration and accounting for $398 billion of assets for Axa Investment Managers, the fund unit of Europe's second-largest insurer. Boston-based State Street is taking on 300 Axa employees in France, Germany, and the United Kingdom as part of the contract, the companies said. Axa's French and German workers joined State Street on Dec. 1, and the transfer of operations in the UK will likely be complete by the end of the first quarter, they said. The Axa contract was the first mandate of its kind to a third-party provider in France or Germany, State Street executive vice chairman Jay Hooley said. (Bloomberg)

Craftsmen to sue Christmas Tree Shops

Two New England craftsmen are suing the Christmas Tree Shops, accusing the company of stealing their original designs. The craftsmen, in a lawsuit to be filed today in US District Court in Boston, allege the company copied their designs, then mass-produced their crafts in China so they could be sold for a fraction of their cost in artisan shops. John Knight, co-owner of Glass Eye Gallery in Eastham, claims the Christmas Tree Shops copied the design of a wall sconce he has been selling for six years. Knight said he sells the handmade sconce for $31.95, while the Christmas Tree Shops sell a product that looks nearly identical for $1.99. The owners of Country Glass Shop, in Marshfield, Vt., claim Christmas Tree Shops copied the design of one of their "Sun Catcher" window hangings and now sells them for $1.69. Sarah Gregory, counsel to Christmas Tree Shops, said the company had not been served with a copy of the lawsuit and was unable to comment. (AP)

Sirius Satellite Radio shares drop 23%

Sirius Satellite Radio Inc., the most active US stock over the past three months, recorded its biggest decline in 21 months after two analysts said the shares were expensive and lowered their recommendations. Shares of Sirius, the second-largest pay-radio service, behind XM Satellite Radio Holdings Inc., had more than doubled in the last two months after signing radio show host Howard Stern to a five-year contract and hiring former Viacom Inc. president Mel Karmazin as chief executive. Sirius shares fell $2.11, or 23 percent, to $6.90. (Bloomberg)
