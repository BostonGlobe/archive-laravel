An ATM approach to renting movies

By Scott Kirsner  |  December 13, 2004

Most people are accustomed to automatic tellers at the mall that spit out cash and stamps. Eduardo Alvarez wants to persuade you to use an automatic teller at the grocery store that will dispense DVDs of recent movies like ''Elf" and ''The Terminal."

His start-up company, Boomerang Movies, is building a kiosk that can cough up any of the top 40 most popular new DVDs in response to a swipe of your credit card. Watch the movie and return it by dropping it in the mail -- a la Netflix -- and Boomerang charges you $4.59 for a three-day rental. Watch the movie and decide to keep it for your collection, and you pay full price for the DVD (minus the rental fee).

''Netflix is not spontaneous enough," says Alvarez, alluding to the Silicon Valley-based online subscription service that requires members to plan DVD rentals in advance, then wait for them to arrive in the mail. ''In our focus groups, 96 percent of people who rent said it was a spontaneous decision." And the bulk of all movie rental revenues come from new releases, hence Boomerang's circumscribed selection.

The first kiosks are being installed this month in Sanborns, a Mexico City department store; early next year, Alvarez expects to install a couple of Boomerang machines at Roche Bros. supermarkets around the Boston area. (Last November, Alvarez conducted a short market test at Bentley College in Waltham.) He pictures his ''movie tellers" finding a home at gas stations, in airports, and at convenience stores.

Boomerang is being launched out of the Lexington offices of IDEO, the well-known product development firm. IDEO helped Alvarez design the Boomerang kiosk in return for a stake in the start-up, an arrangement the firm agrees to only rarely, said Douglas Dayton, head of IDEO's Lexington branch. (Dayton was a key player in the design of Apple Computer's original mouse.) Alvarez has raised $1 million for the company so far. But he estimates that another $1.5 million to $2 million will be necessary.

Alvarez has a compelling long-term vision: He sees the kiosks burning a wide range of CDs and DVDs on-the-spot for customers, and even beaming music and videogames to laptops, MP3 players, and cellphones. (There's already a USB port and Bluetooth wireless technology built into the kiosks.) ''Eventually, this becomes a refueling station for digital entertainment," he says.

I think the odds that Boomerang will succeed are only slightly better than the chances Justin Guarini will one day take home an Oscar. The machines are expensive to build, and the company will either have to pay rent for its locations, give up equity in the company (as it may be doing with Roche Bros.), or offer a share of revenue. ''No one will give away square footage for free," says Mark Hardie, a Nokia executive who tried (and failed) to launch an automatic teller for digital music called MusicTeller.

And there's already stiff competition, even apart from the mailbox-based services offered by Netflix, Blockbuster, and Wal-Mart. A Silicon Valley company called DVDPlay has already inked a deal with McDonald's, and begun installing its kiosks in McDonald's parking lots, at Albertson's supermarkets, and on college campuses. (Students at Tufts and Babson College are already using them.) In Europe, a French company called Logimatiq makes kiosks that rent DVDs and VHS tapes. (Not surprisingly, the second most popular category of rentals, according to Logimatiq, is adult films.) But both models require that renters return the movie to the kiosk; Boomerang lets you drop it in the mail.

Plus, fund-raising for the company will be hard. Even Dayton at IDEO admits that ''money's so hard to come by right now."

But while the challenges may be daunting, I never bet against a dogged entrepreneur like Alvarez.

Sweet tooth
The Boston edition of the trend-spotting website Daily Candy has just been launched -- great news for wanna-be scenesters.

Daily Candy is the kind of website that Carrie Bradshaw would have consulted before a shopping spree or a night on the town. It offers insider-y tips about restaurants, fashion, travel, and services (like the facials at Skin Care Adventure in Chinatown).

Boston's the fifth city to get its own edition of Daily Candy (after NYC, LA, Chicago, and San Francisco). The website's at www.dailycandy.com/boston, but those in the know sign up to get their candy delivered via e-mail.

Fifteen years later
There's something about celebrating high-tech anniversaries that just doesn't sit right with me.

Yes, milestones can help mark the passage of time and put an influential technology into perspective. This month, for instance, is the 15th anniversary of the introduction of Lotus Notes collaboration software.

Today, IBM -- which bought Lotus and Notes for $3.5 billion in 1995 -- estimates there are 100 million Notes users around the world, sharing documents, holding online meetings, and instant messaging each other.

But anniversaries are inherently backward-looking, and the tech sector is so dedicated to looking ahead, designing the new products that will render the old products as obsolete as a steam-powered car.

What is interesting to me is what the developers of Notes (and others who worked on the product after Lotus launched it) are doing today. Almost all of the spinoff companies have something to do with digital collaboration -- making it easy for people to work together virtually, even when separated by great distances.

Ray Ozzie, founder of Iris Associates, is chief executive of Groove Networks in Beverly, which makes a collaboration package called Groove Virtual Office. Just as Iris was initially funded by a bigger company that later acquired it (Lotus), the majority of Groove's funding has come from Microsoft. Ozzie says he has no intention to sell out to Bill Gates. We'll see.

Lotus founder Mitch Kapor is out in San Francisco, where he's running the Open Source Applications Foundation. OSAF aims to make open source software more widely available (and tilt at Microsoft's hegemony). The nonprofit's first product, Chandler, helps users manage their contacts and calendars.

There are at least a half-dozen local companies whose founders were part of the Notes team at some point, including Pepper Computer in Lexington, a start-up that plans to start selling an $800 wireless tablet computer next month; Convoq, a Web conferencing company; Centra Software, an e-learning firm; Intranets.com, which was named to the Inc. 500 list in October; Kubi Software; Adesso Systems; and Cambridge-based Interwise, where former Lotus chief executive Jim Manzi is chairman.

Last week, Ozzie wrote an opinion piece for News.com, looking back on 15 years of Notes. ''We're only at the very beginning of exploring what can be done in the realm of using technology to enable joint work," he said. He pointed to Skype, Wikipedia, and Flickr as interesting models of next-generation digital collaboration. (Skype provides free software for Internet telephony; Wikipedia is an encyclopedia written by Internet users; and Flickr allows users to upload pictures directly from their mobile phones to their Weblogs.)

Another company will be marking an anniversary in January. It will be the ruby anniversary (that's 40 years) for Norwood-based Analog Devices. No word on whether Analog will fill the goodie bags with stock options at the anniversary fete. That might bring even more scrutiny from that perennial wet blanket, the Securities and Exchange Commission.
