BRA gives go-ahead for three projects

December 3, 2004

The Boston Redevelopment Authority approved projects that could add nearly 400 residences in the city, including an expansion in Charles River Park. The BRA gave the green light to developer Equity Residential to move forward with the West End Residences at Emerson Place. The residences plan 306 rental apartments, 15 percent of which will be affordable. Equity said the project will be the first residential development in the West End in 20 years. At 131 Green St. in Jamaica Plain, the BRA approved a ''transit-oriented development project" with 21 town house condos near the MBTA Green Street station. Another project that got BRA approval was one with 29 town houses at 131 Brookley Road in the same neighborhood. (Chris Reidy)

Hub company loses ruling in Germany

A German court has ruled that Energizer Holdings Inc.'s four-bladed Quattro razor does not infringe on Gillette Co.'s European patent on a three-bladed design, Energizer said. In the latest development in a feud between the world's largest makers of wet-shave products, the court also ordered Gillette, the market leader, to reimburse Energizer's unspecified court costs. The ruling came 11 months after a federal judge in Massachusetts issued a similar finding, denying Boston-based Gillette's request for a preliminary injunction in the United States. Gillette spokesman Eric Kraus said the company would appeal, adding, ''We have been consistent in our rigorous defense of our intellectual property and we do not intend to abandon the claim based on this ruling." (AP)

Gaiety Theatre owner seeks demolition

The owner of the defunct Gaiety Theatre has asked the City of Boston for a demolition permit, but a group of preservationists wants to save the nearly 100-year-old building. Kensington Investment Co. wants to tear down the structure and put up an apartment building nearly 300 feet tall. Kensington already has its major approvals from the Boston Redevelopment Authority for the project, at the corner of Washington and LaGrange streets near Chinatown. (Chris Reidy)

Genzyme, partner get OK for wrinkle gel

Inamed Corp. and its partner Genzyme Corp. won US approval to sell the Captique wrinkle gel, a dermal filler product based on Genzyme's nonanimal stabilized hyaluronic acid technology. The treatment is Inamed's first wrinkle filler that is not derived from animals. The Food and Drug Administration cleared Captique to correct moderate to severe facial wrinkles, Inamed said. Genzyme and Inamed disclosed the clearance after US markets closed. (Bloomberg)

Inverness Medical to restate earnings

Inverness Medical Innovations Inc. will restate earnings downward for all previous periods through 2002 because of an error in calculating deferred taxes. Waltham-based Inverness, which restated 2002 earnings this year following a Securities and Exchange Commission review of an intercompany loan, also cut its earnings outlook for 2005 by 12 cents a share, citing the error. The diagnostics company said restatements will widen its 2002 loss of $4.63 a share by 8 cents, and decrease 2003 earnings of 64 cents a share by 10 cents. The adjustments will widen the loss in each of the first two quarters of 2004 by 4 cents a share, and by 3 cents in the third quarter. (Dow Jones)

London paper buys Waltham company

The publisher of Britain's top-selling midmarket Daily Mail newspaper said it has acquired Lewtan Technologies Inc., of Waltham, for up to $62 million. Lewtan provides analysis and surveillance information on asset-backed securities. Daily Mail & General Trust PLC said the acquisition will bolster its information publishing division. DMGT is paying $34 million in cash, with earn-out payments up to a maximum additional $28 million based on future financial performance. (Dow Jones/AP)

Ex-Bain research librarian indicted

A former research librarian at Bain & Co. has been indicted on charges she embezzled more than $800,000 from the Boston office of the business consulting firm. Jodie Walsh, 31, of Norton, was indicted by a Suffolk County grand jury on three counts of larceny, one count of attempted larceny, and four counts each of uttering a forged discharge for money and making false entries in corporate statements, Massachusetts Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly said. As part of her job, Walsh ordered publications and other documents for the library to be used by Bain employees. The indictment alleges that over six years, beginning in 1997, she created phony invoices for research reports, claiming she paid for them with her own money. She also allegedly faked research reports and requested that checks be sent to a phony publisher. Walsh's attorney declined to comment on the charges specifically but said he expected her to be exonerated. (AP)

Plaintiffs drop 4 Clean Harbors lawsuits

Clean Harbors Inc. said a number of shareholder lawsuits filed in November 2003 would be dropped without the Braintree company making any payments to the plaintiffs. The environmental services company said the suits, by four shareholders, were voluntarily dismissed with prejudice. Clean Harbors was sued by a number of law firms seeking to represent its shareholders after the company said in May 2003 that it would renegotiate its loan covenants because it failed to meet their requirements for earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. Clean Harbors blamed the shortfall on its integration of the Chemical Services Division of Safety-Kleen Corp., a business it acquired in September 2002. (Dow Jones)

IBM reportedly looks to sell PC business

Personal computing pioneer International Business Machines has put its PC business up for sale, according to a published report. IBM long ago ceded the lead in the personal computing market to Dell and Hewlett-Packard to focus on the corporate server and computer services business. The sale is likely to be in the $1 billion to $2 billion range and is expected to include the firm's desktop, laptop, and notebook machines, The New York Times reported in today's editions. People close to the negotiations told the Times that IBM is in talks with Chinese computer maker Lenovo and at least one other potential buyer for the unit.

Marsh finds no wrongdoing in UK unit

Marsh & McLennan Cos.'s UK unit didn't engage in bid-rigging or false quotes, according to a probe commissioned by the world's biggest insurance broker. The investigation did find a potential conflict of interest in fee arrangements the unit had with some insurers that have now been abandoned, law firm Freshfields Bruckhaus Deringer said in a report released by Marsh. The broker hired Freshfields to probe the UK unit after New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer sued Marsh Oct. 14, accusing it of rigging bids in the United States and steering clients to insurers that paid the highest fees. ''We have not found any instances where clients have suffered actual prejudice or disadvantage," the law firm said in the report. (Bloomberg)

Bank of America vice chairman to retire

Bank of America Corp. said vice chairman James Hance, who was chief financial officer for 16 years and helped build the third-biggest US bank through acquisitions, will retire at the end of January. Hance, 60, was replaced as finance chief in April by Marc Oken. Hance joined Bank of America forerunner NCNB Corp. in 1987 and began serving on the board 10 years later. He leaves the board next month. Bank of America chief executive Kenneth Lewis and his predecessor, Hugh McColl, relied on Hance to sift through potential purchases and serve as the main person for talks on more than $100 billion of deals, including the $48 billion acquisition of FleetBoston Financial Corp. (Bloomberg)
