Fidelity fights ruling over board leadership

By Beth Healy and Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  December 3, 2004

In June, when the nation's top securities regulators ruled that every mutual fund board should have an independent chairman, Fidelity Investments said it would ''adapt" to the decision, however unhappily.

But six months later, the Boston mutual fund giant has yet to give up the fight.

In defiance of the US Securities and Exchange Commission, Fidelity chief executive Edward C. Johnson 3d continues to wage a lonely battle with the help of a New Hampshire lawmaker who counts Fidelity employees among his largest contributors. Seeking to undo a rule that would remove Johnson as chairman of the $905 billion fund group his father founded, Republican Senator Judd A. Gregg put his clout to work on Fidelity's behalf, tucking a measure into a recent spending bill that requires the SEC to justify, in a detailed report due May 1, the need for independent chairmen.

It is a long-shot maneuver that has surprised much of the industry, including its key trade association, lawmakers, and even Johnson's friends and allies.

''It's hard for me to understand why Fidelity chose to do this," said Representative Barney Frank, the ranking Democrat on the House Financial Services Committee. ''I am puzzled they would risk angering the chairman of the SEC on this kind of issue. It's one thing to disagree. This is a kind of harassment of the SEC."

Fidelity spokeswoman Anne Crowley said it should be no surprise that Fidelity remains opposed to a requirement that a fund chairman be free of ties to the investment firm managing the funds. She said the company will obey the ruling if it ultimately takes effect, on Jan. 16, 2006 -- the day Johnson would have to step down as chairman -- but in the meantime, Fidelity will stand its ground.

''Our views on the rule are well-known. We said all along that we felt the SEC should have refrained from enacting this," Crowley said. Fidelity argues that mutual fund trustees should be able to choose anyone they see fit to serve as chairman.

William H. Donaldson, the SEC chairman, was unequivocal in his view on the independent-chairman rule, a reform he pressed for, upon the commission's 3-to-2 vote on June 23. He said a fund executive serving as chairman ''finds him or herself in an untenable conflict of interest," and is ''trying to serve two masters."

The two ''masters" are the mutual fund company, which earns fees from customers, and the customers themselves, whose interests are meant to be guarded by independent, paid trustees. Donaldson that day said he hoped Johnson would set aside his personal feelings, for the good of an industry that had been tainted by a market-timing trading scandal in 2003.

Robert C. Pozen, a former top executive at Fidelity who is now chairman at MFS Investment Management in Boston, said the battle has gone too far.

''This is a symbolic fight," Pozen said. ''We should not get hung up on titles."

Rather, he said, fund groups should have a lead independent director set the agenda for meetings. And the independent directors must hold sessions where insiders, like Johnson, are not present, he said.

John Hill, the independent chairman of the Putnam Funds board and a prominent supporter of the new SEC regulations, said he was puzzled by Fidelity's continued fight.

''It's very hard for me to understand what Ned is doing here, other than his daughter is his successor," Hill said, referring to Abigail P. Johnson. ''I guess he's trying to protect her ability to be chairman" of the board of trustees.

For Johnson -- a critic of what he deems over-regulation -- the chairman issue has become a matter of principle, according to industry officials who know him and have followed this fight. After the SEC ruled against his wishes, he had an advocate in Gregg, a senator in a state where Fidelity employs 4,200 people, and an influential member of several Senate committees, including appropriations and one that oversees pension policy.

Fidelity employees and the firm's political action committee have contributed nearly $26,000, including $2,000 from Johnson, to Gregg in the last two years, according to Federal Election Commission records and the Center for Responsive Politics. Fidelity's PAC gave $9,000 to Gregg earlier this year, making him the largest recipient in Congress of Fidelity PAC money in the past two years. Fidelity hosted President Bush at its Merrimack facility during a campaign stop in January, an event that Gregg attended.

Officials in Gregg's office declined to be interviewed. Asked how aggressively Fidelity had pursued an appeal to the rule, Crowley said, ''We've certainly continued to make our views known on the subject."

SEC spokesman Matthew Well this week defended the agency's rule, saying it had entailed an ''extremely involved public process."

A report issued Wednesday by two finance professors at the University of Missouri-Columbia will not help the SEC's case. In a study of 448 mutual fund groups, representing 97 percent of all fund assets, professors Stephen P. Ferris and Xuemin Yan found that independent chairmen did not necessarily lead to lower fees or less chance of fraud.

Said Ferris, ''We found that in neither case could we make an argument that the presence of an independent chairman mattered."

Still, many firms that had originally opposed the independent-chairman measure threw in the towel after the SEC ruling. At Vanguard Group in Valley Forge, Pa., chief executive John J. Brennan plans to step down as chairman of the fund board, spokesman John S. Woerth said. ''We haven't taken any further action."

The Investment Company Institute, the industry's Washington trade group, was not a party to the challenge either, officials there said. Some ICI staff members were stunned at the back-door approach Gregg took in pressing for the amendment -- in an appropriations bill, which has nothing to do with securities regulation.

Representative Frank said if the amendment was meant to send Donaldson a message, ''it backfired."
