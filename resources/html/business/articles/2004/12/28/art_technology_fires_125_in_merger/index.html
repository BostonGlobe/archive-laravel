BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Art Technology fires 125 in merger

December 28, 2004

Art Technology Group Inc., which makes software to help companies process Internet orders and manage customer service, fired 125 workers as it combines with Primus Knowledge Solutions Inc. Payroll was reduced to about 350 employees from 475 as of June 30, and some offices were combined, chief financial officer Ed Terino said. Cuts after the $19 million Primus purchase have lowered annual costs by $20 million, and spending excluding some amortization costs will be $85 million to $86 million, $1 million to $2 million less than forecast. Cambridge-based Art Technology said it expects to be profitable in 2005. The company, whose software is used by companies including Best Buy Co. and American Eagle Outfitters Inc., has lost money in seven of the past 10 quarters, and Primus has lost money every year since 1997. (Bloomberg)

National toy chain to close Revere store

Toys ''R" Us Inc., the second-largest US toy retailer, is closing three US stores, including one in Revere, affecting a total of 100 employees. Staff at stores in Revere, New York City, and Rocky Mount, N.C., will be offered positions at other locations, a spokeswoman for the company said. The retailer plans to convert its Manhattan store into a Babies ''R" Us, giving the company a prime location for a fast-growing division that sells baby furniture, apparel, and accessories. (Wire services)

THE NATION
Ex-Fannie Mae CEO's pension disclosed

Former Fannie Mae chief executive Franklin Raines is eligible for benefits including a monthly salary of $114,393 for life, based on his latest employment agreement. Raines, 55, who was ousted by the government-chartered company last week, would also receive deferred compensation of about $8.7 million and a lifetime of medical and dental coverage for himself and his wife, according to a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission. As of Dec. 21, he had vested stock options for 1.63 million shares, and is slated to receive an additional vested options for an additional 368,819 shares under what the company calls his retirement. Raines's firing came after both the SEC and the company's federal regulator said the company made mistakes in accounting for contracts designed to protect its more than $900 billion portfolio from swings in interest rates. (Bloomberg)

Medtronic unit, Purdue in licensing pact

A Medtronic Inc. subsidiary has signed a licensing agreement with Purdue University, giving it access to polymer technology developed at Purdue that can prevent paralysis in dogs with recent spinal cord injuries. Medtronic Sofamor Danek hopes its agreement with the Purdue Research Foundation leads to human clinical trials to test whether the polymer can help human spinal cord patients avoid permanent paralysis, company spokesman Patrick Hirigoyen said. Financial terms were not disclosed. Considerable research still lies ahead to refine polyethylene glycol, or PEG, for possible use in humans on hours- or days-old spinal injuries, he said. Depending on research progress and regulatory approval, human clinical trials are at least two years away, Hirigoyen said. If those trials succeed, it would take at least three more years to obtain approval for commercial use of PEG. (AP)

Research firm to acquire Meta Group

Gartner Inc., which sells technology-industry research, agreed to buy Meta Group Inc. for $162 million to add to the information it offers. Shareholders of Meta Group, which had $122.5 million in sales last year, will receive $10 for each share held. That is 54 percent more than Meta's closing price of $6.48 on Dec. 23. Gartner said its first acquisition in more than two years will bolster its research capabilities and sales staff. Gartner, which had sales of $858.5 million last year, does not expect the purchase to increase earnings in 2005. The deal is expected to close in the second quarter. Shares of Meta Group rose $3.34, or 52 percent, to $9.82. Jamie Tully, who handles public relations for Gartner at Citigate Sard Verbinnen, declined to comment on the transaction. (Bloomberg)

Gasoline prices drop to 8-month low

US retail gasoline prices fell 2.4 cents the past week to $1.791 a gallon, the lowest in more than eight months, the Energy Department said in a weekly report. The latest nationwide average was the eighth consecutive decline and the lowest since $1.786 on April 12, the department said. Gasoline is down 12 percent from $2.034 at the beginning of November. US diesel-fuel prices rose 0.3 cent to $1.987 a gallon, leaving prices 48.5 cents higher than a year ago, the department said. The average price of a gallon of unleaded regular reached a record $2.212 on Oct. 25. (Bloomberg)

Satellite radio companies report gains

Sirius Satellite Radio Inc. and XM Satellite Radio Holdings Inc., the two big players in the emerging satellite radio business, both revealed gains in subscribers as 2004 draws to a close. XM, the larger of the two, said it had recently surpassed 3.1 million subscribers, having started the year with 1.3 million. Sirius said it had passed the 1 million mark, having started 2004 with about 260,000. Competition between the two has been heating up fiercely over the past year, with each one signing contracts for sports and talk programming. Both have enlisted help from automakers in marketing the services, under which users pay a monthly fee for coast-to-coast access to hundreds of channels. (AP)

AOL subscribers getting less spam

The world's largest Internet service provider believes spammers are starting to give up -- at least when it comes to sending junk to its subscribers. The total number of e-mail messages destined for America Online Inc. members averaged 1.6 billion daily in November, down from 2.1 billion daily a year earlier, the company said. The drop was almost entirely in spam, AOL said. AOL spokesman Nicholas Graham attributed the drop largely to spammers moving on after realizing that many of their messages won't get through AOL's antispam filters or that they might get sued for trying. During the same period, the number of spam complaints from members dropped more than 75 percent, after adjusting for a decline in subscribers. AOL had 22.7 million US subscribers as of September, compared with 24.7 million a year earlier, according to the latest regulatory filings by parent Time Warner Inc. AOL also has seen declines in spam messages deleted at AOL's gateways as well as those diverted to spam folders by filters under the control of AOL members. (AP)

Apple sues three software developers

Apple Computer Inc., the maker of iPod digital-music players and Macintosh computers, sued three software developers, claiming they distributed unreleased versions of its OS X computer operating system on the Internet. The lawsuit, filed in federal court in San Francisco last week, seeks to stop illegal distribution of Apple products over the Web. It follows a similar suit filed in California state court this month. In the latest suit, Apple said its financial performance depends on its ability to protect improvements to its operating system, used in its PowerBook, iMac, and other computers. Apple's operating systems represent a ''minority share" in the personal computer market, ''which is dominated by makers of computers using other operating systems," the company said in the lawsuit. Through its Apple Developer Connection program, the company provides unreleased versions of programs to software developers, who must sign agreements prohibiting them from sharing keys that allow them to download the software. The agreements also prohibit distribution of the downloaded Apple products. (Bloomberg)
