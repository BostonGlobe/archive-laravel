BUSINESS IN BRIEF

SBC Communications unit buys Yantra

December 22, 2004

Yantra Corp. of Tewksbury, a privately held software company specializing in order management and supply chain fulfillment applications, was acquired by Sterling Commerce, an SBC Communications Inc. subsidiary, for about $170 million in cash. The deal is expected to be completed in the first quarter of 2005. Yantra was founded in 1995 as a spinoff of Infosys Technologies Ltd. It employs more than 250 people worldwide who will continue to work for Sterling, a collaboration software company. (Robert Weisman)

Profit rises 24% for Bedford company

Progress Software Corp., a maker of business applications, said its profit grew 24 percent in the latest quarter, boosted by higher software sales during the period. Shares rose 99 cents, or 4.3 percent, to $23.75. The Bedford company posted fourth-quarter income of $10.9 million, or 28 cents per share, up from $8.8 million, or 23 cents, in the year-ago period. Excluding amortization charges, Progress said its adjusted earnings in the latest quarter would have been $12.1 million, or 31 cents a share. That compares with estimates of 29 cents per share, according to a Thomson First Call survey of analysts. Revenue for the period was $96.2 million, a gain of 17 percent from $82 million the year before. (AP)

Acquisition of Ilex Oncology completed

Genzyme Corp. of Cambridge completed its acquisition of Ilex Oncology Inc. of San Antonio for $1 billion in stock. The firm said it hired most of Ilex's employees and will maintain offices in San Antonio. Ilex sells Campath as a treatment for types of leukemia that have not responded to other treatments. An FDA review panel recommended that one of Ilex's lead drug candidates, Clolar, be approved for a type of childhood leukemia, and a decision is expected by year-end. Genzyme said it would focus on advancing clinical trials and expand its cancer sales and marketing operation. (Jeffrey Krasner)

Ex-Serono regional director pleads guilty

A former regional director of Serono Inc. pleaded guilty in a federal court to offering kickbacks to three New York City doctors for writing prescriptions for the company's AIDS drug Serostim, the US attorney's office said. Serostim is used for the treatment wasting, a condition involving profound weight loss. The US attorney's office said Adam Stupak worked for the Serono unit responsible for selling Serostim and offered the doctors an all-expenses paid trip to attend a medical conference in Cannes, France, in exchange for writing a certain number of Serostim prescriptions. (Dow Jones)

Biogen Idec to begin Tysabri study

Biogen Idec Inc. and Elan Corp. PLC said they are starting a study comparing their new multiple sclerosis drug Tysabri to Serono SA's Rebif, an older treatment. Biogen Idec and Elan will include more than 1,000 patients in the study and will start enrolling people in the first quarter of 2005, Elan spokeswoman Anita Kawatra said. The companies expect results from the study in the fourth quarter of 2006, Kawatra said. Tysabri, which Elan developed with Biogen Idec, won US approval in November. It is the first new type of drug in eight years to treat multiple sclerosis. Analysts expect the drug to generate sales of more than $3 billion. (Bloomberg)

GTech reports earnings are unchanged

Earnings at GTech Holdings Corp., the world's largest operator of lottery systems, were unchanged in the latest quarter from a year earlier, as higher costs offset revenue growth. Shares fell $2.29, or 8.1 percent, to $26.07. The West Greenwich, R.I.-based company said it earned $45.9 million, or 35 cents a share, on revenue of $315.6 million in the fiscal third quarter ended Nov. 27. The results matched the forecast of analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call. In the same period last year, it had the same earnings on revenue of $254.9 million, but the earnings included a one-time gain that wasn't repeated. In the nine months ended Nov. 27, the company earned $152.6 million, or $1.16 a share, on revenue of $919.4 million, compared to earnings of $135.4 million, or $1.06 a share, on revenue of $771.8 million last year. (Dow Jones/AP)

NMS Communications CFO to resign

NMS Communications Corp. said its chief financial officer, William Gerraughty, will resign as senior vice president of finance and chief financial officer in mid-March. He came to NMS in May 2003 to help complete its financial turnaround. During his time at NMS, the Framingham concern returned to profitability, completed a public stock financing, and launched two products aimed at wireless markets. NMS couldn't immediately be reached to comment. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION
Fitch lowers Marsh & McLennan rating

Marsh & McLennan Cos. had the ratings of some of its debt lowered by Fitch Ratings to one step above speculative grade. The downgrade, disclosed after markets closed Monday, lowered some of the senior debt of the world's largest insurance brokerage firm by one notch to triple-B-minus, the lowest investment-grade rating, from triple-B. Marsh, the corporate parent of Putnam Investments, is girding for what might be a steep settlement of a civil suit by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer. Spitzer alleged that Marsh cheated clients by rigging bids for insurance contracts and steering business to insurers who paid Marsh large so-called contingent commissions, which Spitzer likened to kickbacks. Fitch put all of Marsh's ratings on watch for further possible downgrade (Dow Jones/AP)

Tenet agrees to pay $395m to patients

Tenet Healthcare Corp. said it has agreed in principle to pay $395 million to settle lawsuits claiming patients at Redding Medical Center, one of its former hospitals in California, received unnecessary heart procedures. Under the agreement, Tenet will establish a $395 million settlement fund by year-end that will be distributed among more than 750 plaintiffs. The lawsuits stemmed from allegations that doctors had performed unnecessary cardiac catheterizations and bypass surgeries while practicing at the medical center before November 2002. Separate lawsuits against those physicians were not included in the settlement, Tenet said. (AP)

Stock funds expected to rake in $330b

US stock funds, including mutual funds, closed-end funds, underlying variable annuities and index funds, and exchange-traded funds are on track to take in a net $330 billion this year, topping the record $307 billion of inflows during 2000, fund research firm Strategic Insight estimated. Strategic Insight said it expects further gains for flows to non-domestic funds in 2005. Bond funds are expected to take in a net $6 billion this year, down sharply from $59 billion in 2003. Money market funds are expected to have net outflows of $158 billion this year -- less than the $226 billion of outflows last year. (Reuters)

Dallas Federal Reserve names president

The Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas named a former federal trade official and current business associate of former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger as president. Richard W. Fisher will take over April 4, the bank said. He succeeds Robert McTeer, who resigned last month to become chancellor of the Texas A&M University system. Fisher, 55, who ran unsuccessfully for the US Senate from Texas in 1993, will lead one of the 12 regional Federal Reserve banks. Fisher will also sit on the Federal Open Market Committee, which sets the nation's monetary policy. He will become a voting member next year and every third year after that. (AP)
