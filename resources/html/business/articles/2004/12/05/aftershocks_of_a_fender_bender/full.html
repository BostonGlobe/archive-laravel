CONSUMER BEAT

Aftershocks of a fender bender

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  December 5, 2004

In Massachusetts, where the average annual auto insurance premium is nearly $1,100 and drivers are involved in more accidents than anywhere else in the country, a claim for $4,000 barely raises an eyebrow.

ADVERTISEMENT

But for Stephen Walkauskas of South Boston that's exactly the point. What for him began as a fender bender in a retailer's parking lot in Avon quickly mushroomed into a claim for roughly $4,000 in repairs, more than the value of the 11-year-old car damaged in the accident.

''I now understand why insurance rates are so high in Massachusetts," Walkauskas said. ''Sure, the roads could be better engineered, drivers could be better educated, and laws could be better enforced. But the real problem is what motivates the insurance companies in this system."

Walkauskas says his ordeal has taught him that insurance companies have very little incentive to scrutinize minor claims because the costs of those claims just get passed along to consumers in the form of higher auto insurance rates. In fact, Walkauskas says, the system encourages insurance companies to process claims as fast as possible with as few employees as possible.

With an eye toward change, state regulators, lawmakers, and industry officials are currently studying the state's heavily regulated auto insurance system from every angle. But rarely do they get the perspective of a typical consumer who has experienced the system firsthand.

Walkauskas is not an auto insurance expert, but the 30-year-old software engineer is organized, articulate, and very determined.

He kept asking questions about the handling of the fender bender claim when most consumers would have given up and taken out their checkbook.

''I felt like I really couldn't complain about the system unless I tried to do something about it," he said.

The accident occurred on Aug. 11. Walkauskas's wife was backing out of a parking space at Jordan's Furniture in Avon and collided with a car driven by a woman from North Easton. The woman driving the other car did not return phone calls.

Under the standards of fault used by insurance companies and state regulators, the accident was determined to be the fault of Walkauskas's wife. According to the standards, it is presumed that any driver who is leaving a parking space and hits another vehicle is at fault.

The driver at fault in an accident is hit with surcharge points that can increase premiums substantially for six years. Three surcharge points are assessed for minor accidents, which involve $500 to $2,000 in total damage after deductibles are subtracted. Four surcharge points are assessed for major accidents, those involving more than $2,000 in total damage after deductibles are subtracted.

Walkauskas says he pays about $1,100 annually for auto insurance and estimates that the accident could increase his premium by several hundred dollars a year.

Initially, the damage from the accident appeared to be minimal. The repair estimate for Walkauskas's 1999 Toyota Camry was $418, below his $500 deductible.

But the other driver submitted a claim for $1,778, and that was followed after repair work began by two supplemental appraisals, one for $724 for several new parts and another for $944 for the installation of a rebuilt starter. Finally, there was a $619 bill for a rental car for 23 days.

The four claims came to a total of $4,065. Walkauskas did some checking on the Internet and discovered that the Kelley Blue Book value of the other driver's car, a 1993, two-door convertible Chrysler LeBaron with more than 100,000 miles on the odometer, came to about $1,705.

Why, he wondered, was his insurer, Amica Mutual Insurance Co., paying out more in claims than the car was worth?

Walkauskas raised questions about the size and nature of the claims, particularly the new starter. He said the driver of the other car had told his wife that she had had problems starting her car even before the accident.

Walkauskas said the Amica representative he was dealing with had little interest in challenging the claims.

Walkauskas asked Amica for pictures of the other driver's car. Initially, he said, the Amica representative told him the pictures were so dark and grainy that it was very difficult to tell what you were looking at.

''I found this amusing," Walkauskas said. ''Why would Amica pay $3,000 if the damage isn't even visible?"

Walkauskas eventually did receive copies of some pictures of the other car. The pictures showed a large dent on the side of the car near the front bumper.

Frustrated with Amica's reluctance to challenge the claims, Walkauskas wrote a two-page e-mail to the Globe, voicing frustration with his claims situation and the auto insurance system in general. The Globe forwarded the e-mail to Amica seeking comment.

Amica did not respond to the Globe, but Walkauskas said that after the newspaper's inquiry a new official at the company began handling his case. Walkauskas said the official forwarded appraisals for the various claims to him and began investigating them more thoroughly.

According to Walkauskas, the Amica official, using different guidelines, estimated the value of the 1993 LeBaron at between $2,495 and $2,699, significantly higher than the Kelley Blue Book value but still below the combined repair estimates.

Walkauskas said the Amica representative eventually informed him that the company would not be paying the other driver's supplemental claims for new parts and a new starter. It is unclear whether Amica and the other driver's insurer, Commerce Insurance of Webster, are now battling over who should pay those claims.

An Amica spokeswoman said last week she could not comment about the details of a specific case, but noted that Walkauskas was satisfied with the explanations he had received from the company. A Commerce official declined comment.

Walkauskas is now planning to appeal the ruling that his wife was at fault in the accident. He has tried repeatedly to obtain surveillance videotapes of the Jordan's Furniture parking lot to see for himself how the accident occurred, but Jordan's has refused to release them.

If he is unable to challenge the circumstances of the accident itself, he said he will probably challenge the overall surcharge system.

He said it's silly that $2,000 is the threshold for a major accident, a limit that hasn't been changed in more than a decade despite significant increases in repair and parts costs over that time.

Walkauskas also thinks it's strange that surcharge penalties are the same for an accident with $2,001 in property damage and an accident costing $40,000, or that a drunk driver gets slapped with just one more surcharge point than the penalty he and his wife are facing for her fender bender.

State regulators may want to invite Walkauskas in for a chat. They are currently reviewing proposals for changing the surcharge system.
