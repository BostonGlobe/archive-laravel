Wired to sell

Toy makers hope gadgets energize a flat industry, but some educators worry kids lose out amid the hype

By Ricki Morell, Globe Correspondent  |  December 5, 2004

It's been a long time since Elmo, the ''Sesame Street" stuffed animal, just sat on the shelf and looked cuddly.

ADVERTISEMENT

Eight years ago, Fisher-Price's giggly Tickle Me Elmo, became the must-have toy that flew off the shelves. Since then, Elmo has done, among other things, the Hokey-Pokey and the Chicken Dance. This year, Elmo combines two trends -- high-tech and retro -- by disco-dancing and singing ''E-L-M-O" to the tune of the 1970s hit song ''Y.M.C.A."

''Elmo doesn't just sing the song, he actually bends over and makes the letters with his body," said toy guru Jim Silver, whose Toy Wishes magazine has been putting out a ''Hot Dozen" toy list since 1999 and includes Elmo on this year's list. ''Kids are on computers at age 3. They have so much technology around, you have to wow a kid more than you used to."

This season, toy makers and retailers are hoping that excitement over high-tech gadgets will inject energy into a listless toy industry that is losing momentum to video games. Toy sales totaled about $20.7 billion in 2003 and are expected to remain flat this year, according to figures compiled by the NPD research firm and the Toy Industry Association, a trade group. Meanwhile, sales of video games, which are not counted as toys, totaled about $10.2 billion in 2003 and are expected to grow by 4 to 9 percent this year.

But what's good for the toy industry may not be good for kids, say educators and child development specialists. Electronic toys can rob young children of their ability to imagine and pretend. They can be overstimulating and limiting at the same time, so that after the initial thrill wears off, children get bored.

''The creators of these toys, I think, have good intentions and that's the saddest part," said Rhonda Clements, an education professor at Hofstra University in Hempstead, N.Y., and president of the American Association for the Child's Right to Play, a group made up of teachers, pediatricians, and parents. ''These electronic toys have no play value. Children tire of them quickly."

Adds David Elkind, a professor of child development at Tufts University and author of ''The Hurried Child": ''They're trying to expose kids to the virtual world before they learn about the real world. The best set of toys for a preschooler is a big set of blocks and they're getting harder and harder to find."

Still, high-tech toys sell. A November poll by the National Retail Federation trade association found that video games were the number one toy that parents planned to buy for boys this year. A 2004 NPD Group study showed that, by age 9, boys turn to video games and away from traditional toys.

The study also found that children ages 5 to 12 spend more than four hours a week playing video games. Nearly half of the children in the study began playing video games between the ages of 4 and 5, with 20 percent beginning at age 3 or younger.

''Video games really seem to be gaining heat," said NPD entertainment analyst Anita Frazier. ''The toy industry is recognizing the impact of video games and incorporating those features. The more electronic bells and whistles, the better."

The Toy Wishes ''Hot Dozen" reflects this pressure on the industry. Only a few toys on the list seem to have escaped mechanized mania, including ''retro" toys such as the Cabbage Patch Kids, the chubby-cheeked dolls that were first introduced more than 20 years ago. Nostalgia toys appeal to parents who remember the toys when they were young.

But Zahira Wilson, 8, a Roxbury second-grader, didn't see the appeal. She jiggled the doll's arms for a few seconds before putting it down and losing interest. ''You couldn't really do anything with it," she said. ''It just lays there."

Even Barbie, who has made the list every year, isn't special enough this year without a high-tech element. Press a button on her back and she sings the score to the computer-animated movie, ''The Princess and the Pauper." She also sings a duet with another Barbie.

The Vtech V.Smile, aimed at 3- to 7-year-olds, is expected to be popular. It's a video game for preschoolers that teaches language, math, and problem-solving skills. ''Even the youngest kids are fascinated by video games but sometimes they're just too small to play. No more!" explains the list's description of the toy.

Charlestown parent David Jacoby can't imagine letting his 6-year-old son play with video games, and certainly not his 4-year-old daughter.

''I don't think it's age appropriate," Jacoby said. ''A good game stimulates intellect. They can spend hours in front of a video game and not tell you what they just did."

As children grow up, it gets hard to shield them from video games as technology and computers become part of everyday life. For example, Diane Levin, a professor of early childhood education at Boston's Wheelock College and an outspoken critic of developmentally inappropriate toys, said even she couldn't shield her now-adult son from the lure of Game Boy when it came out years ago. After much negotiation, she bought him one, but limited the time he could use it.

''Video games are going to get in kids' lives no matter what we do," Levin said. ''My position is it's better to keep them out as long as possible. And, as it gets in, there's a lot we can do to keep it appropriate."

Toy Wishes' Silver says just because a toy is high-tech doesn't mean it's bad for kids.

''I find educators to be against any toys that are not basic toys," he said. ''A toy that allows for imaginary play defeats the purpose if the child won't play with it. If all their friends have a dancing Elmo and they don't, they're not going to play with the Elmo that just sits there."

That's what drives toy maker Fisher-Price, a subsidiary of Mattel Inc. in El Segundo, Calif., to invent even more mechanized Elmo dolls.

''With the advent of the first Tickle Me Elmo came a realization that there is something special and intriguing that parents are looking for, and that the gift-giver wants," said Kathleen Alfano, director of child research at Fisher-Price. ''There's something special about toys that come to life."

After the Tickle Me Elmo doll craze eight years ago, Fisher-Price felt pressure to keep up the momentum with even flashier Elmos.

''There's a lot of internal discussion about what can Elmo do next?" said Alfano, who has a doctorate in early childhood and elementary education. ''It's an expectation that we'll have an Elmo that knocks your socks off."
