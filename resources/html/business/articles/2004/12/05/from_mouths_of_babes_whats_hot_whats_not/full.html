From mouths of babes: What's hot, what's not

By Ricki Morell, Globe Correspondent  |  December 5, 2004

How hot are the ''Hot Dozen" toys?

ADVERTISEMENT

To find out, The Boston Globe put six toys from the 2004 Toy Wishes magazine Hot Dozen list in front of 13 kids from the Charlestown Boys and Girls Club. The kids, from 6 to 8 years old, spent an hour last week playing with toys ranging from a Cabbage Patch doll to Tamagotchi Connection virtual pets.

The staff of Toy Wishes, a magazine that bills itself as ''a family guide to entertainment," unveiled its 2004 list in October, after spending a year sifting through tens of thousands of toys. It took 7-year-old Taylor Adams a few seconds to figure out what was hot and what was not.

''Is that a Bratz?" she cried urgently, leaning toward the high-fashion doll. ''I want it!"

The other toy that kids couldn't resist? Ms. Pac-Man, a video game throwback to the '70s.

The list attempts to predict which toys will be so popular that they'll be hard to find by Christmas. About half of all toy sales take place during the holiday shopping season. Aside from the obvious -- whether the toys are fun to play with -- the magazine staff judges products on their novelty, price, and whether they are connected to a popular brand name or TV show. A candidate for Hot Dozen must have broad appeal.

''Our definition of a hot toy isn't the biggest seller," said Jim Silver, the magazine's editor. ''But the one that people are fighting over in the parking lot."

Reyne Rice, a trend specialist with the Toy Industry Association, says the hot-toy list helps create a buzz that spurs sales. ''It instantly raises the profile of a toy," she said.

Cranium, the small Seattle company whose board game, Balloon Lagoon, is part of the Hot Dozen this year, has already seen results from being on the list. Rice said company executives told her that sales were five times higher the week after the list came out than the week before.

The Bratz Tokyo-A-Go-Go Dance N' Skate Club created the biggest stir among the girls. The $89.99 plastic contraption includes a revolving DJ booth that the Bratz doll, which looks like a Barbie on steroids, can stand in while wearing a headset.

''This is awesome," said Laura Reyes, 7, as she stared at a tray of tiny plastic accessories. Even the boys seemed drawn to the Bratz play set, partly because of its electronic component. One boy even admitted that he liked it, before succumbing to peer pressure and saying it was ''yucky."

Ms. Pac-Man was a winner that crossed gender lines. The simple and relatively cheap ($20) video game was easy to set up and use.

''It's really fun to do, and it's really fun to get the little thing that eats you," said Elizabeth Pardy, 7."You get to do a lot of shortcuts," said Ryan Morrissey, 7, as he manipulated the joystick and stared at the maze on the screen.

Another video-type game that plugs into the TV, the VTech V.Smile, didn't go over as well -- even though Silver predicts it will be one of the most popular toys on the Hot Dozen. It was more difficult to set up, and the activities didn't grab the kids.

Balloon Lagoon was a hit, even though its most high-tech element is a musical timer Merry-Go-Round. Kids liked the colorful game board and the hands-on activities at the pretend carnival, including fishing for letters and flipping frogs in a pond.

''It really had challenges and I like that," said Alex Jacoby, 6.

A Cabbage Patch doll and the Tamogotchi Connection virtual pets, which look like a small Game Boy on a keychain, generally got a thumbs-down from this bunch.

The virtual pets, suggested for kids ages 8-14, may have been too complicated for them. ''How can you keep something alive that's not alive?" asked a frustrated Caroline Rogers, 7. ''I hate this!"

As for the Cabbage Patch doll, most everyone rejected its simplicity. Reggie started spanking it. Laura said, ''She scares me. She has big cheeks."

Only Caroline admitted to really wanting to play with it. ''I liked it," she said, ''because you could pretend it was anything."

The 'Hot Dozen'

Balloon Lagoon (Cranium, ages 5 years and up, $19.95) A board game with a carnival theme and a musical timer.

Barbie as Princess AnneLiese and Erika (Mattel, ages 3 years and up, $19.99) Each doll sings songs from the Barbie computer-animated movie, ''The Princess and the Pauper."

Bella Dancerella (Spin Master, ages 3 to 8 years, $29.99) A ''ballet studio" that includes costumes, accessories, and a video.Bratz Tokyo-A-Go-Go Dance N' Skate Club (MGA, ages 3 years and up, $89.99) This club play set includes a lit dance floor, revolving DJ booth, roller rink, speakers, and a Bratz doll.

Cabbage Patch Kids (Play Along, ages 3 years and up, $29.99) Kids can still officially ''adopt" these chubby-cheeked dolls, which were introduced more than 20 years ago.

E-L-M-O (Fisher-Price, ages 18 months and up, $29.99) ''Sesame Street" character Elmo is back, dancing and singing to the tune of the disco-era hit, ''Y.M.C.A."

Interactv (Fisher-Price, ages 3 years and up, $39.99) Kids watch DVDs of their favorite characters (like Barney or Dora the Explorer) and use the electronic tablet to play along.

Ms. Pac-Man TV Games (JAKKS Pacific, ages 5 years and up, $20) This '70s-era video game plugs into the TV and includes such classic games as Ms. Pac-Man, Galaga, Pole Position, Xevious, and Mappy.

Nitro Battlerz (Radica, ages 8 years and up, $39.99) Radio-controlled cars that you can build, race, and crash.

Tamagotchi Connection (Bandai, ages 8 to 14 years, $14.99) The virtual pet is back. These little electronic devices allow owners to interact wirelessly with other owners and do more interactive play.

Videonow Color (Hasbro, ages 6 years and up, $75) This is a kid's personal video player, with full-color videos and portable design.

VTech V.Smile (VTech, ages 3 to 7 years, $59.99) A video game for preschoolers that connects to the TV and teaches language, math, and problem-solving skills.
