Banknorth-TD Bank merger gets backing

December 17, 2004

Banknorth Group's largest shareholder, Private Capital Management, said it plans to vote in favor of the merger with Canada's TD Bank Financial Group after expressing reservations several months ago. The change increases the likelihood the merger will go through. Several of Banknorth's major shareholders had reservations because of the deal's structure. TD plans to acquire a majority stake in the Maine bank, but Banknorth will remain quasi-independent, and its stock will be publicly traded. TD has said it plans to buy the other shares later. In most bank mergers, the acquiring bank buys all the shares at once. In its regulatory filing, Private Capital Management owns about 9 percent of Banknorth stock. The merger is expected to be approved early in 2005, pending regulatory clearances and a shareholder vote. (Sasha Talcott)

Verizon gets OK to end 6 calling plans

Massachusetts utility regulators, after a one-month delay, agreed to let Verizon Communications Inc. phase out six of its local telephone calling plans in Eastern Massachusetts. Existing customers who have plans such as Bay State East and Metropolitan will keep them, but new customers will not be able to sign up for the plans. Verizon said the plans it is phasing out attract very few customers and it wants to cut costs by standardizing local calling offers in the 29 states where it provides local landline service. Verizon wanted the changes to take effect Nov. 22, but the Department of Telecommunications and Energy delayed ruling while it considered, but ultimately rejected, a challenge by AT&T Corp. on technical pricing grounds. (Peter J. Howe)

Gaiety Theatre demolition on hold

The Massachusetts Land Court heard arguments that it should stop a developer from demolishing the defunct Gaiety Theatre, near Chinatown. The Gaiety's owner, Kensington Investment Co., recently was issued a demolition permit. But a Gaiety neighbor, the Glass Slipper strip club, asserted it would be harmed by the theater's demolition. According to the Glass Slipper, the city zoning code allows the demolition of theaters in the theater district only under special circumstances. But, Kensington's lawyers claimed the Glass Slipper failed to demonstrate that it would be harmed by the demolition. Kensington wants to replace the Gaiety and the Glass Slipper with an apartment building. Judge Keith C. Long said he would issue a ruling next week after Kensington agreed not to take any action until then. (Chris Reidy)

Five Fenway apartment buildings sold

Alpha Management of Boston has purchased five rental apartment buildings in the Fenway from a partnership managed by Gould & Co., said Apartment Realty Advisors, which helped negotiate the $32 million sale. In total, the buildings have about 200 units. Rents for the units range roughly from $928 for studios to $2,075 for three bedrooms. The addresses for the buildings in the transaction are 132-136 Hemenway St., 204 Hemenway St., 97 St. Stephen St., 109 St. Stephen St., and 115 St. Stephen St. (Chris Reidy)

Bank of America selling 3 Latin units

Bank of America Corp., the third-biggest US bank, agreed to sell its commercial bank and consumer lending unit in Panama to Banco General SA and plans to sell units in Peru and Colombia. Bank of America decided to sell the businesses after reviewing its Latin American operations. Terms of the Panama transaction weren't disclosed. Bank of America expanded in Latin America when it acquired FleetBoston Financial Corp. for $48 billion in April. The bank also has offices in Argentina, Brazil, Chile, and Uruguay. (Bloomberg)

Marlborough company cuts loss 65%

3Com Corp., which makes switches that direct traffic on corporate data networks, said its second-quarter loss fell to $48.8 million after the company cut costs by slashing 37 percent of all jobs. The loss was 13 cents a share in the period ended Nov. 26, down from $139 million, or 37 cents a share, a year ago, the Marlborough company said. Sales fell 17 percent to $151.1 million from $181.9 million in the comparable year-ago period. (Bloomberg)

State Street wins Singapore contract

Government of Singapore Investment Corp., which manages $100 billion of the city-state's reserves, hired State Street Corp. to provide investment services for fixed-income and equity trading in the United States and Canada. State Street, the world's biggest service provider to institutional investors, will offer settlement services for the company's trading business in North America and Canada, said a spokesman for Government of Singapore Investment. Separately, State Street raised its quarterly dividend by 1 cent a share to 17 cents. (Bloomberg)

Airline, flight attendants in tentative deal

US Airways Group Inc. reached a tentative deal with negotiators for its flight attendants union for $94 million in yearly concessions to help the bankrupt carrier avoid liquidation, union and company officials said. Terms were not released pending review by the executive committee of the carrier's chapter of the Association of Flight Attendants, which represents 5,200 workers and must approve any deal. Also, the full membership must vote. The sides had been discussing pay cuts of roughly 10 percent and work rule changes. The company still wants additional savings of nearly $60 million from flight attendant pensions and medical benefits for retirees. (Reuters)

ATA, Southwest in deal for Midway gates

ATA Airlines said it has accepted a $117 million offer from Southwest Airlines for the transfer of six of ATA's 14 gates at Chicago's Midway airport for $40 million and $47 million in financing. Southwest would also invest $30 million in notes. The deal would increase Southwest's dominant presence at Chicago's Midway International Airport and provide ATA the capital to recreate itself as it emerges from Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection. A federal bankruptcy judge in Indianapolis was expected to approve the bid, officials with both airlines said. (AP)
