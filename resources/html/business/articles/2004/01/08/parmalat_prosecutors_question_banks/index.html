Parmalat prosecutors question banks

Investigators probe institutions' role in company scandal

By Associated Press  |  January 8, 2004

ROME -- Prosecutors in the Parmalat case reportedly spoke to representatives of international banks yesterday, as investigators studied whether the institutions knew the real state of the finances of the food giant before it made a stunning admission of missing billions.

In Milan, investigating magistrates spoke to Citigroup lawyer Nerio Dioda. Prosecutors in Parma, in their third day of interrogations with former Parmalat chief financial officer Fausto Tonna, also spoke with four people whom Italian reports identified as Deutsche Bank officials.

Italian news reports say Tonna has discussed with prosecutors the relations of several international banks with Parmalat, and that he cited Bank of America, Citigroup, and JP MorganChase, among others.

Italian authorities have not accused any of wrongdoing in the case.

Citigroup spokeswoman Susan Tether indicated from London that the company had proposed yesterday's meeting with Milan prosecutors, but she refused to comment on the talks.

Citigroup has conducted financing and advisory work for Parmalat, and says it will help authorities with any Parmalat inquiries.

This week, a pension fund cited Citigroup in its investor suit against jailed Parmalat founder and former chairman Calisto Tanzi and Tonna. The suit alleges the former company officials, Citigroup, and outside auditors and attorneys hatched a deceptive scheme to raise more than $5 billion through sales of securities.

Citigroup is accused of having created a complex financial structure dubbed "Buconero," Italian for "black hole," allegedly used by Parmalat executives to hide debt. Citigroup said Tuesday that the suit had no merit, adding that "regrettably, the legal vehicle associated with this transaction was named Buconero."

The ANSA news agency said four Deutsche Bank officials met with Parma prosecutors yesterday after flying in from London. Investigators there are looking into a bond offering issued by Parmalat that involved the German institution, as well as Deutsche Bank's sale of a stake in the company, the news agency reported.

Italian media say Deutsche Bank's stake in Parmalat dropped on Dec. 19, the day the Italian company acknowledged it didn't have a multibillion-dollar Bank of America account as it had earlier claimed -- an admission that set off the scandal. The timing of the reduction could indicate how much the German bank knew of the company's financial problems.

In Frankfurt, bank spokesman Klaus Winker refused to comment on the reported Parma meeting.

The bank did, however, acknowledge that it had held a stake of 5.157 percent in Parmalat that "later" was reduced to 1.57 percent, but did not specify when it made the move. Bank officials refused to elaborate, saying they were cooperating with regulatory authorities.

Meanwhile, Italians who lost money in Parmalat stocks and bonds clamored for justice. Citizens swamped Milan prosecutors with e-mail requests -- some hoping to recoup losses, others simply offering gratitude.

Judicial authorities in Milan discussed yesterday how to help those who lost money, while a national consumers' rights group announced a demonstration would be held in Rome later this month.

Also yesterday, leaders of both houses of Italy's parliament announced that a joint commission would investigate relations between businesses, financial markets, and regulators, with results by the end of next month.
