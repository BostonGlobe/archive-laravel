Weill disciples Dimon, Prince to be rivals at two biggest banks

By Edward Robinson, Bloomberg News  |  January 25, 2004

On a Saturday evening in September, Citigroup Inc. chairman Sanford Weill and his one-time protege, Jamie Dimon, gathered at the Pierre Hotel in New York to celebrate a wedding.

The groom: Weill disciple Charles Prince, 54, whom Weill had tapped in July to take over as chief executive of Citigroup, the largest financial company in the world. Dimon, by contrast, had been fired by Weill from his post as Citigroup president in 1998 and became chief executive of Bank One Corp. in Chicago.

As the guests sipped champagne under the antique chandeliers of the Grand Ballroom that evening, Dimon, 47, was seeking a marriage of his own -- one that would put him in direct competition with Prince, his former colleague.

With the planned sale of Bank One to J.P. Morgan Chase & Co., revealed Jan. 14, Dimon is in line to become chief executive of an institution with $1.08 trillion in assets, second only to Citigroup, with $1.21 trillion. Under the terms of the deal, Dimon will succeed William Harrison as J.P. Morgan chief executive in 2006.

The acquisition, for about $55.1 billion in stock, is the biggest bank merger since 1998 -- and a testament to the vision of Weill, 70.

With Dimon and Prince at his side, Weill built Citigroup into a financial supermarket offering commercial and investment banking, insurance, and money management services. In doing so, he changed the way Wall Street does business.

Now Dimon and Prince will be rivals.

Prince, a lawyer, has worked behind the scenes at Citigroup since he succeeded Dimon as Weill's deputy in 1998. "Prince is the quiet soldier who always does what Sandy asks," says Vincent Kondaveeti, an analyst at Circle T Partners.

By offering a wide range of services to corporations and consumers, Citigroup has been able to attract investment banking business. Weill's company, for example, has advanced to the number two equity underwriter with an 11 percent market share, from number five, with a 6.7 percent market share in 1999.

Dimon took a page from his mentor's playbook as he explained his strategy at a news conference after the merger was unveiled. "We will be bigger and stronger and have more products and services," he said. "Why have a Chevy if you can have a Ferrari?"

A native New Yorker whose grandfather and father were stockbrokers, Dimon clashed with Weill amid the tensions of merging Travelers Insurance Group Inc. and Citicorp Inc. in 1998. Weill fired him later that year after Dimon passed over Weill's daughter, Jessica Bibliowicz, for a promotion.

Jerry Speyer, the chief executive of New York real estate developer Tishman Speyer Properties, says Weill has tutored both men well.

"They have completely different personalities, but they are both gifted executives who don't just cruise above their companies at 50,000 feet," says Speyer. "They are into the nuts and bolts."

Weill hired Dimon in 1982 after reading a term paper Dimon wrote as an undergraduate at Tufts University analyzing Weill's deal-making. Dimon, who earned his master's at Harvard University, helped Weill plot his acquisitions over a 16-year period as they bought and sold companies for American Express Co. and later for Primerica Corp. and Travelers.

For a time, Weill and Dimon were so close that they shared an office. In 1996, Weill appointed Dimon chairman and chief executive of Salomon Smith Barney Inc., Travelers' investment banking unit. After Travelers acquired Citicorp in October 1998, Dimon was named president of the newly formed Citigroup.

Then the mentor and protege clashed, and Dimon was out -- at 42, an outcast from Wall Street.

Dimon took up boxing, drove across the United States, and in March 2000 joined Bank One. He invested $56.8 million of his own money in the bank's stock. He then cut the benefits of his top 12 executives and instituted a pay-for-performance plan -- just as Weill had taught him to do. He went on to ax 7,000 jobs, slash the bank's dividend, and dump billions in bad corporate loans. In the process, he pulled Bank One into the black.

Since Dimon became chief executive on March 27, 2000, Bank One stock has climbed 41 percent to $45.22 on Jan. 14, when the acquisition was unveiled following the close of New York trading. Bank One shares Friday lost 31 cents to $51.52. Prince entered Weill's sphere in 1986, when Weill acquired control of Commercial Credit Corp., the Baltimore consumer finance company where Prince was working as a lawyer. As Dimon strategized with Weill, Prince served as legal counsel on Weill's acquisitions. He became a trusted adviser.Like Dimon, Prince became close friends with Weill. Last year, Prince bought a $4.5 million, six-bedroom house less than 10 minutes drive from Weill's home in Greenwich, Conn. Prince usually hosts a costumed birthday party for Weill, who turns 71 March 16. One year, the two men clad themselves as knights in armor for the occasion.

Prince, who earned graduate and undergraduate degrees at the University of Southern California, was an attorney with U.S. Steel Corp. before joining Commercial Credit in 1979.

In early 2003, Weill needed a white knight. New York State Attorney General Eliot Spitzer was investigating Citigroup as part of his probe into Wall Street stock research.

Prince went on to negotiate Citigroup's part in the $1.4 billion settlement between 10 banks and regulators. Weill's company paid $400 million, more than any other firm.

Since Prince became chief executive on Oct. 1, 2003, Citigroup stock has climbed 5.3 percent, trailing a 10 percent gain in J.P. Morgan stock and an 11 percent gain in the S&P 500 index.

J.P. Morgan dominates syndicated lending, which has become increasingly important to winning securities underwriting business, with a 30.6 percent market share. Citigroup is number three, with 16.6 percent behind Bank of America Corp., which had 17.8 percent.

In 2006, Dimon is set to take over as chief executive and Harrison will remain as chairman. Weill plans to retire as Citigroup chairman that same year. "The first challenge is to make sure that this transition is as smooth as they say it's going to be," says Michael Madden, a partner at New York private equity firm Questor Management Co.

If Dimon and Harrison can make this marriage work, Weill and Prince had better watch their backs, he says.
