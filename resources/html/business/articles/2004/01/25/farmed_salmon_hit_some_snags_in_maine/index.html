Farmed salmon hit some snags in Maine

By Beth Daley, Globe Staff  |  January 25, 2004

FRANKLIN, Maine -- In the coastal outcrops of Maine, farm-raised salmon was long seen as the great pink hope. And for a decade, salmon farms have delivered on that promise: a skyrocketing growth industry that transformed cold sea water into cash and created jobs by the hundred. Farmed salmon quickly grew into the second-largest fishing industry in a fish-dependent state.

That picture, however, has changed dramatically in the past year. Dozens of salmon pens now sit empty off Maine's rocky coast. One of the state's biggest fish-farming companies is about to pull out. Hit by environmental lawsuits and cheap imported fish from Chile, Maine's salmon production is at its lowest level in almost a decade, and industry officials say that more than 300 salmon-related jobs have been lost. In a crowning blow, a new scientific study singled out farmed salmon as having high levels of toxins, bringing doubts about salmon right into consumers' kitchens.

"Is aquaculture going to stay in Maine? Whether it does or not depends on how this war plays out -- and it is a war," said Steve Eddy, a biologist at the University of Maine's Center for Cooperative Aquaculture Research in Franklin.

In parts of Downeast Maine, where earning a living often means outwitting the elements, farmed salmon represent a rare steady crop. Salmon farms have leased at least 40 sites off the coast, each with a dozen or more fish pens holding tens of thousands of fish. Trailing only lobsters, the state's industry accounted for some $57 million in sales revenue in 2002, and Maine supplies about 2 percent of the world's farmed salmon.

But since a judge's ruling on an environmental lawsuit last May, the industry has been laboring under expensive new rules about how it raises fish. To prevent salmon waste from degrading sensitive seabeds, companies must rotate fish to allow some pens to go fallow. And to prevent the possible spread of nonnative salmon genes to a dwindling wild population, they can no longer raise European salmon, which grow faster and resist disease better than native fish.

Many salmon farmers and their supporters have come to see environmentalists as the enemy, and believe the farms are being unfairly targeted.

"This is a well-regulated industry," said Des FitzGerald, who used to head Atlantic Salmon of Maine and sits on a state task force that will issue recommendations about the industry next month. Listening to testimony on the task force, he said, has made him realize "the depth of ill feeling toward salmon farming. There aren't a lot of people, just a few with shrill voices."

A more formidable enemy, however, may be global trade.

As Maine's salmon farms grew, they traded on salmon's reputation as a hard-to-find delicacy, a rich pink fish available for only a few months each summer. But as farming has transformed salmon from a luxury into a commodity, Maine farms have slowly been losing ground to other countries where salmon could be raised more cheaply.

Most notably in Chile, inexpensive labor costs and looser environmental rules have helped create a salmon industry that can send farmed fillets into major supermarkets far more cheaply than Maine farms. And Chile has other advantages for salmon farms: Because of its climate, fish there do not appear to be at risk from "super chill," or sea water cold enough to freeze salmon blood -- a phenomenon that killed at least 400,000 Maine salmon last year. Although Maine salmon are generally sold to restaurants and food-service industries, while Chilean salmon largely goes to major supermarkets, the competition forced prices down across the board, and companies began to lose money.

"There are economies of scale in Chile. . . . They are able to produce more," said Joe Soja of Urner Barry Publications in New Jersey, which tracks seafood prices.

The salmon wars appear to be claiming their first major casualty this month: Atlantic Salmon of Maine, the largest salmon-farming company in the country just three years ago. A devastating hatchery fire that killed 3 million young salmon last year, the increasingly tight economics of fish farming, and the recent court ruling left them unable to rebound. Now, the Norwegian-owned company is quietly divesting all of its holdings in Maine, including two hatcheries, a Machiasport processing plant, and 14 leased sites. Although employees are attempting to purchase the Maine operation, it is unclear if they will succeed. Atlantic Salmon's parent company, Fjord Seafood, declined to comment, as did its Maine president, Steve Page.

"There is a lot of concern here about job loss," said Christina Therrien, town manager of Machias, near the processing plant. "If fish farming goes, I just don't know any possible way for other businesses to pick up the slack" to employ people. Still to be tallied is the impact of a Science magazine article published earlier this month that hits one of salmon's most touted virtues: its health benefits. The article says farmed salmon had significantly higher levels of dioxins and other potential carcinogens than their wild cousins because their feed concentrates the toxins in their bodies. Funded by the Pew Foundation, the study's findings are based on levels of risk defined by the Environmental Protection Agency and examined 700 wild and farmed salmon from around the world. It noted particularly high levels in farmed salmon from Europe, Canada, and Maine. Chilean salmon fared better.

Other researchers -- and government officials -- quickly rose to the defense of farmed salmon, saying the benefits of the heart-healthy fish far outweigh the potential risk. An environmental group, however, has jumped in and announced plans to sue farmers and distributors under a California law that requires companies to tell the public about hazardous levels of toxins or to reformulate their products.

For all the salmon industry's woes, however, Maine is still placing great hope in its ability to farm fish -- and the federal government is backing it up. Two separate new aquaculture facilities are being built in Franklin, constructed in large part with $28 million in federal dollars, along with help from the state and the University of Maine.

Biologists like Eddy are already looking to the next species of fish that might be farmed, such as halibut and cod.

In giant green tanks, wild 45-pound halibut are being raised to seed the first generation of captive-raised halibut. In another room, 22,000 juvenile cod, raised from eggs in captivity, are preparing to be transferred to an ocean pen later this year. While cod aquaculture is not yet economically viable, Maine industry officials are hoping such research could yield the next saltwater bonanza.

Few critics of aquaculture think that farmed salmon is going to vanish completely from Maine. Yet they question the wisdom of sinking more public money into ocean aquaculture when the environmental complaints about ocean pens are unresolved.

And the industry is now running into friction from neighbors: Attempts to expand the pens further south have met widespread opposition from homeowners worried about the environmental and aesthetic impact of floating pens filled with thousands of fish.

"The money is available and these guys are going for it, but there is no vision here for what the industry is going to look like," said Roger Fleming, an attorney with the Conservation Law Foundation.

But salmon farmers say Maine's ability to preserve a working waterfront -- and hundreds of jobs -- is their vision. Aquaculture is likely to grow in importance globally as the world's oceans face increasing fishing pressures, and Maine hopes to remain a player in the industry, salmon or no salmon.

"The citizens of Maine have to choose what they want their coast to be," said Sebastian Belle, executive director of the Maine Aquaculture Association. "Are we going to be consumers of fish or producers?"
