BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Perini pact from Army could reach $1.5b

January 15, 2004

Perini Corp., the largest US builder of hotels and convention centers, received a US Army contract worth as much as $1.5 billion for work in the Middle East, including Iraq, and in North Africa. The five-year deal allows the Framingham firm to be given design, engineering, and construction work for the US Army Corps of Engineers in 25 countries, the company said. Perini's contract has a ceiling of $500 million for the first year and includes four one-year options, each worth as much as $250 million. It is the second Iraq reconstruction project for Perini, after a $500 million award in April for power-generation projects. (Bloomberg)

Venrock Associates raises $550m

Venrock Associates, the venture firm started by the Rockefellers in the 1930s, has raised $550 million in new capital. A portion of the money came from Rockefeller family members, while a number of endowments and foundations also invested. Until recent years, the fund was not open to outside investors. Venrock, which is based in New York and has an office in Cambridge, also said it hired two new general partners: Michael Feinstein, formerly of Atlas Ventures in Waltham, and Anders Hove, formerly chief executive of a Swiss investment firm. (Beth Healy)

Ex-counsel of biotech firm sentenced

A federal judge sentenced the former highest ranking attorney of a Cambridge biotechnology company to a year and a day in prison for insider trading. Andrew S. Marks, 43, of Wayland, pleaded guilty to charges of securities fraud in October. US District Judge Douglas Woodlock also sentenced Marks on Monday to two years of probation after his prison term and a $3,000 fine. Prosecutors said Marks, who was the chief patent counsel at Vertex Pharmaceuticals Inc., sold more than $476,000 in Vertex stock on Sept. 21, 2001, after learning the company would suspend clinical trials of an arthritis drug it produced. (AP)

THE NATION

Pfizer pressures Canadian pharmacies

Pfizer Inc. is applying tougher restrictions on Canadian pharmacies to discourage them from selling low-cost prescription drugs to Americans. In a letter, Pfizer told Canadian pharmacies they will need authorization to deal with wholesalers, and they had to promise not to ship products south of the border. The letter also said Pfizer would reserve the right to examine the pharmacies' order histories for its products. As many pharmaceutical companies move to choke the supply of drugs to Canadian Internet pharmacies, the online firms are turning to brick and mortar stores to replenish their stock. Pfizer officials could not be reached for comment. (Bloomberg)

Report warns on minimum income tax

A report from an Internal Revenue Service watchdog warned that the alternative minimum income tax system will ensnare a growing portion of US taxpayers soon and is in need of reform. "Compared with the regular tax system, the AMT is a parallel `secret' tax system that most taxpayers have not yet experienced personally . . . In short, it is a time bomb on a short fuse," said Nina Olson, the national taxpayer advocate, in her annual report to Congress. Olson's office is an independent one within the IRS created to help taxpayers resolve problems with the agency. About 12.7 million taxpayers are expected to be affected by the AMT in 2005, up from about 2.4 million currently. (Reuters)

AT&T Wireless shares jump 16.8%

AT&T Wireless Services Inc. stock jumped to a 52-week high following several published reports that it is once again in discussions to be acquired by Cingular Wireless. Shares of AT&T Wireless closed up $1.44, or 16.8 percent, at $9.99. According to The Wall Street Journal, in December Cingular was considering an initial public offering to fund an acquisition of AT&T Wireless. The Journal also reported that a merger could be hammered out relatively quickly, as AT&T Wireless and Cingular held merger talks more than a year ago. Spokesmen for the companies declined to comment. (Dow Jones/AP)

FDA orders Aventis to pull Allegra ads

US regulators ordered drug maker Aventis SA to stop running some TV and print advertisements for the Allegra allergy medicine because they make misleading claims of superiority. The Food and Drug Administration said some ads falsely imply Allegra is better than competing drugs, such as Pfizer's Zyrtec and Schering Plough's Clarinex. Allegra, along with the blood thinner Lovenox, account accounts for 20 percent of sales at Aventis. The product is competing with new treatments as well as cheaper, generic versions of Schering-Plough's Claritin pills. An Aventis spokeswoman did not return a call. (Bloomberg)

Medtronic resubmits request to FDA

Medtronic Inc. said it resubmitted its request to start a clinical trial in the United States of a new cardiovascular device designed to prop open arteries and keep them from reclosing. Robert Ryan, Medtronic's chief financial officer, said the company has provided all of the additional information that the Food and Drug Administration asked for when Medtronic first submitted its request, in November. The company, the world's biggest medical device maker, asked the FDA for permission to begin a late-stage US trial testing its drug-eluting stent, a tiny wire mesh tube covered with medicine. (Reuters)

FCC calls for harsher TV indeceny fines

The chairman of the Federal Communications Commission, Michael Powell, said he supports legislation to increase fines tenfold for indecency and other offenses on television and radio. "Some of these fines are peanuts," said Powell, the top US regulator of telephone companies and broadcasters. The FCC also is reconsidering a decision not to fine stations that broadcast singer Bono's use of an obscenity during a televised awards ceremony last year, he said. The FCC fined Viacom Inc. $27,500 last month for airing listeners' comments about their sexual practices on a Detroit radio station. (Bloomberg)

THE WORLD

UN: World economic growth to hit 3.5%

The global economy will expand by 3.5 percent this year, with the United States serving as the locomotive and China right behind as the new economic powerhouse, the UN forecasts. But while UN economists see stronger growth in the short term, they warn that major imbalances in the global economy pose a serious threat, especially the US budget deficit, which is expected to reach $500 billion this year. How these imbalances are eventually corrected "will have profound implications for the future stability, efficiency, and equity of the world economy," the UN report said. The report urged the major economic powers to keep their policies to stimulate growth in place and focus on the problem of unemployment, rather than on cutting inflation. (AP) . . .
