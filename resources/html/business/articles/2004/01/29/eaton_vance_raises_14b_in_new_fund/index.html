BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Eaton Vance raises $1.4b in new fund

January 29, 2004

Eaton Vance Corp., the largest manager of US mutual funds designed to minimize taxes, raised $1.4 billion in the share sale of a fund that enables investors to benefit from legislation that lowered taxes on dividends. Eaton Vance, which generated proceeds of $6.1 billion in closed-end funds last year, issued 70 million shares in the Eaton Vance Tax-Advantaged Global Dividend Income fund at $20 each, the Boston company said. The fund, managed by Michael R. Mach, Judith A. Saryan, and Thomas H. Luster, invests primarily in dividend-paying common and preferred stocks of domestic and foreign issuers. Under US law enacted May 28, dividends are taxed at long-term capital gains rates, with a maximum of 15 percent. (Bloomberg)

Mass. building permits rose 14% in '03

Massachusetts issued building permits for 19,273 housing units in 2003, up 14 percent from 2002, good news for a state with a tight housing supply and high real estate prices. But there was bad news, too, in an analysis of US Census data by the Home Builders Association of Massachusetts, the group said: The number of permits for single-family homes was 12,726, down 3 percent from 2002 and generally continuing a five-year downward trend. Nationwide, permits for single-family homes rose 9.2 percent in 2003. According to the Home Builders, big growth in Massachusetts came in buildings permitted for five or more housing units. Many of those projects were a result of Chapter 40B, the state's affordable housing law. (Chris Reidy)

AT&T offers local service in most of N.E.

AT&T Corp. is now offering local phone service throughout Maine and Rhode Island and in most of New Hampshire and Vermont, including most larger cities and towns. As it did when it rolled out service in Massachusetts last year, AT&T is offering unlimited local and long-distance calling for $54.95 monthly, as well as local-only plans ranging from $27 in New Hampshire to $36 in Vermont. The AT&T plans use phone lines rented from Verizon Communications. AT&T canceled similar services in Connecticut last year, blaming state regulators for setting excessive rates to use Verizon lines. (Peter J. Howe)

Tenet to sell nearly a third of hospitals

Tenet Healthcare Corp. is putting nearly a third of its hospitals up for sale, including two in Massachusetts, in a major restructuring that includes a $1.4 billion charge against earnings in the fourth quarter. Tenet said it would sell the MetroWest Medical Center's campuses in Natick and Framingham, and Saint Vincent Hospital in Worcester. Overall, the company will sell 27 hospitals, 19 of them in California. Tenet said the sales would lead to net losses for all of 2003 and 2004. It will retain 69 hospitals in 13 states, and remain the second-largest hospital chain, ranked by revenue. (AP)

THE NATION

Damage from Mydoom not extensive

The Mydoom computer worm infestation continued, but there was no sign the malicious program was causing serious disruptions of the Internet or big computer networks run by businesses or government agencies. Sharon Ruckman, senior director at Symantec Corp.'s security response center, warned that machines already infected with the worm will launch a data attack on the website of Utah software company SCO Group on Sunday. She also reported that a variant of Mydoom will attempt to attack computers run by Microsoft Corp. She said her company has seen few instances of this new worm, and has issued an antivirus patch to guard against it. (Hiawatha Bray)

FCC says satellite TV gaining on cable

Powell Satellite television providers such as DirecTV and the Dish Network that did not exist 10 years ago have captured almost one-quarter of all households that pay for TV, the Federal Communications Commission said. The agency's 10th annual report on TV competition also found subscribers of cable and satellite services have a choice of more than 300 channels beyond the conventional network broadcasts, triple the number a decade ago. The report provided support for chairman Michael Powell and the other two Republicans on the five-member commission, who have been criticized by consumer advocates and lawmakers for enacting media-ownership rules that would allow companies to own more TV and radio stations. Supporters of the rules, which have been challenged in court, say increased competition has made the old limits on ownership obsolete. (AP)

AT&T Wireless tops in swap complaints

AT&T Wireless Services Inc., which had its slowest sales growth last quarter and has put itself up for sale, generated the most complaints among mobile-telephone carriers over attempts to move phone numbers between providers. Of 4,734 complaints the Federal Communications Commission received after the government started allowing number transfers on Nov. 24, AT&T Wireless was mentioned in 2,297, the agency said, followed by Sprint Corp.'s wireless division with 1,119; Verizon Wireless with 739; Cingular Wireless, whose parent companies are trying to buy AT&T Wireless, with 699; and Deutsche Telekom AG's T-Mobile USA unit, with 625. (Bloomberg)

US comptroller assailed for override

State officials and US lawmakers of both parties said a federal regulation eliminating state authority over national banks will wipe out effective laws against abusive lending and ignores Congress's intent for overseeing the banking industry. The regulation, issued Jan. 7 by Comptroller of the Currency John Hawke Jr., asserted that his office, not the states, is the primary regulator of national banks. The rule, to take effect Feb. 12, means states can't enforce their consumer protection laws against national banks and their subsidiaries. (Bloomberg)

FCC ripped over weak enforcement

Republicans and Democrats took turns criticizing federal regulators at a hearing yesterday, saying they have not done nearly enough to shield the public from indecent radio and TV programs. The lawmakers supported sharply increasing the fines which the Federal Communications Commission can levy on broadcasters. The hearing was held a day after the FCC proposed a record $755,000 fine against Clear Channel Communications for the "Bubba the Love Sponge" program, aired on four Florida radio stations. House members of both parties have sponsored a bill to increase the maximum fine for indecency to $275,000, from $27,500. The Senate Commerce Committee has approved such an increase, and the Bush administration endorsed the House bill in a letter from Commerce Secretary Don Evans. (AP)
