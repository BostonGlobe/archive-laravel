Earnings Roundup

January 29, 2004

Marsh & McLennan Cos., the world's largest insurance broker and the parent of troubled mutual fund company Putnam Investments, said fourth-quarter profit rose 21 percent as higher premiums fueled commissions. Restitution and other expenses related to allegations of securities fraud at Putnam totaled $24 million. Net income climbed to $378 million, or 70 cents a share, from $312 million, or 57 cents, a year earlier, the New York company said. Analysts expected earnings of 65 cents per share.

Revenue increased 15 percent to $3.03 billion from $2.64 billion.

The SEC is still determining a fine for Boston's Putnam and Marsh & McLennan hasn't set aside money for it, the company said. (Bloomberg)

Reebok International Ltd. said fourth-quarter earnings rose 71 percent after products such as rapper 50 Cent's signature G6 sneakers boosted sales. Net income increased to $28.1 million, or 44 cents a share, from $16.5 million, or 27 cents, a year earlier, Canton-based Reebok said. Sales rose 11 percent to $843.6 million from $762.8 million, about half of the gain coming from a lower US dollar.

Analysts expected the company to earn 42 cents.

The $80.50 G6, introduced in November, and other items unveiled last year such as Premier running shoes helped sales. Chief executive Paul Fireman has rolled out the Rbk line and signed other rappers including Jay-Z to attract younger shoppers, the largest sneaker buyers. Lower returns and slower increases in costs increased profit.

"They put the numbers up," said Adam Friedman of Integrity Asset Management, which owns Reebok shares. "They are [also] benefiting from strength in the euro." (Bloomberg)

Time Warner Inc. had net income of $638 million in the fourth quarter as sales rose at its film and cable units while America Online had a fifth straight quarter of subscriber losses. The company earned $638 million, or 14 cents a share, compared with a loss of $44.9 billion, or $10.04, a year earlier. The year-ago results included a $45.5 billion charge to write down the value of acquired assets. Revenue climbed 6.4 percent to $10.9 billion, the New York company said.

The results were in line with analysts' estimates.

Chief executive Richard Parsons said every unit except AOL posted revenue gains, led by profitable film series such as "The Lord of the Rings" and increases in cable television subscriptions. AOL's revenue fell 7.1 percent as 830,000 of the Internet unit's 24.7 million subscribers stopped paying for the service. (Globe wires)

Boston Properties Inc. continued to struggle with falling rents in the fourth quarter as its earnings fell 77 percent and its funds from operations slipped 4 percent. However, chief financial officer Doug Linde said he believes market conditions bottomed in 2003, and that rents and occupancies will show improvement by late 2004 and 2005.

In the fourth quarter, the Boston real estate investment trust, which owns office buildings in Boston, New York, Washington, and San Francisco, posted net income of $60.6 million, or 61 cents a share, down from $260.1 million, or $2.70 a share, a year earlier. The 2002 per-share figure includes $2.08 related to gains from property sales and discontinued operations. If excluded, earnings would have been 62 cents a share.

The company's funds from operations, often considered a better metric to reflect real estate performance, totaled $106.9 million, or $1.05 a share, down from $113.5 million, or $1.14 a share, a year earlier, before the application of an accounting change related to derivatives. (Dow Jones)

Affiliated Managers Group Inc., owner of stakes in US fund managers, agreed to buy London's Genesis Holdings International to add $7 billion invested in emerging markets. The company also said it expects to earn at least 7 percent more this year than forecast. Terms of the acquisition weren't disclosed.

Genesis, founded in 1989 and with offices in London, Guernsey, Kenya, Chile, and Brazil, will enhance AMG's access to funds invested in some of the world's most lucrative markets. AMG will own 60 percent of Genesis.

Also yesterday, Beverly-based AMG said fourth-quarter net income climbed to $17.3 million, or 78 cents a share, from $13.3 million, or 60 cents, a year ago. Revenue rose 18 percent to $139.6 million, and assets under management totaled $92 billion at the end of December.

Excluding costs such as amortization of intangible assets, AMG earned $1.29 a share. The company refers to that figure, which also excludes depreciation and deferred taxes, as cash earnings, its preferred measure of performance.

Using that method, which doesn't meet with generally accepted accounting principles, AMG's profit rose 14 percent from $1.13 a year earlier. The average analyst forecast was $1.29 a share. (Bloomberg)

PerkinElmer Inc. , a maker of scientific instruments, posted a fourth-quarter profit after a year-ago loss, thanks to cost cuts and a 6 percent rise in revenue due to the weak dollar. PerkinElmer, based in Boston, reported net profit of $27.9 million, or 22 cents per share, compared with loss of $2.6 million, or 2 cents a share, in the same period a year ago.

Earnings from continuing operations excluding intangibles amortization was 24 cents per share, 2 cents ahead of Wall Street expectations.

Sales for the fourth quarter rose to $432.6 million from $409.6 million a year ago. But virtually all of the increase was due to favorable foreign exchange rates.

The firm forecast 2004 earnings from continuing operations of 62 cents to 67 cents per share -- an increase of 44 percent to 56 percent over 2003. Excluding intangibles amortization PerkinElmer projected 2004 earnings of 77 cents to 82 cents a share, up from 76 cents expected on Wall Street.

For the first quarter, the company projected earnings from continuing operations of 6 cents to 10 cents per share. (Reuters)
