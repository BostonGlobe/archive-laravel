Gained in translation

By Steven Syre
Globe Columnist / January 6, 2004

Gillette Co. shares have been on a roll over the past 10 weeks, climbing almost twice as high as the leading indexes tracking the stock market. That strong finish to 2003 topped off the best annual stock performance Gillette shareholders have witnessed in six years. Gillette had its share of good news last year, from new products to dominating market positions. But the company also faced significant new competition in the razor blade business and nagging questions about its battery operation.

So what made 2003, especially the final quarter of the year, so good for Gillette stock and the shares of many other companies that count on overseas sales for the majority of their revenue? A revived stock market certainly helps, but look also to the powerful effect of a drooping dollar.

The dollar, which has been losing ground to other major currencies around the world for more than a year, hit a new low against the euro and its lowest level in more than three years against the Japanese yen yesterday. The dollar's steady decline has turned into a nosedive over the past four months.

Companies that translate large amounts of international revenue from local currencies into dollars for their financial statements are getting a boost from the math. If the euro appreciates by 10 percent, sales in that currency are worth 10 percent more on the American corporate income statement.

The slumping dollar was a plus all last year for companies like Gillette, which counts on overseas business for about 60 percent of its revenue, and the effect could be even more pronounced in fourth-quarter corporate earnings reports due out over the next several weeks.

"The market is absolutely on to the reality for the big multinationals this is going to be an important, positive kick to earnings," says James Weiss of Weiss Capital Management.

Gillette shares have climbed 18 percent since late October. Coca-Cola Co. shares have advanced 17 percent since Sept. 30. Cabot Corp. of Boston, an industrial company that counts on overseas business for a majority of revenue, has seen its stock jump 20 percent since Oct. 27.

Investors are reacting to impressive gains at specific companies thanks in part to the dollar's slump. Gillette estimated nearly half its 11 percent revenue increase in the third quarter was attributable to currency. A full 20 percent of Cabot's operating profit for the fiscal year ended Sept. 30 was currency related.

Currency comparisons will continue to provide a boost through most of 2004, as long as the dollar doesn't reverse course. "You'll have a benefit for at least three quarters more," says Will Braman, chief executive of Fortis Investment Management USA.

The sinking dollar has had a more muted impact on the entire stock market and American business overall. Currency translation accounted for 2 percentage points in earnings growth among the Standard & Poor's 500 companies in each of the first two quarters of last year, according to estimates by Thomson First Call. The impact probably slowed slightly in the third quarter and is estimated at about 2 percent again for the final quarter of 2003.

Currency can be a two-edged sword. Companies with big international sales usually make some of their products overseas and incur costs that become more expensive as the dollar declines. Gillette, which makes razor blades in South Boston, also operates 10 other plants cranking out blades in foreign countries.

But there's no missing the big picture for American companies doing lots of business overseas. The weaker dollar is good for the bottom line.
