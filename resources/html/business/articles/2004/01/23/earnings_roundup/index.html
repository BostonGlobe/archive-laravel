Earnings Roundup

January 23, 2004

Microsoft Corp. said second-quarter profit fell 17 percent on a program to allow employees to sell unprofitable stock options. Net income fell to $1.55 billion, or 14 cents a share, from $1.87 billion, or 17 cents, a year earlier. Both figures include the cost of stock options. Sales rose 19 percent to $10.2 billion in the period ended Dec. 31, from $8.54 billion, the company said.

Net income included 20 cents a share of options costs and expenses for the program that let workers sell unprofitable options. Microsoft in July said it would begin paying workers with stock grants instead of options and would deduct the cost of options already issued from its earnings. The company yesterday gave year-earlier figures that also included those expenses.

Excluding those costs, profit would have been 34 cents a share, exceeding the 30-cent average estimate of analysts polled by Thomson Financial. (Bloomberg)

AT&T Corp. reported fourth-quarter earnings of $340 million and said sales will fall for a fifth straight year because competition is driving down calling prices. Net income of 43 cents a share compared with a loss from continuing operations of $611 million, or 79 cents, a year earlier, AT&T said in a statement. Sales at the Bedminster, N.J., company slid 13 percent to $8.1 billion, the lowest in almost 20 years.

The company was expected to have profit of 40 cents a share on sales of $8.3 billion in the fourth quarter, the average estimates of analysts in a Thomson Financial survey.

Results were helped by a 38 percent decline in income taxes to $139 million, stemming from a research and experimentation credit.

Sales will fall as much as 10 percent this year as mobile-phone use rises and local-phone carriers such as Verizon Communications Inc. crowd into the long-distance market. AT&T has yet to benefit from a pickup in US economic growth, and chief executive David Dorman is slashing costs to compensate. He may ultimately have to sell the company, some analysts said. (Bloomberg)

EMC Corp. said it swung to a fourth-quarter profit on significantly improved demand for its data storage hardware and software products. EMC, a Hopkinton-based firm whose products help data-heavy companies like banks and airlines store and manage electronic information, also said it expects 25 percent revenue growth in 2004, but predicted first-quarter earnings essentially in line with expectations.

EMC earned $220 million, or 9 cents per share in the quarter, including a net tax benefit of a penny per share that offset certain charges. The results compared to a loss of $64 million, or 3 cents per share, a year ago. Analysts were expecting EMC to earn 7 cents per share.

Overall, the company said revenue rose to $1.86 billion from $1.49 billion.

For the full year, EMC earned $496 million, or 22 cents per share, compared with a loss of $119 million, or 5 cents per share, a year ago. Full-year revenue grew 15 percent to $6.24 billion.

The company said it expects earnings of 5 cents per share in the first quarter of 2004, including a 1 cent-per-share charge associated with another acquisition. Analysts were expecting 6 cents per share. (AP)

Sepracor Inc. narrowed its fourth-quarter loss from a year ago, on higher revenue including pharmaceutical product sales. The Marlborough pharmaceutical company posted a loss of $33.9 million for the quarter, or 40 cents a share, compared with a loss of $44.3 million, or 53 cents a share, a year ago.

Fourth-quarter revenue rose 42 percent to $112.3 million from $78.9 million a year ago.

Analysts expected a loss of 71 cents per share.

Zoll Medical Corp. fiscal first-quarter net income rose about 18 percent on strong sales in its North American market, and the company once again backed its guidance for the second quarter and the rest of the year. The Chelmsford cardiac resuscitation device maker said that for the quarter ended Jan. 4, net income was $2.7 million, or 29 cents a share, beating the mean analyst estimate of 28 cents, according to Thomson Financial. Last year, first-quarter earnings were $2.3 million, or 25 cents a share.

Revenue was $50.8 million compared with $43.1 million in the year-ago period.

The company also said its outlook for the second quarter and the year remains unchanged. (Dow Jones)

AT&T Wireless put itself up for sale, confirming it has received numerous merger offers that it plans to entertain. The nation's third-largest cellphone operator would not disclose the names of any bidders, which according to sources at both AT&T Wireless and its suitors include Cingular Wireless, NTT Docomo of Japan, Vodafone of Britain, and Nextel Communications.

Amid the speculation, AT&T Wireless released its fourth-quarter earnings report a week early, confirming fears it had been hit hard by a glitch in a new software system that prevented new customer activations and irked existing subscribers for most of November.

AT&T Wireless said its fourth-quarter loss was $84 million, or 3 cents a share, compared with a loss of $131 million, or 5 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 4.1 percent to $4.215 billion from $4.047 billion a year before. Analysts were expecting a profit of 1 cent per share.

Chief executive John Zeglis told investors on a conference call that existing customers left and the company missed out on several hundred thousand potential new subscribers due to technical and customer service problems. (Globe wires)

Haemonetics Corp.'s third-quarter profit declined 10 percent as the company returned to its normal, higher tax rate. The Braintree maker of automated blood-processing systems reported net income for the quarter ended Dec. 27 fell to $9.3 million, or 38 cents a share, from $10.3 million, or 42 cents a share, a year earlier.

The tax rate for the latest quarter was 36 percent, up from 13 percent a year earlier when the company recorded a one-time tax refund of $4 million. Excluding taxes, fiscal third-quarter earnings rose 22.2 percent to $14.6 million from $11.9 million.

Revenue, however, improved 4.2 percent to $90.7 million from $87.1 million last year.

Haemonetics still stands by its July guidance for full fiscal 2004 revenue growth in the high single digits and net income about flat with fiscal 2003's $1.13 a share. (Dow Jones)

Amgen Inc., the world's biggest biotechnology company, said fourth-quarter profit rose 20 percent as more doctors prescribed its cancer and arthritis treatments. Net income climbed to $546.9 million, or 41 cents a share, from $456.4 million, or 34 cents, a year earlier. Sales jumped 33 percent to $2.35 billion, the Thousand Oaks, Calif.-based company said. Analysts expected profits of 48 cents per share. (Bloomberg)

Chemical maker Cabot Corp. said its quarterly profit fell 12 percent as cost cuts failed to offset a decline in income in its supermetals business.

The Boston-based company reported fiscal first quarter net income of $29 million, or 42 cents a share, down from net income of $33 million, or 48 cents a share, in the year-ago quarter. Analysts had forecast profits of 43 cents per share.

Revenue climbed to $446 million in the latest quarter from $410 million in the prior-year quarter. (Reuters)

NStar reported a 25 percent decline in fourth-quarter earnings as milder weather hurt gas sales and earnings from nonutility operations fell.

The Boston energy delivery company said net income for the fourth quarter ended Dec. 31 was $36.9 million, or 68 cents a share, vs. $49.5 million, or 92 cents a share, a year earlier. The year-ago earnings included a net tax benefit of 19 cents a share on the company's investment in RCN Corp.

The latest results beat Thomson Financial's projection of 66 cents a share. For 2004, NStar issued a forecast of $3.45 to $3.55 a share; analysts were expecting $3.53 a share. (Dow Jones)
