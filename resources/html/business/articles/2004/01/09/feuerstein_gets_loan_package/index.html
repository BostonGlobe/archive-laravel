Feuerstein gets loan package

Approval is viewed as a partial victory in Malden Mills bid

By Ross Kerber
Globe Staff / January 9, 2004

The US Export-Import Bank gave textile executive Aaron M. Feuerstein $35 million in loan guarantees yesterday for his attempt to buy back control of Malden Mills Industries Inc. of Lawrence, on less favorable terms than he sought, but enough to keep alive his mission to regain his family business.

The final version of the package was more generous than what was recommended by the bank's senior officers, and represented a partial victory for Feuerstein, the company's former owner, and politicians of both parties who had lobbied on his behalf.

Among other things, the board agreed the bank would make fewer claims on the plant's property and the commercial value of its Polartec fleece fabric brand, allowing Feuerstein to use those assets for collateral with other lenders.

Now the question is whether lenders will find the terms acceptable enough to fund the redevelopment package Feuerstein has put together with Boston developer Winn Cos. Winn aims to turn part of the mill into housing, a plan that has support from several state and local agencies, but has received little encouragement from the mill's current owners.

Feuerstein would not comment on yesterday's decision, representatives said, but a close adviser called the package "a start," adding, "and if there is a problem completing the transaction because of the provisions in this deal, we believe that the bank will work with us . . ."

"We hope that this is the basis for a bankable transaction in the private sector," the advisor said, on condition of anonymity.

Feuerstein has argued that the mill's current equity owners, led by a unit of General Electric Co., would be more willing to move some of the 1,200 manufacturing jobs overseas to cut costs. Yesterday's decision by the Export-Import Bank implicitly endorsed Feuerstein's view that he represents the best bet to save the domestic jobs.

"This is very much a reflection of the spirit of the Bush administration and what we're trying to achieve, which is to reverse the trend of losing manufacturing jobs in the United States," said one board member, Republican April H. Foley, in a telephone interview after the vote.

Board member J. Joseph Grandmaison, a Democrat and Feuerstein's strongest advocate within the agency, called the package sufficient for the buyback prospects. "I believe that the action that was taken, the amendments passed today, leaves the door open for Aaron Feuerstein to successfully negotiate with a commercial bank, while at the same time fulfilling our responsibility to the taxpayers to provide reasonable reassurance of repayment," Grandmaison said.

The bank's decision amounts to a $15 million increase over the amount it voted to give Feuerstein in August. According to Grandmaison, it caps weeks of internal debate in which the bank's staff suggested Feuerstein deserved the full amount on favorable terms, but was overruled by senior officers who didn't want to accept any risk on his behalf.

The pendulum swung back in Feuerstein's favor somewhat yesterday morning when the board, including its Republican chairman, Philip Merrill, approved a series of amendments during a closed session.

Malden Mills is best known for its signature Polartec fleece fabric. Feuerstein gained national attention for his generosity to workers after a fire in 1995, but was forced to seek bankruptcy protection in 2001. He now holds just a minority stake in the company his grandfather founded in 1906 while he attempts to exercise rights to buy back the remainder.

Political reaction to yesterday morning's decision was mixed. Some Democrats who had supported Feuerstein said the bank wasn't doing enough for his midsize company in contrast to the help it has given larger, better-connected clients.

"The bank cannot justify backing companies like Enron and Halliburton and then hold back on Malden Mills," said Massachusetts Senator John F. Kerry in a statement. Others took a less critical view. "There was some positive movement today, and I am hopeful that Ex-Im will continue working with Malden Mills to save these 1,200 jobs," said Massachusetts Senator Edward M. Kennedy, also a Democrat, in a press release.

New Hampshire Senator John E. Sununu, a Republican, called the vote a signal the bank was willing to work further with Feuerstein. In a statement, Sununu said he hopes General Electric and the company's other equity owners will work "to make the refinancing proposal a reality."

General Electric spokesman Richard Jones said decisions about the company's future are in the hands of its board of directors, all of whom were appointed in the fall. The directors have declined to comment, a spokesman said.
