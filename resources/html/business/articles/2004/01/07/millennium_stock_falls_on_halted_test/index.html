Millennium stock falls on halted test

Cancer drug is found ineffective for some patients

By Ross Kerber
Globe Staff / January 7, 2004

Shares in Millennium Pharmaceuticals Inc. fell 6 percent yesterday after the Cambridge biotechnology company said it halted a clinical trial of its Velcade cancer-fighting drug because it wasn't effective for some patients with colorectal cancer.

The news is a setback for Millennium, which has more than 40 clinical trials underway to prove Velcade is effective in areas other than multiple myeloma. Regulators approved Velcade as a treatment for that form of blood cancer last year, and on Dec. 15 Millennium said it halted a follow-up multiple myeloma study after Velcade showed better-than-expected results.

Millennium forecast $50 million worth of Velcade sales for 2003 but is hoping to generate much more revenue if the drug can be approved to treat solid tumors such as cancer of the colon or other organs. The company also needs the revenue to offset declining sales of its Integrilin heart disease drug.

The company would not say how many patients were enrolled in the phase 2 trial, originally planned to include 175, or detail what standards the drug failed to achieve. Spokeswoman Kelly Lindenboom said the firm would continue several other trials evaluating Velcade's effectiveness on colorectal cancers and in other areas such as on lymphoma and ovarian cancer. "We're not closing the door on colon" cancer, she said. "While this is definitely a setback, we're very encouraged by what we've seen in other areas." Millennium sells Velcade with its partner Johnson & Johnson. Some of the patients in the halted trial were being treated with a combination of Velcade and Pfizer Inc.'s Camptosar.

Medical specialists said the clinical halt raised the stakes for Millennium's other trials. "Obviously the results in the colon cancers are sobering because it suggests some of the promise they saw in preclinical models didn't pan out in patients," said Paul Richardson, clinical director of the Jerome Lipper Center for Multiple Myeloma at the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute in Boston.

Millennium shares closed at $17.85, down $1.16 from Monday. The company said it expected to share fuller results of its trials at a medical conference this year. News of the trial's halt was first reported by TheStreet.com and confirmed in a midday press release by Millennium.
