It's time to use technology to make SUVs safe

By Royal Ford
January 4, 2004

It didn't take just a village. It took the equivalent of a small city. "It" in this case being death by rollover in light trucks, which include SUVs, pickup trucks, and minivans.

The carnage: 51,500 dead from 1991 to 2001. The numbers grew even more appalling in 2002 when, among 42,815 people who died in highway wrecks, 61 percent were killed in SUVs and pickups, and the majority of those were involved in rollovers.

You may think you're riding high and safe, but you're five times as likely to die if you flip your SUV as opposed to a passenger car; twice as likely if you roll your truck.

Why?

Weak roofs and spacious, greenhouse-like glass through which you can be thrown if you're not wearing your seat belt (and many, many do not).

So it was with joy that I greeted the news last month that 15 major auto manufacturers and the insurance industry had agreed on a voluntary pact to make pickups and SUVS safer.

They will lower their stances -- as if a study had been needed to show that tall, narrow objects tip over more readily than low, flat ones -- and put side impact and side window curtain air bags in all of their vehicles. Half of all such vehicles sold in 2007 will have these features; all by 2009.

It is a good move, too long in coming.

And it is a need -- along with other safety features and cleaner, more fuel efficient cars -- that the Union of Concerned Scientists has been stressing for years.

"Automakers have let US consumers down by not putting existing technology to work," maintains the Union, a group whose opinions I respect because they are realistic.

They don't scream for the banning of SUVs or pickups because they understand this about the American driving public: We like our trucks, we like our SUVs, we like big.

But big does not have to be unsafe; big does not have to be dirty.

"SUVs aren't the problem; poor automaker designs and weak government standards are," says David Friedman, director of research for the Union of Concerned Scientists Clean Vehicle Program.

So Friedman and colleagues set out to design, using existing technologies, a safer, cleaner, SUV. And for costs ranging from a few hundred dollars to around $3,000 for the upgrades, they offered up two vehicles: the UCS Guardian and the UCS Guardian XSE.

Build a safer SUV? Friedman asked rhetorically when the

auto/insurance industry pact was announced. "We've already designed it."

And indeed they have, with concept vehicles.

The Guardian uses a sleek, lightweight unibody design, stronger materials in the roof, better seat belts and tires, and a fuel efficient six-cylinder engine. The Union estimates that its introduction would save 2,200 lives per year at a cost just below $750 per vehicle. Chump change in the face of carnage.

Move to the XSE model (at a cost of about $3,000 per vehicle) and you save another 700 lives annually. More fuel efficient (approaching 28 miles per gallon, though I hate to talk about gas and fatalities in the same sentence), it has a six-speed automatic transmission, a powertrain that lets it shut off at stops, window curtain air bags, and stability and rollover control electronics.

Again, we're not talking fanciful notions here. All of this technology exists and, indeed, is already making its way into some SUVs.

The Volvo XC 90, in my opinion, is the safest SUV on the road today.

The folks at Ford, which owns Volvo, were somehow upset when I took an XC 90 onto an airport runway, put it through some serious rollover tests, and gave it a glowing review.

Why?

Because they owned the technology, but it was not going into their popular Explorer and other of their SUVs.

That's changing. The Lincoln Navigator and Aviator SUVs will get the Volvo treatment for 2004; the Ford Explorer, Expedition, and Mercury Mountaineer will follow for the 2005 model year.

And there are other vehicles out there that already contain enough safety options to meet the auto/insurance industry pact, among them the Dodge Durango, Honda Pilot, Nissan Xtera, and Toyota 4 Runner.

The industry needs to pay attention to the Union of Concerned Scientists' report, "Building a Better SUV -- A Blueprint for Saving Lives, Money, and Gasoline."

The government ought to do a couple of things: First, it should mandate what the auto and insurance industries have agreed is a voluntary option. Second, it should not be legal to tie a single optional safety feature (ABS, skid control, side curtain air bags) to other, more expensive and irrelevant options such as sound systems, leather interiors, or air conditioning.

"With the technologies described in this report, automakers can spend the next decade and beyond focused on saving thousands of lives and billions of dollars at the pump every year," the Union concluded in its study.

"The technology exists today; automakers just have to put it to work."
