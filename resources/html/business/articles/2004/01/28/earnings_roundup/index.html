Earnings Roundup

January 28, 2004

Defense contractor Raytheon Corp. swung to a a profit in the fourth quarter and met analysts' estimates, but lowered its outlook for 2004 as it changed its expected rate of return on pension investments. Raytheon, based in Waltham, said it earned $205 million, or 49 cents per share, in the final three months of 2003, compared with a loss of $68 million, or 17 cents per share, during the same period a year ago.

Excluding a $12 million after-tax charge for discontinued operations, Raytheon's operating income was $217 million, or 52 cents per share and in line with expectations of analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call.

Fourth-quarter sales were $5.1 billion, up from $4.7 billion a year ago.

For the full year 2003, Raytheon reported profits of $365 million on sales of $18.1 billion. In 2002, the company lost $640 million on $16.8 billion in revenue.

Raytheon said it expects earnings from operations of $1.25 to $1.35 for 2004, down from previous guidance of $1.50 to $1.60 due to a 25-cent per share increase in pension expense.

Raytheon said it was lowering the rate it assumed its pension investments would earn from 7 percent to 6.25 percent. (AP)

The New York Times Co. said fourth-quarter earnings rose 3.1 percent as advertising and circulation revenue increased at its newspapers. Net income climbed to $110.9 million, or 73 cents a share, from $107.5 million, or 69 cents, a year earlier, the New York company said.

Sales advanced 5 percent to $882.3 million from $840.2 million.

Ad sales at the company's papers rose 6.1 percent last month, and Times Co. said it expects advertising to accelerate this year along with the US economy.

The company has expanded to boost sales in recent years. It added a Friday leisure section to the New York Times in 2002 and gained control of the International Herald Tribune last year.

Times Co. was expected to report a profit of 70 cents a share, according to analysts. Sales were forecast to be $876.5 million. (Bloomberg)

Tweeter Home Entertainment Group Inc.'s profit slipped a bit in its latest quarter, but the results still beat Wall Street's expectations and the company's stock shot up after the news. The Canton stereo and video system retailer reported net income of $5.1 million, or 21 cents a share, for its fiscal first quarter ended Dec. 31. That's down 1.7 percent from the prior year's $5.2 million, or 22 cents a share.

But the latest results still beat analysts' projected earnings of 18 cents a share.

Tweeter said total revenue climbed 2 percent to $255 million from $250 million in the same quarter a year earlier.

Income from operations was $8.7 million, compared to the prior year's $9 million.

(Dow Jones/AP)$55.74-$1.29$61.15$19.61Amazon.com Inc. said fourth-quarter earnings surged to about 28 times the year-earlier level, leading to the first annual profit since the company was founded in 1994, as holiday sales climbed.

Net income increased to $73.2 million, or 17 cents a share, from $2.65 million, or 1 cent, a year earlier, the Seattle company said.

Revenue jumped 36 percent to $1.95 billion, helped by free delivery for certain orders. The company raised its forecasts for this year.

Sales this year probably will rise to as much as $6.7 billion, or more than revenue of as much as $6.25 billion it previously forecast, Amazon.com said. The company said operating income likely will be $355 million to $455 million, more than the $315 million to $415 million it had expected. (Bloomberg)

Northeast Utilities had a fourth-quarter loss after a power pricing agreement cut revenue at an energy-marketing unit. The net loss was $8.93 million, or 7 cents, compared with net income of $56 million, or 44 cents, a year earlier, Berlin, Conn.-based Northeast Utilities said. Revenue rose 11 percent to $1.53 billion from $1.38 billion.

The loss included a previously disclosed reserve of $35.9 million, or 28 cents, for a power contract settlement that reduced revenue of the Select Energy marketing unit.

Utility natural gas sales fell 6.2 percent as warmer weather reduced demand for heating.

Excluding the reserve, fourth-quarter profit was $27 million, or 21 cents a share, Northeast said. Analysts expected the company to earn 26 cents a share. (Bloomberg)

Merck & Co. said earnings declined 26 percent in the fourth quarter as the company began limiting shipments to wholesalers to keep them from stocking up before price increases. Net income had the biggest quarterly drop in a decade, falling to $1.4 billion, or 62 cents a share, from $1.89 billion, or 83 cents, a year earlier. Earnings met analysts' forecasts. Revenue declined 7 percent to $5.63 billion from $6.06 billion. That beat the average analyst estimate of $5.53 billion in a Thomson survey.

Whitehouse Station, N.J.-based Merck expects 2004 profit of $3.11 to $3.17 a share. (Bloomberg)

Millennium Pharmaceuticals Inc.'s fourth-quarter net loss widened nearly 84 percent on restructuring charges and lower gains from securities sales and invested income. The Cambridge company said it posted a loss of $146.4 million, or 49 cents a share, in the latest quarter, compared with a loss of $79.7 million, or 28 cents, for the same period in 2002. Revenue for the fourth quarter fell 12 percent to $85.4 million from $96.8 million a year ago.

Excluding items, the fourth-quarter loss was $91.8 million, or 30 cents a share, a penny a share narrower than expected by analysts. The comparable year-ago adjusted loss was $66.9 million, or 23 cents a share. (Dow Jones)

Brooks Automation Inc. reported a narrower first-quarter loss from a year ago despite a slight decrease in sales, as the company benefits from a recovering chip industry.

The Chelmsford semiconductor equipment maker reported a loss of $8.87 million, or 23 cents a share, for the three months ended Dec. 31, compared with a loss of $70.99 million, or $1.95, last year. Revenue slipped to $82.5 million from $84.9 million last year.

Excluding amortization of acquired intangible assets and other acquisition and disposition-related items, Brooks posted a loss of $7 million, or 18 cents a share in the latest quarter, two cents better than a Wall Street estimate of a loss of 20 cents a share.

For the second quarter ending March 31, Brooks expects revenue to sequentially climb 45 percent, suggesting revenue of $119.6 million. That would surpass the company's previous high of $112 million, set in the second quarter last year. The company also expects to earn 1 to 5 cents a share in the second quarter and 10 to 15 cents, excluding items. Analysts expect the company to earn a penny. (Dow Jones)
