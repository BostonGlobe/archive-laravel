2004 shaping up to be a hot year for cars

By Royal Ford
January 11, 2004

DETROIT -- It was with some bemusement that I greeted the declaration from Ford Motor Co. that 2004 would be "The Year of the Car." Imagine, Detroit a source of cars.

Yet on reflection, it is, indeed, a good move. American companies have, for too long, focused their energies on trucks and SUVs. I recall a General Motors executive telling me they had ceded the small car market to Asian builders.

No more. Even as trucks, SUVs, station wagons, and so-called crossovers with many of the attributes of van, truck and SUV proliferate, this will certainly be a big year for cars.

And at this year's North American International Auto Show, which wrapped up last week, there were many vehicles from many niches (somewhere around 60 debuts) that caught my eye.

FORD FIVE HUNDRED: Ford had already unveiled a prototype of this new sedan but its production model is a real head-turner. I see Audi in it. I see Volvo in it. I see a solid, European-looking automobile from a company that, having launched its F-150 pickup redesign to great applause, sorely needs to build a basic car that will appeal to the masses. Sure, its new Mustang, also introduced in production form, will be a hot seller, and its GT super sports car, at somewhere below $150,000, will cast a bright halo. But the Five Hundred, with its clean lines and crisp interior, is key to Ford's financial future.

PONTIAC SOLSTICE: Another one I'd seen in prototype form. In General Motors' hands, the car will attempt to get American builders back into another neglected segment: the affordable, two-seat sports market. It's a rear-wheel-drive roadster with about 170 horsepower and a price tag somewhere between $20,000 and $23,000. The Miata worked. There's no reason the Solstice shouldn't. Note: GM's Saturn division will also produce a sports car, the Curve, with 2-plus-2 seating and a 200-horsepower engine.

CHEVROLET CORVETTE: Dubbed the C6, the Corvette stays the same even though it changes. Most noticeable: the ugly flip-up headlights have been replaced with fixed beams. And the car is a bit shorter, a bit narrower, and a nicely etched cutout at both doors accents its sleek lines.

PONTIAC G6: Another GM product, this is part of the company's effort to instill a sporty feel into Pontiac's lackluster lineup. The hope is that this sports sedan -- built on the same platform as the Chevrolet Malibu and Saab 9-3 -- will be a worthy successor to the Grand Am. Models will be offered with 175, 200, and 240 horsepower. And expect 2-plus-2 and convertible versions to follow.

KEEP ON TRUCKIN'. . ..

OK, it's the Year of the Car. And Ford, whose F-150 redesign won the coveted North American Truck of the Year Award (voted on by 49 journalists, myself among them) is rightly turning its attention to passenger cars. Yet, no heads should turn too far because a couple of Asian manufacturers have trucks roaring into that competition.

TOYOTA FTX: It's big, it looks like a Tundra, but seems far more vast (my chin just reached the rails around its bed) and it has America's big trucks squarely in its sights. Toyota fiddled for years with six-cylinder engines in pickups in a land where a V-8 was a requirement. Then they built the Tundra with a V-8 and, at less than 300 horsepower, that fine truck got called an 80 percent pickup truck. They're not fooling with the size on the FTX, but there's a twist coming with it (see below).

HONDA XXXX: No name, no price, but what is arguably the "greenest" car builder on the planet is going to build a pickup truck. It's not a giant, as is Toyota's, and I'd consider it, based on the Pilot SUV, a larger version of the Subaru Baja. Yet, here comes Honda, ready to rumble in the land of trucks.

LINCOLN LT: Chalk this up as one I just don't understand. Lincoln needs to build a truck? Been there, tried that, with the horrible Blackwood. Give me a Town Car with steer horns on the hood and a honking loud horn in which to prowl the North 40.

CROSSIN' ON OVER. . ..

LINCOLN AVIATOR: I'd heard it was dead but the shrunken version of the popular Navigator SUV will make a comeback as a crossover. It gets that F-150 chassis and a look that is decidedly Lexus-like. FORD FREESTYLE: Seats seven, rides like a car, looks like a station wagon on steroids -- but a smart move for Ford. In this Year of the Car, you need to cover all bases.

NISSAN/INFINITI ACTIC: Smaller than a minivan, looking like a Murano, with sliding doors, no central pillar, and modular seating, the Actic is a real make-it-what-you-will vehicle.

MERCEDES-BENZ VISION GRAND SPORT TOURER: Glass roof, three rows of seats, and a car I never thought I'd see Mercedes put in anyone's garage. Very much a van -- though they will never acknowledge it -- the Sport Tourer will be built in Alabama.

HYPER OVER HYBRIDS. . .

OK, I voted for the F-150 as Truck of the Year. I also voted for the hybrid gasoline/electric Toyota Prius as Car of the Year (it won). And when it comes to hybrids, to coin a phrase, you ain't seen nothin' yet. Honda's Accord will be a hybrid for next year. So will Toyota's Highlander SUV. Ford's Escape SUV is rolling out as a hybrid. General Motors has pickup trucks coming. The Mercedes crossover/van will offer a diesel/electric power train, and so will that giant Toyota pickup I mentioned above.
