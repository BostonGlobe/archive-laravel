North Enders determined to shape their future

By Chris Reidy, Globe Staff  |  June 15, 2004

The Caparella brothers have big plans for Mother Anna's, the North End restaurant their grandmother opened at the corner of Cross and Hanover streets 60 years ago.

''Maybe we'll add a sidewalk cafe, like the ones you see in Italy," said Robert Caparella, who owns the building, and whose brother, John, runs the family restaurant on the first floor.

A sidewalk cafe at Cross and Hanover, the gateway to Boston's Little Italy, was out of the question for a long time. For the last decade, this part of the North End has been a Big Dig construction zone. And for years before that, the elevated Central Artery and the Sumner Tunnel brought traffic, noise, and fumes to what had historically been the commercial hub of the neighborhood.

But the highway is gone, and it's about to be replaced by the Rose Kennedy Greenway. What today is a torn-up work area will turn into parks just steps from Mother Anna's front door. That could transform not only Cross Street, today a short stretch of mom-and-pop shops and vacant storefronts that serves as the neighborhood's front door, but also the rest of the North End behind it.

As much as many Chinatown residents are worried about the changes in real estate values and residents the Greenway will bring to their ethnic enclave, many North Enders are optimistic about what the parks will bring to theirs. They should be, since their voices were often the loudest at Big Dig planning meetings, insisting throughout the process that their quality of life improve once the dust, very literally, had finally settled.

According to the Greenway's project planners, keeping merchants on Cross Street is crucial. The Greenway needs ''lively, active edges," said Ken Greenberg, the Toronto urban designer hired by the city. Active edges mean cafes and shops. Encouraging longtime merchants to stay is also important to preserving the North End's Italian flavor. As the completion of Greenway causes nearby property values to soar, the temptation will be for some old-timers to cash out.

But Greenberg hopes the parks will inspire property owners to make improvements, maybe by replacing old one-story buildings with new four- or five-story buildings with retail at street level and residences overhead. If that happens, he said, the North End's parks could be ''one of the great public spaces in America."

In the 1950s, when the elevated Central Artery was built, North End residents were not consulted. When the Big Dig got underway Downtown in 1992, residents vowed that they would not be ignored.

''You'd think after 12 years, I'd be worn out," said Nancy Caruso, an unyielding leader of the North End Waterfront Central Artery Committee, who was regularly jolted out of bed by Big Dig construction vibrations. ''But I'm hanging in there."

The group also successfully lobbied for a YMCA that will be built on the Greenway near the neighborhood. A few years ago, the neighborhood lost the North End Union, a community center that provided day care, senior citizen luncheons, and adult education classes. The Y will offer similar services, and will also have a recital hall and a ''walk-in swimming pool" ideal for the neighborhood's many older residents, Caruso said.

Newcomers blending inOnce reviled, gentrification is no longer a constant fear. Newcomers--young professionals and empty nesters, and the high housing prices that follow their arrival--are old news. According to the US Census Bureau, median household incomes in the North End ranged from $43,125 to $52,691 in 2000. ''People who come here don't come to change it," said Joanne Prevost Anzalone of Anzalone Realty. ''They come to meld in."

Paul Caldera, a 42-year-old graphic designer moved from Arizona four years ago. He enjoys living in a neighborhood where the merchants know his name. Longtime residents were initially wary of him, he said, but their reserve melted when they learned he was a family man with two children.

Caldera walks to work at Faneuil Hall Marketplace. It's always been a short but unpleasant journey across the Big Dig. Now the Greenway has the potential to reconnect the neighborhood to Downtown. The parks, said Mark Maloney, director of the Boston Redevelopment Authority, that ''will be the North End's front porch."

Boston City Councilor Paul J. Scapicchio said the North End reached a ''tipping point" seven or eight years ago, when newcomers first outnumbered residents of Italian descent. In 2000, only 38 percent of North End residents identified themselves on US Census forms as being of Italian ancestry. Because of the newcomers, he said, the North End today ''looks a lot more like Beacon Hill."

At his produce store, Bruce Alba sees these changes daily. In the morning, long-time customers speak Italian as they check the freshness of fava beans. In the afternoon, it's newcomers speaking English. They're more interested in peaches and nectarines, ''something they can eat on the run," he said.

The Greenway will drive up property values, and some businesses could be tempted to cash out. Not the Caparella brothers. They, like several others on Cross Street, said they're staying.

''The money is unimportant," said Robert Caparella, 56. ''I'm not going to live forever. I'm not Donald Trump. I'm not in the real estate business. I'm in the restaurant business. The building is not for sale."

Preserving the characterEncouraging local merchants to stay on Cross Street is crucial to preserving the character of the neighborhood, said Frederick P. Salvucci, a senior lecturer at MIT who, as the state's Transportation Secretary during the Dukakis administration, was an early advocate of tearing down the elevated Artery. Meanwhile, Cross Street merchants weigh their options. Martignetti Liquors is considering a ''facelift," said Scott Samost, general manager. A few doors away, Joe Pace Sr. is thinking about knocking down his building and putting up a new one with stores on the bottom, apartments overhead. He also wants to reopen the grocery store he closed during the Big Dig. Pace, 57, has worked in the North End since he was 12 but has never lived there. Now he talks about living above his reopened store. ''I think it's wonderful," he said.

Maria Merola, a fixture for nearly 35 years, isn't so upbeat. She owns Maria's Pastry Shop but not the Cross Street building her store occupies. She worries she'll no longer be able to afford her rent. Calls to her landlord, a trust controlled by the LaFauci family, were not returned.

Plans call for Cross Street's sidewalk to be widened, and Dan McMyler is exploring the possibility of opening an outdoor cafe at his Belvedere restaurant. People who left the North End as young adults are now talking about returning to their old neighborhood when the parks are completed. But they'd better be prepared for higher prices.

With fewer large families, former schools are now condo buildings in which a unit can sell for almost $390,000, nearly double the price of five years ago, according to Listing Information Network. Most apartments, meanwhile, rent for between $900 and $1,900, said Phyllis Paterna, a broker for North End Realty.

The reconnection to Downtown will do away with the North End's insularity, said Thomas O'Connor, a Boston College historian who has studied urban renewal. That, in turn, will continue the erosion of the neighborhood's Italian flavor as old-timers die and newcomers arrive. Still, the North End has too much history to completely lose its distinction.

It's that character that Anna Pellegrino Wasner loves. After raising her children in Belmont, she recently returned to her childhood home. In the suburbs, an older woman living alone can get isolated, she said; not in the North End.

''People here feel like if they see you four times, they can talk to you," she said. ''My children tell me they can see themselves moving here when they get older."

When he and his brother finish fixing up Mother Anna's, Robert Caparella also plans to move back. He hopes to live in an apartment over the restaurant with a view of the North End's front porch that his grandmother never could have imagined.

''I'm definitely going back," he said. ''I'm going to live on the top floor. It's been a dream of mine for a long, long time."
