Boston employers are planning to boost hiring, survey finds

By Diane E. Lewis, Globe Staff  |  June 15, 2004

Boston is expected to experience a hiring rebound in the third quarter in another sign that the state's job picture is brightening, said a survey released yesterday.

Forty-three percent of city employers responding said they plan to expand payrolls from July through September, according to the Manpower Employment Outlook Survey.

The nationwide survey of 16,000 US employers also found that 30 percent of Massachusetts companies are planning to recruit new workers in the third quarter and 8 percent will cut staff, a sign that the state's economy is looking up. The numbers for Massachusetts and Boston have not been seasonally adjusted, according to Manpower.

Nationwide, 20 percent of US companies are planning to add to their payrolls in the third quarter, said Manpower Inc., a national staffing agency.

By contrast, 59 percent of employers foresee no change in hiring prospects and 5 percent are not sure whether they will hire more workers or not. The seasonally adjusted survey has a margin of error of plus or minus 0.8 percent, according to Manpower spokesperson Margaret Gerstenkorn.

The survey results correspond with recent state reports that point to significant improvements in the Massachusetts labor market, which have led to a drop in the jobless rate to 4.8 percent in April, down from 5.1 percent in March. The latest unemployment report will be issued Friday by the state Division of Unemployment Assistance.

Economist Michael Goodman, director of economic and public policy research at the UMass Donahue Institute, said Massachusetts added 5,500 jobs in April after years of weak employment growth. He said many of the new jobs were in professional and business services as well as education and healthcare or medical services.

''This is another piece of evidence that the Massachusetts labor market is turning around in a big way," Goodman said of the survey. ''If just a fraction of that hiring takes place, it will be good news for thousands of workers who have lost jobs."

Lucky job candidates at Waltham-based Raytheon Co. will be part of the hiring surge. The company reports that it will hire 5,000 new employees by the end of the year, including 1,000 in Massachusetts. The defense manufacturer employs 78,000 worldwide, including 11,000 in the state.

''Many of the new jobs will be engineering jobs, particularly electrical engineers, systems engineers, and software engineers," said spokeswoman Sabrina Steele. ''In some instances, we will be replacing retirees or people who left voluntarily."

Steele attributed the increased hiring to strong missile defense sales as well as a $1 billion contract from the US Navy to build the Cobra Judy, a ship-based missile defense radar.

Massachusetts workers can expect job prospects to improve in durable goods manufacturing, transportation and public utilities, wholesale and retail trade, and public administration, Manpower said.

Information technology will see fewer new hires, Goodman said. He said some small technology companies will increase hiring, but larger companies will continue to contain labor costs. Many bigger employers can produce more with fewer permanent workers because of technological advancements. Other employers are sending those jobs abroad in a push to cut labor costs, he said.

In Boston, however, employers in finance, healthcare, real estate, insurance, and other professional services will boost hiring. That's a change from the same period last year when only 20 percent of the companies polled in the city told Manpower they planned to hire more employees.

The survey found that seven other major cities are expected to step up recruiting in the third quarter. They include Los Angeles, where 44 percent of employers plan to increase hiring, and New York, where 20 percent of the workplaces plan to expand payrolls.

Other Massachusetts cities are expected to experience hiring surges in the third quarter. For example, 70 percent of the companies polled in Springfield plan to recruit more workers, according to Manpower. However, an additional 20 percent plan to reduce staff. When plans for staff cuts were deducted from the total number of companies with hiring plans, the staffing agency concluded that 50 percent of those surveyed would hire workers.

Brian Gilmore, executive vice president of Associated Industries of Massachusetts, a trade organization that represents approximately 7,600 companies, said the survey results indicate that employers are feeling more positive.

''Many companies are seeing higher sales, more new orders, and declining back orders," Gilmore said. ''They were slow to recover from the recession, but now they are bullish."
