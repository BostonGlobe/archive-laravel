BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Maxwell, Jones to discuss raising offer

June 10, 2004

Maxwell Shoe Co. and Jones Apparel Group Inc. entered into a confidentiality agreement to explore whether Jones is prepared to further increase its offer for Maxwell Shoe. The firms said the accord will end upon either the agreement's third anniversary or the completion of a deal, whichever comes first. On Monday, Boston-based Maxwell Shoe said its board rejected Jones Apparel's increased offer of $22.50 a share and urged shareholders not to tender their shares. Maxwell said the offer is "financially inadequate" and not in the best interests of shareholders. Maxwell said it expects to meet with Jones to negotiate a higher price that "fully reflects the value of Maxwell." (Dow Jones/AP)

Hospital group to name chairwoman

The Massachusetts Hospital Association expects to name Jeanette G. Clough, 49, as board chairwoman today. Clough, who began her career as a nurse at Massachusetts General Hospital, has been president of Mount Auburn Hospital in Cambridge for over five years. The association is an advocacy and lobbying organization for Massachusetts hospitals. Clough said she will focus on patient safety, including encouraging hospitals to participate in a national initiative to track efforts to prevent medication errors. "We want to be able to compare what we're doing to hospitals around the country," Clough said. She would replace Thomas Porter, president of Morton Hospital and Medical Center in Taunton. (Liz Kowalczyk)

Boston Communications suit dropped

Plaintiffs dropped a class-action lawsuit filed against Boston Communications Group Inc. in Massachusetts state court in November. It alleged Boston Communications hid harmful information about contract negotiations with its top customer, Verizon Wireless. Neither Boston Communications nor its insurers made a payment regarding the suit's dismissal and aren't required to make any future payments, Boston Communications said. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION
>Martha Stewart witness indicted

Larry Stewart, who was the Secret Service's chief forensic scientist, has been indicted on charges he lied at the Martha Stewart trial, his lawyer said. Stewart (no relation to Martha) was arrested last month; federal prosecutors in New York said he lied when he testified that he examined a document the government offered as evidence. Other Secret Service scientists, not Stewart, examined the document, prosecutors said. His lawyer, Judith Wheat had not seen the indictment but was told it contains two counts of perjury. Larry Stewart will appear later to enter a plea. (Bloomberg)

PC sales projected to rise 13.5% this year

Personal-computer sales will rise 13.5 percent worldwide this year to a record 175.4 million as business spending rebounds, said IDC Services, of Framingham. It had forecast an increase of 11.4 percent. The previous record, 154.5 million, came last year and was the highest since 2000, when 140.9 million computers were sold. Business purchases will rise 14 percent from 2003, said Loren Loverde, director of IDC's Worldwide Quarterly PC Tracker. Consumer purchases are expected to rise 12.5 percent, down from 13.3 percent last year. The value of global PC sales will rise 9.2 percent to $194.5 billion, IDC said. The peak was in 2000 at $226.9 billion. (Bloomberg)

Bill would bar Accenture contract

The House Appropriations Committee voted 35-17 to modify the Department of Homeland Security's $32 billion budget to prevent Bermuda-based Accenture from implementing a program that would would track foreign visitors, because the consulting firm is not US-based. Accenture beat out Computer Sciences Corp. and Lockheed Martin Corp. last week to help administer the US Visitor and Immigrant Status Indicator Technology, or US VISIT program, which would track foreigners using digital photos, fingerprints, and other biometric information. Accenture, formerly Andersen Consulting, employs 28 percent of its workforce in the United States through an Illinois subsidiary, Accenture LLP. The bill goes to the Senate. (Reuters)

Drug firm settles for $500,000

Schering-Plough Corp. will pay $500,000 to settle an investigation of a payment by its Polish subsidiary to influence decisions by a government official there. The Securities and Exchange Commission said the subsidiary contributed $76,000 to a charity founded by the official, to help boost its drug sales. The SEC also said the payments were not reflected on books and records, and that accounting controls were inadequate to prevent or detect improper payments. "We're not admitting or denying the commission's findings," company spokeswoman Rosemarie Yancosek said. Last month, the New Jersey company and two of its subsidiaries agreed to pay $27 million to settle accusations that it overcharged Texas and the federal government for prescription drugs. In April, Schering-Plough said it has received a second subpoena in a probe of its marketing practices by the US attorney in Massachusetts. (AP)

Wholesale inventories dip in April

Inventories at US wholesalers fell 0.1 percent in April, the first decline in eight months, as demand for pharmaceuticals, imported autos, and other goods outpaced supplies, the Commerce Department said. Wholesale sales rose 0.8 percent in the month after a 2.9 percent gain that was the largest in more than nine years. Lean inventories suggest that companies may have to step up production and keep adding workers. Companies were caught with too many goods on their hands in the 2001 recession, leading them to be cautious about adding stockpiles. Stockpiles had been expected to rise 0.5 percent. (Bloomberg)

Witness: Tyco official didn't report loans

Tyco International Ltd.'s former general counsel, Mark Belnick, failed to disclose $14 million in loans he received from the company in questionnaires and regulatory filings, according to testimony at his criminal trial by Fatemeh Sadeghi-Nejad, a former lawyer at Tyco. His testimony went to the heart of charges that Belnick committed securities fraud and falsified documents. Belnick also faces grand larceny charges. Sadeghi-Nejad, whose work at Tyco involved handling director and officer questionnaires as well as proxy statements, said Belnick never told her in 1999 or 2000 that he had loans outstanding from Tyco for relocation packages. (Reuters)
