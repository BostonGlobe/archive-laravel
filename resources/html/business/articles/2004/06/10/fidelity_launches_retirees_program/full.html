Fidelity launches retirees' program

Aim is to help make seniors' savings last through golden years

By Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  June 10, 2004

After decades of helping people save for retirement, Fidelity Investments launched a major push to help retirees make those savings last throughout baby boomers' long golden years.

ADVERTISEMENT

The nation's largest mutual fund company yesterday rolled out the Fidelity Retirement Income Advantage, a series of planning and money-management tools, accounts, and investment services designed to help retirees more precisely estimate life expectancy, budget the correct amount of money they will need, and more efficiently spend the savings from their various retirement vehicles.

One tool will help retirees model their financial needs based on numerous variables, such as long-term travel plans, dreams of a second home, or healthcare needs -- whether for themselves or their aging parents. Another creates an account that will serve as a centralized depository for all the various income sources retirees have, from Social Security to pension payments, 401(k) withdrawals, and investment portfolios.

This account can be customized to alert retirees when they get deposits, or have to make a minimum required withdrawal from certain accounts, as well as warn them when the budget and investing plan has gone awry -- that they're spending too much, for example, or that their mix of investments is out of line with the targeted allocations.

''It's like having their own personal retirement manager and administrator, someone who stays on top of things throughout their retirement," said Cynthia Egan, a Fidelity executive vice president.

Longtime Fidelity watcher James Lowell said Fidelity's move is every bit as big as its decision two decades ago to concentrate on workplace retirement savings plans, where it is now the number one provider with $600 million under management.

''This is a seminal moment for Fidelity that had viewed itself as being in a mature industry. This is like finding a second vein in a gold mine that you didn't think existed, and its just as big as the first one," said Lowell, editor of the Fidelity Investor newsletter.

Fidelity is trying to take advantage of an enormous demographic trend, in which the first wave of tens of millions of baby boomers is expected to retire over the next decade or so. Earlier this week, the Massachusetts Institute for a New Commonwealth released a study estimating that 1.87 million baby boomers in the state, an eye-popping 29 percent of the population, will retire over the next five years, and that many of them are woefully unprepared for it financially.

And Fidelity said many retirees don't have a plan to manage their savings, and they also underestimate how long they will end up living. Fidelity said recent research shows there is a 50 percent chance that one member of a healthy 65-year-old couple will live to age 92, yet most people plan to make their money last only into their 80s. ''People will run out of money in their most vulnerable years," Egan said.

Many financial services companies offer a variety of tools to help boomers prepare for retirement. The Vanguard Group, for example, has a series of educational materials available on its website that retirees can use to analyze existing investments, budget expenses, and set withdrawal rates from savings accounts. Vanguard also offers investment products that will automate much of the savings steps necessary leading into retirement, and then the distribution of those funds once they're retired. Vanguard also offers professional financial planning advice to help customers figure out the most appropriate retirement budget for them.

But some retirement industry executives said they are struck by the way Fidelity appears to have melded all of these disparate elements into a single, multi-tiered product for investors of all means. Moreover, it appears to solve what's become one of the financial services industry's greatest fears: That they'll lose the business of retiring baby boomers who will quickly spend down savings under management, or move the nest egg to another financial company.

''What I'm impressed with about Fidelity's strategy, is they're basically trying to turn themselves into the retirees' banker and trying to capture all the assets and income flows that are there," said Chuck Yanikoski, president of Still River Retirement Planning Software Inc., a Harvard company developing retirement planning tools. ''That's pretty clever."

Fidelity said the intent of the services is not to force retirees to invest in company funds, though it will offer investment vehicles that will help retirees maintain their income or even boost their savings -- whether from Fidelity or other investment companies. Right now, the firm plans to charge only a nominal fee for the new retirement services, and will offer it free to customers with $100,000 or more invested at Fidelity.

But Fidelity chief operating officer Robert L. Reynolds did acknowledge yesterday that ''if we do the best possible job" of advising and helping retirees, ''the money will come."

Reynolds said that between 2001 and 2012, the amount of money held by people age 65 or older will balloon to $10 trillion from $2.5 trillion.

''This highlights the huge opportunity at Fidelity, as we look at these numbers," he said, ''and it is an area that we will make every attempt to dominate."
