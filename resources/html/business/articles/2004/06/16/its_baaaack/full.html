It's baaaack!

Rising energy, food costs trigger inflation as CPI jumps 0.6% in May

By Robert Gavin, Globe Staff  |  June 16, 2004

Surging energy and food costs in May drove the biggest jump in consumer prices in more than three years, leaving little doubt that inflation is back.

ADVERTISEMENT

The Labor Department reported yesterday that its consumer price index, a common measure of inflation, posted its strongest monthly advance since Jan. 2001, rising 0.6 percent in May. Through the first five months of the year, inflation is running at a torrid 5.1 percent annual rate, compared to 1.9 percent in all of 2003.

Food and energy costs are largely to blame. In May, gasoline prices at the pump rose more than 9 percent to hit a new record, the Labor Department said. Milk prices rose even faster. They soared nearly 15 percent, the biggest monthly jump since World War II price controls were lifted in 1946.

In Greater Boston, where prices have risen faster over the past two decades than any other metropolitan area in the nation, the bimonthly consumer price index was unchanged from March. Over the past year, inflation in the Boston area ran slightly ahead of the United States, with prices rising 3.2 percent, compared to 3.1 percent nationally.

Renewed inflation is a product of the roaring US economy, and, more broadly, a global recovery that is increasing demand, tightening supplies and pushing prices higher. Last month's jump in consumer prices makes it all but certain the Federal Reserve will boost its key short-term interest rate from the current 46-year low when policy makers meet in two weeks, economists said.

The Fed uses interest rates to manage the economy, cutting them to spur spending and demand when the economy is weak, and raising rates to slow economic growth when inflation threatens. Over the past three years, as the economy struggled through recession, war and an extended ''jobless recovery," the Fed cut rates and kept them low.

But now that the economy and job market have rebounded, economists said, the Fed needs to raise rates to prevent runaway inflation that eats at earnings and corporate profits. For consumers, that will mean higher credit card, home equity and other borrowing costs tied to short-term rates. Long-term rates, such as mortgages, have already moved higher in anticipation of the Fed's action.

For the time being, economists expect the Fed to raise rates gradually. Despite the big jump in overall consumer inflation, the so-called core rate, which excludes the volatile food and energy sector, rose 0.2 percent in May, compared to 0.3 percent in April and 0.4 percent in March.

Economists, including Fed policy makers view the core rate as a more reliable indicator of inflation trends, since food and energy prices are prone to wide seasonal swings. The modest advance in the core rate means the Fed is more likely to stick to its earlier stated policy to boost rates at a ''measured" pace, widely interpreted as quarter-point increments.

The core inflation numbers also brought some relief to investors, who, in the face of strong economic data and sharply rising energy costs, worried the Fed would abandon the ''measured" pace and raise rates by a half-point later this month to contain rising inflation. Both stocks and bonds rallied after the release of the CPI.

Later, markets received more assurance when Fed chairman Alan Greenspan, testifying yesterday at a Senate hearing on his renomination to lead the central bank, said, ''Our general view is that inflationary pressures are not likely to be a serious concern in the period ahead."

The Dow Jones industrial average rose 45.70 points to close at 10,380.43. The technology-heavy Nasdaq Composite index rose 25.61 points to 1995.60.

''There was a lot of fear that core inflation would be worse," said Nigel Gault, a US economist at Global Insight, the Waltham consulting firm. ''But no way does it signal, all clear. We need more good news on core inflation if the Fed is going to stay measured on interest rate hikes."

Financial analysts had anticipated the big jump in overall CPI, primarily because of turmoil in world oil markets. Energy costs jumped 4.6 percent in May, the Labor Department said, and have risen 15 percent over the past year. In the past three months, energy costs have soared at annual rate of nearly 30 percent.

Food and beverage costs jumped nearly a point last month, while housing and clothing rose more slowly, up 0.4 and 0.3 percent respectively. Medical inflation also eased in May, increasing 0.3 after rising 0.4 percent in April and 0.6 percent in March.

In Boston, food, energy and medical costs have risen more slowly than the nation over the past year. Here, food rose just over 3 percent, compared to 4 percent nationally; energy, 9.7 percent compared to 15 percent nationally; and medical care, 4.3 percent compared to 4.6 percent nationally. Housing costs in Boston, however, increased at a far faster pace, rising 3.9 percent compared to 2.4 percent nationally.
