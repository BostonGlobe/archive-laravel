EMC, Connected link for data backup

June 16, 2004

The Hopkinton data storage company EMC Corp. and data backup company Connected Corp. of Framingham have teamed to offer a new data backup service to be sold through telecommunications companies. The service, called EMC Proven Solution for PC Backup, will use Connected's backup and recovery software to let businesses copy data stored on their PCs and send it over the Internet to a remote location. There the data will be archived using EMC's Clariion CX and Centera data storage hardware. Bell Canada and the Dutch telecommunications company KPN are using the new service. (Hiawatha Bray)

State Street Research reimburses funds

State Street Research Investment Services Inc., the mutual fund sales unit of MetLife Inc., paid $636,939 to its funds as compensation for losses resulting from almost two years of improper trading. The restitution was part of Boston-based State Street Research's Feb. 19 settlement agreement with the National Association of Securities Dealers. From 2001 through August 2003, State Street Research allowed Prudential Securities Inc. customers to make trades more frequently than permitted by its funds' prospectuses, the NASD alleged. State Street Research paid more than two-thirds of the reimbursement amount, $426,457, to its $2.4 billion Aurora fund, according to a document filed last week with the Securities and Exchange Commission. The firm also repaid other funds $210,482, the document said. The firm also consented to pay a $1 million fine to the NASD, the securities industry's regulatory agency. (Bloomberg)

TA Associates group sells United Pet

United Industries Corp., which makes lawn and garden products, agreed to buy closely held United Pet Group Inc. for $360 million from Boston-based TA Associates, Friend Skoler & Co., and company management to enter the pet supplies market. The combined company will have annual sales of $1 billion, including $250 million from United Pet, said Dan Johnston, chief financial officer of United Industries. United Pet will be a division of United Industries, which is owned by Boston buyout firm Thomas H. Lee Partners LP. United Industries will finance the purchase with debt to be issued through Bank of America and equity issued through Thomas Lee, Johnston said. The exact amounts of debt and equity will be determined in coming weeks, he said. (Bloomberg)

Pegasus sues DirecTV over contract

Pegasus Communications Corp. has sued DirecTV Group Inc., the biggest US satellite-television provider, and a rural telecommunications organization, claiming they sought to destroy Pegasus by stealing its business. DirecTV and the National Rural Telecommunications Cooperative this month ended Pegasus's exclusive contract to resell service to 8.4 million US households in 41 states, prompting Pegasus's Marlborough-based satellite-TV unit to file for bankruptcy. "Working together, NRTC, NRTC's officers and directors, and DirecTV have conspired to steal Pegasus' business from Pegasus and divide the spoils among themselves," Pegasus said in the suit, filed in US Bankruptcy Court in Portland, Maine. Pegasus made 96 percent of its revenue last year from reselling DirecTV's satellite service. (Bloomberg)

AARP backs Senate drug-import measure

AARP, which represents 36 million US citizens 50 and older, said it will support a bill that would let Americans import drugs from Canada, where government controls keep prescription prices as much as 70 percent lower. The measure, introduced two months ago by Byron Dorgan, a North Dakota Democrat, and Olympia Snowe, a Maine Republican, has the backing of 23 Democrats and Republicans in the Senate. Drug makers such as Pfizer Inc. and GlaxoSmithKline PLC may see a 40 percent decline in revenue from drugs that are bought in Canada instead of the United States, according to Alan Sager, an economist at Boston University. Peter Corr, Pfizer's vice president of science and technology, said the company opposes legalizing imports because of safety risks, such as the possibility of buying counterfeit drugs. (Bloomberg)

Report: Millions lack health insurance

Nearly 82 million people -- one-third of the US population younger than 65 -- lacked health insurance at some point over the past two years, and most of those were uninsured for more than nine months, says a report from the private group Families USA. The problem reaches deep into the middle class, affects African-Americans and Hispanics disproportionately, and is most pronounced among people younger than 25, according to the group's analysis of census data. Ron Pollack, Families USA's executive director, said fast-rising healthcare costs, a soft labor market in which employers are passing more health costs to workers, and reductions in state safety net programs are resulting in substantial increases in the number of uninsured. The study focused on Americans younger than 65 because older Americans are covered by the federal government's Medicare program. (AP)

World Wide Web creator honored

Tim Berners-Lee received a $1.2 million cash prize for creating the World Wide Web and said he would never have succeeded if he had charged money for his inventions. "If I had tried to demand fees . . . there would be no World Wide Web," Berners-Lee, 49, said at a Helsinki ceremony for winning the first Millennium Technology Prize. "There would be lots of small webs." The prize committee agreed, citing the importance of Berners-Lee's decision never to commercialize or patent his contributions to the Internet technologies he had developed, and recognizing his revolutionary contribution to humanity's ability to communicate. Berners-Lee has mostly avoided both the fame and the fortune won by many of his Internet colleagues. Berners-Lee now runs the standard-setting World Wide Web Consortium from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. (AP)

Oracle posts another double-digit gain

Oracle Corp. registered its sixth consecutive quarter of double-digit earnings growth as the world's second-largest software maker continued to capitalize on corporate America's gradual upturn in technology spending. Oracle said it earned $990 million, or 19 cents per share, for the three months ended May 31. That was a 15 percent improvement from net income of $858 million, or 16 cents per share, at the same time last year. Quarterly revenue totaled $3.08 billion, a 9 percent gain from $2.83 billion last year. The quarter marked the final three months of Oracle's fiscal year -- generally the company's peak sales period. The earnings were a penny higher than the mean estimate among analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call. (AP)

U2's Bono joins venture capital firm

Bono, lead singer for the rock band U2, has joined a Silicon Valley venture capital firm that plans to invest in media and entertainment projects, according to The Wall Street Journal. The 44-year-old singer is joining the newly created Elevation Partners, based in Menlo Park, Calif. The new venture aims to raise $1 billion. Elevation Partners declined to comment, citing Securities and Exchange Commission rules for venture capital firms in the midst of fund-raising, according to the newspaper. (AP)
