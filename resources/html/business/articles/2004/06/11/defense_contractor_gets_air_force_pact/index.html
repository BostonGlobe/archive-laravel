BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Defense contractor gets Air Force pact

June 11, 2004

Raytheon Co. received a $369 million award from the US Air Force to build and test airborne radar that can track ground targets and cruise missiles. Raytheon's award is part of an $888 million contract given to Northrop Grumman Corp. in April for work on the radar. Raytheon said it will help build three of the systems for Northrop's Global Hawk unmanned spy plane and three larger versions for the manned E-10A Multi-Sensor Command and Control aircraft. The new radar, called the Multi-Platform Radar Technology Insertion Program, is intended to replace Northrop's Joint Surveillance Target Attack Radar System, which was used in Bosnia, Afghanistan, and Iraq. That system can track only ground targets. (Bloomberg)

Manchester, N.H., airport deploys WiFi

The Manchester, N.H., airport, which serves more than 4 million passengers annually, has deployed a WiFi high-speed wireless Internet access service that costs users $7 a day. Access is free for information such as flight schedules and airport security bulletins. The Airport Network Solutions unit of ICOA Inc. of Warwick, R.I., runs the service. Airport officials said the WiFi coverage extends throughout the airport and does not require special software or a subscription to any other WiFi service. ICOA has also completed WiFi installations at airports serving Fresno and Sacramento, Calif.; Baton Rouge, La.; and Savannah, Ga. (Peter J. Howe)

BHA taps Ameresco unit for energy audit

The Boston Housing Authority has selected Citizens Conservation Services, a unit of Framingham-based Ameresco Inc., for an energy-efficiency audit of 5,000 housing units that the authority expects to yield $60 million in savings over 12 years. Ameresco will take steps including installing more efficient lighting and plumbing, upgrading central heating, and installing renewable energy and cogeneration systems. The 15 housing developments involved are Bromley Park and Heath Street in Jamaica Plain; Commonwealth and Washington Street in Brighton; Franklin Field, Franklin Hill, and Pasciucco in Dorchester; Holgate and Whittier Street in Roxbury; Lenox Street in the South End/Lower Roxbury; Old Colony in South Boston; Washington, Beech, and Roslyn Apartments in Roslindale; Torre Unidad in the South End; and a Charlestown development. The BHA said two earlier audits of properties saved $16 million. (Peter J. Howe)

Charles River Associates earnings jump

Charles River Associates Inc.'s second-quarter earnings rose 43 percent as growing demand for its economic and litigation services led to growth in nearly all of its practice areas. The Boston business consulting firm also said it now expects 2004 results to be at the top end of guidance. Charles River said net income was $4 million, or 38 cents a share, for the three months ended May 14, compared with $2.8 million, or 30 cents a share, a year earlier. Latest-quarter earnings per share include a 1 cent contribution from InteCap, which Charles River acquired on April 30, and reflect a 14.3 percent increase in its outstanding shares. Revenue for the quarter rose 14 percent to $45.7 million from $40.2 million. (Dow Jones)

CMGI third-quarter net drops 9.8%

CMGI Inc.'s third-quarter earnings fell 9.8 percent from last year, bolstered by income from discontinued operations. The Charlestown supply-chain management company said its earnings for the quarter ended April 30 decreased to $69.4 million, or 17 cents a share, from $76.9 million, or 20 cents a share, a year earlier. The latest third quarter included a noncash tax benefit of $74.7 million and income of $61,000 from discontinued operations. In the year-ago period, results included income of $117.8 million from discontinued operations. Third-quarter revenue fell to $105.8 million from $106.1 million a year earlier. (Dow Jones)

Pegasus Communications loses ruling

DirecTV Group Inc., the biggest US satellite-television provider, persuaded a federal judge in Portland, Maine, to let it take away Pegasus Communications Corp.'s rights to resell DirecTV service to 1.1 million rural US households. DirecTV said this month it would end Pegasus's exclusive contract to resell service in 41 states, prompting Pegasus's Marlborough, Mass.-based satellite-TV unit to file for bankruptcy protection. Pegasus then asked US Bankruptcy Judge James B. Haines Jr. to block DirecTV from terminating the deal, citing bankruptcy laws that shield contracts and other assets from creditors' claims. Haines's ruling is a victory for DirecTV, which has been losing subscribers in Pegasus territories and earlier offered $750 million to Pegasus for rights to serve its subscribers directly. Pegasus made 96 percent of its revenue last year reselling DirecTV service. (Bloomberg)

FCC chief to set phone-line access rules

The top US communications regulator said he plans to draft new rules for leasing access to the big local telephone carriers' networks after the Bush administration decided not to defend the old ones. The administration Wednesday said it would not challenge an appeals court ruling that struck down FCC rules forcing the four big local carriers, known as the Baby Bells, to lease access to their networks at government-set rates. Federal Communications Commission chairman Michael Powell, who lost a bitter battle last year to reduce the network-sharing requirements on the Bells, said he would seek rules that would phase in any price increases by the Bells and minimize consumer disruptions. "We will begin immediately," he told reporters after the FCC's monthly open meeting. "I'm quite optimistic with the new guidance and understanding what our limits are." The agency may adopt interim rules as a stepping stone to final rules, Powell said, but he hoped that the work could be completed this year. The old rules struck down will be wiped off the books June 15. (Reuters)

Ex-Tyco director denies Belnick assertion

A former Tyco International Ltd. director testified that the board of directors never approved a special bonus for former top lawyer Mark A. Belnick in 2000. Joshua A. Berman, a director at the conglomerate from 1967 to 2002, said the board wasn't asked to consider a bonus for Belnick at any of three board meetings between April 2000 and October 2000. His understanding was that L. Dennis Kozlowski, Tyco's then-chairman and chief executive, was actually looking to replace Belnick, Berman said. "That would have been extraordinary," Berman said of the bonus. Berman is a prosecution witness in the grand larceny trial of Belnick in New York State Supreme Court in Manhattan. Prosecutors claim Belnick received a special bonus from Kozlowski worth up to $17 million for his work on a Securities and Exchange Commission inquiry in 2000, but that bonus wasn't approved by the compensation committee or the board. The agency closed its probe in July 2000, taking no action. (Dow Jones/AP)

Sara Lee to eliminate jobs, close plants

Sara Lee Corp., the largest US underwear maker, said it will cut about 4,175 jobs from its branded apparel unit and close five plants. The company is consolidating intimate, sportswear, and underwear production, Sara Lee said. About 350 jobs will be eliminated in North Carolina, while the other cuts will affect workers outside the United States. The company, whose brands include Hanes, Champion, and Wonderbra, cited changing production requirements and apparel trade restrictions set to expire at year-end. Sara Lee had $4.75 billion in sales in the third quarter that ended March 27. (Bloomberg)
