Looking forward to a second act

Ex-executives often remake themselves in surprising ways

By Robert Weisman, Globe Staff  |  June 11, 2004

What will David F. D'Alessandro do for his second act?

At 53, D'Alessandro said yesterday he will be ending his 20-year career at John Hancock Financial Services this November, and then spending 12 months writing and contemplating his next move.

''I'm not going to do anything for a year," he said.

But there is life after John Hancock. Second acts are nearly as common in the business world as they are on Broadway. And they often don't have much in common with the first performance.

Consider: Boston software pioneer John J. Cullinane sold Cullinet Software and then worked to bring peace to Northern Ireland. Mitchell Kapor, who founded Lotus Development Corp., moved to San Francisco and reinvented himself as an activist, philanthropist, and angel investor. Microsoft Corp.'s resident genius Nathan Myhrvold left to form a worldwide inventors' consortium based in Seattle.

Some businesspeople feel most comfortable when they are making themselves over. ''Every three or four years, I've just thrown myself into a different environment," said serial entrepreneur Maria A. Cirino of Waltham. ''Some of my friends think I'm crazy to take on a new challenge just when I'm getting to the point where I can relax a little. But when something's hard, it's also very interesting."

For D'Alessandro, a leading figure in Boston business circles, there will be no shortage of possibilities, from writing to sports to politics. Advertising executive Jack Connors, a friend of D'Alessandro's, said the turnaround specialist would be an ideal executive for a consumer products company with either a tarnished brand or problems in the marketplace. Connors said that when the book is written on D'Alessandro, ''Hancock will be known as chapter one. This guy has more future than anyone I ever met."

And as he looks toward the future, D'Alessandro could find inspiration in the many second act stories in the Boston business world. Here are three of them:

Kenan E. Sahin

After selling his first company, Kenan Systems, to Lucent Technologies for $1.48 billion in January 1999, Kenan E. Sahin spent two years running software projects for Lucent and its Bell Labs affiliate in New Jersey. He returned to the Boston area early in 2001 and traveled across Europe with his wife for a chunk of that year.

Sahin's plan was to get involved with nonprofit organizations, buy a small company with five to 10 employees, and maybe return to an earlier career of teaching at a university. ''My concept of retirement is that it is a blending," said Sahin, 63. ''Your mix of activities changes."

But when he read an article in The Boston Globe about the bankruptcy of the Arthur D. Little consulting firm, and the planned sale of its assets, Sahin decided it was his mission to rescue the company's famed Cambridge technology labs, which had created the airline reservation system and patented synthetic penicillin.

Sahin personally financed his $17 million purchase of the labs in April 2002, merging them into a new company, TIAX LLC, and poured in millions more in working capital. TIAX exited information technology and telecommunications, refocusing on energy, homeland security, and home and food technologies. In the process, it reduced its staff by 25 to about 250. ''The challenges were formidable," Sahin said. But by the first quarter of this year, TIAX had broken even.

''For me, this was more of a mission than an investment," Sahin said. ''It was simultaneously a start-up, turnaround, and restoration of an icon in Massachusetts. Once I got in, there was no turning back."

Maria A. Cirino

When her Cambridge technology company i-Cube was acquired by Razorfish Inc. in late 1999, Maria A. Cirino, who had run i-Cube's sales and marketing operation, saw a chance for a fresh start.

The company's center of gravity was moving to New York, and Cirino clashed with the Razorfish management over sales strategy. So she departed the company in February 2000 and, with a pair of former i-Cube colleagues, David Samuels and Dan McCall, founded Guardent Inc. in Waltham the following month. Cirino was the chief executive. ''There wasn't much time to think," said Cirino, 40. ''Before you could even blink, we were in business. I wasn't sure if we were ready, but we didn't really have time to worry about that." Cirino and her partners saw a firm that would remotely manage other firms' data security infrastructure, and in the winter of 2000, they quickly rounded up venture capital. ''When we were at i-Cube, we had seen our customers struggle with security issues. We determined that there was an opportunity."

Over the past four years, Guardent grew to become a market leader in its field. And in February, it was sold to VeriSign Inc. of Mountain View, Calif., for $140 million. Cirino, still based in Waltham, is now senior vice president of VeriSign's managed security services division.

''After that, who knows?" she said. ''After going through the process at Guardent, I really understand what makes a serial entrepreneur."

Louis E. Lataif

Boston University alum Louis E. Lataif, 65, was president of Ford of Europe in 1991 when he was recruited to be dean of BU's School of Management. His return to the university enabled him to fulfill a goal he had articulated three decades earlier.

Back in May 1961, Lataif had been president of BU's student body when the university's dean of men, Staton Curtis, asked him what he'd like to do some day. The two were walking down Bay State Road, and Lataif told Curtis he'd eventually like to be a dean.

Instead, he went into business, spending 27 years at Ford Motor Co. Lataif rose through the ranks, becoming the youngest Ford officer at the age of 42, and ultimately heading Ford of Europe. As he prepared to cross the Atlantic to give a commencement speech at the management school in 1991, a search committee told him the school was looking for a new dean. BU's president, John Silber, had challenged the committee to find a manager, not an academic, to lead the management school. And Lataif was the man they wanted. He accepted the job in July, left Ford on Sept. 16, and started his new job as dean the next day.

Lataif led a drive that raised $110 million to build the School of Management's new building on Commonwealth Avenue. And he reoriented the curriculum to teach management as an integrated system, introducing the MS-MBA program that enables students to earn simultaneous masters degrees in business and information systems. ''There's no way I can separate my Ford experience from my BU experience," said Lataif. ''The leadership and management skills I learned in the business world have been very relevant."
