BOOK REVIEW

A driven man who helped reshape the mass media

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  June 13, 2004

Robert L. Johnson's rise from poor to rich, from graduate school dropout to media mogul, and from founder of a dubious cable network to an enigmatic and unintentional celebrity, is almost cliche.

Born in rural Mississippi, Johnson parlayed his drive and education into a career as a cable industry lobbyist, then turned a $15,000 loan into a billion-dollar fortune, along the way picking up admirers, pals among business and entertainment royalty, a National Basketball Association franchise, a wife, and numerous mistresses.

Determination, a healthy appetite for risk, and a harem: It's the stuff we've heard again and again regarding America's modern, male business elite.

So the brilliance in Forbes magazine reporter Brett Pulley's recent unauthorized biography comes more from the author's grasp of Johnson's significance to the mass-media culture of the late 20th century than from the author's chronicle of Johnson's business acumen.

BET, the now Viacom-owned cable channel, was conceived by Johnson in the early 1980s, as black Americans were just beginning to actualize their political and economic clout.

But those looking for the channel to become the network of black empowerment were consistently confounded by BET's evolution into a purveyor of cheap content (especially music videos full of Mercedes-Benzes, booze, and shaking booties) under Johnson's unflinching dedication to making more than a buck.

Pulley clearly explains why, in the era of Justin Timberlake stripping Janet Jackson in prime time, Johnson's accomplishments will always be derided by critics who, fairly or not, hold him responsible for perpetuating negative images of the very consumers who made him rich.

''As the primary purveyor of black images on television, BET represents blackness. And for many people, that representation is far too narrow, and far too lascivious," Pulley writes.

''Johnson will always be regarded by some as a sort of postmodern robber baron, a hip-hop version of the nineteenth-century capitalists like Andrew Carnegie and J.P. Morgan -- men who built fortunes through iron-fisted ruthlessness."

Johnson has always had critics. When the network was started in the early 1980s, according to Pulley, many black viewers were skeptical of his ownership, a skepticism fueled by white cable magnate John Malone's early investment in BET. Later, the entrepreneur would be lambasted by former executives after he asked them to forgo half of their valuable stock options to lessen the cost of buying BET's publicly traded shares off the market in 1997.

Pulley contends that Johnson is at times as tortured about the criticisms as he is unwavering in his insistence that the bottom line is, well, the bottom line -- the public image of black folks notwithstanding.

''As each day faded to black, people closest to Johnson could see that he was growing exasperated by the criticism his network was garnering," Pulley writes.

Despite making black America's love-hate relationship with BET a central theme of his book, Pulley never forgets that he is indeed writing a biography of a man, not a network. He makes a point of not glossing over personal passions, like Johnson's support of his daughter's equestrian pursuits, or conflicts, like Johnson's long and bitter battles with his sister, who sued him after being fired from BET.

Pulley also delves into Johnson's other entrepreneurial activities, reminding the reader that in building BET Johnson also became a restaurateur, movie producer, and philanthropist.

But while the book chronicles Johnson's long path to owning the NBA's expansion Charlotte Bobcats team, it makes only passing note of other recent ventures, such as his RLJ Development hotel company.

In all, Pulley's account of Johnson's life is complete, if not comprehensive. The book presents its subject as man, mogul, and magnet for critics. It's hard to tell, though, whether devoting so much time to the latter tells readers more or less about the other two.
