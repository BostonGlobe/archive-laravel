Don't blame us, say service station owners

By Peter J. Howe, Globe Staff  |  June 13, 2004

For many retailers, raising prices 35 percent in a year would be a dream. But Tom Pickett couldn't be more miserable.

ADVERTISEMENT

Pickett, who owns Sunoco gas stations in Dorchester and Hyde Park, figures that with the runup to $2-plus gasoline this spring, his profits have slumped 10 to 14 percent from last year.

The reason? The high price of gasoline is making his customers much more demanding comparison shoppers who will drive across town to save a nickel a gallon. To stay competitive, Pickett has to shrink his profit margin -- even as some parts suppliers for his repair business are beginning to tack a ''fuel surcharge" onto their delivery bills.

''There's a big misconception that when the price of gas goes up by 30, 40, 50 cents a gallon, all the guys owning service stations are becoming millionaires," said Pickett, owner of Arthur's Sunoco on River Street in Dorchester's Lower Mills and a second station on Cummins Highway near the Hyde Park-Mattapan line.

''This is the worst market I've seen since the 1970s, and this is just the tip of the iceberg," he added. ''What's going to happen this summer?"

Soaring oil and gasoline prices may be good news for OPEC countries and global multinational petroleum giants. But for Pickett and other owners and operators of the roughly 2,300 Bay State gas stations, soaring prices almost always mean shrinking profits.

''As the price goes up, the retailer makes less and less money," said Fred Rozell, director of retail pricing for Oil Price Information Service's OPISNet.com, a Lakewood, N.J., company that tracks retail gasoline prices. ''You want people to come inside and buy more Slurpees, because you make a lot more money on that."

OPISNet's estimate of the average Massachusetts station's profit margin on a gallon of gasoline, called the ''rack-to-retail" markup, slipped to 11.8 cents this month from 14.8 cents a year earlier, and dipped as low as 7 cents in May. By contrast, in September 2003, as wholesale prices were beginning to head back down after hitting a Labor Day weekend peak, retail prices held firmer, swelling Bay State margins to as much as 34.3 cents a gallon.

Historically, gasoline prices tend to march in lockstep with wholesale price increases, but are much slower to head back down once wholesale prices ease. One indicator that gasoline prices could be easing is that gasoline futures contract prices have dropped about 19 percent on the New York Mercantile Exchange since hitting a record peak on May 24.

But given the chronic threat of supply-disrupting terrorist attacks like last week's strike on a major oil pipeline in Northern Iraq, few analysts are ready to predict a smooth price decline this summer.

During the week ended June 8, the most recent figures available from the Massachusetts Division of Energy Resources, the average price for a gallon of regular unleaded gasoline reached the highest level of the year, $2.09 a gallon. The same week a year ago, it was $1.52, and a year earlier, $1.41.

''What other kind of industry is there where you can pull up to an intersection and do window-shopping of three or four stores?" said Stephen Dodge, associate director of the Massachusetts Petroleum Council. ''It's not an easy business."

The volatile prices are driving gas station owners to making tough choices.

In Arlington, Tom Porter, who owns Porter's Firestone repair shop and a Citgo gas station, said he may stop accepting credit cards -- a measure a nearby competitor implemented recently. Porter worries about losing customers but he may have no choice.

After subtracting the 2.5 or 3 percent processing fee major credit cards charge, ''It's a nonprofit operation at this point," he said.

And sometimes station owners are just making unusual decisions. To keep grumpy customers happy, Quincy Gas station owner Harvey Kertzman hired a blues guitarist last Friday to entertain motorists. One song, composed for the occasion, was titled ''Gas Pump Blues," with the line, ''Costs around $10 to drive around the block; only one solution -- gonna sell my wheels and walk."

''I just figure it's a very timely thing," said Steve Murphy, the blues guitarist. ''People are singing the blues at the pump. Hopefully it eases the pain a little bit with music."

Gas stations began popping up soon after the automobile became a mass-market product in the early 1900s. The first ''gas stations" were rudimentary, often consisting of little more than a barrel mounted on a pole that dispensed super-cheap commodity gasoline by gravity.

It wasn't until around World War I that brand-conscious big oil companies began rolling out the archetypal full-service gas station -- with uniformed attendants dispensing fuel, checking oil levels, and washing windshields, according to John A. Jakle, a geography professor at the University of Illinois at Champaign-Urbana and author of ''The Gas Station in America."

But starting in the 1950s, these full-service stations under famous brands like Esso, Texaco, and Gulf started to fade as cheaper self-serve gas grew popular. Overall, the number of gas stations -- defined as stores that get at least 50 percent of their sales from gasoline and related products -- has dropped by more than half since 1972 in both Massachusetts and the nation, according to industry associations, reflecting the impacts of improved automotive fuel efficiency, industry consolidation, and the proliferation of multi-island self-serve outlets.

And more recently, big-box stores such as Wal-Mart and BJ's Wholesale Club have also entered the retail gas business. Over the last eight years, Wal-Mart alone has added gas at 1,075 of its 3,000 US locations.

To boost profits, gas stations, whether operated by independent owners or franchisees of big brands such as ExxonMobil, Shell, and Sunoco, are adding convenience stores. ExxonMobil Corp., for example, now operates ''On The Run" convenience stores at about 1,000 of its 42,000 worldwide gas stations.

Sunoco Inc., which operates 4,500 stations in 25 states and is one of the top five national brands in Massachusetts, said the squeeze on retail gas margins shows the importance of the brand's continued development of convenience stores, now available at some 800 locations.

For last year, Sunoco reported an average retail margin of 10.5 cents per gallon on 4.5 billion gallons of gasoline, and $700 million worth of convenience-store merchandise such as soft drinks and snacks at a 24.6 percent margin. Sunoco does not offer more specific breakdowns, but those figures suggest convenience store sales at just one-sixth of its stations accounted for potentially one-quarter of gross corporate profits from all 4,500 gas-selling outlets.

Pickett went into the gas business in 1973, after completing a year at Boston College. ''I was really interested in trying to go out and do something with cars," he recalled. That year, he took over a service station on Massachusetts Avenue in Cambridge, now the site of an office-apartment building. In 1987, he bought his current Lower Mills Dorchester location, and a few years later he bought his other station.

Pickett, now 53, is weathering the current oil crisis the same way he handled it in the 1970s: watching all costs.

For years, Pickett has offered one of the only full-serve islands in the Dorchester-Milton area, mainly as an amenity for older drivers or people who prefer not to have to pump their gas. But facing price pressure, he's shutting down the full-serve island at 7 p.m. instead of the usual 11 p.m.

Pickett is also scrutinizing every supplier's invoice, and even looking into whether he might find a better deal for his dumpster rental. ''It's a penny business," Pickett explained. ''If you don't watch what you're doing, you're going to get killed."
