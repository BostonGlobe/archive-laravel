Fuel economy

Rising gasoline prices are hitting drivers where it hurts most - in the wallet; but understanding what's going on can help save money and aggravation

By Royal Ford, Globe Staff  |  June 13, 2004

We have not seen the gas lines or the near panic of the late 1970s, but recent high gasoline prices -- more than $2 per gallon for regular -- are making people cranky. Consumers are looking for ways to save on fuel, from where they should buy it to the cars they drive.

Here are some commonly asked questions -- and their answers.

Gas in Massachusetts last week, at an average price of $2.09 per gallon, cost about 47 cents more per gallon than it did one year ago. How much is that costing me?

This depends, of course, on what you drive and how far you drive. If you commute in a typical sport utility vehicle, according to Jim Kliesch of the nonprofit American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy in Washington, you get about 14 miles per gallon. If you have a 16-gallon tank, every tank of gasoline you burn costs you about $7.50 extra. So if you need to fill the tank once a week, that's an extra $390 a year.

When I hear that crude oil prices rose or fell during the day, will I see that at the pump immediately?

There is a far quicker transmission from crude oil prices to the pump than there used to be, according to Michael Lynch, president of Strategic Energy & Economic Research in Winchester. A change in crude oil pricing can be seen ''within a week," he said, as opposed to the six weeks it typically used to take.

So what does it mean if you hear that the price of crude oil has gone up, say $1 a barrel? Look for a 2- to 3-cent increase in the cost of gasoline, Lynch said.

Is it OK to buy regular gas if my car calls for high octane?

Yes, said John Paul, spokesman for AAA of Southern New England, particularly with newer cars. ''In most cases, today's cars can run safely on lower octane than recommended due to their sophisticated computer systems," he said. So 89 octane in a car that asks for 92 is likely OK.

But drop down too much, Paul said, and the fuel is not burned efficiently so you end up using more gasoline. Still, he recommends against using a higher octane than called for in the mistaken belief that you get better mileage.

What is octane?

Octane, according to the Road & Track Illustrated Automotive Dictionary, is ''an expression of a gasoline antiknock quality." Sensors in your car monitor for ''knock," which occurs when compressed gases ignite prematurely in the cylinders. The sensors then adjust the engine so it runs smoothly. When you're not using high enough octane gas, that's the ping you can hear coming from your engine.

Are gas stations marking up certain octanes to make more money off me?

No, said AAA's Paul. There is a fairly consistent 10-cent jump from one level of octane to the next, dispelling the notion that stations might hike the price of a lower octane, say 89, expecting people to buy 91 grade instead.

But gas stations, according to industry analysts, do make more profit on high-octane gasolines.

Why do smaller, independent stations seem to offer cheaper prices than big brands? And is it OK to buy gas from the lower-priced stations?

According to Jesse Toprak, pricing and marketing analyst for Edmunds.com, stations charge ''whatever they can get" for gasoline. And in some cases, he said, that means the bigger brand names, often sold at newer, fancier stations, give at least ''the appearance they provide better gas, better service."

In fact, he said, except for a few additives, ''gasoline is more or less gasoline."

The larger brand names also have built-in costs, such as heavy advertising. ''When was the last time you saw a Hess commercial?" AAA's Paul asked. ''Mobil advertises nonstop."

How come gas prices can be very different in the same area?

Disparities in stations close to each other, explains Lynch of Strategic Energy & Research, can come about because one owner's tanks may have gone empty and were refilled at a more opportune time than a nearby competitor who paid more to fill his tanks.

Conversely, gas stations near each other often have similar prices. Are the owners conspiring?

''They're not conspiring; they're competing," said Paul. ''In fact, that can be good for the consumer" because you can ''in essence have a mini-gas war" on any given street.

Are there any deals on gas?

In isolated areas on given days, some stations will offer several cents off a gallon. So if you want to be cost-conscious, you can shop around. But keep in mind you'll have to drive to find the deals that pop up occasionally -- and how far do you drive to save $1.50 on a fill-up?

Are New Englanders paying more for gas than the rest of the country?

The national average last week, according to AAA, was $2.04 for regular. Averages were $2.09 in Massachusetts, $2.05 in New Hampshire, $2.08 in Maine, $2.13 in Rhode Island, $2.05 in Vermont, and $2.16 in Connecticut.

There are pockets of pricier gas. ''We see higher prices in metro Boston, on Cape Cod, and the Islands," Paul said. ''But across the rest of New England, prices are remarkably similar."

I'm looking to buy a new car. How much money would I save on gas if I bought a hybrid?

It would depend on what you moved out of. The hybrids get 40 to 50 miles per gallon, so a move from an SUV or a large sedan would probably save you hundreds of gallons per year.

However, moving from an already efficient car, such as a Toyota Camry, into a hybrid might not be cost-effective since the hybrids are priced about $2,000 more than a conventional car.

Can I just buy a more fuel-efficient engine?

Four-cylinder engines, hybrids, and a new engine called ''displacement on demand" are the best bets. Displacement on demand shuts off half the engine's cylinders when cruising on the highway or in gentle driving.

So-called continuously variable transmissions are also on the market now. Audi, Mini, Saturn, and BMW are among those offering them. These transmissions use belts or chains that run between pulleys. The pulleys open and close according to demand and there are virtually limitless ''gears" in which the car can run, meaning there are none of the inefficient gaps that occur at the high end of gears in regular transmissions.

But drivers should be wary of gimmicks, such as additives and engine attachments, that promise better gas mileage. ''Don't fall for those fuel-saving devices," said AAA's Paul. ''They don't work."
