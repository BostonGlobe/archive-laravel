Streamlining work life is often mind over matter

June 13, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- I decided that I could no longer put off some badly needed knee surgery. Although I hadn't reached the knee-replacement stage, serious hiking had become a problem. I told my boss that I would be taking three sick days to have the procedure done. Those days plus a weekend was my planned time for recovery. The operation took place on a Wednesday and I was back at my desk on the following Monday.

But the only way I could get to work and back on my recovering knee was by hitching a ride with my husband. He is a college professor and has more control over the time he works. And he chooses to be home before 6 p.m. So that meant I had to start limping out of my office much earlier than my usual schedule permitted.

And guess what? I was home at 5:45 p.m. and found out what it means to have some time to get things done on a work evening. Once or twice I even read a book in bed for more than my usual five minutes before turning out the light.

Did my work suffer under this schedule? Not a bit. I did save some time by chatting with fewer people because I had to stay put in my office. Meetings came to me or I couldn't attend. I ate lunch at my desk.

Maybe I was working more efficiently. Maybe I was more focused. I knew I did not have the luxury of staying at the office until I accomplished what I wanted to each day, so I prioritized better.

I forced myself not to do the easier things to get them out of the way but to do what was essential for that day. I was better about deleting without reading e-mails that announced various events I couldn't go to anyway. Since my trips down the hall to the ladies' room were an effort, I multitasked, getting water for my plants on the same trip. I streamlined my story of what was wrong with me and didn't pause to listen to everyone else's story about their current or former knee problems.

After three weeks as the more efficient me, I realized that I didn't need the ride with my husband anymore. The first day or so without needing a ride, I was still in the mode of leaving at a decent hour. But as the days passed, I found myself answering just a few more e-mails, or completing one more project. I was sticking my head into people's offices and actually having short social conversations -- maybe even discussing a movie we had seen. I had slipped into my old habits.

I don't mind working later. I learned something important about the nature of work during my convalescence from surgery. It's always there the next day. I also know I can get my work done between 8:30 a.m. and 5:30 p.m. And this is a good thing because I get the feeling that my other knee is going to need some work before too long.
