Some workers investing in backup careers Attitudes changing toward backup careers, specialists say Secondary skills can pay off in an uncertain job market

By Alan R. Earls, Globe Correspondent  |  June 13, 2004

When it comes to your career, it never hurts to have a backup plan.

ADVERTISEMENT

In a fast-paced economy where change is the only certainty, some workers have found it pays to have an alternate career ready in case their real job evaporates.

Jay Foley of Methuen is a technical skills trainer who has ridden the boom and bust cycles of his industry. ''When things go bad, trainers are the first to go," he said. ''After I got laid off in 2002, I finally said, I can't keep doing this. I have to have something to fall back on." In Foley's case, that something turned out to be photography.

''Years ago I took a lot of pictures and had fun but I put that away when I had kids and started a career," he said.

Now, Foley has invested in the latest digital technology, joined a professional photography group, and taken on freelance assignments with the goal of carving out a backup career. While he loves his training position at a Boston-area healthcare organization, experience has taught him that it pays to be prepared.

''Nowadays you need to build secondary skills in case you are suddenly pushed out of your main job," he said.

Foley's situation is hardly unique. In the most recent recession, many professionals found themselves out of work for extended periods. Some turned to the kind of backup career Foley created as a way to help make mortgage payments and buy groceries. But with the pace of job creation and hiring picking up, second careerists may have some explaining to do when they come face to face with hiring managers.

While a backup career may be a necessity for some, specialists warn they can present a risk for job applicants seeking to jump back into their old career. Those doing the hiring are traditionally wary of people whose commitment to their career could be viewed as less than absolute.

''I think you have to be careful about admitting these things," said Marcie Schorr Hirsch, principal of Hirsch/Hills Associates, a management consulting organization in Newton. Hiring managers already have their hands full discerning differences among applicants, she said. Having to explain an unusual career path or a side career is just throwing them a curveball.

''You don't want to be remembered as the applicant who also sews curtains," she said. ''It doesn't add to one's professional allure."

For Steve Tolman, the looks of incomprehension from human resource professionals are familiar. Tolman began his work life in the welding and steel fabrication business and later worked in electronics. Now, armed with a bachelor of science in computer science, he is pursuing a career in high tech and relying on his experience in residential construction work to support himself.

''I am sure that some recruiters and HR personnel don't understand what I have accomplished and tend to put my rsum aside," he admitted.

But some career specialists believe attitudes toward backup careers are changing. ''It is not that uncommon anymore. For many people in fields like networking, telecommunications, and software, things have been bad for so long that many people have had to consider a temporary second career," said Richard Cusic, senior vice president of career consulting in the Boston office of Right Management Consultants.

''Many HR people themselves have been through the same thing in recent years," he added.

What is still important, he says, is avoiding gaps in a rsum. Hiring managers ''want to know what an individual has been doing during a hiatus," Cusic said. ''Something that shows activity and motivation rather than just throwing in the towel is very important."

However, he added, there are also big differences in how different kinds of secondary careers are perceived. For instance, he explained, an out-of-work high technology worker who takes a short-term position that used or expanded professional skills would be viewed much more favorably than one who took a job stocking shelves at Home Depot.

Cusic cites the example of a banker he counseled who decided to move into teaching while a series of mergers created career turmoil among the area's financial institutions. Because the banker has been able to teach subjects that are related to some of his professional skills, Cusic said engineering an eventual return to banking from teaching should be less problematic.

Such career excursions can even be useful. People who have been let go from their job have been handed a unique opportunity, said David Kimmelman, vice president, business development at Lee Hecht Harrison in Boston, an outplacement and consulting firm.

''It is a chance to look at some of the things they may have always wanted to do in the past but couldn't because they were so career-focused," he said.

''Not that it is a good thing to lose your job by any stretch of the imagination," he added, ''but what is important is what you do with the time you have been given."

Kimmelman said he has seen people laid off from corporate jobs move into a wide range of fields on a temporary basis. ''One person I know has gone into teaching music and another went into making custom golf clubs and several have gone into home decorating," he said.

''I always tell people you have nothing to lose by shooting for the moon," he added.

Kimmelman's own experience is another case in point. Downsized from a job as vice president of human resources at an Internet consulting company, he decided to pursue two of his personal passions -- sports and entertainment.

While a job never materialized in either area before he took his current position at Lee Hecht Harrison, Kimmelman said the experience gave him an opportunity to explore new directions.

People who take those experiences with them back to their ''regular" career need to use a little care and creativity in presenting them, said Schorr Hirsh.

''They will need to balance the need to show they are innovative and adaptive with showing they are truly dedicated professionals," she said. ''As people look back on the last few years from a distance, they will no longer think it was so odd to be away from one's regular career for 18 months."
