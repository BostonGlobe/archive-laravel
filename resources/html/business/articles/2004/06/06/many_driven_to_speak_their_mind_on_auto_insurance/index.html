CONSUMER BEAT

Many driven to speak their mind on auto insurance

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  June 6, 2004

Globe readers, in a blizzard of letters, e-mails, and web postings last week, called on Massachusetts officials to address the root causes of bad driving as part of their efforts to revamp the auto insurance system.

Responding to an article last Sunday that examined why Massachusetts drivers file more auto insurance claims than anyone else in the country, readers said the key reasons are inadequate driver training, lax enforcement of traffic safety laws, and a road system that is badly marked and often poorly designed.

Verne Henderson compared Massachusetts to California, where he used to live and where he said officials promote driving safety by focusing on the three "Es" of traffic control: engineering, education, and enforcement.

"We flunk on all three," said Henderson, who didn't give his town. "I believe we create bad drivers with our failure to deliver on the three Es."

Stephen D'Amato, executive director of the Center for Insurance Research in Cambridge, called for a cost-containment summit on auto insurance and suggested that a short-term surcharge on rates might be a way to finance accident-reducing initiatives that would reduce premiums in the long run.

D'Amato's priorities included the three Es, a tougher seatbelt law, and discounts for those who drive safer cars.

A top aide to Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly hinted that making the state's roads safer would be a key focus for Reilly in the deliberations of a special task force charged with revamping the Massachusetts auto insurance system.

"As we see it, safety is an essential element of any change we make to our auto insurance system," said Alice Moore, chief of Reilly's public protection bureau. She declined to be more specific.

Other readers bucked the general trend of blaming Massachusetts drivers or road conditions for the high number of claims here.

Ivan Sever of Needham said the real problem was rampant fraud, effectively condoned by an insurance industry that has no financial interest in reducing claims.

"It is our rotting insurance system that's worse than other states," Sever said. "Let's do away with it first, before we pass judgment on the state's drivers, shall we?"

Most people thought a massive and long-term enforcement and education campaign was necessary to convince motorists that the rules of the road apply to them.

"If you run a red light in Boston, there's almost no chance you'll get caught, but if you linger five minutes extra at a parking meter you'll almost certainly get a ticket. Doesn't this seem a little skewed?" asked Alan Packard of Framingham.

Bud Hodgkins Jr. of Medford, who has had two cars totaled in the last three years because of other drivers running a stop sign or red light, said police need to issue far more tickets.

"I would be shocked if anyone driving in the city of Medford ever got a ticket for failing to stop at a stop sign, tailgating, or failure to yield at a rotary," he said.

Records compiled by the state's Merit Rating Board indicate there was an 8 percent statewide decline in traffic violation convictions between 2001 and 2003, with a 7 percent decline in convictions for failure to stop at a stoplight or stop sign.

Board officials say the overall drop and the decline for specific violations may not be that severe, however, because a number of cases from 2003 are still pending in the courts.

Jon W. Engvall of Waltham said he wanted more enforcement, but he would also like automobile advertisements promoting irresponsible driving (think Mazda's "zoom zoom" campaign) to be toned down and tinted windows that hinder eye contact among drivers to be banned.

Max Fornwalt of Scituate said he thinks the biggest problem is that Massachusetts drivers are ignorant when it comes to good driving habits.

"They don't know the rules in a traffic circle, they pass on the right, they follow too closely behind others, they don't signal, and they use the breakdown lanes even when they are not allowed," he said.

Derek Dover of Revere said he is convinced that if drivers here were retested on road rules every five years, nearly a fifth of them would lose their licenses.

Current state law requires no retesting or retraining after a driver obtains a license unless he or she has five surchargeable incidents in three years or seven in five years.

Driver training is required for new drivers under age 18 and a 1998 junior operator law imposes additional driving restrictions during the first six months after a license is obtained.

But Kathleen Carbonara of Halifax said the training of new drivers doesn't appear to be very rigorous. She said her two daughters both took driver training courses but did not learn the rules of the road. She said they passed the written test to get their driving permits without any preparation.

Shelby Morrison, 17, of Peabody, said she took a two-week driver training course last summer where the instructor had the students watch outdated movies about crashes and often let them watch television or leave early. She said she learned nothing useful during the course.

"Many people like to say that teenagers are too young and too immature to drive, which is not the case at all," Morrison said. "It is simply a lack of training, and we will take these poor driving skills with us on the road forever. Believe me, I hate paying $2,500 a year for insurance all on my own, but until people get better training, the situation will never get better."

411 for wireless numbers

An industry proposal to launch a directory assistance service for wireless phone numbers has generated enormous controversy among consumers who want their numbers kept private and out of the hands of telemarketers and spammers. But if it truly is an opt-in system, it's hard to see what the harm would be.

The Cellular Telecommunications and Internet Association is proposing to compile a directory assistance database for wireless numbers by early next year. With 8 million consumers carrying only a wireless phone and the number cutting the cord on a land line expected to soar over the next three years, the association says a wireless directory's time has come.

Travis Larson, an association spokesman, stressed that participants would have to opt in to the database even if their contract with their wireless carrier gives the carrier the right to publish their name and number. Larson said the database would not be published or made available on the Internet, making it difficult and expensive for spammers and telemarketers to utilize.

Verizon Wireless decided not to participate when the opt-in policy was less clear. Now it says its opposition is based more on the lack of need. "What's the point of having a directory when most of the people will opt out?" asked J. Abra Degbor, a Verizon Wireless spokeswoman.

Degbor said consumers who want to have their wireless numbers listed can put them in existing phone books. Verizon, of course, owns many of those books and charges wireless customers $18 to $26.40 a year to be listed.
