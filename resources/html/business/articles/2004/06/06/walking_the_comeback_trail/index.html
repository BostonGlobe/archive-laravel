Walking the comeback trail

Area businesses add jobs, make plans to expand

By Emily Shartin, Globe Staff  |  June 6, 2004

When Compound Therapeutics, a Waltham biotechnology firm, packed up its Main Street laboratory and moved to a larger space down the road on Beaver Street, it did so with expansion in mind.

"We did not have space to grow," said John Edwards, the company's vice president of commercial development.

And growth is what the young firm, which develops protein therapies for cancer and rheumatoid arthritis, is preparing for. As it readies its drugs for clinical trials, Edwards said, Compound Therapeutics is seeking more venture capital investments and could see its staff of 35 almost double within the next two or three years.

Amid signs of an economic uptick statewide, Compound Therapeutics is one of several companies in the western suburbs that are beginning to create jobs or otherwise lay the groundwork for expansion.

The activity comes as the state reports an unemployment rate of 4.8 percent for April, the lowest since December 2001 and more than a full percentage point lower than the 5.9 percent peak recorded in December 2003. Although it continues to move at a painstaking pace -- a handful of new commercial leases here, a scattering of new jobs there -- several economists and employers say they believe a long-term recovery has finally arrived.

"The news is definitely good," said Michael Goodman, director of economic and public policy research at the Donahue Institute at the University of Massachusetts. A group he is involved with, the New England Economic Partnership, recently reported that employment across New England grew in the first quarter of 2004 for the first time in three years.

Goodman also cited a recent study by the institute showing that small businesses across the state, a key economic indicator, are planning to add jobs within the next few months.

But the promising news is tempered by some sobering realities. While Massachusetts lost 200,000 jobs between 2001 and 2003, Goodman said, economists predict that only 61,000 new jobs will be created during the next year.

Many of those jobs, Goodman said, are also likely to crop up in sectors such as business/professional, health, and education -- not sectors like manufacturing and information technology, where the bulk of the jobs were lost. That means that many applicants will probably need additional training to qualify.

"It makes the finding-a-new-job process that much more challenging," Goodman said.

But increasingly, the jobs are out there. The state reports the creation of 5,500 positions in April. Locally, Franklin-based Eikos, a nanotechnology company that has developed a material used in LCD screens, hopes to double its staff of 18 in the next two months, according to president and CEO Joe Pich. Eikos will look to hire a range of employees, from administrative staff to engineers and chemists.

The company, which has several clients in Japan, recently negotiated with Franklin town officials for a tax break that will allow it to expand its facility off Beaver Street, but owners may still need a second facility, Pich said. If so, the company is likely to look for space in Franklin, he added, because it likes the location and has been pleased by the cooperation of town officials.

"Because of that, we're very loyal to [Franklin officials]," Pich said.

To negotiate the agreement with Franklin, Eikos worked with Lynn Tokarczyk, a Medway-based consultant who advises businesses on tax incentives and other programs enabling them to set up shop or expand operations. She said she is working with several clients along Interstate 495 who are attracted by the region's land availability, road, water, and sewer infrastructure, and nearby colleges and universities.

"The 495 belt is really the hotspot right now," Tokarczyk said.

Farther north on I-495, AstraZeneca International, which has offices in Westborough and Waltham, recently added a new production line at its Westborough facility, where the company manufactures the asthma drug Pulmicort Respules. The expansion, needed to meet growing demand for the drug, added 45 manufacturing jobs, said spokeswoman Kellie Rivest. In mid-2005, the company plans to add another line and another 45 jobs.

The trend toward job creation is starting to be seen in local unemployment groups. Fred Nothnagel, director of a job networking group called WIND (Wednesday is Networking Day), said attendance at networking meetings has dropped, a trend he attributes to more members finding work. (WIND holds regular meetings in four Massachusetts communities, including Westborough.)

Nothnagel noted that jobs are not growing at nearly the same rate that layoffs occurred, but he said that he had heard -- just in the span of the last few days -- from five WIND members who had found jobs.

"It would have been unbelievable six months ago," he said.

Preliminary figures from the MetroWest Economic Research Center at Framingham State College reflect similar drops in unemployment for the month of April: Framingham and eight surrounding communities report an average rate of 3.1 percent, down from 3.7 percent in March.

But if more residents of the area have found work, fewer people overall are looking for jobs, center statisticians reported, a possible indication that many unemployed workers have become discouraged by the prolonged downturn.

"Of course a falling unemployment rate is good news," said Martha Meaney, an economist at the center. "The question becomes why is there such a fall in the labor force itself."

In his 25 years in the business, Bob Davis, president and CEO of The Davis Companies, a Marlborough-based temporary employment agency, said he's never seen a recovery as "conservative" as this. But activity in his firm has more than doubled over the past 18 months, he said. His agency has about 200 job openings in New England, many of which could result in permanent employment.

Given that an increase in contract employment is typically considered a sign of an economic upswing -- companies are likely to hire temporary help before committing to permanent employees -- Davis is optimistic about where the local economy is headed.

"The market has definitely moved in a very positive direction," he said.
