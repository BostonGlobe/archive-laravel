New Internet services offered by wireless telecom firms now give customers a means to use their laptops almost anyplace, along with their cellphones. And the technology is altering the way people do business.

By Peter J. Howe, Globe Staff  |  June 6, 2004

After years of building out nationwide networks, leading wireless telecommunications companies can finally promise to connect your laptop computer to the Internet virtually anywhere you can get your cellphone to work, and the technology is beginning to change the way people do business.

The new services launched by Verizon Wireless, Sprint PCS, AT&T Wireless Services Inc., and others can't come close to the speed offered by so-called WiFi hotspots, areas of airport lounges, hotels, and coffee shops where mobile workers can enjoy megabit-speed wireless connections. But unlike WiFi, which only covers zones of a few hundred square feet, the new coverage is available virtually throughout metropolitan areas and along major highways, at good-enough speeds comparable to dial-up modems.

From UPS delivery truck drivers to utility meter readers, millions of on-the-go American workers have been using wireless data services for years. Many of these services, though, have been oriented around highly specialized devices that may have only limited capacity to get e-mail and surf the Web. What has started to revolutionize the workplace -- separating the concept of ''doing your job" from ''being at the office" -- is the growing availability and reliability of off-the-shelf ''air card" data services that can be used by anyone with a laptop to get virtually the same access to his company computer network as he would have in the office, with the same look and feel of the office desktop.

Earlier this spring, Horizon Beverage, a leading New England liquor and wine distributor, began outfitting 140 of its salespeople with Sprint wireless air cards, replacing a first-generation wireless data service that offered spotty coverage and connection speeds of only a few thousand kilobits per second. ''This whole thing has changed the way we do business," said Lew Pollock, Horizon's director of information technology, giving sales people as much as 60 to 90 minutes in additional productive work time each day as they make sales rounds.

Tony Khouri, a Taunton-based wholesale food and restaurant supply salesman, said with the new Verizon Wireless air card he has been using for more than a year, ''It's like sitting in the office being out on the road. In the customer's office, you have your office with you."

Before going wireless, Khouri, who has worked for Agar Supply Inc. since 1984, might have visited a restaurant or catering customer, found out what supplies he wanted, called a clerk at the office to find out what was in stock, gone back to the client, then written up the order to file back at the office. Or he would have had to cadge a phone line from his customer to plug in his laptop and connect to the office.

''Now you can tell me, 'Tony, I want 600 steaks' or 'I want 100 pounds of swordfish,' and in two seconds, while I am sitting in your office, I can check the inventory with my laptop and order it right then and there," Khouri said -- not to mention the convenience of being able to get his e-mail anytime and virtually anywhere.

''It's made my job, besides being a lot more efficient, a lot more fun," said Khouri, one of 30 Agar salespeople now using the devices. ''It's really one of the reasons I am successful."

As coverage from all six national carriers has widened across the country over the last year, analysts see a mobile data market on the brink of finally taking off. Yankee Group analysts John Jackson and Eugene C. Signorini predict that just over 1 million wireless data air cards -- credit card-sized transmitter-receiver devices that plug into a slot in the side of a laptop -- will be sold nationally this year.

''We're seeing a lot of interest in distribution and sales operations where there's an important immediacy to the information that you are conveying to the customer or capturing from them," Signorini said. ''A lot of the value of the air card comes from the fact that it's a small step from what you've already been doing on a desktop," rather than a handheld or Palm Pilot-style computer that people have to train themselves to use, Signorini added.

Nextel Communications Inc., Sprint, T-Mobile USA, and Verizon have all begun selling services that offer mobile data speeds of 40 to 70 kilobits per second, comparable to a 56 kbps telephone modem. AT&T Wireless last November began activating a so-called EDGE network that offers connections up to 130 kilobits per second, and Cingular Wireless this spring has quietly begun turning on its own EDGE network in Southern New England and other areas totaling about two-thirds of its national cellphone service area.

Software that links the devices to a company network can give employees the same access they would have sitting at their desk, and security features -- including use of passwords and digital pass-keys to get on the company network and high-level encryption of data passing through the air -- make wireless data services at least as safe as connecting by dial-up modem.

Verizon Wireless spokeswoman J. Abra Degbor said the company advises business and institutional customers signing up for its NationalAccess service to manage it exactly the same way they would a landline network connection for a telecommuting worker, with a firewall and ''virtual private network" safeguards.

In the world of high technology, many companies like to brag that they ''eat their own dog food," another way of saying they use the products or devices or software they make and sell to others.

Chad Brigham of Lucent Technologies Inc. is an example of someone who not only eats the dog food but helps make it in the first place.

Brigham, a cell site technician who activates and troubleshoots wireless tower base stations in a territory from Maine to Cape Cod, began using wireless data cards for his personal computer six months ago, and has found they regularly save him one or two hours a day of travel time.

Besides getting a daily work assignment, Brigham also has to frequently download software upgrades for cellular switching gear -- Lucent's version of a Microsoft Windows software update for a personal computer -- that he then installs in base station switching gear. Previously, that meant swinging by his Framingham office each morning, even if it was in the opposite direction from wherever he was destined to work.

Now, Brigham sets up his computer to get an e-mail with his day's work assignment; it also downloads any new software patches overnight. ''Instead of being at the office at 7 o'clock and the cell site at 10 o'clock, I'm at the cell site at 8 o'clock," said Brigham. He can also use the laptop to connect to a Lucent network to consult manuals -- 900-page documents that would be impractical to carry in his truck and that get revised frequently -- and use the wireless connection to tweak software in the base station switches from the front seat of his truck. ''It's made my job 1,000 times easier, to be sure," Brigham said.

Several carriers have plans in the works to move wireless data to broadband office speeds, which could attract millions more subscribers. Verizon Wireless, for example, this year said it would spend $1 billion over the next two years expanding its ''Broadband Access" service nationwide, which offers average connection speeds of 300 to 500 kilobits per second, with maximum potential access at 2 megabits per second.

Verizon began rolling it out last year in the Washington, D.C., and San Diego areas.

Nextel Communications Inc. began testing a similarly high-powered network in the Raleigh-Durham, N.C., area this spring, and Sprint plans to go to a 3-megabit network starting in 2006.

Karen Bressler, Agar Supply's chief executive, said her 30 salespeople who have moved to the Verizon system rapidly found mobile data access to the office network to be indispensable.

''It's like thinking about how did we all live without a cellphone?" Bressler said. ''Wireless computing is really the next generation of that."
