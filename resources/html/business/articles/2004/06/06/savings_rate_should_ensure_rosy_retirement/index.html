THE ADVISER

Savings rate should ensure rosy retirement

By Kenneth Hooker  |  June 6, 2004

Q. I am 45 and my husband just turned 60. I earn $105,000 annually, plus annual bonuses of 10 to 15 percent. My husband works part time and earns about $20,000 per year. My 401(k) account is $94,000, entirely invested in Fidelity Freedom 2020 fund. I contribute 20 percent of my income, some on a pretax basis and the rest after-tax. My employer match is $4,725 a year. My husband's 401(k) balance is $16,000 and he contributes 16 percent of his income; his company match brings in an additional $720 annually. My IRAs total $14,000 and I add $3,000 per year. My husband's IRAs add up to $15,000 and he contributes $3,500 per year. All of these IRAs are in Vanguard Total Stock Market Index fund. Other savings add up to $16,000, with $10,000 in cash and $6,000 in a taxable Total Stock Market Index investment, to which we add $5,000 annually. We have $450,000 equity in our home and have exercisable stock options with a potential gain of $56,000. Our only debts are a mortgage and an auto loan.

If I work until age 65 and get 4 percent average annual raises, I can expect a pension of $5,000 per month. I don't know what my Social Security benefits might be. My husband will have no pension but will continue to work part time until age 65, at which point he will qualify for a $1,200 monthly Social Security benefit. When he retires, he won't take any distributions from his retirement accounts until required.

Do you think our investments are spread wisely? What do you think of using the Total Stock Market Index fund, compared with a Standard & Poor's 500 Index fund? If I can't stay on my job for another 20 years and can't count on a pension, is there a better way to invest so that we can retire comfortably at age 65?

E.M., San Diego

A. You'll be in terrific shape if you can continue saving at your current aggressive level for another 20 years.

If you continue with your own 401(k) and IRA programs, you would accumulate $1,893,008, presuming a conservative 8 percent average annual total return from your investments. Over the next five years, your husband's retirement accounts should grow to $91,609, again presuming 8 percent average returns. If it was then left untouched until age 70, the balance would be $142,034.

Finally, your taxable savings accounts would amount to $324,256 by the time you are age 65, again presuming 8 percent returns and continuing savings at a $5,000 annual rate.

In short, heaps of money there -- more than you would need to fill the gap between your pension and Social Security income and the $102,425 that you are now living on. (I used a median 12.5 percent annual bonus to calculate your current after-savings income.)

My guess is that your real question is what would happen if you retire before putting in another 20 years. According to my understanding of the nation's pension laws, it isn't a question of all-or-nothing on the pension. You would probably have to put in only another five years to get full pension vesting. The longer you work after that, the greater the benefit for which you will qualify.

On the other hand, you are the one who is behind most of the savings. You certainly will have to work another 12 to 15 years to build the balances and pension benefits to the point that you could retire comfortably.

I have no problem with any of your investments, all of which are broadly diversified. I don't feel strongly one way or another when it comes to Vanguard Total Stock Market fund in comparison to a Standard & Poor's Index fund, such as Vanguard Index 500. Although the Total Stock Market Index fund holds a portfolio of 3,331 stocks, the weighting of the portfolio is made according to the market capitalization of each issue, and the 500 stocks that dominate the 500 Index fund also dominate the Total Stock Market Index, with the small positions in the additional 2,831 stocks having a fairly modest effect.

Ginnie Mae investmentsare still a good bet

Q. Do you feel that Vanguard's or any other GNMA fund is a good investment?

R.H., Chestnut Hill

A. I think that investment in Ginnie Mae funds -- or in certificates as well -- is still solid.

Ginnie Mae certificates are bundles of mortgages guaranteed by the government, both for payment of principal and interest. But the primary factor securing these mortgages is the real estate for which the funds have been borrowed. And if a person who has taken a federally guaranteed mortgage doesn't pay, GNMA, through the bank that originated the loan, takes back the property, and usually everybody is paid off. The federal guarantee comes in only in the event that the proceeds of a foreclosure are inadequate to pay off the mortgage.

However, various government policies have created a situation that is likely to lead to a long period of higher interest rates, and for that reason I have been suggesting that new investments in GNMA funds are unwise at this time.

There is little danger that the mortgages that stand behind a GNMA certificate will not be paid as promised, either by the borrower or the government. But despite the federal guarantees, the value of a mortgage is weighed in the markets according to current and projected future interest rates. If rates rise (or are perceived as being about to rise), these mortgages can lose value and can lose value steeply if interest rates rise quickly.

Perceptions are important here. Vanguard GNMA returned only 2.5 percent last year and has a year-to-date loss of .73 percent as this is written just in anticipation of higher future rates. The real blood will flow when the actual changes come.

Vanguard GNMA has a splendid long-term record of bouncing back fairly quickly and substantially from bad interest rate seasons. I do not suggest that current holders (such as myself) sell out now. After all, they bought the shares to establish an income stream, and through good markets and bad that income stream will continue. Moreover, Vanguard GNMA's managers have taken some fairly strenuous steps to protect it from higher rates, primarily bringing the average effective duration of its portfolio down to 2.09 years. This should reduce the downside for the fund as rates rise.

Vanguard's low feesmake it Roth-ready

Q. I am 30 years old and receive a Teamsters Union pension, but I want to start a Roth IRA as well. What company do you think would be good for a Roth IRA, and what investment might be good for a 25- or 30-year accumulation?

C.T., San Diego

A. I think the Vanguard family of mutual funds is the best place for you. One reason is its low fees.

Vanguard is a mutual company, which means that the parent company is owned by the various Vanguard mutual funds, and there is no reason for the parent company to seek to generate profits. That's a tremendous advantage when competing with other fund families.

A typical managed stock mutual fund has an expense ratio of more than 1.00, which means that each year you will pay $1 or more in expenses for every $100 in your account. Vanguard's specialty is index funds, and managing an index fund is a great deal less expensive than hiring managers whose job is to beat the indexes. Combining index investing with no need for profits, Vanguard's expense ratio can go as low as .12, for Admiral shares in the Vanguard 500 Index fund. That represents 12 cents a year in expenses for every $100 in your account.

Vanguard also has a philosophy of conservative investing, giving its managers instructions to prudently follow the markets rather than trying to overachieve.

Finally, a person beginning his or her retirement portfolio needs to begin with a core holding -- a fund with a portfolio that is a blend of growth and value stocks. The Vanguard 500 Index fund, basically designed to track the Standard & Poor's 500 index, is such a fund. It invests in the stocks of the 500 dominant companies of the US economy, and generally reflects the health of the economy as a whole. Since its inception in 1976, it has delivered average annual total returns of 12.39 percent, and that's a kind of return that can look awfully good after 25 or 30 years.
