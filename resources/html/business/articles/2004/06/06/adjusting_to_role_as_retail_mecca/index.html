STOUGHTON

Adjusting to role as retail mecca

IKEA latest evidence of town's popularity

By Sandy Coleman, Globe Staff  |  June 6, 2004

By fall, IKEA, an international furniture store chain, plans to break ground on a 346,000-square-foot outlet in Stoughton. It will be the largest retail shop in town and the latest evidence that the town is experiencing an economic boom not seen in more than 20 years, according to residents and local officials.

Many new big-name businesses have opened in town during the past year, and others are on their way. Designer Shoe Warehouse, or DSW, Bed Bath & Beyond, and Panera Bread stores recently opened in the renovated RK Plaza on Park Street. A Starbucks coffee house opened in March on Washington Street. A Kohl's department store opened in April on Technology Center Drive. A 119,000-square-foot Boston Interiors office, furniture showroom and distribution center is under construction on Page Street.

Two other furniture stores also have been proposed on Technology Center Drive, said Town Manager Mark Stankiewicz. And, Olive Garden and Smokey Bones BBQ restaurants are scheduled to open in the fall on the same street, according to Terry Schneider, president of the Stoughton Chamber of Commerce.

Town officials, planners, and residents attribute the flood of new businesses to water restrictions being eased dramatically since Stoughton hooked up to the Massachusetts Water Resources Authority system last spring. Although land has been available, business developers were wary of setting up shop in town because of the restrictions, aimed at conserving water use, said Joseph Mokrisky, chairman of the Stoughton Redevelopment Authority.

Stoughton has also drawn interest with its easy access to routes 24, 27, and 138, he said.

Schneider said he believes the natural competitiveness of businesses is also driving the boom.

"I don't think the fact that we have water is bringing people in. We haven't put an ad out," he said. "Sometimes other businesses follow other business almost to the point that they accept the other company's market research and they need to be across the street from them.

"If you see a CVS go in, there is almost a guarantee that there will be a Walgreens across the street."

IKEA proves Schneider's point. Spokesman Joseph Roth said IKEA representatives looked north, west, and south of Boston, studying towns within a 30- to 40-minute drive of the city, before settling on Stoughton. The new store would go up on an extension of Stockwell Drive off Route 24, near the Avon town line.

"We chose to go south because there was available land, first of all. Secondly, there was a retail corridor that was developing," said Roth. "Home furnishing is just like any other retail. The more there are in the area, the greater the amount of customers that will come to the area."

Many Stoughton residents welcome the convenient shopping. And town officials say they are pleased the new businesses broaden the town's tax base without burdening the school system the way large-scale residential developments would. (IKEA representative Paul Jean said the company will contribute more than $1 million annually in local property taxes, invest more than $4.1 million in road improvements, and bring about 400 new jobs.)

However, some worry about the additional traffic and the potential loss of remaining open spaces and damage to wetlands as more concrete structures and asphalt parking lots cover the landscape.

"The good thing is that if you get good, quality development, it helps the tax base and employment," said Stankiewicz. "The downside is more traffic congestion."

S. David Graber, an environmental and water resources engineer who has lived in Stoughton for 35 years, questions at what point the benefits of development lose their appeal.

"Beyond a certain point, it seems whatever benefit is gained in increasing the tax base is lost by the reduction in the quality of life, in particular, the loss of open space," he said.

Graber, who lives near the IKEA site, and nine of his neighbors have asked the state Department of Environmental Protection to review the local Conservation Commission's approval of IKEA's plans for water detention basins. Graber said he and neighbors believe the current plan will result in flooding in the area.

Increased traffic and harm to the environment are concerns that residents like Graber are focusing on as development takes off in town. Over the past year, residents have been attending community development forums sponsored by the Metropolitan Area Planning Council to set priorities for open space protection, economic development, and housing.

"One of the major concerns expressed by residents of the town was that they wanted to be aware of the potential conflicts between continued commercial and industrial growth and the abutting residential neighborhoods," said Mark Racicot, manager of government services for the Metropolitan Area Planning Council and a Stoughton resident.

"The development of the community in the manner that meets the goals of the town should be supported. The question is whether or not the currently vacant land is zoned for the uses the residents want," he said.

Racicot said residents at the forums have recommended that town officials carefully reexamine zoning bylaws to make sure they are in keeping with the vision of those who live in Stoughton.

Ultimately, how one feels about development comes down to where one lives in relationship to it, said Cynthia Walsh, a retired elementary school teacher who has lived in Stoughton since 1948. "Most folks see [the new development boom] as a good thing unless it is next to their house or they feel it will impact traffic in front of their house."

Walsh said Park Street, where she lives in the house she inherited from her parents, has gone from a quiet road on which she could play as a youngster to one that carries thousands of vehicles a day. There once was a farm next door, but now the land is occupied by a trucking company, she said. And, the shopping plaza housing the new Panera Bread cafe, a half-mile from her house, was once a turkey farm.

"I've seen a lot of changes," she said.

Walsh said she takes them all in stride -- even the current flurry of activity.

"I'm not one of those people who jumps up and down and screams and yells" because of change, she said. "Anything that brings in tax dollars that would decrease my taxes, I'm in favor of."
