NStar Electric raising rates next month

June 29, 2004

NStar Electric residential customers who are on "default service" will see their monthly bills rise starting next month by nearly $2 a month on average if they are in the former ComElectric service territory, but drop by about 35 to 80 cents a month in metropolitan Boston. The rates, approved this month by state utility regulators, apply only to people who opened an NStar account after March 1998, or more than 40 percent of current customers. "Standard offer" rates applying to most residential customers are not changing until fall. NStar spokesman Michael Durand said for an average residential customer using 500 kilowatt hours monthly, bills will increase $1.86 for customers served by the former ComElectric, and decrease by 76 cents for Boston Edison customers, and 37 cents for Cambridge Electric. Although it operates all three utilities under the NStar brand, they act as separate companies in buying wholesale power, which accounts for the different rates, Durand said. (Peter J. Howe)

Jet maker gets order from Buffett unit

Raytheon Co., the world's fourth-largest maker of business jets, got an order for 40 aircraft worth more than $300 million from NetJets Inc., which is owned by Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway Inc. NetJets, which sells fractional ownerships of planes for the right to fly them for a predetermined amount of time, bought 20 Hawker 800 XP midsize jets and 20 Hawker 400XP light jets. The aircraft will be delivered between 2005 and 2007, Waltham-based Raytheon said. Deepening ties with NetJets, the world's biggest buyer of business jets, helped Raytheon gain share from market leader Textron Inc.'s Cessna unit last year. Since December 2003, Raytheon has won about $1 billion of business from NetJets, including an order for 50 Hawker 400 XPs, which replaced an order NetJets had canceled with Textron. (Bloomberg)

ArcLight Capital opens second fund

ArcLight Capital Partners, a Boston private equity firm, said it has raised a $1.6 billion fund to invest in energy businesses. This is the firm's second fund, bringing its total managed capital to $2.5 billion over three years. ArcLight launched its latest fund-raising effort in October and has commitments from 75 large investors, including the University of Texas endowment and the California Public Employees Retirement System. The fund will do deals in industries related to coal, natural gas, power generation, electricity, and gas. (Beth Healy)

Chief operating officer to resign

Textron Inc., a maker of golf carts, Cessna planes, and Bell helicopters, said chief operating officer Steven R. Loranger will leave the company to join ITT Industries Inc. as president and chief executive, effective immediately. Providence-based Textron said Loranger's post has been eliminated and the company's four manufacturing segments will report directly to Textron chairman, president, and chief executive Lewis B. Campbell. Textron Financial will continue to report to chief financial officer Ted French. Textron also named Barclay Olson as president of the industrial segment to succeed Loranger. Olson has been president of the Tools & Components group since June 2003. (AP)

Moody's might lower AT&T credit rating

AT&T Corp., the largest US long-distance carrier, may have its credit rating cut by Moody's Investors Service after the company lowered its revenue and profit forecasts. About $11.5 billion of debt would be affected, Moody's said. The company rates AT&T's long-term debt Baa2, two levels above noninvestment grade, or junk. AT&T's P2 short-term rating may also be cut, Moody's said. AT&T would pay an additional $16.3 million a year to bondholders for each level Moody's or Standard & Poor's cuts the rating, spokesman Andy Backover said. S&P said Thursday that it might lower AT&T's credit ratings one or two levels because of the forecast. (Bloomberg)

Amazon seeks split from Toys 'R' Us

Online retailer Amazon.com, responding to a lawsuit by Toys "R" Us Inc., is seeking to dissolve their partnership, citing a "chronic failure" by the nation's number two toy seller to adhere to their contract. Amazon.com suggested it should be awarded at least $750 million as compensation for lost shipping revenue and other costs, in a countersuit filed Friday in state Superior Court in Paterson, N.J. A message seeking comment from Wayne, N.J.-based Toys "R" Us was not returned. Toys "R" Us subsidiary Toysrus.com sued Amazon.com in May, charging that the Web retailer violated exclusivity terms by selling, and letting others sell, products that their agreement required can be sold only by Toys "R" Us. Toys "R" Us claimed it paid $200 million over four years for the privilege of having competitors kept off the website. (AP)

Independent truckers strike seaports

Hundreds of independent truckers are on strike at several US ports, slowing shipment of containers that can hold everything from furniture to frozen foods. The truckers are upset over wages, rising fuel costs, and antiunion laws. It's unclear how many are taking part in a strike that started yesterday and is set to last until Sunday. Demonstrators appeared at ports in New Orleans and in Newark. There were also a few in Boston. In South Carolina, dozens walked picket lines outside the Port of Charleston, the nation's fourth-busiest container port. Independent truckers want to form unions and collectively bargain for better contracts from shipping and trucking companies that hire them out. (AP)

Retailer cuts monthly sales forecast

Wal-Mart Stores Inc., the world's largest retailer, reduced its monthly sales forecast for the first time in a year and a half after shoppers spent less than expected on Father's Day gifts amid concerns about inflation. Sales at US Wal-Mart stores open at least a year will rise 2 to 4 percent this month from a year earlier, less than a previous estimate of 4 to 6 percent, the discounter said. The retailer last cut its monthly sales forecast in December 2002. Wal-Mart last week said it expects second-quarter net income to rise to as much as 62 cents a share from 56 cents a year earlier. The retailer was expected to earn 61 cents, the average estimate of 29 analysts surveyed by Thomson Financial. (Bloomberg)

PeopleSoft CEO won't be called to testify

In a surprise move, Oracle Corp. has decided against calling PeopleSoft Inc. chief executive Craig Conway as a witness in an antitrust trial that could make or break the hostile takeover bid proposing to combine the rival business software makers. Oracle's decision deflates a bit of drama from the monthlong trial as the legal showdown heads into his final week. As the mastermind of PeopleSoft's tenacious resistance to Oracle's $7.7 billion takeover bid, Conway was expected to be a combative witness. Before the trial started, Oracle attorneys predicted they would grill Conway for six hours -- far longer than anyone else on the company's list of 25 witnesses. Oracle had subpoenaed Conway to appear at the trial today, but the company's legal team decided over the weekend that most the chief executive's testimony would echo statements and other evidence that had already been presented. (AP)

Justice Department subpoenas Adecco

Adecco SA, the world's largest provider of temporary workers, said the Justice Department issued a grand jury subpoena for a variety of books and records. The company's response to the subpoena has been delayed while it provides information to the Securities and Exchange Commission related to an investigation of the previously disclosed issues in the way Switzerland-based Adecco accounted for North American revenue, according to a regulatory filing. Adecco said in the filing it intends to cooperate fully with both agencies. (Bloomberg)
