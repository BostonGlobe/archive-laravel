Mass. marketing

June 4, 2004

Richard Ireland, chief financial officer of New England Biolabs Inc. in Beverly, said the company has been making plans for several years, even before Romney took office in 2003, to add about 100 jobs as part of a pending move to Ipswich. He said officials from the quasi-public agency MassDevelopment were helpful in securing $2.5 million in research tax credits, however.

Another company that Berke cites as a win, the British vaccine maker Acambis Inc., only loosely counts as a victory. In January, the company said it would close down its research office in Cambridge, England, eliminating 40 jobs, as it abandoned several products lines. That left the vaccine maker with 280 employees worldwide, including 250 in Massachusetts. That those jobs remain, Berke pointed out, is a positive, but executives said it had little to do with state policy.

"That was an internal operational efficiency decision, rather than a strategic move away from UK to Cambridge, MA," wrote chief executive Gordon Cameron in an e-mail.

Alan Clayton-Matthews, professor of public policy at the University of Massachusetts at Boston, said Romney would do better in the long run by increasing funding for the state's education system and finding ways to control housing costs.

"The main attraction for business in Massachusetts is the large, educated workforce," Clayton-Matthews said. Romney "can draw more companies here by focusing on the basics. He can be more effective that way than by talking to executives."

Some industry leaders say they're pleased with the administration's efforts selling the state.

"In the last year, we have seen both the governor's administration and leaders in the State House really step up to their responsibility of promoting the biotech industry in Massachusetts," said Mark Trusheim, interim president of an industry trade group, the Massachusetts Biotechnology Council in Cambridge. "They've come to realize we are in competition with other states across the country."

Wooing biotechnology and other life-sciences companies has become a priority for the administration because they promise both highly paid jobs for people with advanced degrees and decent pay for manufacturing workers, without raising environmental concerns. At the same time, these companies often lose money for years while they develop their treatments so they're acutely sensitive to incentives.

The state has approved some direct transfers, such as a tax rebate life-sciences companies can get for several years after they hire new manufacturing employees. But most of their efforts have focused on relatively soft measures such as helping companies find new sites for their facilities or connecting them with training funds. Last year, even when legislative Democrats wanted to add $100 million in an economic-stimulus package such as offering venture funds for companies, Romney vetoed much of the proposal. The Legislature later overrode the veto. Berke said the funding has been helpful.

State officials have tried to recruit companies through the cooperation of groups including the Massachusetts Office of Business Development, MassDevelopment, and the Massachusetts Alliance for Economic Development. Last year these groups and others like the Massachusetts Biotechnology Council spent $750,000 on an ad campaign using the slogan, "It's All Here." This year they plan to put up to $500,000 more into the effort.

The administration also has been focused on reaching out to European companies, including sending a representative to trade shows and signing a trade agreement with Scottish officials in which both sides pledged to support each others' companies and promote exports.

Berke said the administration isn't trying to take credit for every decision by the employers. But their decisions are a measure of the state's competitive position.

"What we're trying to say is that it's a team effort when we win, not just the Romney administration," Berke said.

Since the biotech conference last year, she added, "it's very clear that Massachusetts is open for business in a way it wasn't before."
