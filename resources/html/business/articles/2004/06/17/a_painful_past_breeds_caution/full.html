A painful past breeds caution

Despite leap in oil prices, firms still wary in Houston

By Charles Savage, Globe Staff  |  June 17, 2004

HOUSTON -- In a hayfield 40 miles west of this energy capital's glass towers squats a rusty pump, apparently abandoned years ago when most of the pool of oil below was drained away. But two black pumps are now chugging away nearby, while a portable drilling rig scours the depleted earth for any remaining precious pockets of oil and natural gas.

A barrel of crude that sold for as little as $10 half a decade ago now costs four times as much -- hammering most Americans at the gas pump but reviving flush times for a lot of oil companies.

So Houston should be rocking, right?

Actually, caution prevails in this region where, in times past, soaring energy prices led to expansive booms as people threw up skyscrapers and splurged on world-class goods. Houston, still recovering from the national recession and the twin blows of hurricane flooding and the Enron collapse, isn't popping champagne corks -- at least, not yet.

Painful memories of the busts that followed past booms have made businesses wary of basing investments on current prices, which economists say are driven as much by ephemeral fears of Middle East violence as real demand. Moreover, the ground under Texas is running out of oil, so jobs created by new prospecting are increasingly likely to be overseas.

Where 80 percent of the Houston economy was connected to the energy business 20 years ago, today half of it is driven by other industries. Part of the remaining energy businesses are refineries, which buy oil and therefore can be hurt by higher prices. And Houston drivers are losing a portion of their income to higher gas prices just like everyone else.

All this means that while $2-a-gallon gas still means short-term riches for energy companies, Texas as a whole has lost much of its ''black gold" sass. Despite the lingering image created by early 1980s TV shows like ''Dallas," the boomtown days for energy cities like Houston may be gone forever, economists and business leaders say.

''Texas went through an incredible depression in 1985 and 1986 when the props got knocked out from under the oil business, and the lesson of the human debris was not lost on anybody tied to the energy industry," said Travis Tullos, an Austin, Texas, economist. ''Whenever they start feeling giddy, they say 'Wait a minute, the last time I felt this way, I paid a heavy price.' "

Raymond Plank, founder of the independent energy company Apache Corp., now based in Houston, said most companies are using the extra money to pay down debt -- improving their balance sheets, but not spending it in ways that would create a multiplier effect for the regional economy.

''People don't believe that it's necessarily here to last, and therefore they aren't jumping up and down with joy," Plank said. ''The other day we saw oil go up 5 percent in a day, and we've seen natural gas rise and fall as much as 7 percent in a day. That kind of price volatility -- gymnastic and dramatic -- does not breed long-term confidence for investments."

Executives in the energy business think that fear of supply disruptions from the Middle East caused by the Iraq war and Al Qaeda attacks in Saudi Arabia has inflated the price of oil by up to $10 a barrel more than it would be according to fundamental market forces, said Tim Hopper, an economist who studies Houston for the Federal Reserve System.

So while they're delighted to pocket the extra cash, they are reluctant to invest it in projects whose viability would depend on the price of oil remaining this high for years, he said.

That means makers of oil equipment are not yet growing, which has always been part of a true regional boom, said University of Houston economist Barton Smith. While more rigs are being leased to drill in the Gulf of Mexico, he said, there are still more stacked up in storage and so there's no need to build more of them.

''Despite the high price of oil today, exploration activity hasn't gotten to the point where you really need to accelerate the production of equipment," he said.

Hopper, for one, predicted that investors' caution about prices may be starting to change as the world economy recovers, led by China's huge economic growth, and demand for energy surges.

''The longer energy prices stay high, the more momentum it will build in Houston," Hopper said. ''The fact is that it's basically been high for two years, since the lead-up to the Iraq war, and that's creating the sense that it's here for awhile."

Strong profits are starting to lead to new hiring and rising salaries at energy companies, he added, which in turn will have ''a huge bleed-over into all sectors of Houston's economy."

According to the Institute for Regional Forecasting at the University of Houston, the city's economy had been in decline since 2002 but turned around in November. It is expected to add 45,000 jobs this year, a growth rate of a bit over 2 percent.

Meantime, Tullos said, sales tax collection figures from March showed consumer spending up nearly 15 percent from a year ago.

The city's constantly expanding suburbs -- Greater Houston is the size of Rhode Island -- have kept housing prices much lower than in other parts of the country, reducing the sense of wealth that helps feed a boom, economists said.

Also depressing the potential for a new construction boom is an oversupply of office space because of the scandals and consolidations of recent years. Said Plank: ''The Enron building finally got taken over by a combination of Chevron and Texaco, but you had an awful lot of space vacated in this city, and we still have something of an overbuilt condition on rental space."

Still, even permanently high prices for oil and natural gas would not have the same boom effect on Houston as they did in the early 1980s because the industry doesn't think there are a lot of new oil reserves left to find in the United States. The fields have been largely picked over, experts say.

That means that when American firms invest in new wells, their money is increasingly likely to be spent abroad. As a result, executives in Houston may get large bonuses and their stockholders may reap greater dividends, but most blue-collar and middle-management jobs will be created elsewhere.

''Certainly the higher prices stop the contraction that was in process here, but the expansion of activity -- the increased employment -- you're more likely to see abroad than you are here," Plank said. For example, a dozen years ago Apache, which spends $2 billion a year drilling wells, only looked for oil and natural gas inside the United States. Now almost half its earnings come from abroad.

But Hopper predicted that Houston still may see more white-collar jobs as well, as computer technicians and engineers come to the executive suite to help make decisions about where in the world to drill. That will eventually help the rest of the community -- from car dealerships to real estate sellers.

But it hasn't happened in a big way yet. At the Houston Grand Opera, where revenues are more affected by corporate health, general manager David Gockley said his group has yet to see a turnaround after three years of declining revenues despite the soaring energy prices.

Corporate contributions have dropped from $2 million a year to half that, he said. Ticket sales have suffered too.

''In this climate people are still laying low," he said. They will come out for blockbuster opera events, ''but will not necessarily come out for things they might in flusher times."

Richard Murray, a University of Houston political science professor, said the caution can best be explained by a popular bumper sticker in Houston from the late 1980s. It read: ''Please, God, send us one more boom. This time, we won't screw it up."

''Most long-term residents don't want that kind of boom to come back," he said. Thousands of people, for instance, lost their homes. ''That's deeply imprinted on local memory."
