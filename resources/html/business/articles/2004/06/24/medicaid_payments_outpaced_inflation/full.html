Medicaid payments outpaced inflation

June 24, 2004

Despite physicians' complaints about low fees, a study indicates that doctors enjoyed healthy pay increases from at least one entity: Medicaid. Physician fees paid by the state and federal insurance program for the poor grew at nearly twice the rate of inflation between 1998 and 2003, according to researchers from two nonprofit organizations, the Urban Institute and the Center for Studying Health System Change. Medicaid fees grew an average of 27 percent during those years, though they remained well below the fees paid by the federal Medicare insurance program for the elderly. Rising Medicaid payments occurred during a period of healthy revenue growth for many states, a situation that has deteriorated. Massachusetts was one of 10 states that raised Medicaid fees significantly above the national average. The study authors said higher fees improve patient access, because in states that pay more, more doctors agree to treat Medicaid patients. (Liz Kowalczyk)

Judge throws out stock-dumping suit

A judge dismissed a lawsuit filed against J. Jill Group on behalf of shareholders who alleged executives of the women's clothing retailer dumped company stock in 2002 while offering investors a rosy picture of the firm's financial health. Attorneys for Quincy-based J. Jill Group argued the company fully disclosed information on declining sales and weakening demand that led the company's stock to plunge as it missed its earnings target in the fourth quarter of 2002. Without comment, US District Court Judge William G. Young on Monday granted a motion to throw out the complaint. The lawsuit alleged chief executive Gordon Cooke and chief financial officer Olga Conley sold a majority of their shares in the company in 2002 as the company's financial condition soured. Executives and board members sold more than $17 million in shares from Feb. 12 to Dec. 4 of that year, the complaint alleged. (AP)

N.H. aims to fine Morgan Stanley

New Hampshire regulators are seeking to fine Morgan Stanley $500,000 for offering improper incentives, including steaks, to its brokers to boost sales of its in-house mutual funds. The New Hampshire Bureau of Securities Regulation contends the brokerage improperly ran contests at its New Hampshire offices to reward account executives for pushing Morgan Stanley investment funds. In court papers filed last week, the agency ordered the company to end the contests and to hire an independent consultant to review its procedures in New Hampshire. The agency also accused a Morgan Stanley broker of selling and trading unregistered stocks that were unsuitable for trading. Morgan Stanley spokeswoman Andrea Slattery said the company fired the broker involved in the unregistered stock transactions more than three years ago. She declined to comment further. (AP)

Bank of America sets 2-for-1 stock split

Bank of America Corp., the second-biggest US bank, said it would split its stock 2-for-1 to make the shares more attractive to investors and boost the quarterly dividend 13 percent to 90 cents. The bank also said chief executive Kenneth Lewis would become president July 1. He is assuming the duties held by Eugene McQuade, formerly FleetBoston Financial Corp.'s chief operating officer, who is leaving the company. Bank of America bought Fleet for $47 billion on April 1. Bank of America last split its shares 2-for-1 in February 1997 and has boosted its quarterly dividend eight times since then, according to data compiled by Bloomberg. The split is effective Aug. 27 to common shareholders of record on Aug. 6. The cash dividend is effective Sept. 24 for shareholders of record on Sept. 3, the company said. (Bloomberg)

Charitable foundations post large gains

Charitable foundations posted double-digit investment gains in 2003, according to a report by the Commonfund Institute in Wilton, Conn., reversing 2002 losses that dented their assets and for some decreased charitable giving. The average foundation saw a 17 percent return last year, up from an 8.7 percent decline in 2002. The largest foundations, with assets of least $1 billion, showed gains of 20.5 percent. Smaller foundations appear to have riskier portfolios than the larger ones, with a 55 percent stake in stocks vs. 48 percent for the giants. (Beth Healy)

Cabot wins decision in sales dispute

Cabot Corp. won a favorable ruling from a Massachusetts court in its dispute over a tantalum sales agreement with AVX Corp. Cabot, a chemical and materials holding company, said the judge granted its motion for partial summary judgment and upheld its long-term tantalum supply agreement. Manufacturers use tantalum in electronic components of cellphones and other devices. Cabot's motion to dismiss a federal case on the tantalum issue is pending, the company said. In October, components maker AVX recorded a charge of $61.4 million, or 35 cents a share, on its fiscal second-quarter earnings for a write-down of tantalum materials. AVX, which continued to purchase tantalum from Cabot during litigation, filed the Massachusetts suit in July 2002, alleging unfair and deceptive trade practices and breach of contract. (Dow Jones)

SEC rules in favor of ex-Shawmut official

Securities regulators dismissed an administrative judge's ruling against a former sales executive at Shawmut Investment Advisers, saying he did not violate rules by directing business to brokers that provided Shawmut research. The Securities and Exchange Commission's opinion reverses a 2003 ruling against Clarke T. Blizzard, a former sales vice president for Shawmut. In that ruling, an administrative judge fined Blizzard $648,233 in penalties and fees. The administrative judge ruled that Shawmut's policy of directing brokerage business to firms that provided research was not adequately disclosed to investors and that Blizzard aided and abetted the inadequate disclosure. In the SEC's opinion, the commission said it wasn't Blizzard's job to disclose the information. (Bloomberg)

Nextel to expand walkie-talkie service

Nextel Communications Inc. is expanding its walkie-talkie style Direct Connect service to cover communications between subscribers in the United States and Mexico. The move follows Nextel's launch of cross-border Direct Connect in May between the United States and Canada, Argentina, Brazil, and Peru. Walkie-talkie calls to Mexico will be free until July 1; after that the service will cost $20 a month for unlimited national and international service or 20 cents per minute for ''pay as you go" service, Nextel said. Nextel estimates that 90 percent of its 13.4 million US subscribers have enabled nationwide US Direct Connect service. (Peter J. Howe)
