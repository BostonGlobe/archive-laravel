ANDOVER

At annual extravaganza, if the shoe fits, buy it!

Daher's sale to run additional weekend

By Joyce Pellino Crane, Globe Correspondent  |  June 24, 2004

Patricia Dennis of Andover was looking at the size 10 narrow Ecco sandals on her feet and laughing with John Daher, owner of Daher's Shoes, about the handsome sales representative who just persuaded her to buy what she termed such "ugly" shoes.

But as the thousands of regulars to this annual shoe sale extravaganza know, beauty lies not only in the quirky look so fashionable around suburbia, but also in the comfort of the footwear -- and the price.

On every Father's Day weekend for 22 years, the Daher shoe sale in Andover has been selling men's, women's and children's shoes at half price. But this year, Daher whispered to an observer, a surprise is in store for the 5,000 to 7,000 customers who make the pilgrimage from Andover and surrounding communities.

For the first time in its history, the sale will be extended to a second weekend, from tomorrow through Sunday, from 9 a.m. to 9 p.m.

"When we started, we were in the street," said Daher, recalling years past when shoe boxes would line Main Street under a canopy and customers would squeeze through aisles searching for shoe sizes left over from the year's sales.

This day, the auditorium on the second floor of Old Town Hall on Main Street is crammed with rows and rows of banquet tables stacked with Munro, Clark, Nike, Borne, Stuart Weitzman, and Cole Haan shoes. And though the aisles are full of shoppers, as they have always been, the organization has greatly improved over the years -- shoes are stacked by size, styles are grouped, and quantities are plentiful.

"We buy especially for this event from our regular vendors," said Daher. "They know this event comes up, and every year they put a package together for us."

Daher acknowledged that despite the frantic pace and organizational challenge required to plan the event every year, it's a profitable endeavor for the privately held company, which donates a portion of the first night's sales to a charity.

This year, the American Cancer Society of Boston was the beneficiary of about $3,500 in memory of Daher's sister Joyce Daher Belko, who died in September of cancer and had always been a presence at the sale. The two siblings ran the successful shoe business together after taking it over from their mother, Sally, who started it in 1970 just before the death of their father, Charles Daher Sr.

"It's funny," said Daher. "I was talking to a customer this morning about Joyce, and I got a call that there was a small fire in the Methuen store. Five minutes later, the alarm went off here [in Andover at Old Town Hall]. Do you think it was just Joyce giving me a hard time?"

Regardless of the temporary evacuation, by about 11 a.m., nearly 125 people were canvassing the shoe box offerings with laser eyes on the uncovered boxes showing which shoes were in the boxes below. About 15,000 pairs are on sale this year.

A giddy Sonya Ameen, 37, of Andover, was trying on black leather boots with her two sisters-in-law, Bea Tiberii, 30, of Boxford, and Karen Lang, 53, of North Andover. Three red cloth shopping bags surrounded Ameen, each containing at least three shoe boxes.

"I bought 22 pairs of shoes last year," she said. This year she said she expected to go home with 15 pairs for herself and would return that night with her husband.

"I've yet not to wear one pair of shoes that I've bought here" in past years, she said.

Caroline Ren Jackson, 37, of Andover, said she knows shoes, having worked for Lexington-based Stride Rite Corp. before staying home to raise her three children, all under age 7. Ren said she was buying shoes for all three children that day, as well as herself.

"I do this every year," she said. "I get a baby sitter, and I come here."

The event employs 60 local students, including Meghan Sullivan, 17, and Mike Nimerowski, both of Methuen and recent graduates of Central Catholic High School in Lawrence. Sullivan said she expected to make about $300 for the weekend's work. She said she is planning to attend Quinnipiac University in Hampden, Conn., this fall.

Nimerowski, 18, who is employed for the second straight summer in the Daher store in Methuen, said he will probably make about $2,000 for the summer's work before he attends Bentley College in Waltham.

"It's a good summer job, and a lot of friends work here," said Sullivan.

Close to the front of the auditorium, where some of the more playful and trendy shoes were placed, Lynn Wilson, 42, of Arlington, was watching daughter Emma, 12, a student at Lexington Christian Academy in Lexington, eye shocking pink sandals made by Bamboo.

"I'm waiting for the shopping queen," Wilson said, pointing to her daughter, who was going home with pairs of Clarks, Steve Maddens, and Esprits, to go along with the Bamboos, which she called her favorites.

For herself, Wilson had only a lone pair of Munros. Joyfully displaying the flat-soled sandals with a wide red strap, she proclaimed, "I always wanted a red pair of shoes. This is like the end of the rainbow for shoe shopping, isn't it?"
