SEC bars insiders as fund chairmen

By Beth Healy and Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  June 24, 2004

WASHINGTON -- The US Securities and Exchange Commission yesterday voted to strip a significant source of power from the hands of mutual fund executives, the most visible change yet to result from the scandals that have engulfed the industry over the last year.

By a vote of 3 to 2, SEC commissioners required mutual funds to name independent chairmen of the boards of trustees that oversee the funds. They also approved several other measures aimed at making trustees more accountable to shareholders. Fund trustees are supposed to represent the interests of millions of average investors when money-management firms are chosen and fees and expenses are negotiated.

The new rules represent a sharp defeat for industry titans like Fidelity Investments' chief executive, Edward C. Johnson III, and Vanguard Group chief John J. Brennan, both of whom have fought to remain chairmen of their firms' fund boards. They, along with fund executives at 80 percent of the nation's funds, now will have to give up the chairman's post, though they may continue to serve as trustees.

A fund executive serving as chairman ''finds him or herself in an untenable conflict of interest," SEC chairman William H. Donaldson said at the end of yesterday's hearing. That executive, he said, ''is by definition trying to serve two masters. Even if it weren't a matter of law, I believe it's a matter of common sense that you cannot wear two hats."

At Fidelity, the nation's largest mutual-fund firm, the fees paid by the funds amounted to $3 billion in 2003. At many large fund firms, such as Fidelity, the company itself manages the money in its funds; at others, such as Vanguard, the trustees choose independent money-management firms to invest the funds. Boston's Wellington Management Co., for example, manages the money in many of Vanguard's largest funds, including the Vanguard Wellington fund, with $29.6 billion in assets.

Donaldson said there was overwhelming support for the independent-chairman rule from investors, from a trustees' trade group, and even from the seven former heads of the SEC.

But many executives in the fund industry intensely fought against the proposal, indicating that an important source of power was at stake.

''You have to ask yourself, why such an extraordinary pleading? Why such an extraordinary effort?" Donaldson said.

Donaldson was referring to, among others, Johnson, who had called the SEC chairman directly to oppose the measure. Johnson also wrote an op-ed column in The Wall Street Journal arguing against it.

''I have the highest regard for Ned Johnson, the highest regard for the Fidelity group, the highest regard for Ned's father," who founded what is now the nation's largest fund company, Donaldson said. ''There may be a bit of pride and emotion associated with a founder of an organization -- an organization that's had such a terrific record."

But on the issue of independent chairmen, Donaldson said, ''I have disagreed with Mr. Johnson. I hope at some point he will agree with this decision."

Fidelity spokesman Vin Loporchio said it is ''untrue" that Johnson was acting out of emotion. Johnson, he said, ''expressed his view on an issue that is important to our shareholders, as he has done in the past." Loporchio said Fidelity ''will adapt to the rule, and we expect the changes to be seamless for our mutual fund shareholders." Johnson intends to remain a trustee.

Trustees are paid by shareholders to act as watchdogs over the firms that manage $7 trillion in assets for American investors. Until recently, the position had been a comfortable sinecure, with high pay for part-time work, according to an in-depth review of trustee compensation by the Globe. The SEC, with its new rules, is looking to make trustees more aggressive and more accountable to shareholders for their actions.

''We believe that a fund board with an independent chairman and independent leadership is more likely to ask tough questions, more likely to say 'No' when necessary, and more likely to be an effective check on fund management," Paul F. Roye, chief of the SEC's investment management division, said yesterday.

John A. Hill, the independent chairman of Putnam funds who sparred with Johnson over the proposal, called the SEC's action ''one of the most revolutionary things they've done for the mutual fund shareholder, ever."

Two commissioners opposed the measure, calling it arbitrary and potentially costly for investors. One, Paul S. Atkins, said, ''morality and ethics cannot be legislated" and noted that the SEC is ''using the coercive power of the federal government," instead of market forces, to change the structure of fund boards.

The five-member commission may not have more than three members from one political party. Chairman Donaldson, a Republican appointed by President Bush, sided with the commission's two Democrats.

In addition to mandating independent chairmen, the SEC rule requires that at least 75 percent of trustees be independent from the money manager, and that those directors have their own staffs, meet without fund-company executives, and evaluate their performance annually.

At Fidelity, for example, 10 of 14 trustees, or 71 percent, are independent. Marvin L. Mann, the lead independent director, declined to comment on how the board would go about replacing Johnson as chairman and meeting the 75 percent threshold.

Many other funds boards will have to add new independent directors, or eliminate company insiders as trustees to comply with the SEC directive.

At Vanguard, Brennan will have to step down as trustee chairman, but spokeswoman Rebecca Cohen said the fund board is already in compliance with the other elements of the new SEC rule. ''We don't honestly expect much to change," she said.

While the seat of power at most fund boards will shift to an outsider, the new SEC rule won't necessarily result in an injection of new blood to the sometimes clubby world of mutual fund boards.

C. Meyrick Payne, a senior partner at Management Practice Inc., a Stamford, Conn., consultancy that advises fund trustees, said that nearly two-thirds of fund boards already meet the 75 percent independence threshold. Most boards, he added, are eliminating company insiders in order to get to that level, rather than bringing in new outsiders. Moreover, vacancies at fund boards are infrequent, leading Payne to conclude of the SEC rule, ''I doubt this affects more than 50 people in the country at any one time."

The Investment Company Institute, the industry's lobbying organization, yesterday gave up its resistance to the independent-chairman rule. ''The institute pledges to help mutual funds implement the SEC's new governance reforms, including the independent-chair requirement," ICI president Paul Stevens said.

In a related matter, the SEC voted to require fund boards give shareholders more details about how they negotiate management contracts with investment companies. The hope is that greater disclosure will pressure trustees to negotiate lower fees and reduce expenses. Included is information about the ''costs of the services to be provided and profits" that the money management firm would make when investing those funds, the SEC said in its proposal.

Although it received less attention than the fund-chairman issue, the matter of contract details could provide investors such as retirement plans with powerful ammunition to pressure fund boards into negotiating lower fees, industry specialists said.

''The independent-chairman proposal pales in comparison to this as far as the effect it's going to have on the industry," said Max Rottersman, who runs a service that analyzes mutual fund expenses.
