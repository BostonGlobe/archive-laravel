Almost a ward of the state

Awaiting a bailout, Springfield suffers with weak economy, poor schools

By Charles Stein, Globe Staff  |  June 18, 2004

SPRINGFIELD -- The new economy never made it this far out the Massachusetts Turnpike.

The industries that powered the Boston economy in the 1990s -- electronics, finance, biotechnology -- did not spread to Springfield in any meaningful way. "The sexy stuff is almost nonexistent in our region," said Bill Ward, who has been training workers here for the past 20 years.

A lot of middle-class people left for better economic opportunities, while the number of poor grew steadily over the past two decades. This shift helped make Springfield one of the poorest cities in Massachusetts.

Economics and demographics don't fully explain why Springfield's city government is on the verge of insolvency. Governor Mitt Romney and the Legislature are haggling over details of a proposed $52 million bailout package for Springfield. The state says Springfield faces a $20 million shortfall in its 2005 budget. In early June, Standard & Poor's downgraded the city's debt to junk bond status.

The Romney administration blames much of Springfield's plight on mismanagement, and points out that the city has been living beyond its means for years. But the weak local economy has left Springfield with few resources to help itself. Tax collections have declined in recent years, and the city's total assessed property value is slightly lower than it was in 1990.

Like other poor cities, Springfield, population 152,082, is practically a ward of the state. Last year 62 percent of the city budget came from state aid. Only Lawrence got a bigger share of its money from Beacon Hill. The most visible project in downtown Springfield, a new civic center, is financed with $66 million in state funds.

Springfield had a golden age, though. Its legacy can be seen in the historic section of Forest Park in the city's southwest corner. The streets are shaded and elegant. The wood and brick homes have 10 or 12 rooms and big porches that face the street. Most were built between 1895 and 1910, when Springfield's factories were booming. The factories turned out cars, motorcycles, guns, and even airplanes. The most famous factory, the Springfield Armory, made weapons for the US military for more than 150 years.

Manufacturing declined slowly and steadily, right up through the past few years. But that hardly makes Springfield unique. Manufacturing employment shrank everywhere. "Our problem is we haven't come up with a replacement for what we lost," said the former mayor, Robert Markel.

Despite the best efforts of government and business, Springfield has had little luck in luring the high-wage services and industries that define success in the modern economy. Exactly why is hard to explain. The area has plenty of well-educated people and good colleges nearby. It has a successful financial services firm, Massachusetts Mutual Life Insurance Co. It has the state's second-largest hospital system, Bay State Medical Center.

And the city is about 90 miles from Boston.

"The truth is the connecting rods between Springfield and the eastern part of the state are minimal," said Robert Nakosteen, an economics professor at the University of Massachusetts at Amherst. "The executives I talk to seem to feel that if they are going to be that far from Boston, they might as well be in another state."

The connecting rods are also minimal when it comes to real estate. A Bostonian can get reverse sticker shock looking at home prices in Springfield. A 10-room, 3,600-square-foot home changed hands in Forest Park recently for $300,000. In Newton it would have sold for over $1 million.

Markel is keenly aware of the difference. The former mayor commutes nearly three hours a day to a job as town administrator in Norfolk, a Boston suburb. He says that if he sold his home in the historic district and moved to Eastern Massachusetts, he might be able to afford a small condominium.

"People from Boston can't believe things are so inexpensive here," said Kevin Sears, a real estate agent in Springfield.

Eileen Hurley works in a very different part of Springfield. A few days ago, a man walked by her office window. As he walked he wrapped a piece of rubber tubing around his forearm in preparation for shooting up heroin. Hurley wasn't startled. The abandoned house next door to her office is a popular spot for drug addicts. The sidewalk in front of the house is littered with discarded orange and white syringes. A pile of construction debris sits in the yard.

Hurley is the executive director of Gray House, which provides social services to residents of the North End of Springfield. A few blocks from her office is a slice of Springfield that constitutes the poorest neighborhood in Massachusetts, according to the 2000 US Census. Household income in the neighborhood is $7,271, below the federal poverty level for a single person. The census found that only one in four adults in the area has a job.

"This place is vibrant and alive, but I'm not sure it is well," said Hurley, on a walking tour of the North End.

While many of the blocks look much better than Hurley's street, there are abandoned homes and apartment buildings throughout the neighborhood. Mayor Charles Ryan complained recently that the city has as many as 100 abandoned buildings that should be torn down.

Last winter, Hurley collected report cards from kids who go to an after-school program at Gray House. They showed that most of the students were failing math. Many of the same students had already missed 20 days of school by Christmastime.

The public schools are a big problem in Springfield, as they are in other poor cities. Low test scores -- Springfield students had the third-lowest grades in the state on the last round of MCAS exams -- make it difficult to bring the middle class back. "Public education scares folks away," said Henry Thomas, chief executive of the Urban League of Springfield. "It is the hardest nut to crack."

Figures published by the school district show that 77 percent of all students are classified as low income.

The business community worries that the upcoming state bailout of Springfield could be another negative mark on the city's permanent record. While they would like to see Springfield's finances strengthened, they are nervous about the bad publicity the fiscal crisis is generating.

"A receivership could create a stigma," said Sears, the real estate agent.

Home prices have been rising at a double-digit clip. Commercial real estate prices are rising too, bolstered by an influx of outside investors who see Springfield's low prices as a lure.

This week, a national survey by Manpower, the temporary-help agency, found Springfield employers among the most bullish in the country in terms of their plans for new hiring. Many are old-line manufacturers benefiting from the upturn in the national and global economy.

"The manufacturers we have left are pretty competitive," said Allan Blair, president of the Economic Development Council of Western Massachusetts.

But some residents, including Markel, the former mayor, have their doubts as to whether Springfield can grow its way out of its problems.

"What is the magic formula for restoring an old city?" he asked. "I am not sure anyone has found it."
