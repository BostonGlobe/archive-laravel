June 18, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Raytheon says missile test successful

ADVERTISEMENT

Raytheon Co., the world's largest missile maker, said Taiwan's second test-firing of its Patriot missiles was successful, based on initial indications. The exercise involved firing two Patriot Advanced Capability-2 missiles against other Patriot missiles being used as targets, Raytheon spokesman Steven Brecken said in an e-mail. Taiwan is one of 10 countries outside the United States believed to be considering orders for the latest version of the weapon, the Patriot Advanced Capability-3 System, or PAC-3, Morgan Stanley analyst Heidi Wood wrote in March. Waltham-based Raytheon makes the PAC-3 system with Lockheed Martin Corp., the largest US defense contractor. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

Senate committee backs Greenspan

Federal Reserve chairman Alan Greenspan won approval from the Senate Banking Committee for a fifth term at the helm of the US central bank, which would extend his 16-year stewardship of the economy. Approval came on a voice vote of about a dozen senators with only Jim Bunning, a Kentucky Republican, dissenting. The nomination now goes to the full Senate for a final vote. Senator Richard Shelby, an Alabama Republican who is chairman of the banking committee, said Greenspan's confirmation ''will move fast." He said he would talk today with Senate Majority Leader Bill Frist, a Tennessee Republican, about scheduling a confirmation vote. (Bloomberg)

Chip equipment sales double in one year

Orders to North American companies that sell equipment used to build semiconductors more than doubled in May from a year earlier, a trade group said. The book-to-bill ratio for North American chip-tool makers was 1.11 in May, indicating they received $111 in orders for every $100 in sales, the Semiconductor Equipment & Materials International said. The ratio measures industry health and was revised to 1.13 in April, compared with 1.09 in March. Above 1 indicates growth. The three-month average of worldwide bookings in May was almost flat at $1.576 billion, from $1.581 billion in April, and jumped from $724 million a year earlier. (Bloomberg)

Archer Daniels settles price-fixing suit

Archer Daniels Midland Co., the world's largest grain processor, said it will pay $400 million to settle a 1995 civil antitrust suit alleging the company conspired to fix prices of its high-fructose corn syrup. The case involved customers who bought the corn syrup in the early 1990s, said Decatur, Ill.-based Archer Daniels in a statement. The settlement is subject to court approval, the company said. The agreement comes three months after privately held rival Cargill Inc. said it would pay $24 million to settle with plaintiffs. The suit claimed that Archer Daniels, Cargill, and other companies colluded to fix the price of the corn sweetener used in soft drinks, candy, and baked goods. Calls to company spokesmen were unanswered late last night. (Bloomberg )

THE WORLD

Iraqi oil exports poised to resume

Iraq could resume exporting limited amounts of oil as early as today analysts said, with repair crews nearing the completion of work on the smaller of two pipelines crippled in a sabotage attack. If no further attacks occur, Iraq could restore normal shipments of at least 1.5 million barrels a day from its southern oil fields by next week, analysts said. An attack on the two southern pipelines serving as Iraq's main export route forced the suspension Wednesday of all crude shipments from the Gulf. The explosion north of the coastal town of Faw was the second such attack in three days. The outage has prompted the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries to signal plans for boosting output to offset the shortfall in Iraqi barrels to keep crude prices from rising. (AP)
