Banks seek to cash in on money wiring

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  June 28, 2004

LAWRENCE -- Hassan Jahm dines almost exclusively on McDonald's crispy chicken sandwiches, which he buys at half price using his employee discount. Saving every penny he can, the recent immigrant from Pakistan visits his neighborhood Western Union office every few months to send $100 or so to his aunts, uncles, and cousins halfway around the world.

It's not just Jahm's pennies that are adding up. Worldwide, the money-wiring business has grown into a $160 billion market, attracting a growing number of banks in New England and around the country that are scrambling to get in on the action.

The banks' efforts mark the clearest sign yet of a revolution in the way financial institutions view immigrant customers. Once largely ignored by a banking industry more focused on mergers and wealthy clients, immigrants increasingly are seen as a critical source of future growth. By developing low-cost money wiring, banks hope to persuade these customers, many of whom avoid banks entirely, to use a host of other products, from checking accounts to credit cards to car loans.

In the last several months, the three major banks that dominate New England's market -- Bank of America, Citizens Financial Group, and Sovereign Bancorp -- have rolled out products to aid money transfers, known as remittances, or are actively considering them. Citizens launched a trial program in Massachusetts last month, and Sovereign says it is exploring options for a similar product. Bank of America, which introduced a remittance product to Mexico in 2002, is considering an expansion of the service to other locations all over the world.

Banking giants Wells Fargo and Citigroup also have jumped into the market.

But, despite their efforts, the banks have not managed to pull much business away from the market's 800-pound gorilla, Western Union, which handles a large portion of immigrants' money-wiring business along with smaller players such as PayPal and MoneyGram. Banks control just 3 percent of the market from the United States to Mexico, according to a study by the Pew Hispanic Center released earlier this month. Other studies have put the figure closer to 16 percent of the entire Latin American market.

''Despite substantial marketing campaigns and a very large investment over the past three years, US banks have captured only a small fraction of the remittance transfer market," wrote the study's author, Manuel Orozco, a senior researcher at Georgetown University.

Bank of America, whose footprint stretches through heavily Latino states such as Florida, Texas, and California, launched a major marketing campaign in April to offer Hispanic customers free checking if they regularly use the bank's SafeSend service to wire money to Mexico. The bank has increasingly targeted the ''unbanked" -- the estimated 8 million Latino immigrants with no relationship with a financial institution -- as a key way to grow.

Executives say this group often does not respond well to advertising, even Spanish-language ads, so bankers fan out to flea markets, Cinco de Mayo celebrations, and other events to make their pitch in person. Bank of America even sponsored a Mexican music concert in Phoenix they named ''Banco Musical."

The bank, which acquired FleetBoston Financial Corp. this spring, said it will introduce the new services in the Northeast in 2005. Fleet currently lets its customers wire cash from bank to bank through a more complicated process but was planning to launch a simpler remittance product before the merger.

Bank of America declined to release the number of customers who use its SafeSend program but said its sales are increasing rapidly. The Pew Hispanic Center, however, said the four largest banks in the market -- among them Bank of America -- still do fewer than 100,000 money wire transfers a month. Still, bank executives said they are optimistic that they will gain market share as word spreads.

''It's still in the infant stages," said Maria C. Mesa, senior vice president of Bank of America's SafeSend sales. ''People take a while to change their behavior. All it will take is time before banks have the lion's share of remittances."

But so far, some customers have been less than impressed with some Northeast banks' efforts. Jahm, the recent Pakistani immigrant, said he stopped at his local Fleet branch a few days ago and asked what it would take to wire money home. Though he has an account at the bank, he said a teller asked him for some kind of international account number that he had no idea how to find. He then went to Western Union instead, where he wired the money home without difficulty.

''With Western Union, we had no problem," he said. ''It's a good service."

The issue of remittances has become one of the most important to US immigrants, who have long pushed financial institutions to lower the cost of sending cash. The money sent from the United States often goes to subsidize basic needs -- food, clothing, and shelter -- to help families survive. In some Latin American countries, including Nicaragua and El Salvador, the amount sent from overseas totals more than one-tenth of their countries' total economic output, ahead of many industries, the US State Department estimates.

Bank of America's service differs from Western Union in one key aspect that adds another step: While the receiving customers pick up cash from a Western Union office -- typically inside a grocery store or other small retailer -- Bank of America hand-delivers debit cards all over Mexico. The receivers can either use the debit cards at stores or get cash at the ATM of a bank affiliated with Bank of America.

Citizens, New England's second-largest bank, also launched in May a pilot money-wiring program in Massachusetts in a bid to get in on the market. The bank has teamed up with the Greater Boston Interfaith Organization to push its services in local churches, community organizations, and schools.

''It was a service that is needed in the community," said Julie Connelly, Citizens' director of community relations. ''We were hearing stories of exorbitant fees charged by some of the remittance companies, and we thought it was money that was better to stay with the consumer."

The increased competition has cut money-wiring prices across the board. Over the last several years, the cost of sending $200 to Mexico has fallen to about $14.60 from as much as $30 in the late 1990s, according to the Pew Hispanic Center. Bank of America and Citizens typically charge about $10 to wire money to Mexico, while Western Union charges about $15 for its ''money in minutes" service or $10 for money that arrives the next day.

A Western Union spokeswoman, Danielle Pereira, said the company welcomes the competition. ''We see ourselves as being convenient, reliable, and absolutely safe," she said. ''Consumers can trust their money will get to where they want, when they want it."

Western Union still retains significant advantages in the remittance market, even with the new players in the industry, said Roberto Suro, director of the Pew Hispanic Center. Until recently, many banks have required customers to open multiple accounts and move money between them, he said. And on the receiving end, Western Union often has more outlets for relatives to pick up their cash, he said.

''It's moving slowly," he said. ''The shape of the market is not going to change very quickly or very dramatically in a short period of time."

The banks also face another problem: Some of their immigrant customers distrust financial institutions of all kinds, especially if they move from countries with shaky financial systems.

''I don't keep money in banks -- banks get robbed," said Miguel Garcia of Lawrence, who says he sends more than $2,000 a month to his family in Puerto Rico. He said he hides his cash, then uses Western Union when he wants to send the money back home. On the rare occasions when representatives from a bank have handed him literature or urged him to open a checking account, he said he has turned them down.

The bankers acknowledge the hurdles, but they say the customer need is too big to ignore.

''For the customers, as well as the strength of the community, it's important for them to have a relationship with a financial institution," said Thomas B. Kennedy, senior vice president of community development for Sovereign's New England region. ''Part of our challenge and objective is to make sure we're accessible."
