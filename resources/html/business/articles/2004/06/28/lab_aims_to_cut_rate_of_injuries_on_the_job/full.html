Lab aims to cut rate of injuries on the job

By Diane E. Lewis, Globe Staff  |  June 28, 2004

HOPKINTON -- The laboratory has the trappings of an avant-garde theater.

ADVERTISEMENT

Giant digital cameras capture a woman striding down a walkway in the darkened room, her coordination monitored by red sensors. Thin black lines track her movements on a large graph as a handful of spectators look on.

The experiment -- on how to prevent slips and falls in the workplace -- is among several routinely conducted at the Liberty Mutual Research Institute for Safety, where scientists in white lab coats battle repetitive strain, collisions, back pain, falls, and other accidents or injuries than cause workers compensation claims to soar.

"Right now, there are 6 million occupational injuries per year in the United States," said Tom Leamon, director of the institute. "Typically, people are injured at work because of lifting or lowering things, slipping and falling, or getting hit by something. Eighty percent of all workplace injuries fall into those categories. My goal is to make sure that we do not lose sight of those injuries or the fact that people should leave work in the same condition that they came to work."

In a field where innovation has long been the domain of academic institutions like the Harvard School of Public Health, the University of Michigan, Ohio State University, and some private engineering and automotive firms, this research facility is an anomaly. Yet workplace safety has been part of the institute's mantra since its creation in 1954 by Boston's Liberty Mutual Insurance Co.

"Insurance companies do not have a long record of this type of research," said Jack T. Dennerlein, an assistant professor of ergonomics and safety at the Harvard School of Public Health. "Some insurance companies look at risk analysis. But Liberty Mutual has taken the lead at looking at prevention of work-related musculo-skeletal disorders. Instead of just looking at risk, it has said, 'What can we do to reduce risk?' "

Some of the institute's most important work revolves around overexertion, slips and falls, and repetitive strain. Those three occupational hazards accounted for nearly $25 billion in costs arising from six or more days of absenteeism due to illness, according to the Liberty Mutual safety index.

In all, on-the-job injuries led to more than $60 billion in workers compensation claims and costs in 2001, the most recent estimate from the Commerce Department. Liberty Mutual, which tracks such injuries, is the largest workers compensation underwriter in the nation, with close to 15 percent of the insured market, analysts say.

The institute's scientists churn out reports and patents based on experiments conducted onsite or in workplaces here and abroad. The company's developmental arm, a separate entity, uses the findings to create safety programs, software, safety devices, and products that Liberty Mutual sells to corporate clients. The institute also shares its research with the public.

In New Zealand, members of Marinz Inc., a meatpacking association in Wellington, rely on an institute device that measures the amount of force used to grip a knife or make cuts while deboning a lamb or slicing a steer.

Research scientist Raymond McGorry, who invented the device in 2001, went to New Zealand to see how the country's meat cutters ply their trade. He said the device provided the first real data on the amount of force meat cutters use while working. At the time, New Zealand was struggling to reduce repetitive strain within the meatpacking industry.

Then, McGorry and scientists at New Zealand's AG Research Limited in Hamilton invented a solution: a portable knife tester that measures the sharpness of blades right on the factory floor. The rectangular metal contraption, on display this month during the institute's 50th anniversary, looks like a miniature guillotine.

"What we found was that the sharper the blade, the less cutting and gripping force and the less stress on hands, arms, and upper extremities," said McGorry. "We also proved to management that the quality of the knife could have a big impact. That had not been done before."

At Walt Disney Parks & Resorts in Lake Buena Vista, Fla., supervisors in the facilities division use Liberty Mutual's portable horizontal pull slip-meter, a metal bar with a force gauge or scale on top, to determine the likelihood of sliding and falling on floors at the company's offices or resorts. The device contains a motor and spool with a string that attaches to a hook. When a button is pushed, the machine pulls on the gauge until it slips. A dial measures the amount of friction, providing a slip index, said Wayne Maynard, the institute's director of ergonomics and tribology.

Said Greg Hale, chief safety officer at Walt Disney: "It tells us how much force is needed for something to slide. We do slip tests on every tiled product before we put it down. The tests help us determine exactly where we should put them."

Liberty Mutual also trains workers onsite. At Florida-based Watkins Motor Lines Inc., insurance company staff relied on institute data on lifting to help Watkins employees. Then, they spent time observing movers load freight before showing managers how to prevent overexertion and injury.

Ronald Chipman, executive vice president of risk management, said the training helped his company slash workers compensation costs by 35 percent per year. The program was adopted five years ago. In 2003, the company saved $2 million on premiums at one of its subsidiaries. Watkins Motor Lines has 7,500 van lines in 42 states, Chipman said.

While falls and physical problems due to overexertion are the top causes of work-related injury in the United States, repetitive strain is the knowledge worker's nemesis. The ailment, known as RSI, affects ligaments, joints, tendons, and nerves. In all, repetitive strain results in more than $2.8 billion in workers compensation costs yearly. Nevertheless, the Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that the incidence of RSI among knowledge workers has increased 10 times since 1999.

Scientists at the institute are studying the interaction between office environments and workers in order to reduce RSI.

In a recent experiment, volunteer Lauren Gwozdz, 37, was asked to complete typing assignments in the institute's state-of-the art facility over 18 days. The level and duration of work varied. Electrodes attached to Gwozdz's neck, shoulders, arms, and hands monitored her movements. A computer and desk that moved up and down allowed her to type while standing as sensors traced the relationship between Gwozdz's actions and fatigue.

Leamon said the Institute's findings reveal that computers are not always to blame for RSI. Instead, he said, longer workdays, improper use of equipment, and the inability to vary one's position are critical factors.

"Keyboards are well designed now and so are screens, but people do not use the technology appropriately," he said. "That is why the next innovation must be to have everyone set up their workplaces in a way that will minimize stresses."

Harvard's Dennerlein agrees, but says collaboration between employers and insurance companies like Liberty Mutual is the best way to make that happen. And once companies fully understand the relationship between prevention and the high cost of injury, he said, such partnerships will become more common.

"Injured workers cost money," Dennerlein said. "A low-back claim, for example, can cost tens of thousands of dollars. The burden to the employer is quite large. So we need to look to partnerships between industry and insurance companies to create innovations. That is when we will see the best and most productive changes, when people in the private sector are working together to create synergies."
