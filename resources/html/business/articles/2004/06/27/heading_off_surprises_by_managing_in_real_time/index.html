BUSINESS INTELLIGENCE

Heading off surprises by managing in real time

By Robert Weisman, Globe Columnist  |  June 27, 2004

Kenneth G. McGee is the business world's equivalent of a tornado chaser.

McGee, 53, group vice president and fellow at Gartner Inc., the Stamford, Conn., technology research and consulting firm, spent five years studying accidents, disasters, and unwelcome surprises.

He started with natural disasters, from hurricanes to earthquakes. He moved on to man-made calamities, such as Three Mile Island and the space shuttle Challenger explosion. Then he set his sights on those unpleasant "surprises" that pop up with alarming regularity in business: earnings shortfalls, market share plunges, management departures, fraud investigations, even stock market crashes.

His conclusion: "There is no such thing as a legitimate business surprise. There is always warning in advance of a business mishap."

McGee lays out the implications of this conclusion in "Heads Up: How to Anticipate Business Surprises and Seize Opportunities First," a book released in April by Harvard Business School Press. He argues that most, if not all, disasters-in-progress can be forestalled if companies retool themselves to be alert for telltale data and take immediate action.

"If managers and businesses focus on capturing and presenting certain material information in real time," McGee wrote, "they can 'predict the present' and put an end to the devastating consequences that follow in the footsteps of business surprises."

The culprit is usually not bad management, McGee said in an interview, but a business culture conditioned to reacting to past data rather than using real-time data to detect worrisome trends and prevent problems before they occur. Executives who wait patiently for a look at their financial results a week after a quarter has closed would never tolerate such delays in other aspects of their lives, he suggested.

"CEOs wake up and listen to the news in real time," McGee noted. "Then they drive to work and see the speedometer in real time. And when they say hello to the guard in the lobby, he's monitoring the building in real time. But when they close the door to their office, that's the end of the real-time feeds. They're running their business blindly."

McGee builds his no-surprises argument around the notion of "precursor events" that are tipoffs to disasters in the making.

In the natural world, hurricanes are preceded by rising sea levels and changes in barometric pressure, and earthquakes by extreme low frequency waves that indicate the shifting of tectonic plates. Inquiries showed that the same kind of faulty relief valve that caused the Three Mile Island meltdown had failed 18 months earlier at an Ohio nuclear plant built by the same company, and that Challenger engineers had warned about the vulnerability of solid rocket motor O rings in cool temperatures; in these cases, advance warnings weren't acted upon.

For businesses, the key is what McGee calls "materiality" -- determining what data are so critical to an enterprise that they must be monitored in real time for evidence of precursor events. This doesn't require companies to rip out their information technology infrastructure and start over. Chief executives may need only a small bit of real-time data to identify pending mishaps, so long as they act on them, McGee said.

McGee's thesis has its skeptics. Bala Iyer, assistant professor at Boston University's School of Management, said the very best real-time data can't protect companies from business cycles.

"People are always enamored of this notion of getting real-time data," Iyer observed. "It's a very sexy concept. . . . But just because we get more real-time data doesn't change anything. We have control over our data, but we don't have control over the larger environment."

Most important for companies, Iyer said, is technology "architecture" that enables executives to sort the data by priority and make sense of them, while empowering the executives to take timely action.

Iyer and McGee agree on one point: companies should take pains to identify and track their priority data. "Businesses need early detection, not just faster response," McGee said.
