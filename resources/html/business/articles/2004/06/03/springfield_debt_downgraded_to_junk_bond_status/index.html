Springfield debt downgraded to junk-bond status

By Scott S. Greenberger, Globe Staff  |  June 3, 2004

The Standard & Poor's investment house yesterday downgraded the city of Springfield's debt to junk-bond status, and Moody's took a step in the same direction, as concern over the fiscal plight of the third-largest city in Massachusetts reverberated from Beacon Hill to Wall Street.

The $20 million gap in Springfield's $400 million budget has prompted the Romney administration to consider a state bailout and the creation of an oversight board to help the city manage its finances. Yesterday's development increases the pressure for state action, which the Legislature must approve.

Aides to the governor have described Springfield's situation as the worst facing a Massachusetts city since the state took over Chelsea's finances in 1991.

No other Massachusetts city is rated as poorly on Wall Street, and only 2 percent of cities nationwide are considered to be such a risky investment. The move by Standard & Poor's puts Springfield in the company of such downtrodden economically burdened cities as Camden, N.J.; Troy, N.Y.; Cranston, R.I.; and Pittsburgh, which is operating under a control board. The downgrade will make it more difficult and expensive for Springfield to borrow money.

The downgrading is an important symbolic statement about Springfield's finances, but as a practical matter it is not expected to have an immediate impact on the city's ability to borrow money. S & P said that Springfield has no outstanding bonds issued under the city's own credit rating. Instead, in recent years the city has sold bonds under the Massachusetts Qualified Bond Act, which allows cities and towns to take advantage of the state's higher credit rating.

Eric Kriss, Romney's budget chief and a primary architect of the state takeover that revived Chelsea more than a decade ago, said yesterday's action on Wall Street "only underscores the seriousness of what we're dealing with" and that it has accelerated the administration's timetable for a bailout proposal to "a matter of days, not weeks."

"We are crafting a package with the Legislature and the city to fully address all the issues that Springfield faces, including the reasons for the downgrade," Kriss said. "Junk-bond status is not totally unprecedented, but it is a very serious step."

In recent years, advisory boards have helped nurse Brockton, Lawrence, and Lowell to fiscal health. It is still unclear what powers the Springfield panel would have, but Kriss said they will probably be less far-reaching than those of Chelsea's receiver, who was empowered to act without the consent of city officials.

Springfield's state legislators are expected to support an aid bill for the city. Several have warned that lawmakers from other parts of the state may want to include help for their communities, many of which have been hurt by recent cuts in state aid, before voting for it. A spokesman for House Speaker Thomas M. Finneran said Finneran did not want speculate about how a bailout might be received in the House. Senate President Robert E. Travaglini did not return a call seeking comment.

A state board may throw a preliminary lifeline to Springfield today. City officials will come to Boston to ask the Municipal Finance Oversight Board -- which comprises the state treasurer, the state auditor, the attorney general, and a Department of Revenue official -- to help it deal with more than $55 million in bond payments that are due in June and July. The city wants to roll over, or reissue, those bonds and is asking the state to cover the interest payments. The move is akin to a homeowner refinancing a mortgage.

The first bond payment of $37 million is due June 18, according to city leaders. State officials could not attach a dollar amount to the interest payments, but they said they wouldn't drain state coffers, since the same amount would be deducted from Springfield's regular state aid. Because Springfield's state aid would shrink, action by the Municipal Finance Oversight Board would help the city meet its immediate financial obligations but wouldn't help it close the estimated $20 million gap in its operating budget.

According to an advisory that Standard & Poor's issued late yesterday, its downgrade of Springfield's debt "reflects the city's growing structural gap, the potential need for state financial assistance to ease cash flow stress, and the possible need for some form of a control board" to help the city manage its finances.

The firm also suspended Springfield's rating, essentially leaving it without a rating, because it said it has been unable to get up-to-date information from the city. "The suspension is based on the lack of comprehensive current financial information, as well as the city's unwillingness to fully respond to our questions regarding its financial position, resulting in an inability to fully assess the likelihood of timely bond repayment," Standard & Poor's said in its advisory.

Karl Jacob, the director of Standard & Poor's public finance department, explained that "when a rating cascades down like that, it's because a problem is growing and the response to the problem is not as timely as it should be."

Separately, Moody's Investors Service said it was placing Springfield's debt on a "watch list for potential downgrade," which could be a prelude to lowering the city's debt to junk-bond status. Moody's cited "narrowing financial operations and a looming budgetary gap."

"Based upon information that we have today, there is at least a 50 percent probability that it will go down," said Yaffa Rattner, a senior vice president at Moody's. "Absent a governmental fix, be it with local officials or state officials, there is a large, looming deficit in fiscal 2005 that can not be accommodated with current resources."

Springfield Mayor Charles V. Ryan Jr., who has been working with Kriss on an aid package for the city, said yesterday that "the ball is in the governor's court."

"I'm not surprised; this is where we are," Ryan said of Wall Street's downbeat assessment. "As quickly as we come to grips with the state, we'll be supplying them with all the information we have."

Kriss said the administration has been concerned about Springfield since it took office 18 months ago. A Department of Revenue report on Springfield's plight, released in April 2003, found that stagnant property values deprived the city of the robust revenue growth that other Massachusetts cities enjoyed during the 1990s. The report said Springfield relied on reserves to balance its budgets between 1998 and 2002, and that each year it spent an average of $3.86 million more than it took in. By January 2003, the report said, the city had depleted its reserves.

One city councilor said last week that Springfield has borrowed too much money to build schools and refurbish municipal buildings. Ryan, who took office last January, blamed the city's predicament on nonschool state aid, which has remained stagnant over the past two decades, as the costs of health insurance, debt service, pensions, and public safety have risen sharply.

Springfield relies on state dollars for more than 60 percent of its overall revenue, and the state's contribution to the city's coffers has increased from $113 million in 1987 to $262 million in the current fiscal year. Almost all of that growth is attributable to increases in education aid, which was required by the 1993 Education Reform Act. Springfield's education aid, which it can't use to pay for public safety or other nonschool purposes, rose from $80.7 million in 1987 to $229 million this year.

In contrast, the city's nonschool aid increased from $32.5 million to $33.8 million, or 4 percent, during the same 18-year period. At the same time, its health insurance costs increased by about 500 percent, debt service went up 436 percent, pensions payments rose by 379 percent, and the cost of the Police Department increased 127 percent.

Ryan notes that Springfield ranks 36th in so-called additional assistance, the primary state pool for nonschool aid, receiving less than $2 million, even though it is the state's third-largest city. Kriss denied that stagnant state aid is exclusively to blame for Springfield's problems.
