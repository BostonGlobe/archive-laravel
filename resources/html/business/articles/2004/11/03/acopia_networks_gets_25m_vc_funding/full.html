BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Acopia Networks gets $25m VC funding

November 3, 2004

Acopia Networks Inc., a Lowell start-up that makes computer networking equipment to improve access to stored data, has landed a $25 million third round of venture capital funding, one of the largest local deals in that sector in years. Acopia, which has about 100 employees, plans to use the funds to expand sales globally. Top New York investment bank Goldman Sachs led the round as a new investor, and prior backers Charles River Ventures, Accel Partners, Star Ventures, and Vesbridge Partners reinvested. Acopia devices help companies and organizations get easier, faster access to information stored in multiple data-storage systems, including systems that do not communicate with each other. (Peter J. Howe)

SEC sues Boston hedge fund manager

The Securities and Exchange Commission accused a purported Boston hedge fund manager, Treyton L. Thomas, of manipulating the stock of a Canadian software company through a fake bid to buy out the firm. The civil suit filed Monday by the SEC said Thomas used his firm, Pembridge Group Ltd., as a front in March 2002 to make a $4 a share tender offer for shares of Vancouver-based Imagis Technology Inc., almost double the stock price The lawsuit said Thomas falsely claimed Pembridge had hundreds of millions to invest, when it did not. Thomas, 48, served on Imagis's board for four years. The tender offer sent Imagis shares soaring to $3.40. Meanwhile, the SEC said Thomas profited, by an unknown amount, by arranging to purchase warrants of Imagis's stock through Pembridge and a second offshore investment company. Thomas's attorney, Michael Collora, said his client will ''vigorously contest the charges." (Andrew Caffrey)

Millennium drug wins FDA fast-track OK

Biopharmaceutical company Millennium Pharmaceuticals Inc. said the Food and Drug Administration granted the company's Velcade cancer drug fast-track designation for relapsed and treatment-resistant mantle cell lymphoma. Shares of Cambridge-based Millennium rose 54 cents, or 4.3 percent, to $13.22 in after-hours trading. The company is currently testing the drug for the indication in midstage clinical trials. A fast-track designation allows a drug sponsor to submit data to the FDA as they become available and gain feedback rather than having to collect data for a submission all at once. (AP)

R.I. company to lay off 160 workers

About 160 workers at Union Wadding Co., one of Rhode Island's oldest manufacturers, received notice they'll lose their jobs this month, while the insolvent company tries to find a buyer. Union Wadding of Pawtucket went into receivership last month, according to court documents. The company ''is far in arrears in payment of its obligations, and there is a danger of dissipation and depreciation" of its assets, according to a court document signed by Kenneth Washburn, its president and a shareholder. Union Wadding makes tinsel, holiday decorations, and other items. (AP)

THE NATION
Martha Stewart Living restates first half

Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia Inc. has filed amended statements with the Securities and Exchange Commission to reflect a restatement of its loss for the first half of the year. The filings confirmed the company's previous estimate for the restatement. MSLO said its net loss was $37.3 million, or 75 cents a share, for the six months ended June 30, compared with the loss of $39.6 million, or 80 cents a share, initially reported. The media company restated because it changed its accounting policy for subscription acquisition costs in the third quarter. MSLO repeated that it expects a loss of 20 cents a share for its fourth quarter ending Dec. 31. (Dow Jones/AP)

Property insurance losses hit $21.3b

The property insurance industry sustained record third-quarter losses this year after a series of storms, including a string of hurricanes, according to an actuarial firm's preliminary estimate. Eight catastrophes contributed to $21.3 billion in insured property loss claims, according to Insurance Services Office Inc. That figure compares to $3.7 billion from last year's third quarter and $19.15 billion in the third quarter of 2001 -- the previous record, which included $18.8 billion in insured property losses from the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks. For the first nine months of 2004, insured losses stand at $24.7 billion, second only to the $26.1 billion from the first nine months in 2001. This year's four major hurricanes together accounted for an estimated $20.5 billion -- just over the $20.3 billion loss caused by Hurricane Andrew in 1992, in inflation-adjusted terms, ISO spokesman Dave Dasgupta said. (AP)

Capital Group, Vanguard lead inflows

Capital Group Cos. and Vanguard Group had the largest net inflows in September, attracting almost half of the new money invested that month, according to estimates by consulting firm Financial Research Corp. Capital Group, the Los Angeles-based parent of American Funds, gained a net $6.1 billion. Vanguard, of Valley Forge, Pa., attracted $4.5 billion, Boston-based FRC said. American Funds, the most popular fund group for the 18th straight month, ran the five best-selling funds this year through September. The firm's flagship Growth Fund of America was the top-selling fund for the month, attracting $1.29 billion. State Street Corp.'s SPDR Trust, an exchange-traded fund, was number two, collecting $1.07 billion. Investors added a net $21.5 billion to stock and bond funds in September, up 63 percent from August, when investors put in $13.2 billion, FRC said. AIM Distributors, a unit of Amvescap PLC, had the biggest redemptions during September as investors pulled $1.72 billion from its funds. Boston-based Putnam Investments, a unit of Marsh & McLennan Cos., had the second-largest net redemptions with $1.51 billion. Investors have withdrawn $20.4 billion from Putnam funds this year through September. (Bloomberg)

Marsh & McLennan to collect incentives

Marsh & McLennan Companies Inc., the insurance brokerage giant, is looking to collect about $230 million in incentive fees at the heart of a bid-rigging probe by New York authorities as a way to help fund a possible settlement over the questioned fees. The company said it will put the market service agreement fees into a separate account to be used as part of any restitution agreement it may reach with New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer. Marsh said its clients owed about $230 million in incentive fees as of Sept. 30. Spitzer has accused Marsh & McLennan of bid rigging and using the incentive fees -- also known as contingent commissions -- to manipulate the sales of corporate property and casualty policies. As a result, businesses have paid more than necessary for their coverage. (AP)

THE WORLD
Hollinger chief executive resigns

Conrad Black quit as chairman and chief executive of Hollinger Inc., the company that controls the publisher of the Chicago Sun-Times, avoiding for now a court battle with a minority investor. The investor, Catalyst Fund General Partner I Inc., wanted an Ontario Superior Court judge to remove Black, contending he directed transactions to benefit Ravelston Corp., his private holding company, at the expense of minority shareholders. Among other accusations, the fund says Hollinger Inc. made an almost $900,000 payment to Ravelston without board approval. By quitting, Black sidesteps that dispute while he pursues a proposal to take Toronto-based Hollinger private. He remains controlling shareholder with 78 percent of the common stock. (Bloomberg)
