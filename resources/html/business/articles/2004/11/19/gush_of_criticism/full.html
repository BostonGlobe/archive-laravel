Gush of criticism

Greenway panel says the proposed fountain lacks a sense of place

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr., Globe Staff  |  November 19, 2004

A fountain that's planned as a centerpiece for a key stretch of the Rose Kennedy Greenway is drawing objections from Boston residents and a design review panel -- just one month before bidding on construction work is supposed to begin.

Critics say the fountain is banal and has no sense of Boston in its design, and they want something more inspired.

"They kept talking about the fountain as being shaped like a nautilus," said Edwin Schlossberg, a member of the Design Advisory Committee that was assigned belatedly to oversee the Greenway planning. "We were sort of disappointed. Why not a quahog? We were looking for something that spoke about Boston."

The criticism, echoing earlier comments about the entire stretch of parkland between Christopher Columbus Park and High Street, has forced the Massachusetts Turnpike Authority's West Coast team of designers -- EDAW and Wet Design -- to rethink the fountain's form.

Although the designers had moved from the nautilus idea to a font of concentric circles -- enhanced by a rose-colored pavement pattern and by lights, fog, and the potential for ice sculpture in the winter -- critics say that concept also needs much more work.

"I suspect there are going to be some further changes," said Gary Hack, chairman of the advisory panel and dean of the School of Design at the University of Pennsylvania, who saw a revised fountain design two weeks ago. "It's a work in progress."

Tensions between the design team and the advisory panel have risen over whether the fountain now being designed qualifies as the icon that was intended for the greenway named after the mother of Senator Edward M. Kennedy.

"The fountain, that whole plaza, is a place we hope is a focal point for the city," Mikyoung Kim, of the Brookline firm Mikyoung Kim Design and a member of the panel, said. "It's not over yet."

The changing fountain, planned for the block adjacent to the New England Aquarium, is symbolic of the meandering path the Wharf District design process has taken.

The Wharf District was by far the most difficult of the three districts of the Rose Kennedy Greenway, the 30-acre strip being reclaimed by the elimination of the elevated Central Artery highway. The two blocks of North End parks and one large plot for Chinatown and the Leather District weren't simple, but community feeling and the design teams' strokes fell into synch in those two locations.

The Wharf District, being larger, has many constituencies: businesses, residents, tourist operations, and environmental advocacy groups. But many who have participated in creating parks there said the design has been hampered by a limited budget.

Furthermore, the City of Boston has never liked what it has seen.

Rebecca Barnes, chief planner for the city and a persistent critic of the Wharf District design, declined to comment on the fountain controversy.

The nearly complete Wharf District design took about two years to come together, with critics asking professionals from across the country to make improvements so the park would be considered unique to the city, as well as "world-class."

But while many believe the collaborative nature of the design process has helped improve the plan, there is still no firm centerpiece.

"EDAW's been extraordinarily responsive -- in fact maybe too responsive, listening to everybody," Hack said.

Panel members said the designers have made improvements to the Wharf District. It now features a "mother's walk" along one edge, where individual bricks may commemorate individual moms. But the panel is still uncertain how the fountain will be coordinated with that pedestrian path.

Chris Fincham, a resident of Harbor Towers and a member of the Wharf District Task Force, a neighborhood group said expectations may have been too high. "I don't know what people mean when they talk about 'world-class' parks," he said.

Barbara Faga, chairman of San Francisco-based EDAW, the landscape and urban design company that led the design, has gone back to the drawing boards more times than she can count. She said extensive, often conflicting public reactions to proposed designs are a common theme these days, but that leads to results most everybody likes.

About two weeks ago, EDAW unveiled the latest version of the Wharf blocks to the task force. "I saw plenty of enthusiasm when they brought that model out," Faga said. "They were thrilled."

Faga herself acknowledges that the parks will evolve, even after the current designs are in place in early 2007. "Pieces change," she said. "We set the stage for ice skating, sculpture, something to be placed there, whether permanent or temporary. There's all those opportunities."

But the Design Advisory Panel, chosen by both city and turnpike officials to offer an overview of the Greenway designs as they near completion, said in a letter last month to both Turnpike Authority chairman Matthew J. Amorello and Mayor Thomas M. Menino, that it was "deeply concerned by the design of the fountain which is to be the centerpiece of the Wharf District park."

Specifically, the committee said it had never been given "a clear explanation of the role of the fountain . . . a visual symbol, a tall plume to draw people to the greenway, or is it to be designed mainly as a space for children and adults to enjoy the water?"

"The design of the fountain falls far short of what Boston deserves as the most prominent symbol of the Rose Kennedy Greenway," the letter said.

J.P. Shadley, a former president of the Boston Society of Landscape Architects, has followed the Wharf District from its inception as a member of the Mayor's Central Artery Completion Task Force.

"The fountain is still a big concern," he said. "We want something that's really very, very special, not just a nice average fountain."
