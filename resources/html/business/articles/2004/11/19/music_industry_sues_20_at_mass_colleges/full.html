Music industry sues 20 at Mass. colleges

November 19, 2004

THE REGION

Twenty people at Massachusetts colleges are among 761 being sued by the recording industry in its latest attack on illegal file-swappers. Since last year, the Recording Industry Association of America has filed thousands of lawsuits against individuals for trading music recordings over the Internet without making payments to recording companies. The latest round of suits includes people connected with several Massachusetts colleges, including Assumption College, Boston College, Boston University, Bridgewater State College, Emerson College, Mount Holyoke College, Northeastern University, and the University of Massachusetts. Association spokesman Jonathan Lamy said he did not know whether the people being sued, who are all unnamed, are students, faculty, or staff members. (Hiawatha Bray)

Casual Male sells last of Dockers outlets

Casual Male Retail Group Inc., which operates Casual Male and Rochester Big & Tall stores, agreed to sell its 32 remaining Levi's/Dockers Outlet stores to an unidentified buyer. The Canton company said it will disclose the sale price and buyer when the deal is completed next week. Since taking over in May 2002, chief executive David A. Levin has been ridding the company of its non-big-and-tall retail operations. He closed other Levi's/Dockers stores, and sold the retailer's 50.5 percent stake in Ecko Unltd. stores. The sale of its remaining Levi's/Dockers stores allows the company to get out of that business one year earlier than it anticipated. Separately, the company said its fiscal third-quarter net loss widened to $1.37 million, from $1.20 million a year earlier. Sales for the quarter ended Oct. 30 fell to $93.9 million, from $97.9 million a year earlier. (Naomi Aoki)

Contractor gets $158m missile pact

Raytheon Co. has won a $158.2 million US Navy contract modification to supply AIM-9X Sidewinder air-to-air missiles and related gear for the United States, Poland, Denmark, South Korea, and Switzerland, the US Defense Department said. The US Air Force will get 178 AIM-9X missiles, and the US Navy will receive 93. Under the US government-to-government weapon sales program, 178 will go to Poland, 60 to Denmark, 41 to South Korea, and one "lot" comprised of a classified number to Switzerland, the Pentagon said. The Sidewinder is described by Raytheon as the most advanced infrared tracking, short-range, air-to-air missile in the free world. (Reuters)

Massport OK's project building changes

The developer of an East Boston residential project called Portside at Pier One said the board of the Massachusetts Port Authority approved its plan to build more condominiums and fewer apartments. Looking to take advantage of a hot condo market, Roseland Property Co. wants to build about 350 condos and 150 rental units, a Roseland spokeswoman said. An earlier plan was for 500 rentals and 80 condos. The change also needs the approval of the Boston Redevelopment Authority, the spokeswoman said. (Chris Reidy)

Charles River Park proposal altered

A plan to build five more apartment buildings at Charles River Park has evolved again. At a meeting this week attended by neighbors concerned about the size of expansion, Equity Residential showed its latest proposal. The tallest buildings would be 14 and 11 stories, down from 16 and 13. Instead of 341 rentals, the new plan calls for 306 apartments and 660 underground parking spaces. Residents in nearby condo buildings fear new buildings could block their views and consume too much open space. An attorney representing trustees of those condo buildings, Lawrence S. DiCara, said of the latest proposal: "We're not there yet, but we're getting closer." (Chris Reidy)

Mass. industry group to form nonprofit

The Massachusetts Medical Device Industry Council plans to disclose today that it is forming a nonprofit unit to assist start-up companies, said Thomas J. Sommer, the council's president. The unit's services will include coaching in obtaining venture capital, business planning, and forming commercial networks. Membership in the fast-growing council now stands at 321 companies, including Boston Scientific Corp. of Natick, the largest life-sciences company in Massachusetts, as measured by market capitalization. The trade group plans to unveil the new unit at its annual investor conference today at the Park Plaza Hotel in Boston. (Ross Kerber)

Software firm has $13m quarterly profit

Novell Inc., a seller of network software and consulting services, had fourth-quarter profit of $13.3 million as sales rose for a fourth-straight quarter. Net income was 3 cents a share in the period ended Oct. 31, compared with a loss of $109 million, or 29 cents, a year earlier, Waltham-based Novell said. Sales gained to $300.7 million, missing the $304.1 million average estimate of 14 analysts in a Thomson Financial survey. Novell is trying to boost revenue by selling products related to the Linux operating system, which competes with Microsoft Windows. Sales of Novell's NetWare program have declined as Windows and Linux gained market share. Excluding any costs or gains that aren't likely to recur, the company was expected to report profit of 5 cents a share, the average estimate of 15 analysts in a Thomson Financial survey. (Bloomberg)

Fed district banks all wanted increase

The Federal Reserve's 12 district banks unanimously recommended an increase in the discount lending rate in September, even as some of them questioned the strength of the US economic recovery, minutes show. At a Sept. 21 meeting, the Fed's board of governors approved requests from directors in the central bank's regional outposts to raise the rate charged on direct loans for banks, known as the discount rate, to 2.75 percent from 2.5 percent. The Fed's Open Market Committee also raised its target for the overnight lending rate to 1.75 percent from 1.5 percent. Regional Fed bank votes serve as signals on the direction of interest rates to the Fed's Board of Governors in Washington. The board, which includes chairman Alan Greenspan and six other governors, must approve the requests. The Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas was the first to seek an increase in the discount interest rate in September before others echoed its call that month. The Fed last week raised the primary discount rate again to 3 percent. (Bloomberg)

Enron bankruptcy reorganization final

Enron Corp., the once-dominant energy trader whose collapse in 2001 led to changes in corporate governance and accounting, completed the second-largest bankruptcy reorganization in US history. Enron's plan to exit bankruptcy has taken effect, the Houston-based company said in a regulatory filing. Company stock was canceled, four board members quit, and a five-member board was named, the filing said. Enron is liquidating and will become a shell company with lawsuits as its primary assets. Enron, the seventh-biggest US corporation in 2000 by sales, won a bankruptcy judge's approval in July of its plan to sell all assets to help pay creditors owed more than $67 billion. The company's December 2001 bankruptcy filing was the largest in US history by debts and the second-largest, behind WorldCom Inc.'s 2002 case, by assets listed in court papers. (Bloomberg)

Disney workers approve contract

Members of Walt Disney World's largest union group, representing 40 percent of the resort's 51,000-person workforce, approved a three-year contract proposal as the current contract was set to expire. The vote -- the fourth attempt at passage -- was 3,771 for and 2,669 against. Disney officials had refused to extend the current contract, as they had previously. The contract would have expired at midnight. (AP)
