BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Companies to settle patent lawsuits

November 25, 2004

3Com Corp., a maker of switches for corporate data networks, and PC-Tel Inc. agreed to end patent-infringement lawsuits. Each agreed to license the other's modem-related patents that were part of the litigation, which began in March 2003. PC-Tel also will supply wireless software and antennas to 3Com. PC-Tel will record as revenue during this quarter $3.2 million it had set aside in case it lost the 3Com lawsuit. PC-Tel, which quit the modem business and now makes wireless software, also has sued Agere Systems Inc. and closely held U.S. Robotics on claims they infringed on PC-Tel's modem patents. PC-Tel sold its modem business in May 2003, a move prompted partly because of the alleged infringements, PC-Tel chief executive Martin Singer said. Neither PC-Tel nor 3Com sells modems any more. Marlborough-based 3Com and Chicago-based PC-Tel declined to provide details of what Singer called a multiyear agreement. (Bloomberg)

Firm says it was hurt by Conn. corruption

Acumentrics Corp., a Westwood fuel cell manufacturer, claims it was the victim of racketeering by top officials of Connecticut Governor John Rowland's administration to funnel money to businessman William Tomasso. The claims, in a federal suit filed in Boston, resemble a US criminal indictment against Tomasso and high-ranking state officials. It alleged kickbacks were paid by Tomasso and his companies to Rowland's co-chief of staff, Peter Ellef, and to Arthur H. Diedrick, executive director of two quasi-public development agencies. Rowland resigned in July. Acumentrics contends the administration offered it $10 million in loans and grants to move to Connecticut but reneged when Acumentrics refused to lease a plant from a Tomasso firm. Acumentrics claims the aborted deal crippled it financially. The Connecticut Development Authority, which was named in the suit, said Acumentrics is "spinning baseless theories." Tomasso attorney Jack Fornaciari said the lawsuit was a "desperate effort not to pay back money." (AP)

15 bids received for Providence hotel

Fifteen potential buyers have bid for the Westin Providence hotel, according to the Rhode Island Convention Center Authority, which began considering bids Tuesday. The state-owned Westin, near the Providence Place mall, is being sold to help shore up state debts of about $285 million on the hotel and convention center. Bidders' names were not released. The four-star Westin's potential sale price hasn't been made public, either. The winner will be required to add 200 rooms. (AP)

THE NATION
Time Warner settlement may be near

Time Warner Inc. may pay up to $500 million to settle probes into whether it improperly booked advertising sales at its America Online unit, people familiar with the matter said. The company and the government still disagree over which, if any, employees may be accused of wrongdoing, said the people. A settlement may lead to one of the largest penalties ever imposed by the Securities and Exchange Commission but allow Time Warner to rebuild credibility with investors, said Peter Goldman, of Chicago Asset Management. The Washington Post reported Time Warner was close to an agreement to pay about $750 million; people familiar with the matter said that figure is too high. The SEC is investigating how Time Warner accounted for subscribers and sales, including $400 million in AOL ad sales to Bertelsmann AG. (Bloomberg)

Airline offers pilots pay-cut ideas

United Airlines has given its pilots union suggestions for making more than $191 million in wage and benefit cuts the carrier says it needs to pull out of bankruptcy -- from a straight 18 percent pay cut to smaller cuts and changes in work rules. The proposal is contained in an analysis by the Air Line Pilots Association negotiating committee that The Associated Press obtained. Pilots' spokesman Dave Kelly called the proposal "an opener" in negotiations with United, which this month said it needed an additional $725 million from its unions on top of the $2.5 billion United employees have already made in annual labor concessions. United agreed the proposal is just a start. United is also proposing that it be allowed to cut pilots' wages another 4 percent if necessary for six months after the airline emerges from bankruptcy. The analysis indicates that if they accept "all the work rule and benefit erosions" proposed by the airline, their pay would be cut 8 percent. Or, the analysis continued, the union "may remove some of the work rule concessions with the additional pay cuts up to approximately 18 percent." (AP)

Judge rejects PeopleSoft settlement plan

A proposal to settle a lawsuit brought last year by shareholders of PeopleSoft Inc. unfairly cuts off their right to sue over the company's resistance to a takeover by Oracle Corp., a judge ruled. "It's just not enough," Vice Chancellor Leo Strine of Delaware's Court of Chancery said. The suit was filed days after Oracle began its long attempt to acquire PeopleSoft. The suit contained allegations of wrongdoing similar to those raised by Oracle itself in its own case against PeopleSoft and its antitakeover measures. Oracle's case will continue in December. Strine ruled the court needed to hear new evidence in the wake of Oracle's latest offer and PeopleSoft's latest rejection. After two weeks of trial in Oracle's lawsuit, the hopeful acquirer raised its offer from $21 per share to $24 and set a Nov. 19 deadline for PeopleSoft shareholders to tender. About 60 percent of PeopleSoft's shareholders responded favorably, but the company's board continues to hold out for a higher price. The decision to reject the proposed settlement leaves PeopleSoft shareholders with the ability to pursue damages against the board. (Dow Jones/AP)

Music companies endorse swap service

Three major recording companies will let their music be shared and sold over a new online file-swapping service that aims to lure fans away from rival services where trading of music and movies remains unfettered. Universal Music Group, Sony BMG Music Entertainment, and Warner Music Group have licensed their music to Saratoga Springs, N.Y.-based Wurld Media, which plans to launch its file-sharing software, Peer Impact, early next year. Details were not released, but the company said it would let consumers buy and share music and video while ensuring artists and rights holders receive "due compensation." (AP)

258 permits granted for FM stations

The Federal Communications Commission's first open auction of FM broadcast spectrum granted 258 station-construction permits to over 100 bidders. A total of 110 bidders won permits, many in remote towns, the FCC said. "We are in new territory with this groundbreaking auction, introducing new owners into the radio market and bringing increased diversity," FCC chairman Michael Powell said. Senator John McCain and others have said the 1996 Telecommunications Act lets large companies like Clear Channel Communications gain too much market share. It owns 1,200 of the 11,000 US radio stations. (Bloomberg)

Complaints about overtime pay rise

Workers' complaints about being denied overtime pay, wages, and job leave rose this year to the highest level in four years, the Labor Department said. Penalties and back-pay awards fell. The department received 31,786 complaints in the year ended Sept. 30, vs. 31,123 in 2003. The increase came as the department was putting in place overtime rules that Democrats warned would strip rights to premium pay for millions of workers. The division assessed nearly $9 million in civil penalties in the past year, compared with nearly $10 million in 2003. It collected $196.7 million in back wages, down from $212.5 million in 2003. (AP)
