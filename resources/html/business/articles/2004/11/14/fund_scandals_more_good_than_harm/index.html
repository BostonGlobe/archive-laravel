Fund scandals: more good than harm

By Chet Currier  |  November 14, 2004

Before the mutual fund scandals of the past 14 months fade from view, a few words of appreciation are in order.

This shock to the system has inflicted little lasting damage on the funds, while rooting out smugness, complacency, and misdeeds in more than a few places.

In big ways and small, fund owners as a group are better served today than they were before Eliot Spitzer, the New York attorney general, came a-knocking Sept. 3, 2003, with charges of trading abuses. Changes continue, and their full effects are still to be measured.

By now, though, it's clear that the post-scandal fund business is healthy, more competitive -- and on notice to mind its methods from here on.

"The scandals took a toll, and the bear market took a toll, too," says Paul Schott Stevens, president of the funds' main trade group, the Investment Company Institute. "But mutual fund investors continue to have a favorable view of fund companies."

Stevens cites an annual ICI tracking survey that found about three-quarters of fund investors were aware of the scandals as of June 2004. Of those, 56 percent said they had a lower opinion of the fund industry, and the other 44 said their view hadn't changed.

The ICI said 72 percent of fund owners gave funds "very favorable" or "somewhat favorable" ratings this year, down from 84 percent at the peak in 2000 but up from 71 percent in 2003.

Dollars-and-cents numbers, which can speak louder than any poll, back Stevens up. Stock funds attracted $138.8 billion from investors in the first nine months of this year, according to ICI data, putting them on track for their best year since a record $309.4 billion inflow in 2000.

"In the context of virtually no stock price appreciation so far in 2004, sizeable equity fund inflows suggest extraordinary confidence among individual investors in America," said Avi Nachmany, research director at New York consulting firm Strategic Insight.

More testimony comes from the stock prices of big fund managers like Franklin Resources Inc., which is up 41.5 percent over the last 14 months, and T. Rowe Price Group, up 33.5 percent -- although there have been exceptions such as Janus Capital Group, down 11.4 percent.

The three largest fund managers -- Fidelity Investments, Vanguard Group, and Capital Group Cos. -- are privately held. They all boast thriving inflows.

Down at the little-things-mean-a-lot level, much of the news is also heartening. Just the other day, Putnam Investments started disclosing how much money its managers invest in their own funds, getting a head start on a new Securities and Exchange Commission rule that takes effect in February.

Fidelity has taken off the gloves in a fee-cutting battle among managers of index funds. Investors aren't going to save a ton of money because of this -- ICI data show that virtually all new money flowing into the largest category of index funds already goes to those with rock-bottom expense ratios. Still, it's the principle of the thing.

One more nagging problem in Fund Land shows signs of solving itself. The push for short-term trading fees to discourage abuses appears to be driving even legitimate market-timers to alternative arenas.

Paul Merriman, a Seattle adviser and money manager, reports he has dropped most mutual funds from his "market-timing model portfolios," and is using exchange-traded funds instead. "Individual investors have found it increasingly difficult to do timing with conventional mutual funds," Merriman says in his FundAdvice.com newsletter.

Though timers may grouse, this should turn out to be a positive development all around. Long-term investors in mutual funds can be hurt by in-and-out flows from timers that increase costs and disrupt a manager's investment decisions.

Exchange traded funds, by contrast, are untroubled by timers since flows aren't an issue with them. They have fixed amounts of shares that are bought and sold like stocks in the secondary market. The number of exchange traded funds for timers to choose among is increasing all the time.

Having pushed investors' patience pretty hard, fund executives know they can ill afford any more scandals. They are fortunate that the turmoil to date has brought more good than harm.

Chet Currier is a columnist for Bloomberg News.
