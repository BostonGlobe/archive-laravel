Crude futures slip on increasing supplies

By Yeoh En-Lai, Associated Press Writer  |  November 5, 2004

SINGAPORE -- Oil futures prices slipped Friday, continuing a downward trend prompted by rising U.S. crude supplies and expectations of a production surge for the Northern Hemisphere winter.

In after-hours electronic deals in Asia mid-afternoon Friday, crude for delivery in December on the New York Mercantile Exchange was trading at US$48.50, down 32 cents from its overnight close.

Light crude fell by US$2.06 to US$48.82 on Thursday, the first time it has settled below US$49 since Sept. 24.

Gulf of Mexico production has been recovering from its battering by Hurricane Ivan in mid-September, boosting U.S. crude supplies and helping prices fall by more than US$6 since hitting a record of US$55.17 a barrel on Oct. 22 and Oct. 26.

"Crude inventories have been building in the United States and that is a result of production that was temporarily stopped due to Hurricane Ivan," said Victor Shum, an oil analyst at Texas-headquartered Purvin & Gertz in Singapore.

"When prices start to go one way or the other, there is a certain momentum," he said.

The U.S. Energy Department said this week that commercially available stocks of crude in the United States rose by 6.3 million barrels to 289.7 million barrels last week -- about 1 percent below year-ago levels.

Also easing pressure on oil prices was Thursday's Energy Department report that natural gas in storage had risen a larger-than-expected 44 billion cubic feet to 3.29 trillion cubic feet.

Markets appeared to largely ignore a seventh straight week of falls in distillates, which include heating oil, kerosene and jet fuel, which are down 12 percent from a year-ago in the United States and similar amounts in Western Europe and Japan.

But Shum and other analysts say there is still time for refineries to build up heating oil stocks.

"The expectation in the market is that with the rising crude oil inventories and the refineries coming out of maintenance in autumn, there is a chance to catch up" before any winter shortage, he said.

In Asia Friday, heating oil stood at US$1.3620 per gallon (3.8 liters) while natural gas stood at US$8.189 per 1,000 cubic feet (330 cubic meters). Both commodities were down a cent in afternoon trade.

Adam Sieminski of Deutsche Bank in London said worries about ventories ahead of winter could push prices higher in the near term, but he expected fears to ease and prices to be closer to US$40 than US$50 by early 2005.

Other factors keeping markets nervous include the suspension in oil supply from northern Iraq after a key pipeline was attacked, a general strike planned for Nov. 16 in Nigeria to protest rising domestic fuel prices and continuing legal woes for near-bankrupt Russian oil giant OAO Yukos.

In the world's second most populous country, India, state-run oil firms said Thursday they would raise gasoline and diesel prices by up to 8 percent to offset rising crude costs.

India imports nearly three-fourths of its crude oil, totaling 120 million tons last year.
