Banking on convenience

Despite their community focus, small players are losing share to big guys

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  November 2, 2004

FRANKLIN -- A middle-aged woman walked in to the Benjamin Franklin Savings Bank a year ago to deposit a check in her son's account. She got to talking with the teller, and after swapping stories of their recent divorces -- and their new boyfriends -- they decided to go on a double date.

To the teller, Elizabeth Bailey, the best part of their outing, an elegant night of prime rib and seafood, was the punch line: ''Come to find out, her boyfriend was a customer of the bank!" she said. The rest of her teller-customer double date, she said, was par for the course for Benjamin Franklin Savings Bank employees, who frequently get invited to weddings, christenings, cookouts, and parties hosted by their customers.

But despite its personal connection to many customers, this six-branch bank, which likes to boast that it is the largest bank in Franklin, has been steadily losing market share to large banks such as Citizens Financial Group, Sovereign Bancorp, and Banknorth Group in the past several years. Its net income in 2003 fell by more than $600,000, down 22 percent from the year before.

The story of Benjamin Franklin Savings Bank, founded more than 100 years ago, mirrors the predicament of small- to midsize banks across Massachusetts. The region's biggest banks are winning huge segments of the market, while many community banks show declines or remain flat. In the last four years, New England's four biggest banks have grown to dominate half the market, up from 36 percent in 2000, according to Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. data in a sample of 10 Massachusetts counties.

Even worse for the state's community banks, many of the giant players have made their gains while aggressively marketing themselves as local banks. Citizens, a subsidiary of the Royal Bank of Scotland, gave out free soccer balls to children; Bank of America Corp. of Charlotte, N.C., which acquired FleetBoston Financial Corp. in the spring, sponsors Little League. The big banks also have ramped up their focus on customer service, which historically has been a strength of community banks. Those efforts have added to the convenience of big banks' branch-on-every-corner approach to persuade customers to switch, bankers say.

''There's an ever-increasing need for convenience," said Robert Mahoney, a Citizens vice chairman. ''People are living further and further away from the city in ever more dispersed areas. The bigger banks have been able to afford more branches."

The large banks also are adept at buying market share: At least a portion of their gains have come through acquiring smaller banks.

The gains in the big banks are not likely to mean the demise of community banks anytime soon, though they may find it difficult to grow. Most of Massachusetts' community banks are not publicly traded, which reduces pressure to meet earnings targets. In fact, many of them are adding deposits, but their growth has not kept pace with the growth of the market.

At Bank of America, executives like to talk about what they call the ''barbell theory" of bank consolidation, in which a handful of giant banks dominate one end, thousands of community banks thrive on the other end, with very little in between. Under the theory, midsize regional banks, such as Sovereign, would increasingly see their profits squeezed until they merge with a bigger player.

That pressure on earnings may hold true for tiny Benjamin Franklin, with the motto ''Bank with Ben." The bank recently said it would acquire Chart Bank of Waltham, which has three branches, bringing its total to nine. It also plans to issue stock to finance the acquisition, a move that ultimately means it could be snapped up by bigger banks.

Still, Benjamin Franklin's chief executive, Thomas R. Venables, insists the acquisition of Chart Bank is not about the need to grow bigger, or its desire to be acquired, but more about ''continuing to offer community banking services" and to add branches in areas where it already does a lot of lending. He attributed the drop in the bank's profits to losses in the stock market several years ago, which still affect the balance sheet.

In fact, he said, Benjamin Franklin is stronger than ever and is gaining customers, even if its market share has not kept pace. He also says big banks will never replace community banks.

''It's hard for them to be as involved in our towns," he said. ''We're actually part of our towns."

Among the big banks, the gains in market share have not been uniform. After Fleet announced its merger with BankBoston in 1999, the bank's deposits began to plummet in Massachusetts, according to the FDIC. In Norfolk County alone, Fleet's market share tumbled from more than 27 percent to 22 percent in the last four years, while competitors added millions of dollars to their books.

But in the last year, the bank turned the corner and started to gain back market share across the state. Executives attribute the turnaround to an initiative to improve customer service, which began before its merger with Bank of America but picked up steam in the months afterward. The bank also introduced free checking and free online bill paying, which executives say has attracted thousands of customers.

''What we bring to the consumer offering is enormous convenience," said Steven Lubetkin, a spokesman for Bank of America. ''We have a lot of different channels for customers to do business with us, like telephone banking, ATMs, and online banking. All of these things pay dividends."

The sample of 10 counties included most of the suburbs in Massachusetts but excluded Suffolk and Worcester counties. Some of the state's big banks are headquartered in those counties, and they typically count a large portion of commercial deposits in those areas. The growth in the remaining counties, on the other hand, is more likely to reflect gains in individual customers.

One chief winner from the Fleet exodus has been Citizens, which has grown from a distant second to a close rival of Bank of America. Citizens has nearly doubled its market share in the 10 Massachusetts counties in the last four years, growing to $14.7 billion in deposits from $5.8 billion. Sovereign and Banknorth also have picked up huge amounts of market share.

Citizens executives say the bank's model for growth is simple: good customer service, incentive bonuses for employees, and creating a massive network of branches to give customers more convenience.

''It's not very magical," said Mahoney, the Citizens vice chairman. ''It's doing what your mom taught you to do: smile, say please, say thank you, and be nice."
