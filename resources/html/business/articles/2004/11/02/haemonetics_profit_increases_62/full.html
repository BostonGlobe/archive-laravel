BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Haemonetics profit increases 62%

November 2, 2004

Haemonetics Corp., a maker of blood collection products for hospitals, said profit rose 62 percent in the fiscal second quarter as prices and sales volume increased. The Braintree company earned $8.9 million, or 34 cents per share, up from $5.5 million, or 23 cents per share, a year ago. At constant currencies, Haemonetics said it would have earned 41 cents per share, up from 33 cents per share a year ago. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call predicted earnings of 33 cents per share. Revenue rose 4 percent to $90.9 million in the three months ended Oct. 2 from $87.5 million during the same period last year. The company said price improvements, increased sales volume, currency, and changes in the mix of products being sold all contributed to revenue growth, partially offset by consolidation in the plasma market. Haemonetics said it continues to expect fiscal 2005 earnings of $1.38 to $1.43 per share on sales growth in the midsingle digits. (AP)

Lincoln Park executives indicted again

The former chief executives of Lincoln Park and its British parent company, Wembley PLC, entered another round of innocent pleas, as they were indicted again on charges connected to an alleged bribery scheme. Former Wembley PLC chief executive Nigel Potter and former Lincoln Park chief executive and general manager Daniel Bucci appeared in federal court in Providence with their lawyers. An attorney for Lincoln Park, the state's largest gambling venue and dog track, also attended to enter an innocent plea on behalf of the company. Potter and Bucci were both released, with no change to the conditions imposed on them after their first arraignment more than a year ago. The new indictment against the two men and Lincoln Park replaces one issued by a grand jury on Sept. 9, 2003. Federal prosecutors say they concocted a scheme to pay million of dollars to the law firm of former Rhode Island House Speaker John Harwood in exchange for the state's Lottery Commission allowing the park to install more video lottery machines, and for lawmakers' continued opposition to a Narragansett Indian casino. Harwood has not been charged. (AP)

LTX lowers outlook for first quarter

LTX Corp. lowered its August guidance for the fiscal first quarter, citing weak semiconductor equipment conditions. The Westwood supplier of semiconductor test systems cut revenue guidance for the quarter ended Oct. 31 to $43 million, down 20 percent from the previous guidance range of $54 million to $57 million. The company expects its first-quarter loss to be 18 to 20 cents a share, wider than its previous loss range of 7 to 10 cents a share. LTX said the guidance doesn't include any provisions for one-time charges. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call are forecasting a first-quarter loss of 9 cents a share. LTX said customers' near-term business outlook is cautionary from a lack of end-user demand, which has affected capital acquisition plans and delayed orders for production test capacity. (Dow Jones)

Kronos profit jumps 51% in 4th quarter

Kronos Inc. posted fourth-quarter profit growth of 51 percent helped by sales of its Workforce Central 5 software, and the company expects fiscal 2005 earnings of $1.58 a share to $1.65 a share. The Chelmsford workforce management concern said fourth-quarter earnings increased to $18.1 million, or 56 cents a share, from $12 million, or 38 cents a share, a year ago. Wall Street expected the company to earn 51 cents a share, according to a Thomson survey of 10 analysts. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION
Supreme Court rejects fen-phen appeal

The Supreme Court declined to hear an appeal from a drug manufacturer that sought to limit evidence that fen-phen users want to present in their lawsuits claiming heart valve damage. In a defeat for drug maker Wyeth, the court let stand a lower ruling that a federal judge went too far when he issued an order restricting the evidence. The Philadelphia judge reasoned that the diet drug users had violated a $3.75 billion settlement agreement with the company. In legal filings, Wyeth argued that former users of its Pondimin and Redux pills violated the agreement not to seek punitive damages when they chose to ''opt out" of the federal class-action settlement to pursue their own lawsuits. The judge properly barred the plaintiffs from using evidence in state court such as those suggesting Wyeth placed marketing ahead of health and safety concerns, because the evidence might encourage juries to award punitive damages, the company argued. (AP)

Valero to buy pipeline operator for $2.8b

Valero LP said it will purchase Kaneb Services and Kaneb Pipe Line Partners in a $2.8 billion deal that will create one of the nation's largest petroleum pipeline and oil terminal operators. Valero will control nearly 10,000 miles of oil and refined-products pipelines after the deal, which is expected to close in the first quarter of 2005. It will also own 101 terminal facilities located in 30 states and abroad and four crude oil storage facilities with a capacity of 85 million barrels, Valero said. The deal is subject to the approval of the unitholders of Valero LP and Kaneb Partners and the shareholders of Kaneb Services, as well as federal regulators. (AP)

AFC Enterprises sells unit for $390m

AFC Enterprises Inc. said it agreed to sell its Church's Chicken brand to a private investment firm for $390 million. AFC, which also franchises and operates Popeyes Chicken & Biscuits, Cinnabon, and Seattle's Best Coffee, said Crescent Capital Investments Inc. will buy Church's for $383 million in cash and a $7 million note from an affiliated company. The sale is expected to generate net proceeds of about $275 million and close by year-end. (Dow Jones)

Wachovia wraps $14.9b SouthTrust deal

Wachovia Corp. said it completed its $14.9 billion purchase of SouthTrust Corp. to form the biggest bank in the Southeast. Shareholders of SouthTrust received 0.89 of a share of Wachovia stock for each share of SouthTrust they own. The process of combining the two companies will be complete by the end of 2005, Wachovia said. Wachovia chief executive Kennedy Thompson agreed to buy SouthTrust in June to help the fourth-largest US bank enter Texas. Combined, the two banks will have about $500 billion in assets, 95,000 employees, 14 million customers, and 3,200 consumer offices stretching form Connecticut to Texas. (Bloomberg)

THE WORLD
Chip sales growth pace hits low for year

Global semiconductor sales rose 27 percent in September from a year earlier, the smallest climb in eight months, on slowing demand by makers of mobile phones and personal computers, according to industry statistics. The $18.4 billion in September sales was 1 percent higher than in August, said the Semiconductor Industry Association. That is the slowest growth since January, when it was 26.6 percent. The results ''reflect continuing growth in demand for semiconductors," restrained by excess industry inventory, association president George Scalise said. Companies such as Intel Corp., the world's largest maker of microprocessors, the engines that run personal computers, have said they overestimated demand in the second half of the year and made too many. Compared to August, September semiconductor sales in the Americas fell 1.7 percent to $3.32 billion; European sales rose 4.1 percent to $3.35 billion; Asia-Pacific sales rose 1.6 percent to $7.79 billion; and Japanese sales fell 0.5 percent to $3.95 billion. (Bloomberg)
