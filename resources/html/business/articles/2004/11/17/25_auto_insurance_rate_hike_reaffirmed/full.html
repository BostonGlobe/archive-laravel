2.5% auto insurance rate hike reaffirmed

November 17, 2004

The state's highest court affirmed an already approved 2.5 percent average increase in auto insurance premiums for this year, but said the insurance commissioner may have to revise her decision in a way that would ease premiums for certain drivers. The Supreme Judicial Court rejected Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly's challenge of a new formula used by Insurance Commissioner Julianne M. Bowler to calculate insurance company underwriting profits, which boosted overall premiums by an estimated $80 million. But the court said Bowler will have to revisit her decision on the rates for bodily injury coverage in excess of the basic limits, which is $20,000 per person and $40,000 per accident. Reilly had argued the rates for that coverage were inflated because of a calculation error. The court said Bowler would have to justify her decision or change it. (Bruce Mohl)

Bank of America donates $420,000

Bank of America Corp., the North Carolina bank that acquired FleetBoston Financial Corp. in the spring, pledged $400,000 to two Massachusetts nonprofits. The bank selected Lawrence CommunityWorks and STRIVE/Boston Employment Services Inc. The bank is funding the donations through its new ''Neighborhood Excellence" initiative, which locally focuses its philanthropy. Bank of America also selected five ''local heroes" and agreed to donate $5,000 to nonprofits of their choice. The local heroes are: Michael Frank, president of the Regan Youth League in Jamaica Plain; Noreen Grice, planetarium operations coordinator for the Museum of Science in Boston; Elizabeth Miranda, Multicultural Youth Tour of What's Now (MYTown) in Roxbury; Dr. Esther Pearson, founder of the Mary McLeod Bethune Institute for Young Women in Groton; and Thomas Wheeler, founder and executive director of the Blues Bank Collective in Portsmouth, N.H. (Sasha Talcott)

Arch Wireless merger clears hurdle

The Department of Justice said it has decided not to challenge the acquisition of paging carrier Metrocall Holdings Inc. by rival Arch Wireless Inc. of Westborough. The unusual deal, disclosed March 30, would give Arch stockholders majority ownership but have Metrocall's management team run the new company from their offices in Alexandria, Va. The Justice Department's antitrust division said it had closed an investigation into the planned transaction because ''the facts did not support a conclusion that this merger would give a combined Arch/Metrocall market power in the markets in which they compete." (Robert Weisman)

Cognex, Oregon firm settle lawsuit

Cognex Corp. and Electro Scientific Industries Inc. signed a settlement and license agreement, which resolves a dispute relating to Electro's alleged infringement on one of Cognex's patents. Natick-based Cognex, a maker of machine vision systems, said it has granted Portland, Ore.-based Electro a license to the patent in dispute, and certain covenants, not to sue under various other patents related to surface mount placement equipment. In consideration, Electro has agreed to pay Cognex an undisclosed license fee. The agreement allows for the possibility of Electro Scientific purchasing Cognex machine vision products. (Dow Jones)

Software pirate sentenced to year in jail

A Holbrook man charged with conspiracy in a nationwide software piracy case has been sentenced to one year in federal prison. John Neas, 49, was sentenced Monday in US District Court in Concord, N.H., the US attorney's office said. Neas was the seventh to be sentenced in the case in which several people pleaded guilty to stealing and distributing computer software around the world. Assistant US Attorney Arnold Huftalen said the scheme involved copying and sharing the software through secret and stolen Internet links at banks, communications companies, and data centers. Huftalen said the case began to develop in 2000 with the help of a New Hampshire resident involved in one of the schemes; and it took authorities three years to compile the case. Among the companies used to distribute the software without the businesses' knowledge was Bank of America in Boston. Neas operated a server in Easton called City Morgue. (AP)

Supply figures drive crude prices lower

Crude oil futures fell after moving above $47 a barrel in intraday trading. December-delivery crude on the New York Mercantile Exchange dropped 76 cents to $46.11 per barrel. The benchmark light, sweet crude remained about $8 a barrel cheaper than its closing record of $55.17, recorded Oct. 22 and Oct. 26. Heating oil for December delivery settled down 1.62 cents at $1.33 per gallon on Nymex, after rising in the session to an intraday high of $1.37. In London, Brent crude for January delivery fell 75 cents on the International Petroleum Exchange, to $42.29. Commercially available crude supplies have been growing for nearly two months, according to the Energy Department, and traders expect the agency's report today to show another increase. But the market remains cautious over the supply of distillates, which has fallen for eight straight weeks in the United States. Distillate stocks include heating oil, jet fuel, and diesel. (AP)

SBC Communications in $400m deal

SBC Communications plans to disclose today that it will pay $400 million to Microsoft for software used to deliver TV programming over high-speed data lines, according to today's editions of The New York Times. SBC will spend more than $4 billion over the next three years on its fiber-optic network to offer faster Internet connections capable of carrying digital programming. Microsoft has long wanted to get into television programming. SBC plans to use Microsoft software to encode and compress programming sent to users and then decode the programs on TV set-top boxes in customers' homes.

Movie industry sues over Internet copies

A trade group representing seven major movie studios filed the first wave of lawsuits against individuals they say are offering pirated copies of films using Internet-based peer-to-peer file-sharing programs. As part of a larger effort to combat piracy, the Motion Picture Association of America also said it would soon make available a computer program that sniffs out movie and music files on a user's computer and any installed file-sharing programs. The MPAA revealed the federal court suits, but did not say how many defendants were sued or where the lawsuits were filed. The group also did not immediately make available a copy of the complaint. Many of the lawsuits are believed to have been filed in areas with large concentrations of high-speed Internet customers. Such connections are required to download the massive movie files. (AP)

Hollinger agrees to sell Jerusalem Post

Hollinger International Inc. said it would sell the Jerusalem Post to an Israeli media company for $13.2 million, marking the second sale of an international newspaper as the company winnows its assets following the ouster of its former chief executive, Conrad Black. Mirkaei Tikshoret Group Ltd. is buying the Post and its related properties and will bring in CanWest Global Communications Corp., the largest newspaper publisher in Canada, as a 50-50 partner. The purchase price represents a significant decline from the $20 million that Hollinger paid to acquire the newspaper in two installments in 1989 and 1990. In 2002, the latest period for which Hollinger has filed an annual report, the Post and its related properties lost about $5.2 million, on revenues of $13.2 million. (AP)
