Former owner to operate WBIX-AM

November 30, 2004

Former owner Alex Langer is poised to resume operations of WBIX-AM this week amid signs the station is about to significantly cut back on its signature business news and information programs. Langer sold WBIX to accused embezzler Bradford Bleidt in 2002 for $10 million, but was still owed $7 million when Bleidt was arrested and charged this month with stealing millions from as many as 140 clients of his investment company. WBIX is now controlled by court-appointed receiver David Vicinanzo, who is attempting to recover money from Bleidt's assets for former clients. Langer had previously offered Vicinanzo help in keeping the station alive. The station is losing around $100,000 a month and Vicinanzo has said the government will not subsidize its operations. Interim operator Christopher Egan will stop funding operations after today. Vicinanzo said more details would be forthcoming today, and people involved in the negotiations indicated programming cutbacks would be disclosed. Langer could not be reached for comment. (Andrew Caffrey)

Biogen Idec operating chief to retire

Biogen Idec Inc. said chief operating officer William R. Rohn will retire effective Jan. 31, and his responsibilities will be divided among the biotechnology company's top management. Rohn is credited with helping to transform the former Idec Pharmaceuticals from a small research firm into a global biotech company after its combination with Biogen. He served as the Cambridge company's president and chief operating officer, then took his current position after the deal closed. Biogen Idec chief executive James C. Mullen, along with other members of the senior management team, are expected to assume most of Rohn's responsibilities starting today. (AP)

FAA urged to update, not replace system

US aviation regulators should scrap plans to install a Raytheon Co. system at four air-traffic centers by 2008 and instead upgrade existing Lockheed Martin Corp. equipment, which takes less than a year, a report said. The need to upgrade control displays at a Denver center is "critical," with workstations locking up almost weekly, said Kenneth Mead, the Department of Transportation's inspector general. FAA officials at Denver, Chicago, Minneapolis, and St. Louis centers worry that component failures block data from reaching controllers' screens, he said. The Federal Aviation Administration "cannot wait three more years" to replace Lockheed equipment at the centers with Raytheon's Standard Terminal Automation Replacement System, or STARS, the report said. Lockheed and Raytheon, two of the largest FAA contractors, are competing to upgrade air-traffic centers that guide planes within 50 miles of US airports. The FAA last April confirmed Raytheon's system will be deployed in 50 of the 162 centers by 2007 at a cost of $1.46 billion. Mead's recommendation would scale back the initial deployment to 47. Lockheed estimated it could upgrade the four centers for about $35 million, compared with $100 million to deploy Raytheon's STARS, Mead said in the report. (Bloomberg)

Integration of grocery units cost $57.5m

Marc Smith, chief executive of Royal Ahold NV's US operations Stop & Shop and Giant Landover, said the integration of the two units has cost $57.5 million and is practically complete. Speaking to reporters in Philadelphia during an analysts' meeting, Smith said a few "post-integration" issues still need to be sorted, but the majority of the plan was completed in August and September. Some 600 jobs were cut while integrating the two units, he said, adding that the annual savings from the integration will reach $62 million beginning in 2005. Stop & Shop has 348 stores and Giant Landover has 198 stores across the Northeast. The chains had combined sales in 2003 of $15.3 billion. Separately, Royal Ahold said it will sell its US supermarket chains Bi-Lo and Brunos to one or more private equity houses or firms before year-end. (Dow Jones/AP)

Sovereign chief financial officer to retire

Sovereign Bancorp Inc. said chief financial officer James D. Hogan will retire in April after helping the northeastern regional bank grow dramatically through a succession of acquisitions and other financial deals. He joined Sovereign in 2001. He will be succeeded by Mark R. McCollom, who is currently chief financial officer of Sovereign Bank and a managing director of strategic planning at the parent company. Hogan's biggest task was the integration of 278 branches acquired after Fleet Financial Group and Bank Boston combined in 1999 and the new entity was required by the Federal Reserve to sell off branches to avoid anticompetition problems. (AP)

Court rules in favor of CEO, chairman

Oracle Corp. chief executive Lawrence Ellison and board chairman Jeff Henley have won a summary decision in a lawsuit that accused them of insider trading in 2001. The decision was issued Wednesday by the same Delaware judge who is presiding over the continuing legal battle in Oracle's attempt to take over rival PeopleSoft Inc. Vice chancellor Leo Strine of Delaware's Court of Chancery found no evidence that Ellison and Henley dumped Oracle stock in advance of sliding results in 2001. Brought by a shareholder on behalf of Oracle to recover alleged insider profits, the case survived an earlier effort to block it when the Delaware Chancery Court found a special committee of the Oracle board lacked independence. The latest ruling, however, concluded there was no evidence showing either Ellison or Henley knew Oracle's profits were headed down when they sold stock in January 2001. (Dow Jones/AP)

Computer makers said to face slowdown

Slower growth and reduced profits will likely squeeze three of the nation's top 10 personal computer manufacturers out of the market by 2007, a research firm predicted. While manufacturers' shipments increased by double digits in the past few years, tougher times lie ahead, according to Gartner Inc. of Stamford, Conn. Gartner did not name any candidates for departure but suggested many are vulnerable. Dell is the only consistently profitable global computer vendor in the past several years, Gartner said. The top 10 worldwide vendors, by units shipped, are Dell, Hewlett-Packard, IBM, Fujitsu Ltd., Fujitsu Siemens Computers, Toshiba, NEC, Apple Computer, Lenovo Group Ltd., and Gateway. Personal computer growth, by unit, is forecast to average 5.7 percent annually from 2006 through 2008, according to Gartner, half the 11.3 percent average of 2003 through 2005. (AP)

Mutual fund assets grew in October

US mutual fund assets grew by 1.2 percent to $7.65 trillion in October, aided by a rise in stock prices, the Investment Company Institute said in a report. Investors placed $14.1 billion in long-term funds, including stock and bond funds, the Washington-based trade group said. Inflows in September totaled $16 billion. For the year, inflows were $169.1 billion, with $146.1 billion going to stock funds. The rise in non-US stocks fueled the increase, as the Morgan Stanley Capital International Europe and Far East index gained 3.4 percent, its best performance since jumping 7.8 percent in December. Stock funds had $7.17 billion in inflows, down from $10.2 billion in September, the ICI said. World equity funds, which invest mostly in non-US stocks, had $4.79 billion in investments, an increase from $4.12 billion in September. US stock funds, in contrast, had $2.38 billion in inflows, down more than 60 percent from $6.1 billion a month earlier. Bond funds took in $3.47 billion in October, an increase from $2.83 billion a month earlier. (Bloomberg)
