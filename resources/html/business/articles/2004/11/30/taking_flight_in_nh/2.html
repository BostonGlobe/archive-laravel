Taking flight in N.H.

November 30, 2004

At the same time, a strong economy and freewheeling business travelers and vacationers fueled growth at Logan. Since all three airports were growing rapidly at the same time, they each continued to carry roughly the same percentage of all passengers that they always had.

But after the terror attacks, Logan and Green lost millions of passengers, while Manchester kept growing. In fact, the total number of passengers going to all three airports dropped by nearly 5 million between 2000 and 2003, according to the airports' data. Manchester's slice grew not only because it attracted more passengers but also because the overall passenger pie shrunk.

The problem for Logan and Green was that most of their service before the attacks was from so-called legacy airlines like US Airways, United Airlines, Delta Air Lines and American Airlines, said Dan Kasper, head of the aviation practice at consulting firm LECG LLC in Cambridge.

Meanwhile, Southwest, which dominated Manchester, continued to add flights and thus lure traffic after the attacks, he said.

"Logan has more of the big boys, and they've been cutting back," Kasper said. "If you look at T.F. Green, there was a lot more legacy traffic there than at Manchester."

Patti Goldstein, a spokeswoman for T.F. Green, conceded the facility lost a lot of business in the past three years because airlines cut back service. Green used to have several carriers that flew to New York, but almost all of that service was cut back after the attacks, and most of it was never restored, she said.

In 2001, flights to New York's LaGuardia Airport from Green dropped to only five from 18, as American Airlines bought now-defunct carrier Business Express and cut service, and Delta cut back service as well.

Today, Continental flies to the New York area from Green via Newark International Airport in Newark, and US Airways still has five daily flights to LaGuardia.

Instead of bolting north to fly from Logan or Manchester, though, it appears many of the former New York passengers have been taking the train instead. Amtrak boarded 239,209 passengers at Providence in fiscal 2003, which ended in September. That's 64 percent more than the 145,864 passengers that boarded trains there in 2000, according to Amtrak. The train trip takes just under three hours and business class seats on Amtrak's Acela Express service start at $166 round trip between Providence and New York.

"There was a point in time when we weren't faring as well as the other airports," said Goldstein. "Last year was a tough year. That's why when you're looking at these numbers, you see us not doing as well."

At Logan, the cost of doing business is higher than at the other two airports, and that made it difficult for some of the ailing airlines to increase their service after the attacks, said Phil Orlandella, an airport spokesman.

For example, Logan charges airlines landing fees of $3.07 per 1,000 pounds of landed weight, including passengers and freight, compared to Manchester,which charges $1.71 per 1,000 pounds, according to both airports.

"It's all about filling their planes and making a profit," Orlandella said. "Then again, you have to justify the cost of going to a smaller airport. You're not going to bring a 747 into a market, and you can't fill it."

Since last year, Logan has shown signs of recovery. Some low-fare airlines started service last year. The new carriers, which include JetBlue Airways, Song and Independence Air, joined low-fare airlines like AirTran Airways, ATA Airlines and America West Airlines in stimulating competition for passengers at Logan.
