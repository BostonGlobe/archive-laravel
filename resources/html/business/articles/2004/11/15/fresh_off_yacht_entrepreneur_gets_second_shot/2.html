Fresh off yacht, entrepreneur gets second shot

November 15, 2004

They paid less than $100,000. Most of the servers and networking gear inside were nearly obsolete, and so they've since spent about $500,000 on new equipment, like switches from Enterasys and routers from Juniper.

Southworth says that when he ran HarvardNet, he made it a "habit" to remain profitable and focus on customer service. "I'm like a corner drugstore type of guy," he says. "This was always run very much as a family business, and we had a lot of pride in it." He hopes to restore those values.

But Web hosting is still a business with incredible oversupply and perpetually plummeting prices. The Charlestown data center lost 90 percent of its customers in the transition from Allegiance back to Southworth. (The business is now called Boston Data Centers.) Its tall black server racks are only about 8 percent occupied these days. And in a way, the hosting business, which provides rack space, power, security, and Internet connectivity for a monthly fee, feels a bit like the hotel business after 9/11. To compete successfully, Boston Data Centers will have to develop a more sophisticated hosting offering. They need to be the Four Seasons, not the Motel 6.

So why did Southworth tie up his yacht, at least temporarily, and return to the rough-and-tumble of entrepreneurial life?

"The price was right, and the subconscious motivation was revenge against the people who screwed it up," Southworth says, referring to the HarvardNet board and the chief executive they brought in, Mark Washburn. (Washburn didn't return my phone calls.) When I jokingly asked Southworth whether he was currently seeking venture capital for Boston Data Centers, he replied, "We're hiding from it. And fortunately, we don't need it."

Peter Claudy was one of the investors at M/C Venture Partners who first put money into HarvardNet in 1998. Buying your old company at a bargain-basement price and trying to rehabilitate it, he speculates, "may be less expensive than therapy."

Segwaying east
This week, you may see a Segway rider in the North End with tired knees and swollen feet. That would be Josh Caldwell, who has been riding a Segway Human Transporter across the country, from Seattle to Boston.

Caldwell and a friend, Hunter Weeks, quit their jobs at a Phoenix software company earlier this year. Like modern-day Kerouacs, they're blogging their trip from coast to coast, and they hope to follow up with a feature-length documentary.

"We're using the Segway to slow down life and be able to capture stories, to capture a sense of America," Caldwell says, from an overnight stop in Toledo, Ohio. "The Segway is a natural icebreaker. People have seen it on TV, but never in real life."

Caldwell spends between six and eight hours a day riding the Segway, though he has to stop every hour to change batteries. Segway LLC, based in New Hampshire, chose not to sponsor Caldwell's journey, but the company did donate four batteries.

Instead, one of the trip's sponsors is Chipotle Mexican Grill, a chain of burrito shops.

"We get all of the burritos we could ever possibly want to eat," Caldwell says.

"I definitely will not be an Olympic athlete after doing this," he adds. "It's definitely not a big cardiovascular event." The website is www.10mph.com.

In other Segway news, the company has rolled out a concept vehicle called the Centaur. It looks like a skeletal version of a four-wheel all-terrain vehicle. (It can balance on two wheels, and maneuver through the woods.) Video at www.segway.com/centaur.
