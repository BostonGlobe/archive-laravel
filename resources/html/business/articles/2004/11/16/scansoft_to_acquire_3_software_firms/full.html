BUSINESS IN BRIEF

ScanSoft to acquire 3 software firms

November 16, 2004

ScanSoft Inc. said it plans three acquisitions. The company will acquire Phonetic Systems Ltd., a provider of automated directory assistance and voice-based programs, for $35 million in cash, and an additional consideration of up to $35 million, based on the achievement of performance targets and the potential vesting of a warrant to buy 750,000 common shares. ART Advanced Recognition Technologies Inc., which develops voice-based and writing-recognition programs for wireless devices, is being acquired for $21.5 million, net of an expected cash balance of $5 million. Phonetic Systems Ltd., which makes programs for converting text to speech, will cost $4.9 million in cash and $1.8 million in ScanSoft stock. Separately, Scansoft said its quarterly loss deepened to $5.9 million, or 6 cents per share, compared to $3.7 million, or 4 cents a share, in the year-ago period. Analysts expected ScanSoft to earn 4 cents per share. (AP)

M-Qube in deal with Warner Music Group unit

M-Qube Inc., a Boston company that provides entertainment content for cellphones, has closed a deal with Warner Music Group to offer more than 1,000 of its artists' songs in formats that can be downloaded as ring tones for wireless handsets. Financial terms were not disclosed. Among artists whose songs m-Qube is making available are Depeche Mode, Bonnie McGee, Goo Goo Dolls, My Chemical Romance, Red Hot Chili Peppers, and Seal. Privately held m-Qube has also set up a ''mobile content store" for Warner's Rhino Records unit and expects to roll out stores for the Atlantic and Warner Brothers labels next month. Users can download the songs to ring when they get a call, with the cost added to their bill with major wireless carriers such as Verizon Wireless, Cingular Wireless, Sprint, and T-Mobile USA. (Peter J. Howe)

N.Y. group protests Banknorth merger

A consumer advocacy group in New York is objecting to Banknorth Group Inc.'s proposed merger with Toronto-Dominion Bank of Canada because it says Banknorth underserves minority and low-income consumers. Inner City Press said it has filed a 15-page protest with the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. Matthew Lee, executive director of ICP, said Banknorth has rejected loan applications from low- and moderate-income customers, blacks, and Latinos at disproportionately high rates. In its protest, Inner City Press is asking for public hearings on the merger. The Federal Reserve board makes the final decision on the transaction. Banknorth spokesman Jeff Nathanson said the bank would not respond to the specifics of the complaint. ''Any issues raised we'll address as part of the regulatory review process," he said. (AP)

StockerYale, CEO face SEC charges

StockerYale Inc. disclosed that it received a Wells notice from the Securities and Exchange Commission, warning the company and chief executive Mark W. Blodgett that they face may civil litigation for alleged securities-law violations. In a regulatory filing, the Salem, N.H., maker of photonics-based products said it plans to ''respond promptly" to the Wells notice and to defend its actions. StockerYale previously disclosed that the SEC had opened a formal investigation related to two company press releases in April and related trading in company securities. A Wells notice gives recipients an opportunity to make a case against civil litigation before the SEC initiates any action. (Dow Jones)

Pension insurer's deficit doubles in year

The government agency that insures the private pensions of 44.4 million workers said that its deficit more than doubled in 2004 to a record $23.3 billion, prompting Republicans and Democrats in Congress to renew calls for action to shore up the system. The Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp. said that as of Sept. 30, it had $39 billion in assets to cover $62.3 billion in pension liabilities. The $23.3 billion deficit was more than twice the $11.2 billion long-term shortfall the agency reported a year ago and reflected continuing troubles in such industries as airlines and steel. The PBGC was created in 1974 as a government insurance program for traditional ''defined benefit" pension plans. Employers pay insurance premiums to the agency, and if an employer can no longer support its pension plan, the agency takes over the assets and liabilities and pays promised benefits, up to certain limits. (AP)

Former Boeing official pleads guilty

Boeing Co.'s former chief financial officer pleaded guilty to illegally hiring a top Air Force procurement officer who admitted giving the company preferential treatment on a $23 billion tanker contract. Michael Sears, 57, pleaded guilty to a single count of aiding and abetting illegal employment negotiations. He faces up to five years in prison when sentenced Jan. 21, but his lawyer estimated that under federal sentencing guidelines Sears most likely faces, at worst, a prison term of up to six months. Sears admitted that he offered Darleen Druyun, 56, a six-figure executive position at Boeing while she was reviewing whether Boeing should get a $23 billion contract to provide new refueling tankers. (AP)

Humanitarian campaign planned

IBM Corp. is set to disclose today that it is joining with a team of foundations, industry groups, and academic institutions to launch the World Community Grid, a global humanitarian campaign that taps unused computing power to research cures for diseases like malaria, tuberculosis, and AIDS. The grid-computing project will seek to harness the computing power of personal computers and unused computer power at companies and nonprofit organizations. Those wishing to volunteer the idle time on their computers will download World Community Grid free software and register at www.worldcommunitygrid.com. (Robert Weisman)

Fannie Mae misses SEC filing deadline

Mortgage giant Fannie Mae missed a regulatory deadline for filing its third-quarter financial results after its independent auditor, KPMG, refused to sign off on the report. The government-sponsored company, recently cited by regulators in the Office of Federal Housing Enterprise Oversight for serious accounting problems and accused of earnings manipulation, notified the Securities and Exchange Commission that it would not file on time. The OFHEO regulators had ordered Fannie Mae to make massive recalculations, and the delay fueled speculation as to whether the company -- which finances one of every five home loans in the United States -- would restate earnings. The company said that if the SEC finds it has improperly accounted for derivatives, the financial instruments it uses to hedge against interest-rate swings, it would show a net loss of $9 billion for the July-September period. (AP)

Cambridge companies Dyax Corp. and Genzyme Corp. said a study showed their codeveloped drug candidate was safe and effective at treating hereditary angioedema, an acute inflammatory disease with no current remedy in the United States. Dyax said 72 percent of patients treated with the compound, called DX-88, reported improvement of symptoms within four hours, as opposed to a 25 percent response rate in a placebo group.
