Regulators block sale of WBIX

SEC aims to ensure station deal reflects fair market value

By Jeffrey Krasner and Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  November 16, 2004

Federal regulators are blocking the planned sale of WBIX-AM and investigating whether prospective buyer Christopher Egan is paying a lowball price for the station as they seek to untangle a multimillion-dollar financial fraud allegedly committed by station owner and financial planner Bradford C. Bleidt.

Regulators from the Securities and Exchange Commission and the state Securities Division sought to discover the extent of what they claim was Bleidt's alleged Ponzi-style scheme yesterday, seizing records at his Portland Street offices and issuing subpoenas to firms the investment adviser used to work with.

More than 80 panicked investors called the office of Secretary of State William F. Galvin claiming to be clients of Allocation Plus Asset Management Co. Inc., leading some officials at Galvin's office to predict the alleged scam could wind up costing victims more than $20 million. ''We've heard from over 80 investors in various degrees of anguish and shock," said Galvin. ''All 80 may not be at risk."

Bleidt tried to kill himself last week after confessing on tape to taking investors' money over a period of 20 years and using some of that money to buy WBIX.

Regulators are focusing on WBIX and the planned sale of the station, which could be Bleidt's sole valuable asset.

''We are being very careful to make sure that fair value was paid for that station," said Walter Ricciardi, district administrator of the SEC's Boston office. ''This guy was desperate for cash, and he might have agreed to accept a price below fair market value."

Neither Egan nor his attorney returned phone calls yesterday. Egan is the son of EMC Corp. co-founder Richard Egan. But Egan went on the air yesterday morning and told WBIX listeners there are no changes to his plan to buy the station from Bleidt this month. Egan has operated the station since August under a lease arrangement, a spokesman for the station said.

''As upsetting as this has been to us on a personal level, we want to assure our listeners that we remain committed to maintaining the station as an important voice in the business community," said Egan in a statement released yesterday.

Bleidt was feted Wednesday at a party celebrating the station's recent move to 24-hour-a-day broadcasting. The following day, he recorded a tape in which he purportedly confessed to stealing tens of millions of dollars from clients in an elaborate scheme he had kept hidden for years, according to a civil complaint the SEC filed in US District Court in Boston Friday. He used some of the ill-gotten proceeds to buy WBIX, he said on the tape, which he called a ''stupid" thing to do, according to court documents. But the scheme collapsed when one client, a Greek Orthodox church, asked for repayment of $1.5 million, according to court documents filed by the SEC.

Bleidt, who rented a house in the upscale town Manchester-by-the-Sea, sent the tape to the Boston office of the SEC via overnight delivery. Friday, the SEC obtained an emergency court order freezing Bleidt's assets. Officials said Bleidt had tried to commit suicide after sending the tape. Regulators said they didn't know where Bleidt is being treated or what his condition is. Nobody responded to a message left at the Bleidt residence.

Galvin said it is unlikely that investors whose money was involved in the alleged scheme will recover the money they are owed.

''We know Bleidt reportedly had $85 million under management," said Galvin in an interview. ''That may have included investments that were properly managed. Our single objective is to make people whole. We're going to check out his assets wherever they lead us. But it's unlikely that the radio station, even if it is recoverable as an asset, could meet the demands from clients that are out there."

According to attorneys involved with the investigation, Bleidt kept two sets of books. Some clients' funds were legitimately invested and properly accounted for. For another group, however, he generated phony monthly statements but deposited their money into a separate bank account.

State and federal regulators are now widening the scope of their investigation to include firms with which Bleidt was affiliated over the years. As an investment adviser, Bleidt took money from clients and placed it in a variety of securities. But for most of his career, Bleidt worked as an independent contractor, executing transactions through registered brokerages and splitting commissions with them. Those firms now face regulatory scrutiny, because they could be held responsible for supervising Bleidt even if he wasn't an employee, according to securities lawyers.

Galvin's office yesterday issued subpoenas to the firm that currently executes trades for Bleidt, Winslow, Evans & Crocker Inc. and to other firms he has been affiliated with, including Fechtor, Detwiler & Co., Commonwealth Financial Network and Phoenix Equity Planning Corp.

Tina Maloney, chief financial officer of Winslow, Evans & Crocker Inc., said she spent much of the day trying to calm investors who may have been defrauded by Bleidt. Officials at Fechtor, Detwiler, Commonwealth and Phoenix could not be reached to comment.
