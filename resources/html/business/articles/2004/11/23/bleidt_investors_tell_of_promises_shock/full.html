Bleidt investors tell of promises, shock

By Andrew Caffrey and Beth Healy, Globe Staff  |  November 23, 2004

On April 18, 1983, Bradford C. Bleidt knelt down inside a meeting hall in the Masonic lodge in Lynn, placed his hands on a Bible, and took a solemn oath not to ''cheat, wrong, nor defraud" fellow Masons, as he was inducted into the fraternal organization.

It was a vow, like so many others, that he would not keep.

Two decades later, when his business empire began to collapse, Bleidt turned to his Masonic brothers to forestall the day of reckoning. Between 1999 and 2001, three of his top lieutenants walked out, wearying of his growing fascination with radio. They took with them clients representing $200 million in assets, or half the company's base, according to associates, putting a deep dent in Bleidt's revenue stream.

He worked Masonic and Odd Fellows lodges on the North Shore, assuring the men whom he socialized with and confided in that he would put their life savings in conservative, safe investments with steady, unspectacular returns.

''There were no promises of greatness," said Mike Tenney, a disabled Swampscott resident who gave Bleidt more than $300,000 -- his life savings -- five years ago and now has nothing to show for it. ''He said, 'I will treat your money as my own, invest it as if it were my own.' "

Instead, authorities say Bleidt tapped the funds to keep his businesses, including radio station WBIX-AM, afloat. He allegedly took money from one client to pay off another, in an elaborate Ponzi scheme that fell apart earlier this month: When St. Demetrios Church in Weston asked Bleidt to return $1.5 million the Greek Orthodox church had invested with him two years earlier, he didn't have the money.

Dorothy Johnson, 74, and her husband, Thomas, 76, had counted on Bleidt to guard their retirement money. From those investments, Bleidt would send them $3,000 a month to cover rent and living expenses, such as Thomas Johnson's treatments for emphysema -- until the checks stopped this month. Dorothy Johnson said she became suspicious of Bleidt when he came to their home in October 2003 to discuss a second round of investing. The couple was unsure how much to invest with Bleidt after the sale of their house. ''I'd really like $170,000," she recalled Bleidt saying.

''I didn't like that," Dorothy Johnson said. She remembers telling her husband, ''Who is he to tell us how much he wants?"

Bleidt, 50, is a West Virginia native with a salesman's charm who now faces federal criminal and civil fraud charges for allegedly running one of the largest pyramid schemes in Massachusetts history. A onetime insurance agent who built a name peddling financial advice to telephone company workers, fraternal organizations, and business owners, Bleidt allegedly robbed his clients of more than $45 million in a trail of deception that fooled his business partners, friends, and regulators.

Bleidt managed to hide his tangled secret for far longer than most modern-day financial thieves, lawyers and regulators said, thanks to an uncanny ability to endear himself to clients and friends. Co-workers call him genial. He was quick with a favor or an ear, and almost never showed a temper. Clients of Bleidt's Allocation Plus Management Co. say they saw him as honest.

''He came across as very confident," said another fellow Mason, S. Raymond King, secretary of the Mount Carmel lodge in Lynn. ''He had a very winning personality. He was easy to talk to. He wasn't patronizing or talking down to you."

Bleidt was a star among the investors he courted. He had appeared on business radio programs for several years. Through the 1990s, he would buy radio spots. From 2001 to 2003, he cohosted the morning show on Business 1060, the station he would buy in 2002, with his wife, Bonnie Kirchner Bleidt.

Bleidt's involvement with radio ultimately proved to be his undoing. It sparked the defection of his three top planners between 1999 and 2001, and with them their clients. By 2001, the market was well into its decline, and Bleidt was working harder to lure business.

His high profile lulled many clients to trust a man who appeared to be investing their money cautiously in mutual funds. After allegedly pocketing customers' money, Bleidt maintained an elaborate system of reporting their holdings, complete with false statements.

''He worked a network of people, people who knew each other," said Massachusetts Secretary of State William F. Galvin, whose office also is investigating Bleidt. ''Like many con men, once he got going, he was able to play one person into another."

Indeed, Bleidt barely had to sell himself once he'd penetrated a close-knit group like the Masons, a secret society and social club. He would hold group seminars on saving and investing, touting phony returns of 10 percent to 20 percent that he had allegedly achieved for past clients.

But on Nov. 11, Bleidt's contorted plan imploded. He sent out taped confessions of his fraud and tried to take his own life, rather than face the shame of a $1.5 million demand from the church that he could not pay.

Bleidt had first been introduced to church officials, by a client, in October 2002. The church had received $2 million in donations and needed advice on how to invest. ''He seemed like a good guy," said Christopher J. Pappas, president of the church's board.

This month, church officials asked Bleidt in a phone conversation for $1.5 million back, and then followed up with a fax. The can-do financial planner's attitude changed instantly. First, Pappas recalled, Bleidt said he hadn't received the fax. Then, he said a fax wasn't acceptable. Then, Bleidt said the transfer would take a little more time. ''We were receiving stall tactics," Pappas said.

On Tuesday, Nov. 9, Bleidt said the transfer ''was in progress."

By Friday, Pappas called Bleidt's office to see where the money was. But it was too late. At 4 p.m. that day, while driving his son to basketball practice, Pappas received a call back from Grover S. Parnell Jr., a lawyer for Bleidt's business. Parnell said the Securities and Exchange Commission had raided the office and taken files. He said there had been a ''serious financial irregularity," and that the church's accounts could be involved, Pappas said.

On one of several confessional tapes Bleidt sent to the SEC, his wife, his mother, and others, Bleidt said he didn't have the money because ''I stole it," according to an affidavit by an FBI agent.

It was a long road to that day of reckoning. Raised in South Charleston, W.Va., the son of a Union Carbide Corp. chemist and a mother who served as a school administrator, Bleidt attended the University of Kentucky until spring 1976 but never graduated, according to the school's records.

He launched his investment career in summer 1982, joining the Boston office of the Phoenix Cos., a Hartford insurance company. By the mid-1980s, Bleidt started a planning business with James S. Wolfson. Bleidt was full of ambition and big ideas, former associates say. Just as things started to go well, in 1991, Bleidt pushed Wolfson out of the business and bought out his share, according to former executives of the company.

To his clients, Bleidt seemed to have all the trappings of success: a downtown office with fine art on the walls, a house in Manchester-by-the-Sea, and an attractive wife, Kirchner Bleidt, 37, a financial reporter on television and a successful financial planner.

Bonnie Bleidt says she knew nothing of her husband's shenanigans. The taped confession her husband sent to her office at WBIX said, according to a person briefed on the contents, ''Bonnie, this will come as a shock to you."

The first obvious sign of Bleidt's financial woes came in June, when he struck a deal to sell the station just two years after buying it for $10 million.

There were other creeping signs of stress. Bleidt could often be found pacing and smoking on the street outside his company's Canal Street office, said people who worked with him. He had given up smoking, as well as drinking, for about two years following the end of his first marriage, in 1997. Last December, he faced a police charge for driving an uninsured car. And he has received five speeding tickets in five years.

Friends and clients say there was no hint of the betrayal to come. ''He took his dog to work with him every single day," said Tenney, the Swampscott Mason. ''A real sleazeball doesn't do that."
