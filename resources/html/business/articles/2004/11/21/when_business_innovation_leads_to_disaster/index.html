BOOK REVIEW

When business innovation leads to disaster

By Joan Axelrod-Contrada, Globe Correspondent  |  November 21, 2004

This book attempts to demolish the notion that ''new is better."

For the most part, it succeeds. Sergio Zyman and coauthor Armin A. Brott build a solid foundation for their argument that innovation can be disastrous for the bottom line. Innovation is like a sugar high, they say -- a quick fix that just doesn't work.

Zyman, a best-selling author as well as chairman of his own consulting firm, and Brott, a business writer, use examples from high-profile companies to illustrate their points. Zyman previously headed the unsuccessful introduction of New Coke as the chief marketing officer of Coca-Cola Co.

Zyman attributes the failure of New Coke, which was pulled after just 27 days, to the company's inability to recognize that Coca-Cola customers value continuity and stability. Coke quickly rectified its errors, replacing innovation with renovation. That, however, is about all Zyman says about New Coke. In the last chapter, Zyman mentions that he'd like to devote an entire book to New Coke.

If that's why Zyman gave the fiasco such short shrift in this book, it's a poor excuse. An in-depth discussion of New Coke might have helped readers understand where renovation leaves off and innovation begins. After all, couldn't New Coke be seen as a renovation rather than an innovation?

The book gets off to a brisk start, however, with its first chapter, ''So What's Wrong With Innovation?" The authors rattle off one colorful example after another to build a foundation for their argument. They point to fast-food giant McDonald's experience in the hotel business as one example of unwise innovation. Customers go to McDonald's to be ''in and out in a hurry," they write. That, they argue, is completely incompatible with what people want from a hotel.

General readers are bound to find such examples more entertaining than the academic-sounding discussions about ''core essence" and ''core competencies" that accompany them. The authors argue that innovation backfires because it leads companies away from what their customers want. In the McDonald's case, the ''core essence" is ''in and out in a hurry."

After building their foundation, the authors start framing their argument. The book is written largely for chief executives and managers, although it might also appeal to business majors and general readers. The charts and diagrams sprinkled throughout the book seem particularly well suited to MBA students.

Such detail gets across the message that renovation is hard work. In one chapter, for instance, the authors make a case for looking beyond demographics to expand a company's customer base. They describe how one newspaper uses readership habits rather than demographics to design promotional efforts to boost circulation.

Some readers' eyes might glaze over when they see diagrams showing ''readership behavioral segments" and ''total demand curve." The diagrams are like those remodeling books that show you exactly how to line up tiles and shelves.

Yet even readers who skim over parts of this book are bound to come away with a new appreciation for how retooling a company is like remodeling a kitchen.
