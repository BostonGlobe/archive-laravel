Local treasure doggedly pursues national plan

By David Segal, Washington Post  |  November 21, 2004

NEW YORK -- The Papaya King at the corner of 86th Street and Third Avenue is basically an indoor hot dog stand, though calling it that is like calling the Louvre a fancy shack with some art inside. The dogs served here -- ''tastier than filet mignon!" according to a registered trademark -- are a greasy, down-market landmark in a city where everyone is forever plotting his next meal.

Legend has it the Beatles stopped by during their 1965 US tour. Julia Child picked the franks as her favorite in a taste test. Bill Cosby is a regular. And there have been countless movie and sitcom cameos here, including the ultimate local cultural touchstone: an episode of ''Seinfeld."

''I don't want a movie hot dog!" whines Kramer, begging to leave the line at a cineplex. ''I want a Papaya King hot dog!"

The special -- $4.19 for two dogs and your choice of papaya, mango, pina colada, coconut champagne, strawberry, or banana daiquiri drink -- routinely shows up on lists of the city's great food bargains. Though the price has inched up over the decades, Papaya King has offered a variation of the same deal, on this very corner, since the Hoover administration.

This local treasure will soon go national, if all goes according to plan. Papaya Kings are supposed to start popping up nationwide in hundreds of malls, airports, and Main Streets in the coming years. This might sound like a fairly conventional goal for some ambitious fast-food entrepreneurs, but here's the thing: No one has ever successfully launched a hot dog franchise that stretches from coast to coast. Nobody has even come close. Not a nearly vanished chain called Dog N Suds, which tried years ago; not Nathan's, which tried more recently; and not Wienerschnitzel, which has 350 stores, mostly in the Southwest, but according to a company spokesman, no plans to head east.

''I'm a marketing professor," says Peter Sealey of the University of California at Berkeley, ''and if a student came to me with an idea for a national fast-food hot dog chain, I'd flunk them. Why has it never worked for anybody else? Why?"

Dan Horan thinks he knows the answer. The chief executive of Papaya King since 1999, Horan was hired to turn this bustling little legend into a powerhouse brand. He thinks his product is tasty enough and cheap enough to sell anywhere, and he's bet five years of his life on that hunch.

There's the trick of exporting an institution as quirky as Papaya King. When you look at the particulars -- the menu, the staff -- it doesn't look like the sort of operation you can order from a kit.

''This place is mecca," Horan said recently, leaning against the Papaya King counter and gesturing around his fiefdom of 450 square feet. ''There's only one mecca. The question: How do you take what's great here and replicate it?"

The Papaya King story started in 1932, soon after a Greek immigrant and deli owner named Gus Poulos came across papaya and mango concoctions on vacations in Florida and Cuba. When he couldn't find anything like them in Manhattan, he closed his deli and started selling juice.

The neighborhood was then composed largely of German and Eastern European immigrants, so hot dogs were added to the menu of Hawaiian Tropical Drinks in 1937, giving rise to what's got to be one of the strangest food-and-drink combinations in the history of takeout. Poulos changed his company's name after a customer dubbed him ''Papaya King."

Nearly everyone in this place, it turns out, has worked here for more than 10 years, and some for more than 20. It's a good perch for a view of New York life at its weirdest, and there's never a shortage of celebrity sightings. Rodney Dangerfield used to come by in a limo, then send his driver out to pick up a couple of dogs, waving from the back seat. Woody Allen filmed a scene for ''Manhattan" here, but the scene was cut.

In 1997, almost a decade after Gus Poulos had passed away, a group of investors bought Papaya King and began hunting for an executive with the energy and expertise to expand in every direction. They hired Horan, 38, who has an MBA from Yale.

There are two stores today -- the other is in Harlem -- and a lease was recently signed to open a third near the West Village. By June, Horan says, he expects to open a store every month, and within five years he thinks the count will stand at about 500. Double that in 10 years.

''We're Mighty Mouse," Horan says. ''We have great bang for the buck. We have a very focused menu and it's inexpensive."

It's also wickedly good. Papaya King dogs are thin, have a slight hint of smoky garlic, and are browned enough to pop a little when you bite. The buns are toasted, the onions grilled and fresh. In the most recent New York edition of Zagat's guide to restaurants a diner declared them ''the best dogs in the free world."

If you look only at the numbers, you'd assume hot dog chains are scattered all over the country. About 20 billion were sold domestically last year, according to the National Hot Dog and Sausage Council -- about 57 million dogs per day. If you divide total consumption by the total population, every man, woman, and child eats 68 hot dogs a year.

Of course, it's not that simple. Everyone in the food business has a theory about why every attempt at franchising these numbers into a fortune has failed. Some say dogs are a regional dish that don't lend themselves to a national market. Sealey, the Berkeley professor, cites a potentially more serious problem.

''The more difficult a food is to prepare, the better the franchise you've got," he said. ''You can make a pizza at home, but it's an undertaking. You can make a burger at home but not a Big Mac, and very few people make fries. Fried chicken? It's difficult. The trouble with hot dogs is that you can make them at home and they taste just as good as what you buy in a restaurant. That's why it's never been a successful franchise."

Horan doesn't buy it.

''Look at Starbucks," he said. ''They sell coffee. Anyone can make coffee at home, but Starbucks is one of the most successful companies of the last six years. Why do people buy beer in a bar?"
