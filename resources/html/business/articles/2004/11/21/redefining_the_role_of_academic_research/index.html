BUSINESS INTELLIGENCE

Redefining the role of academic research

By Robert Weisman, Globe Staff  |  November 21, 2004

Charles M. Vest, who will be stepping down next month as the president of MIT, personifies his school's famed ''engineering mentality." Simply put, this is the inclination to find a problem and fix it.

Vest, 63, had no shortage of problem-solving opportunities in his 14 years at the helm of one of the nation's top research universities. Bookending his MIT tenure were principled stands that put him at odds with the US government. In the early 1990s, Vest successfully fought a Justice Department complaint that would have constrained MIT's ability to award financial aid solely on the basis of need. And, in the post-Sept. 11 era, he has argued for retaining openness in research. He also served on the President's Council of Advisors on Science and Technology in Republican and Democratic administrations.

But mostly Vest wrestled with the problem of redefining the role of the research university at a time when the federal government is scaling back funding for academic research and much of the private sector is moving toward short-term product development. These trends have shifted the responsibility for long-term basic research onto the shoulders of such educational institutions as MIT. At stake is nothing less than preserving America's technological lead in a global market.

''We could lose it," Vest warned in an interview. ''I don't think that we have, nor do I think we're about to. But as the rest of the world evolves, science and technology are universal undertakings. And people in China and India and Vietnam are just as smart as we are. I hope people in the United States don't lose the urge to excel. We've got to keep inventing and keep innovating. We've got to drive the new things."

Vest explores these issues in a new book, ''Pursuing the Endless Frontier," a collection of essays on MIT and the role of research universities. His prescriptions for universities range from taking a more international outlook to rebuilding public trust in technology to fostering partnerships with business and government. In one essay, Vest posits that what we don't know remains more important than what we do.

''The ultimate rationale for supporting a university system derives more from the unknown than the known," Vest says in the essay. ''It is the romance of discovery that draws young people to study and to pursue careers in science. It is the dream of creating entirely new devices, materials, and techniques that drives engineers."

Vest will begin an eight-month sabbatical next month, though he will maintain an office in the Ray and Maria Stata Center, one of many new buildings that architecturally transformed the MIT campus during his tenure. He also will lecture at the University of California, the California Institute of Technology, and the University of Michigan. And he'll serve on the Commission on the Intelligence Capabilities of the United States Regarding Weapons of Mass Destruction, to which he was named by President Bush.

Surveying the changing technology research landscape, Vest said the end of the Cold War reduced the federal government's appetite for underwriting academic research projects at the very moment US corporations were absorbed with reinventing themselves for the new global economy. ''That's left us, the universities, as pretty much the only game in town when it comes to fundamental research," Vest observed.

From his MIT post, Vest also led a move to align engineering curricula with the issues and trends companies face in the real world of business. In a sense, he said, this marked a return to the more pragmatic philosophy of American engineering schools from an earlier era. ''We'd just gotten too far away from our industrial roots," Vest said.

The shift in focus has coincided with a change in student perceptions, Vest said. ''If you go back 20 years at universities, almost no one was interested in manufacturing and engineering," he said. ''Now these are hot, exciting topics among our students."
