European-style conditioning motivates fitness clients

Ukrainians set training pace

By Davis Bushnell, Globe Correspondent  |  November 18, 2004

CONCORD -- As an elementary school student in Ukraine, Yury Klimovitsky was considered, he recalls, a pudgy nonathlete who would never make the grade on the playing fields. But, through perseverance, he would fool them all, becoming a sprinter for that country's junior track-and-field team and later, from 1992 to 1997, head track coach for the Ukrainian Regional Olympic Training School.

Since the spring of 2003, Klimovitsky, 43, has been the president-owner-founder of Personal Training International, based at Damonmill Square, off Route 62 in West Concord.

He now has more than 160 clients, up from 35 initially, who range from teenagers to those in their 70s and older. Fees vary, depending on the package selected. Twenty private sessions with a senior trainer, for example, cost $1,320. Seniors, or those 65 years of age and older, receive a 10 percent discount.

In July of this year, Klimovitsky was joined by a fellow Ukrainian, Alexander Ponomarenko, who has similar track-and-field credentials. Ponomarenko, 47, a senior trainer and director of a training program for young, promising track-and-field specialists, previously had been the top track coach at the regional Olympic training facility in Ukraine.

"We bring to our clients European-style conditioning, starting with general conditioning regimens and going on to sports-specific training," Klimovitsky said, noting that his clients are actively involved in a variety of sports, including running, cycling, tennis, and hockey.

Unlike many conventional fitness centers, his studio, Klimovitsky asserted, is goal-oriented. "We look at conditioning as building a pyramid, with enhanced performance being the top pyramid."

And always working toward a goal is a beneficial approach to staying in shape, said Mary Beth Orfao, 47, of Concord. "I've used personal trainers in the past who had everyone do the same routine things. But Yury tailors exercises to the individual. For example, he has helped me condition my small muscles, and that has made me a more limber tennis player."

Orfao met Klimovitsky four years ago when he was a personal trainer at The Thoreau Club, also in West Concord. In 1997, he emigrated to the Boston area with his wife and two daughters. They became citizens in the fall of 2002 and now live in Sudbury.

Klimovitsky has been on the run from the start. "At one time, I had three part-time jobs, including delivering pizza," he said. He now works six days a week developing his business, which, he said, is outgrowing its space in the basement of an old, renovated mill building. "We're looking for 4,000 square feet of space that we could move into next spring or summer."

Rhonda Phalen, 58, of Sudbury, who was Klimovitsky's first client at The Thoreau Club, said, "You have to admire Yury for what he's been able to accomplish in the last seven years. He arrived from the Ukraine without anything except his knowledge of physiology and his experience with Olympic athletes. He learned English by working with his clients."

Ponomarenko, who now lives in West Concord with his wife and their two teenage children, is also boning up on English and American culture by putting young athletes and older clients through their paces. He and his family have three-year visas, which were granted due to the efforts of Klimovitsky and some clients.

Said Klimovitsky: "I worked for 7 years on getting Alex here, not only because he is a longtime friend, but also because of his unique coaching experiences and his ability to condition anyone for any sport."

Ponomarenko said that, although it takes time to adapt to new surroundings, clients are helping him make the transition faster. "They have accepted me, and that's very good," he added.

Right now, he's training seven track-and-field specialists from Concord-Carlisle and Lincoln-Sudbury Regional High Schools and 10 women triathletes from the area.

Fiona Romeri, 16, a junior at Lincoln-Sudbury, said she's learning advanced running techniques along with "ways to prevent injuries," and is being shown how to increase her strength. Running techniques, she said, are critical for her specialty, pole-vaulting.

Anna McFarlane, 18, who is a three-time medal winner at the US High School Nationals in pole-vaulting and cleared 13 feet in 2003, when she was a junior at Concord-Carlisle, worked out two summers ago with Ponomarenko in Ukraine. "Alex is the most amazing trainer," McFarlane said, " because he comes up with creative exercises for improving your performance. I wish I could take him wherever I go." She is now a freshman at Stanford University.

McFarlane has also worked out with Klimovitsky, as has Lindsay Donaldson, 18, of Lincoln, who last fall was the state's interscholastic cross-country champion as a senior at Lincoln-Sudbury. Learning running strategies and how to hone "my competitive drive" kept her ahead of the pack, Donaldson said. Last month, she enrolled at Yale University.

Klimovitsky was the cross-country coach and assistant track coach at Lincoln-Sudbury from 1998 to 2003.

Older clients also say they have gained strength and agility under Klimovitsky's and Ponomarenko's tutelage. One of the oldest, at 77, is Wendell Sykes of Carlisle.

"When I met Yury seven years ago, he hadn't trained the elderly," said Sykes, a scientific consultant to the federal government. "But we've both found that the training techniques for young athletes are applicable to older people, too."

Through various exercises, his arm strength has improved greatly and his heart rate "has been significantly lowered," Sykes said. His wife, Betsey, a 66-year-old psychologist, has benefited similarly from her workouts, he said.

Sykes, an avid tennis player, said he's also taking advantage of Ponomarenko's sports wisdom. "Alex can analyze any sport and prescribe improvement exercises." For him, that has included tossing tennis balls back and forth to enhance hand-eye coordination, he said.

"In short, I'm making the most important investment I can in my physical and mental health," Sykes said.

Klimovitsky said, "This is a business, certainly, with all of the challenges of any small firm. But it is also about building lasting relationships with clients."
