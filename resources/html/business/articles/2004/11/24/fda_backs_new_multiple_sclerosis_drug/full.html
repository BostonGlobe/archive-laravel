BUSINESS IN BRIEF

FDA backs new multiple sclerosis drug

November 24, 2004

THE REGION

The Food and Drug Administration strongly endorsed Biogen Idec Inc.'s new multiple sclerosis treatment, putting the Cambridge firm in position to expand the market for MS drugs and win greater market share. The drug, formerly called Antegren but renamed Tysabri, was developed with Elan Pharmaceuticals of Ireland. The FDA approved Tysabri for use on its own and in combination with Avonex, an MS treatment Biogen Idec already sells. Eric Schmidt of SG Cowen predicted annual sales could reach $3 billion. The drug is also being developed to treat Crohn's disease and rheumatoid arthritis. (Jeffrey Krasner)

Stent maker wins exclusive use of drug

Boston Scientific Corp., the world's largest maker of stents, obtained exclusive rights to use Angiotech Pharmaceuticals Inc.'s drug paclitaxel in its Taxus devices. Boston Scientific, which already pays royalties on the drug, will pay an additional 1 percent to Angiotech and $14 million for the right to ''sub-license" paclitaxel to third parties. The agreement replaces one in which Natick-based Boston Scientific had to share the rights to Angiotech's compound with Cook Group Inc., which stopped developing its drug-coated stent. The tiny mesh tubes are inserted into arteries after they are cleared. They are more effective at preventing new tissue growth when coated with drugs. US Taxus sales have surpassed $1 billion since the device was approved in March. Angiotech reported third-quarter Taxus-related royalties of $40.4 million. The agreement will boost its Taxus royalty revenue by about 14 percent, Angiotech said. (Bloomberg)

Finucane to run bank's foundation

Bank of America Corp. yesterday appointed Anne Finucane as head of its foundation and public policy group. She will oversee US philanthropic and lobbying efforts and continue in her role as president for the Northeast. She is one of a handful of former FleetBoston Financial Corp. executives to secure new roles in Bank of America after the banks merged. Two top Fleet executives, Bradford Warner and Eugene M. McQuade, left earlier this year. Finucane and another top Fleet executive, Brian Moynihan, retain top roles in the combined bank, and both are adding responsibilities. Chad Gifford, Fleet's former chief executive, is Bank of America's chairman. (Sasha Talcott)

Oil sellers to honor fixed-price contracts

Two North Shore heating oil dealers will honor fixed-price contracts and pay $10,000 as part of an agreement with Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly. In August, Peter and Anita Davekos, owners of Astrofuel and two other heating oil businesses in Swampscott and Marblehead, allegedly offered fixed-price contracts for the winter that ranged from $1.09 to $1.29 a gallon. In late September, however, they allegedly reneged on the deals. The average price of heating oil in Massachusetts has hovered around $2 a gallon recently. (Bruce Mohl)

Bain acquires discounter Dollarama

Bain Capital LLC, a Boston buyout firm with more than $20 billion in assets, acquired Montreal discount retailer Dollarama. Terms were not released. Lawrence Rossy, Dollarama's chief executive, said he sold closely held Dollarama to Bain last week. He declined to comment further and referred all calls to Bain. Daniel Peels, a spokesman for Bain, did not return calls for comment. The Daily Deal reported an $885 million purchase price, citing unnamed sources. The purchase was at least the fifth for Bain this year. According to the report, Dollarama, which specializes in items that sell for a dollar, turns a profit of more than $84 million. Rossy will remain an investor in Dollarama and run the company for Bain. Dollarama has about 360 stores in Quebec. (Bloomberg)

AIG hopes to settle fraud allegations

American International Group Inc., one of the biggest US insurers, has a tentative agreement with federal authorities to settle allegations it helped two companies commit accounting fraud. AIG said Securities and Exchange Commission lawyers agreed to recommend a settlement offer to the five SEC commissioners. The company also reached an agreement in principle with the Justice Department. Terms were not specified. The SEC was considering suing AIG over several 2001 transactions with PNC Financial Services Group Inc. The Justice Department was weighing criminal prosecutions in the PNC matter and one involving cellphone distributor Brightpoint Inc. AIG settled the Brightpoint case with the SEC in September 2003, agreeing to pay $10 million to resolve allegations it helped the company hide losses. AIG is also among several big insurers named in a suit by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer against insurance brokerage Marsh & McLennan Cos., which he accuses of bid-rigging, price-fixing, and taking payoffs. (AP)

Regulators probing gifts to fund advisers

The Securities and Exchange Commission and the National Association of Securities Dealers are examining whether brokerages lavished golf outings, Super Bowl tickets, and other gifts on mutual fund advisers to win business. The inquiry aims to determine whether the practice -- which could harm investors' interest -- is widespread. ''The concern is whether the lavish gifts may have trumped the best interests of investors," SEC spokesman John Nester said. NASD rules prohibit gifts of more than $100, and fund advisers must disclose potential conflicts. (AP)

6m kept numbers, switched phone firms

One year after the government required wireless phone companies to let subscribers keep their phone numbers when switching carriers, just 6 million Americans have done so, according to the industry's main trade group. The 3.5 percent of subscribers taking advantage of ''wireless number portability" is far short of some analyst projections that up to one-quarter of the 170 million US wireless subscribers would change providers. The Cellular Telecommunications & Internet Association said 500,000 people have switched a landline phone number to a cellphone, another innovation made possible by new rules. (Peter J. Howe)

Bank of America reorganizes division

Bank of America Corp. reorganized its debt and equities businesses in its New York-based securities division. Chief executive Kenneth D. Lewis in October approved a plan to spend $600 million expanding the corporate and investment bank in the United States, Europe, and Asia. Al de Molina, who heads the division, will start adding sales people, traders, and investment bankers next year. Pat Augustine, head of global structured products, will be in charge of equity structured products and prime brokerage. Peter Forlenza was named head of equities and Tom White head of global credit products. Karin Hirtler-Garvey will be chief operating officer for global markets. (Bloomberg)

Oil futures soar, then retreat

Crude oil futures briefly surpassed $50 a barrel before retreating sharply. Light, sweet crude for January delivery soared as high as $50.25 per barrel before settling at $48.94, an increase of 30 cents, in volatile trading in New York. In London, Brent crude futures rose 7 cents to $44.45. Heating oil for December delivery traded as high as $1.483 per gallon but settled at $1.445. Tom Bentz, of BNP Paribas Commodity Futures, said the early move higher could be due to a number of factors, especially worry about winter fuel supplies. (AP)
