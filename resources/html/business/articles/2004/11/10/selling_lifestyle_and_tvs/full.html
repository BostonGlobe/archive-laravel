Selling lifestyle and TVs

Tweeter turns focus to services to stand out among its rivals

By Naomi Aoki, Globe Staff  |  November 10, 2004

Tweeter is caught in the Wal-Mart pickle.

ADVERTISEMENT

Known for top-of-the-line electronics and custom installations, the Canton retailer has been squeezed ever since Wal-Mart began selling televisions en masse.

That's because rivals like Best Buy and Circuit City began offering more support and installation services to set themselves apart from Wal-Mart's "Always Low Prices." With those chains encroaching on Tweeter's sweet spot, where was Tweeter to go?

"We're going to change the way discerning people buy consumer electronics," said Mark Richardson, Tweeter Home Entertainment Group Inc.'s chief brand officer.

Today, Tweeter is expected to unveil its vision for selling consumer electronics beginning with a new advertising campaign and a concept store. Forget shelves lined with TVs of all brands and sizes, forget Sunday circulars that do nothing but tout discounts to undercut rivals and lure customers, forget the chaos that is the world of consumer electronics today. The new Tweeter is about experience, possibility, and lifestyle. It is architects, designers and consultants.

"Strategically, Tweeter is moving in the right direction," said Darrell Rigby, head of Bain & Co.'s global retail practice. "The move into services has its risks, but staying where they were is certain death."

Televisions are the new battleground of the consumer electronics industry. Prices are dropping. The Consumer Electronics Association forecasts that the wholesale cost of a digital TV will fall to $1,134 in 2006, down 33 percent from 2002. That's bringing these high-end TVs, some which retail for thousands of dollars, into the price range of an increasing number of consumers. And according to a federal mandate, networks must convert all of their broadcasts to high-definition by 2006, a move prompting many with an analog set to switch over to digital TV.

But with 175 stores nationwide, Tweeter can't compete on price in a world where everyone from wholesale clubs like Costco Wholesale Corp. to mass retailers like Wal-Mart Stores Inc. sell flat-screen plasma and liquid crystal display TVs. So after spending 18 months and half a million dollars researching its customers, Tweeter decided to do more of what it already does better than its mass-market rivals: serve its customers.

That means everything from making sure a customer goes home with the right cables for a new TV set to installing an entire sound and home theater system. Instead of displaying an array of TVs and speakers, a new Tweeter concept store opening near Las Vegas in January features a design center where shoppers can create a custom system with the help of "entertainment architects." There will even be a concierge to greet shoppers at the entrance.

"What Tweeter is doing is taking the other road," said Christian Davies, creative director of FRCH Design Worldwide, the Cincinnati firm that designed Tweeter's concept store.

No road is without its perils, however. On average, selling services is more profitable than selling products. But the track record of other retailers that began offering services leaves much to be desired, said Bain's Rigby. In direct contrast to the opportunity, a Bain study found, the retailers' sales and profits fell below those of their product-only rivals. Retailers often underestimate the cost of the infrastructure need to offer quality service in a prompt manner.

As it is, Rigby said, Tweeter's installation force doesn't turn a profit for the company. That's in spite of the fact that the force is so busy that consumers sometimes have to wait as much as three weeks to have a system installed. If that portion of the business doesn't turn a profit this year -- as Tweeter has predicted, Bain said -- it doesn't seem to bode well for the future.

"Right now, Tweeter needs traffic," Bain said. "And this service strategy better help them to build it or it will be very costly."

In print and television ads that debut this week, Tweeter shows people emerging from the confusion of the consumer electronics world to enjoy some peace. Instead of messing with cables and wires, a family is gathering for movie night or a woman is cooking what appears to be a gourmet dinner.

Radio ads boast the campaign's tagline, "We Can Untangle Your Mind," while the song "Alright Now" plays in the background. One spot promotes Tweeter's automatic price protection program, sending checks to anyone who bought a product a rival advertises in the newspaper for a lower price within 30 days of the purchase.

The concept store in Summerlin, Nev., and another in Henderson, Nev., that opens this spring will put the new strategy to the retail test. A series of living spaces take up nearly one-third of the store's selling floor. Though not separated by walls, rooms have just enough furniture to identify them as the living room, bedroom, or kitchen. They're equipped with state-of-the-art sound and home theater systems so shoppers can see and hear what's possible.

Staffed with "entertainment architects," a design center takes up another large chunk of the store. There, standing at tall tables with pull-out drawers (the kind you might find at a real architect's office), Tweeter employees will help customers design a system that fits their needs without crimping their home's style.

The store's design is more about fashion, simplicity, and experience than products. In-store signage is more likely to speak to lifestyle than sale prices. A sign in the bedroom might read: Forget Sleep, for example. One in the at-home sports bar might say: It's more than a game. Instead of the bright reds and blues of sports uniforms, the colors used in the concept store and the advertising are warmer hues of greens, yellows, oranges, and blues.

"People have in their mind's eye an image of what would be great in their house -- a place to watch movies, a family gathering spot," said Gib Trub, a principal at Partners + Simons, the Boston agency that created Tweeter's ad campaign. "That's how people think about this. They're not just thinking about a TV or another component. If you realize that, you approach this a little differently."
