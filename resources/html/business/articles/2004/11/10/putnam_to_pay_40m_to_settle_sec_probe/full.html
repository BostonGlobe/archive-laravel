Putnam to pay $40m to settle SEC probe

Parent firm Marsh plans to lay off 3,000

By Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  November 10, 2004

Putnam Investments will pay $40 million to settle yet another regulatory probe, while corporate parent Marsh & McLennan Cos. yesterday said it would cut 3,000 jobs in the fallout from the price-fixing and bid-rigging scandal enveloping its insurance brokerage unit.

Putnam's $40 million "agreement in principle" with the Securities and Exchange Commission relates to the firm's failure to disclose it directed trades to investment firms that sold its mutual funds, a practice authorities said creates a potential conflict of interest that could exclude investors from the best prices for trades.

Putnam has since ended the use of "directed brokerage" as it's known, and the proposed settlement covers practices prior to 2004.

The SEC has been investigating cozy business arrangements and sales practices among mutual funds and brokerage firms, and this is the third such case it has brought this year alone. In March, MFS Investment Management agreed to pay $50 million, and in September PA Fund Management and two related firms agreed to pay $11.6 million in related cases.

Neither Putnam nor the SEC would comment in detail about the proposed settlement.

And Putnam's legal problems aren't over. Yesterday the firm said the SEC notified it in September that Putnam and its employees "may have violated" federal law related to two separate incidents. One involved a bungled investment transaction in 2001 that cost a corporate client $1.5 million, in which efforts to fix the problem also went awry. The second matter involved whether Putnam improperly used fund fees to pay for a consultant who provided information services work.

Putnam spokeswoman Nancy Fisher said the company discovered the incidents under investigation and "appropriate action was taken," including firing employees involved. Fisher said Putnam was "cooperating fully" with regulators.

The Marsh & McLennan job cuts will come from the company's offices worldwide, and most of the positions will be in the firm's Marsh Inc. brokerage operation. Company officials were unable to say if any positions at Marsh's Boston office would be eliminated.

Putnam's current share of those layoffs is around 70 employees, who were let go three weeks ago as part of the merger of its retirement-plan servicing unit with the human resources outsourcing arm of its sister company, Mercer Inc.

The layoffs will save $400 million annually and are one of the many steps the reeling Marsh & McLennan is taking to recover from New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer's charges three weeks ago that its brokerage unit received payoffs from insurance companies for steering clients to them instead of finding the best prices for customers.

Marsh & McLennan has also said it would no longer collect the type of "contingency commissions" from insurance companies that are at the heart of Spitzer's investigation, and will insist insurance companies disclose all commission rates on policies to customers.

The firm yesterday reported that third-quarter earnings fell a whopping 94 percent from the prior year, in part because funds have been put aside for legal settlements and some commission revenue wasn't collected because of the Spitzer probe.

Marsh & McLennan's stock price has lost more than 40 percent of its value since Spitzer's charges Oct. 14, and several high-profile executives are out of jobs, most notably chief executive Jeffrey W. Greenberg.

He resigned two weeks ago to clear the way for settlement talks with Spitzer, who had previously said he would not negotiate with company management. After Greenberg's departure, Spitzer said Marsh & McLennan would not face criminal charges, but that the company may be required to pay "far" more than $500 million to settle charges with his office.

The company also replaced its top executives at Marsh Inc. Yesterday Marsh & McLennan's new chief executive, Michael G. Cherkasky, said in a conference call that "freedom from criminal culpability is not up to our standard for executive leadership."

While Putnam itself has yet to lose any investment contracts because of Marsh & McLennan's troubles, the scandal is a new black cloud over a firm that was just beginning to recover from the disclosures of employee trading abuses last year that resulted in Putnam settling federal civil fraud charges.

In the conference call yesterday, Putnam chief executive Charles "Ed" Haldeman Jr. said he "suspects we will have a little bit of a slowdown" in winning new business from institutional investors, such as pension funds. "The person with the least negatives wins. It may be a little easier" for an institutional investor "to eliminate us in a three-person contest" for its business, he said.

Putnam continued to see investment money wash out of the door, although there are now signs that the outflow is ebbing. Assets under management at Putnam dropped by $6 billion in the third quarter ending Sept. 30, to $209 billion. By at the end of October, assets were still at $209 billion. Putnam had $270 billion under management at the end of the third quarter of 2003.
