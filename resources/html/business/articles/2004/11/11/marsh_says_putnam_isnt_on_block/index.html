Marsh says Putnam isn't on block

Officials move to quell speculation about fund unit

By Beth Healyand Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  November 11, 2004

Officials of embattled Marsh & McLennan Cos. yesterday moved to squelch speculation that its Boston money management firm, Putnam Investments, will be divorced from its corporate parent, either in a sale to an outside buyer or through a private buyout engineered by Putnam executives.

Marsh & McLennan chief executive Michael G. Cherkasky said in a conference call with employees that he ''emphatically" had ''no intention of selling any one of our three pieces of business," which also include the company's Marsh Inc. brokerage unit and a large consulting arm, said executives who listened in on the teleconference.

Meanwhile, Putnam chief executive Charles ''Ed" Haldeman Jr. in an interview yesterday denied that his executive team was interested in pursuing a buyout of the firm from Marsh & McLennan.

''There are no discussions going on with anybody, by senior management, about any change in ownership," Haldeman said. ''We are part of the MMC companies and they have been loyal to us when we had a difficult period, and we're very happy where we are."

A possible sale of Putnam has been raised by analysts and others in financial circles since Marsh & McLennan was hit with bid-rigging and price-fixing charges by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer in mid-October. Spitzer's charges have resulted in Marsh & McLennan's stock losing more than 41 percent of its value. Earlier this week, the company said it would lay off 3,000 employees because of the effects of the legal case and the elimination of millions of dollars in revenue the company formerly received in payouts from insurers.

Some high-level managers at Putnam are in the early stages of exploring whether they could buy the company back from Marsh & McLennan, said two Boston investment executives who are aware of the discussions.

But such a deal would be pricey -- in the $4 billion to $6 billion range, the executives said -- rivaling the largest leveraged buyouts of investment firms. And Marsh & McLennan could well refuse to let such a deal happen.

On the phone call, placed during a visit to Putnam's Boston headquarters, Cherkasky said Marsh & McLennan has deep enough pockets to cover the penalties it faces from regulators, and that he believes it's in the investors' best interest to keep the company together.

Moreover, he said Marsh & McLennan could not fetch top dollar for Putnam at a time when the parent company is in crisis.

Mark Lane, an analyst at William Blair & Co. in Chicago, agreed that this would be a bad time to consider selling Putnam.

''The only reason they would do it in the near term is if you need the money, and that's not the case," Lane said.

But inside Putnam and among some of its large customers, there is a growing sense that the investment firm could thrive independently, particularly given its owner's troubles. The scandal comes as Putnam is working in earnest to repair its own reputation, after last year's market-timing charges against several investment managers.

Lane, the analyst, said, ''I don't think it makes sense for Putnam to aggressively seek a split from Marsh, if Marsh doesn't want to split, because it doesn't help the stability of the organization or the view from clients."

Still, investment bankers won't rule a potential spinoff of Putnam, especially with Marsh & McLennan facing at least $500 million in penalties from regulators. Putnam, with $209 billion under management, would be a prize for a number of financial firms that are looking to build their presence as stock-fund managers. The list of potential acquirers includes Bank of America Corp., Mellon Financial Corp., Alliance Capital Management Holdings, and BlackRock Inc., investment executives said.

Private-equity executives say they are touching base with Marsh & McLennan executives as well as Putnam executives. It's too soon to tell whether any transaction will take place, they said.

But the picture will become clearer once the investigation of Marsh & McLennan is complete. Cherkasky yesterday said he expects the bulk of the probe to be completed in the next 10 days.
