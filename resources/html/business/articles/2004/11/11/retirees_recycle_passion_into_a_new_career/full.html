Retirees recycle passion into a new career

Couple lead tours on mountain bikes

By Susan Klein, Globe Correspondent  |  November 11, 2004

It's a fall weekday, and retired Bedford residents Bob and Lee Evans are hard at work in their new career: leading mud-splattered, middle-aged suburbanites on mountain bikes over a root-laced trail through Chelmsford's Cranberry Bog Reservation.

As proprietors of Get Up'N Go Adventures, the couple leads eight short bike tours in Boston's northern and western suburbs each week. The two-hour weekday rides cover 10 to 22 miles, depending upon class level, of back roads and woodland trails.

The Evanses, both 65, have been leading the tours since 2001, after Lee retired from the Winchester Recreation Department, and Bob, a nuclear engineer, retired from a career in the defense contracting industry.

The couple caters to, and is a model for, baby boomers about to enter retirement, according to Peggy Hothem, professor of recreation and leisure studies at Gordon College in Wenham.

Better educated and healthier than previous generations, the upcoming crop of retirees will seek meaningful activities in their remaining decades, Hothem predicted. Many, like the Evanses, will turn avocations into enterprises that supplement retirement income.

"People will start with what they love doing," Hothem said. "They'll be involved on a volunteer basis. Maybe they'll establish an informal group. Then they'll see there's a specific target market that they can go after, that they can charge for their services."

Options like Get Up'N Go outings help retirees make the transition from work to retirement, Hothem added.

"They are now seeing the potential of free time. If work has been the central meaning of their lives, they're going to approach that free time with fear. This provides a very healthy outlet for people to get involved" and make social contacts, she said.

Bob began commuting by bike in the early 1970s to avoid gasoline lines and later took up racing. He has also spent 20 years scouting and leading mountain bike rides for the Northeast Bicycle Club.

Lee took up cycling in her 50s -- after the couple's four children were grown -- to spend more time with Bob. She has since racked up a dozen national and two world titles in master women's road racing events.

The Evanses are zealous about sharing their passion for pedaling.

"Each new person is a kind of a treasure, whose life we may be able to impact in some fashion," said Bob.

Recreation departments in towns from Burlington to Wellesley have offered the Evanses' cycling classes in six- or eight-week sessions. The classes generally fill fast -- mostly with mothers with school-age children, professionals with flexible work schedules, and adventuresome retirees.

Enrollees range from people who haven't been on a bike in years to fit mountain bikers seeking new routes.

Wellesley Recreation Department director Jan Kaseta summed up the reasons for the program's popularity: "It's nearby. You typically don't have to travel far to do it. You get to know the community. You don't have to be a hard-core cyclist. It fits into people's time frame."

Class fees average $10 to $13 per ride, depending on recreation department pricing policies. Enrollment is capped at 18 per class. The Evanses are compensated on a per-rider basis.

David Singer, 74, a retired Lexington physician who still teaches at Harvard Medical School, is one of those riders making the transition from work to leisure. He has ridden with the Evanses for three seasons.

"I was giving my life for my work. There wasn't a minute to exercise. One of the really important things in organizing my new time was finding time to exercise," Singer said.

"I've lived in Lexington 40 years. I really thought I knew all the highways and byways. They know all these places I never knew existed, places that are absolutely beautiful," he added.

The Evanses estimate they each put in three to four hours a day administering the business on top of the five to six hours getting to and leading the rides. Their talents appear to complement each other.

"I have a terrible sense of direction, and he can't remember anyone's name," Lee quipped.

Bob plans the routes and writes directions to starting points. He also developed and maintains the Get Up'N Go Web site (www.getupngoadventures.com). It's a resource for cycling neophytes, with chatty tutorials on buying a bike, shifting gears, and riding safely on roads and in woods.

Lee, the liaison to recreation departments, also handles registration and accounting. The couple hired a lawyer to advise them on liability coverage and other business matters.

Most of the problems they've encountered are the result of their success. They learned early on that riders are alienated if the group is too big or mixes riders of different abilities. They went from a single class level to three and are now mulling a fourth.

Recently, they started offering direct registration, bypassing recreation departments. They felt obliged to do this because differing publication schedules for recreation brochures made it difficult for riders to enroll in towns with later deadlines. Lee estimated that 75 percent of their riders this fall are carryovers from previous seasons.

But recreation departments were the source of all their riders initially and are an excellent feeder system for new riders, they said. The Evanses grapple with how to guarantee the departments they've worked with in the past enough slots to warrant inclusion in the brochures. This fall, rides were offered only through Winchester and Wellesley.

The Evanses continuously adjust their offerings. Last summer, they added evening rides. This winter, they will lead hikes for several recreation departments. They are also considering publishing their routes -- perhaps as a collection of rider cue cards.

For now, there are no plans to expand beyond what the two of them can handle.

"If it gets any bigger than this, I'll be the one staying at home doing administration," Lee protested. That is clearly not an option.
