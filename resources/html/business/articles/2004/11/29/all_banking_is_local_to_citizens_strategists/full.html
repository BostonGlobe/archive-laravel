All banking is local to Citizens strategists

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  November 29, 2004

When his nephew wanted a job in financial services, Brian Rothwell, the chief executive of Bay State Milling Co. in Quincy, needed advice on the industry.

ADVERTISEMENT

So he talked to the company's new personal banker, Lawrence K. Fish, the chief executive of Citizens Financial Group, who met with the young man and offered him a position in Citizens' training program for branch managers. Just months before, Fish had personally persuaded Rothwell to switch much of the company's business to Citizens from FleetBoston Financial Corp. with the promise to become the point man for Bay State Milling's account.

"We've given them more and more business as they've earned it," Rothwell said. "It has been a great relationship."

In the last year, Fish and the rest of Citizens' senior management say, they have wooed more than 90 corporate clients from other banks, including brand names such as Legal Sea Foods, with the promise to serve as their personal bankers. They give out their home and cellphone numbers, take clients' calls on lost ATM cards, and answer questions about accounts.

The idea sprang from an intense, three-week initiative last year that pulled together a handful of employees from across the bank on the eve of a massive upheaval in the market, Bank of America Corp.'s $48 billion acquisition of Fleet. Citizens sequestered some of the bank's "rising stars" in Boston's Park Plaza Hotel, forbade them from going home, gave them toys to play with such as crayons and coloring books, and promised them an unlimited budget, with one clear mission: Come up with new ways for Citizens to grow.

"I had a vision of the movie 'Animal House' where they had the food fight," said Fish. "I told them, 'Think of it as a sprint. Work really hard and have a lot of fun.' "

The ideas the team produced have turned into both wild successes and frustrating failures for New England's second-largest bank, which grooms itself as local and friendly, despite its ownership by the Royal Bank of Scotland and its ambition to become one of the largest banks in the United States. One of Citizens' biggest new initiatives began this month, when the bank rolled out an aggressive effort to encourage customers to switch to Citizens. The bank pairs customers with a "concierge" to handle issues such as setting up online billpay and direct deposit, and to close customers' old bank accounts for them.

Citizens already is implementing other ideas from the group, which executives dubbed its "Dreamer Team." The bank this year distributed thousands of green Citizens Bank soccer balls to children across the Northeast, launched an ad campaign to promote the seven-day-a-week banking in its supermarket branches, and gave a pot of money to branch managers to use however they chose.

It also flirted with more unorthodox ideas. Team members wanted to brand the name Citizens Bank on Boston's Freedom Trail. They envisioned Citizens cups and Citizens popcorn boxes at movie theaters. They wanted to form a marketing partnership with the king of New England chain restaurants, Dunkin' Donuts. None of those plans has worked so far.

During the three-week initiative, which took place between Thanksgiving and Christmas last year, the Citizens Dreamer Team members ditched their staid bankers' suits to dress primarily in jeans, some with gaudy wigs or oversized cowboy hats intended to spark creativity. During meetings, they jumped up to play with Frisbees and other toys lying around the team's makeshift offices. Some of the men shaved; others did not.

"We'd throw balls at each other," said Sue Sgroi, the group's leader. "We might have a war. We'd get on the different sides of the office and throw them at each other."

Fish and the rest of the bank's executives stopped by the Park Plaza Hotel frequently, even on nights and weekends.

Early on, Fish outlined three expectations for each idea: It had to be nice, it had to be friendly, and it had to be local. As they brainstormed, team members tested each idea against those standards.

The Freedom Trail idea fell short because executives worried it would be too narrow in scope. When Citizens looked more closely at movie theaters, the bankers could not find a chain willing to implement the idea across all of Citizens' markets.

When group members suggested the marketing partnership with Dunkin' Donuts during one of Fish's visits, the chief executive initially challenged the idea, saying he was worried that the large chain would not fit Citizens' desire to act locally.

But one of the group members, Debra Miller, argued back, saying that no one in her town of Londonderry, N.H., viewed the town's Dunkin' Donuts stores as a "global big huge organization."

"I said, 'Everyone will think that's local,' " said Miller, who is in charge of Citizens' community reinvestment in New Hampshire.

The strength of the argument convinced Fish, who gave the team the green light to pursue the idea. But after weeks of negotiations, the two companies could not come to an agreement, Fish said. Citizens favored a promotion that would allow customers to bump up the size of their coffee, but Dunkin' Donuts wanted to push its Coolattas instead, he said.

A spokesman for Dunkin' Donuts, Glen Schwartz, said, "Unfortunately, it didn't work out at the time, but we still see them as a great potential partner."

The group worked for three weeks straight, starting around 7:30 a.m. and often continuing conversations over dinner and during drinks in the hotel bar late at night. "You didn't want to miss the drinks because we were continuing to brainstorm ideas," said Gail Long, president of Citizens Capital Inc., Citizens' private equity subsidiary. Some participants even trekked to FAO Schwarz to go Christmas shopping, still talking about bank ideas.

Most saw their families only once, some not at all. Miller, the New Hampshire executive, said she was so worried about leaving her husband and children that she called a family meeting to discuss the issue. She cooked them a special dinner, beef stew with noodles, gravy, and biscuits, then explained Fish's idea. To her surprise, she said, her family volunteered to keep the house clean and do wash in her absence. When she came back home after the three-week session, they met her at the door with flowers.

"It was a huge sacrifice for us," Miller said. "We're a very close family."

Despite Citizens' efforts, especially in the commercial arena, its biggest competitor, Bank of America of Charlotte, N.C., denies that it has lost any business. "Bank of America's business in the Northeast is strong," said Alexandra Trower, a Bank of America spokeswoman. "We're both retaining existing clients and adding new clients every day."

Still, Fish and Citizens are undeterred. They say the bank's local mindset gives it an edge over the North Carolina bank.

These days, Fish said, he tells his corporate customers, "Your niece loses her ATM card while traveling in Italy? Call me." But he also seems to acknowledge the challenge Citizens faces as it grows.

"You don't want to be so big you can't deliver locally," Fish said.
