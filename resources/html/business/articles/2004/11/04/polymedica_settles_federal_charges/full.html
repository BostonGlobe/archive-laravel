BUSINESS IN BRIEF

PolyMedica settles federal charges

November 4, 2004

Medical supply company PolyMedica Corp. has reached a tentative $35 million settlement with the federal government over alleged Medicare fraud. ''It is important for the company and for our investors to put this matter behind us, so that we can focus all our energies on serving our patients and continuing to grow and develop our business," said PolyMedica chief executive Patrick Ryan. Under the tentative settlement, the company would make a single lump-sum payment to the US government and would not have to admit wrongdoing, according to the Woburn company. The federal government began investigating PolyMedica in August 2001, when dozens of FBI agents, including 19 computer specialists, raided four offices in Florida of two PolyMedica subsidiaries, Liberty Medical Supply and Liberty Home Pharmacy, and the homes of two employees. At the time, former employees said the company shipped diabetes test strips to people who didn't order them and did not reimburse Medicare for returned packages. (AP)

Bank plans to pick up 2.2m new accounts

Bank of America Corp., the third-biggest US bank, plans to add 2.2 million checking accounts this year, 69 percent more than in 2003, consumer banking chief Liam McGee told an investor conference in Boston. The Charlotte, N.C.-based bank will also boost home equity loan volume by 47 percent to $56 billion in 2004, said McGee. ''We have a significant capacity to grow," he said. Bank of America, led by chief executive Kenneth Lewis, got about 45 percent of its third-quarter profit from consumer banking. The company is the largest US consumer bank and expanded to 5,828 branches in 29 states and Washington, D.C., after it bought FleetBoston Financial Corp. in April. (Bloomberg)

SEC proposes more rules for exchanges

The New York Stock Exchange and eight other markets will get their first look next week at drafts of federal regulations intended to protect integrity by requiring a majority of the exchanges' directors to be independent. The Securities and Exchange Commission will ask the exchanges for comments on the proposals, which include requiring full independence for members of nominating, governance, audit, compensation, and regulatory committees. The SEC will present the draft Nov. 9. The NYSE replaced its board with an advisory panel and a slate of independent directors last year after learning the NYSE board had awarded former NYSE chairman Richard Grasso compensation of about $188 million. Current chairman John Reed dissolved the exchange's 27-member board, which had been dominated by executives of exchange-listed companies. The exchanges would be required to report significant changes in ownership and to restrict the voting levels of individual members to no more than 20 percent, the SEC said in on its website. (Bloomberg)

American Airlines plans more job cuts

American Airlines, struggling to compete with lower-overhead carriers, will cut more jobs to reduce costs, chief executive Gerard Arpey said. The company had already disclosed that it would lay off up to 650 mechanics and 450 pilots, Arpey told an investors meeting. ''We will see more cuts across the board, all workers, in the months ahead," Arpey added. American and other carriers are losing money as they are squeezed by high fuel costs and tough competition that makes it hard to raise fares. American's parent, AMR Corp., reported recently that it lost $214 million in the July-September quarter and expected an even bigger loss in the fourth quarter. AMR would have made money in the third quarter if fuel prices had remained at last year's levels. Arpey said American would pay about $500 million more for fuel in the fourth quarter than it did during the same period last year. ''Were it not for Exxon getting most of our money this year, we would have been able to make some progress," he said. (AP)

More allegations for Marsh & McLennan

Marsh & McLennan Cos. and other insurance brokers, sued by a client in August for taking fees from insurers, were also accused of steering business to insurance firms that agreed to buy reinsurance from them. The brokers neglected the interests of their clients and increased the cost of insurance by favoring some insurers, the customer, OptiCare Health Systems Inc., said in an amended complaint filed on Oct. 19 in the US Court for the Southern District of New York that seeks class-action status. The allegations about reinsurance, through a practice known as tying, go beyond New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer's Oct. 14 suit against Marsh, the world's largest insurance broker. Spitzer said Marsh colluded with insurers to rig bids and favor insurers that paid Marsh the most fees. The amended suit named additional brokers and insurers including American International Group Inc. and added evidence from Spitzer's suit. Marsh and Aon spokesmen declined to comment. (Bloomberg)

N.Y. Times Co. to shut TV production unit

The New York Times Co. plans to shut down its television production unit, resulting in the loss of about 24 jobs, as it focuses its efforts on the Discovery Times Channel, a 50-50 joint venture it formed with Discovery Communications Inc. a year and a half ago. The production unit had about $15 million in annual revenues and produced Times-branded shows as well as shows for other channels, including PBS, A&E, and Showtime, company spokesman Toby Usnik said. The company declined to release other financial details about the production unit. About half a dozen employees remain with Times Co. to coordinate the production of programs for the Discovery Times Channel. (AP)

Clothing retailer delays quarterly report

Tommy Hilfiger Corp. said it would delay the filing of its quarterly report while it reviews issues related to a government investigation. The clothing company also reported weaker pretax results for its second quarter. Shares fell 51 cents, or 5.4 percent, to $8.94. Tommy Hilfiger disclosed in September that the Justice Department was probing whether some of its units inappropriately paid commissions to Hilfiger's British Virgin Islands unit, as well as other tax matters. The company said it's providing documents to the US attorney's office for the Southern District of New York. Its quarterly report would have been due at the Securities and Exchange Commission Nov. 9. (Dow Jones/AP)

Gas dealers sue Shell, ChevronTexaco

Shell Oil Co., ChevronTexaco Corp., and a unit of Saudi Aramco were sued by a group of US gasoline dealers who claim the companies conspired to fix the price of fuel sold to about 20,000 service stations nationwide. Four New York service stations filed a price-fixing suit on behalf of Shell and Texaco dealers. The station owners say the oil companies created two joint ventures, Equilon Enterprises and Motiva Enterprises, as a guise to overcharge dealers. The lawsuit follows a June 1 ruling by a federal appeals court in California reinstating a similar case against the companies. That day, the General Accounting Office said a study indicated the joint ventures may have led to higher gas prices in some cities. The suit in Manhattan federal court claims that in 1996, senior officers of Saudi Refining Inc., ''Shell and Texaco met and entered into an agreement to raise, fix, peg, and stabilize the price of motor fuel sold to Shell and Texaco dealers." A Shell spokeswoman, the US arm of the Royal Dutch/Shell group, Europe's second-largest oil and natural gas producer, declined to comment. A spokesman for ChevronTexaco, the second-biggest US oil company, didn't return a call. (Bloomberg)
