BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Investor sues over Arch Wireless deal

July 1, 2004

Metrocall Holdings Inc., a wireless-paging company, and Arch Wireless Inc., which plans to buy Metrocall, were sued by a Metrocall investor who claims the planned purchase is unfair. A shareholder said in a Delaware Chancery Court lawsuit filed in Wilmington that the deal is unfair to Metrocall shareholders, who would get $150 million in cash and about a 28 percent stake in the combined business, while Westborough-based Arch shareholders would get about 73 percent. Metrocall would account for 36 percent of the combined company's revenue, he said. Arch and Metrocall, which both exited bankruptcy in 2002, plan to combine to create Wizards-Patriots Holdings Inc. in Alexandria, Va., where Metrocall is based. The transaction, which was disclosed in March, would allow the companies to compete with larger carriers such as Nextel Communications Inc., which sell paging services along with voice calling. An Arch spokesman did not immediately respond to a voice-mail message seeking comment. (Bloomberg)

Contractor wins torpedo deal

Waltham-based Raytheon Co., the world's largest missile maker, won a US Navy contract worth as much as $500 million over five years to make antisubmarine torpedoes. The initial award is $70.2 million for one year, and could rise if four one-year options are exercised, David Bancroft, Raytheon's chief engineer for the torpedo programs, said. It includes production of 51 MK54 lightweight torpedoes and 111 MK48 Advanced Capability heavyweight torpedoes. This contract is the third and final order for the MK54 under low-rate production, and the Navy is expected to decide this year whether to proceed with full-rate production, Bancroft said. Work under the contract will be done in Keyport, Wash., and Portsmouth, R.I., the company said. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Supreme Court lets 3M penalty stand

The Supreme Court refused to overturn a nearly $70 million judgment against the maker of Scotch tape in a monopoly case. A jury had ordered 3M to pay damages to LePage's Inc., which specialized in making store-brand tapes for big retailers. An appeals court said 3M lured away LePage's customers by offering bundled rebates that retailers could collect only by selling multiple 3M product lines. LePage's had sued 3M, claiming it lost business from some of its largest customers, including Kmart and Staples, because the rebate program involved payments as high as $1 million to retailers who agreed not to buy from competitors. A federal jury had found in 1999 that 3M unfairly used its market power to hurt sales of LePage's tape. (AP)

SEC seeks control of hedge funds

The Securities and Exchange Commission will vote in two weeks on requiring hedge funds to register with the agency, a move the SEC chairman says is needed to head off potential blowups that could hurt ordinary investors. The high-risk funds, with an estimated $750 billion to $1 trillion in assets and largely unregulated, are ''in many ways . . . an accident waiting to happen," SEC chairman William Donaldson said. The rule would open the funds' books to SEC examiners and make them subject to an array of regulations, including accounting and disclosure requirements. Donaldson's fellow Republican commissioners, Paul Atkins and Cynthia Glassman, have expressed opposition to the proposal. But with the two Democrats on the panel supporting it, the proposal can secure a majority. After being opened to public comment for several months, it could be formally adopted by the SEC. (AP)

FCC chairman urged to hold hearings

Federal Communications Commission member Michael Copps urged chairman Michael Powell to hold as many as a dozen public hearings in the next six months on changes that should be made in FCC media-ownership rules. A US appeals court in Philadelphia last week ordered the FCC to review its new rules that allow companies such as News Corp. and Viacom Inc. to buy more television stations and newspapers. ''We should start hearings right away," Copps, a Democrat who has led the fight against media consolidation, told reporters in Washington. ''People are interested. Let's do it right this time." A spokesman for Powell, David Fiske, declined to comment. Powell, a Republican, resisted requests from Copps last year for more public hearings before reaching a decision on the media-ownership rules. The FCC voted 3-2 along party lines to adopt the rules in June 2003. (Bloomberg)

Freddie Mac earnings fell 52% last year

Mortgage giant Freddie Mac saw its earnings plummet 52 percent last year amid volatile interest rates and losses as it hedged against risk while grappling with an accounting crisis. The second-largest US buyer of home mortgages reported its net income fell to $4.9 billion, or $6.79 a share, in 2003 from $10.1 billion, or $14.18 a share, in 2002. The report of its annual results was delayed by the accounting problems as the government-sponsored company untangled a $5 billion misstatement of earnings -- mostly underreported -- for 2000-2002. Freddie Mac's chief financial officer, Martin Baumann, said the company is ''making substantial progress overhauling [its] financial reporting and accounting systems, but this process is a challenging one and more hard work is in front of us." Freddie Mac also said it financed homes for some 6 million families in 2003, including 2.5 million low- and moderate-income households. (AP)

$100 discount for Apple iPods turn-in

Dell Inc. is running a promotion that gives customers a $100 discount for some Dell Digital Jukebox music players if customers send in their old Apple iPods to be recycled, the company said. The mail-in rebate offer applies to Dell's 15-gigabyte music player, which carries a regular price of $199, the company said. The player has a battery life of about 20 hours, about two times that of Apple Computer Inc.'s market-leading iPod, Dell said. Apple's iPod and iPod mini-players, which range in cost from $249 to $499, depending on their capacity, have more than 50 percent of the market for digital music players, and even more when measured by revenue, Apple has said. (Reuters)

UAL raises most international fares 5%

United Airlines initiated a 5 percent fare increase on most flights to international destinations from the United States, citing rising fuel costs as the reason. The increase is the latest of numerous attempts by US carriers this year to try to have passengers share the burden of soaring jet-fuel prices. Last month, United rescinded a $10-per-round-trip fuel surcharge on most North American fares after only a day when American Airlines was the sole competitor to follow. There was no immediate word whether United's rivals were matching this move. (AP)

THE WORLD
Canada rejects music industry claims

Canada's music industry suffered a second legal defeat in three months when that country's supreme court rejected its argument that Internet companies should pay royalties for pirated music. The high court rejected an argument from the music industry that Internet service providers, such as BCE Inc. and Time Warner Inc.'s AOL, do not have to pay royalties to musicians and their publishers to cover music their customers download. ''The capacity of the Internet to disseminate works of the arts and intellect is one of the great innovations of the information age," one justice wrote. (Reuters)
