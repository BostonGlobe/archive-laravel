BUSINESS IN BRIEF

No new stent recall seen

July 31, 2004

The US Food and Drug Administration said it has "no indication" there will be another recall of Boston Scientific Corp. cardiac stents. The agency is continuing to review the Natick medical device firm's two recalls it issued this month due to a manufacturing problem that led to three deaths and 43 injuries. The FDA's inspection of Boston Scientific's manufacturing plant in Maple Grove, Minn., is complete, an agency spokeswoman said. "There were no serious problems found," said Sharon Snider in an interview. A separate inspection of a plant in Ireland, by the FDA and Irish officials, is continuing. (Jeffrey Krasner)

Schering-Plough settles

Massachusetts officials say that Schering-Plough Inc. has agreed to pay the state nearly $10 million to settle charges that it concealed pricing information and deliberately avoided its obligations to pay rebate funds to the Massachusetts Medicaid Program. The payment is part of a national civil settlement that requires the pharmaceutical giant to pay $345.5 million to settle criminal and civil charges that it overcharged Medicaid. Federal prosecutors say the Kenilworth, N.J., drug maker offered what amounted to price discounts for its allergy medication to private insurers, but disguised the discounts to avoid offering similar discounts to the government's Medicaid program, as required by law. (Globe wire services)

THE NATION

Airline seeks concessions

Delta Air Lines chief executive Gerald Grinstein told pilots the company's survival depends on a minimum $1 billion in concessions from them, saying the union plan for up to $705 million in cuts was not enough. The letter follows last week's proposal by Delta pilots to cut their pay by 23 percent and agree to other rules and scheduling changes that would save the airline between $655 million and $705 million a year. The union offer was roughly twice as much as its previous one. Delta's management has said that without deep wage concessions it would need to file for bankruptcy. Analysts say the airline, which had $2 billion in unrestricted cash at the end of the second quarter, has until the fall to get the cuts it needs, or enter Chapter 11. (AP)

NYSE mulls trading change

New York Stock Exchange chief executive John Thain might introduce a plan to let investors trade large blocks of shares electronically, speeding up share sales on the nation's largest stock exchange. Thain has outlined the plan to some NYSE members, including institutional traders and floor brokers. The exchange's chief is trying to maintain its market share in the face of proposed regulation by the Securities and Exchange Commission that would let competing exchanges fill orders faster, even if they don't offer the best price. The NYSE is defending its specialist system after five member firms paid $242 million to settle SEC allegations that they violated trading rules. The plan would allow for trades to be made as long as the buy order is within 5 cents of the sell order. If the spread is wider, trading would be routed to an auction between specialists and brokers on the exchange floor. (Bloomberg)

Delta, Northwest hike fares

Delta and Northwest airlines matched a $10-per-round-trip price increase by American Airlines on many routes, but other carriers were still considering whether to go along. American Airlines on Thursday raised fares on most of its domestic flights, citing the rising cost of jet fuel. American, a unit of AMR Corp., said that every 1-cent rise in the price of a gallon of jet fuel costs it more than $30 million a year. American and its regional affiliates spent more than $900 million on fuel in the second quarter, an increase of nearly 42 percent from a year earlier. Northwest has often forced other carriers to abandon price increases by refusing to raise fares. This time it matched American's increase only on deeply discounted fares, which could have the same effect, said Terry Trippler, an air fare analyst in Minneapolis. (AP)
