LOTS & BLOCKS

Pipeline's still full of luxury condos

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr., Globe Staff  |  July 31, 2004

How many luxury condominiums does Boston need?

Anthony Pangaro, who built the Ritz-Carlton Towers and is now selling 231 condos at One Charles, on the edge of the Theater District, says the market is good for what's in the pipeline. And perhaps for another cycle's worth, those getting permitted for completion in a couple of years. After that, who can tell?

Ron Druker's Atelier|505 in the South End has sold out, at prices from $600,000 to $3.3 million. One Charles -- closer to downtown, with many units having views of Boston Common and some with a glimpse of the State House -- has even higher "price points."

A friendly fellow named Solomon used to wedge cars in on a surface parking lot on this block, between the Four Seasons Hotel and Stuart Street, next to the nicely rehabbed Motor Mart Garage and across Charles Street from the state Transportation Building. Now, Millennium Partners-Boston (Pangaro and two partners, working with Millennium Partners LLC of New York) are finishing up 17 floors of high-end living space.

The penthouses won't be complete till spring, but lower floors will begin to be occupied in November. Druker's philosophy is to begin selling early in construction. Pangaro wants people to be able to walk through the building, so marketing has begun. He's also negotiating to put a restaurant in the first-floor retail space.

Designed by Gary Edward Handel + Associates, One Charles has Beacon Hill-like brick, though in shades from black to burnt sienna, with large bay windows of aluminum and untinted glass. The windows open. Unlike most new construction, it has concrete block walls, not steel studs and sheets of plaster-like composite, behind the brick.

The mix of unit types is a study in a developer's detailed strategy to meet almost any niche of market demand, all in one building. There are 45 styles among the 231 condos. They range from studios to three-bedrooms, with 9- to 11-foot ceilings. There are also 14 penthouses with almost 3,000 square feet of living space each. A "split two" has bedrooms on both the east and west sides of the building and a living room between.

One Charles is actually two buildings, connected above an entranceway, with separate lobbies. That makes for shorter, more intimate corridors. It has six corners, plus setbacks at upper floors, providing 39 units with balcony-like terraces.

A tour showed how squeezing value out of a large residential project is a work in progress. Pangaro said when he noticed in certain rooms that wires and conduits overhead were close to the walls, he ordered changes. Bovis Lend Lease LMB Inc., the contractor, heightened the center of the room's ceiling even though it was designed flat. That can give a relatively small bedroom the feel of a lot more space. Pangaro moved a bedroom wall two feet after it was already in, eliminating one closet; it just felt too small to him. "These little things drive a contractor crazy," he said.

Sailing away
Those graceful ship models are still in the windows at Russia Wharf, 540 Atlantic Ave., but Lannan Ship Model Gallery has jumped across the old Central Artery corridor to 99 High St. Equity Office Properties is preparing to redevelop the three Russia Wharf buildings and add a hotel and office tower. Though completion is five years away, and the gallery decided to move now. Its current building, on the Rose Kennedy Greenway, will be condos.

Water walk
Boston Redevelopment Authority officials joined Vivien Li, executive director of the Boston Harbor Association, at East Boston's Piers Park this month to "unveil" the website www.bostonharborwalk.com, a guide to the 47-mile continuous walk around Boston Harbor, 31 miles of which have been completed. In five years, there will be gaps of only about two miles remaining, Li predicted.
