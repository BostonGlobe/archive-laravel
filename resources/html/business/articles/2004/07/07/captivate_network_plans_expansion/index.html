BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Captivate Network plans expansion

July 7, 2004

Captivate Network Inc., a Westford company that operates news and advertising television screens inside 4,500 office-tower elevators in Boston and other US cities, said it will expand into another eight metropolitan markets this year. Captivate, which was purchased by media conglomerate Gannett Co. in April, said it is adding coverage in Hartford, Denver, Minneapolis, Memphis, Philadelphia, Seattle, Washington, D.C., and Columbia, S.C., as part of a project to deploy 4,000 more elevator screens by the end of 2005. Captivate estimates its screens are seen by 1.5 million people a day. (Peter J. Howe)

Verizon activates 5 cellphone towers

Verizon Wireless said it has recently activated new cellphone transmission towers in Boston and four other Eastern Massachusetts communities, which should improve call coverage. Besides a new unit on Washington Street in the South End, Verizon said, it has recently turned on cell sites at Alewife Center covering East Arlington and North Cambridge; in Gloucester near downtown, its second site in the city; in Milford, to improve coverage on routes 16 and 140 in the town center; and in Sherborn, to add coverage and capacity along routes 16 and 27 as far as Holliston, Medfield, and Natick. Verizon, which is owned jointly by Verizon Communications Inc. and the European cellphone giant Vodafone Group PLC, said the new sites are part of its $4 billion annual network upgrade effort. (Peter J. Howe)

Aspen Technology, FTC near settlement

Aspen Technology Inc. and the Federal Trade Commission's Bureau of Competition reached a tentative settlement, with the FTC moving to withdraw its complaint against Aspen from adjudication. In August, the FTC challenged Aspen's acquisition of Hyprotech Ltd., a Canadian software firm. In the complaint, the FTC said the $97 million acquisition hindered competition in the market for engineering software used by petroleum engineers. With the acquisition, the FTC said Aspen controlled 82 percent of the engineering process-simulation software market. The software and implementation services company said the administrative trial that was scheduled to begin July 30 will not occur. The proposed consent decree has been submitted to FTC commissioners for approval. (Dow Jones/AP)

Canadian firm to open Newton office

The Canadian Internet search company Copernic Technologies Inc. is setting up a US headquarters in Newton and has tapped a high-tech veteran as the company's new chief executive. David Burns formerly served as chief executive of FAST Search and Transfer, a Wellesley company that makes search technologies for government and corporate use. Before that, he was director of product marketing at Lycos, a Waltham-based consumer search company that was acquired by a Spanish Internet conglomerate. Copernic makes software that lets users run simultaneous searches of multiple Internet sites. (Hiawatha Bray)

EU upholds Harvard mouse-gene patent

The European Patent Office upheld a Harvard University patent on a mouse genetically altered to develop cancer, but restricted its wording so that it applies only to mice and not to all rodents. The pan-European patent, granted in 1992, protects the method of producing the animals. But the ruling, which closes a years-long legal battle with environmental groups, added further qualifications to a 2001 ruling that limited the patent to rodents, rather than to mammals in general. The patent office acknowledged concerns about ethical questions and animal rights, but said the medical uses of the patent must be weighed. The US Patent and Trademark Office granted a patent to the Harvard mouse in 1988, the first "transgenic" animal patent to be granted. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Comcast names chief operating officer

Stephen Burke, the president of Comcast Corp.'s cable division, was named to the additional post of chief operating officer of the nation's biggest cable television company. Burke will remain as head of Comcast Cable, which will operate with a restructured leadership team. Before joining Comcast in 1998, Burke was an executive with the Walt Disney Co., where he was president of ABC Broadcasting. Comcast made a takeover bid for Disney but dropped the $54 billion offer in April, after Disney management rebuffed it. Under the reorganization, Dave Watson will be executive vice president for operations, and Mike Tallent will be executive vice president for finance and administration. (AP)

Sprint, MCI raising some calling rates

Sprint Corp. and MCI Inc. are raising prices on a handful of wireline calling plans, saying rates are increasing across the industry. Both companies say the increases are not the result of recent regulatory decisions that could raise the wholesale rates they pay regional Bell companies. Sprint's new $3.95 monthly fee on its Sprint Sense Domestic and Sprint Sense Day Domestic plans will affect 4 to 5 percent of its customers, a spokesman said. At MCI, monthly fees for the Nationwide plan will increase from $2.95 to $4.95. The plan offers 5-cent anytime state-to-state calling. Connection fees for another flat-rate plan, called Anytime Calling plan, will increase by 14 cents a call to 49 cents. (AP)

THE WORLD
Israeli company offers free e-mail

An Israeli company has begun offering a free e-mail service that includes one gigabyte of data storage per user. Walla Communications Ltd. launched its Walla Mail service at www.walla.com. Walla is the latest company to respond to Gmail, the one-gigabyte free e-mail service planned this year by Google, the Internet's most popular search service. Gmail is still being tested and is not yet available to the public. The Spymac website, at www.spymac.com, has launched a one-gigabyte mail service, while major Internet companies like Yahoo Inc. and Microsoft Corp. have expanded the amount of storage included in their free e-mail services. (Hiawatha Bray)
