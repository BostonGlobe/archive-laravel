Partnership dispute imperils neighborhood's rejuvenation

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr., Globe Staff  |  July 14, 2004

Two developers behind the creation of Boston's emerging SoWa neighborhood in the South End are suing each other over property that could be worth $100 million, a dispute that threatens to dissolve their 20-year partnership and stall the area's revitalization.

The area south of Washington Street, or SoWa, is on the edge of the South End and until a few years ago was largely made up of vacant red-brick factories, some that were used to make pianos. Over the past 15 years, it has turned into a mix of artists lofts and galleries and commercial and retail space, with residences springing up as well.

Now underway is a high-stakes suit and countersuit involving Mario Nicosia, who while a student at Northeastern University in the 1970s rehabbed a South End building and later turned it into a small empire, and his partner, Cambridge architect Graham Gund. Gund created the Cambridge Hyatt Regency hotel and Disney's Coronado Springs Resort and Convention Center in Florida.

Over the past 20 years, the two have invested in the neighborhood, developed, and now own more than 800,000 square feet of commercial, artists' and retail space. Nicosia says they agreed their partnership would last through 2014.

When the pair couldn't agree over the last two years on making changes in their original business agreements -- that Nicosia decided were unfair to him -- each accused the other of wrongdoing. Nicosia says Gund's representatives threatened to end the partnership. Gund's suit says Nicosia charged him and another partner with ''breach of fiduciary duty" and ''a pattern of misbehavior dating back to at least 1990." They deny the charges.

Gund also charges that Nicosia threatened he would block further development of the properties and ''has failed and refused to carry out responsibly his duties," which Nicosia denies. ''The disagreements and dissension . . . are of a serious, irreconcilable, and permanent character," says the lawsuit Gund filed, naming his partnerships as plaintiffs.

Gund has asked the court to dissolve the partnerships that own and control the property, and to give each party his share. The cases were recently consolidated in Suffolk County Superior Court.

Nicosia said he is in the process of major rehabilitation of some of the old warehouse buildings and worries that a breakup of the business relationship will jeopardize not only the continuing improvement of individual buildings but the further development of the neighborhood. About 90,000 square feet of leases are now being negotiated, and a change in ownership could worry prospective tenants, he said.

Businesses and residents that have existing leases would not be affected, he said. But some buildings owned by the partnerships house dozens of artists and tenants paying relatively low rents, he said. If a sale is forced, a new owner could put and end to those arrangements and bring in higher-paying occupants.

The bitter disagreement is being played out while business entities jointly controlled by the two men continue to lease and operate the buildings, which are located between Harrison Avenue and Albany Street.

''This may sound a little crazy to you, but we're still getting along fine, signing leases, with money in the bank," Nicosia said in a recent interview.

But the dispute threatens to destroy Nicosia's dream. He envisions a Boston version of New York's or London's SoHo cultural districts, thriving neighborhoods of artists, businesses, and residents. ''He's not in danger of being impoverished," said Nicosia's lawyer, David L. Kelston, of Adkins, Kelston & Zavez. But, ''He'd lose what he's worked on building for the last 20 years."

Gund declined to comment for this article, as did his lawyer at the law firm Sullivan & Worcester LLP of Boston.

Specifically, the disagreement involves Nicosia's charges that Gund -- through the law firm, which Nicosia says represented both of them at the time -- set up ownership structures that favored Gund and put Nicosia at a disadvantage.

For example, Gund had a right to acquire Nicosia's property interest upon his death, the complaint alleges, also charging that Nicosia was restricted from transferring his assets even to his children.

The suits involve multiple business entities and buildings that have and have not been rehabbed, including 450, 560, and 580 Harrison Avenue, and 38 Wareham St.

Nicosia said he has been the active partner in the business relationships, owning half or almost half of most of the properties, with Gund involved mostly as an investment partner.

In the 1980s, they jointly bought 450 Harrison Ave., the beginning of a series of business dealings and the birth of SoWa. In 2000, Nicosia conceived the name SoWa and began marketing the area as such.

Nicosia and Gund were not close social friends but worked well together for years, cosigned notes for loans, and never sold a developed property, according to court documents.

Gund's partner in his share of the properties is Peter Madsen, former managing director of Pembroke Real Estate, Fidelity Investments' private real estate company. Madsen could not be reached to comment.

''Nicosia has, over the years, and with Gund and Madsen's enthusiastic and complete support, made them millions of dollars . . .," Nicosia's suit says.

So how did this profitable partnership start to unravel?

About two years ago, during a dispute with another partner in one of the companies, Nicosia said, he had a legal expert look over the documents that had bound Gund and himself together in business.

When he discovered that some of the provisions were unfavorable to him, he said, he suggested to Gund and Gund's lawyers that they be changed. In court documents, Nicosia accuses Gund of having ''systematically overreached in order, without disclosure, to advantage themselves and disadvantage Nicosia."

Nicosia said he didn't have a good explanation for why he didn't object to the offending provisions when they were first inserted. ''I rely a little bit more on relationships than I do on protection," he acknowledged.
