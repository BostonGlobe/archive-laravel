Firm agrees to compensate UMass

July 14, 2004

CytRx Corp. said it has agreed to pay the University of Massachusetts roughly $175,000 in cash and stock earmarked to go to a licensing consultant after the state medical school raised an ethics concern about his role. CytRx, of Los Angeles, has licensed from the medical school in Worcester therapies based on RNA interference techniques and a HIV vaccine now in clinical trials. A spokesman for the school, Mark L. Shelton, said officials realized that a Kansas consulting firm used by CytRx to arrange the RNA agreements, Rip Grossman and Associates, also had done work for the medical school in a previous matter, which the school worried could create the appearance of a conflict of interest. The state ethics commission is also reviewing the matter. The Kansas firm did not return calls for comment. (Ross Kerber)

Senate backs bill to protect borrowers

The Massachusetts Senate approved a bill that would provide more protection to consumers who are offered home loans with high fees and interest rates. The House passed a similar bill, and the minor differences between the two versions should be resolved shortly, said Senator Andrea F. Nuciforo Jr., a Pittsfield Democrat and a sponsor of the legislation. ''Home loans that strip away home equity or leave borrowers with unaffordable payments have no place in the practices of any responsible lender," added Nancy Cook, a board member of the Association of Community Organizations for Reform Now, an advocacy group that supports the bill. (Chris Reidy)

THE NATION
Red Hat to restate results for 3 years

Linux distributor Red Hat Inc. said it would restate results for the past three fiscal years to correct the way it recognizes subscription revenue, adding that federal regulators have raised questions about an annual report. Red Hat, which provides update and support services for Linux software, said the restatement would result in significant changes to operating profit and net income for some periods. Shares plunged $4.62, or 22.7 percent, to $15.73, adding to a slump that has erased nearly two-thirds of a yearlong rally fueled by investors seeking to ride the growth of Linux. Red Hat said it started discussing possible restatements with its auditor, PricewaterhouseCoopers, on June 16, two days after chief financial officer Kevin Thompson gave his resignation and one day before it reported quarterly earnings. (Reuters)

Duke Energy to pay $207.5m to 3 states

Duke Energy Corp. will take a $104.9 million second-quarter charge to help foot a $207.5 million settlement of claims it overcharged for wholesale electricity during the California energy crisis, the company said. The company said the pretax charge will be reflected in results it is scheduled to release July 29. The balance of the settlement will come from $102.6 million in previously reported reserve and associated offsets, Duke said. The settlement involved federal regulators, the states of Washington and Oregon, and California's three largest investor-owned utilities, Duke said. The settlement needs approval from the California Public Utilities Commission and the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission. (AP)

Delta to record $1.65b in charges

Delta Air Lines Inc. warned it will post $1.65 billion in noncash charges in the second quarter and said it will no longer recognize income tax benefits, which will boost its net losses. Its shares fell 66 cents, or 9 percent, to $6.09. The charges are being taken in part because Delta's actual and projected financial performance for 2004 has been significantly hurt by higher than expected fuel costs and lower returns from its US business, chief financial officer Mike Palumbo said. As a result, he said, it is unclear when the company will be able to generate sufficient taxable income to use its deferred income tax assets, which involve certain expenses that Delta can use for income tax deductions. Some of the charges are also due to an increase in pilot retirements, Delta said. (AP)

Shares of American Airlines' parent drop

Shares in American Airlines' parent, AMR Corp., tumbled almost 6 percent after Smith Barney downgraded the shares and Prudential cut its earnings estimates for the world's largest airline. Smith Barney analyst Daniel McKenzie said in a research note that his brokerage has downgraded shares of AMR one notch, to ''hold," because American will be at a cost disadvantage to some key competitors. McKenzie said the brokerage cut its price target for AMR shares to $13, from $19. AMR shares fell 42 cents, or 4.1 percent, to $9.93. It was the first time since August that AMR shares had traded under $10. A Prudential Equity Group analyst, Daniel Hemme, said in a research note that his firm was cutting its estimate of results per share in the second quarter of this year for AMR from a profit of 28 cents to a loss of 12 cents per share, as items such as increased fuel prices have slammed the carrier. (Reuters)

Separate sentencing for Stewart's broker

Martha Stewart's ex-Merrill Lynch & Co. broker, Peter Bacanovic, who'll learn Friday whether he's going to prison for obstructing justice, will be sentenced separately from his former client, a judge has ruled. US District Judge Miriam Cedarbaum granted Bacanovic's request to be sentenced separately. Stewart will be sentenced Friday morning and Bacanovic in the afternoon. Both face from 10 to 16 months in prison under federal sentencing guidelines. ''We feel we've been disadvantaged by being in the shadow of Martha Stewart," Bacanovic spokesman Lou Colasuonno said. ''We feel he should be sentenced under the merits of his own case." (Bloomberg)

THE WORLD
Diamond producer pleads guilty

De Beers pleaded guilty in US federal court to a decadeold price-fixing charge, freeing the world's dominant diamond producer to compete directly in the lucrative US market. De Beers, the South African diamond concern that is 45 percent owned by mining conglomerate Anglo American PLC, was fined the maximum $10 million by US District Judge George Smith under terms of a plea agreement. The charge of fixing prices on industrial diamonds in 1991 and 1992 had previously been dismissed against De Beers' original codefendant in the 1994 case, General Electric Co., for lack of evidence, while De Beers refused to acknowledge US jurisdiction. (Reuters)
