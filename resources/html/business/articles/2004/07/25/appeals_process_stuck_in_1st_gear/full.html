CONSUMER BEAT

Appeals process stuck in 1st gear

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  July 25, 2004

Massachusetts drivers are challenging -- and reversing -- a large number of the hefty auto insurance premium increases that typically follow an accident.

ADVERTISEMENT

Drivers last year filed 54,000 appeals of insurance company surcharges -- the points assessed for at-fault accidents that boost a driver's annual premium -- and won a favorable judgment slightly more than half the time.

But the 23 percent increase in appeals last year overwhelmed the state's team of eight hearing officers and resulted in a huge case backlog. Currently, drivers are waiting an average of 16 to 18 months to argue their case before a hearing officer. Some people are waiting two years or longer.

"That's wrong," said Roger Babb, chairman of the Board of Appeals, who is taking steps to shorten the delays. "It's the old saying, justice delayed is justice denied."

Babb said the combination of tough economic times and the high cost of insurance surcharges is contributing to the increase in appeals. A surcharge can raise a driver's step classification and annual premium by several hundred dollars or more and remain in place for a period of six years.

"People are feeling the pinch more and when they're feeling the pinch they look around for ways to save money," Babb said.

The high success rate on appeals could have implications for all of the state's drivers, not just those filing the challenges. The points added after accidents are part of the state's safe driver insurance plan, which rewards good drivers with credits and penalizes bad drivers with surcharges. The plan is internally self-funded, so when drivers win appeals on surcharges there is less money available for credits for good drivers.

Massachusetts is the only state in the nation where drivers found to be more than 50 percent at fault in an accident by their insurer can appeal the decision directly to a state hearing officer. In other states, insurers say, drivers are forced to file an appeal with the same insurer who initially ruled against them or take their business to another carrier who might view the circumstances of the accident differently.

In Massachusetts, the filing of an appeal doesn't block a premium surcharge from taking effect. Drivers must start paying the surcharge immediately; they get the surcharge money back, with no interest, if they prevail on the appeal.

In interviews with drivers waiting for hearings at the Division of Insurance offices downtown, a couple of them said it was unfair that they had to pay the surcharge while they waited nearly two years for a hearing. The drivers, who asked not to be identified, said it seemed as if they were guilty until proven innocent.

Several of the appellants also said the long wait time for a hearing was an inconvenience. Beverly Sampson of Braintree said she was filling in for her son at his hearing because he had been called out of state on business that day. She brought pictures and detailed notes on the accident, which occurred on Nov. 10, 2002, but she said the long wait made it difficult.

"It's hard to go back two years," she said. "You've got to keep a file, be organized."

Widlene Toussaint of Arlington said she had no illusions that she would get her premium surcharge back with interest if she prevailed in her hearing.

"I assume there won't be any interest," she said. "We're dealing with the state here."

The high cost of surcharges may be prompting more appeals, but there are also just more cars -- and accidents -- on the roads.

Over the last eight years, the number of surcharge appeal requests has increased nearly 31 percent. The biggest jump occurred last year, despite the fact that the filing fee doubled to $50. Over the same eight-year period, the number of cars registered in Massachusetts has increased 14 percent to 4 million vehicles and the number of property damage claims, a good barometer of accidents, increased 12 percent to nearly 282,000.

Daniel Johnston, president of the Automobile Insurers Bureau of Massachusetts, said the number of successful appeals of insurance company rulings is not as large as it might seem when placed in proper context.

Last year, for example, there were nearly 282,000 property damage claims and more than 54,000 surcharge appeals filed in Massachusetts. Johnston said those numbers would indicate drivers appealed insurance company rulings only about 19 percent of the time. Assuming the appellants prevailed on half of their appeals, Johnston said, that would mean only about 10 percent of the insurance company decisions were overturned.

Although some of the appeals filed by drivers are frivolous -- state officials say one driver recently blamed his accident on an "evil car" -- Babb said most of the drivers challenging insurance rulings are doing so because they believe they have a strong case. He noted insurance companies have to follow strict rules in assessing fault in accidents and are not allowed to consider mitigating factors. By contrast, he said, hearing officers are given wide latitude.

In 2001, hearing delays began approaching 15 months and the Division of Insurance vowed to cut the backlog. Officials recruited lawyers working elsewhere at the division to hear cases, but Babb said the drain on the rest of the agency eventually became too much. When the lawyers returned to their old jobs, hearing delays began mounting again and even grew longer as the number of appeals increased.

Babb, who came out of retirement six months ago to head the Board of Appeals, said he won't make the same mistakes this time. He said he has won approval to hire two more hearing officers and convert two part-time clerical workers to full-time status.

Babb is also seeking approval to upgrade the board's computer system, which would reduce the clerical burden on hearing officers. He is even looking outside the agency for lawyers who could be loaned temporarily to serve as hearing officers.

"We're looking everywhere," he said.
