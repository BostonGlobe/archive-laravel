Venture funds invest $916m in N.E. firms

By Hiawatha Bray, Globe Staff  |  July 27, 2004

Venture capitalists pumped $916.6 million into New England firms in the second quarter, the most since in late 2001, according to a new study.

''There is definitely renewed optimism among the venture capitalists," said Matthew Littlewood, a partner at PricewaterhouseCoopers in Boston. ''Fundamentally it's driven by the steadily improving economy."

Nationally, investment reached a two-year high as venture capital firms placed $5.6 billion with 561 firms, according to the MoneyTree Survey by accounting firm PricewaterhouseCoopers, financial publisher Thomson Venture Economics, and the trade group National Venture Capital Association. Many of the investments represented first-time financing of young companies.

''There's definitely been a shift to more start-ups, more early-stage deals being done," said Jesse Reyes, vice president of Thomson. Reyes said that during the economic downturn, VCs concentrated on the health of their existing portfolios rather than on new investments.

Tracy Lefteroff, who is global managing partner of private equity and venture capital for PricewaterhouseCoopers, said the increase in funding bore little resemblance to the dot-com boom of the late 1990s, when an inordinate amount of investment flowed into Internet companies. This time, ''There isn't a breakaway industry or a red-hot niche," Lefteroff said. For example, 51 software companies got $250 million in early-stage funding, while 39 life sciences firms got $232 million.

''Unlike a few years ago, everybody isn't trying to do the same thing," said Bob Pavey, a general partner at Morgenthaler Ventures of Menlo Park, Calif., a venture capital firm. ''There's a lot of diversity."

Another trend is an increase in the number of business veterans seeking to launch new ventures.

''We've seen a lot of experienced entrepreneurs coming back into the market to start a second or a third company," said Chad Waite, a partner at OVP Venture Partners in Seattle.

In addition, venture investors are being more conservative.

''The companies that were starting up a couple of years ago with double-digit millions being contributed to their early financing are way down," Waite said. Instead, start-ups are making do with first-time investments of $5 million or so.

New England venture investing increased for the third consecutive quarter, as investors placed big bets on a number of computer technology and pharmaceutical companies.

Leading the list was TechTarget, a Needham computer industry magazine publisher, which rounded up $70 million from Technology Crossover Ventures of Palo Alto, Calif., and Polaris Venture Partners of Waltham.

Alan Spoon, managing general partner at Polaris, said that TechTarget's stable of specialized publications and websites made it an attractive investment. ''Online media with this kind of focus is gaining market share from traditional media," he said.

Another company to benefit from the venture revival is AmberWave Systems Corp. of Salem, N.H., a maker of advanced silicon material for use in microchip production. AmberWave raised $21 million in the second quarter, money the company will use to bring its product to market.

''We saw and continue to see an improvement in the venture market," said Bryan Lord, vice president of corporate development at AmberWave. ''Right now there is a lot of money sitting on the sidelines that's waiting to be put to work."

Indeed, Lord said he's seeing a return of competition to the market, with venture firms vying for the chance to invest in the most attractive companies.

But Lord also saw healthy caution in the venture market, as companies tried to avoid the irrational exuberance of the late 1990s. ''We will continue to see discriminating venture capitalists who don't want to relive the bubble years where they were throwing money at anybody with a PowerPoint presentation," he said.
