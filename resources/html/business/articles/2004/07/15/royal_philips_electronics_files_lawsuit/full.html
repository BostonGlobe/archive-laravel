BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Royal Philips Electronics files lawsuit

July 15, 2004

Royal Philips Electronics said it filed suit against Gillette Co. in US and German courts, alleging the Boston company's soon-to-be-introduced Oral-B Sonic Complete toothbrush violates Philips patents for Sonicare toothbrushes. Europe's biggest consumer electronics maker, Philips advertises that its Sonicare microprocessor helps its bristle tips move three times faster than other electric toothbrushes. Gillette, the world's biggest seller of manual and electric toothbrushes, said it is confident that its Oral-B products do not infringe upon any Philips patents. Gillette's tooth care business accounts for about 14 percent of its sales and 10 percent of its profits. (Naomi Aoki)

Genzyme extends charitable relationship

Genzyme Corp. of Cambridge said it will extend for five years its collaboration with Project Hope to distribute its Cerezyme treatment to patients worldwide suffering from Gaucher disease. Project Hope, an international charity promoting health education and research, identifies patients with the genetic ailment, trains doctors to administer the drug, and delivers the drug to foreign countries. Since 1999, the partnership has identified and treated more than 200 patients in 17 countries. Genzyme donates the drug to Project Hope and provides support for the program. Gaucher patients lack an enzyme to break down fats, which build up causing catastrophic health problems. (Jeffrey Krasner)

Feuerstein undergoes heart operation

Aaron M. Feuerstein, president of Lawrence textile maker Malden Mills Industries Inc., underwent cardiac surgery at UMass Memorial Medical Center in Worcester on Tuesday and is recovering, representatives of the company and the hospital said. Neither would discuss when Feuerstein, 78, might return to work. Feuerstein is trying to buy back control of Malden Mills following the company's emergence from bankruptcy protection last year. (Ross Kerber)

THE NATION
2 casinos said to be in merger talks

Harrah's Entertainment Inc. is near an agreement to acquire Caesars Entertainment Inc. for roughly $10 billion, including debt, creating the largest casino operator in the world, a source close to the situation said. Harrah's and Caesars together would have $8.8 billion in annual revenue, 54 casinos, and a sizable presence in nearly every major legalized US gambling market. Talks between the two largest US gambling companies are at a sensitive stage, but the source said the chances of the deal being struck in the next day or two were very high and that there were no apparent stumbling blocks. Caesars has a market value of about $4.5 billion and it had $4.5 billion in long-term debt in March. (Reuters)

Crude oil prices top $40 per barrel again

Crude oil prices closed at six-week highs above $40 a barrel after the US government in a weekly report said crude oil stockpiles in the United States declined last week. At the New York Mercantile Exchange, crude oil for August delivery closed $1.53 higher at $40.97. The day's high of $41.05 was the highest since June 2, when NYMEX crude hit a record $42.45. In London, August Brent crude rose $1.85 at $38.55. Potential oil supply disruptions out of the Mideast Gulf and threats of terror attacks on the United States and its allies continued to make oil markets jittery, with big speculative funds active, traders said. (Reuters)

United Airlines delays pension funding

United Airlines is deferring a required quarterly payment of $72.4 million to its employee pension funds, underscoring the pensions' vulnerability as the cash-strapped carrier seeks financing to exit bankruptcy. United said in a four-sentence filing with the US Securities and Exchange Commission that deferring the contribution enables it to ''best manage its resources and preserve its options going forward" as it works to secure the financing needed for it to emerge from Chapter 11. Spokeswoman Jean Medina called deferral a ''prudent, responsible step" and said it doesn't mean a decision has been made to stop pension contributions. ''We have until Sept. 15 of '05 to make these payments," she said, citing the final deadline. The union representing United ramp workers and public contact employees warned that it will fight any move by United to make the deferral permanent. (AP)

Court denies 113 cases against diet drug

Abbott Laboratories Inc. said a federal court has dismissed 113 cases in which plaintiffs alleged they were harmed by using the company's Meridia anti-obesity drug, the lion's share of all cases pending in the United States. Abbott said the US District Court in Cleveland last week ruled that the plaintiffs failed to come forward with sufficient scientific evidence to support the claims. Abbott spokeswoman Laureen Cassidy said only seven other product-liability cases remain pending against Meridia, all in various state courts. The public watchdog group, Public Citizen, had asked US regulators to recall the medicine, claiming it posed a variety of health risks. (Reuters)

Impact of overtime rules disputed

Disputing Bush administration estimates, a liberal think tank said new federal rules will remove overtime protections for at least 6 million US workers. The study by the Economic Policy Institute was released a day after three former Labor Department officials said in a report requested by the AFL-CIO that ''large numbers" of employees entitled to overtime would no longer get it when the new rules take effect Aug. 23. ''These latest studies are a rehash of misinformation that the AFL-CIO put out about the department's final overtime security rule in April, assertions that were completely discredited in congressional hearings," said Labor Department spokesman Ed Frank. On a party line vote yesterday, the House Appropriations Committee rejected an amendment by Representative David Obey, a Wisconsin Democrat, that would have prohibited implementing any portions of the new rule that would reduce the number of workers eligible for overtime. (AP)

THE WORLD
German company settles US charges

Bayer AG, Germany's second-biggest drug and chemical maker, agreed to pay $66 million to settle a US criminal charge it participated in a global conspiracy to fix prices of chemicals used to make rubber. Bayer agreed to assist the US investigation that has already netted the guilty plea of Crompton Corp., which was fined $50 million for its role in the cartel, the Justice Department. European Union and Canadian authorities also are investigating the cartel. Bayer agreed to plead guilty in federal court in San Francisco to one charge of conspiring with other participants to fix prices between 1995 and 2001. The guilty plea will expose Bayer to possible civil damages sought by tire makers and other purchasers of chemicals for making synthetic rubber. (Bloomberg)

Bank said to have helped conceal funds

A Washington bank with long ties to the diplomatic community helped former Chilean dictator Augusto Pinochet conceal his wealth and evade efforts to seize his assets, Senate investigators charge in a report. Riggs Bank managers, working with Pinochet from 1994 to 2002, set up phony offshore companies and hid the existence of his accounts from US examiners, according to the report. The US government recently fined Riggs a record $25 million for allegedly violating money laundering laws for the way it handled Saudi Embassy accounts and those held by officials of Equatorial Guinea. The Senate report also details a pattern of lapsed oversight by regulators. (AP)
