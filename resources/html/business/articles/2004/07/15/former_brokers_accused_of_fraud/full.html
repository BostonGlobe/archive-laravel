Former brokers accused of fraud

SEC complaint alleges $1.3 billion of illegal trades

By Beth Healy and Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  July 15, 2004

Using fake identities and other deceptive tactics to defraud 52 mutual fund firms, five former Prudential Securities brokers and their Boston manager made $1.3 billion worth of market-timing trades from 2001 to 2003, the Securities and Exchange Commission said yesterday in a new complaint.

The scope of the alleged improper trading was far larger than state or federal regulators previously had charged. For the first time, regulators said that the nation's largest mutual fund firm, Fidelity Investments, was hit by market timers.

Fidelity, like all the mutual fund firms named in the complaint, did not knowingly allow its funds to be market timed. The SEC said the Prudential brokers made $34.1 million worth of trades through several Fidelity funds. "If there has been any financial harm to our shareholders despite all of our significant efforts to combat market timing, we'd be interested in pursuing the matter," said Fidelity spokeswoman Anne Crowley.

The SEC filed the new complaint, which is several hundred pages long, after a federal judge last month dismissed the fraud charges for failing to be specific enough. Judge Reginald C. Lindsay gave the agency 30 days to refile.

"In response to Judge Lindsay's order, we have filed an amended complaint with additional particularity of the circumstance constituting fraud," said Walter Ricciardi, district administrator for the SEC's Boston office.

According to the complaint, the Prudential brokers, led by Martin J. Druffner, 35, orchestrated a scheme on behalf of seven hedge-fund clients to make thousands of overnight trades in portfolios of virtually all of the nation's largest mutual fund groups.

The AIM Funds were worst hit, as the brokers pumped $166 million worth of fund trades through the Houston firm. The Franklin Templeton funds of San Mateo, Calif., experienced $87.3 million of in-and-out trades from their funds, while Putnam Investments of Boston was 10th on the SEC's list, with $42.6 million in market timing. Spokesmen for the fund groups were unavailable for comment late yesterday.

Market timing, or rapidly trading into and out of funds, is not illegal but is prohibited by most fund companies, because short-term traders rack up expenses for ordinary investors, who hold funds for months or years at a time. Market timers typically deal in foreign-stock funds and junk bond funds, buying shares based on market news that has not yet been priced into the fund, and reselling the shares the next day to lock in quick profits.

Since September, when New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer kicked off a national investigation of market timing, 20 firms have been implicated and agreed to pay $2 billion in fines and restitution.

The government is not alleging any wrongdoing on the part of the fund companies in the Prudential case. Rather, the SEC painted the fund companies and their customers as the victims of five renegade brokers and their manager, Robert E. Shannon, who approved the timing activity. The brokers, by using scores of different account names, broker identification numbers, and even misspellings of their own names, the SEC said, made it "difficult for the fund companies to detect the clients' market timing, thus misleading the fund companies to process transactions they would otherwise have rejected."

The fund groups collectively sent Prudential more than 1,000 letters and e-mails, the SEC said, to try to block the timing.

The brokers, who included Druffner; Justin F. Ficken, 29; Skifter Ajro, 35; John S. Peffer, 41; Marc J. Bilotti, 34; and their manager, Shannon, 52; all resigned under pressure in September, after the Massachusetts Securities Division launched an investigation of the office. Lawyers for the former Prudential employees declined to comment late yesterday but in the past have insisted their clients did nothing wrong.

Prudential Securities, which became part of Wachovia Securities last July, had maintained until last fall, when the Globe detailed the market timing in the Boston office, that it did not permit such trading. But when Wachovia took over the Prudential brokerage offices, in a joint venture with the New Jersey insurer Prudential Financial Inc., Richmond-based Wachovia cracked down on the illicit trading.

In most of those cases, fund companies themselves were involved in the misdeeds. By contrast, the Prudential case involves a small group of brokers running a lucrative business for themselves and the company, by breaking the rules of many mutual fund companies, the SEC said.

"The broker defendants profited handsomely from their misconduct," the SEC said in its complaint. Between January 2001 and September 2003, the brokers' market timing generated more than $4.6 million in net commissions for Druffner, Ficken, and Ajro, and more than $600,000 in net commissions for Peffer and Bilotti, the SEC alleged.

At Fidelity, the brokers disguised their $34 million in trades for four hedge fund clients by opening 46 accounts, the SEC alleged. "In most instances, Fidelity blocked certain brokers . . . from making further purchases or exchanges in some or all of its funds," the regulators said. But the Prudential brokers continued to use other identification numbers to make trades after those restrictions were made. The Fidelity funds were the company's Advisor funds, which are sold through brokers.

Putnam, which has had its own improper-trading problem involving fund managers and a pension client, sent at least 10 letters or e-mails to Prudential, warning that it was restricting trading by those brokers, the SEC said. It also blocked outright certain accounts from making trades. In several instances, the brokers and their clients got around the blocks by making trades under different broker or account numbers.

The SEC said the hedge funds that ordered up the trades at Prudential were Chronos Asset Management of Cambridge, Ritchie Capital of Illinois, Jemmco Advisers of New York, and two British funds, Headstart Advisers Ltd. and Pentagon Asset Management. In order to hide millions of dollars in daily trades for those clients, the SEC said, the Prudential brokers broke the trades into small slices, requiring hundreds and even thousands of separate daily transactions. Druffner and his colleagues used 79 names for Headstart and 22 for Chronos, according to the complaint.

A managing director at Ritchie did not return a call seeking comment; no one was available to take calls at the other firms late yesterday.

Two other firms, both based in Salem, also were Prudential customers, the SEC said: Global Analytical Capital, an investment adviser for hedge funds based in the Netherlands; and Summa Capital, which acted as an investment adviser for a hedge fund based in the Cayman Islands.

The SEC is looking to force the former employees to return their profits from the illicit trading, and will seek civil penalties against them. The agency said its Prudential investigation is ongoing.
