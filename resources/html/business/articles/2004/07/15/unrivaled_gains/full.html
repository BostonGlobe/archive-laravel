Unrivaled gains

Bank of America dampens competitors' hopes of stealing big business after merger

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  July 15, 2004

Bank of America said yesterday it gained thousands of customers in the Northeast in the past three months, a troubling sign for rivals who had planned to steal business as a result of the bank's merger with FleetBoston Financial Corp.

Bank of America added more than 106,000 checking and savings accounts in the Fleet footprint from April to June, fueled in part by its earlier introduction of free checking and free online bill paying for customers.

"The merger is going very well," Bank of America's chief financial officer, Marc Oken, said in an interview yesterday after the bank released its second-quarter earnings. "We're upbeat and very optimistic. It's all positive at this point in time."

Most banks have historically lost customers during mergers, especially if the banks mess up customers' accounts or bungle the efforts to integrate computer systems. Rival banks also tend to ramp up their advertising and run special deals in hopes of winning customers from the merging bank.

Bank of America's gains provide an early indication of its strength as it begins to stitch together two of the largest banks in the nation. Still, it is far too early to count out rival banks, such as Citizens Financial Group and Sovereign Bancorp. Bank of America does not plan to change the Fleet signs on branches until later this year, and it will not convert the bank's computer systems -- the trickiest part of any bank merger -- until 2005.

Despite Bank of America's gains in the Northeast, some of its competitors also say they are winning new accounts. Boston's Eastern Bank, which ran a promotion through June offering customers $100 if they opened a new checking account with direct deposit and closed their old one, said it gained 5,400 accounts -- partly from Fleet -- during that time.

A spokeswoman for Citizens, the second-largest bank in New England, said it has "continued to gain checking and savings accounts," though she did not provide specifics. A Sovereign spokeswoman, Ellen Molle, said: "We've been very pleased with our results." But she declined to comment on whether the bank had gained or lost customers to Bank of America. The banks tally their own new checking account figures each quarter and often report them to shareholders and analysts.

But the idea that all of New England's major banks won customers brought disbelief from James E. Moynihan Jr., a bank stock specialist with Advest Inc.

"Everybody can't gain checking accounts," he said. "I don't see how that's possible. If they're gaining, someone else has to be losing."

One of Bank of America's biggest challenges in the coming months is to replace Fleet's brand with its own -- first in upstate New York, followed by the rest of the Fleet franchise, with Boston in December -- without disrupting the bank's gains. Already, the bank has put up its logo at Yankee Stadium and is changing the Fleet signs in Fenway Park. The bank also last week signed a sponsorship deal with Major League Baseball as the league's "official bank," and began a televised ad campaign during Tuesday's All-Star Game in Houston.

Bank of America has pledged to add more than 150,000 checking accounts in the Northeast by the end of the year, a significant jump from the 54,000 so far.

The bank also has taken several steps in recent months to persuade Wall Street to warm to its merger with Fleet. The bank told analysts in April that the merger would actually save more money than previously thought -- $1.4 billion instead of $1.1 billion.

To help meet those ambitious goals, the bank has said it will cut 12,500 jobs from its workforce, including more than 500 in Massachusetts. In the last three months alone, Bank of America has cut 3,800 jobs across the country, though some of them stemmed from a slowdown in mortgage refinancing and were separate from the Fleet merger.

The bank's chief executive, Kenneth D. Lewis, decided after he revealed the Fleet merger to work without an employment contract, a move some on Wall Street took as a sign the merger would work. The bank's stock has also been rising since the beginning of the year, though it closed yesterday at $84.30, down 83 cents.

While many analysts roundly criticized the $48 billion cost of the deal when it was proposed, some have since come around. Nancy Bush of NAB Research LLC said Bank of America's results in the Northeast show the deal was worth the expense.

"We didn't know how positive it would turn," she said.

The bank said yesterday its net income rose 41 percent to $3.9 billion, or $1.86 per share, from $2.7 billion, or $1.80 per share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 35 percent to $13.2 billion from $9.8 billion over the same period. The earnings from this year reflect the addition of Fleet, which Bank of America acquired this spring. Last year's results reflect Bank of America's earnings without Fleet.

During the second quarter, the bank benefited from strong consumer loan growth and domestic deposit growth, but were partially offset by lower large corporate and foreign loan balances.

Bank of America's gains in the Northeast were led by the New York-New Jersey area, where Fleet had pursued an aggressive expansion plan even before the merger. The bank also saw strong momentum in New England, said Anne Finucane, Bank of America's president of the Northeast.

Fleet had been gaining checking accounts and improving its customer service before its merger with Bank of America. But the Charlotte, N.C., bank accelerated the process by introducing the free checking suite, and retraining Fleet employees to put more emphasis on friendliness and building relationships with customers, she said.
