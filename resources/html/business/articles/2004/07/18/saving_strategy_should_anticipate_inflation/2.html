Saving strategy should anticipate inflation

July 18, 2004

Page 2 of 3 -- Now let's look at a much less gloomy picture involving the growth of your savings.

Assuming that all the options you hold will eventually vest and be exercised, your total savings (including the $60,000 in cash) now amount to $329,000. With your additions at current levels, plus your employer match, you would have $2,243,937 after 13 years, presuming average annual portfolio returns of 9 percent.

But suppose, as seems likely, that these options are not a one-time thing, and that each year your savings would grow by about $16,000 through the options program. If you factor that into the savings level, the accumulation would be $2,636,191.

Now, none of those figures approaches even the lowest level that we calculated you might need to retire comfortably. But there are two additional factors that can brighten the picture. One is that your dollar savings level will presumably rise during the next 13 years, reflecting anticipated higher earnings. These increases would swell the accumulation.

The second is the enormous investment you have in your home. Would you need to live in San Diego in retirement? Given that it is among the more expensive real estate markets in the country, you could sell your home there and replace it with a property of equal attractiveness in a less expensive real estate market, perhaps pocketing $1 million or more on the swap. Adding such an amount to your retirement nest egg could make a dramatic difference.

I presumed that your investment portfolio would grow at a 9 percent average annual rate. That would be unlikely if you hold large cash positions. Since you can see your way to financing your daughter's college education with $60,000 left in your cash account, why not put the lion's share of that $60,000 in a solid stock investment, such as shares in Vanguard Index 500 fund?

If my assumption is correct that you will continue to earn stock option grants from your employer, you will want to take care that your positions in your company's stock -- both through shares held in your 401(k) account and those obtained through the exercise of options -- don't take a dominant position in your retirement portfolio.

As your position in your company's stock builds, periodically sell off shares to assure that the position doesn't exceed a predetermined level, perhaps 20 to 25 percent. Nobody should overly rely on one common stock. Selling shares of an employer's stock isn't disloyalty; it's diversification.
