OVERSEERS & OVERSIGHTS

Regulators overstep with mutual fund trustees

By Beth Healy, Globe Staff  |  July 18, 2004

Second in a series of occasional articles.

On the day William P. Carmichael learned that most of his colleagues on the Nations Funds board of trustees were to be fired, he was 9,000 miles from home, vacationing in New Zealand.

The news was shocking, mostly because of who did the firing: Bank of America Corp., the institution based in Charlotte, N.C., that manages the Nations mutual funds.

By law, no bank or investment firm can dismiss fund trustees. It would be like a corporation's top executives trying to remove the board of directors; the hierarchy would be turned on its head. Just as corporate directors are responsible for selecting a company's top officers, at mutual funds the trustees have the ultimate authority. They decide who serves on the board. And they get to hire and fire investment firms.

Yet in the Nations Funds case, just the opposite happened, tossing trustee oversight into uncharted territory.

It was March 15, two days before Bank of America executives had to stand before stockholders and seek approval of their $47 billion acquisition of FleetBoston Financial Corp. For weeks, they had held secret negotiations with the New York State attorney general, Eliot Spitzer, and with the US Securities and Exchange Commission, over charges that the banking giant had permitted an elite client to pump money in and out of the mutual funds for three years, hurting ordinary investors.

Seeking to clear its name before the vote, the Bank of America agreed to pay a record $375 million in fines to settle the case, and pledged -- at the urging of the regulators -- to serve up the resignations of the fund's trustees.

Spitzer, whose office broke open the mutual fund scandal in September, warned of ''a new phase in the effort to clean up the mutual fund industry." And he put on notice the 3,000 trustees who serve on mutual fund boards across the country.

But in their zeal to punish fund companies, Spitzer and the SEC dealt a blow to the notion of trustee independence, some critics say. The Bank of America settlement was forged behind the backs of the trustees who are paid to serve as watchdogs over the funds. And the agreement was reached just as the SEC was rewriting key governance rules aimed at setting a higher standard for board oversight of the $7.5 trillion that Americans invest in mutual funds.

''If the SEC thought the directors did something wrong, they can dismiss them directly, not indirectly," said C. Meyrick Payne, a partner at Management Practice Inc., a firm based in Stamford, Conn., that consults to mutual fund boards.

Such a move would require bringing charges against the trustees, Payne said. That would be up to the SEC, he added, ''not the decision of the Bank of America."

The Bank of America agreement reveals that regulators went too far. Two senior officials of the agency declined to comment publicly, but acknowledged privately in interviews that the commissioners will face a conundrum when they meet this summer to give final approval to the bank's settlement: Either rebuke Spitzer and the SEC staff for trying to fire the Nations trustees improperly, or risk diluting the SEC's fledgling efforts to make trustees more accountable.

''There are a lot of people with a sort of crooked smile on their face, to see how the SEC extricates itself from this situation," said Harvey E. Bines, a partner with the Boston law firm of Sullivan & Worcester and a specialist in the investment business.

After years of little scrutiny, trustees are now under fire for sitting idly by amid the biggest mutual-fund scandal since the 1930s. An examination by The Boston Globe last month of the way trustees operate found that many are retirees who make six-figure pay for part-time work, and who do not stand up vigorously for shareholders on matters of fees, investment performance, and regulatory compliance.

At the Nations Funds, for instance, the trustees were paid an average of $121,000 each in 2003; their average age was 68. Before their problems surfaced last fall, they met quarterly, according to documents filed with the SEC.

Certainly, the Nations Funds trustees are not blameless in the misdeeds at the funds, according to regulators. In a meeting in May 2002, they voted to take action against short-term traders, called market timers. These traders buy and sell shares of foreign funds overnight, to lock in quick profits at the expense of other investors. The board instituted a 2 percent penalty fee for anyone holding shares less than 90 days, in order to dilute any profits from timing.

But at that same meeting, according to Spitzer's allegations, Nations Fund managers persuaded the trustees to give one elite client a free ride. According to the minutes of the meeting reviewed by Spitzer's investigators, Bank of America wanted to let a hedge fund, Canary Capital Partners, market-time at will, without paying the fees. The eight trustees present at the meeting (two missed the session) agreed to the arrangement, Spitzer said.

People who work with the Nations trustees say they believed the agreement, as presented by management, would not harm the funds. Until last fall, few trustees anywhere understood either market timing or the scope of the abuse in the industry. But the trustees did not challenge management on the request to break the rules.

The chairman of the trustees at the time was A. Max Walker, a former Merrill Lynch executive who had served on the Nations board for three decades. He died of cancer last August, at the age of 81, a month before Spitzer brought the charges. In March 2003, Walker was honored as ''Trustee of the Year" at a black-tie affair in New York City that was hosted by Institutional Investor magazine. The trade publication's annual spring awards event is a who's who of the industry; fund companies pay $10,000 a table to attend.

The board also included General Carl E. Mundy Jr., 68, a retired commandant of the US Marine Corps. There was a Duke University business professor, a trustee of the Duke endowment, and Cornelius J. Pings, 74, a retired engineering professor who lobbied Congress to protect patent rights for research universities as head of the Association of American Universities. Carmichael, 60, who succeeded Walker as chairman, is a former Sara Lee Corp. executive who serves on corporate boards and raises money for the Chicago Symphony and Indiana University, his alma mater.

They heard the news that they were to be fired as the rest of the world did -- on the Internet, on television, and on the radio. Carmichael was out of the country and could not be reached.

"To the best of my knowledge, there has been no contact with or deliberation with the board," Mundy, the trustee, said at the time. He expressed alarm that the bank would be permitted to dismiss trustees: ''I can't imagine what that might portend."

In October, shareholders received a letter signed jointly by Carmichael and H. Keith Winn, president of Bank of America's investment division, promising reforms and internal inquiries as a result of Spitzer's allegations. The trustees had no idea they were part of the bank's settlement negotiations, trustees said, and their newly hired lawyer was not consulted as part of the talks. The attorney who had advised the board for many years, Carl Frischling, had just stepped down due to a conflict: His firm, Kramer Levin Naftalis & Frankel, was representing Canary Capital, the hedge fund.

At Spitzer's office and at the SEC, investigators declined to say why they had not dealt with the trustees directly. They said they had assumed the bank had kept the trustees in the loop.

But that was not the case. On the day the settlement was unveiled, a bank spokesman, Robert Stickler, said of the trustees: ''There was an attempt to get to them this morning. I don't know if it was successful."

Weeks later, Bank of America's chief executive, Kenneth D. Lewis, did not back away from his company's agreement to see that the trustees were gone within a year. He said, ''I expect that the agreement will be followed."

Officials at the SEC said there were ''external pressures" to get the settlement done, not least among them the pending Bank of America-Fleet merger and Spitzer's ambition to shake up the fund business. Spitzer's staff agreed that he had led the charge in the case.

The SEC went along with his conclusions, lawyers there say, despite the mixed message it was sending on trustee independence.

''This vehicle gives us the opportunity to bring fresh blood to the board," one official at the agency said. In its news release, the SEC said that Bank of America had ''agreed to implement certain election and retirement procedures for the Nations Funds trustees that will result in the replacement of the Nations Funds trustees within one year."

Even as that agreement was hammered out, elsewhere at the SEC, staff members were pressing to give trustees more power in negotiating with fund companies. They argued that 75 percent of the trustees on every fund board must be free of links to the executives managing the funds, and they called for independent chairmen to oversee every board, a measure that firms like Fidelity Investments and Vanguard Group would fight until the commissioners passed it in June.

Officials for the regulatory agencies declined to comment on the handling of the situation in the Nations case. The Nations trustees have also been silent on their predicament, at the urging of their lawyer, except to say that they feel they fulfilled their duty to the shareholders.

Meanwhile, trustees around the country, traditionally passive and accustomed to privacy, have lashed out at the regulators. At a trustees conference in Palm Desert, Calif., in late March, the settlement was the subject of a panel discussion and a source of heated discussion among attendees. On April 22, officers of the Mutual Fund Directors Forum, a trade group for fund trustees, wrote SEC chief William H. Donaldson, urging agency commissioners to reject Bank of America's offer to fire the trustees.

''By accepting the proposition that Bank of America has the capacity to settle charges by causing the replacement of mutual fund trustees, the Commission would be suggesting that Bank of America, not the trustees, controls the funds," the trustees wrote. They sent the same letter to Spitzer in May, who has taken no action against other trustees.

Thomas C. Theobald, a Chicago executive who is chairman of the Columbia funds, a group of funds run by Fleet that is supposed to merge with the Nations Funds, said that his board was waiting to see what the SEC commissioners decide. If the regulators stand by the effort to force out the trustees, he said: ''Nobody knows what are the ramifications of that."
