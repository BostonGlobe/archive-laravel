BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Interest steep for Salada Tea Building

July 9, 2004

Bidding was strong for the purchase of 330 Stuart St., a 104,000-square-foot, 10-story office and retail building in the Back Bay. Although executives at Spaulding & Slye Colliers, who are handling the sale of what is known as the Salada Tea Building, would not comment, others in the industry said bidders included investors who would keep it as an office building, and those considering conversion to residential space. The restaurant Grill 23 & Bar is on the first floor of the building, at the corner of Berkeley Street. A list of finalists among bidders is expected in about two weeks. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

Putnam names head of investments

Putnam Investments named Steve Oristaglio to be sole head of investments after Charles ''Ed" Haldeman, who was cohead, was promoted to president and chief executive following the departure of Lawrence Lasser. Oristaglio, 48, a senior managing director, has been with the Boston-based unit of Marsh & McLennan Cos. since 1998 and was elevated to cohead of investments a year and a half ago, spokeswoman Laura McNamara said. Haldeman, 55, will focus on ''overseeing the overall business," she said. Putnam, the seventh-largest US mutual fund company, also named Kevin Cronin, a managing director and chief investment officer for fixed income, to the position of deputy head of investments. (Bloomberg)

Boston Scientific starts filter trials

Boston Scientific Corp. said it is testing whether a special net-like filter can trap tiny blood clots and reduce complications that arise from procedures to clear clogged heart arteries. Natick-based Boston Scientific said it has begun a 500-patient clinical trial of heart attack patients who are having their arteries cleared to restore blood flow to the heart. Patients in the trial will undergo angioplasty, a procedure in which a tiny tube is inserted into an artery in the thigh and fished through the blood vessels to the heart. A balloon is then inflated to restore blood supply, and a tiny wire-mesh tube, or stent, is frequently inserted to prop open the vessel. The trial, which will take place in 60 US cities and 20 international locations, will evaluate the FilterWire embolic protection system's ability to trap clot-causing debris. (Reuters)

Retailer's shares fall after profit update

Talbots Inc. shares fell 15 percent after the company, which sells women's and children's clothing, said second-quarter profit would be hurt if slower sales seen in June persist during July. Shares of Hingham-based Talbots, which operates 1,001 stores in the United States, Canada, and the United Kingdom, dropped $5.91, or 15.1 percent, to $33.28. The company said it expects second-quarter profit of 33 to 35 cents ''if the slowdown in customer traffic that we experienced later in June continues throughout" July when Talbots hosts a semiannual clearance event to sell the vast majority of its remaining spring and summer inventory. Talbots was expected to earn 38 cents in the second quarter, the average estimate of 15 analysts surveyed by Thomson Financial. The company earned 32 cents a share in the same period a year earlier. (Bloomberg)

State Street picks up 2 funds to manage

State Street Corp., which has $1.2 trillion in assets, has become the investment manager of two renamed exchange-traded funds. State Street will manage the StreetTracks Stoxx 50 and StreetTracks Euro Stoxx 50 funds, the Boston-based company said. The funds have been offered by the Fresco Index Shares Funds brand and managed by UBS Global Asset Management Inc. Shareholders approved the management change last month. Exchange-traded funds are based on indexes and enable investors to buy and sell an entire portfolio of stocks as a single security. State Street is the world's second-largest manager of ETF funds, data compiled by Bloomberg show. The company has $63 billion in ETF assets, including $55 billion in the United States. (Bloomberg)

Chain settles sweepstakes case with N.Y.

CVS Corp., the second-largest US drugstore chain, reached a settlement with New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer's office over claims that a sweepstakes didn't allow some shoppers to enter. CVS agreed to change how it displays entry forms and also will pay $77,000 in civil penalties and costs, Spitzer's office said. The contest for a trip to Hawaii didn't allow entries unless customers bought photo prints and used a store coupon card to make the purchase, Spitzer's office said. ''Companies that conduct in-store sweepstakes must, according to state law, provide a level playing field for those customers who do not make purchases," Spitzer said. A spokesman for Woonsocket, R.I.-based CVS didn't return a phone call. (Bloomberg)

Ground Round franchise rights awarded

A judge has awarded a group of Ground Round franchisees the bankrupt restaurant chain's franchise rights, despite objections from a Dallas real estate company that made a higher bid. US Bankruptcy Judge William Hillman approved the franchisees' $4.85 million bid over protests from U.S. Restaurant Properties, which bid $6.5 million. Hillman said his decision was influenced by the recommendation of the previous owner's lawyers to accept the lower bid because it would avoid potential claims for more than $40 million filed by the franchisees, who together own 69 Ground Round restaurants nationwide. Hillman's decision shifts the headquarters of the chain from Braintree to Freeport, Maine. (AP)

Owner to renovate Fore River shipyard

Car dealer Daniel Quirk, who owns the Fore River shipyard and stores about 3,000 cars there, wants to renovate the facility to be able to handle commercial cargo ships. ''The waterfront is probably one of the most valuable assets of the shipyard," Quirk told The Patriot Ledger of Quincy. ''(Using) that waterfront is essential." The more than 100-acre former General Dynamics shipyard that sits on the Quincy-Braintree line has virtually been shuttered since closing in 1987. A mid-1990s shipbuilding venture at the yard failed. The shipyard is one of 11 designated port areas in Massachusetts, which means all development there must be water-dependent unless the state approves another use. (AP)

THE NATION
Mortgage rates fall for 3d straight week

Rates on 30-year and 15-year mortgages dropped for a third straight week to levels that home buyers haven't been able to take advantage of since the spring. Freddie Mac said in its weekly nationwide survey that rates on benchmark 30-year, fixed-rate mortgages fell to 6.01 percent this week, down from 6.21 percent last week. It was the lowest level since mid-April. Thirty-year rates hit a low this year of 5.38 percent the week ending March 18. Rates for 15-year, fixed-rate mortgages dropped to 5.42 percent this week, down from 5.62 percent last week. It was the lowest level since the end of April. Rates on one-year adjustable rate mortgages also were down, falling to 4.05 percent from 4.19 percent the previous week. (AP)

THE WORLD
China ends tax break, settles WTO case

China has agreed to end a tax break that the United States said discriminated against US and other foreign semiconductor makers, settling a high-profile dispute and possibly easing trade tensions between the nations. The Bush administration praised the deal as a victory in its efforts to level the economic playing field with the Asian nation. The accord ends the first-ever World Trade Organization case against China. US chip makers argued that China, already the world's third-largest chip market, was using the preferential tax treatment to unfairly support its bid to become a major supplier of semiconductors. (Los Angeles Times)
