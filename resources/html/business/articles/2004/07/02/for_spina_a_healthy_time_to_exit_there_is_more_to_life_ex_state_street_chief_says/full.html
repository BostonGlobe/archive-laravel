For Spina, a healthy time to exit 'There is more to life,' ex-State Street chief says

By Beth Healy, Globe Staff  |  July 2, 2004

David Spina says it's not about the money any more.

ADVERTISEMENT

The 61-year-old spent his entire career on a path leading him toward chief executive of State Street Corp., a job he finally won in 2000 -- and resigned from suddenly on Wednesday. A heart attack in May 2003 changed everything, he said. While recuperating from bypass surgery last spring at his vacation home in New Hampshire, Spina said, his tennis buddies asked him, ''Why are you going back, anyway?"

He was back at the office by late July. Working at the Boston financial services company was something he'd done every weekday for 34 years.

Spina in 1969 joined what was then a much smaller company, engaged largely in banking. He came straight from a tour of duty in Vietnam as a Navy officer. The Holy Cross graduate earned his MBA at Harvard Business School in his early years as a banker and by 1976 was spotted as a rising star at State Street. He became assistant to the chief executive at the time and went on to be chief financial officer -- a numbers man in a bright bowtie -- before rising to the number two job of president, under former chief Marshall Carter.

In his four years as chief executive, Spina oversaw the toughest period at the company in two decades and was well-paid for his work: He made more than $20 million between 2000 and the end of 2003 and leaves with stock and options valued at about $100 million. On top of that, he's due $1.4 million in annual retirement checks, according to the company's March proxy statement. His full exit package will be detailed in State Street's next quarterly filing with regulators.

Though Spina said he's in good health today, the heart trouble jarred him. ''It's one of those things that provokes deep thinking -- something that commercial bankers aren't often known for," Spina joked yesterday. ''I love my desk. But there is more to life."

Spina said he plans to take the summer off, then go cycling in Austria with his wife in September. He's traveled the world as a businessman, he said, but, ''I want to see it from the parks and the woods and the museums, instead of from the offices and hotel dining rooms."

His departure surprised investors Wednesday, when State Street announced Spina's retirement after the market had closed. Past successions at the company, which handles recordkeeping and accounting for $9.4 trillion of mutual funds and pensions and also invests $1.2 trillion, have been public and long-planned. State Street shares fell 1 percent yesterday morning before rebounding to $49.08, roughly flat for the day.

''We had all thought that he'd be around for two to three more years," said Lee Forker, president of the Boston investment firm New England Research & Management. He said Spina made some mistakes but had grown into a strong leader who helped bring in new business after two difficult years in 2001 and 2002, when the stock market was in a nosedive.

A private man, Spina telegraphed his thoughts of an early retirement only to a small circle of people.

The board was not surprised by the news, two directors said, and had begun mulling a successor for Spina almost immediately after his surgery last year. For the past few months, a director and several executives said, it was clear that Ronald E. Logue, State Street's president, was the leading candidate to take the reins.

Late on Wednesday afternoon, Spina summoned the members board for a special meeting, rather than wait for a scheduled meeting later this month and risk letting word leak out. Many of the directors attended by telephone conference. Spina said he was retiring immediately, and the board named Logue chairman and chief executive.

Logue, 58, who has served on State Street's board since 2000, said in an interview that after Spina's health incident, ''We ramped up the succession process, obviously, and the board spent more time thinking about that. This just didn't happen yesterday."

A South Shore native with two degrees from Boston College, Logue joined State Street in 1990 from the former Bank of New England. He has been a vice chairman since 1999 and was on the short list of executives considered to become CEO when Spina got the job. Logue has been president and chief operating officer since late 2001 and is a known face of State Street among Wall Street analysts and investment managers.

Carter, the former State Street chief executive, said Logue has been well-groomed for the top post at the company. ''His strengths are 25 years of in-depth knowledge of the technical aspects of State Street's biggest business, which is mutual-fund servicing," Carter said. He added that Logue also is familiar with sales, marketing, systems, and operations at the company, and was intricately involved in the recent acquisition of Deutsche Bank's securities-services business.

Logue takes over after a turbulent period for the company. The $1.5 billion Deutsche deal, for instance, was a departure for a company that hadn't ever done a major buyout. Also last year, the company offered an employee buyout, in a bid to save $125 million. When a throng signed up -- 3,000 instead of the 1,800 the company was hoping for, State Street was caught off guard. Even the company's general counsel took the offer. By July, the company said it needed to hire 800 to 1,000 new people to refill vacant posts. Severance packages for the departing employees cost the company nearly $300 million, almost double the $175 million charge it had expected.

Another costly move on Spina's watch was the decision to lease new downtown office space at One Lincoln St. The company signed a 20-year lease for more than 1 million square feet of space in 2000, at what turned out to be the market's peak. Logue moved into the new building seven months ago and will stay there, a spokeswoman said, in a departure from the tradition of State Street's CEO keeping an office at 225 Franklin St., the building with the company's name emblazoned on top.

Spina said he'll probably sell a portion of his shares by the end of the year, ''to get my financial assets diversified." But he said he will remain a significant stockholder ''for a long time."
