BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Ex-Fidelity officer to run SEC risk office

July 2, 2004

The Securities and Exchange Commission named Charles Fishkin, a former Fidelity Investments executive, as director of its new Office of Risk Assessment, which aims to help the agency do a better job of foreseeing problems in the investment industry and gather information from SEC staff and industry executives so it can stay abreast of developing abuses. Last year's market timing scandal in mutual funds, for instance, took the SEC by surprise. Fishkin, 44, of Wellesley, was vice president of risk at Boston-based Fidelity. (Beth Healy)

Neiman Marcus to open in Natick Mall

Neiman Marcus will be one of two anchor stores -- the other being Nordstrom -- for a planned addition to the Natick Mall. The Dallas retailer plans to open a two-level, 100,000-square-foot store in 2007. The mall aims to attract higher-end retailers that will appeal to the area's increasingly affluent shoppers. Dubbed Natick Mall 2, the expansion will add 550,000 square feet. General Growth Properties Inc., the mall's owner, hopes to attract four restaurants and 65 other retailers. In May, Nordstrom disclosed its plan to open at the mall. Neiman Marcus has 35 stores, including one in Boston. (Naomi Aoki)

Ilex shareholders OK Genzyme buyout

Shareholders of San Antonio's Ilex Oncology Inc. approved its $1 billion acquisition by Genzyme Corp. in a merger the Federal Trade Commission is still reviewing. The companies, which are both developing cancer treatments, said an overwhelming majority of votes cast at a meeting in Boston favored the all-stock deal, disclosed Feb. 26. The deal was structured so Genzyme shareholders do not vote. The board of Cambridge's Genzyme has already approved the merger, which the companies expect to close this summer. (AP)

Microsoft settles with Vt., Minn.

Microsoft Corp. will give $184.2 million in software vouchers to consumers in Minnesota and Vermont to settle lawsuits claiming people were overcharged for the Windows operating system. A Minnesota state judge gave preliminary approval to a $174.5 million settlement reached in April. The Vermont lawsuit was settled for $9.7 million. Microsoft has agreed to pay at least $1.8 billion to end consumer antitrust suits in 13 states and the District of Columbia. The amount of the Minnesota settlement had not been previously disclosed. Microsoft has also settled suits in Arizona for $104 million and Massachusetts for $34 million. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Terror, supply fears push crude price up

The price of crude briefly jumped above $39 a barrel in a rally fueled by concern about heating oil supplies and fear of a possible Fourth of July terrorist attack. After climbing as high as $39.10, light crude for August delivery settled at $38.74, or $1.69 higher, on the New York Mercantile Exchange. On London's International Petroleum Exchange, Brent crude gained $1.57 to $36.07. The initial spark for the buying, analysts said, was the upcoming long weekend -- a frequent catalyst as traders hedge bets against unforeseen events. "Then it just started feeding on itself," said Ed Silliere, a trader who says the concern about heating oil is overblown. (AP)

Stent maker delays application

Guidant Corp., the third-largest maker of heart stents, will delay for six to eight months its application for further testing of its Champion drug-coated device. The Indianapolis company plans to file an application in early 2005. In May, it said structural defects in the stainless steel device would delay the application. Guidant still plans to begin selling the product in Europe by mid-2005 and is trying to catch Boston Scientific Corp. and Johnson & Johnson in the $5 billion-a-year stent market. Stents prop open cleared arteries, and studies have shown that drug coatings help keep them open. (Bloomberg)

SEC split on shareholder nominations

The Securities and Exchange Commission is split on a plan to let shareholders nominate directors, stalling a change championed by chairman William Donaldson. Democratic commissioners Harvey Goldschmid and Roel Campos support the measure; Republicans Cynthia Glassman and Paul Atkins are trying to modify it. Donaldson will cast the deciding vote on an issue that could define his tenure, said Sarah Teslik, executive director at the Council of Institutional Investors. The SEC voted 3-2 last week to require mutual funds boards to have independent chairmen. Donaldson voted with the two Democrats to pass the rule. The shareholder proposal would let investors with at least 5 percent of a company's shares nominate a candidate to challenge a director who received a 35 percent "no vote" in annual elections. It is opposed by business groups. Pfizer Inc. chief executive Henry McKinnell said it would bring "the worst of American politics" into boardrooms. (Bloomberg)

Greenspan's holdings unchanged in '03

Federal Reserve chairman Alan Greenspan essentially treaded water in terms of his investments in 2003, which were little changed from 2002. A disclosure form released by the Fed showed the value of his assets was between $3 million and $6.4 million, nearly the same as in 2002. (The figures need only be given in broad ranges.) Greenspan has kept all his holdings in money market accounts and Treasury securities, considered the world's safest investments, to avoid any appearance of conflict of interest that might be raised by stock holdings. Greenspan was paid a $171,900 salary last year. His wife, NBC News correspondent Andrea Mitchell, had separate holdings valued at between $1.3 million and nearly $3 million. Her holdings include stock. (AP)

THE WORLD
European bank leaves rates unchanged

The European Central Bank left interest rates unchanged, with president Jean-Claude Trichet saying more growth is on the way, along with potential risks of higher inflation -- a clear recipe for rate increases. But the bank faces a different situation than does the US Federal Reserve, which raised interest rates Wednesday: Europe's economic recovery is still considered fragile, and a rate increase could blunt or halt it. (AP)

Russian oil firm fears bankruptcy

The Russian oil company Yukos was slapped with another huge tax bill after it warned it could be bankrupted by $3.4 billion in back taxes it must pay within five days. The Interfax news agency quoted a tax ministry source as saying Yukos owed $3.4 billion in back taxes for 2001, almost identical to the bill for 2000. The company said a court ordered its bank accounts frozen. "That means in five days we are going to be unable to carry out our operations properly," spokesman Alexander Shadrin said. News of the freeze came after the market close, but Yukos shares had already lost 12 percent of their value on fears the new tax bill would drive the company into state hands. (Reuters)
