BUSINESS IN BRIEF

GTech gets SEC subpoena for documents

July 30, 2004

GTech Holdings Corp., the world's biggest seller of computerized lottery systems, received a subpoena from the US Securities and Exchange Commission for documents on its Brazilian operations. The investigation involves allegations of wrongdoing during negotiations for a contract extension, the West Greenwich, R.I.-based company said. GTech has said that it acted appropriately and that it is cooperating with the probe. GTech said in May its contracts to provide services to Caixa Economica Federal, a Brazilian bank and operator of Brazil's national lottery, were under scrutiny by the SEC and Brazilian regulators. In March, federal attorneys with Brazil's Public Ministry recommended that criminal charges be brought against GTech and Caixa executives. (Bloomberg)

State Street in French government pact

State Street Global Advisors, the world's largest investment manager for institutional clients, won a contract to manage currency risk and provide financial advice for France's state pension fund. The contract will run for three years, the Fonds de Reserve pour les Retraites fund said. It didn't give financial details. The fund has been set up to plug future shortfalls in France's state pension finances. France is following Ireland and Norway in setting aside revenue to curb looming gaps in state pension funds as birth rates decline and life expectancy increases. Today, France has two taxpayers for every pensioner. In 2040, there will be only one. (Bloomberg)

Dow Chemical to close R.I. drug plant

Citing changes in the biotech industry, Dow Chemical Co. will close its drug manufacturing facility in Smithfield, R.I., the company said. The closure eliminates about 62 jobs, and represents a setback for the state's fledgling biotechnology industry. Dow said it will continue to develop its biopharmaceutical manufacturing business, but will do so by expanding its efforts at a San Diego site, and through partnerships with other companies. Dow plans to stop operations at the facility by September, and is exploring selling the property. (AP)

Regulators investigating Krispy Kreme

Krispy Kreme Doughnuts Inc., the second-biggest US doughnut chain, said the Securities and Exchange Commission is investigating how the company accounted for the repurchase of franchises and earnings forecasts. Shares fell $2.95, or 15.8 percent, to $15.71 after the company disclosed the informal SEC probe. Chief executive Scott Livengood has been buying stores back from franchisees in such markets as Michigan and Northern California, with some investors and analysts questioning the prices paid for those purchases. Krispy Kreme, which had its first loss as a public company in the last quarter, cut its annual profit forecast in May, triggering its biggest share drop ever. Livengood said Krispy Kreme is "confident in our practices" and the company will "cooperate fully" with the SEC inquiry. The probe "generally concerns" franchise reacquisitions and its "previously announced reduction in earnings guidance," the company said. (Bloomberg)

Computer maker sees fewer job losses

IBM Corp., the world's largest computer maker, expects to lose about 2,000 of its US workers when their jobs are moved overseas, fewer than the 3,000 employees previously forecast. The company is offering new positions to employees whose jobs are transferred overseas, a company spokesman said. IBM is moving some US jobs to lower-wage countries such as China, India, and Brazil. Computer programmers in India earn about $12,500 a year, one-sixth the average US programmer's salary, according to a study by Forrester Research Inc. A union-backed proposal to study whether the company's pay policies encourage sending jobs to offshore contractors failed to win shareholder approval in April. (Bloomberg)

Mortgage rates rise 1st time in 5 weeks

Mortgage rates rose for the first time in five weeks, according to Freddie Mac, a large purchaser of mortgages. The average was 6.08 percent for a 30-year-fixed-rate mortgage for the week ending July 29, up from 5.98 percent last week. "Mortgage rates rose a little this week in response to expectations that the Federal Reserve Board is willing to move more aggressively if inflation should become an issue," Freddie Mac economist Frank Nothaft said in a statement. (Chris Reidy)

Parent to close Parade of Shoes stores

Payless ShoeSource Inc., the biggest US shoe seller, said it will stop operating its 181 Parade of Shoes stores by the end of its fiscal year in January. Payless, whose sales have fallen for three consecutive years, said it won't comment on the financial impact until its second-quarter conference call with analysts and investors Aug. 12. Payless is leaving the midprice market to concentrate on selling discount shoes, chief executive Steven J. Douglass said. A company spokesman didn't return a call seeking comment. Parade has stores in 23 states. (Bloomberg)

4 firms get 77% of mutual fund inflows

Capital Group Cos., Vanguard Group, Barclays Global Investors, and Fidelity Investments attracted 77 percent of the money invested in US stock and bond mutual funds in the first half of 2004, Financial Research Corp. said. The biggest outflows occurred at Putnam Investments, the money-management unit of Marsh & McLennan Cos., and Janus Capital Group Inc., according to Financial Research, a Boston-based firm that tracks money flows. Capital Group, the Los Angeles-based parent of American Funds, accounted for 31 percent of net new investments in the first six months, with $49.9 billion. Pennsylvania-based Vanguard attracted $35.8 billion, or 22.2 percent; Barclays received $20.6 billion, or 12.8 percent; and Boston-based Fidelity got $17.8 billion, or 11 percent. Overall, $161.2 billion poured into stock and bond funds during the first six months, up 25 percent from a year earlier. Putnam shareholders have pulled $14.8 billion this year, including $2.4 billion in June, the company's 37th consecutive month of withdrawals. Janus, which also has had outflows every month since June 2001, has had $10.5 billion withdrawn this year. (Bloomberg)

CEO shakes up corporate hierarchy

Tom Freston shook up the ranks of the businesses under his leadership at Viacom Inc., promoting one executive while another resigned in the first big overhaul since he became copresident and cochief operating officer in June. Bob Bakish, MTV's head of ad sales and strategy, is being elevated to the new role of executive vice president. He will help Freston set budgets and strategies for the parts of Viacom under Freston's control: book publisher Simon & Schuster; music publishing, theme parks, cable networks including MTV, VH1, and Nickelodeon, and the Paramount movie studio. He will also assume control of Viacom's music publishing and theater businesses from Tom McGrath, who is leaving as part of the reorganization. (Bloomberg)

Apple at odds with RealNetworks

Apple Computer Inc. responded to RealNetworks' creation of iPod-compatible software by calling it the technological equivalent of breaking and entering. "We are stunned that RealNetworks has adopted the tactics and ethics of a hacker to break into the iPod, and we are investigating the (legal) implications of their actions," Apple said. RealNetworks Inc. said over the weekend that it had developed software that allows songs purchased from its online music store to transfer to Apple's iPod. An Apple spokesman did not return phone calls seeking comment. (AP)
