QUARTERLY MUTUAL FUNDS

In scandal fallout, small firms bear big burdens

By Matthew Keenan, Bloomberg News  |  July 4, 2004

Hennessy Advisors Inc., a California money manager, cut an administrative assistant job to help pay for hiring a government-required compliance officer after last year's US mutual-fund trading scandals.

Ariel Capital Management LLC, a Chicago-based manager of $5.5 billion in mutual funds, added three workers to its 74-employee payroll in the last six months to monitor legal issues. Muhlenkamp & Co. in Wexford, Pa., manager of the $1.2 billion Muhlenkamp Fund, raised investor fees by 0.8 percent last year to pay for congressional mandates and is girding for more demands from the US Securities and Exchange Commission.

''Everything's going up for what a handful of people did wrong," said Neil Hennessy, 48, president of Hennessy Advisors, which is based in Novato, Calif., and manages $1.2 billion. ''It's no cheap date."

As big mutual fund companies, including Putnam Investments and Janus Capital Group Inc., pay $2.3 billion in fines and reduced fees to settle enforcement actions, smaller firms are starting to pay a price of their own. In December, the SEC issued a rule requiring more compliance personnel, and forthcoming regulations may also mean higher computer and software costs.

Smaller firms may be put at a disadvantage should expenses become too burdensome, according to Merrill Lynch & Co. analyst Guy Moszkowski. The pressure may even lead to some consolidation in the $7.6 trillion mutual-fund industry, he recently wrote.

More than 270 of the 375 mutual fund firms that are members of the Investment Company Institute, a Washington-based trade group, manage less than $10 billion apiece. Combined, their assets come to about half that of the industry leader, Fidelity Investments, which has some $860 billion in fund assets among the more than $1 trillion it manages.

Don Phillips, managing director of Morningstar Inc., a Chicago-based mutual-fund rating firm, said small companies approach the business with a different mindset since they're often operated by the money managers who founded them.

''They love money management, and they don't like all of the red tape," Phillips, 42, said. ''They're seeing more bureaucracy surrounding mutual funds, and much of the bureaucracy is the result of the failures of the big firms. And now they feel that, 'Hey, now we're picking up the burden for this.' "

In investigations begun last year, the SEC, New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer, and other authorities found deals permitting market timing, or frequent trading that hurts long-term investors. Other firms allowed late trading, in which investors made illegal transactions after fund prices were set each day. Regulators reached separate settlements with individual companies.

To prevent similar abuses, the SEC is requiring each fund company to designate, by Oct. 5, a chief compliance officer who reports to fund trustees. The officer would review a firm's policies to ensure that they meet federal securities laws. Smaller companies say they'll have to hire additional help to fulfill that requirement.

The SEC is also considering other rule changes. One would require a larger percentage of independent fund trustees. A second would limit fees charged to investors to defray marketing costs and broker compensation.

Another proposed rule would limit ''soft-dollar" arrangements under which funds have paid brokers extra commissions, often not disclosed to investors, in exchange for research and other services. The SEC is studying whether the payments buy legitimate research.

The SEC may also require electronic proof of the time a trade is made and new fees for short-term, in-and-out trades. Both would require upgrades to computer systems.

Thomas Putnam, chairman of the Investment Company Institute's Small Funds Committee, warned the US Senate Banking Committee in March against ''a one-size-fits-all approach," saying companies managing smaller asset pools would be disproportionately affected.

''Small fund groups, with their smaller asset bases and thinner profit margins, may find that the considerable costs associated with [regulatory] changes are prohibitive," Putnam, 60, testified. He founded and runs Fenimore Asset Management Inc. in Cobleskill, N.Y., a manager of $700 million in mutual funds.

The May report by Merrill's Moszkowski estimated that adoption of the most stringent rules being considered in Washington could cut the average profit margin of mutual fund companies he researches to 27 percent from 34 percent.

Moszkowski said he expected lesser measures to be adopted, with margins trimmed by as little as 2 percentage points. Larger companies have ''scale, research, and compliance budgets" to match the demands of regulation and can absorb new expenses if the industry can cut costs, he wrote.

The more profit a fund company earns, the more easily it can absorb additional costs. Franklin Resources Inc., which is based in San Mateo, Calif., and is the biggest publicly traded US mutual fund firm, last year reported net income of $502.8 million. Most small, independent companies aren't publicly traded and don't reveal their profits.

The SEC itself cites the potential costs of its actions. In making its December proposal on compliance officers, the agency said, ''The new rules will result in some additional costs for funds and investment advisers, which, in the case of funds, we would expect to be passed on to investors."

Ronald Muhlenkamp, 60, who manages the Muhlenkamp Fund, raised fees last year because of regulatory changes mandated by Congress under the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. The law requires more detailed reports from mutual fund companies.

Total fees in his funds now come to 1.18 percent of assets, up from 1.17 percent. The increase, which amounts to $1 per $10,000 invested, was necessary to cover $84,000 in extra administrative, legal, and communications costs, Muhlenkamp said. He's concerned he'll have to hire more compliance help and install at least one more trustee because of mandates growing out of the fund scandals.

''That's the first installment," Muhlenkamp said. ''That ain't the end of it."

Hennessy has refused to raise investor costs. Instead, he dropped the administrative assistant job to help pay for a compliance chief, keeping total employees at 10.

He also removed two fund trustees, including his brother, to meet the percentage of independent trustees proposed by the SEC.

''I said, 'I love you dearly, Brian, but you're gone, you're fired,' " Hennessy said.

Dropping marketing fees could stunt Ariel's growth, said president Mellody Hobson, 35. Ninety percent of its sales come through such fund supermarkets as Charles Schwab Corp. that charge as much as 0.4 percent of assets for ''shelf space." Ariel charges investors 0.25 percent to partially defray those costs.

''It's just a way for small companies to get the opportunity to compete with the big guys," Hobson said of the marketing fees.

Hennessy said he worries about an elimination of soft-dollar arrangements that add commissions in return for research. A ban, he said, would ''squeeze" his company, which made $1.1 million in net income in its latest fiscal year, according to an SEC filing.

He said he objects to companies that, caught in scandal, now urge changes that could hurt him. Boston-based MFS Investment Management, which paid $401 million to settle SEC and state complaints about its sales and trading practices, later announced the elimination of soft-dollar arrangements in a press release titled ''New Standards for the Mutual Fund Industry."

''Since when do [violators] start to make the rules?" Hennessy said.

The ramifications of the scandals could include more mergers and acquisitions. Investment bank Berkshire Capital Corp., based in New York, said in a report that smaller firms might look for partners or sell rather than face the difficulties of competing as ''independent or subscale" outfits.

Geoff Bobroff, an industry consultant in East Greenwich, R.I., said most smaller firms will adapt to increased regulation by hiring outside specialists.

New York-based Bisys Group Inc., which provides administrative services to financial firms, began offering a compliance-officer program in April. By this week, nine existing clients had hired Bisys to fill the role, the company said.

Guinness Atkinson Asset Management LLC, which manages three funds with a total of $225 million in assets, plans to hire a law firm to fill the compliance requirement. Scandal-related costs will be minimal and will result in beneficial changes for shareholders, said Jim Atkinson, 46, chief executive of the Glendora, Calif. firm.

''This is unpleasant," Atkinson said. ''Somebody else misbehaved, and we're getting spanked for it. But that's life."
