For investors in mutual funds, it was a quarter of ho-hum gains

By Bill Deener, Knight Ridder  |  July 4, 2004

DALLAS -- If this continues, mutual fund investors might count on reaching their retirement goals somewhere around age 95.

The minuscule second-quarter gain in the average large-cap growth fund -- one of the largest fund categories -- is hardly worth mentioning, at 0.2 percent. Combine that tiny profit with the first quarter's take and investors still have to squint to see the 1.4 percent gain for the first half of the year, according to Lipper Inc.

However, just because the market as a whole didn't move much, that doesn't mean nothing happened. As the economic recovery accelerated, some types of stocks hit a turning point. Several of the mutual fund categories that had been soaring -- small-capitalization stocks, real estate, and gold, for example -- lost ground.

Not surprisingly, with the major stock indexes meandering, investors curbed their enthusiasm for mutual funds. Investors put an estimated $24.1 billion into stock funds in the second quarter, compared with $85.8 billion in the first quarter, according to TrimTabs.com, which tracks fund flows.

''The market was really stuck," said John Linehan, portfolio manager of T. Rowe Price Value fund. ''It was very hard to make an argument for expanding stock prices given all the uncertainties."

His fund ended the quarter up about 2 percent, which was slightly better than the average large-cap value fund, with a 1 percent gain.

The average US stock fund -- a category that includes more than 6,000 large-cap, mid-cap, and small-cap funds -- eked out a 0.09 percent return for the quarter and was up 3.1 percent year-to-date, according to Lipper figures.

Within this broad category, the best performers were the funds that mirror the Standard & Poor's 500 index. These funds returned an average of 1.5 percent, besting the actively managed funds. That's not a big surprise because historically about 75 percent of active managers don't beat the indexes.

This quarter's worst-performing category among all US stock funds was small-cap growth, which fell 3.8 percent. This category remains barely positive for the year, up only 0.04 percent. Small-caps are retreating basically because they were on a terrific run last year and need a breather, said Jeff Tjornehoj, research analyst at Lipper.

Even with the second quarter's downturn, small-cap growth remains up 23 percent over the last 12 months. Small-cap companies typically do well during the first stages of an economic recovery, which occurred in 2003, but then as the recovery advances, these companies retreat, he said.

''Small caps grow fast in a recovery because a little extra revenue has a big impact on their profits," Tjornehoj said. ''But now, given that the first year of the recovery is past, investors are beginning to take profits and rethink their allocation to this sector."

Two other sectors that performed well last year -- real estate and technology -- also took a drubbing in the second quarter. The average real estate fund dropped 5 percent, making it the worst-performing category among the sector funds. And technology funds declined 2.3 percent.

The commercial real estate sector caught fire more than a year ago in anticipation of the economic recovery, which created more demand for office and retail space, apartments, and hotels.

Like the small-cap sector, however, some of the quarter's downturn is simply due to profit-taking, said David Lee, portfolio manager of the T. Rowe Price Real Estate fund.

His fund, for example, gained a sparkling 12.1 percent in the first quarter, so this quarter's 4.3 percent reversal is palatable.

''We went over a year where we had several consecutive quarters of positive growth," Lee said. ''It would be rare for any investment to just move in one direction."

He said, though, that demand for office space has been lagging throughout the economic recovery. That's mainly a sign of what's being referred to as a jobless recovery. Companies that weren't hiring more workers didn't need more space, he said.

On the technology front, investors seemed to have doubts that the sector will be able to maintain its robust earnings growth of the last year, said Art Russell, senior technology analyst at Edward Jones & Co. After gaining 50 percent last year, the Nasdaq composite index is basically flat for the quarter.

''Earnings at many of these companies have been growing at 20 to 30 percent a quarter, but from here maybe they slow to 16 percent, and the next quarter only 8 percent," Russell said. ''That is what the market is worried about."

However, he said, as tech earnings have grown and stock prices remained flat, many of these companies have become more attractive buys. For example, the price-to-earnings ratio of Texas Instruments Inc. has dropped from about 29 six months ago to 22 today.

Thankfully, this quarter's worst-performing mutual fund category -- gold-oriented funds -- is one of the smallest. The 49 gold funds tracked by Lipper experienced an 18.2 percent downturn.

Gold funds typically move with the price of gold because most of the funds invest in shares of gold mining companies. Gold began the quarter at about $430 an ounce, and at one point dropped to about $370 an ounce before rebounding to the current level of $390.

Until the second quarter, gold prices had been moving higher for several months on fears that as the economy expanded, so would inflation, said Joe Foster, manager of the Van Eck Gold fund in New York. Historically, investors buy gold -- and other hard assets -- as a hedge against inflation.

But now, with the Fed raising rates, the gold market apparently believes inflation will remain tame.
