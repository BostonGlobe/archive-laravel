AT&T Wireless finishes network upgrade

July 28, 2004

AT&T Wireless Services Inc. has completed an upgrade to its Boston-area network that allows public safety officials to have top priority for placing calls during a crisis, when calling circuits may be overloaded. The so-called Wireless Priority Service, which is overseen by the federal government's National Communications System office, lets predesignated safety officials get preferred access to the network so they get connected ahead of the general public. Deutsche Telekom's T-Mobile USA unit has been the only other carrier to offer the priority service locally, which Nextel Communications Inc. is also currently deploying in New England. Cingular Wireless LLC is also planning to add the service over the next year. Federal officials have pushed for the priority service since the Sept. 11, 2001, terrorist attacks, when many public safety officials experienced all-circuits-busy conditions on their wireless phones. (Peter J. Howe)

RCN boosting download capacity

RCN Corp., which provides cable television and telecommunications services in parts of Boston and 15 suburbs and in other big US metropolitan areas, said it is raising its top broadband Internet service to 7 megabits per second next month for downloading content, with no increase in price. Over the next month, RCN said customers who currently get its "Mach 5" 5-megabit service will be automatically upgraded to 7, and those who have 3-megabit-per-second service will automatically be boosted to 5. RCN sells broadband cable modem connections in bundles ranging from $57 to $145 per month. The changes do not affect upload speeds. (Peter J. Howe)

Hancock unit buys stake in power plants

An affiliate of Manulife Financial Corp.'s John Hancock Life Insurance Co. exercised an option to buy El Paso Corp.'s interests in 10 power plants in California for $28 million. The plants were among 25 in the United States that El Paso agreed to sell to Northern Star Generation LLC, which is jointly owned by affiliates of American International Group Inc. and Ontario Teachers' Pension Plan, El Paso said. Redwood LLC, an affiliate of John Hancock and a partner in the 10 plants, exercised a first right of refusal to buy El Paso's stakes in the plants, El Paso said. The sales closed Friday. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
SEC accuses ex-financial chief of fraud

The Securities and Exchange Commission filed a civil fraud charge against a former executive of a Qwest Communications International subsidiary, alleging he concealed $112 million of improperly recognized revenue. The complaint, filed in US District Court, alleges former Qwest Wireless chief financial officer Michael Felicissimo concealed an overstatement of revenues from mobile phone products and services. Felicissimo did not return a phone call seeking comment. The complaint alleges that between January 2000 and September 2001, Qwest Wireless improperly recorded revenue from mobile phone accessories that were given to customers for free as an inducement to buy other products and services. The accounting group informed Felicissimo of the errors, but rather than report it to the president of Qwest Wireless or other Qwest executives, he concealed the resulting overstatements and directed others to do the same, the complaint said. (AP)

Cardinal Health shares drop 12.8%

Cardinal Health Inc. shares plunged 12.8 percent after the provider of medical supplies and services said its chief financial officer quit and its annual results would be delayed. Shares fell $6.47 to close at $44.00. That's down from its 52-week high of $76.54, reached May 6. Chief financial officer Richard Miller resigned Monday, saying it was in the best interests of the company. Cardinal Health had disclosed in June that its audit committee, the Securities and Exchange Commission, and the US attorney's office for the Southern District of New York were investigating how the company classifies revenue from its pharmaceutical distribution business. The company previously said the SEC was investigating Cardinal's handling of $22 million it received from vitamin manufacturers that overcharged the company. (AP)

Chairman of Stewart's company resigns

Venture capitalist Jeffrey Ubben stepped down as chairman of Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia Inc., the company said, ending his 14-month tenure as chairman in the wake of Stewart's stock trading scandal. Thomas Siekman, a lawyer and former Compaq Computer executive, was named as his successor. Ubben, whose company, ValueAct Capital, is a primary investor in Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia, will remain on the board. Siekman has served on the MSO board since Aug. 1, 2003. A source close to the matter told the Associated Press that the decision was an attempt to move the company beyond Stewart's legal woes to focus on growing the company's media properties and improving its brand image. The board felt the timing was "natural" after Stewart's sentencing this month, the source said. In addition, the company revealed the resignation Arthur Martinez, former chairman and chief executive of Sears Roebuck & Co., from the board. Martinez had been serving as lead director, a position that will no longer be filled. (AP)

Effect of Web worm said to be lessening

A computer worm that disrupted some of the world's most popular sites, including Google, tapered off yesterday as computer users patched up their defenses, analysts said. But computer security firms warned the MyDoom worm had left many computer systems' vulnerable to follow-on attacks by allowing the mysterious author of the worm to go straight to MyDoom-infected computers in the future to launch other viruses rather than scan the Internet to find vulnerable systems. Computer users can keep their systems protected from such attacks, sometimes referred to as "back-door" attacks, by regularly downloading antiviral software updates from computer security companies. Another twist to Monday's MyDoom episode is that a new virus that began moving on the Internet yesterday is preying on systems infected by MyDoom to potentially bombard Microsoft Corp.'s website with messages sent from infected computers, according to Symantec Inc. A Microsoft representative was not immediately available for comment. (Reuters)

World Kitchen to cut 200 jobs in Ohio

World Kitchen Inc. plans to cut 200 jobs, or about 9 percent of its US workforce, when it closes a bakeware manufacturing plant in Massillon, Ohio. The plant will close in September because its equipment is outdated and too expensive to replace, said Doug Arnold, vice president of human resources. The company manufactures and markets bakeware, dinnerware, and kitchen and household tools sold under the Pyrex, Corelle, Revere, Baker's Secret, and Ekco brands. World Kitchen will use a supplier in Asia to make the products now made in Ohio. (AP)

THE WORLD
German business outlook improves

The closely watched Ifo Institute index of German business sentiment improved in July in a sign that Europe's biggest economy is continuing to gain momentum, the institute said. The institute's business climate index rose to 95.6 from 94.6 in June, reversing two months of declines. Both of the main subcomponents -- current conditions and future outlook -- rose, and optimism increased even in the battered retail sector, the institute said. The index is based on a survey of several thousand business executives; it is considered a leading indicator because it suggests where the economy may be headed in the months ahead. Most economists see growth gaining speed in Germany in the second half of the year after shrinking 0.1 percent last year, the third straight year of stagnation. The economy has shown increasing signs of life since then, and grew 0.4 percent in the first quarter over the previous three months as demand for exports has risen. (AP)
