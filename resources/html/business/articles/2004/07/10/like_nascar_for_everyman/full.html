Like NASCAR for Everyman

Racing karts growing in popularity, fueling large, lucrative industry

By Royal Ford, Globe Staff  |  July 10, 2004

EAST BRIDGEWATER -- It may look like a country club, but here you try to stay on the hard cart paths and avoid the greens.

This is, however, no golf course, and kart is spelled with a 'k." It is a racetrack named F-1 Outdoors, and it is on the cutting edge of what is estimated to be a multibillion-dollar industry. It puts speed-loving businessmen, blue-collar workers, and children on the same course and in the same seats that draw NASCAR, Formula One, and sports-car racing professionals.

Randy Kugler, president of the World Karting Association, calls F-1's 1.5 mile tortuous twist of hairpin turns, straightaways, undulations, and a sweeping banked turn "one of the finest circuits in the country."

His group sanctions 150 tracks in the United States, this one included, but that is only 10-20 percent of the tracks that are available to enthusiasts. The association and "outlaw" circuits include dirt tracks, tarred ovals, and Formula One road courses such as this one.

Children as young as 7 can drive a kart here in a training program, and race after they have graduated, but tracks such as this bear little relation to what you find at amusement parks. This is serious training for racing -- or racing in its own right.

Consider the names that have come out of karting: NASCAR racers Tony Stewart, Jeff Gordon, Ricky Rudd; Indy racer Sara Fisher; Patrick Long of the Porsche Factory Team; and the F1 brothers Michael and Ralf Schumacher.

Top-end racing karts will easily exceed 100 miles per hour, and even at speeds as "slow" as 60-80 miles per hour, reaction times on the winding, compressed setting of a kart track require lightning-swift dexterity.

Go-karts are essentially low-to-the-ground tubular frames powered by high-revving engines. Their tires are small but wide for grip, and top end karts have aerodynamic shells to enhance speed. A very fast kart, such as the RM1 ROMAX 125 often used at F-1 Outdoors, has an engine equal to that of a small motorcycle, but powerful enough to propel this lightweight vehicle (under 200 pounds) at high speeds.

And 70 miles per hour sitting an inch off the ground, with a straightaway vaporizing before your eyes in seconds, can feel like 175 miles per hour.

Indeed, that is why many pros return to karts again and again to hone their skills. Gordon, Dale Earnhardt Jr., and Michael Waltrip have all driven karts on this very track. And regular folks with a sheer need for speed are drawn as well.

Thomas Young, 29, president of Charles Warner Co., a financial services firm, made the two-hour trek from his home in Glastonbury, Conn., in his Ferrari last week. He had been to F1 Indoors and was told to check out the outdoor track.

"I immediately bought one," he said, referring to the RM1 European racing kart that is featured here.

"We all have pretty high-stress jobs," said Matt Mintzer of Boston, a kart owner who works in Putnam Investments' retirement marketing group. "This is a great escape."

Yet it is a venue and a sport that offers training, excitement, competition, and even stress relief, to folks from all walks of life.

"It is blue collar; it is white collar; we've got CEOs," said Richard J. Valentine, president of the MBA Group of companies and prime force behind F1 Outdoors (he also is an owner of F1 Indoors in Braintree).

"Speed doesn't care how much money you make," Valentine said.

The track hosts "arrive and drives," with customers showing up to learn the sport. There are corporate events, racing leagues, private outings for families and organized groups. Racers looking to move on to national and international competition can qualify here under professionally timed trials. There are even endurance races where teams race for four to six hours at a time.

The growth in interest in the sport has spawned a multibillion dollar industry, Kugler said, made up of not only tracks, but cart manufacturers, parts manufacturers, suppliers of helmets, racing suits, racing shoes, and other paraphernalia. He said there may be nearly 200 such related companies in the field.

While overall industry revenue may be sizable, the actual fees to drive are more modest. It costs $10 for a one-day license and $25 for 15 laps of the track. A season license costs $25, and it remains $25 per 15 laps for as many times as you return.

"You can't go racing for less money," said Steve Tully of Franklin, owner of a small machine shop, and a racing kart.

And the grassy 45-acre facility will grow, Valentine said, to resemble a country club even more, with more lush landscaping, a clubhouse for members, and roofed garages such as you'd find at full-size racing venues. Already, kart owners can store their karts and have them maintained here.

Nearly 900 youngsters, aged 7-17, have graduated from the training program.

"I'm hoping the first American F1 racer comes out of this program," said Valentine.

While corporate users, and "arrive and drive" customers get 9-horsepower karts capable of 50-60 miles per hour, those with training can move up by purchasing -- for around $7,200 -- the RM1 with a ROTAX 125 engine. That kart will reach upwards of 80 miles per hour and, tinkered with, can top 100 miles per hour.

Mintzer said that since he took up the sport, he has had to start working out regularly and has lost 30 pounds. "I had to stay competitive," he said.

But despite the intensity of the competition among the serious racers, the head-to-head challenge can sometimes be more intimate.

Take the case of Mark Pellegrini, owner of Gold's Gym in East Bridgewater.

He thought karting would be good for his son, Mark Jr., who is 10. He went to watch him run, Pellegrini said, "and I saw how much fun I was missing out on."

Now he has a kart, but he often runs under the watchful eye of the boy he thought was the pupil.

"He's out there saying, 'Dad, you're doing this wrong; you're doing that wrong,' " Pellegrini said. "And he's usually right."
