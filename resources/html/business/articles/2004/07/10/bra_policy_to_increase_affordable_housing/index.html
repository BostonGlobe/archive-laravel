BRA policy to increase affordable housing

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr., Globe Staff  |  July 10, 2004

The Boston Redevelopment Authority has quietly increased by 30 percent the number of lower-cost units that developers must provide when building a residential project.

That increase of affordable homes in any approved project -- from 10 to about 13 percent -- will mitigate the high cost of housing for some new residents but will make it more difficult to finance new housing projects, developers said yesterday.

And a Massachusetts Institute of Technology housing specialist said it will almost certainly increase the density in neighborhoods where new housing is built.

Since 2000, the administration of Mayor Thomas M. Menino has required developers of multiunit housing -- whether for sale or for use as rental apartments -- to make a minimum of 10 percent affordable to local residents.

That means, according to criteria set by the US Department of Housing and Urban Development, anyone making between 80 and 120 percent of the median income for the area should be able to afford to live there. According to developers, condominium units that might sell for $300,000 to $500,000 at market rate, for example, would be sold for $180,000 to $200,000. The increase in the cost associated with affordable housing is shared by the developer and purchasers of the market rate units.

In the past four years, 246 of the so-called affordable units, both for rent and for sale, have been created as part of new residential construction in the city, authority officials said yesterday.

But, under a pilot program in effect since November, the city has been requiring developers who propose new projects of 10 units of housing or more to build a little over 13 percent of the total units in the affordable range. That policy has worked out well, with seven new projects, including 280 homes, now underway with the increased percentage of affordable units, according to Mark Maloney, director of the BRA.

The new policy was made permanent as of May, he said, as part of Menino's "Leading the Way" strategy to ease the housing crunch in Boston.

"We tried to figure out to what extent we could push the market without impacting projects to a point where they became infeasible," Maloney said. "We calculated they could afford 13.04 percent."

That means that, in a new 100-unit condo or apartment building, 13 or possibly 14 units would be sold or rented to those who qualify for affordable status. A lottery is used to determine who buys or rents the cheaper housing.

Tod McGrath, a faculty member at the MIT Center for Real Estate, said in many cases a developer will be allowed to build a few more units than he otherwise would, to cover the cost of having to build additional affordable housing.

"He goes to the BRA and says, 'I can do this, but you [have to] give me a little relief here and let me build a few more market-rate units'," said McGrath. "That's actually a very effective way of bridging the gap between developers and advocates for affordable housing. Density is the mechanism."

But some neighborhood activists may not like that, even though they support affordable housing. City officials say no such direct tradeoff is made.

"I don't think we do that in any kind of official way," said Timothy McGourthy, director of policy for the BRA. "But we recognize that developers have financial constraints. As they build in the 'affordable' they're actually creating public value."

Developers weren't complaining loudly about the change yesterday. Most interviewed said the goals of creating more affordable housing were laudable but would make financing the projects more difficult because expected revenue from sales or rents would not be as high as if more of the units were at market rates.

"The more affordable housing we have the better," said Roger M. Cassin, managing partner of Cassin/Winn LLC, developer of the planned Columbus Center mixed-use development on the edge of the South End. "There is real expense in creating it, so it makes that incrementally tougher."

Aaron Gornstein, executive director of the Citizens' Housing and Planning Association, praised Menino for increasing the affordability requirement.

"There was a consensus among a broad group of people that this was a positive thing," said Gornstein, who was on the City Hall committee that studied ways to improve the housing situation.

David I. Begelfer, chief executive of the National Association of Industrial and Office Properties, questioned whether an additional requirement of developers will drive some of them away. "What is the straw that breaks the camel's back?" he asked. "You add on and on and the thing doesn't work -- people say it's the economy, but it's all the things that have been added to a project."

But Dan Hart, director of development for E.A. Fish Associates LLC, a development company, called the change "a positive."

Hart, who was part of the committee that considered the increase in the affordable housing requirement, said, "That slight increase is going to help the overall affordability."

"This type of thing, although it hurts us in some respects, we can see the greater good," he said. "It's not something so radical it's going to prevent development from happening."
