YOUR MONEY

Discipline can be an investor's best bet

By Chuck Jaffe, Globe Correspondent  |  July 8, 2004

At the Morningstar Investor Conference last month, a surprising number of fund managers talked about holding more cash than they'd like to.

For some, increased cash is about not being able to find stocks that meet their selection criteria. They can't find enough issues they consider undervalued or with the kind of growth rates they favor. For others, the increased cash is the result of a broader uncertainty about the stock market.

Some of the individual investors in attendance were likewise trying to decide whether this is the time to head to the sidelines.

But for every ordinary investor looking for the safety of cash or considering a change in strategies, the Morningstar conference provided a solid reminder of why making the switch might not be the best move.

It came from keynote speaker Chris Davis, manager of the Selected American Shares fund, who reminded investors that the average annual return on a stock fund from 1984 through 2002 was a touch more than 10.2 percent.

The average stock fund investor, however, earned less than 3 percent. The difference between what the average fund earned and what the average investor got is found in portfolio moves, in switching from one investment to the next, typically in an effort to find the hot hand.

And current market conditions are just the types that make otherwise sane investors go chasing performance. These are the times that try an investor's soul.

''Rallies never issue invitations, and the stock market moves in surges and bursts," explains Craig Callahan, chief investment officer at ICON Advisers, which runs the ICON funds. ''What is confusing the issue right now, even for the pros, is that we have been in a trading range, with a lot of different issues making people nervous, so that whenever the market moves higher, people get scared and the market moves back down. It's tough to read, and that is when investors make mistakes."

Average investors need to understand that what makes a strategy work for a fund manager does not necessarily work when they try to replicate it on their own. The fund manager owns individual issues; the investor owns funds and is allowing the manager to make the calls. Changing funds trying to jumpstart a portfolio means second-guessing the manager, which typically is a mistake.

Say, for example, you own the Yacktman Fund, where manager Donald Yacktman generally wants to be fully invested but currently holds more than 25 percent of his assets in cash. That low-yielding chunk of money may explain why the fund trails its peer group this year, and just lagged the average mid-cap value fund in 2003.

But an investor getting frustrated with Yacktman -- and it would be hard to argue with the 33 percent gain he posted last year, even if it did lag the peer group by a hair -- is missing the point of owning the fund, which is Yacktman's vision and discipline. If the guy running the fund can't find enough stocks that work for him, the fund investor making a change looking for a faster rocket right now is practically begging for trouble.

The issue boils down to this: For the foreseeable future, we're likely to be in a low-return market, both for stocks and bonds. The uptick in interest rates hasn't helped much, given that rates were at historic lows.

And while there are many different market metrics worth looking at, any investor who relies on price/earnings ratios as a key indicator knows that high ratios tend to be associated with below-average returns moving forward. In spite of the bear market, it's still a high ratio market today, which does not bode well for the immediate future.

Again, that is the kind of alarming forecast that makes many individuals clamor for something better.

''We don't have a lot of confidence that people will be able to outguess which way the markets will go in the short run, so we are steady as she goes," says investment adviser Roger Gibson of Gibson Capital Management in Pittsburgh. ''But we do see the potential for people to get into trouble. There are more than a few experts looking at this environment and saying it's a good time to abandon diversification and become much more active. It's almost as if they are so displeased with the future they see that they would rather double up or triple up on the bets that are still working and hope it turns out well."

That kind of move is what winds up giving average investors returns that are much worse than they could have achieved by staying the course.

More importantly, investors must decide what they want driving their returns, whether it is asset allocation or investment selection.

Asset allocation is the art of mixing different types of investments in a way that produces a desired overall return. It typically involves spreading money around stocks, bonds, international issues, and more, hoping to deliver a smooth ride in all market conditions.

Allowing a portfolio to be driven by investment selection means hoping that the current choices deliver, and that they are likely to be out when they lag. It's great when it works, but it can be devastating when it doesn't.

The discrepancy between what the average fund gets and what the average investor gets from the fund highlights what happens when the focus tends to be on what's hot right now.

So when the headlines make you think it might be time for a change, consider whether you are truly managing your money or just placating your fears. Letting headlines and a sideways market scare you into action is the exact opposite of a savvy manager playing a consistent strategy and waiting for the market to reward patience again.
