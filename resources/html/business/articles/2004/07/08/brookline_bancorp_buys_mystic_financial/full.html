Brookline Bancorp buys Mystic Financial

July 8, 2004

Brookline Bancorp Inc., parent of Brookline Bank, agreed to buy Mystic Financial Inc. for about $65.1 million in cash and stock to expand in the Boston area. The transaction is expected to be completed in the first quarter of 2005 and boost earnings in the first year of operation, Brookline Bancorp said. Mystic operates Medford Co-operative Bank, with seven offices in Medford, Lexington, Arlington, Bedford, and Malden. Medford-based Mystic had total assets of $428 million on March 31. Mystic shares jumped $5.48, or 16.7 percent, to $38.28. Brookline Bancorp shares fell 14 cents, or less than 1 percent, to $14.64. "We are a $1.6 billion bank right now and will be in excess of $2 billion once the transaction is completed." said Brookline's chief financial officer, Paul Bechet. (Bloomberg)

Acusphere, Norwegian company in deal

Acusphere Inc. of Watertown will get up to $70 million in a deal it signed with Nycomed Pharma AS of Norway to sell its test for heart disease in Europe. The test, currently in late-stage clinical trials, uses Acusphere's microscopic particles in an ultrasound procedure to test heart function. If approved, it could supplant a more expensive test that uses radioactive dyes. The deal sent Acusphere's shares up $1.36, or 20.9 percent, to $7.86. "The size of this transaction demonstrates that a third party can see the enormous potential for this market," said Sherri C. Oberg, chief executive. "In the US, our plan is to hold on to the marketing rights and build our own sales force." Acusphere went public in October, and its shares had lost more than half their value prior to yesterday's disclosure. (Jeffrey Krasner)

Union aims to block plant owners' plan

The union representing 80 percent of workers at the Pilgrim Station nuclear power plant in Plymouth said it will ask the Nuclear Regulatory Commission to reject the plant owners' plan to use managers and replacement workers to operate Pilgrim if union members, as threatened, go on strike Tuesday. Gary Sullivan, president of Local 369 of the Utility Workers Union of America, said plans by plant owner Entergy Corp. violate NRC safety rules. Pilgrim spokesman David Tarantino said Entergy is confident "we can adequately staff this operation" with well-trained workers, including many brought in from nine other Entergy plants. NRC spokeswoman Diane Screnci said Entergy's plans "have been acceptable based on our review so far," but the agency would evaluate and respond to any union petition. Local 369, whose contract expires Tuesday, and Entergy are wrangling over staffing levels and other issues at the 670-megawatt plant. (Peter J. Howe)

Biogen Idec CEO expects large gains

Biogen Idec Inc., the third-biggest US biotechnology company, predicts it may gain as much as 40 to 50 percent of the multiple sclerosis market with Antegren, the drug it is developing with Irish partner Elan Corp. "We think the lion's share of market growth is going to go to Antegren and that current products will plateau in a year or two and then slowly decline," chief executive Jim Mullen said in Zurich. "We see Antegren with 40 percent to 50 percent of the global market ultimately." Antegren last week was granted priority review by the US Food and Drug Administration, meaning the agency may decide whether to approve the drug within six months. Cambridge-based Biogen Idec is counting on Antegren to make up for slowing growth of older medicines, such as its top-selling MS drug Avonex. (Bloomberg)

T.H. Lee to acquire Canadian company

Thomas H. Lee Partners, a Boston-based leveraged buyout firm, agreed to acquire Progressive Moulded Products Ltd., a Canadian maker of instrument panels for cars and other interior parts. Terms weren't disclosed. Progressive Moulded has annual revenue of about $379 million and sells to General Motors Corp., Ford Motor Co., and DaimlerChrysler AG's Chrysler unit as well as suppliers. For the buyout firm, started in 1974 by former bank-loan officer Thomas H. Lee, now 60, the purchase is its sixth in a year. "The business is growing at two to three times the growth in the industry because US automakers are spending a lot of time doing what the Germans and Japanese have done: paying attention to interiors," said Kent Weldon, 37, a Thomas H. Lee managing director. He said revenue is growing "in the double digits," but declined to comment on the firm's financial performance. (Bloomberg)

Appeal halts Maine blueberry settlement

An appeal by the lone holdout in a price-fixing lawsuit against blueberry processors will delay the payout to Maine's estimated 500 blueberry growers. Superior Court Justice Joseph Jabar approved the $4 million settlement for two blueberry processors but a third processor, Allen's Blueberry Freezer, appealed to the state Supreme Court. Allen's Blueberry Freezer objected not to the monetary award but the way future prices for growers would be set by two other processors, Cherryfield Foods Inc. of Cherryfield and Jasper Wyman & Son of Milbridge. (AP)

Knight Trading to pay $79m to settle

Knight Trading Group Inc., the biggest matchmaker for buyers and sellers of Nasdaq stocks, reached a $79 million settlement of claims it overcharged customers. The agreement with the Securities and Exchange Commission and the National Association of Securities Dealers will lower earnings in the quarter ended June 30, and might wipe out profit in the quarter, the company said. Under the settlement, Knight will disgorge about $41 million in profits and pay $13 million in interest and $25 million in penalties, the company said. The SEC and NASD will determine how the money is distributed. Knight said it expects the proposed settlement to take the form of an administrative order, in which it would "neither admit nor deny the findings." (Bloomberg)

Defense rests in ex-Tyco lawyer's trial

The judge hearing the larceny trial of Mark Belnick, Tyco International's former top lawyer, denied a defense motion for a mistrial after a prosecutor tried to use a document in a way the defense called prejudicial. Assistant District Attorney John Moscow was discussing the Tyco loan activity of former chief executive L. Dennis Kozlowski when he produced a document showing that many of the ousted CEO's loans were forgiven and never repaid. Moscow's apparent purpose was to show that Belnick knew about Kozlowski's alleged looting of a loan program meant to help employees pay taxes on maturing Tyco stock. He alleges Kozlowski later bought Belnick's silence with a bonus worth up to $17 million. (AP)

Class-action bill stalls in Senate

Legislation to rewrite the rules on class-action lawsuits became mired in procedural wrangling in the Senate over whether to allow unrelated amendments on issues like immigration and drug reimportation. Seeking to bar unrelated additions to the bill which could complicate its future in negotiations with the House of Representatives, Senate Majority leader Bill Frist, a Tennessee Republican, last evening moved to limit the debate to germane amendments. A vote on this motion was set for tomorrow. Frist said he welcomed relevant changes to the bill, which has supporters in both parties. (Reuters)
