BUSINESS IN BRIEF

NitroMed drug helps; shares up 73%

July 20, 2004

NitroMed Inc., a maker of cardiovascular treatments, said its experimental drug BiDil boosted the survival of black patients with heart failure in a study. Shares jumped $4.31, or 73 percent, to $10.21. The trial was halted and all of the more than 1,000 patients enrolled will have the opportunity to get the drug, Bedford-based NitroMed said. Patients taking BiDil in combination with other therapies suffered fewer cardiovascular problems, were hospitalized less often, and survived longer, the company said. The data will be submitted to the Food and Drug Administration and NitroMed plans to begin selling the drug by early 2005, pending approval. BiDil boosts levels of nitric oxide, which is deficient in some blacks and may contribute to heart disease, NitroMed spokeswoman Kathleen O'Donnell said. (Bloomberg)

Use of Fidelity 'life cycle' funds increases

Fidelity Investments, the world's largest mutual fund manager, said more employers are selecting ''life cycle" funds instead of money market options for workers who don't pick their own investments. Retirement plans served by Fidelity have nearly tripled their use of life-cycle funds as default options since 2001, according to data from the Boston-based firm. About $19 billion has been invested in Fidelity's Freedom funds for 860,000 ''unengaged" or ''less engaged workers," who neglected to designate a fund or had their employers choose for them, said Fidelity spokeswoman Jenny Engle. Life cycle funds invest in a mix of stocks and bonds and invest more conservatively as an employee's retirement date approaches. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

NASD fines 3 major investment firms

Three major investment firms have been fined $250,000 each by securities regulators for allegedly failing to provide required documents to investors bringing claims against brokers in 20 arbitration cases in the past two years -- even after being ordered to do so. The National Association of Securities Dealers, the brokerage industry's self-policing organization, said the civil fines were against Citigroup, Merrill Lynch and Morgan Stanley. The NASD also censured the Wall Street firms for the alleged violations of brokerage industry rules related to securities arbitration proceedings. None of the three either admitted to or denied the allegations in the settlements. They also agreed to install new written procedures to ensure that future such violations are brought to the attention of senior officials at the firms. (AP)

2d request denied for Ebbers trial delay

A federal judge refused to push back the Nov. 9 trial of former WorldCom chief executive Bernard Ebbers, denying a second request by his lawyer for a delay. Lawyer Reid Weingarten had asked US District Judge Barbara Jones to move the trial to January or February, citing turmoil over a recent Supreme Court decision that calls federal sentencing procedures into question. Ebbers is accused of fraud and conspiracy in the collapse of WorldCom, which changed its name to MCI when it emerged from bankruptcy in April in hopes of distancing itself from its $11 billion accounting scandal. The Supreme Court decision said Washington state judges could not impose harsher sentences than state guidelines based on information not presented to a jury. (AP)

Regulators split on bank rules proposal

After months of protest by consumer and advocacy groups, federal bank regulators are split over a proposal to ease a community lending law by demanding less frequent government reviews for a greater number of small banks. The Federal Reserve and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, a Treasury Department division, said Friday they were withdrawing the proposed rule changes to the Community Reinvestment Act, the 1977 law that requires banks to make loans in low-income and minority areas where they operate as a condition for opening new branches. The proposal, also made in January by the other major federal agencies that oversee banks -- the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. and the Office of Thrift Supervision -- had been open to public comment for several months. But the thrift office, another Treasury agency, said it was going ahead with the move and proposing to raise the threshold for smaller banks. (AP)

Cheaper, longer-lasting iPods unveiled

As rivals nip at its market dominance, Apple Computer Inc. introduced its fourth-generation iPod portable music players with lower prices and longer battery life. Apple said the new models have up to 12 hours of rechargeable battery life, compared to about 8 hours with the current crop. The prices are $299 for a 20-gigabyte model and $399 for a 40-gigabyte model -- each $100 less than their earlier counterparts. Competitors also boasted better battery life -- the Dell DJ has about 20 hours while the Samsung YP-910GS lasts about 10 hours. The upcoming Creative Zen Touch promises 24 hours of battery life while the Sony Network Walkman, set for August, promises up to 30 hours of playback. (AP)

Chip equipment orders rise 3% in June

North American semiconductor capital equipment makers reported a 3 percent rise in orders in June from May levels, sustaining a run-up in business as chip makers upgraded and expanded factories, a US trade group said. Orders for equipment used to produce microchips reached $1.61 billion in June from $1.56 billion in May, said the group, Semiconductor Equipment and Materials International. Orders were more than twice as large as the same month last year, when the slumping chip industry ordered only $722 million in equipment. Billings, or shipments, were $1.48 billion in June, up 5 percent from May and up 91 percent from a year earlier. The ratio of orders to shipments, known as the book-to-bill ratio, was 1.08 in June, meaning that for every $100 of products shipped, $108 in new orders were received. (Reuters)

THE WORLD

EU OK's Sony-Bertelsmann unit merger

The European Union approved the merger of Sony Music and Bertelsmann AG's BMG unit in a deal that gives the four ''majors" control of about 80 percent of the world music market, Bertelsmann said. The EU's executive body, the European Commission, had been widely expected to endorse the decision of Mario Monti, the EU's competition commissioner, to approve the merger. Monti reversed his merger team's initial objections to the deal after two days of closed-door hearings in June with the companies and third parties, including independent labels and companies involved in the emerging legal market for online music downloading. (AP)

Airbus ramps up; Boeing gets $3b order

Airbus SAS, the world's largest plane maker, will boost production of medium-range planes 50 percent by mid-2006, while Boeing Co. unveiled an order worth $3 billion, evidence that an aerospace recovery is underway. Airbus chief executive Noel Forgeard said the company will deliver more aircraft this year than last year's 305 and will increase production in 2005 and 2006. The increases suggest a record 456 plane deliveries in 2006. Boeing won an order from Emirates airlines for four 777 aircraft with an option for nine additional planes. (Bloomberg)
