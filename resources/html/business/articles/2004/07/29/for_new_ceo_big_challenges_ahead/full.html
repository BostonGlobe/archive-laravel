For new CEO, big challenges ahead

Malden Mills faces $80m in debt, Asian competition

By Ross Kerber, Globe Staff  |  July 29, 2004

Since taking over as chief executive of Malden Mills Industries Inc. this week, Michael Spillane said friends and business contacts have been asking the same question: Is he crazy?

Spillane, 44, a career textile executive, said he sees their point. ''If I was looking for something easy, I wouldn't be here," he said in a telephone interview yesterday.

The Lawrence textile company faces issues that would challenge any manufacturer. Just out of bankruptcy protection, it carries $80 million in debt and faces tough competition from Asian factories whose fleece fabrics cost less.

To top it all off, Malden Mills remains closely identified with its longtime family owner, Aaron M. Feuerstein, a forceful personality who remains a minority shareholder even though he recently quit his posts at the company to try to wrest back control. He gained national acclaim as a benevolent corporate leader in an era of cost cutting because he paid idled workers after a devastating fire struck the business in 1995.

Spillane, a former executive at Tommy Hilfiger USA, said it's too soon to discuss his long-term plans for the 1,000-employee workforce, or how much production he might move to mills overseas, or just how he plans to restore profits. But he added that he took Feuerstein's role into consideration when deciding whether to accept the job as the first outside leader since Feuerstein's grandfather founded the business in 1906.

''Aaron has my complete respect, and whether I get to know him personally or from afar, I hope to earn his," Spillane said.

At the same time, Spillane speaks bluntly about flaws he sees in Malden Mills' business plans.

''You have had nothing strategic done here in 10 years, in terms of positioning the brand and finding new markets," he said. ''In terms of having a formal plan to get new things going, that hasn't happened."

Spillane attributed the shortcomings to the twin distractions of the 1995 fire, and the bankruptcy protection the company operated under from 2001 to 2003.

Spillane declined to discuss specific financial matters, saying he's still reviewing the company's books.

The company is roughly breaking even, said David Orlofsky, its interim chief financial officer.

Spillane arrives at a time of transition for Malden Mills, which has grown increasingly dependent on military contracts for outdoor combat gear, arranged with the help of state politicians. But during the rebuilding, demand for some products fell off, contributing to the business filing for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection at the end of 2001. By the time it emerged last year, creditors led by a unit of General Electric Co. had pushed aside Feuerstein. He held the largely ceremonial titles of president and nonexecutive chairman and only about 5 percent of the equity in the business.

Meanwhile, Feuerstein's efforts to buy back the company ran into this complication: the 78-year-old nearly died while watching a movie on July 12. During a viewing of Michael Moore's film ''Fahrenheit 9/11" -- a documentary that attacks the policies of the Bush administration --Feuerstein developed chest pains. A helicopter took him to UMass Memorial Medical Center in Worcester, where surgeons repaired an aortic dissection, a condition in which the large artery comes apart.

''Something very, very awful was going on," said Feuerstein's doctor, Christian T. Campos, who added he expects Feuerstein will make a full recovery within two months.

Feuerstein's oldest son, Daniel, who's also involved in the buyback attempt, said he wouldn't allow his father to be interviewed for this article. ''It's not appropriate to air the buyback process publicly."

Daniel Feuerstein added the family wishes Spillane ''every success."

The two men haven't spoken yet, though Spillane grew up in Brookline's Coolidge Corner area, not far from where the elder Feuerstein has lived nearly his whole life.

After graduating from UMass-Boston with an economics degree in 1982, Spillane worked as a stockbroker in Boston's Financial District until 1989, then wound up in the New York area garment trade.

Spillane said he liked the business because ''it was the tangible nature of it. You got to touch something, to feel it." He's worked at various textile companies since, most recently at a unit of Tommy Hilfiger with revenue of $250 million, he said, before stepping down in March.

''I felt it was time to try something else," he said.

One of Spillane's first tests will be dealing with Malden Mills' biggest union, whose contract expires Nov. 30. Employees made concessions when the company fell into bankruptcy, but now want some wages and benefits restored.

''We went a long time with pay freezes, and pay cuts, a lost holiday pay, and overtime rights, and heavier workloads," said Paul Coorey, the leader of the company's largest union.

Spillane said it was too soon to discuss Coorey's comments or how many workers the firm will need in coming years. But he said he considers the company's relationship with its workers and community important. ''We'll be the best citizens we can be when we're a healthy employer," he said.
