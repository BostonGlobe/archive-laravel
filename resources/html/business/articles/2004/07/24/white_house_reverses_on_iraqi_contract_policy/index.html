White House reverses on Iraqi contract policy

Move would allow nations that opposed war to bid on pacts

By Stephen J. Glain, Globe Staff  |  July 24, 2004

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration moved to allow countries that did not support the war in Iraq to share in the reconstruction process, reversing a policy that strained US relations with some of its European allies.

A senior trade official said the Department of Defense last week requested that the US trade representative waive restrictions on postwar rebuilding funds "to allow it to broaden the pool of eligible bidders for Iraq reconstruction contracts to firms from all countries except those that are state sponsors of terrorism." The waiver, which the official said was being processed, would allow such countries as France and Germany, which opposed the war, to bid for lucrative prime rebuilding contracts.

"This is a very favorable development," said Todd Malan, executive director of the Organization for International Investment, a group that represents the US subsidiaries of foreign companies. "The previous policy was, frankly, a bonehead policy. It was contrary to US interests."

The request marks a significant reversal of a policy that impaired trans-Atlantic ties already damaged by disputes over the Iraq war. In December, deputy defense secretary Paul Wolfowitz said that only countries that supported the war would be issued prime contracts. Not only did the exclusion anger important US allies, analysts said, it also forced the Pentagon to scramble for postwar funding because there was not a substantial coalition to help spread the costs.

"They assumed things would get easier and they didn't," said Pat Towell, a visiting fellow at the Center for Strategic and Budgetary Assessment in Washington. "It's one more graphic illustration of the downside of overly optimistic presumptions on which they proceeded. We've had shortfalls before, with the Balkans campaign in the 1990s, for example. The difference is that a lot of people were warning up front that the assumptions were too rosy."

Before the war, Wolfowitz and other White House officials told lawmakers that much of the bill for rebuilding Iraq could be covered by selling oil from the country's ample petroleum reserves. In April 2003, weeks after the fall of the Iraqi regime, a senior United Nations official disputed such assurances and warned that the costs of occupying and rebuilding Iraq would be overwhelming without a robust UN presence to help pacify and rebuild the country.

"I have to say that my numbers don't add up quite like theirs do," Mark Malloch Brown, the administrator of the UN Development Program said at the time. "Our own view is that the oil industry of Iraq needs a sustained burst of new investment to modernize it and increase both its efficiency and its daily outputs before it is able to contribute significantly to the capital costs of reconstruction."

Iraqi oil production has reached about 2.5 million barrels a day, slightly below its prewar output of 2.8 million barrels.

News of the waiver was first reported in London's Financial Times. A defense department spokesman said he was unaware of any waiver filed by the Pentagon to the Commerce Department, nor was he aware that the Pentagon needed such a waiver to alter its procurement policies.

"It may be we're getting ready to bid out another tranche of projects, and this is part of it," the spokesman said. "I don't know."

While the United States managed to transfer sovereignty over Iraq to interim local government on schedule late last month, the costs of the war have spiraled well beyond the Defense Department's earlier estimates. On Wednesday, the Government Accountability Office, the federal government's nonpartisan accounting agency, reported that the Pentagon would have to find $12.3 billion beyond the $65 billion Congress approved last year to finance wartime operations in Afghanistan and Iraq.

This week, Congress passed a $416 billion defense spending bill that includes $25 billion in emergency funding for both wars requested by the Bush administration in its 2005 budget.
