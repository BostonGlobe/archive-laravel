Stocking up before showtime

Hub restaurants, stores rush to take deliveries, fearing DNC delays

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  July 24, 2004

The streets of downtown Boston may be choked and closed next week with commuters trying to get into and out of town during the Democratic National Convention. But Boston's high-end hotels and restaurants won't let that get in the way of the fun: They are stockpiling everything from Amstel Light to organic macaroni to serve to delegates in case deliveries get delayed.

Trucks lined up two deep in the Back Bay yesterday morning, blocking traffic, cutting one another off, and even breaking bottles of red wine in their haste to unload. Most drivers said their clients have asked for twice their normal loads.

"I already brought down two kinds of Perrier," said Scott Phillips, stocking case after case of water outside the Fairmont Copley Plaza yesterday. "There are a lot of prestigious people staying here."

The pace grew so feverish that at one point a blue Bud Light truck pulled up and double-parked alongside an identical Bud Light truck, which was idling by the curb at the nearby Westin. Both unloaded a predictable cargo: dozens of boxes of Bud Light.

But all the stockpiling has created a bit of a problem at several restaurants and stores: There's not enough room for storage. Davio's, an upscale restaurant on Arlington Street, has ordered so much house wine that it even has to put some in its coat closet. "It's good that this is a summer event," general manager Eleanor Arpino said. Verizon Wireless's store on Boylston Street is so overstocked with plastic bags and cellphone gear that workers are storing extra stuff in the men's bathroom, one deliveryman said.

Yesterday marked one of the busiest supply days of the year as hotels and restaurants received final shipments before the road closures start. Though Mayor Thomas M. Menino held a press conference in June to say that the convention would not be as bad as the Blizzard of '78, hotels apparently decided not to heed his words.

Among the favorites in the hotel loading areas yesterday: Perrier, boxes of Heineken, and case after case of Ravenswood marked with the slogan "no wimpy wines." To satisfy the connoisseurs among the conventioneers, brand name has become especially important. Driver Joe Gwozdz, unloading a truck outside the Westin, says he has been shipping huge quantities of Grey Goose and raspberry Stolichnaya vodkas for delegates. "Martinis are back," he said.

Even businesses not associated with the convention have been beefing up inventories: Newbury Street salons are ordering extra shampoo, and even banks are stocking their branches with extra cash, according to deliverers. Verizon Wireless has ordered an extra half week's supply of accessories, phones, and even plastic bags for its two downtown stores in case its regularly scheduled resupply is delayed, spokeswoman J. Abra Degbor said. She said she would "neither confirm nor deny" that the Boylston Street store put items in the men's restroom, but said the store temporarily puts the items "wherever space is available."

Davio's, which plans to play host to a full slate of convention parties, ordered more than 1,500 extra linen napkins, enough cans of tomatoes and boxes of pasta to feed a small army. The restaurant also asked repairers to take a look at its air-conditioning system, its water filtration system, and even its cappuccino machine -- just in case.

Arpino, the general manager, said the restaurant is taking no chances. "Come Monday, we may see our linen delivery guy hassle free, but we're not going to bank on that," she said.

Still, there have already been a few surprises, as hotels and restaurants try to guess what the out-of-towners will like in order to stock up. Already, the early-arriving journalists have been ordering white wines -- chardonnay and pinot grigio -- at Davio's, throwing the restaurant for a loop. The restaurant normally sells three bottles of red for every one of white.

As busy as it was yesterday downtown, some vendors of fresh foods did not see a noticeable jump in business, partly because the best hotels and restaurants won't order meats and fruits too far in advance. At the New England Produce and Boston Market terminals on the Chelsea-Everett border, wholesalers said they were concerned about the chance that hordes of people would flock there Monday morning, looking to buy fruit. The market will open at 4 a.m., an hour earlier than normal, until the end of the convention.

For the week of the convention, planners have encouraged delivery drivers to finish their routes downtown as early as possible to reduce traffic. That means many hotels and restaurants will have to start taking packages as early as 2 a.m., and they will have a hard time getting it later than 8 a.m., the drivers say. Though most say their companies will try to keep things moving next week, they said they do not know how well it will work.

Jim Duggan, a contractor for FedEx Ground, said his company wants him to leave part of his route to another driver and get out of town by 2 p.m. If protesters block the streets, he said he might get stuck.

"I've experienced the Marathon, parades . . ." he ticked off on his fingers. "I'm worried this could be the same or worse."
