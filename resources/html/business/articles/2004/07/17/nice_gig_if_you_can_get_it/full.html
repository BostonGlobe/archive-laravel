Nice gig if you can get it

Area hopefuls are working overtime for Battle of the Corporate Bands

By Hiawatha Bray, Globe Staff  |  July 17, 2004

The drummer couldn't make it to the practice session but the band soldiered on without him, the jangling of their four guitars ringing off the brick walls of a loft on Drydock Avenue, not far from Boston Harbor.

The loft isn't a recording studio or a funky after-hours club. It's the headquarters of Partners + Simons, a local advertising agency. President and founder Tom Simons is one of the musicians. Three of the four players work here, and in their spare time they play for the company's house band, the Loomers.

Don't expect to see them on the cover of Rolling Stone, but they've got a chance of making it to the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame and Museum in Cleveland. All they have to do is beat out five other groups from the region, made up of some of corporate America's best part-time musicians. On July 24, the Loomers will crank their amplifiers to 11 in New York City, for a showdown in the Battle of the Corporate Bands.

''I've been practicing every night for the past six weeks," said Simons, who'll be facing tough competition from bands that include representatives of such local firms as Friendly Ice Cream Corp. and Avid Technology Inc. The winner of the showdown will get a ticket to Cleveland for the finals.

Victory will mean little fame and no fortune; indeed, finalists must pay $7,500 to take part in the Cleveland clash. It's all about raising money for the nation's primary repository of rock music history. ''It's a fund-raiser essentially for the museum," said Hall of Fame spokesman Todd Mesek, ''but this is a development effort that's cooler and more exciting than anything else out there." Between the entry fees and the sale of tickets to each contest, Mesek expects to rake in about $140,000.

This is the fourth go-round for the Battle of the Corporate Bands, a concept dreamed up by museum officials and the business magazine Fortune. But it's surprising that Simons himself didn't think of it. He's a fervent music buff who believes that music is vital to his firm's character. ''The business needs to have a pulse," said Simons. ''It needs to have a rhythm to it."

Simon has installed a high-quality sound system throughout his company's offices. Music plays in the background throughout the work day, and every employee is encouraged to bring in favorite recordings and play them for colleagues. Nobody is allowed to turn off someone else's music, no matter how dreadful. ''If you want to be successful here," said Simons, ''you have to have a fair amount of tolerance for other people's music."

Fortunately, the Loomers' music isn't too hard to take; it's mellow guitar pop, reminiscent of Hootie & the Blowfish.

Two of Simons' bandmates are also colleagues. Copywriter Jon Svetkey formerly worked as a professional musician, and founded the Loomers before coming to work at Partners + Simons. A second member of the Loomers, bassist Jack Cavalier, also joined the company and works as controller. Guitarist Everett Pendleton is a karate teacher and a customer service representative for Parlance Corp., a voice mail systems maker in Medford. The missing one, drummer Michael Cahill, is an air quality engineer and part owner of CK Environmental Inc. in Canton. He was in Paris to watch the Tour de France, but expected to land in New York the day before the showdown.

As for Simons' contribution, ''I play guitar and I don't sing. I would not have a role in the band if I were to attempt to sing."

The band's name was sparked by an eccentric engineer who constantly complained that members of the band were looming over him during a recording session. ''You're a loomer," he said repeatedly. There are plenty of odd names to go around in this contest. The Loomers will be up against the house band of the floral delivery firm 1-800-Flowers.com Inc. Its name? The Millennium Vase of Doom. By comparison, the band at Wilbraham-based Friendly's seems refreshingly normal: The Friendly Zone.

The band at Avid Technology of Tewksbury is called Cricket. It's not named after the band of rock legend Buddy Holly. ''It came from an inside joke, which basically had to do with the sound you hear when nobody applauds," said Cricket member David Schleifer, director of Avid's TV broadcasting product line.

Cricket claims to have an eclectic sound. ''It's everything from U2 and Pink Floyd to Chrissie Hynde and the Pretenders," said Schleifer, a guitarist. The band was formed to play at a company picnic. It has since played at other corporate events, including a gig at the Las Vegas Hard Rock Cafe during a convention.

Maybe Cricket's gig at a major venue has toughened it up for the showdown ahead. But then, the last-minute arrival of their jet-lagged drummer will give the Loomers just the right world-weary rock star ambience.

Or maybe they'll both be ground to powder by The Millennium Vase of Doom. The answer will be revealed at Irving Plaza in New York City on July 24.
