Space. The final frontier

More stuff, both real and virtual, inspires innovations in storage. You are how you store.

By Robert Gavin, Globe Staff  |  July 26, 2004

ARLINGTON -- Once upon a time, Raymond Santos and Danielle Buckley had a two-bedroom apartment, a 14-gigabyte hard drive, and plenty of room for everything. Then they bought a one-bedroom condo and a digital camera, and quickly learned that living happily ever after required something more: storage.

Faced with squeezing their possessions into a smaller place and ever-bigger digital files onto the hard drive, Santos and Buckley have spent a good deal of time and money to create places to put their stuff, both actual and virtual. They built a new closet, added cabinets, installed shelves, and rented a self-storage unit. On the digital front, they bought a new computer with 80 gigabytes of memory and recently added a second 120-gigabyte hard drive.

"Life is so much easier," said Santos, "when you have lots of storage."

Santos's sentiments are shared by millions of other Americans whose battles with 21st century clutter are making storage, if not the next big thing, at least a big thing. Whether mini-warehouses, mini-hard drives or mini shoe bins, the demand for space is only expected to grow, and as it does, storage -- pardon the expression -- is coming out of the closet.

Increasingly, you are how you store. Self-storage warehouses are moving from the industrial hinterlands into well-traveled retail corridors, embracing distinctive building designs such as medieval castles and Southern plantations, and including facilities to store wine, works of art, and even RVs. Data storage is a hip accessory: Car maker BMW just introduced a watch that includes an MP3 player with 256 megabytes of storage, while the icon of utilitarian chic, the Swiss Army knife, now boasts a model with a 64-megabyte memory "blade" that pops into a computer's USB port to store and retrieve data.

Closets, meanwhile, are no longer places to hide a mess, but rather features to show off, like new furniture or remodeled kitchens. Companies such as California Closets, of San Rafael, Calif., and the Container Store, of Coppell, Texas, are growing rapidly by offering closet organizing systems of hardwood-accented shelves, hangers, and bins that cost hundreds, if not thousands of dollars.

"Closets are much more a factor in being hip and cool," said Garrett Boone, chairman and cofounder of the Container Store, which will open its first New England location at the Chestnut Hill Shopping Center in the fall. "When I have people over, I take them to my closet."

While a variety of factors -- from soaring housing costs to falling technology prices -- help drive this rush to storage, industry analysts say two main forces underlie the trend: mobility and consumer culture. People moving from city to city rent self-storage units while they look for new homes. Consultants jetting from client to client carry data-filled flash memory cards on their key chains.

Singles who become couples expand closet space when combining the belongings of two households. And families add hard drives to hold mementos produced by digital cameras and video recorders.

The result: a storage boom. Self-storage units have doubled to 13 million over the last decade, according to MiniCo Inc., the Phoenix-based publisher of the Self-Storage Almanac, while unit sales of flash memory cards are expected to quadruple between 2002 and 2007, according to IDC, a Framingham technology market research firm.

Sales at the closely held Container Store are growing an average of 25 percent a year, according to company officials. And the all-time bestseller for Real Simple, the magazine for busy professional women? The 2003 closet issue.

"We live in a society of material affluence that puts emphasis on more and emphasis on new, and sees shopping as entertainment," said James Ennis, a sociology professor at Tufts University. "People just have more stuff."

For Santos, 29, and Buckley, 27, the storage challenge began when they decided to give up their two-bedroom apartment with basement to buy a new home. They soon found all they could afford in Boston's hot housing market was a one-bedroom condo without a basement.

The soon-to-be-married couple held a yard sale and dramatically reduced their inventory by selling off their "doubles": the extra TV, stereo, and furniture they ended up with after they combined possessions accumulated as singles. Then they tore out the bedroom closet, invested about $700 in materials, and, following a design by Buckley, an architect, built a new closet that boosted hanging space by one-third and tripled the shelf space.

They added cabinets and wire shelving in the kitchen, but still squeezed, rented a self-storage unit in Waltham, where they keep, among other things, Christmas decorations in summer, and their air conditioner and bikes in winter. "If we're not organized," said Buckley, "we'd be overwhelmed."

Meanwhile, Santos's photography and video hobbies began to overwhelm their computer's 14-gigabyte hard drive. The problem, in many ways, was caused by advancing storage technology, which increased the capacity of digital camera memory cards from holding dozens of photos to holding hundreds. Better technology also provided the solution.

For about the same money they paid for their old computer, they bought a new one with an 80-gigabyte hard drive and a DVD burner. Then, for about $60, they added a second 120 gigabyte drive.

The storage adventures of Santos and Buckley illustrate the demographic, economic, and technological trends that analysts say will continue to drive the race for space. Charles Wu, managing director for BayNorth Capital LLC, a Boston real estate investment firm, noted the convergence of rising housing prices, which often mean smaller homes, and the rising age of marriage, which typically allows each partner more time to accumulate things. "Suddenly," said Wu, "you have two toaster ovens."

In addition, baby boomers, the nation's dominant demographic, are nearing retirement, with many saving heirlooms to pass on to their children. That means lots of people with lots of stuff moving into smaller homes, forecasting even more self-storage. BayNorth and its affiliates have invested about $100 million in self-storage projects in recent years, said Wu.

As advancing age fuels demand for real storage, advancing technology is doing the same in the virtual world. Faster Internet connections, for example, mean bigger digital files getting e-mailed, swapped, and downloaded, which in turn creates the need for bigger cyber closets.

Back in their tiny condo, Santos and Buckley say storage is never far from their thoughts, even at work. Santos, a public relations professional, represents a data storage company, while Buckley, an architect, finds herself designing increasingly elaborate closets for clients. Their home, meanwhile, is a living catalog of storage solutions, from the plastic boxes of winter clothes kept under the bed, to the dining room table with a flip top, under which they store napkins and table linens.

The computer with the extra hard drive sits a few steps from the dining room table. So far, said Santos, 200 gigabytes of memory is more than enough to hold his digital possessions. He's only used about 15 percent of its memory.

"If only we could store our dining room table on the computer," said Buckley.
