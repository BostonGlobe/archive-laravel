Yukos warns oil exports may be interrupted

By Vladimir Isachenkov, Associated Press Writer  |  July 22, 2004

MOSCOW -- Russian oil giant Yukos, which produces about 2 percent of the world's oil, warned Thursday it could run out of money within a month and may not be able to fulfill export contracts as it faces an enormous bill for back taxes.

Yukos renewed its plea to the government to be allowed to stagger payment of its $3.4 billion tax bill for 2000, but the government has shown no sign of interest and analysts interpreted the call as a reflection of desperation rather than hope.

Justice Ministry officials announced this week that Yuganskneftegaz, Yukos' largest production unit, was being readied for sale by the state in a move to settle the tax claim.

"The loss of Yugansk would be a very serious blow to the company, and would certainly put our ability to continue as a going concern under question," Yukos CEO Steven Theede said at a news conference.

Theede wouldn't comment on when the company could be forced to file for bankruptcy, but warned that if Yukos is forced to stop production, the global market would be certain to feel the impact and "how long it will take for things to smooth out only time will tell."

Yukos exports about 75 percent of its 1.7-million-barrel daily production, primarily to Eastern European countries such as Hungary, Slovakia and Poland whose economies are still laboring to recover from decades of communist mismanagement.

The economy of the former Soviet republic of Lithuania could be particularly vulnerable to disturbances in supplies by Yukos because the company controls the country's major refinery, said Kakha Kiknavelidze, an oil and gas analyst at Moscow's Troika Dialog brokerage.

Selling off Yuganskneftegaz would significantly reduce Yukos' revenues and "in this way, even if the current liquidity crisis is overcome, the company ... may still be forced to file for bankruptcy and it may not be able to fulfill export contracts," the Yukos statement said.

Yukos shares dropped more than 13 percent in trading in Moscow after the company's warning.

"If no alternative solutions are found, the restricted access we have to our cash means that we will essentially run out of cash and not be able to fund our business operating expense and obligations some time during the first half of August," Theede said.

The company also faces a tax claim of $3.3 billion for 2001, and further large bills are expected.

Yukos, which is blocked by court order from selling assets itself in order to meet the tax payment, has sought compromises including being allowed to pay the 2000 bill over two years. However, the government has shown no willingness to compromise.

Theede said that 11 letters Yukos sent to the government over the past six weeks in a bid to negotiate a settlement have been left unanswered.

"We're not sure exactly what they want," he said. "We will continue to try and come up with other alternatives that may be more or less interesting to them."

But analysts saw Yukos efforts to reach a compromise as futile.

"It's like at high school when you keep sending letters to a girl in class who never responds. They still don't get the message," said Ronald Smith, an oil and gas analyst at Moscow's Renaissance Capital.

The government's stance has raised speculation that its ultimate goal is to carve off major pieces of the company, possibly to sell them into Kremlin-friendly hands.

"I don't know," Theede answered wearily when asked whether the Kremlin wants to destroy Yukos. "I hope that's not true. I don't think it's in the best interests of the country."

The tax bill is part of a series of legal actions against Yukos and its former CEO Mikhail Khodorkovsky that many analysts see as a Kremlin-initiated drive to punish Khodorkovsky for funding opposition political parties and to stifle his own presumed political ambitions.

Khodorkovsky has been jailed since October on charges of fraud, forgery and tax evasion. The trial of him and a close associate, Platon Lebedev, is underway in Moscow. Khodorkovsky has proposed to surrender his stake in Yukos to settle the tax debt, but there has been no official reaction to the offer.

Yukos accounts for about 20 percent of Russia's oil production and employs more than 100,000 people. Its disintegration could affect production of Russia's prime export commodity and undermine President Vladimir Putin's prestige abroad.

It owns production units outside Russia, including Mazeikiu Nefta in Lithuania and Transpetrol in Slovakia. Company officials said it does not intend to sell those units.
