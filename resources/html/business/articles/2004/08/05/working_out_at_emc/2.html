Working out at EMC

August 5, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Technical support engineer Deborah Ohman, 51, of Whitinsville, used HealthLink to research Ultracet, a medication prescribed for back and neck pain. She said the doctor who prescribed it for her felt she could take it for life.

Ohman logged onto HealthLink, which is powered by Web MD, and discovered Ultracet cost $135.63 retail. Her copayment: $40. EMC's payment was $95.63. The website also revealed a similar medicine, Tylenol 3, cost $28, with a $10 copayment. The company's payment was $18.

Ohman also learned that Ultracet should not be taken more than five days in a row; it can cause liver damage. She said the data she gathered should convince her physician to alter her drug regimen. "This gives me ammunition to talk to the doctor and say, 'Well, there are other pills that are comparable,' " Ohman said.

With obesity taking a toll on Americans' health, EMC is counting on DASH to reduce hypertension, or abnormally high blood pressure often triggered by excessive weight gain. Currently, obesity accounts for $70 billion or 7 percent of the nation's total healthcare costs, reports UnumProvident Corp., the disability insurance provider. Of that, US employers spend $12 billion because of absenteeism, higher medical costs, strokes, and other serious ailments.

DASH lets workers enter personalized data in their computers about weight, blood pressure, and exercise. Then, it sends e-mails about nutrition, exercise, diet, or hypertension. The dietary program is run by Boston University Medical Center, which has tested it at EMC for more than two years. About 5,000 workers are enrolled in DASH. EMC is the only company in the country using the program, but the medical center plans to offer it to others soon.

EMC's spokeswoman Anne Pace said the information workers submit to DASH is confidential.

DASH is paying off, said Dr. Thomas Moore, acting provost and director of human research at BU Medical Center. He said nearly 4,000 EMC workers have shed an average of 3.2 pounds per person and also reduced their blood pressure significantly. High blood pressure can lead to strokes and heart disease.

Brad Barker, 49, of Boylston, lost 40 pounds since he switched last year to the DASH diet. He also used HealthLink to customize his exercise plan. Barker said his doctor warned him last year that his blood pressure was "on the higher end of normal." Today, however, his cholesterol reading is down and so is his blood pressure. And he's lowered his resting heart rate.

"I had ballooned to 200 pounds," said Barker, who is 5 feet 8 inches. "I had too much fat in my diet, not enough protein and not enough fruit and vegetables. I was averaging 2,500 calories per day. Now, I am at 1,500 calories. This morning, I was at 157 pounds."

While EMC has had some early success, specialists say it can take years for employers to see real cost savings from wellness programs like DASH and HealthLink.

"Many of the things you can do to change health will have a savings 10 to 15 years off," said Robert J. Blendon, professor of health policy and political analysis at the Harvard School of Public Health. "If people stay with a company for their whole lives, there might be a justification for that. But if I have workers for four years and they get involved, then leave and go somewhere else, there is less savings."

EMC believes it will see immediate savings if it helps workers keep serious illnesses at bay. But getting workers to maintain their commitment can be difficult. The company concedes that its biggest challenge will be helping workers stay healthy. Last year, EMC enticed them to use HealthLink with incentives like discounted Red Sox tickets. The company says it will find new ways to keep workers engaged.

"Now that we have them signed up in HealthLink, we want them to keep using it," said EMC's Pace.
