Auto insurance plan splits coalition

August 5, 2004

Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly's proposal to modify the plan for assigning high-risk drivers among automobile insurers has split the once-united front for auto insurance reform. The plan before Insurance Commissioner Julianne M. Bowler would assign drivers no company wants to insure voluntarily to companies on a random basis based on market share. Reilly backs the plan but has proposed limiting the discretion of companies in making assignments, requiring that drivers with a clean driving record for three years be excluded. An estimated 70 percent of the state's drivers would qualify. Reilly's proposal has the support of two critics of the overall plan -- consumer advocates and Commerce Insurance of Webster, the state's largest auto insurer. But a coalition representing nearly every other auto insurer has come out against Reilly's amendment, urging Bowler to approve the overall plan as is while continuing to study Reilly's proposal. Bowler is expected to rule this month. (Bruce Mohl)

Putnam assets dropped by $3b in July

Putnam Investments' assets under management declined by $3 billion in July even as the company, which has been suffering from client defections, began including money overseen by a subsidiary. Putnam, itself a unit of Marsh & McLennan Cos., said on its website that managed assets stood at $210 billion on July 31, down from $213 billion at the end of June. Boston-based Putnam previously counted half of PanAgora Asset Management's $16 billion in assets and is now claiming the rest in its total. Chief executive Charles Haldeman has been trying to restore investor confidence since last fall, when regulators sued the company for permitting excessive trading by fund managers. Putnam agreed in April to pay $110 million to settle the cases. The company, already hurt by poor performance in the three-year bear market, saw withdrawals accelerate after the scandal. In the 10 months ended July 30, the company's assets dropped 23 percent while the benchmark Standard & Poor's 500 index gained 11 percent. Putnam managed about $272 billion Sept. 30, before it was sued by regulators. (Bloomberg)

Unit-pricing exemptions to be increased

Governor Mitt Romney is expected to sign a bill that would slightly expand the number of retailers that don't have to post standardized unit prices for the products they sell. The proposed law would allow any store with less than $5 million in sales to avoid the requirement of posting prices based on standard measurements, such as price per ounce or price per pound. Charles Carroll, deputy director at the state's Division of Standards, said he favors exempting small convenience stores that don't carry a wide assortment of products, but he worried that larger drugstores might also be exempt. He said he contacted CVS and was assured the chain had no intention of abandoning unit pricing even if a store did qualify for the exemption. "I think the impact of the law is going to be minimal," Carroll said. (Bruce Mohl)

Berkshire Realty, investors settle suit

Berkshire Realty Co. shareholders who sought more money when the owner of apartment buildings went private in 1999 agreed to settle their lawsuit against the company for $6.25 million, court papers show. The settlement is subject to approval by Chancery Court Judge William B. Chandler III at a Tuesday hearing in Georgetown, Del. Lawyers for the shareholders will ask for $2.45 million in fees and expenses from the settlement fund, the papers say. Boston-based Berkshire, a real estate investment trust, was sold in 1999 to a group of investors led by chairman Douglas Krupp and real estate funds managed by Blackstone Group Inc. and Goldman, Sachs & Co. The sale price included $660 million in cash, $600 million in debt, and $60 million in other costs. Under the agreement, the dissident shareholders will receive an additional 17 cents per share for their holdings, court documents show. Berkshire's sale price in 1999 was $12.25 per share. (Bloomberg)

Ohio hires Fidelity, Smith Breeden

Fidelity Investments and Smith Breeden Associates Inc. were hired to each manage about $300 million of US bonds by the Ohio Public Employees Retirement System, the 10th-largest public pension fund with $60 billion in assets. Morgan Stanley was fired from managing the $600 million in bonds because the pension decided it wanted the money solely in investment-grade US bonds, said the fund's investment manager Roger Fox. Morgan Stanley's portfolio included some high-yield and emerging market bonds. Boston-based Fidelity is the largest US mutual fund company with managed assets of $1 trillion. Smith Breeden is a research and trading firm based in North Carolina that manages $13 billion, according to the pension fund. (Bloomberg)

Windows security update expected soon

Microsoft Corp. is close to releasing the biggest update ever for the Windows operating system, aiming to plug holes that have led to massive security problems for computer users the world over. Microsoft senior product manager Matt Pilla said it is expected to release the update for Windows XP, called Service Pack 2, "in the coming days." He would not be more specific. Microsoft has previously said it expected to release Service Pack 2 this month after delaying the release to improve compatibility between the new security functions and other applications. The update comes in response to a series of Internet viruses and other attacks that have slowed businesses and hurt consumers. Service Pack 2 bolsters security on a number of fronts. Among the changes, it automatically turns on a firewall to better guard against attempts to infiltrate personal computers. The update also fortifies protections on the Internet Explorer browser and offers tougher policing against e-mail-borne attacks. It also provides a user with much more information about what programs are trying to contact the computer, and gives the user more chances to accept or decline. (AP)

Pilots union rips Delta concessions plan

The pilots union at Delta Air Lines said the struggling carrier will edge closer to bankruptcy if management continues to seek $1 billion in concessions from them without asking other stakeholders to tighten their belts. The chairman of the union's executive committee, John Malone, made the comment in a strongly worded letter to other pilots. "Management's proposal appears to have only one purpose -- to exploit the current situation and attack our profession by destroying our contract," Malone wrote. The letter was a response to the airline's request Friday for the pilots union to make $1 billion in concessions, including a 35 percent pay cut along with work, scheduling, and pension changes. The union, which represents 7,500 active Delta pilots, had proposed a 23 percent pay cut and other concessions that would save the airline up to $705 million a year. Delta, the nation's third-largest airline, has warned that it may have to file for bankruptcy if it doesn't get deep concessions from pilots. (AP)

FAA pushing to fix delays at O'Hare

Persistent delays at O'Hare International in Chicago have a cascading effect through the nation's air system. So federal officials now are warning that they will cap flights there if the airlines do not fix the problem. Marion Blakey, head of the Federal Aviation Administration, summoned executives from every major domestic airline to FAA headquarters in Washington to jawbone them into agreeing to reduce their O'Hare schedules. The discussions were to continue today. O'Hare has more takeoffs and landings each year than any other airport in the world. Seven in 10 passengers flying to O'Hare connect to other flights. About two-thirds of arrivals are on time this year, compared with the 82 percent systemwide goal the FAA has set. "If it weren't for O'Hare, we'd be making that goal," Blakey said. (AP)
