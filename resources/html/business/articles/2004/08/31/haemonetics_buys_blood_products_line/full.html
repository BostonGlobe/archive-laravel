BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Haemonetics buys blood products line

August 31, 2004

Haemonetics Corp. acquired Harvest Technologies Corp.'s Bloodless Surgery Station line, in an effort to expand its surgical blood salvage products and to enter the surgical patient management market. Haemonetics said it paid an undisclosed amount of cash to buy Plymouth-based Harvest Technologies' Bloodless Surgery Station, which is an integrated product for blood conservation and surgical blood salvage. The Braintree maker of automated blood-processing systems said it plans to integrate the manufacturing and marketing of Bloodless Surgery Station products into its operations over the remainder of the fiscal year. Haemonetics plans to service the small number of existing customers during the integration period and expects to begin a broader, limited market release in the first quarter of fiscal 2006. (Dow Jones)

Airline, pilots union resume talks

Talks between US Airways Group Inc. and the union representing pilots have resumed, the Air Line Pilots Association said. The pilots' negotiating committee met with management Sunday, a week after talks broke down, said Jack Stephan, spokesman for the pilots' group. The pilots offered a proposal Saturday, and the company presented a counterproposal Sunday, said Stephan, who declined to comment on specifics. Discussions resumed yesterday after both sides recessed for the night, union officials said. US Airways has said that new labor deals with unions are necessary to avoid a return to bankruptcy court and possible liquidation. The airline has warned that bankruptcy looms if deals are not reached by at least Sept. 30. (AP)

Earnings forecast for year lowered

Shares in Tyson Foods Inc. fell more than 8 percent after the company lowered its earnings forecast for the fiscal year, saying attempts to hedge grain prices backfired as demand for beef weakened. Costs associated with closing plants also will affect its bottom line. The company said it now expects to earn between $1.08 and $1.15 per share for the fiscal year ending Oct. 2, down 18 cents a share from its earlier forecast, which was raised after third-quarter profits more than doubled. Tyson said its earnings will include a charge of 11 cents per share for the effects it has felt from the drop in beef business that followed the mad cow scare. Tyson also said it expects to see earnings reduced by 7 cents per share for plant closings in the first nine months of the fiscal year. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call had forecast Tyson would earn $1.45 per share before charges. Shares fell $1.47 to $16.26. (AP)

Fannie Mae warns on house prices

US housing industry economists cautioned that rapid house price gains in some areas of the country may not be sustainable. ''There is an increasing risk of price declines in some of those areas, especially those in which job growth has been the most anemic," said David Berson, chief economist at mortgage finance provider Fannie Mae. Berson spoke on a conference call organized by the Homeownership Alliance about the outlook for the housing industry. Economists forecast a strong second half of 2004 for the industry -- in spite of rising mortgage interest rates -- as the economy gains strength. Some regions of the country, such as Las Vegas; Anaheim, Calif.; Baltimore; and Washington, D.C., have seen sharp home price gains, the economists said. At the same time, other areas, including South Bend, Ind.; Syracuse, N.Y.; Little Rock, Ark.; New Orleans; and Dallas, have had 2 percent or less appreciation for the year, they said. (Reuters)

Plans set to recall flight attendants

United Airlines said it will recall 375 flight attendants from voluntary furlough this fall as it operates more international flights that require larger crews. The bankrupt number two US airline, a unit of UAL Corp., said increased aircraft utilization also contributed to the need to recall flight attendants. United will recall 175 flight attendants in October and 200 in November. It will begin notifying flight attendants this week. The 375 recalls are in addition to the scheduled return of 371 flight attendants on Oct. 31, the company said. (Reuters)

Millions of homeowners underinsured

Millions of US homeowners are underinsured, largely because of a change in the way policies are now sold, according to a story in today's editions of The New York Times. In the late 1990s, insurers began phasing out coverage that guaranteed the replacement of a destroyed home despite the cost to the insurer. Firms substituted a policy in its place with a similar-sounding name that paid only the amount stated on the policy plus perhaps 20 to 25 percent. AIR Worldwide in Boston estimates that many upper-income homes in New England are undervalued by 30 to 40 percent.

Stewart's broker agrees to lifetime ban

Martha Stewart's former stockbroker, convicted of helping her lie about a 2001 stock sale, has been barred from working for brokers or investment advisers. Peter Bacanovic and the Securities and Exchange Commission agreed to the ban Friday. Stewart and Bacanovic were each sentenced to five months in prison and five months of house arrest after their convictions in March. They are free pending appeal. Bacanovic would have to reapply to the SEC if he wanted to work again for a brokerage. It was not immediately clear whether he would have to go through a waiting period before any application. (AP)

Circuit City picks Verizon Wireless

Circuit City Stores Inc. said it will stop selling T-Mobile USA mobile phones and give the business solely to Verizon Wireless, its other current mobile phone partner, which will open in-store sales counters. Verizon Wireless, the largest US mobile-phone company by subscribers, will staff sales areas in more than 570 stores, said Circuit City, the second-largest US electronics chain. Circuit City hopes concentrating on a single provider will boost store traffic and sales of higher-priced items such as digital televisions and DVD players. Circuit City will end T-Mobile sales Sept. 7, T-Mobile USA spokesman Richard Brudvik-Linder said. (Bloomberg)

Deals near for purchase of 4 casinos

A group led by Colony Capital LLC is close to reaching an agreement to buy the Atlantic City Hilton and three casinos in the Chicago area and Mississippi for about $1.26 billion, according to several people familiar with the deal. Two of the casinos are owned by Harrah's Entertainment Inc. and the other two are owned by Caesars Entertainment Inc., and the deals are fallout from Harrah's planned acquisition of Caesars. By selling off assets in areas where the Harrah's-Caesars combination would dominate the local market, Harrah's aims to smooth any antitrust concerns of the Federal Trade Commission or state regulators, said a person familiar with Harrah's. (Wall Street Journal)

US appeals cable Net classification

The government has asked the Supreme Court to reverse an appeals court ruling that could lead to heavier regulation of cable companies' high-speed Internet service. The Federal Communications Commission asked the high court to overturn a federal appeals court in San Francisco, which ruled that cable broadband should be classified as a telecommunications service rather than a more lightly regulated information service, the FCC said. The appeal was filed Friday by the FCC and the acting US solicitor general. ''Applying taxes, regulations, and concepts from a century ago to today's cutting-edge services will only stifle innovation and competition," FCC chairman Michael Powell said. (Reuters)
