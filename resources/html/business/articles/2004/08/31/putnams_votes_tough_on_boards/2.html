Putnam's votes tough on boards

August 31, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- The Vanguard funds, which own nearly $500 billion in stocks, said they voted against at least one director last year at 60 percent of the companies they hold. All told, the firm withheld votes for 30 percent of the 19,000 directorships that it voted on.

Vanguard's support for directors actually increased last year, the Valley Forge, Pa., firm disclosed on its website yesterday: A year ago, it voted against at least one director at 70 percent of the companies in which it invested.

Most firms have not released such summaries of their votes yet. Mutual fund firms face a deadline today of disclosing for the first time all their proxy votes for the past year. In addition to filing those records with the US Securities and Exchange Commission, most are posting them on their websites as well, to satisfy the new federal regulations. Under the new rule, firms must report their proxy votes publicly every Aug. 31.

Julie Gozan, a corporate governance specialist at Amalgamated Bank, a New York bank that is an activist investor, said the new disclosure will permit investors in 401(k) plans and other retirement vehicles to hold their mutual fund managers accountable.

''Once the mutual funds start disclosing how they are voting," Gozan said, ''then all the individuals who may have a vested interest will know what's being done in their name."

Amalgamated filed several proxy measures at major corporations last year, for shareholders to vote on. The biggest issue for the bank, she said, is executive compensation.

''When executive compensation is out of whack, in terms of very excessive compensation with poor performance, that can be an indicator of a whole host of other problems at a company. That can indicate that there isn't enough independence on a board," Gozan said.

Putnam voted in several instances last year against poison pills, or antitakeover plans; in favor of putting severance deals to a shareholder vote; and in favor of putting directors up to a shareholder vote every year.

Across all proxy issues, a Globe analysis of the data found that Putnam voted with management 81.4 percent of the time, and against management 18.6 percent of the time.
