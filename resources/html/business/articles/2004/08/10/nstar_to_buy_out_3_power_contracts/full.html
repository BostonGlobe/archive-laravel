NStar to buy out 3 power contracts

August 10, 2004

NStar, the largest utility company in Massachusetts, said it expects to save customers $85 million from buying out three power purchase agreements from wholesale power producers. The utility is asking the Massachusetts Department of Telecommunications and Energy to recover the undisclosed buyout costs from customers, according to filings with the agency. The savings estimate includes the cost of the buyouts. State legislation from 1998 required utilities to sell generating plants and reduce reliance on long-term contracts. The completed agreements cover 385 megawatts, or enough to supply about 310,000 homes. NStar supplies 1.3 million customers in Boston and northeastern Massachusetts. The company since 1998 has been buying power for its retail customers through six-month and one-year contracts obtained from competitive bidding, said an NStar spokesman. (Bloomberg)

Putnam hires stock manager in London

Putman Investments, a mutual fund company that's aiming to double the assets it manages in Europe, hired Mark Pollard as its first stock fund manager based in London. The 45-year-old is taking on the new role of European chief investment officer, spokeswoman Laura McNamara said. He joins Anton Simon, a bond manager, in working in the Boston-based company's London office. Pollard originally joined Putnam in 1990 and a decade later left for Lazard Asset Management in London. Last year, he resigned from Lazard to form hedge fund company Jura Capital LLC with two colleagues. Putnam, with $210 billion in assets worldwide, wants to oversee at least $28 billion for clients in Europe by 2008. The firm has been losing clients in the United States since being implicated in the probe into improper mutual fund trading. Its assets fell about 23 percent in the 10 months through July. (Bloomberg)

Ruling lets Nike bar ex-official from rival

Nike Inc., the world's largest maker of athletic footwear, can enforce a contract that bars a former employee from working at Reebok International Ltd. for one year, a federal Appeals Court ruled. The 9th US Circuit Court of Appeals in San Francisco upheld a court order that prevented Eugene McCarthy, who was sales director for Nike products named after former basketball player Michael Jordan, from becoming vice president of US footwear sales at Reebok for a year. Reebok, the second biggest US athletic shoe maker, is focusing on selling sneakers to city teens in an attempt to take sales from its larger rival. Nike argued that McCarthy was privy to confidential information, such as debut dates for Jordan brand shoes through spring 2004, that gave Reebok an unfair advantage. A Reebok spokeswoman said the company doesn't plan to appeal the decision because the restriction period ends this month. (Bloomberg)

Bookkeeping errors reach $453m

MCI Inc., which sought bankruptcy protection in July 2002 after disclosing the largest accounting fraud in US history, said it made $453 million of bookkeeping errors during its reorganization. MCI mistakenly reduced accounts receivable by $301 million when it restated 2000 results, the company said in a quarterly Securities and Exchange Commission filing. MCI erroneously reduced some 2003 liabilities by $152 million, the company said. The company spent $250 million over 21 months to unwind $11 billion in fraudulent accounting. The accounts receivable error inflated last-year's net income by 1.2 percent, while the misreported liabilities represent less than one percent of the 2003 total. (Bloomberg)

Clients withdraw $1.1b from Janus funds

Janus Capital Group Inc., the money manager that settled regulatory complaints of improper mutual fund trading in April, said clients withdrew a net $1.1 billion from its mutual funds in July. The amount was down from the $1.4 billion individual and institutional investors took out in June. The company had $129 billion in assets under management as of July 31, a drop of 4.7 percent from $135.4 billion a month earlier. Most of the decline was due to ''market depreciation," Janus said. The ''total net outflows were the lowest they've been since August of last year," chief executive Steve Scheid said. Janus, whose assets under management peaked at more than $300 billion four years ago, said last month that a client plans to redeem $5 billion in funds. The undisclosed customer will take out the money by the end of the year, the company has said. (Bloomberg)

Dow Jones, main union reach agreement

Publisher Dow Jones & Co. said its main union approved a new contract covering 1,600 employees after a bitter labor dispute that had spurred a byline strike at its Wall Street Journal newspaper. The new agreement replaces a contract with the Independent Association of Publisher's Employees that expired in April 2003, and calls for wage increases of 2.5 percent in 2004, 3.25 percent in 2005, and 3.5 percent in 2006. No wage increase will be paid for 2003, during which a freeze on salaries was in effect. The contract, which was approved 819 to 96, also provides for lump-sum payments of $2,000 to union employees and changes some healthcare benefits, including the introduction of employee-paid premiums. (Reuters)

Citigroup buys Knight Trading line

Citigroup Inc. said it is purchasing the derivatives markets business from the Knight Trading Group Inc. for $225 million in cash. James Forese, head of global equities at Citigroup, said the transaction ''will add significant scale to our US equities business and provide Citigroup with top-tier order-routing and market making capabilities in our growing derivatives business." Citigroup said the purchase will give it ''electronic and floor-based market-making capabilities in listed equity and fixed-income options." The New York bank said the transaction was subject to regulatory approval and was expected to close in the fourth quarter. (AP)

Shipper pays $120m for Parcel Direct

FedEx Corp. said it is paying $120 million cash for Parcel Direct, a company that consolidates shipping for catalogue and Internet retailers. Parcel Direct will become a subsidiary of FedEx Ground, the shipping giant's trucking company. Primary business for the new subsidiary is expected to come from companies selling their products over the Internet or through catalog retailers for home shipment to consumers. ''It's a specially designed network for slower moving, lightweight packages," said Jess Bunn, a FedEx spokesman. FedEx said the acquisition will close in the second quarter and is not expected to have a material effect on 2005 financial results. (AP)

5 more states subpoena Caremark Rx

Caremark Rx Inc., the pharmacy benefit manager under investigation in 19 states, said that five more attorneys general have subpoenaed the company on its business practices. The company is now under investigation by 23 states and Washington, D.C., Caremark said in a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission. State attorneys general are conducting investigations of major pharmacy benefit managers' business practices. Medco Health Solutions Inc., Caremark's biggest rival, paid 20 states $29.3 million in April to settle charges the company took payments to switch patients to certain heart drugs. New York State Attorney General Eliot Spitzer accused Express Scripts Inc., the third-biggest in the industry, of fraud last week. Caremark ''intends to fully cooperate" with the investigations, the company said in the filing. A spokesman for the company did not immediately return a telephone call seeking comment. (Bloomberg)
