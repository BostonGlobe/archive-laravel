Iraq set to resume normal oil production

By Bruce Stanley, AP Business Writer  |  August 10, 2004

LONDON -- Production resumed at Iraq's vast southern oil fields after authorities reached an accord with militant Shiites who had threatened to attack the country's vital export pipelines for crude, an Iraqi oil official told The Associated Press late Tuesday.

Oil markets welcomed the news, with U.S. crude futures falling by 32 cents a barrel in late New York trading.

Iraq's South Oil Co. reversed a decision it made the previous day to curtail output as a precaution against possible sabotage by supporters of radical Shiite cleric Muqtada al-Sadr. The cleric's followers had warned they might attack pipelines in southern Iraq unless the government halted crude exports.

Iraq exported 1.75 million barrels a day before South Oil curtailed production. Global oil markets -- already worried about possible supply constraints on crude from Russia and Saudi Arabia -- grew anxious about the sharp reduction in Iraqi output.

"It slowed down considerably. Now it is resuming full blast," said the official, speaking to AP from Baghdad on condition of anonymity. He didn't quantify the earlier, lower volume of output.

Iraq has the world's second-largest proven crude reserves, most of them in the southern region, and oil is the country's only major source of export earnings needed to rebuild its devastated economy.

The Iraqi official credited South Oil's general director Jabbar El-Leaby with having reached an agreement with al-Sadr's supporters.

"They maintain good relations with him, and they respect him. He's from the South and is a Shia and has every kind of qualification to tackle the problem," the official said.

El-Leaby could not be reached for immediate comment.

South Oil had reduced crude output and pipeline shipments to storage tanks on the southern Faw peninsula, but it was still able to use oil in storage to load tankers waiting at offshore terminals in the Gulf. One tanker was loading crude late Tuesday at the rate of 15,000 barrels per hour, the official said.

"Loading is more or less normal," the official said.

Oil futures reached new intraday highs earlier Tuesday, with September contracts of U.S. light crude hitting $45.04 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange. U.S. crude contracts slid in afternoon trading to $44.52, down 32 cents from Monday's record New York close of $44.84.

In other Nymex trading, unleaded gasoline for September delivery fell 0.52 cent to settle at $1.2349 per gallon, while September heating oil fell 1.58 cent to $1.1698 per gallon. September natural gas rose 9.8 cents to $5.791 per 1,000 cubic feet.

In London, September contracts of Brent crude touched $41.69 a barrel on the International Petroleum Exchange. They dropped in late trading to $41.28, down 28 cents from Monday's all-time high closing price of $41.56.

Attacks on Iraq's southern pipelines have marked an escalation in the insurgency there. Iraq exports almost all its oil from two loading facilities in the Gulf -- the Basra Terminal and Khor al-Amaya -- and the pipelines supplying them have become the country's economic lifeline.

On July 15, saboteurs punctured one of two key crude export pipelines in the south, breaching a line that had resumed operating barely a week earlier, after a shutdown that had halved exports from the south for almost five days.

Until recently, saboteurs had limited their attacks to Iraq's other major export route -- a pipeline from oil fields near the northern city of Kirkuk to the Mediterranean port of Ceyhan, Turkey. This northern pipeline has seldom operated since the U.S.-led invasion due to the frequency of sabotage.

Attacks on oil facilities have extended to office buildings in the capital, Baghdad. Mortar shells have fallen several times on or near the heavily guarded Oil Ministry itself, and the headquarters of the State Oil Marketing Organization, the government-run agency that arranges all official sales of Iraqi crude, has recently become another prominent target.

Early Tuesday afternoon, the Iraqi oil official said, armed men in a van and a pickup truck fired a volley of bullets at the SOMO building. Security guards shot back, apparently killing one of the attackers. Although the building was full of employees at the time, no one inside was hurt, and the structure suffered minimal damage.

Gunmen also fired a few bullets at the building on Sunday, but the latest attack was the biggest so far, the official said.

"This time it was serious business," the official said.

Iraqi Interior ministry spokesman Sabah Kadhim, speaking in Baghdad, said he had received no information about such an attack.
