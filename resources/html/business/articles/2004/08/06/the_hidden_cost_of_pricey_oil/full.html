The hidden cost of pricey oil

Expensive energy hits spending, jobs, and fuels inflation worries

By Charles Stein, Globe Staff  |  August 6, 2004

Wal-Mart Stores Inc. yesterday reported its second straight month of weak sales. The main culprit: high gasoline prices.

ADVERTISEMENT

Like a tax, expensive energy takes money out of consumers' pockets that might otherwise be available to spend on other items. And like a tax, it has a depressing effect on the economy.

Oil prices spiked to more than $44 a barrel yesterday on the New York Mercantile Exchange, a new 21-year high, on reports of problems facing the Russian oil giant Yukos and fresh worries about terrorism. The news triggered a major slide in the stock market. The Dow Jones industrial average fell 163.48 to close at 9,963.03. The Nasdaq Composite index fell 33.43 to 1,821.63, its lowest finish of the year.

Oil prices have stayed stubbornly high for months, defying predictions that renewed production from Iraq would calm world markets and bring prices down.

High oil prices won't sink the economy, analysts say. But if prices stay in the $40 range, the result will be higher inflation, fewer jobs, a weaker stock market, and crankier consumers who might be less inclined to vote for the incumbent -- President Bush -- in this fall's election.

"High gasoline prices make people nervous about inflation, and they reinforce all of our concerns about energy dependence on the Middle East," said Mark Zandi, chief economist at Economy.com, a Pennsylvania forecasting firm.

Democratic presidential nominee John F. Kerry has talked about gasoline prices, but he has not made the issue a top priority in his campaign thus far. In his acceptance speech in Boston, Kerry devoted one sentence to energy issues. In other appearances he said he would spend more money on alternative energy research and provide tax credits to companies making fuel-efficient cars. "He's been talking about the long term," said Thorsten Fischer, an energy specialist at Economy.com. "He isn't addressing the current problem at all."

Bush offers a starkly different approach. In a campaign stop in Arkansas this week, Vice President Dick Cheney said the United States needs to boost energy production at home with more drilling. "We've taken large chunks of the country and put it off limits to any exploration or development," Cheney said. Even if domestic drilling were to increase, say analysts, increased production would take years to come on line.

Virtually all energy specialists agree there is enough oil on the market to meet the needs of a growing world economy. But to meet those needs, most oil-producing countries are pumping as much as they can. As a result, the reserve capacity, or cushion, is very small. Among the OPEC countries only Saudi Arabia has the ability to increase production in the near future. The upshot: every time there are hints of trouble in the oil business -- in Russia, Nigeria, Venezuela, or Iraq -- frightened traders panic and bid up the price of oil.

"Every bit of bad news has a disproportionate impact on prices," said Fischer. He still expects oil prices to retreat to a more normal $30 a barrel eventually, but similarly optimistic forecasts have been off the mark for months.

High energy prices have a direct effect on economic output, increasing costs across the board. Nariman Behravesh, chief economist for Global Insight in Lexington, says a $10 increase in the price of a barrel of oil roughly shaves a half a percentage point off the growth in the gross domestic product. In other words, if the economy grew 4 percent a year with $30 oil, it would grow 3.5 percent with $40 oil. According to Behravesh, a year of $40 oil would cost the US economy about 500,000 jobs. It would also boost inflation about half a percentage point. Consumer prices have been rising at a 3.2 percent clip this year.

Expensive energy does most of its damage by depressing consumer spending. Wal-Mart offers a case in point. When gasoline prices first jumped this year, Wal-Mart executives estimated that their typical customer had seen his weekly gasoline bill climb by $7 over the course of a year. For a store that caters to high-income customers, that difference would be trivial. But for Wal-Mart, which serves mainly middle-class and working-class customers, $7 a week makes a difference.

If expensive energy is a tax, it is a regressive one, because it hurts those of modest means far more than the well-to-do.

Certain industries also have felt the sting of high oil prices. The nation's airlines are paying far higher prices for aviation fuel than they were a year ago. Northwest Airlines recently reported that its fuel bill for the second quarter was $499 million, up from $375 million in the same quarter a year ago. Yet because Northwest is battling competition from discount rivals, it has been unable to pass those costs on to customers. "The airlines are eating the entire increase," said Daniel Kasper, an airline consultant based in Cambridge.

Harder to measure is how much impact expensive energy has on consumer attitudes. Still, most economists and pollsters say the impact can be considerable. "Gasoline prices are a visible signal to people that prices in general are going up," said John Gorman, president of Opinion Dynamics, a Cambridge polling firm. Gorman says his polling work suggests that consumers have a tendency to overestimate how much higher energy prices actually cost them.

Richard DeKaser, chief economist at National City Corp. in Cleveland, has been trying to gauge how voters feel about the economy. He believes that three key variables -- job growth, wage growth, and inflation -- shape voter opinions. To the extent higher energy prices increase inflation and depress job growth, they will spur some voters to blame President Bush for their troubles. Said DeKaser, "Our models show that higher energy prices will detract from the president's share of the vote."
