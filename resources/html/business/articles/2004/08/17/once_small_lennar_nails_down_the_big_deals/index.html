Once-small Lennar nails down the big deals

By Chris Reidy, Globe Staff  |  August 17, 2004

Lennar Corp. and LNR Property Corp. made big news in Boston last week when they agreed to buy Fan Pier for an estimated $125 million, but the purchase would not be the biggest deal for the Florida team.

The two publicly traded sister companies recently paid nearly $1 billion for land near Los Angeles where they plan to build 21,000 homes.

Such deals have propelled Lennar from its 1954 start as a small Miami home builder to the forefront of the industry. In 2003, Lennar ranked third in Builder Online's top 100 list with $8.9 billion in revenue and closings on 32,180 homes, many in Sun Belt markets. Homes had an average 2003 sales price of $256,000.

Given the scale of earlier projects, Lennar officials said their team is well qualified to execute the long-stalled plan for Fan Pier hammered out by the City of Boston, the state, and Fan Pier's seller, the Pritzker family. That plan seeks to transform 21 acres on the South Boston Waterfront into nearly 3 million square feet of housing, offices, a hotel, retail, and parks.

According to chief executive Stuart Miller, Lennar's strategy is not to barge into a new market and tell the locals what to do; instead, it seeks to work with local specialists and partners.

Wall Street analysts praise Lennar. In a June report, Margaret Whelan of UBS Investment Research wrote: "Lennar continues to enjoy the most sophisticated land-buying expertise among its peers."

Michael Jaffe, of Standard & Poor's, added: "It's a big powerful well-run builder."

But not everyone is a Lennar fan. In 2001, Sierra Club member Lynne Plambeck worked to get a court order to stop Lennar from uprooting century-old oak trees on a subdivision site near Santa Clarita, Calif. Before the judge's ruling, Lennar bulldozed 27 trees, she said.

"They're pit bulls that push forward to get their way," she said. "They have endless funds to fight things. They donate to local charities, then ride roughshod over natural resources."

Lennar vice president Jon Jaffe described Plambeck as intent on stopping big development projects. Lennar had permits to remove the trees and agreed to plant 150 trees to replace what was lost, he said.

In 2002 Eugenio Santiago, a structural engineer, was hired by The Miami Herald to see whether developers were complying with tougher building codes implemented after 1992's Hurricane Andrew, which devastated much of what was in its path, from homes to office buildings and military facilities.

In reviewing designs for homes in a Florida subdivision two years ago, Santiago said some designs, including some by Lennar, did not measure up.

"Developers, when they do big projects, try to save money," he said.

Marshall Ames, a Lennar vice president, disputed Santiago's conclusions.

"Structural engineering isn't an exact science," Ames said. "It can be a matter of opinion."

In a separate earlier investigation, Lennar was "exonerated" by Florida officials who sought to determine whether shoddy construction by various developers aggravated Andrew's damage, Ames said. After Andrew struck, Lennar voluntarily helped customers with damaged homes to rebuild, Ames said.

According to J.D. Power and Associates, Lennar fared well in 2003 homebuyer satisfaction surveys in various markets. In the Orlando and Southern California markets, Lennar received the top score for quality workmanship.

Near Boston, LNR is involved with a plan to redevelop the former South Weymouth Naval Air Station with housing, offices, and retail. It's a huge, contentious project. Weymouth Mayor David M. Madden thinks Boston could have an easier time with Fan Pier because Fan Pier is already permitted.

The team of Lennar, LNR, and a third partner, luxury condo specialist Turnberry Associates, has said it will "keep faith" with the plan for Fan Pier, once the sale is completed, which is expected in October.

Meanwhile, in Weymouth, the vision for the naval station evolves. LNR has been "very open in terms of communications," Madden said; a meeting next month "will give us an understanding of how well they've been listening."

No Lennar homes have been built near Boston because Lennar prefers to buy vast tracts of land to develop, and there is little such available land in Eastern Massachusetts, said Gopal Ahluwalia, a vice president at the National Association of Home Builders. Lennar's top markets are Texas, Florida, and California.

Lennar expanded into commercial projects in the 1980s. In 1997, the commercial business became a separate company, LNR. Lennar and LNR continue to collaborate.

This team has everything it needs to be successful at Fan Pier, said George J. Fantini Jr., chairman of Fantini & Gorga, a Boston mortgage firm.

"It's well capitalized and it wants to move forward," Fantini said.

David Hall, who heads LNR's Northeast office, reiterated the team's plan to comply with the city's vision for Fan Pier, including plans for a new Institute of Contemporary Art.

"We couldn't be happier to have that element as a fantastic amenity for the project," Hall said.
