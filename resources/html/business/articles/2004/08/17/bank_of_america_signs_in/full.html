Bank of America signs in

First Fleet branches switch to the new name as N.C. company steps up its branding campaign

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  August 17, 2004

They served up a giant Bank of America sheet cake, festooned the bank branch with balloons, and dispatched smiling greeters to welcome customers in upstate New York yesterday, as the first FleetBoston Financial Corp. branches formally switched to Bank of America.

The branches will serve as a test market for Bank of America to iron out the kinks as it prepares to convert 1,500 Fleet branches and 3,500 ATMs in the Northeast this fall. Though the branches in Boston will not change signs until December, more than a year after the North Carolina bank said it would acquire Fleet, Bank of America already has begun an intensive branding campaign designed to introduce Fleet customers to the new face in town.

In the last several weeks, Fleet has stopped running its old radio ads that featured the voice-over of actor Matt Damon, replacing him with Fleet ads that use the same background music and other content as Bank of America ads. The Charlotte bank also plans to turn Fleet's green-and-blue HomeLink website red, white, and blue next month, perform its philanthropy under the Bank of America name, and is preparing a bombardment of television and print ads to target current and potential customers. New England Patriots fans attending games this season will find the old Fleet signs changed to Bank of America.

Fleet customers throughout the Northeast will get a large dose of the new bank this week when they tune into the Olympics, of which Bank of America is a sponsor. The bank has ads in prime time and during the day, and it is promoting its program to fly the families of Olympic athletes to Athens. Bank executives view the Olympics as a way to bolster its brand name throughout the country, but especially in the Northeast.

"It's a very important platform for us," said Catherine P. Bessant, Bank of America's chief marketing officer, who said the Olympics dovetails with the bank's motto of "Higher Standards." "In terms of mass marketing, it's a great introduction to the company."

In Manhattan, where Bank of America had no branches before the Fleet merger, the bank is playing up its Olympic affiliation in a festival at Rockefeller Center, including giant video screens broadcasting the games and demonstrations of the trampoline and tae kwon do.

Bank of America already has a formidable advantage in its branding campaign: The vast majority of Fleet customers have heard that their bank is being acquired -- and they think the deal will benefit them, the bank's internal research shows. About 85 percent of Fleet's customers view the deal as neutral or positive, said Anne Finucane, Bank of America's president of the Northeast.

She said Bank of America's brand is similar to Fleet's.

"Fleet is the dominant bank in the region, while Bank of America is the dominant bank in the country," Finucane said. "Both banks had an interest in speaking to the value we provide to our customers in terms of making good financial decisions and being part of the community."

But at least one of Bank of America's rivals, Banknorth Group of Maine, said Bank of America's national brand may not go over so well for customers who want to deal with decision makers in New England.

"If consumers want to do business with the local New England bank, maybe the national brand is a weakness," said Christopher Bramley, president and chief executive of Banknorth Massachusetts. "A bank like ours is New England-based and New England-oriented. That'll be our focus."

In the transition, however, Bank of America could gain ground through talking up its benefits over Fleet, said Jay Nagdeman, president of Suasion Resources Inc., New Jersey marketing consultants that deal with the financial services industry.

"I don't think Fleet has ever had a really favorable aura in the marketplace," he said. "Bank of America has an opportunity to take that slate and wipe it clean. They can go after the perceptions they want to create, and that's what they're doing."

Though Bank of America likely will not directly compare itself to Fleet, the bank plans to emphasize the value that it brings to the marketplace over its competitors: more branches, more ATMs, and better products for customers. The bank plans to use direct mail, television ads, and a print campaign that escalates later in the year as it moves closer to changing the signs.

The bank also will send customers welcome letters that answer questions about the changeover. On television, its "portraits" ad campaign showcases bankers' involvement in the community.

Fleet is still running print and radio ads -- largely focused on its products, rather than its overall brand -- but it has stopped advertising on television. Both the print and radio ads explain that Bank of America now owns Fleet.

"You're beginning to see us step up the Bank of America presence, and you can expect that to continue," said Bessant, the chief marketing officer.

"We think this is an exciting and important beginning."
