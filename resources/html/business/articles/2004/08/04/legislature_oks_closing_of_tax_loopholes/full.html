Legislature OK's closing of tax loopholes

August 4, 2004

The Legislature has approved a loophole-closing bill sought by the Massachusetts Department of Revenue, but it deleted a controversial provision that would have increased taxes on hotel rooms purchased through online firms like Hotels.com and Expedia.com. The bill, awaiting Governor Mitt Romney's signature, is expected to increase state tax revenues by about $70 million by cracking down on tax-avoidance schemes used by business trusts and by narrowing the sales tax exemption for the printing of promotional materials. (Bruce Mohl)

Labor contract extended amid talks

A three-year contract covering about 6,000 employees of Shaw's Supermarkets in Southeastern Massachusetts, Rhode Island, and Maine will remain in effect through tomorrow, said Peter Derouen, spokesman for the United Food and Commercial Workers, Local 791. The contract extension ''gives us more time to talk," said Terry Donilon, a Shaw's spokesman. The contract expired Saturday, and a federal mediator was brought in to help resolve the central issue in the labor talks: health insurance. Workers on Sunday rejected a plan by Shaw's that would have added three company-sponsored health plans as options for workers, besides their longstanding union plan. Shaw's said the new plans would contain its soaring costs associated with the union plan. The union said the plan raises costs to employees who remain in the plan and shifts them into plans in which Shaw's has control over increases in copayments and premiums. (Kimberly Blanton)

Transkaryotic hires firm to make drug

Transkaryotic Therapies Inc. hired Switzerland's Lonza AG to produce Dynepo, a potential rival to anemia treatments made by Amgen Inc. and Johnson & Johnson, and plans to introduce the drug in Europe by March 2006. Transkaryotic hasn't been able to market Dynepo because of a patent dispute with Thousand Oaks, Calif.-based Amgen, the world's biggest biotechnology company. The European patent on Amgen's anemia treatment Epogen, which also protects Johnson & Johnson's Eprex, will expire in 2006. (Bloomberg)

Smith & Wesson files suit against insurer

Smith & Wesson Corp. sued a unit of American International Group Inc. for failing to pick up the costs of defending the company in three dozen lawsuits filed by US cities in the 1990s. Smith & Wesson, based in Springfield, claims National Union Fire Insurance Co. of Pittsburgh violated an agreement to defend the gun maker in court under seven policies sold to its former parent company, London-based Tomkins PLC. The suits against Smith & Wesson and other gun makers were filed by cities and groups including Atlanta, Chicago, the District of Columbia, and the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People. They sought to make the companies pay public costs resulting from injuries and deaths stemming from the design, manufacturing, marketing, and sale of firearms. (Bloomberg)

SEC, state regulators looking at Putnam

Marsh & McLennan Cos. said the Securities and Exchange Commission asked for information about the administration of 401(k) retirement savings plans at its Putnam Investments unit. The SEC's Boston office and Massachusetts state regulators are looking into an error in handling a client's account, and expenses related to consulting services, Marsh & McLennan said in a quarterly filing with the SEC. Boston-based Putnam fired four employees, including the head of its 401(k) business, this year. The company said at the time the employees mishandled a retirement account, costing a customer $1.5 million. The quarterly filing did not say which events the SEC was investigating. A Marsh & McLennan spokeswoman declined to comment. (Bloomberg)

Oil climbs on supply, terrorism fears

Oil prices climbed to new highs, rising above $44 a barrel in the United States, as global supply concerns and terrorism fears made for jittery trading at a time of strong demand. Light crude for September delivery rose 33 cents to settle at $44.15 on the New York Mercantile Exchange, topping the previous record, set Monday, of $43.82. Traders are investing largely on psychological factors, analysts said. Early yesterday, the president of OPEC warned the cartel could not immediately increase output to help curb prices. Purnomo Yusgiantoro, who is also Indonesia's oil minister, said that Saudi Arabia, the world's largest oil exporter, had spare capacity, but could not bring more crude oil to the market in the short term. Futures were also pressured by fears of terrorist attacks in the United States. US authorities on Sunday warned that al-Qaida was planning attacks on five key financial institutions in New York, New Jersey, and Washington. (AP)

Drug maker sues 5 Internet pharmacies

Pfizer Inc., the world's biggest drug maker, sued five Internet pharmacies and sought to seize the domain names of more than two dozen others to stop the sale of counterfeit Viagra, the top-selling male impotence drug. Pfizer filed the lawsuits in US District Court for the Southern District of New York, Jeff Kindler, Pfizer's general counsel, said in an interview. The New York company said in a statement that it's working with government agencies to block the counterfeit drug sales. In addition to competition from illegal copies, Viagra has been losing sales to rival products such as Cialis, sold by Eli Lilly & Co. and Icos Corp., and GlaxoSmithKline PLC's and Bayer AG's Levitra. Viagra's worldwide revenue dropped 7 percent to $389 million in the second quarter, Pfizer reported. Shares of Pfizer rose 25 cents to $32.45 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. They have fallen 8.2 percent this year. (Bloomberg)

Martha Stewart earnings suffer a loss

Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia Inc. posted a loss as sales fell for the seventh consecutive quarter because advertisers shunned its flagship magazine while Martha Stewart awaited a sentence for obstructing justice. The second-quarter loss was $19.3 million, or 39 cents a share, compared with a profit of $931,000, or 2 cents, a year earlier, the New York company said in a statement. Sales plunged 33 percent to $44 million. It forecast a third-quarter loss that's wider than the 33-cent loss estimated by four analysts surveyed by Thomson Financial. Martha Stewart Living said it will close its money-losing catalog business, which sells items such as vases and lamps, by year-end and cut about 12 jobs. Martha Stewart Living shares fell 15 cents to $11.25 in New York Stock Exchange trading (Bloomberg)

Company to release all drug-test results

Eli Lilly and Co. said it would release results of all its clinical trials through a publicly available registry, responding to disclosure plans by rival drug makers. The move comes amid criticism that drug companies may hide negative results of clinical trials of their drugs and follows a lawsuit by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer against British manufacturer GlaxoSmithKline. Spitzer said Glaxo suppressed information about clinical trials where its Paxil antidepressant proved ineffective in children. Glaxo, which denied the allegation, said it would create an electronic database to make data from its trials accessible on the Internet. Lilly said it would begin posting results of all human trials later this year on a website (www.lillytrials.com) and announce the initiation of all late-stage testing. Indianapolis-based Lilly, maker of Zyprexa for schizophrenia and Prozac for depression, said it would disclose results of early, mid-stage and late-stage trials once a drug has been approved. For trials conducted after any drug has been approved, Lilly will release results as soon as possible, but no later than one year after a study has been completed, the company said. (Reuters)
