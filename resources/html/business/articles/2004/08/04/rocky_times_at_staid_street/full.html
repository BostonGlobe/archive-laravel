Rocky times at 'Staid Street'

New CEO looks to get the firm back on track through focusing on execution

By Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  August 4, 2004

Not long after Ronald E. Logue was hired to run State Street Corp.'s US mutual-fund servicing operations in 1990, a company management review identified him as a budding executive who could go all the way to the top.

It says something about both the prescience of Logue's State Street bosses, as well as his own performance, that Logue was tapped to be chief executive in an abrupt change of power on June 30.

''He has an understanding of the whole business," said A. Edward Allinson, the former State Street executive vice president who hired Logue away from the then-failing Bank of New England, where Logue ran mutual fund operations.

Though founded in 1792 as Union Bank, State Street is no longer a bank, and Logue is no banker. Now the largest provider of services to the investment industry, State Street is more a technology firm, providing everything from trade clearance to daily pricing and accounting for mutual funds, and securities lending to foreign exchange services. It provides investment research and its State Street Global Advisors arm is the world's largest institutional money manager.

''There are people who have presence, whom people naturally follow." said Drew Pace, a former senior vice president who recently left State Street after 14 years to run global institutional sales for Mellon Financial Corp. in Boston. ''He's one of those guys. People look to Ron and say, 'He knows where he's going; he's going in the right direction, and I'm going to follow him.' "

Logue had better know where he's going, for he is taking over at an unusually turbulent time for a firm sometimes derided as ''Staid Street." Though the company has some recent impressive accomplishments, it is also coming through a period of turmoil and frustration -- and may be looking at yet another one just ahead.

The stock is down a whopping 16 percent for the year, as investors worry whether Logue and his managers will truly be able to weather some difficult matters: keeping control over unexpected expenses and minimizing investment losses during a period of rising interest rates.

Long-term State Street shareholders are unaccustomed to such uncertainty. Under former chief executives William Edgerly and Marshall Carter, State Street put together 24 straight years of double-digit earnings growth, with stock prices rising from roughly $1 a share in 1984 to $65 in 2000.

That streak was broken under Logue's predecessor, David Spina, who had the misfortune of taking over just as the bull market ended. But Spina compounded his own problems when he cut expenses with an overly generous buyout package that led to the company's first quarterly loss in 26 years.

But Spina also suffered a heart attack in May 2003. And though he recuperated and was back at work just months later, Spina said his heart trouble caused him to reevaluate his personal priorities. After 34 years at State Street, Spina suddenly resigned June 30, the day the company closed on its second quarter. In an interview the following day, Spina said, ''There is more to life" than work.

Spina was a numbers man. Logue is an operations man. He brings a keen understanding of its technological and administrative needs and challenges.

''My style is to make decisions quickly," he said in a recent interview. ''I grew up in this business. I know so many of the parts of this business. I can size up the situation pretty quickly and make a pretty informed decision pretty quickly for no other reason than over the years I've been involved in a lot of those pieces."

Affable and self-effacing, the 58-year-old Logue is a Holbrook native who graduated from high school with White House chief of staff Andrew Card, then got both graduate and undergraduate degrees from Boston College. His Boston accent is one indication of how Logue doesn't sound the part of the stuffy executive.

Indeed, friends and colleagues describe him as easygoing, the type of manager who does his work by walking around buttonholing staffers for updates and ideas. But the verdict on his leadership is still out on Wall Street.

''You want a management who can project control of its environment, not a management who projects that the environment is in control of its company," said Richard Bove, who follows State Street for Hoefer & Arnett Inc. Bove worried that Logue may be too much of a technician and not enough of a big thinker to steer the company over the longer term.

Logue said the company is already set on a strategy to pursue a new trend in the investment industry: winning outsourced ''middle-office" operations, or all the administrative functions that support the core job of investing money. Logue said the business has potentially higher profit margins, and gives it opportunities to win yet more business that its new clients now outsource to other vendors.

In Logue's view, State Street is about execution, not finding a new vision. ''People pay us to be consistently right and on time, and we are going to do absolutely nothing to endanger our reputation for consistently high execution," he said.

Indeed, he argued State Street won three new outsourcing contracts precisely because it did a good job melding operations from the largest acquisition in State Street's history: the $1.5 billion purchase last year of Deutsche Bank's securities servicing business. While competitors had suggested they would pick off plum portions of Deutsche Bank's customer base, State Street has retained 90 percent of those clients. The integration efforts were led by Logue, in his prior role as State Street's president.

While the new outsourcing contracts will become profitable within 18 months of signing the deal, the company faces high costs to implement them.

Those costs were one reason Wall Street reacted so negatively on July 13, pushing the stock down 9 percent after Logue's first public presentation as chief executive in which he disclosed second quarter earnings.

Though State Street's revenue grew by more than 19 percent, the company also had several unsettling expenses: a 10 percent increase in employee costs, a securities processing error that cost it $16 million, as well as the outsourcing-related costs.

Logue said he learned an important lesson that day, his first earnings call as chief executive. ''We could have been a little more prepared. It was quick, granted -- the first time I did it, granted. But I did learn that preparation is very, very important," he said.

Still, for company followers, the unexpected costs were too reminiscent of last year's buyout snafu. Analysts said such continuing problems raise questions about whether State Street's management can ever get control over its expenses.

But Logue suggested these bumps in the road won't deter State Street from pursuing bigger, longer-term deals that will drive future earnings growth.

Those problems are ''behind us," he said. ''We're at a point where we can begin consolidating our gains and taking advantage of all the things we did the last two years."
