Business in brief

August 24, 2004

THE REGION

Hotel chain plans high-speed Net service

ADVERTISEMENT

The Red Roof Inn hotel and motel chain plans to offer high-speed wireless Internet service in all rooms and public areas at its 359 US locations in coming months. The WiFi service, to be provided by Deutsche Telekom's T-Mobile USA unit, will be available to nonsubscribers for $10 a day or $6 per hour. Many other hotels and motels are adding WiFi only in public areas, but Red Roof, a subsidiary of Accor North America, will cover all of its 39,000 rooms and suites, too, as part of a $200 million chainwide renovation. The chain has inns in Framingham, Mansfield, Saugus, Southborough, and Woburn. (Peter J. Howe)

THE NATION

Overstocked, chip maker cuts prices

Intel Corp., the world's biggest chip maker, cut the prices of some Pentium and Itanium processors by as much as 35 percent after inventory surged. Intel slashed the cost of 28 models for desktop and notebook computers and servers that run corporate networks, according to the Santa Clara, Calif., company's website. Last month, chief financial officer Andy Bryant said the company would slow some factories and lower prices to work off inventory, which jumped 15 percent in the second quarter. That will hamper Intel's gross margin, he said, predicting it will be about 60 percent this year, 2 points less than predicted earlier. Intel lowered the price of the Pentium 4 560 desktop PC model to $417 from $637, and cut prices on five of its seven Itanium models. (Bloomberg)

Kmart to sell 18 stores to Home Depot

Kmart Holding Corp. said that it will sell 18 stores to Home Depot for $271 million. Initially, Kmart had said it would sell up to 24 stores. "The stores we are retaining from the originally announced transaction have been operating profitably and better than plan for this year," chief executive Julian Day said. Kmart said it expects to have received $259 million by the end of the third quarter in October and the remainder by the end of the fourth quarter. It has already received $54.6 million from the transaction. About 1,600 people work at the Kmarts being sold. About 230 will be offered jobs at other locations. In June, Kmart said it would sell up to 54 stores to Sears, Roebuck and Co. for up to $621 million. (AP)

Airline says it's cutting 88 flights

Southwest Airlines Inc. plans to cut 88 flights and shift planes to more profitable routes, mostly in and out of Philadelphia. The Dallas-based low-cost carrier said it hopes the changes will increase revenue by $60 million. "It's just taking the planes and redeploying them. The capacity remains the same," said spokesman Ed Stewart. "We should see increased revenue by putting planes in places where lots of people want to fly." The Oct. 31 changes will result in 41 daily flights at Philadelphia, where Southwest began service in May. The changes will affect about 3 percent of Southwest's 2,800 flights; it will reduce some round-trip flights between Kansas City and Chicago, Dallas and Tulsa, and Houston and New Orleans. Southwest has been hurt by rising costs for jet fuel and labor and saw second-quarter profits slide 54 percent. (AP)

Net sales forecast to double in 6 years

US retail sales on the Internet will more than double over the next six years as four out of 10 households make online purchases, Forrester Research predicted. Online sales in 2010 will represent 12 percent of all retail purchases, up from 5.4 percent last year, Cambridge-based Forrester said. Sales will rise to $316.1 billion from this year's estimated $144.6 billion, a 14 percent annual gain. The number of US homes shopping on the Web will increase to 44.7 million from 36.8 million this year, Forrester said. Faster Internet connections and more options for shoppers, such as in-store pickup, will spur growth, the firm said. (Bloomberg)

Delivery firm raises earnings outlook

FedEx Corp. raised its earnings outlook, citing more business for its international, ground delivery, and less-than-truckload services. The Memphis delivery company said it expects per share earnings of $1 to $1.10 for the quarter ending Aug. 31, vs. previous estimates of 90 cents to $1.00, and $4.40 to $4.60 for the year, compared with $4.20 to $4.40. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call had been expecting first quarter earnings of 95 cents per share and annual profits of $4.47 a share. FedEx acknowledged that high fuel costs could affect the worldwide economy, but it expects to continue to see strong demand. As a result, FedEx will increase capital investments in its international express, ground, and freight networks to between $2 billion and $2.1 billion in the year ending in May 2005. FedEx shares rose 2.1 percent, to $80.95. (AP)

Mylan gets early OK on 2 generic drugs

Mylan Laboratories Inc. won tentative approval to sell generic versions of two Pfizer Inc. drugs, the depression treatment Zoloft and the allergy medicine Zyrtec, once patents on them expire in 2006 and 2007, respectively. Zoloft generated $3.12 billion in sales for New York-based Pfizer last year, while Zyrtec brought in $1.34 billion. Mylan, the largest US maker of generics, had $1.37 billion in revenue in the year ended in March. The Food and Drug Administration ruling came days after the company lost a lawsuit in which it sought to begin marketing a generic version of Johnson & Johnson's Duragesic pain-relief skin patch. Mylan spokeswoman Heather Bresch said it's up to the FDA to determine when the generic versions would be approved. She declined to comment further. Mylan also won final clearance to sell a generic version of Novartis AG's Lopressor hypertension drug, the company said. (Bloomberg)

White Mountains posts big gain

Shares of White Mountains Insurance Group Ltd., the Bermuda-based insurer whose investors include Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway Inc., had their biggest gain in almost two years after the company reported losses from Hurricane Charley would be less than $45 million, before taxes -- less than investors expected. Shares rose $33, or 7.1 percent, to $500, their biggest gain since Oct. 10, 2002. The stock had fallen $54.50 Thursday and Friday, partly on concern that claims from Charley would be "much larger," said Gary Ransom, an analyst at Fox-Pitt Kelton Inc. Berkshire Hathaway in May exercised warrants to purchase 1.72 million shares of White Mountains, or about 16 percent. (Bloomberg)

Ex-HealthSouth officer barred by SEC

The Securities and Exchange Commission barred a former HealthSouth Corp. assistant comptroller, Kenneth Livesay, from serving as an accountant, officer, or director at a public company. Livesay was accused of ordering other HealthSouth employees to record fictitious assets. HealthSouth, of Birmingham, Ala., is the nation's largest provider of outpatient surgery, diagnostic imaging, and rehabilitative services, with nearly 1,700 locations. In March 2003, the SEC alleged the company overstated earnings by at least $1.4 billion. Chief executive Richard Scrushy still faces charges. He has denied wrongdoing. Livesay accepted the ban without admitting or denying wrongdoing. (Dow Jones/AP)
