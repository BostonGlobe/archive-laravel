Business in brief

August 20, 2004

THE REGION

Regulator to join financial institution

ADVERTISEMENT

Bank of America Corp., one of the first companies ensnared in the mutual-fund trading probe, has hired the deputy chief of the Securities and Exchange Commission's mutual-fund division. Cynthia Fornelli, 43, will become a senior vice president at the bank's Charlotte, N.C., headquarters in a newly created position in charge of risk assessment and ensuring the bank's compliance with securities laws. At the SEC, Fornelli helped lead the most extensive overhaul of mutual fund oversight since the 1940s. The SEC has scrutinized Bank of America's mutual fund trading, its analyst research, and the withholding of documents during an investigation of insider trading. Last September, the bank was accused by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer of permitting mutual fund trading violations that harmed long-term investors. Fornelli, who has been at the SEC for five years, will leave the agency at the end of next month. (Bloomberg)

City OKs Northeastern development plan

The Boston Redevelopment Authority approved plans by Northeastern University to construct a residence hall and a home for the John D. O'Bryant African-American Institute. The proposal included a request to convert an off-campus Roxbury apartment building at 10 Coventry St. into student housing with 161 beds. Several elected officials, including state Representative Byron Rushing, a Boston Democrat, said that putting 10 Coventry into Northeastern's other request was a back-door attempt to expand the campus without sufficient community input. Northeastern officials said they followed appropriate notification guidelines but promised to do a better job of having a dialog with abutting neighborhoods in the future. In other action, the BRA approved separate requests for projects proposing 22 condos at 11 Wyman St. in Jamaica Plain and 14 condo units at 18 Robert St. in Roslindale. Between the two projects, five units will be affordable housing. (Chris Reidy)

Citizens Financial to cut jobs in 2 cities

Citizens Financial Group said it would cut several hundred jobs in Cleveland and Rochester, N.Y., as part of its pending merger with Charter One Financial Inc. of Ohio. The bank told "hundreds" of employees, mostly managers and technology workers, that their jobs would be eliminated. Charter One is headquartered in Cleveland. In Rochester, Citizens plans to lay off about 400 workers in Charter One's auto dealer-finance operations as it consolidates that unit into its own in Rhode Island. Most of those told that they would lose their jobs received nearly a full year's notice, and they will be able to apply for other jobs within the bank, said Melodie Jackson, a Citizens spokeswoman. She said Citizens will not cut branch staff and that total layoffs in the merger will number less than 1,000. Citizens said it plans to grow in Charter One's territory, opening 100 branches and adding staff to its small-business operations. The bank said there will be no layoffs in New England. (Sasha Talcott)

THE NATION

Average mortgage rate at 4-month low

Mortgage rates declined again this week, with the US average for a 30-year mortgage remaining below 6 percent for a third consecutive week, Freddie Mac reported. The mortgage giant said its latest nationwide survey showed rates on 30-year, fixed-rates mortgages fell to 5.81 percent, on average. That was down from the average of 5.85 percent last week and is the lowest level for 30-year mortgages in more than four months, since they averaged 5.79 percent during the week of April 8. Rates on 30-year mortgages hit a high this year of 6.34 percent the week of May 13. Since then, they have slowly drifted downward, reflecting a significant slowdown in economic activity in the late spring and early summer. The survey showed that rates on 15-year, fixed-rate mortgages, a popular option for refinancing, also declined this week to 5.19 percent, on average, down from 5.24 percent last week. For one-year adjustable rate mortgages, rates dipped to 4.01 percent, down from 4.08 percent last week. (AP)

Ohio says chain sold used goods as new

Best Buy Co., the biggest US electronics retailer, was sued by Ohio's attorney general, who accused the company of deceptive sales practices, such as repackaging used merchandise and selling it as new. The civil suit also alleges Best Buy failed to pay rebates and refunds and failed to honor extended service contracts, state Attorney General Jim Petro said. Petro said his office received hundreds of consumer complaints over the past few years against Best Buy. "The sheer number of complaints coupled with the types of allegations my office received prompted us to file this lawsuit," Petro said. Best Buy declined to comment. In May, the company agreed to pay $133,500 to New Jersey after an investigation found it had failed to honor rebates and engaged in deceptive advertising. (Bloomberg)

Ex-Enterasys VP pleads guilty to fraud

A former executive of Enterasys Networks Inc. pleaded guilty to fraud charges in an accounting scheme that prosecutors estimated cost investors more than $1 billion. Gail Spence Luacaw, 45, of Newfields, N.H., pleaded guilty to conspiracy to commit securities fraud as part of a scheme to inflate the company's revenues to meet Wall Street projections. Her plea came as part of an agreement with federal prosecutors. Luacaw was vice president of inside sales and executive assistant to the chief executive. Acting US Attorney Peter Papps said Luacaw admitted that in the fall of 2001 she altered company books with other Enterasys officials. She faces up to five years in federal prison and a $250,000 fine when she is sentenced in March. Also to be sentenced in March are former assistant controller Anthony Hurley, 34, of Georgetown, Mass., and Gary Workman, 57, of San Ramon, Calif., both of whom pleaded guilty in May to wire fraud. (AP)

Chip equipment sales doubled in July

Orders to North American companies that sell equipment used to build semiconductors more than doubled in July from a year earlier, a trade group said. Growth from a month earlier was the smallest in a year. The so-called book-to-bill ratio for North American chip-tool makers was 1.05 in July, indicating they received $105 in orders for every $100 in sales, the Semiconductor Equipment & Materials International said. The ratio measures industry health and was revised to 1.07 in June, compared with 1.10 in May. Above 1.00 indicates growth. July's ratio was the closest to 1.00 since November, when it registered 1.05. The three-month average of worldwide bookings in July rose to $1.61 billion from $1.609 billion in June and jumped from $706.9 million a year earlier. The $1 million increase in bookings from June was the smallest month-on-month rise since bookings began a run of 12 straight monthly gains last August. Billings rose to $1.54 billion in July from $1.5 billion in June and increased 96 percent from $785.9 million a year earlier. (Bloomberg)

Ex-Enron CEO doing community service

Former Enron chief executive Jeffrey Skilling is working for Habitat for Humanity to fulfill a community service requirement that allows him to remain free on $5 million bond pending trial, his lawyer said. Skilling, 50, is working in a warehouse and a supply outlet store run by Houston's branch of the volunteer group, which builds and restores homes for low-income families. Skilling has pleaded not guilty to nearly three dozen counts of conspiracy, fraud, and insider trading stemming from Enron's 2001 crash. In May, his lawyers told a judge that a government freeze of his assets had prevented him from running a private investment company he founded after he resigned from Enron. A US magistrate said he should perform community service instead. (AP)
