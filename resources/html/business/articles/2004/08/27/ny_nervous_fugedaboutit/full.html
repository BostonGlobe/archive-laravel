N.Y. nervous? Fugedaboutit

For city that's seen it all RNC likely to be more diversion than disaster

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  August 27, 2004

NEW YORK -- In a city where terror attacks and a massive blackout have come, gone, and failed to stop business as usual, a few thousand Republicans are unlikely to slow things down.

ADVERTISEMENT

As the Republican National Convention, with its expected 50,000 delegates and media members prepares to descend on midtown Manhattan's epicenter, Madison Square Garden, the prospect of massive protests and heightened security around one of the city's major commuter centers is being greeted as a diversion, not a disaster.

"What do you want me to do, close the store?" when the convention starts, asked Uri Aldad, manager of Today's Woman 2, a women's apparel shop on West 34th Street between Seventh and Eighth avenues, one block north of the Garden.

Despite New York's indifference, things will be different for Midtown establishments next week. While hotels expect a jump in business from what normally would be a sleepy week, restaurants, retailers, and even Broadway shows face less traffic as many New York commuters are likely to stay away from the convention.

"We are expecting a decrease in traffic from New Yorkers," said Elina Kazan, a spokeswoman for Macy's flagship Herald Square store, a block from the Garden. "I'm sure retail is going to be affected."

Despite expected security hassles, at least some tourism-related businesses expect a boon from the convention. The Republicans are coming the week before Labor Day, a notoriously slow week that typically marks the close of the summer tourism season for Manhattan hotels.

"Normally, this last week in August is a soft week. People are in back-to-work, back-to-school mode," said Kathleen Duffy, New York spokeswoman for the Marriott hotel chain. Marriott's two hotels closest to Madison Square Garden are expected to be sold out next week, while a third will be about 90 percent full, she said.

"This year, we're finding ourselves with large group bookings as far as the RNC. There are still a lot of tourists walking around New York City."

Overall, New York City hotels are expected to be 87 percent full next week, compared with 73 percent full during the same week last year, according to a study by PriceWaterhouseCoopers.

New York Mayor Michael Bloomberg is predicting a $265 million boost to the city's economy from the convention. But economic analysts say that estimate is inflated and doesn't account for business that the city stands to lose from commuters and tourists fleeing Manhattan.

Boston's Beacon Hill Institute at Suffolk University, however, is estimating that the New York economy will reap a net gain of $163 million from the convention.

Boston officials had forecasted the Democratic convention's economic impact at $154 million, while the Beacon Hill Institute predicted a shortfall due to lost tourism business. Ultimately, the institute concluded that Boston had a net gain of $14.8 million, which it called a "little bounce."

Unlike Boston, where 40 miles of road were periodically closed during last month's Democratic National Convention, only several blocks of Seventh and Eighth avenues in Midtown will be closed to vehicle traffic during the Republican convention. Several subway and commuter rail stations closed during the Boston convention, but not even the Penn Station transit hub directly under Madison Square Garden will close here.

That isn't to say there won't be disruptions when the Republicans come to town; it's a given that things change when the president, an estimated quarter of a million protesters, and various politicos plop down in the center of Manhattan for a week.

Penn Station will remain open during the convention, but six of its eight street-level entrances and exits are expected to close, said Marcie Golgoski, a spokeswoman for Amtrak, which controls the station. An estimated half-million Amtrak, commuter train, and subway passengers use the station daily, meaning there could be bottlenecks getting in and out of the station next week, she said.

That's prompting some New York commuters to avoid coming to work altogether.

Charlotte Vinci, a publicist who lives in Fairfield, Conn., and whose office is three blocks north of Madison Square Garden, said most of her colleagues ride trains to work and also plan to stay home next week. Her office will remain open, she said.

Even though she commutes to work via the Metro-North commuter train, which stops at Grand Central Station across town, "I'll be taking the four days off," Vinci said.

Some New York restaurateurs worry that added security measures will scare away their customers.

Tracy J. Nieporent, director of marketing for Myriad Restaurant Group, which owns six Manhattan eateries, fears the Republican convention will bring a repeat of what happened earlier this month when officials closed the Holland Tunnel to Manhattan-bound trucks because of a terrorism warning.

"That cost a lot of money and we lost a lot of reservations because of that," Nieporent said. The convention "was being promoted as a windfall, but I don't see that as being the case," he said.
