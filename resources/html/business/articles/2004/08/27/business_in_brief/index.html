Business in brief

August 27, 2004

THE REGION

Convention center books trade show

ACM SIGGRAPH, an annual trade show for graphic designers, will hold its 2006 meeting at the Boston Convention and Exhibition Center. It's the 69th large event booked as a "definite" for the $800 million center, the Massachusetts Convention Center Authority said -- meaning the organizers have signed a contract for either the convention center or for hotel rooms. The authority said it also has 18 events with contracts pending and 132 tentative events scheduled through 2010. ACM SIGGRAPH is expected to bring 25,000 visitors to Boston. (Keith Reed)

SEC approves Vaso Active settlement

Drug maker Vaso Active Pharmaceuticals Inc. said regulators approved a settlement over allegedly misleading disclosures about Food and Drug Administration decisions. Vaso said the firm and former CEO John Masiz agreed to settle with the Securities and Exchange Commission without admitting or denying the allegations. The Danvers firm is enjoined from violating antifraud laws, and Masiz is prevented from serving as an officer or director of a public company for five years. He will stay on to provide consulting services through 2008. (Reuters)

LTX posts profit in 4th quarter

LTX Corp. swung to a profit from a loss in its fourth quarter, but the Westford firm said growth in its industry has stalled, and predicted a first-quarter loss of 7 to 10 cents a share. LTX, which provides testing gear to chip makers, posted net income of $8.9 million, or 14 cents a share, compared with a year-earlier loss of $81.5 million, or $1.61 a share. Last year's quarter included a $48.5 million inventory write-down as well as costs tied to research and development. Sales jumped to $80.4 million from $33.7 million. Wall Street was expecting fourth-quarter earnings of 13 cents a share, on sales of $79.5 million. (Dow Jones)

Providence aims to expand hotel

Providence's quasi-public Convention Center Authority, owner of the 364-room downtown Westin Hotel, seeks proposals to buy and expand the facility as part of a plan to add hotel rooms. The buyer must agree to develop no fewer than 200 more rooms, to help the convention center attract more meetings. The decade-old Westin was built with $68 million of state money as part of a $356 million project that included the convention center and garages. (AP)

THE NATION

Oil prices fall for a fifth consecutive day

Oil prices fell for a fifth consecutive day yesterday despite a sharp drop in Iraqi exports after pipelines were sabotaged -- evidence the bull market may have lost steam. The price of oil has plunged 11 percent in the past week, erasing nearly all gains since the start of the month. Light crude for October delivery dipped 37 cents to $43.10 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange. In London, Brent crude futures dropped 35 cents to $40.33. News of the attack was the kind traders had cited to explain rapidly rising prices. But yesterday the trend was bucked, with prices falling as low as $42.50. Some analysts said markets are in the midst of a "technical correction" of prices that raced ahead too fast. (AP)

Probes targeted $215m in Net crime

A summer-long effort targeting Internet crime has resulted in dozens of arrests and convictions on charges including the use of "spam" e-mail to steal credit card numbers, computer hacking, and online fraud, the Justice Department said. Suspects were identified during more than 160 federal investigations into crimes that victimized about 150,000 people and caused $215 million in estimated losses, Attorney General John Ashcroft said. Identity theft alone, he said, costs an estimated $50 billion a year. Among the cases: Jay R. Echouafni, chief executive of Orbit Communications Corp. in Massachusetts, was indicted this week on charges he hired hackers to attack his firm's online competitors. Prosecutors say the attack cost the competitors $2 million and temporarily disrupted websites for the Homeland Security Department and Amazon.com. (AP)

Flu vaccines' delivery to be delayed

California-based Chiron Corp., the world's second-biggest seller of influenza vaccines, will delay shipments of its Fluvirin flu shots until October because testing indicated some products might not be sterile. The company will deliver 46 million to 48 million Fluvirin doses to the United States, short of the 50 million it planned to supply. That may cause Chiron to lose ground to Sanofi-Aventis, the world's biggest supplier of flu shots, analysts said. (Bloomberg)

Drug maker will post negative test data

GlaxoSmithKline PLC agreed to release negative data on the safety and effectiveness of its drugs to settle a lawsuit by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer that accused it of misrepresenting data on prescribing its antidepressant drug Paxil to children. GlaxoSmithKline will put summaries of all its studies since December 2000 on its website. The London company is the first major drug maker to agree to disclose all its studies. It also will pay $2.5 million. Spitzer sued for fraud in June, alleging the company withheld negative information. A spokeswoman said the settlement includes disclosure policies the company was working on before Spitzer sued. (AP)

New passenger-screening system due

The government will take over from the airlines responsibility for checking passenger names against terrorism watch lists and will begin testing its system within two months, the Transportation Security Administration said. The "Secure Flight" system will verify domestic travelers' identities by comparing passenger information supplied to the airlines against government databases. The goal is to reduce the number of people selected for additional screening and more accurately target those who require additional checks. The current Computer-Assisted Passenger Pre-screening System was criticized as having insufficient privacy protections. (AP)

Van Wagoner barred from top fund jobs

Garrett Van Wagoner, who oversaw top-performing mutual funds in the late 1990s, and his firm agreed to pay $800,000 to settle allegations they misstated the value of funds' "illiquid securities." Van Wagoner, 48, also will resign as president and be barred from serving as a fund director or officer for seven years, the Securities and Exchange Commission said. Van Wagoner was accusd of obscuring the fact that funds had too much money in closely held companies that are difficult to value because they rarely trade, misleading investors. The Van Wagoner Emerging Growth Fund declined 59.7 percent in 2001, after a drop of 20.9 percent in 2000 and a gain of 291 percent in 1999. (Bloomberg)

Etc.

The stock of Boston Beer Co., brewer of Samuel Adams, fell 14 percent to $23.43 after the company on Wednesday reiterated its earnings forecast of 82 to 90 cents a share for the year, short of Wall Street's $1-a-share estimate . . . Nickolas Stavropoulos, president of KeySpan Energy's New England gas operations for two years, has been promoted to president of KeySpan Energy Delivery, overseeing New England and New York. KeySpan owns the former Boston Gas, Colonial Gas, and Essex Gas and has 2.5 million customers and 3,600 employees in Massachusetts, New Hampshire, and New York. (Globe staff and wires)
