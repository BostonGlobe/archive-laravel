Business in brief

August 25, 2004

THE REGION

Online retailer to halt cigarette sales

Dirtcheapcigs.com, a Kentucky-based online retailer, agreed to stop selling cigarettes in Massachusetts and pay $125,000 to settle charges it sold to minors. It was one of four companies targeted in a 2003 sting by Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly's office that were accused of selling cigarettes without verifying the buyers were 18 or older. Dirtcheapcigs, which sold cigarettes for as low as $7.99 a carton, did not collect state excise tax of $15.10 a carton. State officials used shipment records to identify 1,250 residents who owed taxes. (Bruce Mohl)

CMGI's chief executive resigns

CMGI Inc.'s chief executive, George McMillan, has resigned, the company said. No reason was given. Succeeding him is Joseph C. Lawler, 54. Lawler was executive vice president for R.R. Donnelley & Sons Co. McMillan is credited with turning CMGI, of Boston, into a provider of printing, packaging and mail services from a venture firm that bought Internet companies. Lawler expanded services and acquisitions while at Chicago-based R.R. Donnelley, which prints Sports Illustrated and TV Guide, CMGI said. "George came in as a turnaround guy," chief financial officer Tom Oberdorf said. "Now we're going to the next level and getting someone . . . that better suits the business." (Bloomberg)

Investor confidence rising, index shows

State Street Corp. said its investor confidence index gained in August as a stock-market decline made equity prices more attractive. Confidence rose 2.4 points from July's revised 84.6. Boston-based State Street's index had fallen in six of the previous seven months. Its report contrasts with a survey by UBS AG and the Gallup Organization that found a decline in August in investors' optimism about the economy and financial markets, stemming from concerns about growth, unemployment, stock market performance, and inflation. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

2 banks suspected of money laundering

The Treasury Department has acted against two foreign banks it suspects of laundering money. The department proposed to cut the banks off from the US financial system. It designated First Merchant Bank of the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus and Infobank of Belarus as "primary money laundering concerns" because they have not acted to combat the problem. The designation is available under the USA Patriot Act. Treasury said Infobank "laundered funds for the former Iraqi regime of Saddam Hussein that were derived from schemes to circumvent the United Nations' oil-for-food program." First Merchant Bank "has been used as a conduit for the laundering of fraudulently obtained funds," Treasury said. The department also alleged individuals who own and operate the bank "have links with organized crime." (AP)

Suit accuses news company of fraud

A federal lawsuit accuses Belo Corp. of defrauding people who bought stock in the media owner by overstating circulation figures at its flagship newspaper, The Dallas Morning News. The suit says misrepresentations inflated financial results and stock prices. Dallas-based Belo did not immediately return calls seeking comment. Belo disclosed Aug. 5 that the Morning News had overstated sales by 5 percent on Sundays and 1.5 percent on other days. Last week, the company said it would pay $23 million to compensate advertisers. The suit, for which class-action status is sought, seeks damages for all who bought Belo stock between May 12, 2003, and Aug. 6, 2004. Belo owns 19 TV stations and four daily papers, including the Providence Journal. (AP)

Boeing to supply 18 planes to airline

Boeing Co., the world's second-largest maker of commercial aircraft, won a $3.7 billion order from Singapore Airlines Ltd. for 18 long-range 777s, beating out Airbus SAS, said people familiar with the plan. General Electric Co. will supply the engines, in a $540 million order that would be its first from Asia's most profitable carrier. An agreement in principle is to be outlined today, the people said. Singapore Airlines' fleet includes fifty-three 777s and five Airbus A340s. Singapore Airlines is luring more passengers as tourists and business travelers return to Southeast Asia after last year's outbreak of severe acute respiratory syndrome emptied flights. Rolls-Royce PLC is the engine supplier for all 777s in Singapore's current fleet. (Bloomberg)

Cisco shares fall after news on options

Shares of Cisco Systems Inc. fell after the maker of network gear increased the number of stock-option grants to employees, including chief executive John Chambers. In a regulatory filing, Cisco said it has granted 165 million options in the current fiscal year, compared with 141 million shares last year. Chambers received 1.5 million options in fiscal 2005, but none last year. This month, Cisco disclosed that its compensation committee reinstated the CEO's $350,000 annual salary, reduced at his request to $1 a year during the tech downturn. Chambers' options won't become effective for seven years as long as he remains CEO. Others' options will vest over five years. (AP)

Delta's creditors want more information

Some of Delta Air Lines Inc.'s creditors insist the carrier must provide more information on its turnaround plan before they will respond to its proposal for more flexibility in restructuring debt. A committee of 34 financial institutions with $1.3 billion of Delta debt wants substantial information about financial conditions and the restructuring plan. The panel said it believes other debtholders also will seek such information. Delta had no comment. Last week, Delta outlined a plan that involves certain equity certificates issued by investor groups that lease aircraft to the airline. Delta is asking the holders of those certificates to remove any restrictions that would prevent the airline from buying or holding the securities. Delta is considering swapping some of its $20 billion in debt for equity. It's also seeking $1 billion in concessions from pilots. (AP)

Northwest plans fee for non-Web tickets

Northwest Airlines Corp. plans to begin charging customers and travel agents extra fees for domestic tickets not booked via its websites, to lower its cost of selling all tickets to about $5, or the same as it now spends selling them at www.nwa.com and www.worldagentdirect.com. The changes will save about $70 million annually, bringing Northwest's distribution costs in line with those of low-cost carriers such as JetBlue, Southwest Airlines, and Independence Air, Northwest said. Beginning Sept. 1, Northwest will bill travel agents who use global distribution systems $7.50 of the average $12.50 cost charged Northwest by the GDS for each round-trip domestic ticket. Beginning Friday, Northwest will charge $5 for each domestic ticket issued through Northwest offices and $10 for each domestic ticket issued at Northwest's airport locations. (AP)

Phone firms seek to end leasing to rivals

Verizon Communications Inc. and Qwest Communications International Inc. asked the US Court of Appeals in Washington to block temporary rules that force them and other local phone companies to share lines with rivals for another six months. They said the Federal Communications Commission should prove competitors need access to the Baby Bells' lines. A court in March threw out the FCC's previous rule, which required Verizon, Qwest, SBC Communications Inc., and BellSouth Corp. to lease to rivals at government-set rates, in order to promote competition. The FCC released temporary rules Friday, after the Bush administration decided not to appeal the court ruling. The FCC rules set a six-month freeze on wholesale rates. Verizon and Qwest contend the FCC has no power to extend the requirement for leasing lines. (Reuters)
