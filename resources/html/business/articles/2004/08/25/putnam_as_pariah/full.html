Putnam as pariah

Despite changes, improved performance, investors wary

By Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  August 25, 2004

The flagship George Putnam fund has managed to beat the Dow Jones industrial average by four percentage points in this year's trying market.

But investors are not rewarding that and many other winning Putnam funds with the prize typical for such performance: new money to invest.

Indeed, months after the Boston firm settled fraud charges with federal and state regulators, investors continue to avoid Putnam's funds. Putnam's total assets under management dropped by $12 billion in the second quarter this year, and since September, when the market-timing trades by several of its portfolio managers and analysts came under investigation by regulators, investors have withdrawn a staggering $83 billion.

Other fund companies that were also involved in the market-timing scandal that has roiled the industry are also seeing customers remove money or not invest new amounts, but none on the sheer scale of Putnam, Boston's second-largest fund family, after Fidelity Investments.

Unlike Fidelity, which sells funds directly to the public, Putnam's funds are sold through brokers and investment advisers such as financial planners, who act as key gatekeepers, and right now they don't have the comfort and confidence in Putnam to recommend the funds to their clients.

''We are not yet at a point where we can put new money in any meaningful way into Putnam," said Donald Sowa, whose East Providence, R.I., financial planning firm has $500 million under management and has had clients at Putnam for more than 15 years.

Besides the taint of the scandal, Putnam still suffers from its disastrous bet on risky stocks during the bull market of the late 1990s that ended with many equity funds losing enormous amounts of money during the subsequent bear market.

Putnam has undertaken numerous steps to both buff its profile as an ethical company and improve its performance. It has adopted stricter controls on employee trading, and a more conservative investing style that downplays risk and favors consistent long-term performance over big, splashy short-term gains.

It cut fees to make its funds more cost competitive, and advertised on television to emphasize Putnam's roots as a prudent investor. Chief executive Charles ''Ed" Haldeman Jr. has crisscrossed the country to visit brokerage firms and investment advisers to argue the firm has taken steps to earn back investors' confidence -- and ultimately, their money. Haldeman was installed in November after Putnam parent Marsh & McLennan Cos. was unhappy with former chief executive's Lawrence J. Lasser's response to the trading scandal.

And still Putnam keeps getting smaller, with assets now down to $210 billion from $272 billion at the end of September.

Big institutional investors such as the Massachusetts pension fund pulled $40 billion out of Putnam following the scandal, and that money has yet to return. The latest losses are largely in the mutual fund side of Putnam's business, which haven't had a single month of net positive flows of money from individual investors since April 2001.

Putnam officials said the funds are now experiencing a normal redemption rate, as investors withdraw money for retirement, college tuition, or other planned expenses.

But those withdrawals are not being replaced by new monies from other investors.

Haldeman emphasized that funds that once performed horribly against their peers are now beating them. And Putnam has some genuine bright spots, fixed-income and value funds, to name two. But the firm's overall performance remains average.

This record of ''mediocrity," Sowa said, won't remove the ''negativity" created by the trading scandal. Instead, Sowa said Putnam needs to cut fund fees further than it already has and produce ''better than average performance. Average isn't going to cut it. People need a reason to move their money from where they are over to Putnam."

So far this year, 13 of 29 Putnam funds with assets of $1 billion or more are performing above the average of their peers, while five ranked at the bottom of their categories, according to Morningstar Inc. Over a three-year period that captures last year's bull run and portions of the bear market, just 10 Putnam funds were above average, and six were at the bottom.

''Performance looks adequate," said Morningstar's Russ Kinnel. While praising Putnam for the recent changes, Kinnel said Haldeman and his staff have ''a lot of work to do on the investment side and that's not going to be fixed immediately."

The challenge for Haldeman, though, is that the current boycott from investors is biting deeply into Putnam's operating profits, but it may take years for his changes to result in better investment performance. But Haldeman said he is not tempted to have Putnam funds pursue big gains in the short-term in order to woo back investors.

''It would be immoral, wrong, and a violation of our clients' duty to try to seduce them," Haldeman said in an interview. ''We are trying to do what's best for them and assume that if we do that long enough, people will seek us out."

Haldeman said he is ''relaxed" about Putnam's condition and the changes he's instituted. Despite the drop in assets and revenue -- down 3 percent over the first six months of this year -- Haldeman said Putnam is not in immediate danger of becoming a much smaller company.

Internally, Haldeman has a powerful tool to prevent an exodus of talent. For one, Marsh & McLennan is keeping Putnam's bonus pool at constant levels through this year. But more important, so many top executives who took outsized bonuses in recent years have left the firm recently -- 12 of the 20 highest paid are gone, including former chief executive Lawrence J. Lasser, who alone made $105 million in his last five years at Putnam -- that Haldeman has more money to spread among a wider number of employees.

Haldeman said he is trying to make Putnam a ''more equitable feeling place. When you have really great amounts being made by a small number of people, you have more feelings of resentment."

One important step Marsh & McLennan took recently was to combine Putnam's service that administers 401(k) plans, including The Boston Globe newsroom employee plan, with the human resources outsourcing operations of its Mercer Inc. subsidiary. The idea is to create a large benefits and retirement administration provider similar to what Fidelity Investments has successfully launched, with one hope that the Mercer operation will be able to capture new investment business for Putnam funds.

But whether brokers steer their clients back into Putnam remains very much an open question, especially as West Coast rival American funds continues to capture the lion's share of new money flowing into the mutual-fund business this year. Like Putnam, American is a fund family that sells exclusively through brokers and other middlemen, and is widely praised for its low fees and a conservative investment style that produces consistent returns.

Daniel Galli, a financial planner in Rockland who had clients switch from Putnam to American funds, among others, said it is unlikely they would switch back. ''There's no need for them to," Galli said. ''They're at American funds, and they're happy."
