Trendy Chinese slippers are the soles of summer

By Wendy Lee, Globe Correspondent  |  August 1, 2004

Move over sneakers and flip-flops, there's a new shoe tearing up the streets.

They're Chinese slippers that women are snatching up by the dozen -- and then some -- because they cost as little as $3 a pair.

Thirteen-year-old Sharon A. Noel on warm weather days trades in her sneakers and heels for the mesh flats decorated with sequined flowers. She owns 15 pairs in blue, white, and pink.

''They're in style," said the Boston teenager. ''You can wear it with whatever you want. You can wear them with pajamas."

The plastic slippers, imported from various makers in China, are sold in many places, including Chinatown shops, trendy retailer Urban Outfitters, and department stores. The prices range from as little as $3 a pair from a Chinatown street vendor to about $20 at Filene's. While there are no numbers to quantify how many slippers have been sold, retailers say the style is one of the summer's big fashion hits.

''Everybody's wearing them," said Brian Hull, store manager for Urban Outfitters in Cambridge.

Indeed, it's not just young girls, but young professionals, some Democratic National Convention delegates, and older women who are pairing the low-maintenance shoes with power suits and evening gowns. Those who bought them say they are cheap, comfortable, and outperform the average flip-flop.

''Flip-flops can't put in mileage," said Susan Duane, 48, of Wellesley, who owns three pairs of slippers. ''I'm not really into trends, but it's so inexpensive, I decided to go for it."

The Chinese slippers are the latest to make their footprint in the casual shoe category. In a backlash to spike heels, more women are reaching for dressy sneakers and flip-flops.

Casual and active casual are among the highest growth categories in the footwear industry, generating $12.7 billion in sales year over year, according to Port Washington, N.Y., market research firm NPD Group.

The casual shoe category, which includes loafers and clogs, rose 8.4 percent from a year ago, while active casual, which includes non-athletic sneakers, grew 6.7 percent from a year ago.

Women typically wore the Chinese flats as house slippers in Hong Kong and Tokyo but they became fashionable when socialites wore them in chic cafes in Paris, said Joan P. White, president of the Paris Fashion Institute in Boston. These trendy slippers are not to be confused with other types of simple shoes imported from China, which lack the color and sparkle of the popular slippers.

As for why women are buying as many pairs as if they were Imelda Marcos, White justified the behavior. ''It's not like you're buying a Prada shoe," she said. ''Nobody feels guilty about it."

Noel, the owner of 15 pairs, said she buys in bulk because she wears them so much they begin to fray. ''So if one breaks you have another pair to wear," she explained.

The nice price, said Mark Montano, a New York fashion designer, is a big reason why the Chinese slippers are gaining on the flip-flop and are becoming ''the sneaker of the yuppie generation."

And unlike the open-toe look of flip-flops, the Chinese slippers are the perfect shoe for women who think they have ugly toes, said Barbara Thornton, owner of Boston footwear merchant, DesignerShoes.com. And unlike sneakers, the mesh design gives women's feet air conditioning, she added.

But even though the slippers are relatively inexpensive, stores and vendors selling the product can take healthy profits.

One of them, Berk's Store in Harvard Square, hawks the slippers for $10 a pair, selling as many as 10 pairs a day on weekends and about five pairs each weekday, said assistant manager Marc Wolfson.

Berk's buys the slippers from Cheng's Enterprises Inc. of Carlstadt, N.J. The slippers purchased in bulk usually cost retailers around $1.75 for each pair, said Cheng Paing, sales manager for Cheng's Enterprises.

''That's the hottest slipper in town right now," Paing said, adding his company gets wholesale orders that range from 60,000 to 100,000 pairs per week.

Men are even buying the shoes -- as gifts for their lady friends, said Maria Yee, wife of Rich Yee, who owns Man Ken Co, a gift shop in Boston's Chinatown. And some customers, like 26-year-old Rachelle Willard of Cambridge, buy the slippers and send them to friends abroad.

But don't expect the trend to last long, said Jay Calderin, founder and executive director of Fashion Week Boston. ''The look can be charming with the right outfit," he said, ''but they often miss the mark by being matched with inappropriate garments."

Twelve-year-old Tiffany Hammond of Dorchester said she thinks the trend will last for at least a few more months. The owner of 15 pairs said she plans to sport her Chinese slippers into the fall, when school starts -- that is ''if they're still in style."
