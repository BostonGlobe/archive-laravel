Business in brief

August 26, 2004

THE REGION

Stratouly reacquires Wayland property

Boston developer Dean F. Stratouly reacquired the Wayland Business Center, a 55-acre property that he turned back to his lender, a subsidiary of Credit Suisse First Boston, late last year. Stratouly said he and a New York partner, an investment fund of Praedium Group LLC, purchased the land and 410,000 square feet of office space in five buildings for an undisclosed sum. Stratouly bought the building in 1997 with a division of Fidelity Investments and redeveloped the five buildings for about $55 million. Polaroid Corp. signed a 15-year lease for 340,000 square feet, but then filed for bankruptcy protection and gave up the space. The Credit Suisse unit took the property to cover a loan of $50 million but then put the property up for sale. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

FDA approves blood test for newborns

Wellesley-based PerkinElmer Inc., which reached an agreement with regulators in February to resume producing medical tests, won federal approval for a kit to screen newborns for a group of inherited diseases. The rare diseases can cause seizures, mental retardation, and death, the Food and Drug Administration said. The kit, using a blood sample, measures for excess amounts of amino acids and other signs that a baby's metabolism isn't working properly, the FDA said. PerkinElmer spokesman Dan Sutherby didn't return a call. (Bloomberg)

Blood vessel device gets go-ahead

Boston Scientific Corp. received clearance from the Food and Drug Administration to market a device that treats blockages in blood vessels. The company plans to market the device immediately. The Natick medical device company said its peripheral cutting balloon, a microsurgical dilatation device, is basically a miniature balloon that has tiny microsurgical blades on the surface. Boston Scientific's balloon is an alternative to traditional treatments that may put more pressure on the vessel wall. (Dow Jones)

Three receive jail terms in fraud case

Three midlevel Allied Deals Inc. employees were sentenced for their roles in a $700 million metals-trading fraud that duped 10 banks, including FleetBoston Financial Corp. and PNC Financial Services Group Inc. Josielynn Salumbides, Manoj Nijhawan, and Kaushik Amin were convicted in May in federal court in New York of bank fraud, money laundering, and conspiracy to commit wire, bank, and mail fraud. Salumbides was sentenced to five years in prison and ordered to pay restitution of $683.6 million, interim US Attorney David Kelley said. Nijhawan was sentenced to 3 years in prison and ordered to pay $683.6 million and Amin received an 18-month sentence and was ordered to pay $2.2 million in restitution. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

2 companies win US antimissile contract

Teams led by BAE Systems and Northrop Grumman were chosen to build and test prototypes for antimissile systems to defend US commercial planes against shoulder-fired rockets. The Homeland Security Department said the companies will each get $45 million over the next 18 months. It's the second phase of the agency's effort to determine whether affordable, effective antimissile systems can be deployed on commercial planes. Last January, BAE, Northrop, and United Airlines were chosen from among 24 companies that sought $2 million contracts to develop plans for using antimissile technology. All three came up with plans that use lasers to redirect heat-seeking rockets away from aircraft engines. United's plan was not considered as advanced, so it was dropped. (AP)

Former Enron executive pleads guilty

Enron's former top investor-relations executive pleaded guilty to charges he helped paper over the energy company's shaky financial condition before its collapse. Mark Koenig, 49, pleaded guilty to aiding and abetting securities fraud, which carries up to 10 years in prison. In a related civil action brought by the Securities and Exchange Commission, Koenig agreed to turn over to the government $1.49 million in forfeited assets and civil penalties and cooperate with government investigators. As the head of investor relations, Koenig served as the main link to investors and analysts. He oversaw the company's earnings announcements -- exercises that proved fraudulent -- and coordinated conferences with stock analysts. Federal prosecutors said he participated in and knew about efforts on the part of senior management to fool investors into believing the company was financially healthy before it collapsed in December 2001. (AP)

THE WORLD

Asian airline gives Boeing mixed signal

Singapore Airlines, Asia's most valuable flag-carrier, gave Boeing Co. some good news and bad news, ordering up to 31 of the airplane maker's long-range jets but saying it would hold off on deciding whether to buy the new 7E7 Dreamliner. The contract is a big win for Boeing in its dogfight with European rival Airbus. But the hesitation on the Dreamliner likely rankles, as Boeing's commercial future is heavily reliant on the new model's success. Singapore Airlines said it had placed a firm order for 18 Boeing 777-300ER jetliners, to be delivered between 2006 and 2010. The company also said it had reserved the option to buy 13 more of the airplanes. In total, the deal would be worth $7.35 billion in list prices, including the cost of spare parts and spare engines. (AP)

Yukos must pay $223m to settle tax bill

Russian oil giant Yukos, struggling with an enormous back-taxes bill, must pay $223 million for missing a payment deadline, a court ruled. Yukos already owes $3.4 billion in back taxes for 2000 that it warns it cannot pay, and the total claims against it for the period 2000 to 2003 are expected to swell to some $10 billion. Russian law allows government bailiffs to levy fines of 7 percent of the amount they are collecting if payment is not made within the prescribed time. Bailiffs had initially tried to assess the fee on July 9 after Yukos missed a deadline for the 2000 bill. But a court blocked the move this month, after Yukos's lawyers argued the bill was so enormous that it could not be paid within the period. The government appealed, and the latest ruling means Yukos will have to pay. (AP)

EU to probe Microsoft-Time Warner deal

Risking another trans-Atlantic tussle over Microsoft Corp., European Union antitrust regulators said they were investigating whether the software giant's deal with media conglomerate Time Warner Inc. to develop antipiracy software might lead to a new monopoly. Microsoft -- already fighting to overturn last March's EU ruling that it abused its dominant position with the Windows operating systems -- and Time Warner both said they were ''fully cooperating" with the EU probe after initial efforts to head it off fell short. In opening the in-depth probe, the European Commission said it was worried about Microsoft's ability to ''tip" burgeoning demand for so-called ''digital rights management" in its favor, turning what is already a ''leading position" in that market into a dominant one. (AP)

Indian software company begins trading

Shares of India's top software firm, Tata Consultancy Services Ltd., soared in their first day of trading. Tata Consultancy raised $1.2 billion by selling 55.4 million shares in the Indian stock market's biggest-ever initial public offering by a private company. The offer closed Aug. 5 and there were more than seven bids for each share sold. On the Bombay Stock Exchange, shares opened at $23, 23.5 percent higher than the offer price of $18. They closed at $21, still up more than 16 percent. (AP)
