CHUCK JAFFE | YOUR MONEY

Did wake-up call come too late?

By Chuck Jaffe  |  August 26, 2004

While the "vice mail" stock fraud scheme was captivating investors recently, the big story from this mess will be played out in the coming months.

It centers on the question of whether regulators exercised common sense.

Vice mail is the pump-and-dump ploy the Securities and Exchange Commission warned investors about last week. In this swindle, a phony voice-mail message is delivered to recipients as if it was a wrong number, with a woman inviting her friend -- the person for whom the message was supposedly intended -- to get in on a hot stock tip.

Insiders at the SEC suggest that roughly 10 different stocks have been used in this scheme -- one was Maui General Store -- and thousands of investors have been taken in.

The investor alert was designed to be a wake-up call for the public. But did that call come too late?

Regulators say that because the trading in stocks like Maui General was all electronic, they will be able to reconstruct records quickly and efficiently. The investigation aims to identify who is behind the stock fraud by looking for the guy who pocketed the cash by promoting the stock and selling into the mania.

Several securities regulators I talked to say they expect to complete the case, with charges filed, inside of six months.

But they had better catch the crooks, because in all of the coverage of the investor alert, the media overlooked one key question: What took so long?

Regulators first heard about vice mail roughly three weeks before they took action. They knew at least some of the stocks involved, and should have been able to stop the trading in those issues or raise a red flag -- but they didn't do it.

In their defense, I should point out that I heard about the scam the first week of August, too, and spent two weeks trying to get the story nailed down tight. That may have been a mistake, because I have talked to people who lost money in the week before the SEC announcement, and a story might have saved them some pain.

Part of the concern for regulators was how the story might tarnish the stocks. Management in all cases has disavowed any knowledge of the scam. It's not hard to hype a stock and get it cooking without the involvement of company officials.

If regulators let these scams percolate just long enough to have the information to catch the bad guys, and then warned investors to stop chasing this kind of garbage, then the outcome may be fine.

But if regulators can't nail the bad guys and use that to deter future scams, they missed the boat. So did I in holding the story a few days longer.

Document comes of ageFor all the regulatory reforms made in the wake of stock blow-ups like Enron and scandals in the mutual fund industry, most average investors can't name one "improvement" that has changed the way they invest. For savvy investors, that might have changed this week, when new rules regarding Form 8-K disclosures went into effect.

The 8-K is a heretofore barely noticed document, a disclosure that public companies must make whenever they have a "material event" that a prudent shareholder would want to know about because it might influence his decision to buy, sell, or hold.

The news that triggers an 8-K filing can range from signing a big contract to losing a key deal, a top manager or director, significant layoffs, and more.

Given that scope, you might have expected the 8-K to have always been a help to investors, but the SEC used to give companies days or weeks to file papers on key events. It allowed some to slip by undisclosed altogether. By the time investors could get an 8-K, the material had worn thin or there were holes in the information.

The new reforms close the loopholes and force companies to file the 8-K in short order, frequently within 24 to 48 hours of the triggering event.

What's more, the penalty for failing to get the job done on time is severe. A company that misses the requirements wouldn't be able to use SEC "short form" registration documents the next time it files to raise capital. The long version would be more costly to management and likely would turn off prospective lenders.

For a savvy investor, the new 8-K could make for improved decision-making. Simply put, delving into the SEC database to examine recent 8-K filings before making a buy decision would make a lot of sense.

"There are a handful of things that will be meaningfully different to the sophisticated investor, rather than the mom-and-pop investor," says lawyer William D. Sherman, senior partner in the securities practice at Morrison & Foerster in San Francisco. "Investors will be able to find out about a lot of things without having to wait until the end of the quarter, and they can use the filings to make sure that nothing has changed in their assumptions about, say, the stability of management or in some big contract.

Investors who decide to make an 8-K review need not rush in. While the speedy disclosure rules went into effect this week, old documents weren't updated and remain their tired, old, largely useless selves. New documents won't build up a critical mass and tell a real story about a company for a while.
