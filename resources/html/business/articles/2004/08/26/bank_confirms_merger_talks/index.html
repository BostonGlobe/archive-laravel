Bank confirms merger talks

Maine firm seen seeking partner to aid expansion

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  August 26, 2004

Banknorth Group of Maine confirmed yesterday that it was in merger talks with TD Bank Financial Group of Canada, a move that would end Banknorth's short reign as New England's largest independent bank.

Banknorth, which has marketed itself in recent months as a local alternative to the giants in New England's market, has spent years building a strong branch network that stretches from Maine to Connecticut. But in a market driven increasingly by acquisitions, some analysts said yesterday that Banknorth may be running into trouble expanding beyond its home in New England and wants a hands-off financial partner to finance its expansion.

The deal also would benefit Toronto-based TD Bank, which has long coveted a broader US retail presence. TD Bank also owns the TD Waterhouse brokerages in the United States, which are known for their ads featuring ''Law & Order" star Sam Waterston.

Banknorth executives could not be reached yesterday. A TD Bank spokesman declined to comment further. Though the talks are not final yet and could fall through, preliminary discussions would give TD a controlling stake in Banknorth that could be worth $2.2 billion to $3.5 billion.

If the deal happens, TD would become the second foreign company to control a major New England bank. The Royal Bank of Scotland owns Citizens Financial Group, the region's second-largest bank. A Canadian insurer, Manulife Financial Corp., also acquired Boston insurer John Hancock Financial Services Inc. in the last year.

The Citizens Bank agreement with the Royal Bank of Scotland could become a model for Banknorth's transaction. While other foreign acquisitions of US banks have yielded mixed results, Citizens is growing rapidly and has become an increasingly important part of the Royal's operations.

The Royal owns Citizens, but Citizens is run by Americans and has wide latitude to make business decisions. At the same time, the Royal has the deep pockets to finance acquisitions that Citizens might not be able to afford on its own.

James Ackor, an analyst at RBC Capital Markets, said Banknorth's deal likely would leave most things at Banknorth the same: same management team, few job cuts, and the same name on branches.

''You're talking about a nice bump for your shareholders, retaining thousands of jobs, and the addition of a corporate parent that gives Banknorth a mandate to continue to grow," he said. ''It's going to look a lot like Citizens."

But Banknorth also would take on a foreign parent at a far different time than the Citizens deal. The Royal bought Citizens in 1989, before most of the consolidation in New England's banking landscape. Now, if Banknorth wants to continue to acquire other banks, it will have to look mostly outside New England.

Banknorth's chief executive, William J. Ryan, has indicated to analysts that the bank is interested in moving more heavily into New York. ''As we look forward, it continues to be Massachusetts, Connecticut, maybe upstate New York," Ryan said on the bank's second-quarter earnings call in mid-July. ''And for the first time, I'll hint to you that maybe it's time for us to look at coming down into New York in some fashion into Westchester County or New York City even."

Banknorth also may face problems closer to home as it tries to compete for deposits with North Carolina giant Bank of America Corp., which is moving into New England through its acquisition of FleetBoston Financial Corp. Fleet had been New England's largest bank before the merger closed in April. Analyst Nancy Bush of NAB Research wrote in an Aug. 10 research note that Bank of America's management ''is not conceding even one point of deposit market share" and that Ryan, Banknorth's chief executive, and others in the region ''may find themselves coming up short on gathering the deposit riches that were projected to fall from the sky as the integration of the Fleet franchise proceeds."

Banknorth, which has more than 350 branches in New England and upstate New York and assets of $29.3 billion, has been viewed in recent years as both an acquirer and a potential takeover target. TD Bank, based in Toronto and owner of Toronto-Dominion Bank, has assets of nearly $240 billion and more than 1,000 branches across Canada.

TD has made previous attempts to enter the US market. Canadian banks are interested in the US market because there are relatively few banks -- and therefore few merger opportunities -- in Canada, said Ackor, the analyst at RBC Capital Markets.

Last year, TD was in talks to merge its TD Waterhouse brokerage with online broker ETrade Financial Corp. The merger would have created the second-largest discount broker in the United States, but talks were called off in January after the two sides said they couldn't agree on terms.

TD's stock, traded on the New York Stock Exchange, closed at $34.17, down $1.02. Banknorth's stock closed at $34.88, up $3.18.
