In poor job market, a rush to nursing

Schools around country flooded with applicants

By Charles Stein, Globe Staff  |  August 11, 2004

Lisa DeFalco has a bachelor's degree, a master's degree, and more than a decade of experience counseling troubled kids. But not long ago, she gave it all up to go to nursing school at the University of Massachusetts at Boston. She felt she could have more impact on people's lives as a nurse.

Then there is the money.

"Counseling was a dead end salary-wise," said DeFalco, 39, who never earned more than $40,000 in her old career. She is expecting to land a job at a Boston teaching hospital by January with a starting paycheck of about $50,000.

With a baby on the way, she also hopes to work a flexible schedule that will allow her to minimize the need for day care.

Welcome to nursing, one very popular profession.

Nursing schools in Massachusetts and around the country are flooded with applicants, many of them refugees from other fields. In a weak job market, where job security is hard to come by, nursing has come to be seen as a stable, reasonably well-paying profession with a bright future.

"I'm not sure our students know what nursing is all about, but they know it is a job with a good salary," said Marion Winfrey, an associate nursing dean at UMass-Boston. Winfrey this year received 130 applications from students who wanted to transfer into the nursing program from another area of study. She had room for 20. At UMass-Lowell, 567 students applied to the nursing school, up from 240 two years ago.

At Middlesex Community College the competition for spots in the nursing program is intense.

"It may be harder to get in here than at Harvard," said Brenda Loucks, a spokeswoman for the school. She is only half joking. Middlesex Community had 617 applicants for 60 nursing slots. Like Harvard, Middlesex Community accepted about 10 percent of those seeking admission.

Nursing schools say a surprising number of those applying are older students with degrees and job experience in other industries. Their backgrounds are varied: high technology, retail, education. What they have in common is a belief that nursing represents a better alternative.

Nursing has long been sensitive to shifts in the economy. When the Massachusetts economy was booming in the late 1980s, the vacancy rate for nurses at local hospitals skyrocketed. When the state's economy tanked in the early 1990s, the nurse vacancy rate plunged from 10 percent to 2 percent. In good times, economists say, people with nursing degrees find better opportunities in other fields. In tough times, they return to nursing as a reliable standby.

In the prosperous late 1990s, the nurse vacancy rate went back to 10 percent. To bring it down, hospitals, desperate to find talent, began to boost wages.

According to the Massachusetts Hospital Association, over the past four years the average nursing wage at Boston-area hospitals has climbed 30 percent, to $35 an hour.

Starting hospital nurses today can earn $40,000 to $50,000 in metropolitan Boston. Experienced nurses can earn up to $80,000 in the suburbs and up to $100,000 at Boston's teaching hospitals, administrators say.

The paychecks come with good benefits and an opportunity to work different schedules, something that is especially appealing to women with children. While there have been periodic layoffs of nurses in the past, the job comes with more security than most other jobs today. The US Department of Labor has predicted nursing will be the fastest-growing occupation between now and 2012. With thousands of nurses expected to retire in coming years, the need for new nurses should remain strong for the foreseeable future.

The profession itself has worked hard to spread the message that more nurses are needed. Around the country nurses regularly visit schools, including middle schools, to talk up the advantages of nursing as a career.

The message has gotten through. "We've had a nice labor market pickup," said Peter Buerhaus, a nursing expert at Vanderbilt University. More than 100,000 nurses were added last year to payrolls nationwide, he said. Many of them were older nurses, who rejoined the workforce in response to new incentives. Nursing school applicants, Buerhaus said, are responding to the same signals.

Many of the people coming to nursing from another profession say they were inspired by personal encounters with nurses when their parents were treated for serious illnesses or when their children were hospitalized. For Ann Milone of Salem, N.H., who recently graduated from UMass-Lowell, it was the experience of seeing her third child, born prematurely, treated by the nurses at Brigham and Women's Hospital.

"They were calling the shots on my son's life," she said. "It was so impressive."

Milone, 43, previously worked as a healthcare administrator.

Others say they did not find what they were looking for in their previous careers. Bonnie Jarabek worked for years in retailing before she decided nursing was what she wanted to do. "Retailing just wasn't fulfilling," she said. "It was hi-bye. In nursing there is the human contact. As you get older you appreciate that more." Jarabek, 40, is enrolled at Brockton Hospital School of Nursing.

But practical considerations are there as well. Erik Parena, 21, is the son of a nurse, another common feature among nursing students. He is impressed by the fact that his mother, a Filipino immigrant, has done well enough to afford a home and support her children.

"Those are the same goals I have for my life," he said.

Like many other students, Parena also wants some assurance there will be a job for him when he graduates. So far, the job market remains solid. "Nursing," Parena said, "isn't like computers where they will outsource jobs."
