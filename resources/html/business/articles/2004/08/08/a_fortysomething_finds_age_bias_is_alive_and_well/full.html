VIEW FROM THE CUBE

A fortysomething finds age bias is alive and well

By Coco McCabe, Globe Correspondent  |  August 8, 2004

I've been thinking about a boss I once had. She had short-cropped gray hair and a voice gravelly from cigarettes she inhaled by the carton. She was an old-school newspaper editor and I was a twentysomething cub reporter. We had both been hired at about the same time. I found her scary -- not just because she chewed me out for my sloppy spelling but because she seemed so old and alien in a newsroom full of people a fraction of her age.

How, I wondered, had she managed to get the job?

Fast-forward a quarter century. I could be that boss, minus the hairdo and the bark. At 48, I'm probably a few years younger than she was and maybe a little better preserved. But I'm quite sure there will be twentysomethings eyeing me with the same uneasiness as I lumber into their work lives.

I'm shopping for a job, a regular job with a paycheck and benefits. For that, I have to show my face. Is it an employable face? I used to think so. Now -- creeping up on 50 -- I'm not so sure. It all depends on who's doing the hiring.

If it's someone like the young man who was part of a youthful hiring committee I recently appeared before, I won't stand a chance. He kept his nose in his notebook through most of the interview, as though he dreaded making eye contact with me. I know he thought I was old enough to be his mother. The idea that one of her frumpy peers might crash his work party probably threw him for a loop.

But at least I got the interview. At my stage of the game, I realize now, that is a big part of the battle. There is no doubt about the reality of age discrimination in the work world. Twenty-five years ago I was feeding it; today I'm feeling it.

What I can't figure out, though, is the contradiction in all of this. Consider Vice President Dick Cheney, 63, white-haired and balding. His supporters take comfort in his age. To them, it represents experience and gravity -- qualities they want in a guy holding one of the country's top jobs. Then there is the boyish-looking John Edwards. At 51, he's too young and too green, says the Cheney camp.

What I wouldn't give to be considered young by the crowd that controls the cubicle scene. Unfortunately for the average job hunter, the real world doesn't work like the one Cheney operates in. For us, age remains a major obstacle in the job market.

So, what do we do about it?

Well, there's always Botox. I read about one desperate man who had been job shopping in a field where youthful looks were as important as the experience detailed on his resume. He was getting nowhere and needed help. He went to a plastic surgeon for some hits of the bacterial nerve toxin to wipe out his wrinkles.

I'm not up for Botox shots. I'd rather deal with the reality of the situation head on. For that, there's nothing like a bit of smart advice from an adolescent boy.

The one in question is a redhead. His hair is thick and sprouts exuberant tufts that refuse to be restrained. That might torture a less confident child, but not this one. On bad hair days, his solution is simple: He avoids his reflection. What he can't see can't bother him.

If outrageous hair can't perturb a boy at the height of adolescent self-consciousness, then why in the world should I let a wrinkle or two get in my way? No more fretting in front of the mirror. It certainly helps my attitude. But it doesn't change the math: Any human resources professional can still look at my resume, do a quick calculation, and know I graduated from college many years ago. How old does that make me? Too old, way too old.

Of course, no human resources person would ever dare come out and say that. It's illegal, and has been for almost 40 years, ever since the federal Age Discrimination in Employment Act was passed. It says that if your company employs 20 or more people, it can't discriminate against any of them over the age of 40, and that includes recruiting and hiring them.

Still, it seems certain employers ignore those rules. For instance, some job descriptions ramble on about the multitude of skills required for the post. You'd have to work for 100 years -- and grow old enough to be totally decrepit -- before you could master all that stuff. Read to the bottom of the description and the last line goes something like this: Seeking candidates with two or three years of experience. That's shorthand for forytsomethings need not apply.

You know and I know that some companies see older workers, with our hefty mortgages and costly health insurance, as too expensive to hire. They want someone they can get for cheap. But how do they know what I'm willing to settle for unless they ask?

They'll never know, until I can figure out some way to get my foot in the door. Some job counselors say one way to slip under the age radar and snag that initial interview is to remove a few key dates from your resume; starting with the year you earned your bachelor's degree. The problem with that is the dumped date would leave a glaring hole. I'm sure the human resources rep would be quick to ask: What's she hiding?

No, omission isn't the solution. So what is?

Sometimes, I think what I need is a front woman -- someone to pretend she's me, someone who is two decades younger and wrinkle-free. She could peddle my resume, door to door, with a firm handshake and direct eye contact. I'd do the work if she could get the job.

Come to think of it, that's what every jobless worker my age could use: an impersonator. There are already personal chefs, personal shoppers, and personal organizers to keep us fed, clothed, and uncluttered. Why not add personal applicant to the list of services?

In this market, it would be a growth industry.
