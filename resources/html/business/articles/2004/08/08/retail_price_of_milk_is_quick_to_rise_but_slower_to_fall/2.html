Retail price of milk is quick to rise, but slower to fall

August 8, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Stop & Shop's pricing approach seems to contradict signs that were posted above the dairy cases in some of its stores.

ADVERTISEMENT

The signs, addressed to ''our valued customers," explain that wholesale milk prices have increased dramatically on a national basis due to reduced supplies and increased demand.

''Our new prices reflect only the increase in the cost of raw milk," the signs said. ''We will continue to monitor the market and adjust prices to offer you the best value possible."

Shaw's Supermarkets, the region's second-largest supermarket chain, has also moved slowly in responding to the downturn in raw milk prices. It cut the price of its store-brand milk at most stores to $3.51 a gallon in July. Last Thursday, the same day Stop & Shop lowered its price, Shaw's cut its price at most stores to $3.29 a gallon.

Other chains have lowered their milk prices more swiftly or kept them low during the earlier runup. The Market Basket chain was charging $2.49 a gallon for its store-brand milk last week, while Johnny's Foodmaster was charging $2.69 a gallon. Roche Bros. put its store-brand milk on sale for $2.99 a gallon early last week.

Garelick Farms makes the store-brand milk for most chains in the region, so even though prices differ from chain to chain the product is exactly the same.

Looking ahead, federal regulators and industry analysts are forecasting that raw milk prices will remain roughly at the current level in September and then rise slightly in October and November, before dipping back down in December and January.

A recent report by Deutsche Bank Securities indicated one reason why Stop & Shop and Shaw's may be able to move slowly on price decreases is the ''benign competitive environment" in this area.

The report said the two chains have a higher level of profitability and more sales per square foot than the rest of the industry. It praised management at the two companies but also said limited competition was a factor.

''In our view, the limited presence of discounters as well as the quasi-duopoly structure of the market has allowed food retailers to pass along most of the higher commodity prices to consumers," the report said. ''New England should remain a cash cow market in the medium term."

B trunk to goComcast is shutting down the ''B trunk" this week on its Boston and Brookline cable systems, relieving customers of the need to switch back and forth from A to B to watch channels.

Officials at the cable firm say customers will need to learn new station numbers. (Attention Red Sox fans: NESN is moving to A13). But the officials said that inconvenience will be more than offset by not having to switch back and forth between trunks, improved system reliability (60 percent of all trouble calls dealt with the B trunk), and the addition of new programming.

The two-trunk system allowed Boston and Brookline residents to get twice as many channels as their counterparts in other communities 20 years ago, but advances in technology in recent years have made the two-trunk system obsolete and inefficient.

Along with the B trunk, Comcast is eliminating a number of the grandfathered cable packages in Boston and Brookline left over from the Cablevision era. In many instances, the changes will force customers to move to new packages that cost more.

Comcast is also removing a number of premium channels (Showtime, Encore, pay-per-view) from analog and putting them exclusively on digital to conserve bandwidth. Customers can still subscribe to those channels at the same price, but they'll need a digital box that costs roughly $2 more a month.

Jens Meyer of Boston estimates the changes will cost him about 40 percent more a month if he decides to keep the same channel selection as he has now.

''The end result is a pretty strong desire to move Comcast customers to the digital cable packages, at a higher cost to the customer when comparing matching analog and digital cable offers," he said.
