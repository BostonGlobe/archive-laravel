Know what facts to include, and exclude, on resume

By Roni F. Noland  |  August 8, 2004

My resume is a bit of a problem. I have worked for many small, family-owned companies on the South Shore. Every time these businesses felt economic pressures they would cut expenses via layoffs. For all but one of the jobs on my resume, the company was the one that ended my employment. My resume shows all of these as short terms of employment. This makes it appear as though I jump from job to job frequently. I am currently working with several employment agencies. Some of these agencies have told me that my situation is fairly common. I'm not so sure that is correct. What do you recommend that I should do to overcome the short stays on my resume?

ADVERTISEMENT

Layoffs during difficult economic times are not uncommon. However, some prospective employers still consider serial employment as a "red flag" when choosing candidates. A series of short-term work assignments -- especially in the absence of any solid work history -- is suspect.

You do not have to be a prisoner of your work history. You worked for three companies for approximately two years each, according to your resume. Highlight those positions and talk about your major accomplishments in each.

Eliminate some of your positions from your resume. Listing a job that you held for only a month or two is foolish -- it only reinforces an employer's perception of you as a job hopper.

A resume is not a job application. What differentiates a resume from an application is that on a resume you have choices about what to include and what to exclude. On a resume, you have the opportunity to highlight your accomplishments and to present yourself in the most flattering way possible.

Think about each position you held and decide whether including it will enhance your resume or detract from the perception of you as a capable job applicant

You will have more than enough work experience without including every one of your jobs. Another suggestion would be to group all your short-term positions under a single heading.

You could say something like "several short-term positions in small family-owned accounting firms on the South Shore." Then you could bracket the dates of those positions as "1996-present."

In addition, you could move the three longer-term positions that you held before 1996 to a more prominent position on your resume. You could explain the deviation from a strictly chronological format by heading that section "Major Accounting Experience."

Then the statement about your short-term positions would appear later on your resume, under the heading "Additional Accounting Experience" or "Small-Firm Experience." Use only the years of employment rather than detailing months and years.

The benefits of reworking your resume to focus on your strengths rather than your liabilities as a job seeker are threefold:

You will eliminate the "red flag" that your current resume raises for employers.

You will feel better about yourself as a job seeker.

You will be empowered to talk more positively about your past accomplishments and your future benefits to a prospective employer.

As you interview for a new position, emphasize that you are now seeking a long-term relationship with an employer and are eager to make a commitment.

You may need to research thoroughly a prospective employer to determine how solvent the company is before you accept a new position. Your goal is to find a position that can be a stable career move for you.

Target large publicly held companies rather than small businesses. You may need to expand your job search geographically as well, to include downtown Boston and Greater Boston, rather than confining yourself to the South Shore.

Start long distance

job search with the Net

I live in New Hampshire and I want to relocate to the Ft. Lauderdale area. I would appreciate any tips on how to secure a position long distance. I have to have a job before I can move, and I am finding this rather daunting. I am a professional with a graduate degree working in the human services field.

Thanks to the Internet, you can conduct much of your research on jobs in the Ft. Lauderdale area by sitting at your computer in New Hampshire.

There are many online resources you can access, including a number of nationwide job databases.

It is unlikely that you will actually be able to secure a new position via the Internet. Your long-distance job search will require several trips to the Ft. Lauderdale area for interviews. Let's outline a possible plan for your search.

Begin by familiarizing yourself with the job market in the Ft. Lauderdale area. Subscribe to a local newspaper, either online or by mail. Read it thoroughly, to get a sense of what's going on in the area that will affect your job prospects.

As a human services professional, you will want to become knowledgeable about city, state, and federal funding patterns and priorities that could affect the solvency of the agencies to which you will be applying. Simultaneously, you will be checking the classified ads and applying for any appropriate positions.

Register with several reputable national job databases, and ones targeted to your profession. If you were a member of any professional associations, a great resource would be the Florida chapter head or membership chair.

Now comes the harder part: networking. If you know people in Ft. Lauderdale, explain to them what kind of work you are looking for and send them your resume. Talk with professional colleagues here to see if anyone knows people in your field in the Ft. Lauderdale area.

Once you have identified several solid professional contacts, schedule a visit to Ft. Lauderdale to meet in person with as many people as possible.

Exploratory interviewing is especially useful when relocating because:

You can gather first-hand knowledge of your field from "insiders."

Key players in your field will get to know you.

You may uncover some job leads that are not yet advertised.

You will identify social service organizations that are congruent with your interests, values, and experience

If in the process of exploring social services in Ft. Lauderdale, you uncover an appropriate job opening, all the better. If not, you will have started building professional relationships in the social service world there and will be known by some of the social service hiring managers.

Leave your resume with anyone you meet; ask permission to continue to be in touch; and be sure to write sincere thank you notes for the advice and information these contacts provide.

A long-distance job search may take you longer than a local one, unless you know that your particular skill-set is in demand in another geographic area.

An alternative strategy to consider might be to relocate without a job, with the intention of getting temporary, interim, or contract work. If it is in an unrelated field but helps you to pay the bills, then you would still have the advantage of being able to respond more quickly to job openings, network more often and more effectively, and to potentially shorten the time it will take for you to find professional employment.

Professional honesty

best in filling the gaps

I have been in the financial industry for 18 years in both sales and retail with few gaps in employment. In April 2003, I had to leave my employer at the time because of a family illness that demanded my full attention. I expected the circumstances to last only a few months but it turned out to be over a year. I am now actively looking for employment and have gone to several informational and formal interviews. They have all gone well, and I have had some offers. I have three more interviews in the next week. How should I answer questions about the gap?

When interviewing, "professional honesty" is often the best policy.

Professional honesty means telling the employer only enough so he or she can make an informed decision about you. If I were you, I would say: "Due to a family illness, I took a year off in 2003. The precipitating circumstances for the leave no longer have an impact on my ability to accept and to successfully perform full-time employment."

Some employers might even be impressed because you had taken more than a year off from a successful career to tend to an ailing relative.

They would see this as a reflection of your values and priorities. How an employer responds to your taking family leave could serve as a test of the company's own values and priorities.
