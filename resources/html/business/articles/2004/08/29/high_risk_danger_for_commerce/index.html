High-risk danger for Commerce

With Romney seeking to heighten competition between car insurers, the company, which has contributed heavily to candidates, finds its political clout and profits threatened

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  August 29, 2004

Early in 2002, Commerce Insurance of Webster placed a nearly $23,000 bet on the gubernatorial candidacy of Democrat Shannon O'Brien.

O'Brien hadn't won her party's nomination yet, but the checks, most of them for the maximum $500 donation, began pouring in from company officials, directors, lobbyists, and their family members. Nearly all of the checks were deposited by the campaign on the same day, May 17.

It was a watershed moment for the state's largest automobile insurer. After more than a decade of lavish support for Republican gubernatorial candidates whose administrations were very helpful to the company's bottom line, Commerce was changing parties. It was putting its money on the ''sympathetic" Democrat it had known for years and taking a pass on Republican businessman Mitt Romney, a newcomer to Beacon Hill who was preparing to invest nearly $7 million of his own money in his campaign.

Two years later, Romney has become a major headache for Commerce. Entrenched in the governor's corner office, he's launched a top-to-bottom shakeup of the state's auto insurance system, with increased competition as his goal, and Commerce finds itself on the defensive with few friends in the administration.

The company, once a consummate inside player on Beacon Hill, is now on the outside looking in as its bread-and-butter business is restructured in ways that could dampen earnings. It has estimated that one pending proposal will boost its expenses $3.6 million over just the next year and a half. Company officials, unable to work things out behind the scenes, are out testifying at hearings, talking to reporters, threatening to sue the insurance commissioner, and even siding with consumer advocates.

Commerce officials say they don't oppose change or the introduction of competition. They just don't like some of the approaches being taken.

In interviews, both Romney and top Commerce officials said the insurance company's support for O'Brien in 2002 has been a nonissue in the debate over auto insurance reform. Romney laughed at any suggestion his administration might be seeking revenge for Commerce's support of his rival. He said he had never heard of Commerce until nine months ago and insisted he was not even aware Commerce had backed O'Brien.

''Reform should benefit consumers," said Romney. ''It shouldn't necessarily impact companies, but my interest is to find a way to make the system work better for consumers."

In carrying out that mandate, the Romney administration has viewed Commerce, which has thrived under the existing regulatory system, as a roadblock to change and tried to maneuver around it. That has meant keeping initial efforts at reworking the system out of the Legislature, where Commerce has considerable political influence and four lobbyists.

It also has meant keeping Commerce on the periphery of decision-making. In April, Beth Lindstrom, director of Romney's office of consumer affairs and business regulation, set the tone with a letter to the governor saying the real challenge ahead on auto insurance was ''to overcome the lobbying and political clout of those insiders who benefit from the current system." The letter didn't name ''those insiders," and Lindstrom said later she wasn't talking about any specific person or company.

When Insurance Commissioner Julianne M. Bowler asked two industry officials to form a working group to develop a plan for distributing high-risk drivers among insurers, Commerce was not initially invited to participate. One of the officials, James Harrington, executive director of the Massachusetts Insurance Federation, said Commerce wasn't invited because it had opposed earlier reform efforts.

''Frankly, we did not perceive that Commerce was in favor of reform," he said.

Romney has discussed auto insurance changes with several Massachusetts insurers as well as large national companies that he would like to see come here. But he's never met with Commerce, which insures one of every four vehicles in the state. Commerce officials say they never requested a meeting with the governor.

Founded in 1972, Commerce has succeeded by paying close attention to insurance fundamentals and the minutiae of insurance regulation, particularly the arcane rules for apportioning drivers that no company wishes to insure voluntarily. Those drivers often end up being unprofitable for insurers. Commerce says it plays by the same rules as everyone else, but competitors say the company's past influence with regulators has allowed it to bend those rules to its advantage.

Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly, in a letter sent to Bowler in 2002, said that Commerce has been able to ''game" the complicated apportionment system, avoiding its fair share of the overall losses of the state's high-risk drivers and boosting its net income by nearly $171 million between 1996 and 2000.

As competitors have pulled out of Massachusetts -- many complaining about overregulation or how the market favored Commerce -- the company has consolidated its hold on what had been a fractured auto insurance market. Its share of total state premiums increased from 14.5 percent in 1992 to nearly 28 percent last year. Its stock is flying high, closing at $48 last Friday. Earlier this year it was the top company in the Globe 100.

The driving force behind Commerce is chairman Arthur J. Remillard Jr., who at 73 doesn't fit the stereotypical image of an insurance executive. He dresses casually and talks bluntly. In a business where all rates are set by state regulators, he has made politics a part of his life and his business. One of the company's lobbyists, John W. Spillane, also serves on Commerce's board of directors.

In the 2002 gubernatorial election, Remillard personally donated the maximum $500 contribution to O'Brien and $500 to both Romney and his running mate, Kerry Healey. Yet despite his personal backing for the candidates from both parties, Remillard said he pushed for company-wide support for O'Brien because he had known her a long time.

''I like what she stands for. She's always had a sympathetic ear to concerns," he said.

O'Brien, who used to serve on the Legislature's Insurance Committee, said she recalled Remillard and some of his family giving her contributions in 2002, but she didn't remember holding discussions with company officials about any specific issues. ''Generally these people play both sides of the fence," she said.

As for Romney, Remillard said he doesn't know him well. He said he met the governor at an August 2002 gathering of GOP contributors at Romney's house. (Romney said he doesn't recall meeting Remillard.) Remillard said he showed up in what he considered appropriate attire for a barbecue and found virtually everyone else dressed in coats and ties.

''The Republicans in general are kind of stiff," said Remillard, who quickly added that former governor William F. Weld was the exception to the rule.

Remillard said he doesn't think Romney pays close attention to his contributor list. He described the governor as a ''big-idea guy" who became interested in revamping the state's auto insurance system when he saw ads on TV from national insurers and wanted to know why he couldn't buy their products here.

Even though donations by Commerce officials appear coordinated, Remillard said he doesn't order company directors, employees, and lobbyists to give to the candidate or candidates he prefers. Remillard said he urges donations but leaves it up to the individuals to decide whether they want to give or not. ''I go and I proselytize," he said.

A look back over the last several elections shows how effective and disciplined a fund-raising machine Commerce can be.

A Globe Spotlight series in 1993 found Commerce officials were by far the biggest corporate givers to Weld and his lieutenant governor, Paul Cellucci. From 1990 to 1993, company officials donated $122,775 to the two Republicans.

The Spotlight series indicated the company had unparalleled access to all levels of the administration and saw its gross profit rise about $3 million after an 11th-hour change within the Weld administration in 1992 that resulted in auto insurance rates rising higher than expected. Commerce's two top lobbyists also ran a strategic consulting business with Weld's chief fund-raiser, Peter Berlandi.

In 1994, Commerce donated close to $34,000 to Weld and Cellucci, with nearly all the money flowing to Cellucci, who was already laying plans for his own run for governor. Commerce continued to help Weld, however, contributing $66,000 in 1995 when the governor launched a fund-raising drive for California governor Pete Wilson's presidential run.

Weld said in a telephone interview that he knew Remillard and members of his family well and always thought of them as Democrats.

''I saw a lot of Arthur in a fund-raising context. He raised a lot of money for me," Weld said. The former governor, who now lives in New York City, said he didn't remember dealing with any specific issues involving Commerce but said it would be fair to characterize the company as a friend of his administration.

''They certainly had access to members of the administration," he said. Gloria Larson, a lawyer who previously had high-level oversight of insurance issues in the Weld administration, declined to comment.

In 2002, Commerce's 12-year infatuation with Republican gubernatorial candidates came to an end. Commerce contributed $22,525 to O'Brien and $1,950 to her running mate, Chris Gabrieli. Romney and Healey received only $2,800 from Commerce officials until days before the election, when $9,500 poured into Healey's campaign. All of the late contributions, which were deposited by the campaign seven days before the election, came from company officials and directors who earlier had donated to O'Brien.

Most of Commerce's directors didn't return phone calls, but those who could be reached denied they were hedging their bets at the last minute.

''I was solicited and I responded. It's as simple as that," said H. Thomas Rowles, a Commerce board member who lives in Rhode Island. He donated $500 to O'Brien in May 2002 and $500 to Healey on Oct. 29, just before the election.

John J. Kunkel, another board member who made the same donations on the same days, said he often talks politics with Remillard, but he couldn't remember to whom he gave money in 2002. ''I only remember what I did yesterday," he said.
