CONSUMER BEAT

Locking in the price of heating oil is no safe bet

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  August 29, 2004

Heating oil prices are unusually high right now, so high that some analysts are wondering whether it's worthwhile for homeowners to try to lock in a price at these levels for the winter.

A state survey conducted this month found the average price of heating oil in Massachusetts was $1.60 a gallon, up 26 cents from last year at this time and just one cent less than the highest average price reached during all of last winter.

The high cost of heating oil is being driven by the price of crude oil, which was hovering around $43 a barrel last week, about $13 more than it was a year ago. Oil prices have moved up and stayed up amid concerns about supply disruptions and surging demand, particularly in China and the developing world but also among recovering Western economies.

Larry Chretien, executive director of the Massachusetts Energy Consumers Alliance, a Jamaica Plain heating oil cooperative, said he is nervous about this winter because no one knows what's going to happen to the price of oil.

''We ought to be prepared for anything," he said. ''It's kind of like getting on a roller coaster with a blindfold on and not knowing when the peak is going to come."

David O'Connor, the commissioner of the state Division of Energy Resources, said heating oil prices could stay as high as they are now or even rise if the US economy keeps growing and the winter is unusually cold. But O'Connor said the odds are also good that oil prices could fall if the winter is mild and the US economy slows down.

The price of oil on the futures market fell slightly last week, but remained well above year-ago levels.

In such an uncertain market, homeowners are faced with a tough decision. If they lock in a price now for the winter, they are basically betting that the price of oil is going to keep rising. If it doesn't, they could end up paying a lot more for oil than if they had just purchased it at market-level prices.

One option offered by many local oil dealers is a price cap, which sets a ceiling on the price the homeowner will pay but allows the consumer to take advantage of lower prices if the cost of oil falls. It sounds great in theory, but the cost of such downside protection in the form of hedge contracts taken by the dealer can be substantial, as much as 10 to 15 cents a gallon. That means the price of heating oil might have to fall as much as 10 to 15 cents below the cap price before the consumer actually benefits.

The Energy Consumers Alliance wanted to offer some form of protection for its customers this winter, but Chretien said the high cost of oil has made that unrealistic. The alliance, which charges members $15 annual dues ($5 for seniors and nothing for those on fuel assistance), said its retail prices are typically 15 to 30 cents less than the statewide average.

A Globe survey of local heating oil dealers indicates several have not decided whether to offer any form of price protection this winter. Others are charging high prices for their fixed-price and cap contracts right now and some are only offering the protection to a narrow range of customers.

Petro, one of the biggest heating oil dealers in the area, is offering new customers a fixed price of $1.65 a gallon and a cap price of $1.75 a gallon. The Petro cap price is roughly 45 cents higher than it was last winter. Alliance Express, another major dealer, is offering new customers a cap price of $1.699, which is 50 cents higher than last year.

All of the prices cited in the Globe survey are for deals offered to new customers. Existing customers often pay higher per-gallon prices and often pay additional fees, which tend to be waived for new customers.

For homeowners looking for the best deal, a new website has just opened that may make the shopping a bit easier.

OrderOil.com allows consumers to shop by ZIP code for market-price deliveries as well as fixed-price contracts for the entire winter. The website's founder, Rob Fawcett, said cap-price contracts should be available in about a month.

Fawcett is soliciting consumer ratings on the 69 dealers on his site, who are located from Virginia to Maine. He said the ratings, which are just beginning to trickle in, should give consumers a way to evaluate the service of heating oil dealers.

''OrderOil is much more than a price comparison tool. Through our rating system, we hope to bring consumer service to the forefront," Fawcett said.

As for whether consumers should lock in a price now or take whatever the market brings, Fawcett begged off. ''I'm no better than anybody else in predicting where the market is going," he said.

Natural gas more costly?

Heating oil customers are facing much higher prices this winter, but the odds are pretty good that people who heat with natural gas will be even worse off.

Last winter, KeySpan Energy Delivery was charging a Boston customer using 150 therms of gas a month the heating-oil equivalent of $1.98 a gallon, well above the current $1.60 statewide average for heating oil. KeySpan officials say prices for this winter won't be made public until next month.

Phishing picks up

Phishing, the practice of using phony e-mails to wangle personal financial information out of people, is starting to target local financial institutions more aggressively.

Scam artists sent out a slew of e-mails last week that appeared to come from Citizens Bank, asking the recipients to supply private financial information to qualify for a bill-pay promotion.

Bank officials say last week's wasn't the first and likely won't be the last attempt to steal financial information from customers.

The officials said the bank would never call or e-mail a customer asking for private information, such as an account number, a Social Security number, or a mother's maiden name.
