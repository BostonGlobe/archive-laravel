Finding the fish faster -- and safer

By Andrew Caffrey, Globe Staff  |  August 16, 2004

Before we launch the kayaks from Deer Island, Patrick Lacchia reaches into the vest pocket of his flotation jacket and pulls out his GPS unit, a Magellan 310 that's about the size of an older cellphone.

Lacchia, 35, and his friends have agreed to let me, a novice kayak fisherman, paddle along to see how they use GPS and sonar technologies as sporting and safety tools. The combination of the two devices allows the group to navigate over dark waters to favorite fishing spots, spy for fish underwater, and keep a record of their travel to prevent them from getting lost.

For, eh-hem, strictly work purposes, I am testing an Eagle Electronics Cuda 240, a combination fish-finder and GPS unit that retails for around $200. A little bigger than a GameBoy, the Cuda has sonar that sharply defines the ocean bottom, drop-offs, and rock outcroppings on a four-inch screen as I paddle to an outer flank of Deer Island.

The unit's GPS functions are prodigious -- too much for me to quickly learn as we head out. So Lacchia and his two friends -- Shaun Ruge, a 25-year-old account manager at State Street Corp., and Ray Avitable, a 35-year-old mechanical engineer who moonlights as a fishing guide -- lead the way. This time of year the trio goes on nighttime fishing outings as often as several times a week.

''Before we leave, we mark our coordinates so we can find our way back," Lacchia says, holding his GPS unit up to get a clear shot at the satellites above. With just a click of a button, Lacchia locks our launching position into the GPS: 42 degrees 21' 23" N latitude, 70 degrees 58' 02" W longitude.

From here, Lacchia can record our path by storing locations along the way, known as waypoints, in the GPS. By highlighting the original coordinates, we can navigate back by following the direction of the arrowhead cursor as it points the kayaks home.

Once in the water Lacchia lights up his fish-finder, an older Humminbird Piranha 5 made by Techsonic Industries Inc. that uses sonar soundings to measure water depth, draw the shape of the ocean bottom, and, best of all, detect any finny friends hiding below. The whole package is then displayed on the fish-finder's 3- by 5-inch screen, which he has mounted on a ridge of the kayak that sticks between his ankles.

The combination of the two technologies makes fishing more efficient. For example, Lacchia scouts online charts for coordinates of shoals, rocks, and drop-offs where fish usually prowl. He plugs the numbers into his GPS and then navigates directly to those sites, instead of guessing at possible fish locations.

''The GPS is going to bring you to the rock, and the sonar is going to tell you, 'I'm on the rocks, but I don't see any fish.' So you go to the next rocks you've entered into your GPS, and all of a sudden, bing, you're marking fish" with the sonar, Lacchia says.

The plan is to navigate to a shoal just east of Deer Island called Great Faun Bar. Then Lacchia's fish-finder will snoop for signs of fish. For the next three hours we remain in sight of land, using the bulb-shaped structures of the Massachusetts Water Resources Authority treatment plant as a navigational guide home, instead of the GPS.

But such visibility can be fleeting. ''There's a lot of fog around here, especially in the summer. You can be a mile offshore and not know which direction land is," Ruge says.

During a lull in the fishing, Avitable suggests -- a little too seriously to my ear -- that we head across the harbor to Lovell's Island. By then, Lovell and many of the other islands at the outer edge of the harbor are invisible against the dark horizon.

If we had taken up the challenge and headed out blindly, the powerful tides sluicing through the harbor shipping lanes could have thrown us miles off course without us knowing it. But with a previous trip to Lovell's stored in the GPS units, we could continually adjust course to keep along the electronic beeline drawn between our location and destination.

Looking over Ruge's shoulder, I make out the outline of Deer Island at the top of the display, and our position opposite it indicated by the cursor.

In between is a shaded shape with looping sides: Faun Bar, just ahead of us. Lacchia is there first, and as we paddle up, the alarm in his fish-finder starts dinging. ''I'm marking fish," Lacchia shouts, as he swings sideways to cast into what he describes as a sizable school visible on his screen.

About a minute later Avitable yells that he's hooked something -- a bluefish that promptly bites through his line. After tying on a new lure, Avitable hooks a second bluefish, only to have that one bite free as he brings it to the kayak. He soon has another hit: a big bass that strips line off his reel, only to drop the lure after getting Avitable's blood pressure pumping.

Few hits, no fish in the boat. In other words, the kind of outing I usually have. Ordinarily, I'd be out on the water without any guidance, electronic or human, flailing around and muttering about being in the wrong spot. But this night, I know I'm in a place with actual fish. The GPS and sonar take half of the guesswork out of fishing, but alas, they don't come with any guarantees.
