BUSINESS IN BRIEF

LoJack buys Canadian tracking company

August 18, 2004

LoJack Corp., which makes devices for recovering stolen cars, agreed to buy Boomerang Tracking Inc. for about $48 million to expand into Canada. LoJack will pay about $4.32 a share in cash or a combination of cash and stock for the Montreal maker of stolen vehicle recovery equipment, LoJack said. Westwood-based LoJack also gets Boomerang's cash balance of $12 million. LoJack said the purchase won't affect earnings this year. The company still expects profit to rise 20 percent in 2004 and sales to increase 10 percent. It expects to complete the acquisition in the fourth quarter. (Bloomberg)

Quincy man sentenced in theft of funds

John F. Kilcommons, a onetime aide to former Boston Mayor Raymond L. Flynn, was given a suspended two-year jail sentence and ordered to pay $285,000 in restitution after pleading guilty to charges he stole funds that 200 Boston city employees entrusted him to invest. Suffolk Superior Court Judge Janet Sanders suspended the jail sentence sought by Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly for five years, put Kilcommons on five years' probation, and sentenced him to perform 200 hours of community service. Kilcommons, 43, of Quincy, a former representative for John Hancock Financial Services Inc. and other firms, pleaded guilty to diverting funds from September 2000 through January 2003 that Boston police officers and other municipal workers directed through payroll deductions to Hancock accounts. Kilcommons has already returned $50,000. (Peter J. Howe)

Kopin shares jump 11% on new product

Kopin Corp. shares rose 11.4 percent after the company unveiled a microdisplay product that it said can dramatically extend the battery life of mobile consumer electronics. Shares rose 38 cents to $3.71. Taunton-based Kopin posted sales last year of $76 million and makes both specialized semiconductors and LED displays for consumer products. The new product uses power at less than a quarter the rate of existing microdisplays, with no loss of image quality or overall performance, according to Bor-Yeu Tsaur, head of the company's display operations. (Dow Jones)

Halliburton to get full pay from Army

Investors in Halliburton Co., the largest US Army contractor in Iraq, got conflicting reports on whether the company will be paid in full for feeding and housing US troops. Linda Theis, a spokeswoman for the Army Material Command, said Halliburton would get full payment for now, reversing a decision four hours earlier that 15 percent would be withheld. Yesterday, Halliburton said it had assurances that a previous waiver, which expired Sunday, would be renewed. The Pentagon has said the company hasn't provided paperwork to justify some of its Iraq costs. The dispute has contributed to a 14 percent drop in Halliburton shares this month. Government rules call for withholding 15 percent of a contractor's payments until the company and government auditors can agree on a final price for services. For Halliburton, that would have meant about $60 million a month, Halliburton spokeswoman Wendy Hall said. (Bloomberg)

ING to withdraw $5b from Janus funds

Janus Capital Group Inc. identified ING US Financial Services as the client planning to withdraw $5 billion from the mutual funds giant. Janus, which recently settled improper trading allegations, revealed the planned withdrawal in late July without naming the client. Janus spokeswoman Shelley Peterson said Janus would work with ING to transfer as many of the assets in kind as possible, meaning the financial services giant would take its portion of the underlying securities instead of a cash withdrawal. The ING withdrawal is equal to nearly 4 percent of Janus' total assets under management. ING officials did not immediately return a phone call. Most assets will be taken out of the Janus Aspen series of funds offered for sale by insurance companies, she said. Janus plans to voluntarily waive any fee increases that would result from the redemption. (AP)

N.Y. website helps with drug costs

A New York State website will help consumers faced with high prescription prices save up to $100 on a single drug purchase while driving down the market price. State Attorney General Eliot Spitzer launched the site, www.NYAGRx.org. A help line is also provided for those without computers (800-771-7755, option 3.) The website allows consumers to shop online for a prescription, giving comparisons by store, address, and price throughout the state. Although not comprehensive, Spitzer said the information covers 170 pharmacies including major chains in 25 counties. It will be updated at least monthly. The site will allow consumers to target stores within a few miles of their home or workplace, according to Spitzer's office. The site uses prices that all drug stores must report under state law on common drugs. (AP)

RealNetworks discounts downloads 50%

RealNetworks Inc. began selling Internet music at half price as it steps up efforts to draw customers from Apple Computer Inc.'s iTunes website. The temporary price of 49 cents a song coincides with the release of RealNetworks's Harmony software, which can download music directly to Apple's iPod digital music player and other devices, RealNetworks senior vice president Dan Sheeran said. RealNetworks will run newspaper advertisements highlighting the alternative to Apple, which designed the iPod to work only with its iTunes online music service. Harmony was designed without Apple's cooperation. The pricing could catapult RealNetworks to number two in online music sales behind iTunes, which has sold 100 million songs, said Josh Bernoff, an analyst at Forrester Research Inc. of Cambridge. (Bloomberg)

Travelers to pay $500m to settle lawsuit

A federal bankruptcy judge approved a plan by Travelers Property Casualty Corp. to pay $500 million to settle claims against the insurer by people with asbestos-related diseases linked to Johns-Manville Corp. More than 600,000 people sued Travelers claiming it knew about the hazards of asbestos and failed to disclose them over the course of some 30 years as Manville's primary insurer. The settlement means asbestos victims will receive extra compensation as the trust Johns-Manville set up in 1988 to pay asbestos claims runs out of funds. The trust resolved 623,400 claims for roughly $3.3 billion and held investments valued at $1.6 billion as of June 30, court papers show. It has 37,000 claims pending. US Bankruptcy Judge Burton R. Lifland approved the agreement at a hearing in New York. (Bloomberg)

Airline union aligns with Teamsters

A majority of 3,100 America West customer service representatives have voted to join the Teamsters, the union and the airline said. The election results were made public at the National Mediation Board. The workers are in 50 airports and two reservation call centers across the country. The airline said it was disappointed by the outcome, but America West chairman and chief executive Doug Parker said the company will "respect the decision made by the majority of our customer service employees." The customer service workers were the only segment of America West employees not represented by a union. (AP)
