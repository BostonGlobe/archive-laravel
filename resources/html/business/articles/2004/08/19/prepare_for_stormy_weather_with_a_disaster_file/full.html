YOUR MONEY

Prepare for stormy weather with a 'disaster file'

By Chuck Jaffe  |  August 19, 2004

As we watched television coverage of Hurricane Charley last weekend, my wife and I reminisced about a time nearly two decades ago when we lived in Florida and got the word to evacuate.

A hurricane was coming north into the Gulf of Mexico, and our one-bedroom newlywed apartment was in a complex a stone's throw from the water. Police drove through, urging everyone to leave. Television reports told us where to go.

We had about an hour to secure the apartment, gather some belongings, and head for higher ground.

Neither Susan nor I can recall precisely what we packed. The cats came with us, and an irreplaceable painting wound up riding around in the car for a few days, but nothing else stood out.

We certainly never grabbed our key financial data.

So as we watched the weather reports and wondered aloud what we would take if we were again ordered to evacuate -- we still live close to the water -- it became clear that, as organized as I am, I don't have my financial papers in perfect order.

Given a few minutes to pack up and leave, I'd be wasting time trying to think about what I needed and where to go so that, if disaster struck, I could rebuild my family's financial life.

Around here, Charley acted only like an average rainstorm. But the storm motivated me to make some changes. Chances are good you might benefit from taking the same precautions.

Preparing for trouble -- flood, fire, earthquake, or something else -- starts with simply locating what's important, making a record of it, and creating a "disaster file" that can be grabbed on a moment's notice.

One-of-a-kind documents, like birth certificates and marriage records, belong in a safe-deposit box, but some specialists suggest keeping certified copies in the disaster file so that you can produce them if a government agency requires them before processing your assistance.

As one person noted: "The same tornado or hurricane that destroys your house can destroy the bank and your safe-deposit box, too."

As for the key to the safe-deposit box, specialists disagree on what to do with it. Generally, it is not something you want to carry around, but some say keeping a key in your "disaster file" makes sense. Others recommend leaving an extra key with a trusted friend or relative, away from your home.

The next step involves getting your financial records and account numbers, as well as contact details for those accounts. One way to simplify the process is to copy your next month's worth of bills, plus bank and brokerage statements. Those copies should show account numbers and customer-service phone lines, which you will need in an emergency.

Your file also should include mortgage and loan information, employee-benefit statements and, perhaps most important, copies of your insurance policies.

It might seem as if having credit-card numbers handy is frivolous, but past disasters have been rife with tales of consumers who had to battle creditors over late fees and other charges. A well-placed call immediately after the big event frequently leads to some measure of leniency from lenders; ignoring financial responsibilities, even after an emergency, tends to increase the potential for hassles.

Opinion is divided on whether the disaster file should include copies of recent tax returns. If you have a tax preparer, he or she may keep copies -- although that office could be vulnerable to the same disaster.

Some suggest that tax paperwork is simply not necessary in an emergency, and I have to admit it's hard to imagine that in the middle of a hurricane I'd be thinking about the Internal Revenue Service. The one plus to keeping tax records in the disaster file is that it forces you to update your paperwork every year, creating a logical time to make sure your records are current.

A number of software packages include organizers that will help you inventory key financial information. But a software program is only as good as the person using it, and copying information on your computer won't help much if the disaster destroys your hardware.

With that in mind, you might prefer to stay low tech. The financial services firm TIAA-CREF has an outstanding free booklet called "Your Financial Organizer," available on the "publications" page of the company's website or by calling 877-518-9162.

Once your disaster file is ready, put it in a safe container, such as a fireproof box, and in a spot where you can grab it on a moment's notice.

Finally, make sure that a loved one knows about the disaster file, as it will be invaluable if anything happens to you away from the home and someone is required to take over your affairs.

None of these preparations will ensure that you avoid disaster, but they will help to mitigate the financial problems that can spring out of a tragedy.
