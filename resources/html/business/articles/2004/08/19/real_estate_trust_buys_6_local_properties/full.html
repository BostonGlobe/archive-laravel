BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Real estate trust buys 6 local properties

August 19, 2004

Equity One Inc., a real estate investment trust that owns shopping centers primarily in the South, agreed to buy six retail properties in the Boston area from Charlesbank Capital Partners LLC for about $120 million to expand into the Northeast. The centers are located in Cambridge, Medford, Quincy, Swampscott, West Roxbury, and Plymouth, with 390,979 total square feet, the North Miami Beach, Fla.-based company said. Five are anchored by Albertson's Inc.'s Shaw's Supermarkets, and one by a Whole Foods Market Inc. grocery store. Equity One's chief financial officer, Howard Sipzner, said the company is assuming as much as $12 million in debt from closely held Charlesbank, a Boston-based equity firm with more than $1 billion in assets. The transaction is expected to close by Sept. 30. Equity One is talking to real estate brokers in Boston to learn about other available locations. Equity One seeks properties anchored by supermarkets that are number one or number two in their markets, Sipzner said. (Bloomberg)

R.I. won't authorize casino referendum

Wembley PLC, a United Kingdom-based dog track owner with assets in the United States, said Rhode Island won't hold a referendum on allowing a casino development by Harrah's Entertainment Inc. that could draw away gambling revenue from the British company's venues. ''This is as a result of rulings by the Rhode Island courts precluding the referendum from being put to the ballot on constitutional grounds," Wembley said. Harrah's Entertainment had hoped a ''yes" vote in a referendum would overrule a veto by Governor Don Carcieri. Carcieri this year blocked a proposed vote on a plan by Harrah's to build a $600 million casino with local partners. Starwood Capital Group LLC and casino operator Kerzner International Ltd., operating as BLB Investors LLC, scrapped plans to buy Wembley for $536 million last month because of the ''political environment" surrounding Lincoln Park, a Rhode Island race track that generates 90 percent of Wembley's earnings. (Bloomberg)

Brokerage's bid wins Mass. bonds

Merrill Lynch & Co. bought $300 million of Massachusetts general obligation bonds that were sold by competitive bid, topping five other investment banks by agreeing to receive interest of 3.3657 percent. Jeffrey Stearns, Massachusetts' deputy treasurer for debt management, said ''the market rallied the last couple days, so that's been to our advantage. We certainly picked up some ground compared to where we thought we were a week ago when yields were 5 to 10 basis points higher." Merrill, the biggest securities firm by capital and the world's biggest brokerage, offered the highest price and lowest overall interest costs, beating an offer of 3.3661 percent by Citigroup Inc. Massachusetts, with the second-highest tax-supported debt per capita among US states after Connecticut, sold the bonds to pay for capital projects. The state's $21.4 billion of debt is rated Aa2, third highest of 10 investment grades, by Moody's Investors Service. Fitch Ratings and Standard & Poor's rate the state one lower at AA-. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

Ivax sells generic of firm's epilepsy drug

Ivax Corp. launched a generic version of Pfizer Inc.'s blockbuster epilepsy drug Neurontin as a years-old patent fight between the companies dragged on. If Pfizer wins its case, Ivax would likely have to pay back the profit it gained from launching its drug before the litigation was settled. Officials for Pfizer could not be reached for comment. CIBC World Markets analyst Elliot Wilbur called the launch timing ''surprising," noting the district court has handed down no decision on the patent at issue in the six-year-old case. Summary judgment motions were filed over 18 months ago and no trial date has been scheduled. (Dow Jones/AP)

Rates ease, mortgage applications rise

New applications for US home loans rose last week while refinancings surged, as 30-year mortgage interest rates fell to their lowest level in over four months, an industry group said. The Mortgage Bankers Association said its seasonally adjusted market index, a measure of mortgage activity, rose for the week ending August 13 by 11.9 percent to 689.4 from the previous week's 616.1. The Washington trade group's seasonally adjusted refinancing index jumped by 20.9 percent to 1,982.7 in the week ended August 13 from the previous week's 1,640.5. Thirty-year mortgage rates, excluding fees, averaged 5.75 percent, down 0.05 percentage point from the previous week and down 0.47 percentage point from a year ago. The 30-year rates fell to their lowest level since the week of April 2, when they also averaged 5.75 percent. (Reuters)

Freddie Mac may face civil action

Freddie Mac, the government-chartered mortgage company that restated earnings by $5 billion, said it may be the target of a civil action by the Securities and Exchange Commission. The SEC may pursue a permanent injunction and a possible fine, McLean, Va.-based Freddie Mac said in a statement distributed by PR Newswire. The SEC is investigating possible violations of a regulation that covers fraud and deceptive practices, the company said. It is the latest setback for chief executive Richard Syron, who was hired in December to overhaul the company's senior management and regain investor confidence. The restatement has already resulted in a $125 million fine for Freddie Mac, and Treasury Secretary John Snow has called for tighter regulation for the company. Freddie Mac received the so-called Wells notice from the SEC yesterday, a spokeswoman said. A Wells notice advises that SEC staff members have completed a review of the case and may recommend action by the full commission. An SEC spokesman declined to comment. (Bloomberg)

Compuware lambastes IBM court tactic

Compuware Corp. is accusing IBM of attempting to ''sandbag" it with new evidence just three months before the software company's piracy claim against the technology giant is to go to trial. IBM dismissed the accusation as ''a litigation tactic." Compuware said it filed a motion requesting a default judgment in the case after IBM produced source code that it previously had said it did not have. Compuware wants the court to exclude the new evidence from trial and order IBM to reimburse it for legal expenses that stemmed from IBM's earlier failure to produce the source code. Compuware sued IBM in March 2002, claiming IBM had copied its software products and was reselling them under the IBM nameplate. A second Compuware suit filed in July 2002 said IBM used its dominance in the market to undercut competitors on software prices. IBM has denied the charges and filed six counterclaims of patent infringement on its software by Compuware. (AP)

THE WORLD

Parmalat sues auditors for $10b

Italian dairy company Parmalat sued Grant Thornton International and Deloitte Touche Tohmatsu for $10 billion, saying the auditors overlooked fraud and looting that nearly led to the company's downfall. The lawsuit, filed in Cook County Circuit Court in Chicago by the insolvent conglomerate's new management, alleges the company has lost more than $10 billion because of Grant Thornton's and Deloitte's audits and other actions. Grant Thornton International said it ''does not accept that this is a legitimate action and will defend its position vigorously." Deloitte's branch in Italy issued a statement saying it had not seen the lawsuit, which it nevertheless called ''unjustified." ''It was the actions of Deloitte Italy which led to the fraud being uncovered," Deloitte said. (AP)
