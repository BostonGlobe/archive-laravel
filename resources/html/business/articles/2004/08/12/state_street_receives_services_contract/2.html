August 12, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Boston Options Exchange cites savings

The Boston Options Exchange, the newest US market for stock options, said it saved traders a cumulative $1.4 million in its first six months with an electronic auction not available at competitors. Options give the bearer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset by a set date at a certain price. Investors use stock options to guard against or bet on changes in the price of companies' shares. The auction ensures that customers trading options contracts get a price that's at least a penny better than what's available at other markets. More than 10 percent of the 6.8 million contracts the exchange has handled since its Feb. 6 start went through the auction, the market said. BOX developed the auction to make it easier for brokers to take the opposite side of a trade with their own clients. It requires brokers doing so to guarantee a price that's 1 cent better than at competing exchanges, and to allow other traders to bid for the business. The auction lasts three seconds. (Bloomberg)

Art Technology buys Seattle company

Art Technology Group Inc., which makes software that helps companies do business on the Internet, agreed to buy rival Primus Knowledge Solutions Inc. for as much as $33 million in stock. Art Technology will exchange 1.3 to 1.4 shares of its common stock for each Primus share, Cambridge-based ATG said. Based on Tuesday's closing price, the company values the acquisition at $30 million to $33 million. Art Technology plans to cut as much as $15 million in annual costs by closing facilities and eliminating jobs, spokesman Cliff Conneighton said. He said he didn't know how many jobs would be lost. Art Technology employs 324 workers and Seattle-based Primus has 155. The acquisition may close in the fourth quarter and is expected to boost 2005 earnings. (Bloomberg)

Charges stand against Stewart witness

A federal judge declined to throw out perjury charges against the ink expert who testified at the Martha Stewart trial about a stock worksheet that was used as evidence. Martha Stewart and former stockbroker Peter Bacanovic, sentenced to five months in prison apiece for lying about a stock sale by the celebrity homemaker, used the ink expert's alleged lies to argue unsuccessfully for new trials. Larry Stewart, no relation to Martha Stewart, is accused of lying repeatedly on the witness stand, mainly by exaggerating his role in ink-analysis testing of the worksheet. His lawyers say the Secret Service ink expert stands by his testimony, and argued that his alleged perjury was not "material" to the jury's consideration of the case. Larry Stewart remains on "indefinite suspension" from the Secret Service, said his lawyer, Judith Wheat. The ruling paves the way for the case to go to trial Sept. 20. (AP)

Armed forces get digital music service

Napster has struck an agreement with the government to offer its online music download service to members of the military and their families at a reduced price. As part of the arrangement with the Army and Air Force Exchange Service, Napster agreed to discount the price of its song downloads and subscription service by 10 percent, a Napster spokesman said. Several AAFES websites that cater to military and other government officials stationed overseas have begun offering the Napster service. About 11 million current and retired members of the military, as well as their families, are eligible to access the service, the company said. (AP)

Motorola may owe $500m in back taxes

Motorola Inc. said the IRS claims the cellphone maker may owe $500 million in additional tax liability for the years 1996 through 2000. The IRS completed a field examination in June of the company's tax returns for 1996 through 2000, and notified Motorola of certain proposed adjustments, primarily related to transfer pricing, according to Motorola's second-quarter report filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission. Transfer pricing refers to prices at which individual units within a company sell goods or services to one another. Motorola said it disagrees with these proposed adjustments and intends to dispute the matter through applicable IRS and judicial procedures. The transfer-pricing adjustments requested by the IRS would alter the way the company recorded profits and taxes on the sale of products worldwide, said a company spokeswoman. Motorola said the IRS may make similar claims for years subsequent to 2000 in future audits. (Dow Jones/AP)
