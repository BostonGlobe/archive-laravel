The big picture

High-tech billboards push the envelope in moving their message

By Chris Reidy, Globe Staff  |  May 3, 2004

Forget old-fashioned ideas about highway billboards. In the Burma Shave era when cars had fins and Elvis was king, an ad was painted on a billboard's face or pasted up like wallpaper. Not anymore. Some of today's newest billboards are essentially giant $1 million TV screens. Instead of a single ad that stays up for two months, these high-tech billboards rotate a series of still images much like a slide show. (Full-motion video is mostly a freeway no-no. It's too distracting to drivers, regulators say.)

According to an industry trade group, the billboard has been around since pharaohs used obelisks to give ancient Egyptians the high sign. But to ensure that it remains a marquee medium for another millennium, one of the most primitive forms of advertising is exploring innovative ways to add value by using wireless communications, flat-screen TVs, radio scanners, global positioning satellites, and car computers and toll-booth transponders.

"We are definitely on the cusp of several technical advances," said Drew Hoffman, president of Clear Channel Outdoor Massachusetts.

A billboard that can change ads every 10 seconds is only part of the story. One possibility is a billboard connected to a nearby sensor that detects what radio station a motorist is listening to. Say a motorist is tuned in to Willie Nelson. The sensor captures enough emissions to identify the radio station. Knowing that the station's listeners are generally fond of pickup trucks, the computer then automatically alerts the billboard to display an ad for a Chevy Silverado.

Something like that scenario is already happening in California, where three companies have joined forces to push the envelope of billboard technology. Smart Sign Media is responsible for the electronic billboards. Mobiltrak has developed the sensors that can pick up radio emissions from passing cars. And the Media Audit supplies the radio-station demographics.

Technology isn't the issue here; it's already available. The challenge is wrapping it around a business model that delivers advertising messages with such added frequency and impact that billboard companies can generate more revenues from their signs.

In New York City, Clear Channel Outdoor just rolled out a network of about 80 mini-billboards positioned at Manhattan subway entrances. Costing $40,000 apiece, these 30-inch-by-60-inch screens have are hooked up to a computer in Las Vegas. That computer can program all screens to show the same ad at the same time or show different ads in different neighborhoods. Typically, ads change every 10 seconds, and they can be either a still image or a full-range video.

Such flexibility benefits advertisers in many ways. On electronic highway billboards in California, McDonald's can run ads for its breakfast menu in the morning and ads for the Big Mac at lunch time, said general manager Tom Langeland of Smart Sign Media.

What's more, the billboards are linked to a password-protected website that enables a company to change ads in real time. That feature has been popular with radio talk-show hosts, Langeland said, because the text in the billboard ads can be continually updated to reflect the topic of the hour. A talk show producer can access the billboard's website, and update the text to read, for example, "Join Rush now for a discussion of gay marriage."

Said Langeland: "We're basically taking the billboard and turning it into a bulletin board."

Bulletin-board-like signs could be coming some day to some of Boston's bus shelters. Those shelters are provided to the city by Wall USA Inc. In return, Wall USA has the right to sell advertising on them.

According to Wall USA president Martin McDonough, the MBTA is testing satellite triangulation software that tracks a bus's progress. The hope is that the shelters will have an electronic sign that can then alert commuters, "The No. 1 bus will arrive in seven minutes."

McDonough envisions using similar technology to replace one or more of the shelter's ad posters with an electronic sign that can display changing ads. As he sees it, shelters might have two electronic signs, one for commuter information, the other for ads.

This ability to change ads every 10 seconds has the potential to change the strategy of billboard advertising. In the past, advertisers were stuck with the same message for at least a month and that pretty much limited them to broad brand messages.

Even today, when many billboard ads are 100-pound vinyl printouts made by a special computer, it takes about an hour for a four-person crew to replace one ad with another. (These vinyl printout are much like a fitted bed sheet that is attached to the billboard's frame.) But a billboard that can update ads in real time gives billboard advertisers options they didn't have before. For example, retailers can now "advertise on price and make limited time offers," said Stefanie Geddis, a vice president at Boston ad agency Hill Holliday Connors Cosmopulos.

One possibility that Mobiltrak has toyed with was placing one of its radio-station sensors at the entrance of a shopping mall's parking lot. Around the lot would be deployed 48-inch video screens. According to managing partner James Green, a shopper would pull into the lot. The sensor would detect the radio station the shopper was listening to. And the video screens would display ads that matched the ad airing on the radio station. The result? A sales pitch delivered with a memorable one-two punch.

Other firms are using cheaper technologies to make ads memorable. For added dramatic effect, some billboards and electronic signs are incorporating scrolling messages much like the scoreboard at a baseball park; other billboards are using lenticular screens that trick the eye into giving motion to still images.

Just as important as using technology to update billboard content is using technology to determine who the billboard ads are reaching. One advantage that newspapers and TV and radio stations have had over billboards is that they could give advertisers precise data on the size and demographic makeup of an audience tuned in to certain shows or reading certain parts of the paper. Armed with such data, a diet company might buy ad time on "Ally McBeal" reruns, or a hair-weave firm might insist that its newspaper ad appear in the sports section. Until recently, the billboard industry didn't have similar capabilities. Now some media ratings services are hiring groups of consumers to wear GPS tracking devices so billboard companies can show advertisers not only how many consumers are seeing their signs but also more precise information about who those consumers are.

For much the same reason, the Media Audit is working with the Mobiltrak sensing devices -- the ones that can tell which radio stations motorists are listening to. By working with the sensors, the Media Audit can create a demographic profile for a single billboard location, said vice president Phil Beswick. The next step is to develop a timeline. The timeline might note that between 10 a.m. and noon, many motorists driving by this location listen to classical music. The billboard might then be programmed to show ads for Subaru and American Express, two brands popular with fans of Beethoven and Mozart, Beswick said.

Creating demographic profiles and timelines for specific billboard sites has big implications for the industry. As devices such as the remote-control and TiVo enable viewers to bypass TV ads, the $5.5 billion outdoor industry can argue that its innovative billboards are truly an "unavoidable medium" that deserve a bigger share of the estimated $128.3 billion that advertisers spent to buy US media for their ads last year.

Looking ahead, the outdoor industry sees potential in on-board computers that most new cars are likely to be equipped with by the end of the decade. An on-board computer can notify an emergency road crew in the event of a highway breakdown. In theory, it could also interact with a billboard displaying an ad for Dunkin' Donuts. If they wanted to, motorists could wirelessly download directions from the billboard to the nearest Dunkin' location. Transponders that let motorists pay highway tolls electronically offer similar potential. As a consumer rolls through a toll booth, a sign could flash an ad message targeted to that individual. Such possibilities raise privacy concerns and talk about Big Brother. Not surprisingly, transponder companies would rather discuss how Texans can now use toll-booth transponders to pay for airport parking or lunch at some McDonald's drive-through windows.

Maybe advertising scenarios involving on-board computers and transponders will be feasible some day. But one thing is certain now: Americans are spending more time in their cars every year, noted Paul Meyer, chief executive of Clear Channel Outdoor.

"As the mileage of the average commute increases and the average speed during rush hour decreases," he said, "candidly, that only makes our signs more valuable to advertisers."
