Lower risk seen in drug 'rescue'

Firms look to find gold in resurrecting existing treatments

By Scott Kirsner, Globe Correspondent  |  May 3, 2004

A can of Coke seems like an improbable ally in the campaign to bring a once-abandoned cancer drug to the market. But caffeinated beverages, combined with some fresh insights from Cambridge-based Xanthus Life Sciences, could eventually help make available a new treatment for breast cancer and prostate cancer.

Xanthus is just one of a cluster of local companies trying to resurrect drugs that never made it to pharmacy shelves or to discover new uses for existing products. Some call it "dumpster diving," or, more politely, drug rescue. Rather than trying to develop a new substance from scratch -- and then test to see whether it's harmful or helpful to humans -- these companies are prospecting for promising chemical compounds that were developed by someone else and are already known to be at least safe for ingestion by humans.

The dumpster-diving approach allows small life sciences companies to reduce their risk of failure and get their products into clinical trials more quickly -- both of which make their backers happy. But will dumpster diving lead to a decrease in drug development that begins with a blank sheet or paper or an empty test tube?

Back, for a moment, to that ice cold Coke. Xanthus is developing a system for personalized dosing -- figuring out the optimal amount of a drug for a given patient using a new type of test. The first drug the company is working with, Xanafide, was developed and first given to humans in the 1990s. But the drug's initial developer, Knoll Pharmaceuticals (now part of Abbott Labs) ran into problems: Some of the participants in its clinical trials experienced an alarming drop in white blood cells.

Despite the side effect, the drug seemed to successfully combat breast and prostate cancers in some patients. But it was hard for Knoll to find a dose that was high enough to be effective without triggering the side effect. So the company shelved the drug after several expensive trials, which involved more than 1,000 patients.

Xanthus thinks it has solved the dosage problem. If a patient downs a Coke, a cup of coffee, or even a can of Red Bull, and then submits to urinalysis four hours later, an oncologist can determine how quickly the patient will metabolize Xanafide, the drug. The caffeine in the beverage serves as a surrogate for Xanafide; the company calls it a "metabolic bar code." (Xanthus also slightly modified the structure of the drug itself, mostly so it could secure its own patent.) A clinical trial in breast cancer is already underway, with a trial for prostate cancer planned later this year.

"Companies and investors are looking for lower-risk strategies -- ways to get into the clinic with a reduced risk of fall-out, or not delivering a product," says Richard Dean, who was named Xanthus's chief executive earlier this year. The company raised $30 million in venture capital last December.

And Xanthus isn't alone in trying to avoid the risks of designing a drug from scratch. Dynogen Pharmaceuticals of Boston thinks that a drug that was once developed in Japan for depression and Alzheimer's disease, but never approved for sale, may help patients who suffer from irritable bowel syndrome. CombinatoRx is trying to mix-and-match drugs that are already known to be safe for consumption, to see if they might effectively combat diseases in combination that they don't address on their own. Worcester-based Hypnion examines drugs that list sleepiness as a side-effect, to explore whether they might be re-engineered expressly to cause sleepiness in red-eyed insomnia sufferers.

There are several forces behind dumpster diving. If a drug, for example, fails to show enough promise during clinical trials, pharmaceutical companies often drop it cold, rather than trying to refine it or study whether it works better against a different disease.

"Very few companies feel that they can afford to take a drug that has failed and put more money behind it," says Roger Longman, managing partner of the publishing firm Windhover Information in Norwalk, Conn.

In a recent research report, Longman wrote that "finding unexploited potential" in existing drugs was "the industry's most important new product strategy."

Another factor is the availability of vast databases of drugs already in existence. Think of it as Google for chemists.

"You used to have to spend $1 million to find out what Novartis was doing and dig through lots of microfiche," says Alfred Ajami, chief scientific officer at Xanthus. "Now, I do a couple of key clicks, and I can look at any molecule -- the structure, patents, abstracts of meetings. Everything is in one place."

That allows Xanthus and other companies to look for known drugs that might have unknown possibilities.

Finally, the rising cost of developing a new drug, combined with the impatience of venture capitalists, seems to be prompting more companies to consider dumpster diving for a drug that they may be able to bring to market quickly.

"Developing a new chemical entity takes a long time and costs a lot," says Nicholas Galakatos, a venture capitalist with MPM Capital in Boston who is also the chairman of Hypnion and Lexington-based Transform Pharmaceuticals. "You're talking about 12 years and $1.25 billion on average."

For that reason, it's pretty challenging to start a company to design drugs from scratch; that's perceived as the domain of academia and the vast R&D groups at major pharmaceutical companies. But that has been true for a while.

"For years, people in the venture community have looked skeptically at people who've tried to do a small molecule drug from scratch," says Noubar Afeyan, senior managing director of Flagship Ventures, which funded CombinatoRx and Hypnion. (Small molecule drugs can be thought of as traditional pills; large molecule drugs, like insulin, must be injected.) "Vertex has been trying to do that, and it has been a tough, tough road."

Flagship has 21 life science companies in its portfolio, and Afeyan says a growing number of them are considering making dumpster diving or drug rescue part of their strategy.

There's plenty of history of drugs being applied in new ways, and moving from one company to another. Viagra was originally developed by Pfizer to treat angina; arousal was only a side effect. And thalidomide, infamously used to treat morning sickness, is now being used to treat multiple myeloma, a form of cancer. It's rare, though, for one company to find a gold nugget in another company's garbage.

And there are potential potholes for would-be dumpster divers: hassles licensing an abandoned drug from a pharmaceutical company; inadequate patent protection; and the possibility that big pharma may eventually wake up and start diving in its own dumpsters.

Thomas Klein, cofounder of Bionaut Pharmaceuticals, believes that eventually the dumpsters will be pretty picked through.

"The window of opportunity for dumpster diving will have been exhausted in less than 10 years, and maybe less than five," Klein says. "It'll be hard to make money doing it."

Bionaut is experimenting with a combination of drugs to treat cancer that were originally developed for other purposes.

For now, though, Klein says, "if you start with [drugs] where a lot is already known about them, that lets you start on square five or square six, instead of at square one."

To the rescue

A number of companies are taking a new look at some old drugs, using a variety of methods to unlock previously hidden capabilities. Among the techniques these "dumpster divers" use are:

More personalized dosing. Xanthus Life Sciences is currently testing a drug that was originally developed, then shelved, by Knoll Pharmaceuticals. Xanthus is hoping that by more precisely testing the optimal dose of a drug, Xanafide, it will be effective in the treatment of breast and prostate cancers.

Creating new combinations of existing drugs. CombinatoRx is mixing and matching drugs to see if the new combinations will yield capabilities that are not present in the individual formulations.

Test an existing drug on entirely new diseases. Dynogen Pharmaceuticals is hoping that a drug that was once developed in Japan to treat depression and Alzheimer's disease will help patients who suffer from irritable bowel syndrome.

Change the composition slightly to make an existing side effect the main effect. Hypnion is studying drugs that list sleepiness as a side effect, to explore whether they might be reengineered expressly to make sleepiness the main effect.
