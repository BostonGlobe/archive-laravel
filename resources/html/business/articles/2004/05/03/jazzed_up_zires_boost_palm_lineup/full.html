Jazzed-up Zires boost Palm lineup

May 3, 2004

PalmOne has two new entries in its affordable line of Zire hand-held computers: a color-screen version of its low-end unit, the Zire 31, and an upgrade to its higher-end Zire 72, with beefed-up multimedia and Bluetooth networking.

The company is trying to keep reeling in first-time buyers and budget-conscious types who've snapped up more than 3 million Zires in the last 18 months, including a $99 unit sans color. Seventy percent of folks who bought the first crop of Zires are first-time PDA owners.

While hand-held sales have been slumping as buyers turn to "smartphones" that offer PDA and multimedia features, strong sales of the cheap Zires have helped keep palmOne at the top of the heap in the flagging market.

Palm Inc., famous for putting hand-helds on the map with the Palm Pilot, became palmOne when it spun off PalmSource, the maker of the Palm OS, and acquired rival Handspring last year. The company offers three product lines: the Zire hand-helds, aimed at a general audience, and the higher-end Tungsten hand-helds and Treo smartphones, which are geared toward business and power users.

The new entry-level color Zire 31 ($149) and the Zire 72 ($299) improve on previous models with more memory, faster processors, a focus on multimedia, and updated software. The 72 replaces the Zire 71. The Zire 21 will continue to be sold for $99.

Hand-helds aren't just PDAs anymore. Like other devices they've become convergence gadgets that can take snapshots and play digital music as well as manage your calendar, contacts, and to-do list.

In addition to the color screen, which palmOne is claiming as the first in its price range, the Zire 31 comes with 16MB of memory (double the Zire 21), an expansion slot that accepts MMC, SD, and SDIO expansion cards, a 200 Mhz Intel ARM-based processor, and a USB HotSync cable. The front of the unit now sports a five-way navigator button for one-handed operation, as well as quick launch calendar and contact buttons. On the software side, the unit runs Palm OS 5.2.8 and programs for viewing photos, playing MP3s, and, of course, handling PDA-type chores.

At the other end of the spectrum is the Zire 72. PalmOne is hoping young professionals on a budget will be drawn to the unit's multimedia and productivity applications, not to mention the unit's snazzy blue metallic case and funky accessories like a '60s-look case.

Looks aside, the 72 has a built-in 1.2-megapixel digital camera with 2x zoom, and it will record video and sound, a first for palmOne PDAs. The camera lens, a microphone, and speaker are on the back of the device. The unit's bright 320 x 320 high-resolution color screen serves as the viewfinder.

I found it much easier to frame up a shot on this size screen than squinting at a tiny cellphone display. You can shoot up to a maximum resolution of 1280 x 960. While the camera has a low-light function, it won't save you in extreme low-light situations, and there's no flash. The included photo management software is easy to use, but basic. You'll find the usual lineup of digital photo management features including album view, e-mail sharing, and slideshows. I liked the ability to zoom in and pan around a photo.

Capturing video was a snap, but you'll need an expansion card to use this feature. Mac users should be forewarned that the unit will capture but not play back video. The company advises you to look for third-party software to handle playback. The 2x zoom works for video as well.

For digital music, the 72 comes with the RealOne Player, and you'll need an expansion card to use it. There's also a voice recorder, DataViz Documents To Go for editing Word and Excel files, and updated Personal Information Management software, as wells as applications to wrangle e-mail, and PDF files. You can also send short text and multimedia messages using the palmOne Messages application.

In the nice-touch department, you can now add a photo to your Outlook contact entries and as a background in calendar "agenda" view or on the Home screen.

The Zire 72 also runs Palm OS 5.2.8, and it comes with 32 MB of memory, an expansion slot for adding more storage, and a 312 Mhz Intel PXA270 processor. You sync the unit via the included USB cable. In addition to the five-way navigation button, you get a couple of extra quick launch application buttons that aren't on the Zire 31: calendar, contacts, camera, and MP3 player.

While it's standard on palmOne's higher-end units, the 72 is the first in the Zire line to include Bluetooth for wireless applications.

The Zire 31 and the Zire 72 certainly offer more bang for the buck than their predecessors, raising the bar at the low end of the palmOne line. The 72 is fun, functional, and easy to use, but I kept thinking it would be really great if it was a cellphone, too. That's one reason it may not fly with the young professional crowd it's gunning for. Sales of smartphones are predicted to outsell hand-helds for the first time this year.

If you don't care about having it all in one package, the beefed-up Zires are a great deal.
