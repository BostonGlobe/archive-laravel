Bankruptcy as strategy

As the stigma fades, more troubled firms make use of Chap. 11 to deal with debt

By Robert Weisman, Globe Staff  |  May 18, 2004

For Boston developer Donald J. Chiofaro, it's a strategy to lower interest payments and retain his ownership and management of the International Place office towers in the city's Financial District.

Cable, phone, and Internet provider RCN Corp. has embraced it as a tactic to negotiate more favorable terms with its bondholders.

US Airways Group Inc. is weighing it as a tool to reduce labor costs in the face of competition from discount carriers like JetBlue Airways.

None of these parties is technically broke, and all insist that their businesses continue to hold long-term value. But facing cash crunches that could escalate into financial crises, all are gravitating to the second-chance elixir of American business: bankruptcy.

While the number of US business bankruptcy filings dipped to 35,037 last year from a recent peak of 40,099 in 2001, a spotty recovery and upward pressure on interest rates may be planting the seeds for a new crop of bankruptcies -- especially in lagging sectors, like airlines or telecommunications, or those especially sensitive to interest rates, like commercial real estate.

"We've been in a favorable interest-rate environment, so companies that have had debt have been able to refinance their loans," said Michael P. Richman, senior bankruptcy partner at the law firm Mayer, Brown, Rowe & Maw LLP in New York and president of the American Bankruptcy Institute, a professional association. "There's no question that rising interest rates are starting to have an impact on companies that are right on the edge now in a variety of industries."

Indeed, even as the economy picks up, Chiofaro, RCN, and US Airways are among the principals filing, or threatening to file, for Chapter 11 bankruptcy-court protection, a practice that has emerged as a popular strategy for financially squeezed companies seeking to hang on by restructuring debt, jettisoning assets, or taking on partners.

"Much of the stigma that used to go along with a Chapter 11 filing has disappeared," said bankruptcy specialist Stuart Gilson, professor of business administration at Harvard Business School. "So it really comes down to a decision on the costs and benefits."

Increasingly, companies have been using US Bankruptcy Court as a platform for selling a business saddled with debt. Many potential buyers now insist on a Chapter 11 filing to avoid "successor liability" by having debts disclosed and creditor disputes resolved in court before they take possession of a business. This often involves a "cramdown," when a bankruptcy judge forces different classes of creditors to accept a restructuring of their debt.

"It's a more frequent outcome, and always an option to consider," said Howard Brod Brownstein, principal at turnaround management firm NachmanHaysBrownstein Inc. in Philadelphia.

Companies still don't turn to Chapter 11 lightly.

"It's always a sign of a company in bad shape, and it makes customers nervous," said Michael A. Cusumano, management professor at MIT's Sloan School. "But if the alternative is oblivion, it's something worth trying."

And many companies are giving it a try. Managers who have brought their companies through a bankruptcy process, preserving value, can even gain cachet these days as turnaround specialists.

"Having it on your resum shows you know how to do it," Brownstein noted. "You don't faint at the sight of blood. It might make you in demand."

In fact, the US bankruptcy system is designed to protect much of the value of a business, save jobs, encourage entrepreneurial risk, and, if possible, enable a company to reorganize and emerge as a viable, if not stronger, concern. This is in stark contrast to many other countries that are predisposed to punish debtors and make creditors whole.

"It's a powerful tool to restructure a business," said Daniel J. Carragher, partner in the Boston office of the Day, Berry & Howard law firm and a specialist in Chapter 11 bankruptcy cases. "And if a company decides they can go into a proceeding and can reemerge, without losing their base of customers, they'll frequently do it."

But while bankruptcy professionals often cite a laundry list of companies that have rebounded from bankruptcy -- from Continental Airlines to Filene's Basement and, most recently, Malden Mills Industries Inc. -- Carragher said they are the high-profile exceptions and their success is often achieved largely through a post-bankruptcy sale.

"It's difficult to come out the other end as a successful company," he said.

Still, for struggling businesses, there can be a number of benefits to seeking bankruptcy protection: Companies in Chapter 11 don't have to pay or accrue interest on debt, enabling them to use cash to run their businesses. They can review their portfolios of leases and terminate some of them. They have an expedited process for selling assets. And they have access to a category of loans, called debtor in possession financing, available only to companies in Chapter 11.

Even where there is no clear reorganization or exit strategy going in, a Chapter 11 filing can buy time for a distressed business.

"It's an option available to you," Chiofaro said. "You have to overcome the fact that it's not your first, second, or third choice. But if you have assets worth preserving, it may be something you have to do."

On the downside, bankruptcy filings are seldom popular with shareholders, customers, tenants, or debt holders. They also can create public relations nightmares for service companies, such as retailers or airlines, that depend on the good will of consumers. Filings can be risky, difficult, expensive, and unpredictable, specialists say.

Definitive data on the outcome of bankruptcies are hard to come by, partly because so many companies initially emerge from Chapter 11 reorganized but later stumble and slide back into Chapter 11 (a scenario sometimes disparaged as "Chapter 22"), sell their assets, or file for liquidation under Chapter 7 of the US Bankruptcy Code. Even among the "success stories," many succeed under new ownership. Boston shoppers still visit Filene's Basement, for example, but the reorganized company is owned by Value City Department Stores Inc. of Columbus, Ohio.

In general, the odds of a positive Chapter 11 outcome, especially for existing management, increase with the size of the company. Larger companies are more likely to have the cash and resources to bring a reorganization to fruition, while smaller companies are apt to sell the business or many of its assets. Regardless of company size, though, it is almost always a last resort.

"It's a strategy for a company when they get into cash-flow constraints," Cusumano said. "You don't want to get to the point where the utilities come in and turn off your gas or your electricity."
