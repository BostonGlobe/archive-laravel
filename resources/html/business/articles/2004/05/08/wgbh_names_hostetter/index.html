BUSINESS IN BRIEF

WGBH names Hostetter

May 8, 2004

THE REGION

Amos B. Hostetter Jr., cofounder and former chief executive of Continental Cablevision, now part of Comcast Corp., has been named chairman of the board of trustees of Boston's WGBH public television and radio system. Hostetter, chairman of Pilot House Ventures and a leading Boston philanthropist, succeeds the late Charles J. Beard as chairman of the flagship US public broadcasting station. (Peter J. Howe)

Harvard Bioscience drops

Shares of Harvard Bioscience Inc., which makes drug-development tools, plunged 45 percent after the company reported a first-quarter loss of $51,000 because of poor sales in its gene-research unit. The company, which broke even on a per-share basis, had a net income of $776,000, or 3 cents a share, a year earlier. Sales rose 14 percent to $22.2 million from $19.5 million, the Holliston-based company said. Shares fell $3.85 to $4.70. (Bloomberg)

R.I. Blue Cross CEO to quit

Blue Cross & Blue Shield of Rhode Island, the state's largest health insurer, said its president and chief executive will resign next week, as the company works to counteract claims of poor management. Critics say Ronald Battista's resignation is a step forward and contend more needs to be done to restore public confidence in the nonprofit, which has been criticized for corporate perks, low payments to healthcare providers, and premiums that rose 30 percent in the past two years. The board elected James Purcell, executive vice president and chief operating officer, to serve as acting president during the search for a replacement. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

Crude oil hits $40 a barrel

With the peak summer driving season just around the corner, the price of oil climbed to $40 a barrel, and analysts said motorists should brace for more expensive gasoline. West Coast retail prices for regular gasoline were already averaging $2.08 per gallon at the start of the week, according to the Department of Energy. Oil prices shot up nearly 7 percent in the past week. Oil for June delivery rose as high as $40 on the New York Mercantile Exchange before settling at $39.93 -- the highest level in more than 13 years. (AP)

US Air mulls bankruptcy

US Airways Group Inc., the seventh-largest US airline, might seek bankruptcy protection for the second time in two years unless costs are reduced to compete with low-fare carriers. US Airways will consider ''asset sales or a judicial restructuring" if it can't achieve costs similar to JetBlue Airways and America West the airline said in a filing at the Securities and Exchange Commission. US Airways is briefing employees this week on a new strategy and plans to seek additional pay and benefit concessions in the next few months. (Bloomberg)

Skilling under court order

A federal judge in Houston ordered former Enron chief Jeffrey Skilling to stop drinking and put him on a curfew in response to a drunken scuffle outside a New York City bar last month. Prosecutors wanted US Magistrate Frances Stacy to increase Skilling's bond from $5 million to $7 million, prohibit travel outside of Texas, and impose a midnight curfew. Stacy ordered the curfew but left details to be worked out with court officials. The judge also ordered Skilling to undergo alcohol abuse treatment, which his lawyer said he began two weeks ago. (AP)

Prudential sees sanctions

Prudential Financial Inc. said some of the federal and state investigations of the company's mutual fund and variable annuities trading practices are ''likely" to result in regulatory action. In its first-quarter report, Prudential said it believes that ''certain of the matters under investigation are likely to lead to proceedings." The filing didn't identify the regulatory bodies or subject matters that it thinks are likely to result in proceedings. Prudential didn't say whether it has received a so-called Wells notice from the SEC. A Prudential spokesman wasn't available for comment. (Dow Jones)
