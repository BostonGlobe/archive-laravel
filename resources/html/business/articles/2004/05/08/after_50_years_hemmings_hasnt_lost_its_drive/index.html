After 50 years, Hemmings hasn't lost its drive

By Bill Griffith, Globe Staff  |  May 8, 2004

BENNINGTON, Vt. -- Hemmings Motor News, the self-proclaimed ''bible of the collector car hobby since 1954," is a funky publication, headquartered in a funky converted industrial building (the old Sibley Machine Shop) in this funky city.

Each month, this book-size, catalog-style publication of 500 to 800 pages goes out to 210,000 loyal subscribers, and another 50,000 copies are sold on newsstands nationwide for $5.99 each.

The May issue lists more than 6,500 cars and trucks for sale and another 8,500 ads for parts and services.

Not bad for an advertising flyer founded by Ernest Hemmings in Quincy, Ill. Hemmings knew he was on to something when he sold 500 copies of his four-page inaugural issue at 50 cents apiece.

The late Terry Ehrich recognized its potential when he assembled a group of investors to buy the publication and move it to Bennington in the late 1960s. For 30 years, Hemmings Motor News quietly flourished, becoming a welcome presence at all big auto events and a major source for parts, services, information, and vehicles for the auto hobbyist. Its content remained 99 percent advertising with a few letters and photos submitted by subscribers.

If you wanted stories, you could subscribe to the company's Special Interest Autos, a bimonthly magazine launched in 1970, which just celebrated its 201st -- and final -- issue.

Now, as part of American City Business Journal's media holdings, Hemmings continues to expand.

Here's what the folks in Bennington have been up to of late:

For the last year, the flagship Hemmings Motor News has contained 24 to 32 pages of editorial content each month, covering auctions, collectibles, technical Q & As, helpful tips in the never-ending search for parts, and ''extra special" special-interest vehicles.

Hemmings Muscle Machines, a slick monthly dedicated to American performance cars from the 1950s to the present, made its debut in 2003. It passed the 100,000-subscriber milestone with its eighth issue and has done well on the newsstands. This May's issue features a story on a project to race 1960 Thunderbirds on the NASCAR circuit with photos and a drive of a wonderfully preserved and street-worthy survivor.

Why the new magazine? The way the folks at Hemmings saw the collector car hobby going, ''baby boomers now want the cars that they remember from their youth, cars they dreamed about when they were kids," said Richard Lentinello, editor in chief of the magazine. Its publisher is Jim Menneto.

To promote the magazine and interact with readers, Hemmings is sponsoring Muscle Palooza, a combination car show and vintage muscle car drag race on May 30 at Lebanon Valley Dragway, located on Route 20 in New York, just west of Pittsfield, Mass. ''Just a short drive from the end of the Mass. Pike," said Lentinello.

A relaunch of Special Interest Autos as Hemmings Classic Car magazine is scheduled for a September issue slated to come off the presses in August.

''We're going to focus on American cars from 1900 to the 1980s," said Lentinello. ''Special Interest Autos was a great publication, but it had a lot of black-and-white photos and was bimonthly. This will be a full-size, full-color monthly magazine on high-quality paper."

The home offices. Did we say Hemmings Motor News was a funky operation? Well, compare it to the Globe. At the newspaper's plant on Dorchester's Morrissey Boulevard, delivery trucks are parked and serviced in the company's garage. It works a little differently at Hemmings. There, the company's constantly changing fleet of 25 to 30 collector cars is on display in a combination garage/museum. It is located underneath the converted industrial building that houses the 100-plus employees who work in the editorial offices.

The gas station out front does sell gas, but the repair bays have been converted to sell auto memorabilia of all sorts. Fortunately, the shelves are commercial grade, because the variety of auto-related books contains reading for most any auto enthusiast.

The Hemmings auto collection is an eclectic display of 40 vehicles, though heavy on the sedan-delivery trucks that the magazine's owners have restored and used for promotional purposes over the past 35 years. Recent editions include a Shelby Mustang and a 1960 Cadillac DeVille.

Visitors to Bennington don't have much trouble finding Hemmings's 216 Main St. location. After all, the fiberglass front end of a 1955 Chevy is mounted over the gas pumps, and an early-'40s Dodge truck nose appears to be plowing through the side of the main building. And Hemmings regularly draws 125 cars to its every-other-Tuesday Cruise Nights. This summer's schedule begins June 1.

As part of its 50th-anniversary celebration, Hemmings is giving away an all-original 1954 Pontiac Chieftain (note the 50-year-old symmetry). Today, it sits on the lot in front of the Company Store, exposed to the elements but ready for a new owner to turn the key and drive it off. The winner will be drawn Oct. 8 at the Hemmings booth during a Hershey, Pa., fall swap meet.

Why a Pontiac? It's considered neutral ground among the Ford-Chevy-Mopar brand-loyalists of the hobby.

And, if you don't win, you can always wait for the next issue and find one of your own to buy.
