Insurers post prices for medical care

By Liz Kowalczyk, Globe Staff  |  May 29, 2004

Insurers are trying a new tactic to educate patients about medical costs and in some cases make them think twice about undergoing expensive procedures: posting prices.

Harvard Pilgrim Health Care this month became the latest insurer to put on its website what it pays doctors and hospitals for 49 outpatient tests and procedures, from as much as $278 for an echocardiogram, a heart rhythm test, to between $723 and $1,032 for a diagnostic colonoscopy. Tufts Health Plan allows members to look up the cost of 30 procedures, while Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Massachusetts began posting prices last year and will expand its offerings this summer.

‘‘We’re in a world where everything is behind the curtain,’’ said the Harvard Pilgrim chief executive, Charles D. Baker. ‘‘The doctor says you can have two more physical therapy visits, or we can just do an MRI. That’s presented as the same. But the PT visits cost $30 a piece, and the MRI costs $800. They’re not the same at all.’’

Posting prices is part of a movement insurers and employers call ‘‘consumer-driven healthcare.’’ Such health plans offer ﬁnancial incentives to members to watch how much they spend on medical care. The plans typically include high deductibles — often $1,000 or $2,000 — so patients generally must spend their own money before most insurance coverage kicks in.

Many companies deposit a lump sum into an account that employees can draw from to cover part of their deductible. And the plans charge signiﬁcantly lower monthly premiums than regular HMOs, which beneﬁts the employer and the employee. The employee, however, can run into higher out-of-pocket costs if he gets sick and uses a lot of medical services.

Paul Ginsburg, president of the Center for Studying Health System Change, a nonproﬁt research organization in Washington, D.C., said that giving consumers more ﬁnancial choice and responsibility has dangers. ‘‘Incentives can become barriers if you don’t have much money or if you have substantial medical needs,’’ he said. But, he added, many patients didn’t like traditional managed care, either, because it was too restrictive and offered too few choices.

Membership in high-deductible plans is growing — Harvard Pilgrim’s Best Buy now has 42,000 enrollees — meaning more consumers are deciding which medical services to buy with their deductibles. And some health care analysts believe ‘‘consumer-driven’’ plans could end up largely replacing traditional managed care, in which insurers tightly control costs by denying care they believe is unnecessary.

This has led to plans to publish more information on both the price and quality of care. ‘‘We want people to understand what they’re purchasing,’’ said Dr. David Cochran, Harvard Pilgrim senior vice president for strategic development.

The paucity of information became clear to Baker last fall, when he visited his doctor for a nagging cough and a fever. His physician did a physical exam and then recommended a chest X-ray to rule out pneumonia. Baker, who’s enrolled in the high-deductible plan Best Buy, would have to pay for the X-ray himself and he wanted to know the price. He was surprised his doctor didn’t know. Eventually, his physician ﬁgured out that an X-ray cost $40. Baker paid it and was diagnosed with a slight case of pneumonia. ‘‘If consumers are supposed to take more ﬁnancial responsibility, then they need the information,’’ he said.

Some of the prices insurers pay for the latest technology are surprisingly high. Harvard Pilgrim pays $717 to $1,240 for a brain MRI, which is commonly used to look for tumors, and $2,141 to $3,180 for a knee arthroscopy, a minor surgical procedure to repair torn ligaments. Blue Cross, which lists the cost of 22 outpatient and overnight procedures, pays providers $2,000 to $3,500 for a cardiac catheterization to measure heart function, and $5,600 to $6,600 for a routine childbirth.

Prices for older and more routine medical prodecures are so inexpensive in comparison, they almost seem quaint. Harvard Pilgrim pays $3 to $5 for a urinalysis and almost any type of X-ray costs less than $100. The most common medical procedure — an annual physical — costs Blue Cross $150 to $190.

Insurers post a price range for each procedure because they pay individual hospitals and doctors different fees based on the contracts they’ve negotiated. Prices also vary because some patients may require a more involved version of a particular procedure. Insurers will release prices over the phone to members who don’t have Internet access.

Dr. Richard Parker, an internist and medical director of the Beth Israel Deaconess Physician Organization, said posting prices is ‘‘a good thing’’ in that it educates patients about what’s driving up health care costs. But given that only a few procedures are discretionary, he doesn’t think it will discourage spending.

For example, if a doctor promises he can give relief to a patient’s painful knee with arthroscopy, the patient may decide to take anti-inﬂammatories and live with the soreness, rather than spend his $1,000 deductible. But most of the time, ‘‘when a doctor advises a patient to have a procedure, it’s because the patient needs it,’’ he said. ‘‘If they think you need a stress test to rule out coronary artery disease and potentially prevent a heart attack, you don’t want the patient saying ‘I don’t want to pay for that, I’ll take my chances.’ ’’

Companies that offer so-called consumer-driven plans said it makes sense for some employees.

Kevin Goguen purchased Harvard Pilgrim’s Best Buy two years ago when his employer, Life Care Center of Acton, began offering the plan. Goguen, 40, was healthy and ﬁgured he wouldn’t need to spend the $1,000 deductible. And his premium under the plan was just $5 a week, compared to $25 for a regular HMO. The extra $20 would have cost him $1,040 over the course of the year anyway.

Last October, Goguen, the plant operations manager, was diagnosed with lung cancer. He immediately spent $1,000 on an MRI and visits with his doctor. Under these circumstances, he said, he would not have been swayed by a price list showing the high cost of procedures.

Insurers have not yet taken the step of posting what they pay individual hospitals and doctors — a move that is likely to be controversial because it could drive business away from more expensive providers. But some executives say such full disclosure is likely within the next few years as consumers demand information that allows them to compare where they get the best quality at the lowest cost.
