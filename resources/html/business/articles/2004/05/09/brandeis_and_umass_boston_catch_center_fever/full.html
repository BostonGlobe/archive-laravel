Brandeis and UMass-Boston catch center fever

Both buildings embrace openness, but they send different messages

By Robert Campbell, Globe Correspondent  |  May 9, 2004

Maybe it's because college students spend so much time hunkered down in front of computer screens in dimly lighted rooms. Are these poor creatures starved for air and light? Whatever the reason, there's a sudden epidemic of new campus centers in colleges and universities in New England and elsewhere, and most are virtual explosions of bright daylight and open space.

Two of these are recent arrivals in the Boston area. The Shapiro Campus Center at Brandeis was designed by Somerville architect Charles Rose, and the UMass Boston Center on Columbia Point is by the Boston firm of Kallmann McKinnell & Wood.

Campus centers are a relatively new kind of building. Each is a kind of mini-downtown for students, where they can eat, drink, study, perform, party, log on to a computer, run the student paper, buy a book or a T-shirt, and sometimes do business with university staff. Smith College has just opened a particularly spectacular one. Amherst, Bowdoin, Columbia, Mt. Holyoke, Middlebury, and Williams, among others, boast new campus centers.

The two in our area, like most of the others, are organized around glassy multi-story atriums. In both, the dominant color is white. In both, openness is a fetish. Both open themselves wherever possible to views of the outdoors, and they open vistas indoors too, so that those participating in different activities tend to be visible to one another.

But in other ways these two campus centers are very different. Shapiro at Brandeis is loose, hip, relaxed, unpretentious. UMass-Boston, by contrast, aims for a kind of traditional permanence and civic grandeur.

UMass is much the bigger of the two. At 330,000 square feet of floor area, it's five times the size of Shapiro and features two atriums instead of one. Looking down the marching colonnades of its handsome halls, you feel at times you must be in a state capitol.

The sense of grandeur is no accident.

"I wanted the spaces and finishes to say to the students, `You are really important,' " says Michael McKinnell. "UMass was conceived as a commuter college. Now it's a serious university, but there was no physical manifestation of bringing people together." University planners, he says, wanted to create a place that would attract students to stay after classes, mingle with one another, learn from one another, and build a community.

The other goal was to fight the grimness of the old campus, most of which is built of dark brick with small windows. "It's a place of dungeonlike spaces and introverted rooms," says McKinnell. "We wanted a sense of openness to light and activity."

One of the things UMass opens to is Boston Harbor. For once, on this inward-looking campus, you're aware of the water. The campus center reaches out to embrace the harbor with a dramatic, boldly curving limestone facade. A projecting roof gives it a memorable silhouette. This is a building that asserts its presence in the landscape.

If there's a problem, it's the inevitable result of so much openness. Acoustics can be raucous. And everyone is pretty much stuck with the noise. There are lots of small private offices for university staff and faculty, with windows looking out over the atriums. But there's no glass in the windows, and the offices also lack doors. Thus, says McKinnell, "the administration is seen to be open and accessible."

True enough, but this may be a case where an idea has been pushed a little far. It will be interesting to see how much togetherness people will put up with. That said, though, this is an impressive piece of architecture and an impressively generous investment, too, by the university.

Where UMass celebrates its size, pulling itself up to its full height to address the Atlantic Ocean, Brandeis goes the other way. Though his building is much smaller, architect Rose worried that it would look too massive. "The problem was to find a way to make it into an aggregate of smaller pieces," he says. He fragments the building by dividing it into two wings, connected by a three-story atrium. He then breaks the wings into angular, shardlike shapes -- typical of this architect's work -- that seem to be assembled almost randomly on the site. Where UMass employs the classical geometries of rectangle and circle, Brandeis is all acute angles, erupting sometimes in bold, freely sculpted forms. Different parts are finished in different materials, some copper, some limestone.

The atrium is where Shapiro differs most from UMass. The atriums at UMass are lined with serene rows of columns that remind you of classical or Renaissance architecture. The atrium at Brandeis is crisscrossed with stairs and bridges that connect parts of the upper levels. It's jazzy and busy, even, deliberately, a little hectic.

Both buildings are making social statements. UMass, by its architecture, is asserting that it's arrived as a major university. And Brandeis, which may feel less need to make such a statement, is asserting instead that it's funky, inventive, and student-centered.

But both buildings have more in common than their differences. They're variants on a standard type, which perhaps derives from the American shopping mall. Both feature those atriums, flooded with daylight. Both put the student, not the professor or administrator, in the foreground. Both are flexible, with movable furniture that can be rearranged for different activities. Both are informal, lacking the paneled oak of a Yale or Oxford. And both, too, connect with the nature around them. UMass's sweeping curve shapes a circular lawn. At Brandeis, the earth was literally reformed to create, on this hilly campus, a broad, level picnic-and-Frisbee field.

Why so many campus centers today? An article in a recent Chronicle of Higher Education by Michael J. Lewis of Williams College asks that question. He argues that one reason is computers. "Studying is no longer an affair of reading and typing. Studying has become more intermittent, more gregarious and more mobile," writes Lewis. Students thus seek to move out from their desks and cubbies into a more social world.

A second reason is that students have become consumers. "The process by which students select colleges is increasingly made as a consumer decision, through comparison shopping among competing brands. The student center serves as a crucial device of product differentiation."

And a third reason is that today's students, who are likely to have grown up with their own rooms, prefer to live without roommates in college. Less socializing takes place in the dorm and more moves to the campus center, which, writes Lewis with wry eloquence, "is industrious, hospitable, and somewhat prone to insomnia."
