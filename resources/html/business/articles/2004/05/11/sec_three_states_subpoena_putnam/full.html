BUSINESS IN BRIEF

SEC, three states subpoena Putnam

May 11, 2004

THE REGION

Putnam Investments has received subpoenas from the Securities and Exchange Commission's Philadelphia and Chicago offices, related to broker payments and revenue-sharing deals aimed at promoting fund sales, according to a quarterly filing yesterday by Putnam's parent, Marsh & McLennan Cos. The disclosure also said Boston-based Putnam has received information requests from the Florida Department of Financial Services, West Virginia's secretary of state and state auditor, and the Vermont Securities Division. Those requests are related to market timing, which are charges Putnam has settled with Massachusetts regulators and the SEC. (Beth Healy)

AG, Cambridge Credit Counseling settle

In the wake of a lawsuit by Massachusetts Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly, Cambridge Credit Counseling Corp. of Agawam cut its fees, brought in more outside directors, and reorganized its relationship with its for-profit affiliates. Reilly last month accused the nonprofit firm of charging consumers seeking advice about managing their debt upfront fees of $200 to $500 and funneling more than $60 million over a 2-year period to its for-profit affiliates. Under the changes, Cambridge Credit said it will charge customers an upfront fee of $75 and a monthly maintenance fee capped at $50. It said the for-profit affiliates, valued at $50 million, are being transferred at no charge to the nonprofit, which will capture their earnings. The company also said four new outside directors are being added to the board to review executive compensation, which Reilly called excessive. (Bruce Mohl)

TA Associates buys 50% of Numeric

TA Associates, a private equity firm in Boston, has agreed to buy 50 percent of Numeric Investors of Cambridge, an investment firm that manages $7.5 billion in mutual funds and hedge funds. Under the deal, Numeric's employees, led by founder and president Langdon Wheeler, would still own half the company. TA bought its half from Strategic Investment Group in Washington, D.C., at a price, analysts estimate, that values Numeric at more than $225 million. Numeric uses a quantitative approach, or computer models, to select stocks. The firm was founded in 1989 and employs 54 people. (Beth Healy)

State Street wins Lufthansa contract

State Street Corp., the world's largest custodian of assets, said it was awarded a multibillion dollar contract to provide custody, accounting, and analytics services to Deutsche Lufthansa AG. The deal builds on State Street's 2003 purchase of the global securities business of Deutsche Bank AG, the Boston-based company said. Spokesmen for both companies declined to comment on the size of the contract. Lufthansa, Europe's third-largest airline, ended last year with $5.2 billion in pension obligations. The German company said it would transfer German pension liabilities into a special securities fund overseen by State Street and HSBC Trinkaus & Burkhardt KgaA. (Bloomberg)

Martha Stewart Living issues a warning

Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia Inc. said it may face further operating losses, additional litigation, and the loss of key employees as a result of the criminal conviction of its founder and corporate namesake. In a federal filing, the media and merchandising company said it may change some of its brands and publishing content, and cut costs, following the conviction of Stewart in March for lying to investigators about a personal stock sale. She was forced to resign as an officer and a member of the company's board. "We may experience further material adverse impacts as a result of the trial outcome, including, without limitation, additional revenue declines, additional litigation . . . additional (costs) and loss of key personnel," the company said in a quarterly filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission. It added that the company, which last week posted a wider first-quarter loss as advertising revenue fell, said it may sustain "substantial operating losses in future periods" and that its cash position may be materially adversely affected. (Reuters)

Welch mulls return to business world

Jack Welch, retired chief executive of General Electric Co., left open the possibility that he might return to the business world, but gave no clue where he might land. Rumors have swirled that Welch is ready to run another company. "They are sort of true but not all true," Welch, 68, said about the rumors. "Offers float all the time," he told a packed auditorium in Radio City Music Hall during the World Business Forum in New York. "You can do more" after retirement, he added. A New York Post article on April 26 said Welch was "itching to run a major company" and that he might want to run Walt Disney Co., where dissident shareholders have been pressuring chief executive Michael Eisner to resign. But the Disney board on April 27 gave its full backing to Eisner and his team of executives after a two-day meeting. (Reuters)

New World Pasta seeks Ch. 11 protection

New World Pasta Co., whose Prince and other brands make it one of the world's largest dry pasta makers, filed for bankruptcy protection, a casualty of low-carbohydrate diets and balky accounting. New World Pasta, which is more than 80 percent-owned by JLL Partners of New York, said it had roughly $450 million in debts in filing for protection under Chapter 11 of the bankruptcy code in US Bankruptcy Court in Harrisburg, Pa. The popularity of low-carbohydrate diets such as the Atkins diet led to a 7 percent drop in pasta sales so far this year, on top of a 5 percent decline in the fourth quarter of 2003, and price discounts are common. In addition, the company's finances have been clouded for the past two years by a faulty computer software installation, which damaged its bottom line during a 2002 acquisition, vice president Cary Metz said. (Reuters)

Monsanto drops plans for biotech wheat

Monsanto Co. said it has halted development of genetically engineered spring wheat, a decision hailed by opponents of biotech crops. The biotech wheat would have been -- like corn, cotton, and oilseeds produced by Monsanto -- able to withstand Monsanto's Roundup herbicide. Monsanto cited economic factors in its decision: a 25 percent decrease in US and Canadian spring wheat acreage since 1997, and a lack of demand for biotech wheat. Genetically engineered crops remain a difficult sell in parts of the world because opponents have questioned the safety of the crops. Japan, America's top wheat importer, has said it will accept no wheat -- conventional or biotech -- from any nation that grows biotech wheat. (AP)

THE WORLD

Japanese pension scandal widens

Japan's top opposition leader said he would resign after admitting he missed payments into the troubled national pension system, the second high-profile politician to step down in the scandal. Naoto Kan, head of the Democratic Party, had resisted quitting but pressure built over the weekend for him to atone for failing to make payments while he was health and welfare minister in the 1990s. "I caused great trouble for the Japanese people by increasing distrust in politics. My responsibility in this regard is very great," Kan said at meeting of Democratic Party lawmakers. Kan is one of an expanding group of Japanese politicians who have confessed to missing pension premium payments. The scandal comes as the government tries to reform the system to assist a growing number of retirees. (AP)
