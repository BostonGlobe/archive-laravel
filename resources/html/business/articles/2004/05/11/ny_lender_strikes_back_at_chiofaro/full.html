N.Y. lender strikes back at Chiofaro

Court asked to throw out bankruptcy case

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr., Globe Staff  |  May 11, 2004

The New York company that holds developer Donald J. Chiofaro's mortgage on International Place yesterday said that Chiofaro filed for bankruptcy in bad faith, and charged that "if ever there were a case which deserved to be dismissed, this case is it."

Court documents filed in US District Court in Boston showed that Chiofaro, who petitioned for bankruptcy on Friday as a tactic to keep Tishman Speyer Properties from taking control of the downtown skyscrapers, owes about $772 million in loans, interest, and deferred interest payments.

However, Chiofaro is not behind on his payments. He has only about $1.5 million in other bills, was fully paid up on them as recently as February, and has $7 million in cash, lawyers for Tishman Speyer told the court.

Although the two Chiofaro partnerships that have ownership interests in International Place are not now in default on their loan payments, they would have been by the end of the year, Tishman Speyer lawyers said Chiofaro's 2004 budget showed.

Tishman Speyer, which since March has held the notes on the two office towers, asked the court to throw out the case, also alleging that Chiofaro has refused to show them the books to determine whether money is being spent properly.

"The only issue here is whether Mr. Chiofaro will be allowed to abuse the bankruptcy process to evade the financial commitments of the Debtors" -- Tishman Speyer -- "while keeping his equity stake and personal contracts in place," Tishman Speyer's lawyers wrote in their motion for dismissal.

At an emergency hearing before Bankruptcy Court Judge Joan N. Feeney, lawyers for the two sides argued over details of what expenses Chiofaro should be allowed to pay over the next two weeks, before a more permanent order is issued. Feeney issued a decision yesterday afternoon allowing Chiofaro to continue to pay his bills, issuing liens on behalf of Tishman Speyer to protect its interest in International Place, and ordering "reasonable access" to Chiofaro's books and records.

The two sides -- especially the combative Chiofaro -- came out swinging on Friday after several weeks of negotiation over control of International Place, given the difficulty Chiofaro was expected to have keeping up with loan payments he said amount to almost $50 million a year.

Chiofaro called his new lenders "pirates" and accused Tishman Speyer of wanting to seize control of ownership and management of the property. Chiofaro is seeking to lower his interest rate on the loans through the bankruptcy proceeding.

Yesterday, Chiofaro toned down his rhetoric, issuing a statement that said, "We believe that our court action is an entirely appropriate use of Chapter 11, which was designed to protect borrowers from overly aggressive lenders."

Of the numbers submitted in Tishman Speyer legal documents, Chiofaro said, "We do not acknowledge, understand or accept those calculations, and all of those issues will be dealt with by the court."

The 2004 budget of the partnerships that own One International Place and Two International Place show "an inability . . . to pay their debt service throughout the year, with a shortfall of more than $20 million," one filing said, adding, "a payment default under the Loan Documents was inevitable."

Lawyers from Mintz Levin Cohn Ferris Glovsky and Popeo PC, representing Tishman Speyer, cited a statement by Chiofaro's lawyer, Harry Murphy of Hanify & King PC, as proof that Chiofaro's bankruptcy filings were "to protect the ownership and control of International Place and do not indicate financial insolvency."

The business operations of International Place do not need to be reorganized in bankruptcy, Tishman Speyer lawyers said. The office towers "will emerge from Chapter 11 as they went in -- the same buildings, the same tenants, the same operations."

Richard E. Mikels of Mintz Levin said Tishman Speyer has not been allowed enough access to the books to know the full picture. "We don't know if anything is wrong," Mikels said. "We want to find out what in the world has happened to our collateral."

All of the dozen parties that bid to purchase the International Place loans from their previous holder, Teachers Insurance and Annuity Association of America, knew that "a monetary default was imminent," according to the motion to dismiss the case.

Tishman Speyer paid $596 million for a loan with a face value of about $650 million and "additional accrued interest" of some $121 million. The Chiofaro partnerships' obligations, then, total about $772 million. Those partnerships, Fort Hill Square Associates and Fort Hill Square Phase 2 Associates, are owned in the majority by Hillman Properties Inc., with Chiofaro and his partner, Theodore A. Oatis, as minority owners.

According to a document filed by Tishman Speyer, the market value of International Place is less than the amount owed on the loans.

One of Chiofaro's lawyers, Charles R. Bennett Jr. of Hanify & King, asked the court to allow Chiofaro to continue to do business as it has "for the last 20 years," paying utilities and maintenance bills. "We anticipate . . . payment in full" to all of the creditors, he said.
