Index forecasts growth ahead

Leading indicators rise 0.1% in April

By Bloomberg News  |  May 21, 2004

An index of leading US economic indicators rose 0.1 percent in April and the March figure was revised to show the biggest jump since May 2003, supporting forecasts that the economy will accelerate. Philadelphia-area manufacturing expanded for the 12th straight month.

The number of Americans filing first-time unemployment claims rose last week to 345,000 from a revised 333,000, the Labor Department said in Washington. The four-week average still fell to the lowest since November 2000 and fewer people remained on jobless benefit rolls. The Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia said its regional measure of factory employment jumped to a 31-year high.

"It's hard to see a second-half slowdown here," said Ian Shepherdson, chief US economist at High Frequency Economics in Valhalla, N.Y. The increase in the index of leading indicators during the past year is "consistent with GDP growth of about 6.75 percent."

The Conference Board said its index of leading indicators, a gauge of how the economy will perform in the next three to six months, rose 4.9 percent since April 2003, the biggest 12-month gain since a 5.7 percent increase in March 1984. The New York-based board revised its March index to growth of 0.8 percent from 0.3 percent.

The economy is "strong and getting stronger" and the labor market is improving, President Bush said in an interview with television reporters at the White House.

Economists had expected the number of jobless claims to fall to 328,000, according to the median forecast in a Bloomberg News survey. The four-week average of the number of Americans filing initial claims for unemployment insurance declined to 333,500 from 336,250, the Labor Department said.

The Philadelphia Fed's manufacturing index registered 23.8 in May, compared with 32.5 in April. Positive readings indicate growth. The regional Fed's index of factory employment was 22.6, the highest since April 1973.

The Treasury's benchmark 10-year note rose on the increase in jobless claims and the decline in the manufacturing index, which was more than expected. Demand for government debt also rose after Federal Reserve governor Ben S. Bernanke said in a speech yesterday that inflation "appears likely to remain in the zone of price stability" into 2005. Inflation erodes the value of fixed-income investments.

The 4 percent note maturing in May 2014 gained 0.50, pushing its yield down 6 basis points to 4.71 percent. US benchmark stock indexes were little changed, wiping out earlier gains after the manufacturing report.

The dollar rose against the euro and 11 other major currencies after the Philadelphia index showed a rise in prices paid by manufacturers as well as in factory employment.

The latest jobless claims report covers the week that the Labor Department surveys employers for its May employment statistics, which will be released June 4. Earlier this month, the department reported the economy added 288,000 workers in April and 337,000 a month earlier, the biggest two-month gain since early 2000.

Joshua Shapiro, chief US economist at MFR Inc. in New York, said claims of about 335,000 suggest employment growth of 200,000 a month, "which is sufficient to achieve our forecast of 3 percent to 3.5 percent growth in real consumer spending in coming quarters."

Stephen Stanley, chief economist at RBS Greenwich Capital, said he now predicts the United States added 260,000 jobs this month. Since the labor market turned around in September, the economy has created 1.1 million jobs. More hiring boosts the odds the Federal Reserve will begin raising interest rates next month.

The central bank has left borrowing costs unchanged at 1 percent since June and said it will take a "measured" approach to raising the overnight bank-lending rate from a four-decade low as hiring increases. In his speech in Seattle yesterday, Bernanke said the Fed's pledge on the pace of increases is "not an unconditional commitment."

"The pace of tightening will of necessity respond to evolving economic conditions," particularly the labor market's strength and "developments on the inflation front."

"There is a lot of uncertainty, a lot of changes in the economy," Bernanke told reporters after the lunch sponsored by the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco and the University of Washington economics department.

Among the changes are the "primarily negative" effects of terrorism and the Iraq war on the US economy, evidence of remaining "slack" in labor markets and the "risk factor" of higher oil prices, he said. Crude oil futures in New York have jumped 11 percent this month and are up 40 percent from a year earlier.
