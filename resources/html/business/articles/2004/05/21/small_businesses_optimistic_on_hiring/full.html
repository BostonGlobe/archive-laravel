BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Small businesses optimistic on hiring

May 21, 2004

A significant number of New England small businesses plan to expand and hire over the next few months, according to a new quarterly survey that is a joint venture of Citizens Financial Group Inc. and the University of Massachusetts. The finding adds to evidence that the region's economy and labor markets are recovering. In fact, the Citizens Bank/UMass survey found that small businesses in New England have a brighter outlook than their counterparts nationally: 39 percent said they believe the next three months will be a good time to expand, compared to 24 percent in a similar national survey. Also, 24 percent of New England small businesses said they planned to hire workers in the next quarter compared to 14 percent nationally. High wages and healthcare costs, however, appear to be leading some New England firms to hire only temporary workers, if not put off hiring altogether, the survey found. (Robert Gavin)

State Street CEO says firms must act

After a spate of high-profile scandals, the financial-services industry needs more self-policing initiatives to regain the trust of investors, said State Street Corp. chief executive David Spina. "We need to take positive steps and make the effort to build trust with our clients," he said at TowerGroup's Annual Financial Services Business & Technology Conference. "[But] the trust issue hasn't gone away," as high-profile corporate and investment-industry scandals continue to play out in the courts and in the media, reinforcing a view that wrongdoing is widespread, he said. Among his proposals for self-policing by investment firms are tougher corporate codes of conduct, adding to the compliance staff to see that such measures are adhered to, and greater disclosure to the public of fees and internal rules. (Dow Jones)

Lawsuits seek to unlock generic drugs

A pair of lawsuits filed against GlaxoSmithKline this week are the latest in a string accusing pharmaceutical companies of improperly preventing cheaper, generic brands of top-selling medications from coming to market. New York City filed federal antitrust lawsuits in Virginia and Pennsylvania on Tuesday claiming the pharmaceutical giant improperly extended its monopoly on the antianxiety drug Paxil and the antibiotic Augmentin by obtaining new patents for the drugs when old patents were set to expire, a practice critics call "evergreening." GlaxoSmithKline has denied any impropriety. "We've obtained our patents in full compliance with the law," said spokeswoman Mary Anne Rhyne. (AP)

Microsoft, Oracle form partnership

Microsoft Corp. and Oracle Corp. revealed a technology partnership that could signal a thaw in the combative relationship between two of the software industry's fiercest rivals. Under the arrangement, software developers will be able to use Microsoft's development tools to write programs for Oracle's database systems. It is the first formal agreement between the two companies, according to executives on both sides. Oracle chief executive Larry Ellison said Oracle and Microsoft are discussing additional efforts, including a joint advertising effort to promote the use of Oracle's database software on top of Microsoft's Windows operating system. That would be notable because Microsoft's own database software, SQL Server, competes directly with Oracle's offerings. (Dow Jones)

Starwood wins breach-of-contract case

Starwood Hotels & Resorts Worldwide Inc., which owns the Sheraton and Westin chains, successfully defended a seven-year, $1.4 billion breach-of-contract suit it inherited when it took over ITT Corp. After a two-month trial without a jury, Superior Court Judge John Fratto in Camden, N.J., found that a contract between Intelnet International Corp., a now-defunct phone and Internet services company, and ITT was invalid because it was improperly backdated, said Richard Ben-Veniste, a lawyer for Starwood. The case was filed in December 1997, before Starwood completed its $13.2 billion takeover of ITT in February 1998. (Bloomberg)

AT&T rejects $1b offer for stake in Rogers

AT&T Wireless Communications Inc. has rejected a $1 billion offer for its 34.1 percent stake in the Rogers wireless phone company from longtime partner Rogers Communications Inc. Rogers Communications said its $31-a-share bid for AT&T's stake was rejected after a 21-day negotiation period, which ended Wednesday night. AT&T Wireless, in the process of being taken over by Cingular Wireless, now has 60 days to come up with a buyer who would pay more than Rogers, which had the right to make the first offer after AT&T Wireless declared its intention to sell. Rogers Communications currently owns about 56 percent of Rogers Wireless. (AP)

Witness says Tyco auditor knew of loans

Tyco International Ltd.'s auditor undertook "thorough" reviews of its books and records, including documents that showed suspect loans and bonuses given to the company's former general counsel, according to testimony. Prosecutors charge that Mark Belnick, Tyco's general counsel, received at least some of those loans and bonuses without the knowledge of the company's board and have charged him with grand larceny and securities fraud. But Sheila Rex, a Tyco accountant called as a witness by the prosecution, told jurors that outside auditor PriceWaterhouseCoopers had regular access to records of the Belnick payments. "They did a very thorough review of all those accounts on the ledgers," she said during cross examination by one of Belnick's attorneys. (Reuters)

Playboy sued over age discrimination

A Playboy editor who was fired after more than 30 years with the company filed a federal suit, claiming he was dismissed because the magazine wanted a younger staff that might attract younger readers. The suit, filed by John Rezak against Playboy Enterprises in US District Court in Chicago, seeks unspecified damages, including back and future pay. Rezak, 54, was an associate managing editor when he was fired last November. His suit said Playboy Enterprises wanted more readers ages 18 to 34. According to the complaint, executive vice president Howard S. Shapiro said in March 2003 that the company believed the only way to change the "demographics of the magazine was to change the demographics of the people who put the magazine out." A spokeswoman for Playboy said the company denies the allegations. (AP)

Company sues chip maker for $500m

Intel Corp., the world's largest computer-chip maker, was sued by a Canadian company that seeks $500 million and claims the Pentium chips infringe a patent. Closely held All Computers Inc. owns a patent issued in 1996 for circuitry used to replace slower-speed microprocessors with faster devices in computers. All Computers claims it pioneered the technology that helped companies make faster personal computers. All Computers was founded by Canadian Mers Kutt, who helped develop personal computers in the 1970s. The Toronto-based company hasn't made any products in about 10 years. "The business of the business right now is to enforce this patent," said All Computers lawyer Edward O'Connor. (Bloomberg)
