A wave of demand for docks

May 31, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Still, many marinas couldn't expand for a technical reason: They needed to dredge harbors to make room for boats, but had no inexpensive way to dispose of the sludge.

All that changed last year. After three decades of controversy, marinas were given a low-cost place to dump dredged material in designated spots in Providence Harbor and offshore.

Meanwhile, industry officials say the demand for slips from boaters in Rhode Island and neighboring states has continued to climb, as boat ownership increased since 2001, when many Americans curtailed their overseas travel.

Massachusetts dock space is so stretched that the wait for a permanent boat slip or mooring can take a decade or more. The wait in Rhode Island is shorter, though it still can take months or more, marina owners say.

"We want the boaters here," said Ken Kubic, president of Rhode Island Marine Trades Association.

In many ways, Rhode Island is a boater's dream, and not just for the tax benefits. With more than 400 miles of shoreline bordering protected inlets, bays, and beach, the state's coast is a short trip from Martha's Vineyard, Montauk Point on Long Island, and other popular destinations.

"It's convenient, and with all the protected harbors, it's nice," said Stephen Miller, a boat owner from Attleboro who switched from a slip in Massachusetts more than 15 years ago to one at Apponaug Marine in Warwick.

Near such main thoroughfares as Route 24 and Interstate 95, several Rhode Island marinas estimate that 20 to 30 percent of their seasonal slip renters are from Massachusetts. They say they would probably draw more if more slips were available.

Rhode Island does not have a clear count of how many slips exist in the state, or how many are definitely proposed. A review of proposals known by the Globe showed at least 3,200 slips planned.

Marinas are built on private coastal land, but their docks extend into public waterways. Marina owners rent the slips, and the business can be lucrative. With rent based on the length of a boat, prices can range from a few hundred dollars for a stay of a few nights to thousands for a seasonal slip.

Despite private ownership, marina owners say they represent a public amenity.

"The marina lets people use the water," said Joe Grillo Jr., owner of Champlin's, which offers a bakery, bumper boats, and, in the last few years, long waiting lists for slips on busy summer days.

Other marina supporters note that if marinas weren't built on the water, houses would probably be built on the coast, blocking public access completely. "We allow the public here; we aren't trying to stop access," Grillo said.

Marina opponents say they aren't against boats. They say they don't want marinas preventing them from canoeing near shore or from shellfishing.

Others say the piers can wreck water views or even swimming locations. Still others worry that bodies of water can handle only so many boats.

Not all the marina development is contentious. In Portsmouth, longtime private plans to build a 1,500-slip marina on former Navy land appears to have the backing of many residents and town officials, who see it as an improvement in the area.

The head of the Rhode Island Coastal Resources Management Council points out that many plans, such as the recently approved Little Compton marina, require the owner to provide some sort of free public access, such as allowing the public to walk on docks or park on the property.

"Marinas provide access; it's very important," said Jeff Baris, Warwick harbormaster. "Without them, there are no public resources to support our boating population. Unfortunately, however, I haven't found anyone to say how much is too much. That is the question that needs answering."
