BUSINESS IF BRIEF

Stent maker claims 70% of new market

May 19, 2004

THE REGION

Boston Scientific Corp. said it sold $78 million worth of its new Taxus drug-coated coronary stent from May 1 to May 14, giving the Natick company by its own estimate more than 70 percent of the market for the new technology. Stents are mesh tubes used to prop open clogged arteries. The newest ones release drugs to inhibit scar tissue from narrowing the vessels again. Johnson & Johnson, the only other firm with such a product on the market, said it won't discuss sales or market share until the quarter ends. Previously, Johnson & Johnson said the two firms split the stent market about evenly. (Ross Kerber)

Biogen to seek European OK for drug

Biogen Idec Inc. said it would seek European approval this year to sell its Antegren drug for the treatment of Crohn's disease, a chronic inflammatory bowel disorder. Biogen Idec, of Cambridge, is developing Antegren with Elan Corp. of Dublin and hopes to gain regulatory approvals for several uses. A Biogen spokeswoman said the companies will probably need to conduct another clinical trial before seeking US approval for the treatment of Crohn's. The companies still expect to apply for approval of Antegren in the United States and Europe by the end of the second quarter for the treatment of multiple sclerosis. (Ross Kerber)

Implant Sciences up 22% on Army pact

Stock of Implant Sciences Corp. rose 22 percent after the Wakefield firm said the Army had ordered 12 portable explosives detectors, representing the first commercial-scale order by the government. An earlier order by the Navy was for prototypes. The Army's order for the device, called the Quantum Sniffer, is worth $300,000, the company said. The detectors come in hand-held and desktop configurations and analyze ions to detect the presence of trace vapors of explosives in air samples. Shares of Implant rose $2.45 to $13.60. (Christopher Rowland)

Millennium shares up on Velcade study

Shares in Millennium Pharmaceuticals Inc. rose 7 percent after the Cambridge biotechnology company disclosed study results showing patients with a type of advanced blood cancer treated with its Velcade drugs had a better chance of survival, compared with a control group. Millennium released the preliminary results Monday after the close of trading; its shares rose 93 cents to $14.49 in Nasdaq trading on Tuesday. Last year, the company won conditional approval to sell Velcade for patients who have already undergone two other treatments. The company hopes further studies will help it to gain approvals to sell Velcade for use in earlier stages of the disease. (Ross Kerber)

Senate rejects delay in base closings

The Senate narrowly defeated a fiscal 2005 defense authorization bill amendment that would delay for two years a process that threatens to close domestic military bases, including Hanscom Air Force Base in Bedford and the Army Soldier Systems Center in Natick. A similar bill, also calling for a two-year delay, is expected to come to a vote in the House today. A delay would buy time for advocates of the two research-oriented bases. ''We're in the middle of a war, and the Defense Department has a lot of different priorities," said Cort Boulanger, vice president of the Massachusetts High Technology Council, explaining the momentum that has been building for the delay. (Robert Weisman)

AltiComm to quit Mass. phone market

AltiComm, a Canton reseller of telecommunications services under the names Servisense and Eastern Telephone, is pulling out of the Bay State residential market, forcing 3,500 customers to find alternative suppliers for local and long-distance service by May 27. The Department of Telecommunications and Energy said AltiComm owes so much money to Verizon Corp. that its local service is about to be shut off. Neither AltiComm nor Verizon would say how much is owed. (Bruce Mohl)

South End condo groundbreaking held

A groundbreaking ceremony was held for Gateway Terrace, a 133-unit condo project in the South End. Developer Demetrios Dasco, of Atlas Investment Group, said 75 of the project's 120 market-rate units are under agreement. Mayor Thomas M. Menino said the $60 million project will enliven Washington Street. He added that 13 units will be set aside as affordable housing. Plans call for Gateway Terrace to have retail space on the first floor. (Chris Reidy)

Lacking financing, Cometa to shut down

Cometa Networks, a consortium that planned to build wireless Internet hot spots throughout the United States, will close for lack of financing. Cometa was launched with fanfare in late 2002 with funding from Intel Corp., IBM Corp. and AT&T Corp. Its goal was to set up networks of WiFi wireless data services in large metropolitan areas. That would enable users of laptop or hand-held computers to get Internet access practically anywhere in Cometa's service area. Kent Hellebust, vice president of marketing, said a trial program in Seattle did well, but Cometa couldn't raise additional funds it needed. (Hiawatha Bray)

Prosecutor asks Invesco for information

Invesco Funds Group, a Denver mutual fund company, said the US attorney in Manhattan made ''limited inquiries" about ''certain specific Invesco Funds, entities and/or individuals," according to a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. Invesco and former CEO Raymond Cunningham face lawsuits by the SEC and New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer alleging they permitted select investors to make frequent trades in their funds, raising expenses for ordinary shareholders. Such trades generated millions of dollars in fees for the company before it ended last July, regulators said. (Bloomberg)

House vote on overtime rules blocked

For the second time in a week, House Republicans have blocked a Democratic attempt to force an election-year vote on the Bush administration's new overtime pay rules, sought by Democratic George Miller of California. His provision would require the Labor Department to retain the eligibility of all workers who currently qualify for overtime pay. The House also rebuffed Miller last week. Had Miller succeeded, the vote would have been largely symbolic, but would have forced Congress to take a stand on a pocketbook issue important to many voters and labor unions. Rules taking effect in August will exempt about 100,000 workers eligible for overtime pay, the department says; Democrats say the number will be much higher. (AP)

Witness: Adelphia indictment flawed

Former Adelphia Communications Corp. vice president James Brown testified in New York that the indictment of founder John Rigas and two sons contained errors. The 102-page indictment charges that Adelphia falsely claimed 5,300,517 basic subscribers on May 15, 2000. After reviewing documents, Brown said the actual number was 5,003,517 subscribers, a number misstated in both the original indictment in September 2002, and an amended one filed nine months later. The testimony may undercut the prosecution's case. Brown has been testifying against John Rigas, 79, and sons Michael, 50, and Timothy, 47, who are accused of hiding $2.3 billion in debt, stealing $100 million, and lying about revenue and operations at Adelphia before its bankruptcy in June 2002. Brown, 42, pleaded guilty to a role in the fraud. The indictment also misstated when Adelphia falsely claimed subscribers in Venezuela, Brown testified. (Bloomberg)
