US picking up pace of device inquiries

Probes to focus on allegations of fraud, abuse

By Ross Kerber, Globe Staff  |  May 19, 2004

The US Department of Health and Human Services plans to accelerate its investigations of alleged fraud and abuse by medical device companies, a senior official said.

The inquiries will likely center on kickbacks and other improper sales and marketing deals between doctors and companies in the fast-growing industry, Lewis Morris, chief counsel to the department's inspector general, said in a phone interview.

The focus echoes a federal campaign against fraudulent practices by drug makers driven partly by human services' inspector general's office. Last week, in the latest action against drug companies, Pfizer Inc. agreed to pay $430 million to settle allegations its Warner-Lambert unit promoted its Neurontin epilepsy drug for unapproved uses.

Morris said executives in the medical device industry can expect even more actions within a year to 18 months as the workload from pharmaceutical investigations winds down. Since last year the inspector general has helped prosecutors reach settlements totaling more than $700 million from device makers, including Abbott Laboratories and Guidant Corp.

Cases against other device makers ''are going to be coming into the pipeline," Morris said. As with cases of fraud against drug makers, many of the investigations into medical device companies stem from whistleblowers at companies who are bringing prosecutors internal documents, Morris said.

Morris said the office is aided by legal provisions that give whistleblowers in health-related cases a share of the recovery, providing a financial incentive to come forward. ''Right now, the people making the money are the pharmaceutical sales people who have got the goods on their employers," Morris said. ''The medical devices salespeople are engaged in the same fraud . . . and they're starting to come in."

Morris' comments confirm a fear that had already spread throughout the devices industry and its trade group, the Advanced Medical Technology Association. On Jan. 1, it issued a new code of ethics restricting entertainment expenses, consulting fees, and gifts its members can give to doctors.

''Good ethics is good business," said Blair Childs, the trade group's executive vice president. ''The code is an effort to be ahead of the IG, [inspector general] in terms of getting the industry at as high an ethical level as possible and to not be reactive."

Companies that drafted the principles include Boston Scientific Corp. of Natick, the largest life sciences company in Massachusetts, which has previously disclosed a Justice Department investigation of its recall of a medical stent in the late 1990s.

Industry executives also want to distinguish themselves from pharmaceutical companies that have received a lot of negative attention lately, including the Neurontin settlement. In addition, in a trial in US District Court in Boston, prosecutors allege that 11 current and former executives of TAP Pharmaceutical Products defrauded the government of millions of dollars by bribing doctors and hospitals with incentives such as cash, free drugs, baseball tickets, and trips to resorts.

The defendants say they were only following standard industry practices. TAP itself paid a record $885 million fine in 2001 to settle similar charges.

Though it has drawn less attention, the crackdown on medical device makers is already underway. Last year Guidant, an Indianapolis devices giant, agreed to pay $92.4 million and plead guilty to 10 felony counts to settle charges its EndoVascular Technologies Inc. subsidiary hid medical problems with a device to treat aortic aneurysms, which may have contributed to a dozen deaths.

Other cases center on billing or marketing policies. In August, Orthofix International of North Carolina agreed to pay $1.7 million, without admitting wrongdoing, to settle federal allegations that it wrongly billed government health care programs for off-label uses of a electromagnetic pulse product meant to stimulate bone growth. The Food and Drug Administration had approved the device for helping to heal fractures in arms and legs, but at the time it wasn't approved for parts of the spine.

Last summer, Abbott Laboratories said it would pay $614 million to settle an investigation of its Ross Products division, which makes feeding pumps for patients in hospitals. According to prosecutors, Ross violated antikickback laws by furnishing free pumps to nursing homes in exchange for agreements to buy supplies for the pumps, encouraging nursing homes to bill Medicare and Medicaid for the free pumps, and providing improper incentives such as educational grants to get them to buy Abbott products.

The payments included a $200 million criminal fine. Like the TAP executives, Abbott insisted it was only following common practices.

Abbott's case was prompted partly by concerns from Morris' office, now headed by acting principal deputy inspector general Dara Corrigan and meant to be a watchdog for the health department's services such as the Medicare and Medicaid programs.

Joseph J. Leghorn, an attorney at Nixon, Peabody in Boston who says he has represented several medical device companies in such cases, said the government is opportunistically swooping in to demand big fees from companies with deep pockets. In general, companies that need approvals for their products from the FDA will often pay a cash settlement faster than companies would otherwise, he said.

''With the high cost of healthcare a hot political issue, pharmaceutical and medical device companies with profitable businesses are easy prey for politicians and prosecutors, even through it is proven that cutting-edge pharmaceuticals and medical devices reduce the long-term cost of healthcare," he said.

Alarms went off at medical device companies last year when they noticed a footnote in an inspector general's report recommending changes to pharmaceutical companies' dealings with doctors. In the footnote, the agency said its guidance ''may also have application to manufacturers of other products that may be reimbursed by federal healthcare programs, such as medical devices and infant nutritional products."

The resulting code by the device industry's trade group is similar in many ways to one adopted by the drug industry's trade group, Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America, such as banning gifts to physicians that have no direct benefit to patients.

But device companies also wanted rules that left a little more leeway to pay training or travel costs for doctors to learn about their devices. The device makers say they need more hands-on contact with physicians, such as showing them how a heart value is manufactured at its factory or how to operate MRI machines that are too big to move.

For instance, Medtronic of Minneapolis says it continues running programs to teach physicians how to use its new catheters on animal models such as sheep cadavers. Spokesmen couldn't say how many of these programs it runs each year, but say the company still pays some travel costs for some physicians to attend them.

''You can't learn to do that sitting in a room and watching slides," said Sheva Sanders, a company attorney who helped write the new code. The company did, however, stop handing out golf balls with its name on them at trade shows.

Joe Metzger, a spokesman for Britain's Smith & Nephew PLC, which runs a large unit in Andover making endoscopy instruments to view hollow organs, said it no longer lets managers cover doctors' entertainment expenses such as ballgame tickets. It still hands out water bottles with the company name at trade shows -- in theory they offer a patient benefit that golf balls don't.
