BUSINESS IN BRIEF

EMC reiterates second-quarter forecasts

May 6, 2004

At its annual meeting in Southborough, EMC Corp. stuck with its forecast for second-quarter sales of $1.95 billion to $1.98 billion and for revenue growth of more than 25 percent this year. The firm said April 15 that it expected profit of about 8 cents a share this quarter, matching the average analyst estimate. The sales forecast exceeded the $1.92 billion forecast by analysts then. Shareholders rejected a plan by the Sheet Metal Workers' National Pension Fund to cap pay for the Hopkinton firm's chief executive at $1 million and senior executive bonuses at 100 percent of salary. The plan also called for awarding restricted shares, not options, that wouldn't exceed $1 million on the grant date. Investors also reelected John Egan, Michael Ruettgers, and David Strohm as directors, and ratified PricewaterhouseCoopers LLP as EMC's independent auditor for 2004. (Bloomberg)

Raytheon investors want yearly elections

Shareholders of Raytheon Co. voted to elect the company's directors yearly, the fourth time in the past five annual meetings that the proposal received a majority of votes. The board hadn't acted on the previous votes and recommended rejecting the nonbinding proposal at yesterday's meeting in Washington. Board members are currently elected to staggered three-year terms. Shareholders also voted for a plan to require their approval before the company can adopt a ''poison pill" defense against hostile takeovers, with 74 percent in favor. A third proposal to treat stock options granted to executives as an expense got support from 65 percent of voters. Directors reelected were chief executive William Swanson, and John Deutch, Michael Ruettgers, and William Spivey. PricewaterhouseCoopers LLP was ratified as the company's independent auditor. (Bloomberg)

Repligen, MIT sue ImClone Systems

Repligen Corp., an unprofitable developer of treatments for autism and immune-system diseases, filed a lawsuit demanding that ImClone Systems Inc. pay royalties on sales of the cancer treatment Erbitux. Repligen and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology filed a lawsuit in federal court in Boston against ImClone, claiming that Erbitux infringes a patent for technology that increases protein production in mammalian cells. MIT owns the patent and Waltham-based Repligen is the exclusive licensee. ImClone began selling Erbitux this year, with $17.5 million in US sales in its first five weeks on the market. Analysts said the medicine may help the company reach its first full-year profit. Repligen is seeking royalties on those sales. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION
Citigroup acknowledges SEC probe

Citigroup Inc., the nation's largest financial institution, disclosed that the Securities and Exchange Commission has begun an investigation of the bank's accounting treatment of 2001 and 2002 operations in Argentina. At the time, Argentina was in financial turmoil after a four-year recession drove the country to devalue its peso and limit dollar conversions. Argentina eventually defaulted on more than $140 billion in public debt, though it has since worked out a new repayment program. Citigroup said in its quarterly filing with the SEC that the agency ''is conducting a nonpublic investigation, which the company believes originated with the company's accounting treatment regarding its investments and business activities . . . with respect to Argentina in the fourth quarter of 2001 and the first quarter of 2002." (Bloomberg)

Lockheed wins $1.6b missile contract

Lockheed Martin Corp. the largest US defense contractor, won an order to develop and build a common missile for the Army, Navy, and Marine Corps, a program the company estimates could be worth as much as $5.5 billion over 20 years. Lockheed beat Boeing Co. and Raytheon Co. in the competition initially worth as much as $1.6 billion to develop the Joint Common Missile. The order also covers the first two production batches, or as many as 2,700 of 55,000 missiles, the Army said. The military wants to cut costs by fielding a common missile. The new 70-inch missiles would be launched from Army helicopters, Navy fighters, and unmanned spy planes, and would replace Raytheon's Maverick and Lockheed's Hellfire missiles. (Bloomberg)

McDonald's CEO undergoes surgery

New McDonald's Corp. chief executive Charlie Bell underwent successful colorectal cancer surgery, less than three weeks after replacing CEO Jim Cantalupo, who died of a heart attack. Bell, 43, will remain CEO during what the company said would be a brief recovery. Spokesman Walt Riker said the company would provide no more information about Bell's condition out of respect for his privacy. Bell became the sixth CEO in the company's 49-year history on April 19. Cantalupo and Bell were both responsible for the company's recent turnaround. McDonald's said worldwide same-store sales, results in restaurants open more than a year, rose 9.4 percent in its most recent quarter, the biggest increase in nearly 20 years. (AP)

Domestic-partners insurance gaining

Private employers are introducing domestic-partner health insurance benefits to gays at the rate of three companies a day, according to a study by the educational arm of a gay rights advocacy group. The analysis by the Human Rights Campaign Foundation found more than 1,000 private employers and colleges and universities added domestic-partner benefits in 2003, or 18 percent more than the year before. About 40 percent of the Fortune 500 companies now offer domestic-partner benefits, including nearly 70 percent of the 50 top businesses. The report said 7,149 private employers and colleges and universities provided health coverage to employees' domestic partners in 2003, though that was less than 1 percent of the total number of such employers. (AP)

Metro starts N.Y. edition of free tabloid

Metro International, the global newspaper company, launched its New York edition of the free daily commuter tabloid that debuted in the United States four years ago. According to a company release, 300,000 copies of the Metro are being distributed in Manhattan and along commuter routes into the city. Metro already has papers in Philadelphia (circulation 150,000) and Boston (circulation 170,000). Metro's arrival in New York creates a new kind of newspaper war there. In October, former Boston Metro publisher Russel Pergament and the Tribune Co. launched their version of a free tabloid aimed at the youth market called amNew York. Pergament said yesterday that amNew York, with a circulation of 209,000, ''is on track." (Mark Jurkowitz)

Treasury to offer inflation-indexed notes

The Treasury Department said it is adding two securities to help finance the national debt, which recently topped $7 trillion. The department is adding a 20-year, inflation-indexed bond, which it has not offered before, and a five-year, inflation-indexed note, which was auctioned twice in 1997 and then discontinued, Treasury officials said. The first auction of the 20-year inflation-indexed bond will be held in July and the first auction for the five-year inflation-indexed note will be in October. Auctions for each security -- known as Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities, or TIPS -- will be held twice a year. These two securities will join the current inflation-indexed 10-year note now sold. (AP)

Graphic Packaging Corp. will close a folding-carton plant in Bow, N.H., by the end of 2005. The move is part of a plan to save as much as $15 million a year, the Georgia company said. (Globe wire services)
