Errors in tax forms needn't be forever

May 30, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- In some cases new laws and rulings by the IRS and courts have been retroactive, such as a boost in write-offs for business equipment and opening up a medical deduction for treating obesity. Amending a return in these situations can yield a deduction for past as well as future spending.

The IRS has also become more liberal in retroactively allowing longer than 60 days between a withdrawal of money from one IRA or retirement account and a redeposit of the funds into another tax-sheltered account.

The IRS has excused taxes and penalties and allowed more time to complete a rollover for people who suffered serious illness, a death in the family, or were thwarted by a financial firm that didn't properly carry out a transfer.

People who paid extra tax because they missed the deadline may be able to file an amended return if they get a waiver from the IRS because of extenuating circumstances.

Spouses who filed separate returns in the past because of marital discord but are now on speaking terms may be able to file an amended joint return, which often results in a lower tax bill. If emotions have cooled after a divorce, ex-spouses who filed separately before the breakup may be able to refile jointly for the years they were married. (People who originally filed jointly, however, are not allowed to later amend those returns to file separately.)

States also allow amended returns, and taxpayers who change their federal numbers typically also must do so at the state level. In Massachusetts, people who use Telefile can use a worksheet to amend their return by phone. Others use Form CA-6.

Be sure the payoff is worth the trouble, which can be considerable. You can pay $100 and often much more to have a professional do the fix-up. Saving money by navigating the 1040X yourself can be a strain. You'll have to gather information on the change, review the rules and rates in effect at the time of the original return, locate a copy of your old return, and explain on the form why a change is justified.

''I typically wouldn't bother with this unless the tax involved is at least a few hundred dollars," says Eva Rosenberg, a California tax adviser.

Don't skimp on documenting your case. Tax professionals say they often provide greater substantiation of transactions on an amended return than they do on an original one. Certified public accountant Neil Becourtney, at J. H. Cohn in Roseland, N.J., says he might include copies of property tax bills, for example, if those deductions were at issue. ''You want to avoid any doubts," he explains.

Neatness also counts, says Marvin Michelman, a tax director in New York at Deloitte & Touche accountants. Making a good impression with a legible and complete amended return can help your case in the first stage of review, he says. ''Remember a human being is going to be looking at it."

It's that human look that gives some filers the jitters. They worry that even during this time of diminished IRS auditing the nonroutine nature of an amended return will boost their chance of being examined.

Advisers share the concern, but say a taxpayer is generally safe from extra scrutiny unless the amended item involves a suspect area -- for example, a home office deduction, entertainment and business expenses, shelter investments, and big gifts of property to charity.

''If what you do on an amended return would not have signaled something for a potential audit had it been on your original return, then filing an amended return does not increase your chance of an examination," says the IRS's Burke.

Nevertheless, ''you are giving the IRS a second look at your return," cautions accountant Becourtney.
