State of bad drivers

May 30, 2004

Page full of 3 -- Why are Massachusetts drivers so bad?

ADVERTISEMENT

The question is taking on new urgency now as state officials begin efforts to revamp the Massachusetts auto insurance system. One of the chief reasons the average premium in Massachusetts costs more than $1,000 a year is the fact that drivers here file more auto insurance claims than anyone else in the country.

Getting to the root of the problem -- the key to finding a way to improve the situation -- is not so easy. Based on interviews with highway safety, insurance, and law enforcement officials, there is no single cause for the high level of accidents here. It appears to be a combination of unusually aggressive drivers, periodic bad weather, lax law enforcement, insurance fraud, and too many cars being driven on an antiquated and confusing road system.

Daniel Johnston, president of the Automobile Insurers Bureau of Massachusetts, estimates the average premium could be cut by about 20 percent if the state could just lower its claim-filing rate to the level in Rhode Island. Rhode Island ranks third-highest in the nation on claim frequency, but its rate is still about 25 percent less than Massachusetts.

''There are a lot of savings at stake," Johnston said. ''But it's almost an imponderable feat to get down to that level of accidents. The number in Massachusetts never seems to move very much."

Massachusetts has led the nation in auto insurance claims for at least two decades. In 2002, according to figures supplied by the Insurance Research Council, 7.2 property damage liability claims were filed per 100 insured cars in Massachusetts, well above the national average of 4.1.

The disparity between Massachusetts and the rest of the nation was even more pronounced for bodily injury liability claims. Massachusetts residents filed 2.5 claims per 100 insured cars, more than twice the national average.

''We are known nationwide as being a state of aggressive drivers," said Christopher Goetcheus, the spokesman for the state Division of Insurance. ''The numbers tell the story and the reality is we are bad."

Possible solutions to the problem of bad driving range from more and tougher enforcement of traffic laws; a crackdown on fraud; mandatory driver training programs; road improvements, including the elimination of rotaries; and carrot-and-stick insurance measures aimed at changing the behavior of high-risk drivers.

''There's no one thing," said Donald Bashline, an insurance industry consultant and co-owner of a Boston company called RightFind Technology, which is trying to isolate the personality traits of bad drivers. Bashline said Massachusetts is out of the mainstream when it comes to its claims experience. ''It's part of a culture here," he said.

During the 1990s, the number of registered vehicles in Massachusetts increased by 25 percent to 5.1 million, and the number of licensed drivers rose 6 percent to 4.4 million. Over that same period, the annual crash total in the state increased 13.5 percent to 189,000.

''You add all those cars and drivers to a road system that isn't growing and you're bound to have more accidents," said Brook Chipman, a spokesman for the Governor's Highway Safety Bureau.

Traffic enforcement also hasn't kept pace with the number of cars and drivers on the road, a situation insurance industry officials say can foster an indifference to traffic laws.

State Police Trooper Scott Schubert said trooper levels have held relatively steady over the years as the number of cars and drivers has increased and the duties of troopers have expanded. In April 1999, the state had 965 State Police troopers. Trooper strength peaked at 1,126 in September 2002 before subsiding to 998 in April, a net gain over the five-year period of 3.4 percent.

The state's top crash sites, as compiled by the Massachusetts Highway Department using late-1990s data, are all places where traffic is heavy and merging. Ten of the top 20 sites were highway locations. The intersection of Interstate 95 and Interstate 93 in Reading was the state's number one crash site, with 678 crashes over the 1997-1999 period.

Four of the top 20 crash sites were rotaries or circles. The state has already eliminated three of the rotaries in the top 20 and would like to eliminate all 70 in Massachusetts, even though they are making a comeback in a handful of other states where officials are convinced they promote safety by forcing drivers to slow down.

Massachusetts drivers also have to contend with road and street signs that are often confusing or nonexistent, streets that aren't laid out on grids, road junctions where five and six streets come together, every-man-for-himself intersections with flashing red lights, and potholes that can grow to the size of craters.

Larry Meister, vice president of the Independent Taxi Operators Association and a veteran of Boston's roadways, said the roads here are bad but the drivers may be worse. ''There's a lot of congestion so there's a lot of aggravation," he said. ''Drivers here take their aggression out on the pedal."

James Hodges of Hamilton, who was interviewed recently after appealing an insurance surcharge for rear-ending another car two years ago, said he came to this area from Buffalo and immediately noticed a big difference.

''The drivers were a lot more courteous in Buffalo. They're not as aggressive," he said. ''You can drive from Buffalo into Boston and see the difference. Boston is the only place I've been given an obscene gesture by another driver."

Peter Robertson, an insurance industry attorney working with Bashline to develop a bad-driver personality profile that they hope to sell to insurance companies for underwriting purposes, said there is no indication that Massachusetts has a greater concentration of bad-driver personalities than other states.

Their research has shown that drivers who get into a lot of accidents are impatient, aggressive, type-A personalities who feel they are entitled to speed and go through stop signs as long as they don't hurt anyone. Robertson said bad drivers often blame their accidents on other drivers.

James Narkewich of Burlington fits the profile. He's a Step 35 driver, the worst possible, who says he has been involved in numerous accidents and currently pays about $4,500 a year to insure his car. He recently appealed an insurance surcharge for causing another accident last year at a rotary in Woburn.

Narkewich admitted he's often not careful enough behind the wheel, but he said the accident last year was largely the fault of the other driver. He said he was recently in Florida and noticed the traffic there traveled at higher speeds with no problem. ''Everyone goes with the flow down there," he said. ''We're all uptight here. It's much more herky-jerky here."

Ivan Sever, Massachusetts coordinator for the National Motorists Association, said he thinks drivers here are no worse than in other states. Adjusting for the number of miles driven, road fatalities here are the second-lowest in the nation, behind Vermont.

Sever thinks Massachusetts drivers file more insurance claims than the residents of other states because the crowded and confusing road system is an incubator for fender benders and because the heavily regulated insurance system essentially encourages the filing of claims. ''Every little scrape gets reported," he said.

State officials openly acknowledge that fraud is a problem in Massachusetts, particularly in connection with bodily injury claims. A crackdown in Lawrence last year led to a 35 percent drop in reported accidents over a three-month period. Other cities are now beginning to launch their own crackdowns. Goetcheus at the Division of Insurance said he did not know whether Massachusetts had more insurance fraud than other states, but he said any reduction would help cut costs by reducing the number of claims.

As state officials have begun revamping the auto insurance system, their primary focus has been on reducing regulation and introducing competition. They haven't focused on the bad-driver issue, although some proposals are starting to address the problem.

Governor Mitt Romney, for example, has asked a task force to develop proposals that would charge bad drivers more when they have accidents and do away with subsidies for inexperienced drivers, primarily teens and young adults, who represent 10 percent of the population but account for 20 percent of the accidents.

The State Police recently completed a rare two-week crackdown on aggressive driving that was paired with an advertising campaign reminding drivers that speeding tickets not only carry a hefty price tag but can increase insurance premiums for years to come. The crackdown involved 1,070 hours of extra police enforcement that resulted in 2,772 citations, two-thirds of them for speeding.

Gerald Fels, executive vice president and chief financial officer at Commerce Group of Webster, the state's largest auto insurer, suggested all new drivers should be required to take driver training programs. State records indicate that in 2004 more than 35,000 new drivers had not taken driver training and that they pay significantly higher insurance rates because of the greater likelihood they will be involved in an accident.

Representative Ronald Mariano, Democrat of Quincy, chairman of the Legislature's Insurance Committee and a member of a Romney task force on auto insurance, said lawmakers are considering increasing the penalties for drivers caught speeding for the first time. Instead of just losing one safe-driver credit, officials are considering tacking on points as they do when multiple offenses occur. The points increase insurance surcharges and last six years.

''What you've got to do is take some of the aggressiveness out of the drivers in Massachusetts," he said.
