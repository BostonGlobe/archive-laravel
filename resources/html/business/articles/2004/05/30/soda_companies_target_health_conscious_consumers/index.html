Soda companies target health-conscious consumers

Soft drink firms seek boost from mid-calorie line

By Margaret Webb Pressler, Washington Post  |  May 30, 2004

Major soda companies have been spinning out new flavors for years with little success at jump-starting sales, which have been growing annually at a paltry half-percent since 1998. So the soft drink giants are trying again -- with colas aimed at the newly health-conscious, weight-watching, carb-counting consumer.

Coca-Cola and Pepsi hope for big results from the release of C2 and Pepsi Edge, which fall halfway between the zero calories and sugar of a diet soda and the calorie-packing sweetness of regular cola. The new beverages are being trumpeted as the first wave in a new category of mid-calorie soft drinks.

"It's the first new product in a long time that has a functional benefit versus just a new flavor benefit," Pepsi spokesman Dave DeCecco said. "There definitely is interest in the marketplace for that."

Industry experts are unsure whether the concept will be flat or fizzy in the minds of consumers. They say the soda market has been hurt by competition from other types of beverages, so it's hard to know how hot -- or lasting -- the new mid-calorie market will be.

Part of the problem assessing the impact of the new drinks is identifying who will buy them and how many of the guzzlers will just be defectors from other Coke or Pepsi brands.

"It could potentially cannibalize existing products," said Todd Stender, a beverage analyst with investment banking firm Crowell, Weedon & Co., which does not have an investment banking relationship with either Coke or Pepsi.

Some analysts say the mid-calorie rollouts are best seen as a good excuse, like Diet Coke with Lemon or Pepsi Vanilla before them, to get in the public's face with lots of advertising promoting the core brand.

As sodas lose ground to other drinks, the only way to fight back is to keep rattling the cage and encouraging shoppers to walk down the soda aisle again.

"It's a reason for retailers to stock more product, to be on display, and it shows that at least you're awake," said Bryan Spillane, a beverage analyst with Banc of America Securities who does not own stock in either company. Banc of America does have investment banking relationships with both Coke and Pepsi.

"It's a very low-cost way to create some news," he said. "If it works, then great. If it doesn't, it doesn't cost them a whole lot."

Pepsi Edge will be released in two stages, in mid-June and a month later, but its advertising won't begin until the product unveiling is complete.

Coke still leads Pepsi in market share, 44 to 32 percent. Yet Pepsi, which owns Frito Lay and Quaker Oats, has been better able to withstand the softness in the soda market.

Both companies say they're after anyone who is watching calories or carbohydrates. Since sugars are counted as carbs and both new sodas use a combination of high-fructose corn syrup and artificial sweeteners, the drinks are easily marketed to the millions of consumers who now try to limit their intake of carbs or calories.

"We think it's an answer to a global consumer trend," Coke spokesman Kelly Brooks said, noting the mid-calorie Coke should have particular appeal to adults in their 20s and 30s, who are young enough to be soda drinkers but old enough to be watching their weight. Company research indicates the products will bring new soda drinkers to the market, Brooks said. The goal is not just to add incremental sales volume but to "further strengthen and build the Coca-Cola trademark."

Analyst Spillane isn't holding his breath for a major boost to the soda market. The market of people who are more health conscious may be vast, he said, but mid-calorie sodas are likely to appeal most to those who are watching their calories and don't like the taste of diet sodas. "The target they're trying to reach is there, but I think it's like threading a needle," he said.

The companies are open about that to a degree. Pepsi's reasoning for its new drink focuses on "dual users": consumers who drink both diet and regular soda. "That number of dual users has increased 75 percent in the last two years," Pepsi's DeCecco said. If those consumers come from other Pepsi brands, so be it. "That's the low-hanging fruit -- people who are torn between the regular and the diet," DeCecco said. "The second target is those that have left the cola franchise altogether or maybe used to have three regular Pepsis a day but might now . . . have two Pepsis and peach iced tea."

Few analysts doubt these enormously powerful marketing engines can get that first target audience and maybe a bit more, at least at first.

"The companies do a terrific job of getting new products into stores, getting a marketing buzz," said Gary Hemphill of Beverage Marketing Corp., an industry research company. "The question is, are people going to come back again and again?"
