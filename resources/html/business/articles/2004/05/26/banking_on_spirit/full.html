Banking on spirit

Fleet staffers get lessons in Southern hospitality

By Sasha Talcott, Globe Staff  |  May 26, 2004

They pack auditoriums across the country, blasting upbeat music, clapping their hands, and dancing in the aisles. Then, as the excitement crests, a group of "Spirit leaders" in Bank of America shirts take the microphone onstage and launch into rousing speeches laced with phrases such as "customer delight." With skits and motivational videos and outsized tales of banking good deeds, the leaders are on a mission: persuading the bankers, often a staid group, to get in touch with their emotional sides.

Now, the Bank of America Spirit program -- the bubbly three-hour training seminar that the North Carolina bank credits for much of its recent success -- is coming to the Northeast.

Less than two months after Bank of America officially took over FleetBoston Financial Corp., executives are already talking up their roles in "Team Bank of America," smiling more with customers and even planning daily spirit huddles in Fleet branches.

"It really is to encourage us as associates to make more of an emotional connection with our customers, to get out of our heads and into our hearts," said Liam McGee, Bank of America's president of consumer banking. "We're in the business of making our customers' dreams come true."

They're not the only ones to think all that friendliness will pay off in profits. As mega-companies bred in places like Arkansas and North Carolina make their way up North, their cheery, down-home values are supplanting the gruff style of New England service epitomized for decades by the grouchy waitresses at Durgin-Park.

Wal-Mart is famous for its greeters, and its bouncy morning cheers. Home Depot boasts that its employees will never walk by a customer without saying hi. And Walt Disney, the model for much of Bank of America's Spirit program, sends out a small army of perky employees in Mickey Mouse and Donald Duck costumes to cuddle the youngsters in its theme parks.

"Robert Frost said, 'Good fences make good neighbors,' and I think a lot of people in New England believe that: You know your boundaries, and you keep a respectful distance from others," said Michael Sales, a principal at New Context Consulting in Newton. "Whereas some people from the West and the South can feel like, 'Boundaries? Whoever heard of that? Let's get close and hug each other.' "

But as Bank of America works to establish itself in the Northeast, a few jaded Northerners are already taking the Charlotte bank to task.

"I swear to God; it's Muffy with her pompons," said Colin Yapp, a former employee at Banc of America Securities in New York, who said he quit several weeks ago in part because he did not like the Spirit program. "They're all so perky. They look like they're going to jump out of their skins."

Bank of America is forging ahead with its cultural shift, however, and it has even gone so far as to pass out a glossary of terms to help Fleet employees adapt. Here are some translations of the company lexicon: The "Associate Portal" is actually the name of the company's Intranet site. Then there's the "Knowledge Channel," Bank of America's closed-circuit video network that broadcasts live meetings around the world, and "Hoshin Planning," the new bank's planning process.

Some Fleet employees have begun to joke that they know instantly when their managers receive cushy jobs in the new Bank of America: The lucky bosses begin to send out e-mails laced with the ubiquitous "customer delight."

Like Disney, Bank of America's Spirit program creates a distinction between "onstage" (facing customers) and "offstage" (on breaks).

When onstage, employees are required to wear their nametags, smile, and be energetic. Away from customers in the break room, they are free to let down their guards. The bank even warns employees that if they have trouble demonstrating the bank's motto of "Higher Standards," they should remove their nametags, Bank of America shirts, and other "branding" when they leave work.

"We were expecting something big -- they had their high standards -- and they got to us," said Berardino D'Onofrio, a senior business specialist at the Fleet branch in Harvard Square, who recently attended a Bank of America Spirit session in Boca Raton, Fla. "It's like the meeter-greeter program that's been going on for so many years, but on a different level. It ultimately should create a memorable experience for the customers."

The bank reinforces its Spirit program with morning huddles in the branches, where the manager "reminds associates of the desire to connect with customers," said McGee, the Bank of America consumer president. Some branches have multiple Spirit huddles a day.

Bank representatives declined requests to view one of the morning Spirit huddles, and said more of the giant rallies are not planned until the summer. But the bank did make available several Fleet and Bank of America employees to describe the events.

Bank of America insists that its spirit program is paying off. By its own measure, "customer delight" -- people who rank the bank a nine or a 10 on a one-through-10 scale -- has increased to 52 percent from 43 percent at the end of 2001. Delighted customers are three times more likely to do business with the bank and four times more likely to refer Bank of America to their friends and family, the bank's statistics show.

"I love this idea that you are onstage when a customer is in front of you, and you've got to make sure every interaction is a positive interaction," said Dianne Durkin, president of Loyalty Factor, a specialized training and consulting firm in Portsmouth, N.H. "Fleet has never had that type of mentality. They say in many of the branches, 'We can't do that. It's not our policy,' versus 'This is what the customer needs. We should have a policy like that.' "

Bank of America's rivals in New England are not impressed. Citizens Financial Group, New England's second-largest bank, said it has long encouraged employees to attend its two-day "Wow" customer-service training, stocks some branches with Hershey's kisses and lollypops, and has even asked tellers to wear nametags with conversation-starters like "My daughter just got into college."

Sovereign Bancorp's "Red Carpet Service" program offers to pay customers $5 if they find a broken ATM or wait in line more than five minutes.

Before it puts up new signs on branches later this year, Bank of America plans to train all Fleet employees in the Spirit program. A few hundred of the bank's top-performing employees already received a preview in Florida before the banks' merger closed.

Based on that experience, Bank of America executives say they will have no problem persuading its New England workforce to brighten their smiles.

Other companies that have implemented similar customer-service programs agree. Representatives from both Home Depot and Wal-Mart say they did not modify their training program in any way when they opened stores in New England. "Wal-Mart associates are terrific everywhere you go," said Sharon Weber, a Wal-Mart spokeswoman.

Bank of America hopes the same will hold true for its new Northeast workers. When Fleet employees complete the Spirit program, the bank will pass out red-and-white cards with a reminder of how the new company expects them to act.

"The Bank of America Spirit is the innate, heartfelt best in each of us, motivated by pride and enhanced by teamwork and diversity," the card reads. "When we unleash our spirit every day, we humanize banking, enhance our communities, and create memorable experiences for each other, our customers and our clients."
