Artery greenway plans far behind schedule

Delays may lead to razing of roads

By Thomas C. Palmer Jr., Globe Staff  |  May 7, 2004

Plans for a prominent portion of the Rose Kennedy Greenway are so far behind schedule that the streets bordering the parks will be completed first -- and may have to be demolished and redone to accommodate the final park designs.

At a meeting of the Mayor's Central Artery Completion Task Force yesterday, a Massachusetts Turnpike Authority official said roadway construction is set to begin in about two weeks and it's too late for a proposed substitution of cobblestones for asphalt on the streets that cross the new corridor replacing the Central Artery.

It would be cheaper to lay down asphalt and tear it up next year when the parks are built than to change a multi-million- dollar construction contract that has already been altered several times due to ever-changing park designs, said Fred Yalouris, director of architecture for the Turnpike.

Delays in determining details of the Wharf District blocks, from Christopher Columbus Park down to High Street, have resulted from the continuing absence of a clear long-term owner and overseer for the 30 acres of public space, members of the task force agreed yesterday.

Instead, the Massachusetts Turnpike Authority and the City of Boston have dueled for years over control of the process, which is being dominated by the Turnpike, manager of the $14.6 billion Big Dig highway and tunnel project.

"So much of the detail has to do with operation and maintenance" of the parks, said Rob Tuchmann, co-chairman of the task force, a group that monitors and informally advises the park designers. "We gotta, gotta, gotta have operation and maintenance' done soon -- like yesterday."

Almost two years after a proposal to create a trust to fund and manage the newly created open space collapsed, city and Turnpike officials continue to bicker over design details.

Yesterday, Richard Garver, deputy director of planning at the Boston Redevelopment Authority, and the city's top representative at the task force meeting, objected when the professional design team working on the Wharf District suggested putting cobblestones instead of pavement on the surfaces of streets between the parks, including State and Milk.

Many participants in the design process support distinctive paving -- something other than asphalt -- on east-west streets crossing the corridor, as a means of emphasizing the historic wharfs that once dominated the city's waterfront. But Garver said there would be additional maintenance costs to the city if cobblestones were added, and he said city officials were not included in any discussion of the possible change and consequent costs. Turnpike officials did not know what it would cost to replace the pavement.

"We have a problem here," said Yalouris. "We have already made enormously costly changes."

Asked about the possibility of adding cobblestones now, Yalouris said, "It would be cheaper to do it afterwards, actually."

Already, a tunnel access structure on the surface, which was required before the tunnel could open, is being relocated because it is in the way of a recently proposed park feature, a north-south pedestrian walkway. The cost is significant, Yalouris said, but Turnpike officials yesterday could not offer a dollar figure.

Meanwhile, the evolving Wharf District parks designs drew stiff criticism from the Design Advisory Committee for the Greenway, a group of professionals chosen by the city and the Turnpike to ensure that what replaces the old elevated highway is of high quality and there is consistency along the mile-long corridor.

The committee, led by Gary Hack, dean of the University of Pennsylvania School of Design, has been meeting and quietly reviewing the near-complete designs for the two blocks of North End parks, as well as the four blocks in the substantial Wharf District.

Along the Wharf region, "we believe that too many things are being attempted in too little space," Hack wrote in an April 30 letter to Turnpike chairman Matthew J. Amorello.

The four-page letter, assessing current plans by the design team EDAW, listed a promenade, a border of dense plantings, a two-block "City Room" public gathering area, a tidal pool, a landmark fountain in the shape of a nautilus, and "a variety of interpretative devices incorporated in paving and the landscape."

"And perhaps more items that we have missed," the letter concluded. "This is quite a lot for a small area!"

"This is the point in the project where each element needs to be given hard-nosed scrutiny," the committee's letter about the Wharf District said, adding that "we are not suggesting how the choices should be made -- simply that they need to be made ...."

The Design Advisory Committee, which some thought could bring together the battling forces of the Turnpike and city planners, made some modest suggestions for improvement in the North End park designs. It is unclear whether or how their suggestions for improvements will be incorporated into the corridor's conceptual designs.

Despite ongoing differences over details like the types and placement of trees and shrubs, and of paving surfaces and lighting structures, the broad landscape designs for all three regions of the corridor -- the North End, the Wharf District, and the Chinatown/Leather District -- are nearly wrapped up.

Among other uncertainties surrounding the Surface Artery corridor design process is the role of a renowned urban design specialist hired by city officials to ensure that the emerging parks fit in with the surrounding city blocks.

Ken Greenberg, cqprincipal of Greenberg Consultants Inc., of Toronto has recently offered his analysis of how neighborhoods can be connected and new public squares created along the corridor, if cross streets are sufficiently emphasized. The presentations have been well received, but subjecting the existing park designs to any significant reconsideration or change could be costly and drawn out, further postponing completion of the public space.

The Big Dig is scheduled to be completed in 2005, and the public and open spaces connected with the project were supposed to be done by then.

In the April 2004 annual report of the Central Artery Environmental Oversight Committee, which tracks the project's commitments, executive director Anne Fanton wrote that in the Wharf District, "Disagreements over the design goals and details have made progress difficult. Construction is hoped for in 2006."
