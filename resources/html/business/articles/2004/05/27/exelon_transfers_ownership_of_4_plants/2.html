Page 2 of 2 -- Microsoft to sell portable music players

ADVERTISEMENT

Microsoft Corp., the world's largest software maker, will begin selling portable music players for as much as 80 percent less than Apple Computer Inc.'s iPod. The Microsoft-branded devices will ''look and feel" as good as the iPod for as little as $50, said Yusuf Mehdi, corporate vice president of MSN at Microsoft, at the Goldman Sachs 5th Annual Internet Conference in Las Vegas. The iPod sells for $249 to $499. Microsoft will release a number of music players when it launches its online music service this year, giving customers more choices than Apple, Mehdi said. Apple's second-quarter shipments of iPods rose more than eightfold from a year earlier, helping to triple its profit. (Bloomberg)

Two more Rite Aid executives sentenced

Two former Rite Aid Corp. executives were sentenced to prison for their roles in a financial scandal at the drugstore chain four years ago. The company's former chief financial officer, Franklyn M. Bergonzi, was sentenced by US District Judge Sylvia H. Rambo to 28 months in prison and fined $5,000 for his guilty plea to one count of conspiracy. ''I just want you to know that my actions were not actions of greed," Bergonzi told Rambo. ''I let down the company for which I spent 30 years of my life helping to build." Prosecutors said Bergonzi conspired to commit an accounting fraud that inflated the value of Rite Aid stock. Rambo also sentenced former Rite Aid vice president Philip Markovitz to one month in jail and five months of home confinement, and fined him $5,000. ''I cannot tell you how deeply I regret my misconduct, nor how ashamed I feel at disappointing people who mean so much to me," Markovitz told the judge. Eric S. Sorkin, former vice president for pharmacy purchasing, on Tuesday was sentenced to five months in prison and five months of house arrest for one count of conspiring to obstruct justice. (AP)

Md. governor signs tougher antispam bill

Internet spam purveyors who hide behind false e-mail addresses could face up to 10 years in jail and fines of $25,000 per day under a state law signed by Maryland Governor Robert Ehrlich. The Maryland Spam Deterrence Act allows state officials to arrest and fine those who engage in a variety of deceptive tactics to send junk e-mail. The law was hailed as the nation's toughest by Internet provider America Online, which has helped officials in other states track down spammers who send out fraudulent messages. A previous Maryland law allowed consumers to sue spammers directly, but it was voided by the national Can Spam Act, which took effect in January. However, Andrew Barrett, executive director of the antispam watchdog group Spamcon, said the law would do little to stop unsolicited bulk messages because most of those already violate antifraud laws. (Reuters)

Report of ex-Tyco lawyer's pay at issue

The onetime head of human resources at Tyco International Ltd. testified she was told not to include the compensation of Mark A. Belnick, the company's former top lawyer, in the company's 2000 proxy. Patricia Prue, the conglomerate's former vice president of human resources, said L. Dennis Kozlowski, Tyco's former chairman and chief executive, told her he preferred the compensation of its operating presidents, not staff members, be in the proxy. Belnick is on trial in New York State Supreme Court in Manhattan, charged with grand larceny, securities fraud, and falsifying business records in connection with as much as $32 million in bonuses and loans he received while working at Tyco. (Dow Jones/AP)

Sun Life to liquidate 11 mutual funds

Sun Life Financial Inc., Canada's second-biggest insurer by assets, plans to liquidate 11 mutual funds that have only $100 million in combined investments. The funds will be eliminated Aug. 6, the company said in a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. Funds with assets of less than $100 million find it hard to make a profit after paying for salaries and marketing costs. Sun Life owns MFS Investment Management of Boston. (Bloomberg)
