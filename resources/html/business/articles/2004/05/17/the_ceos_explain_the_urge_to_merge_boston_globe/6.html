The CEOs explain the urge to merge

May 17, 2004

Page 6 of 11 -- GLOBE: It’s not just real estate prices, though —

ADVERTISEMENT

D’ALESSANDRO: It’s the real estate prices, it’s the quality of the workforce and what you have to pay them. The quality of the workforce is quite good here but you have to pay for it. And there’s no reason that has to happen. So the business climate in my view is going to be the ability of people to understand and capture this patchwork quilt that we put together. ... And we need to see people like the hedge-fund managers and mutual fund companies step out more and be more a part of this community because for guys that make as much money as they do they are as penurious as the day is long when it comes to community giving, when it comes to political involvement. The mutual fund companies, with the exception of a couple of very large ones, there are a lot of small investment companies in this town now that have cleaved for a long time and I see these guys on the weekend in the suburbs and ...they’re not involved enough in Boston. So it’s a mini-lecture.

GIFFORD: I couldn’t agree more. It’s the same old suspects supporting this community. It’s Fleet. It’s John Hancock. It’s Fidelity. And I know some people say it’s not going to go away; well we’ve responded that I don’t believe it is going to go away. New England is a little microcosm of the United States. The big issue is the outsourcing of foreign jobs. You go to India you get better product, better work quality, at a heck of a lot cheaper price.

GLOBE: Outsourcing has obviously been an issue at Bank of America. Do you think customers locally will react negatively?

GIFFORD: I think customers will react with the service that is provided to them ... When you’re a company you invest in your strength. The strength of this economy in New England economy, as David said, is not a large manufacturing base... The strength of the economy, however, is the small spinoffs coming through either the teaching hospitals or MIT or some of the universities. That’s what we need to capitalize on. They also happen to be potentially very high-paying jobs. And I happen to believe, unlike 10 years ago, that the leadership in the Commonwealth understands and has done a pretty darned good job.

GLOBE: Do you think the government should be doing something specific?

D’ALESSANDRO: The governor I think’s doing a good job of trying to grasp what I was just talking about. If you eliminate the universities and hospitals of Boston, you have Toledo. That’s what you have. You have Toledo. ... And what people don’t really recognize is, for example, Partners Health Care is the second-largest employer in the state, next to the state government. And what I think the governor’s starting to do, which I think is right, is beginning to understand that asset. That set of assets.   Continued...
