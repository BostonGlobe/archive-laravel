The CEOs explain the urge to merge

May 17, 2004

Page 9 of 11 -- GIFFORD: There are so many eclectic and interesting people and institutions here that whether your interest is medicine, education, business, sports, culture. You’ve got it all in an incredibly historic respected city. Go anywhere and how many people just find Boston interesting. It is a fun place to be. On the other side, we’re parochial. We’re tribal. ... I agree with him. If I answered first I would say much of what he said. In Boston, it doesn’t matter whether you’re in business, politics, sports, you name it, You rise to a position of prominence, somebody somehow figures out where to put a bull’s-eye on you.

And instead of celebrating or trying to do that, we ought to spend more time celebrating how rich this place is. It’s some of the huge successes we have seen. We don’t do enough of that.

D’ALESSANDRO: It’s a very unusual place, too, if you think about it. This a city or state that’s considered extraordinarily liberal and yet we will have had, by the time Romney’s term ends, 16 years of a Republican in the governorship. You ask most people in the United States is the governor of Massachusetts a Democrat or a Republican? Most will say a Democrat. How many places do you have a Mormon governor, a very Italian-American mayor, and you have a tremendous smattering of individuals that are unlike some other places. You got [John] Silber and [Larry] Summers... You’ve got guys like John Henry, right? and Bob Kraft. You have this group of people that are just unlike many other cities. There’s no sameness here.

GIFFORD: I’ve gotten into a conversation with a couple of leading medical docs. There are an incredible number of unknown investment management operations that you probably never heard of whose compensation — you guys like to write about our compensation — we can’t pay their taxes and they need to engage more but they’re also fascinating people and they’re very influential in this country and they’re all over these buildings.

D’ALESSANDRO: The fact is there are literally hundreds if not thousands of these people that are making a great deal of money but have not been on the spot to be more involved in the community. You talk about the industry here. There is a subterranean industry that has been mutated in the last 25 years off of our companies and off of mutual fund companies. The hedge fund players, the guys that cleaved out of Harvard, there’s thousands of those venture capital firms. That is a subindustry here that I never see anyone talk about because they can’t put their arms around it. And they prefer it that way. They want the anonymity. I can tell because... They have a lot a lot of people youngish people 35 to 40 years old with whales on their pants. There are all these investment management people. They’ll introduce themselves and they live in $10 million, $15 million houses and have places in Nantucket. There’s a lot of those people around.   Continued...
