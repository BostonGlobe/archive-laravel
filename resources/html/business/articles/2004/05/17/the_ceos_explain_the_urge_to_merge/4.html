The CEOs explain the urge to merge

May 17, 2004

Page 4 of 11 -- D’ALESSANDRO: This culture thing is overrated... There aren’t that many organizations that have cultures that are peculiar. There are few, certainly. And they tend to be peculiar because when they have been family-owned, for example, or entrepreneurally owned. Most institutions that are public companies have a blending. The thing you have to get used to is where the power bases truly are. Because they’re not on paper. You find in one company for example is that the human resource department is a lot more powerful in how they operate. In Manulife’s case, they were also founded in the 1800s. Most insurance companies were founded in that period of time and blossomed after the two big wars of the 20th Century, are not dissimilar. Both companies [Hancock and Manulife] were full of quants, quantification people. There’s no shortage of people with mathematical backgrounds. It’s just part of the nature of the beast and there’s no shortage of people that are selling and marketing people...The differences [between Manulife and Hancock] are that in each company the power bases are somewhat different. I think the power base with Manulife is a very, very strong chief financial officer and a very strong investment division. They pay a great deal of attention to the finite numbers. While I thought Hancock did, Manulife is a very, very disciplined financial enterprise. That causes a whole series of different meetings that take place. You’re suddenly you’re taking some things for granted in the past in terms of product pricing, as an example... You’re talking about culture that’s what I see as one of the differences. How do things get done in a company.

GIFFORD: With Fleet and BankBoston there were clearly different cultures.

GLOBE: And that created problems.

GIFFORD: It created adjustments ... Fleet was clearly more disciplined ... very analytical.

D’ALESSANDRO: ... The first day I had a meeting with Manulife senior executives and the Hancock senior executives. First thing out of my mouth was we’re not going to have a discussion about cultures because as far as I’m concerned there’s only one culture, it’s called Manulife. If anyone ever says that’s not how we do things at John Hancock I will fire you. And so for the last eight months we haven’t had any cultural problems. And that’s because you get into these power struggles because people start saying things like, `That may be how you do things but we don’t do it that way.’ And I said to people, `Look, we’re not going to take the best of both cultures because I don’t know what that means.’ Organizations need to run at a certain level of focus and discipline and I think one of the reason mergers don’t work sometimes is you have both organizations struggling with power structure to do things the way they always have and individuals who don’t get on the train have buckled.   Continued...
