Hancock names mutual funds unit chief

ADVERTISEMENT

John Hancock Financial Services Inc., the Boston life insurer that was bought by Canada's Manulife Financial Corp., named James A. Shepherdson chairman and chief executive of its mutual funds unit. Shepherdson, who will take up his post immediately, had been president of retirement services, the Boston company said. He replaces Maureen Ford Goldfarb, who decided to resign after the merger. Shepherdson will report to John DesPrez III, senior executive vice president of John Hancock Wealth Management. (Bloomberg)

Gillette signs English soccer star for ads

David Beckham, the England soccer captain and international "style icon" has signed a three-year marketing agreement to promote Gillette razors and blades, the company said. The 29-year-old Real Madrid midfielder will be featured in Gillette's worldwide consumer advertising and promotional campaigns for its grooming products. Gillette did not reveal financial terms, but spokeswoman Michelle Szynal said it was nowhere near the $70 million that had been rumored in London. Ads with Beckham using Gillette's new M3Power micropowered shaving system will appear in newspapers and magazines, on billboards and on TV, in a "Feeling of Gillette" campaign starting in July in Europe and later in the United States. (Reuters)

NStar extends air conditioner trade-in

NStar Electric said it is extending through Saturday a deal under which customers bringing in old, functioning air conditioners to Yale Electric in Dorchester can get a $75 rebate toward a new energy-efficient air conditioner certified by the government's EnergyStar program. People visiting Yale Electric's Freeport Street store, near the Southeast Expressway, must bring an NStar bill to prove they are a customer to take advantage of the program between 10 a.m. and 3 p.m. today and tomorrow. (Peter J. Howe)

Joint venture wins $959m Navy contract

Lockheed Martin Corp. and General Dynamics Corp. received contracts worth as much as $959.3 million combined for the design and early construction of a class of small, fast ships capable of fighting in shallow waters, the US Navy said. Raytheon Co. was eliminated from the competition as the Navy narrowed the field of bidders to two from three. The Navy plans to spend $6 billion through fiscal 2009 to research, develop, and build the first 13 Littoral Combat Ships, a new stealth vessel that can fight submarines and small attack craft. The total program may call for as many as 60 ships and cost as much as $12 billion, said Ronald O'Rourke, naval analyst for the nonpartisan Congressional Research Service. General Dynamics' Bath Iron Works unit in Bath, Maine, got a contract worth as much as $536 million for a 16-month final design study and options for construction of two vessels based on its design, the Navy said. Included in that is $78.7 million awarded for continued design work. (Bloomberg)

Pilots lose appeal on retirement plan

US Airways Group Inc., the seventh-largest US airline, won a federal Appeals Court ruling upholding its cancellation of a retirement plan for more than 7,000 active and retired pilots. The 4th US Circuit Court of Appeals in Richmond kept in place lower court rulings that allowed US Airways to end the pilots' pension plan in March 2003. Ending the plan helped US Airways slash $1.9 billion in costs and exit bankruptcy the following month. "Reinstating the large unfunded pension liability on US Airways at this stage would undermine the conditions that the company's lenders placed upon their loans," chief Judge J. Harvie Wilkinson wrote in the opinion. The ruling leaves intact US Airways' bankruptcy recovery plan, which gave Alabama's state pension fund 37 percent of the company's shares, the Air Line Pilots Association 19 percent, other workers 11 percent and management-level employees 8 percent. (Bloomberg)
