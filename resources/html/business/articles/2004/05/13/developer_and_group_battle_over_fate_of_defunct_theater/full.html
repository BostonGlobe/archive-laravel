Developer and group battle over fate of defunct theater

By Chris Reidy, Globe Staff  |  May 13, 2004

The developer of a proposed high-end apartment tower that was embroiled in a debate last year over the definition of an acre is now in a dispute over the definition of a theater.

ADVERTISEMENT

Kensington Investment Co. wants to put up a tower with 346 rental apartments on the corner of Washington and LaGrange streets near Chinatown, on the site of the Glass Slipper strip club and of the defunct Gaiety Theatre, which opened in 1908. Before the theater can be torn down, Kensington needs to apply for a demolition permit from the city's Department of Inspectional Services.

A group calling itself Gaiety Theatre Friends, which is hoping to save the theater before the wrecking ball arrives, wrote a letter to the department last month claiming that issuing a demolition permit would violate a city zoning code provision designed to protect historic theaters in the Theater District.

Kensington hasn't yet applied for a demolition permit, but it has taken some steps needed to qualify for the permit, such as removing asbestos and standing water from the building. Once an application is filed, the department would conduct an inspection of the site before deciding to allow the demolition to proceed, said department spokeswoman Lisa Timberlake.

In his own letter to the department, Matthew J. Kiefer, the attorney for the Residences at Kensington Place, argued the Gaiety no longer meets the code's definition of a theater because it hasn't been used as one in nearly 20 years.

If Kensington was confident of getting a demolition permit, it wouldn't be arguing that the Gaiety isn't a theater, said Lee Eiseman, the clerk of Gaiety Theatre Friends. ''According to our reading of the code, the theater doesn't need to be in use to qualify as a theater," he said. ''It's a strategy on the developer's part to say it's not a theater."

Eiseman said if the demolition permit were granted, the Gaiety Theatre Friends would likely not pursue the matter in court. ''We're not a litigious group," he said.

The Kensington project has already been approved by the Boston Redevelopment Authority and the Boston Zoning Commission. In addition, the Boston Landmarks Commission said last year: ''We feel the theater is worthy of preservation, but we don't believe it rises to the level of landmark status."

According to Kiefer, the issue of whether the Gaiety should be saved has already been addressed, and he expects that the argument of the Gaiety friends will carry little weight with Inspectional Services and that a demolition permit will be granted.

Eiseman disagreed. Earlier hearings focused on how tall a tower Kensington could build and on whether the Gaiety qualified as a landmark. ''The issue of whether you can demolish a theater hasn't been argued before," he said.

The Kensington project is no stranger to controversy. Its proposed tower is 290 feet high, above the neighborhood height limit of 155 feet for projects smaller than an acre.

Developers can sometimes negotiate an exception to the height limit if the footprint of a project is one acre or more. To reach that acre threshold, Kensington stretched its boundaries to include city sidewalks and streets, something critics claimed was unprecedented. That interpretation of an acre touched off two lawsuits in state Land Court, which are still pending. Kiefer said both are without merit.

By the 1970s, Kiefer said, the Gaiety was mostly a movie house, one that sometimes showed ''porn films." The intent of the zoning code, he said, was to protect theaters ''important to the city's cultural life." Developing an apartment building that will set aside 61 units of affordable housing serves the city far better than saving a rundown theater with a dubious past, Kiefer said.

At ArtsBoston, a group that promotes artists from the Boston Symphony to small dance troupes, executive director Catherine Peterson noted that the city has been involved in preserving several downtown theaters. The Paramount Theatre got a facelift from Millennium Partners-Boston, developer of the Ritz-Carlton Towers at Boston Common. And Clear Channel Entertainment helped restore the Opera House, which is set to reopen in July.

With resources scarce, preservationists have had to prioritize which theaters can be saved. While some theaters have attracted benefactors, no one is coming up with any money for the Gaiety, said Peterson, adding, ''I haven't heard any of our members clamoring to use Gaiety's space."

''You only have to look inside to see that it's a theater, and the whole purpose of zoning and planning for that area for the last 15 years has been to restore and revitalize theaters like this one," Shirley Kressel, one lawsuit plaintiff, said of the Gaiety.

Kensington still faces the issue of the Glass Slipper, which continues to operate. The BRA is now trying to take the club by eminent domain to help Kensington move ahead with its tower. Attempts to reach the Glass Slipper's owner and attorney were unsuccessful.
