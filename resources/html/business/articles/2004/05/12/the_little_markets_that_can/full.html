The little markets that can

Being friendly, calling customers by name, and having great locations make these grocery stores competitive

By Joe Yonan, Globe Staff  |  May 12, 2004

Washington Street in the South End is chockablock with juxtapositions: low-income housing developments across the street from million-dollar loft spaces, and a restaurant that serves sauteed pea tendrils next to a dollar store that sells frilly wall hangings labeled "Grandma's House."

The contrasts in this still-diverse section of a gentrified neighborhood continue right into the aisles of Foodie's Urban Market.

Miracle Whip sits two shelves below a bottle of aged balsamic vinegar that is 10 times the price. Truffle oil is right above Pam spray, simple queso

mexicano is almost adjacent to black peppercorn pecorino, and, in the takeout section, rice and beans are offered along with flank steak and grilled asparagus. "We feel like we need to be just what most stores don't like to be -- and that's all things to all people," said David Erickson, the market's cheese and specialties buyer.

In the Boston area, some small, independent markets have managed to succeed by carving out a niche in the neighborhoods. Store owners at Foodie's -- and at Cambridge's Fresh Pond Market and Jamaica Plain's City Feed and Supply -- say a friendly attitude, a focus on customer service, a location

near pedestrian commuter routes, and smart product choices have helped keep them competitive against stores 10 times their size, some only a mile or two away. As the median space of supermarkets nationwide hovers around 44,000 square feet (up from 35,000 a decade ago) and chains represent an ever-larger share, small markets struggle to show that they still have a place. Despite consolidation in the industry, analysts say competition is alive and well. "Small retailers not only compete with supermarket chains and other independent operators but with everyone who sells food, and that can be drug stores,

club stores, convenience stores, sometimes evenbookstores," said Michael Sansolo, senior vice president of the Food Marketing Institute, a trade association of food retailers and wholesalers.

This isn't lost on a store like Foodie's. Opened by Victor Leon in 1998 directly across from the Cathedral of the Holy Cross, the shop took about 3 1/2 years to show a profit. After the 17,000-square-foot Wollaston's Market closed in 2001, Foodie's became the neighborhood's only full-service grocer; the nearest supermarkets are a Shaw's in the Prudential Center and a Stop & Shop at South Bay. What's working against small markets are economies of scale. With only 8,500 square feet, Foodie's can't store -- or move -- the kinds of quantities that allow other stores to lower prices.

"Sometimes you feel like it's you against the world, in terms of the economics," said Leon, 54, a real estate developer. Leon is plowing the profits back into the business with an ambitious renovation that is scheduled for completion this fall.

The store has added an organic produce section and expanded their cheeses. Produce and butcher work that previously had been done on the other side of a wall have been brought into the open.

Elizabeth Cornaro, who oversees Foodie's marketing, is committed to the full spectrum of neighborhood customers. While the gourmet products generate a profit, for example, at the end of the month, when low-income people are short on food stamps, the store often runs specials. Even smaller than Foodie's is the venerable Fresh Pond Market in Cambridge, where brothers Marc and Crosby Najarian are running a business that their late grandfather, Nishan Semonian, built in 1930.

Fresh Pond Market is the kind of place where the owners know everyone's families. "The big stores are so impersonal," Marc, 57, said on a recent weekday afternoon. As if on cue, a customer turned the corner, and he rushed over, said, "Paula, I'm so sorry to hear about your dad," and gave her a bear hug.

The Najarians have a distinct financial advantage over many small markets: They own the building, as well as the block across the street. At least one of the brothers, and usually both, works at the store each of the six days a week it is open. And they still accept "house charges"; some are decades old. "Fifty years ago, there were loads of these stores, four or five in the same neighborhood," said Marc. "You would go to the guy who treated you the best." Nowadays, the 4,000-square-foot market is the only such game in the immediate neighborhood, selling beer and wine in addition to groceries to a now largely affluent community. A Whole Foods Market anchors the Fresh Pond Shopping Center nearby, and just down Huron Avenue is the upscale Formaggio Kitchen. At Fresh Pond, the spice aisle carries the pricey Morton & Bassett line as well as good old McCormick and Durkee brands.

In Jamaica Plain, the tiny City Feed and Supply strives for its own kind of balance, tilting toward natural foods but not excluding Doritos and Coke. Neighborhood couple Kristine Cortese and David Warner opened in 2000 after seeing a vacant storefront. They were pining for a good cup of coffee and failed to convince any of their friends to do what they ended up doing themselves.

The couple got a good deal on the rent, partly because it's not on heavily commercial Centre Street. The location has another bonus: Cortese and Warner never have to worry that another store may open to compete with them. City Feed, in a residential zone, is grandfathered in.

They bank heavily on commuter traffic to and from Stony Brook MBTA station, selling coffee, espresso drinks, sandwiches, groceries, pastries, and bread, and offering a little seating area, local artwork, and a jampacked bulletin board. "It's a little convenience store that's not a 7-Eleven," said nearby resident Caitlin Waldron, 24, carrying an iced coffee and bottle of water for her classes at the University of Massachusetts at Boston.

Even without lottery tickets and cigarettes, the store showed a profit within 18 months of opening. The only thing that keeps City Feed from being a full-fledged grocer is the lack of fresh meats.

"Everything comes in big cases geared toward the family consumer, and we can't deal in those kind of sizes," Warner said. "We can't sell a four-pound pack of chicken. Our primary customers are singles and couples. They want, like, two breasts."

Like Foodie's and Fresh Pond Market, the store carries an assortment of high- and low-end products. It has a special fondness for those made locally, such as Toscanini's ice cream, Deborah's Kitchen jams, Jamaica Plain Honey, and Haley House cookies. And every July, the coffee counter gets cleared to make room for organic produce from Stillman's Greenhouses and Farm Stand of New Braintree.

On every aisle, little pads and pens allow customers to jot down product ideas. "We'll try carrying anything. We'll buy a case of it, try to sell it, see how it goes over," Warner said.

"Almost anything," his wife corrected him. "We didn't get that slushy machine."

Slushes or not, the business is such a hit that Cortese and Warner have considered the possibility of trying to replicate the formula, but worry about imposing their own philosophy on a neighborhood where they don't live. "That would be a matter of projecting, rather than reflecting," Cortese said.
