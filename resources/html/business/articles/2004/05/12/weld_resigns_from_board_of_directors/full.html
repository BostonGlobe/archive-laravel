BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Weld resigns from board of directors

May 12, 2004

THE REGION

Former Massachusetts Governor William F. Weld has stepped down as a director of Affiliated Managers Group Inc. of Beverly, the investment firm where Lieutenant Governor Kerry Healey's husband, Sean M. Healey, is president. The disclosure was made in AMG's proxy statement, filed with the US Securities and Exchange Commission. Weld, a partner in a New York private equity firm, Leeds Weld & Co., said in an interview that he is leaving because he is too busy to serve on additional boards. ''That's the only reason," he said. Weld has been an AMG director since 1997. (Beth Healy)

Land purchase to help BC's credit rating

Boston College's $99.4 million purchase of land from the Archdiocese of Boston should help make the college more competitive and benefit its long-term credit, Moody's Investors Service said. BC agreed to purchase the property adjacent to the school to use for expansion. ''Based on the plans to complete the purchase without any new debt, we believe that this transaction will enhance BC's competitive and financial position over the long run," Moody's said. BC is funding the purchase out of unrestricted funds, which have doubled over the past year due to good investment performance, Moody's said. Moody's rates $479 million of the college's debt Aa3, the fourth-highest level, with a ''negative" outlook, meaning a rating downgrade is possible. (Bloomberg)

Report backs power generators' actions

The operator of New England's power grid has concluded there was no market abuse involving a high number of shutdowns by natural gas-fired generators during a January cold snap. ISO-New England had warned of potential rolling blackouts that never occurred and Connecticut Attorney General Richard Blumenthal accused the grid and generators of acting ''deceptively and misleadingly." During the cold spell Jan. 14 to 16, real-time prices approached $1,000 a megawatt-hour, about 20 times average, and an unspecified number of generators shut down to sell their gas supplies that were trading at record highs. In a report released yesterday, the grid operator said the actions of gas-fired generators were ''generally consistent with expected competitive behavior." (Bloomberg)

Harvard manager seeks change at fund

Harvard Management Co., which runs Harvard University's $23.5 billion endowment, filed a shareholder resolution to remove Deutsche Investment Management Americas Inc. as manager of the closed-end Korea Fund Inc. Harvard Management said Deutsche's efforts to cut the discount at which the fund trades haven't been adequate, according to a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. Harvard Management president Jack Meyer declined to comment. Harvard, which has been a Korea Fund shareholder for four years, owns 9.9 million shares, or about 22 percent of the fund, according to the SEC filing. The 20-year-old, $886 million fund, managed by John Lee, trades at a 15 percent discount, according to Bloomberg data. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

FTC chairman Muris stepping down

Federal Trade Commission Chairman Timothy J. Muris is stepping down and the Bush administration has tapped a former Justice Department official to replace him. Muris held the job for three years. It was not immediately clear why he decided to leave. The disclosure of his resignation was included in a list of nominees for various posts put out by the White House. Deborah Majoras, a former Justice Department deputy assistant attorney general, was picked to succeed him. (AP)

Ex-Warnaco CEO to pay $1.3m to settle

Former Warnaco Group chief executive Linda Wachner agreed to relinquish $1.3 million of her $6 million 1998 bonus and former company auditor PricewaterhouseCoopers will pay a $2.4 million civil fine to settle federal regulators' allegations of false financial reporting by the big apparel maker. The Securities and Exchange Commission accused the company of securities fraud for issuing a ''false and misleading" press release regarding its financial results on March 2, 1999. Under the accord, Warnaco -- whose brands include Warner's, Calvin Klein jeans and underwear, Chaps by Ralph Lauren, Nautica, Speedo, and Catalina -- will have to hire an independent consultant to review its internal controls and policies and to adopt the consultant's recommendations. A Warnaco spokesman said the company will hire accounting firm BDO Seidman for that purpose. (AP)

US plans world's fastest supercomputer

Viewing supercomputers as crucial to scientific discovery, the Energy Department will unveil plans today to build the world's fastest computer at a research laboratory in Tennessee. The supercomputer to be built at the Oak Ridge National Laboratory will be funded over the initial two years by federal grants totaling $50 million. Energy Secretary Spencer Abraham is expected to call development of the computer for general science ''critical to our nation's competitiveness." The project submitted by Oak Ridge scientists envisions a computer capable of sustaining 50 trillion calculations per second. (AP)

Bush delays on Greenspan nomination

With fewer than six weeks left in Alan Greenspan's term at the helm of the Federal Reserve, some analysts are wondering why President Bush has not yet renominated the 78-year-old central banker. Fed watchers expect Bush will soon tap Greenspan for a fifth, and what would be final, four-year stint as chairman. Greenspan's term is set to expire June 20 and, while he would stay in place as acting chairman even if it did run out, Wall Street has noticed the clock ticking. ''The longer the White House waits to reappoint him, the more it will invite speculation that the appointment is being held up to lean on him over hiking rates so close to the election," ISI Group said in a note to clients Monday, adding it does not believe that is the case. (AP)

THE WORLD

Sony cuts price on PlayStation 2 console

Sony Corp. cut the price of its leading PlayStation 2 video game console by as much as 25 percent in the United States, matching the price of Microsoft Corp.'s Xbox, whose sales have soared recently following a similar move. In Los Angeles, at E3, the video game industry's annual trade show, Sony also showed off its upcoming PlayStation Portable handheld device which will play DVD-quality movies and music as well as games nearly as advanced as its market-leading console. Sony said it would start promoting a new price of $149 for the PS2 in the United States, including an optional network adapter for online games. The stand-alone PS2 had been selling for $179, and for $199 with the adapter. (Reuters)
