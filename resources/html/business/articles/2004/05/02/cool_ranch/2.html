Cool ranch

May 2, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Now, some predict a renaissance of the ranch. Last year, Random House published Katherine Ann Samon's "Ranch House Style," a glossy paean to the ranch with suggestions for updating the house for the new century. Some of the renovated ranches include foyers with toile-patterned wallpaper; one ranch in the Hamptons had an Asian-influenced decor.

And the ranch has seen some recent converts among its original occupants: aging baby boomers who no longer want to navigate stairs and retire to a one-story house.

Two years ago, the National Register released new guidelines to help preservationists document modern suburban houses, including ranches. Although there are applications underway, none in Massachusetts are yet on the register, which includes ranch house suburbs in Tucson, Ariz., and Englewood, Colo.

Arthur and Inge Uhlir paid $40,000 for their house in 1959, a decade after the founders bought part of the former Trapelo Golf Club for the development. The Uhlirs, who had two children, liked the sprawling design of the house. They fell in love with an indoor garden, sun-fed by a skylight. And they were drawn to the ethnically and culturally diverse neighborhood.

The neighborhood was packed with kids -- after the Uhlirs had their third child, their cul-de-sac of three houses held 12 children (11 boys and one girl) between them. Kendal Common's board of directors has never allowed homeowners to carve off their backyard with fences, so the packs of children could roam freely -- fishing golf balls from the pond, zipping down the playground's sliding board on waxed paper.

"The idea was to make everybody's lot a part of the whole," said Inge Uhlir.

The children are grown and gone, and the Uhlirs, now in their 70s, still see advantages in their one-story house, where the bedrooms are separated from the rest of the rooms by just three steps. They can hold neighborhood meetings in their home because a wheelchair-bound neighbor can maneuver her way around.

Kendal Common still looks much as it did during the 1950s, largely because the community's board of directors must approve any exterior changes to the houses. Although some owners have expanded their houses -- the Uhlirs added a section where a backyard patio once lay -- they have generally retained the simple style of the original homes.

"There hasn't been as much mansion-building here as in other parts of Weston," said Arthur Uhlir, a retired Tufts University dean. The control exercised by the board of directors may lower property values, he said.

"On the other hand, the neighbors can't do something that you won't like," he said.

Andrew Tabak, who moved to Kendal Common Road in 1996, is president of the neighborhood's board. He was drawn to Kendal Common by the private yards and large windows that dominate his two-story house.

"You're sitting here in your living room and you're virtually outside," he said. "We've had deer walk within three feet of the sliding glass doors when we're sitting there eating supper. You can practically reach out and touch them."

Kendal Common was created by amateurs, academics and scientists, who had a vision, said Pamela W. Fox, who wrote "Farm Town to Suburb: The History and Architecture of Weston, Massachusetts 1830-1980." The founders had a motto: "Kendal Common . . . building towards a better life."

The group also included an architect named Carl Koch who had studied with Walter Gropius at Harvard's Graduate School of Design in the 1930s. Koch, who later transformed Boston's Lewis Wharf into condominiums, was dubbed "The Grandfather of Prefab" by Progressive Architecture magazine in 1994.

The Kendall Common founders bought the lower 40 acres of the golf course and divided it into the 22 house lots nestled along curvaceous roads --a stark departure from an era that worshipped the grid. They added community lots, for the playground, the pond, and open space. They hawked the land themselves in a black-and-white brochure, printed about 1952, that still dances off the page with suburban earnestness: "Santa Comes to Kendal Common" and "Paper Bags Become Hallowe'en Costumes."

There are fewer children in Kendal Common than in the days when groups would roam from house to house. At least five of the original families remain. Tabak, at 48, feels young for the neighborhood.

Although there are fewer community activities, the neighbors still gather at least twice a year, once for the annual meeting and once, usually in the spring, to clean up and prune trees.

"There was sort of a search for new ways to live, not just new forms of architecture," Fox said. "You don't find that everywhere."
