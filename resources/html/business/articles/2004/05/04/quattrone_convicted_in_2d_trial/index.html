Quattrone convicted in 2d trial

Ex-banker is found guilty of obstructing IPO investigation

By Brooke A. Masters, Washington Post  |  May 4, 2004

NEW YORK -- Frank P. Quattrone, who helped drive the 1990s stock boom as one of Wall Street's most prominent bankers, was convicted yesterday of obstructing two federal investigations into the way his employer handed out hot new stocks.

Quattrone's conviction was a victory for federal prosecutors, who decided to press the case a second time after his first trial ended in a hung jury more than five months ago.

The six men and six women on the second jury deliberated less than eight hours over two days before finding him guilty of three charges of interfering with a grand jury and a Securities and Exchange Commission investigation into the way Credit Suisse First Boston, his former firm, handled the allocation of shares in initial public stock offerings.

Quattrone, 48, was one of the major architects of the technology bubble, helping to shepherd hundreds of high-tech IPOs in the 1990s. He supervised CSFB's technology bankers who brought companies to market and the analysts who wrote reports on them for investors. CSFB has paid a total of $300 million to settle investigations into biased research and the way it handed out desirable IPO shares.

Quattrone's case turned on a two-line e-mail that he sent Dec. 5, 2000, which "strongly advised" nearly 400 subordinates to follow an earlier memo telling them to "clean up" IPO files.

Prosecutors argued that Quattrone was trying to impede the investigation because he had been told just hours before he sent the e-mail that he needed a personal lawyer and might be called as a witness by the grand jury. But the Los Altos, Calif., resident testified that he believed the IPO probe was aimed at a different part of the bank and was not thinking of it when he sent the e-mail.

Jurors said after the verdict that they did not buy that excuse from Quattrone, who earned $120 million in 2000 as head of CSFB's technology group. "Frank Quattrone was at the top. You don't get to the top without being aware a lot of things," said Manhattan resident Sheldon R. Silver, a receptionist and former actor. "This man was used to handling five different things at once. It just seemed like too much of a coincidence to us."

The conviction provides prosecutors a boost after Quattrone's first case and the six-month state court trial of Tyco International Ltd. chief executive L. Dennis Kozlowski both ended in mistrials.

"I am heartened to see that prosecutors are not only able to bring this case a second time but able to win and send a very strong message to executives," said New York University law professor Jennifer H. Arlen. "Even if they use innocent-sounding words, the juries are going to look through the form to the substance of what they were trying to do and say, 'You're guilty.' "

Quattrone sat stone-faced as the verdict was read. Under federal sentencing guidelines he faces 10 to 16 months in prison when he is sentenced Sept. 8. The guidelines also allow US District Judge Richard Owen to tack on up to five more months if he rules that Quattrone lied on the stand. That issue could arise because Quattrone was convicted after testifying in his own defense.

The former banker still faces civil charges from industry regulator NASD, formerly known the National Association of Securities Dealers.

Quattrone's mother and sister also sat silently during the verdict. But both burst into tears when he came over and gave them a hug at the end of the proceedings.

Outside the Manhattan courthouse, Quattrone, who remains free on bail until sentencing, declined to comment. But his attorney John W. Keker said, "We are obviously grossly disappointed. I feel like we failed Frank. He is innocent."

The defense attorney vowed an appeal, alluding to a series of evidentiary rulings that went against the Quattrone team. "There was an awful lot of evidence that the jury didn't hear," Keker said.

Acting US Attorney David N. Kelley, whose office brought the case, said in a written statement, "The government's ability to fully and fairly investigate allegations of wrongdoing in our financial markets depends on the integrity of grand jury and SEC investigations. When we learn of efforts to obstruct or interfere with those investigations, we must and we will prosecute the cases to the fullest extent permitted by law."

Unlike most criminal defendants, Quattrone took the stand in his own defense at both trials. Jurors in the first case said they thought Quattrone contradicted himself on the crucial question of whether he was involved in IPO allocations. This time, the banker said upfront that he sometimes provided "input" into the way CSFB doled out hot shares, and he kept his cool throughout his cross-examination by Assistant US Attorney David B. Anders.

"We thought he came across as very confident. He was very self-assured," said juror Susan Poryles, a human resources consultant from Manhattan. "I started out thinking he was just too smart to do something wrong."

Witnesses told the jury that the first "clean up" memo was not Quattrone's idea, and Quattrone testified that he was not thinking of the grand jury when he fired off his own e-mail endorsing the plan to cull IPO files. But prosecutors Anders and Steven R. Peikin were able to show that the banker could have stopped the initial memo and also waited 24 hours before sending his own e-mail endorsing it.

"The crux of the case was all around Frank Quattrone's intent and state of mind when he sent that e-mail," said juror Jonathan Miller, a computer technology consultant from Westchester County.
