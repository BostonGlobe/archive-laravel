New technology drives advances in screening

By Raja Mishra, Globe Staff  |  May 4, 2004

Cancer has long been a catch-up game for physicians, whose screening tools often detect the disease only when life-threatening tumors sprout and bulge from the body.

Today's best cancer tests can't see tumors until they're bigger than about a billion cells. At that point -- considered an early catch -- there's a chance that some cells already will have migrated beyond the tumor, making the cancer far more dangerous.

"By the time cancer gets large enough to detect, it's larger than we want it to be," said Robert E. Lenkinski, a chemist on the radiology staff of Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center. "As hard as we try and as much as we do, we know the earlier cancer is detected, the better the prognosis."

But dozens of researchers across the country and the world are closing in on new developments that could transform cancer screening over the next decade.

Advances are expected on at least three major fronts -- genetic profiling, protein analysis, and improvements to existing imaging technology. These new approaches rely on the fact that cancer cells are fundamentally different from normal ones. To find cancers, scientists are searching for the disease's "markers" -- the specific DNA or protein patterns that signal the presence of malignant cells, and hold clues about the cancer's aggressiveness.

Prostate cancer, for instance, can linger in a man's body for decades doing little harm, or it can be fatal.

"The hope is we'll be able to say, `Aha, you have this genetic pattern in your prostate cancer and your cancer will be lethal,' " said Dr. Philip Kantoff of the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute. "I think we need to do better than the current test. We want to detect the cancers that kill people."

A new test for ovarian cancer -- tracking the pattern of proteins that make up the malignancy -- could be on the market as soon as this year. Much of the research is focused on improving screening tools like MRIs to catch cancers early.

"If you think you can find and identify molecular defects across tissues using scanning, you may be able to change screening for the better," said Dr. Judy E. Garber, a breast cancer specialist at the Dana-Farber. "I really think molecular imaging is the future."

Magnetic resonance imaging is an incredibly powerful tool for looking deep inside the body. But it isn't always easy to tell what you're looking at. There isn't a red flag highlighting the small cluster of cancer cells surrounded by billions of healthy ones -- so doctors are trying, almost literally, to make one.

Lenkinski, who studies breast cancer, is developing what he calls "smart contrast agents," proteins engineered to attach to the surface of cancer cells. The agents would also contain a fluorescent chemical tag that would light up under MRIs. The result -- easy-to-spot tumors glowing on screen.

Doctors at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center are now testing an imaging technique called 3TP (for three time point) that calls for taking three MRI pictures of a suspected breast or prostate tumor, once before and twice after the injection of a dye that highlights cancer cells.

Dozens of other researchers around the world are working to develop similar agents for other cancers. Experts predict clinical trials testing the efficacy of the approach in detecting all the major cancer types will be underway in a few years.

Refinements in computer and imaging technology are also leading to improved screening techniques, most notably the virtual colonoscopy, where a CAT scanner maps out the colon.

Though still somewhat controversial -- competing studies show different levels of effectiveness -- virtual colonoscopies are much less invasive than traditional ones in which the camera has to be threaded through the rectum.

"There are a lot of social reasons why people put [colonoscopies] off," said Dana-Farber colon cancer expert Dr. Robert J. Mayer, who hopes the milder test will encourage more people to get screened. Three-quarters of the 50,000 annual colon cancer deaths could be eliminated if screening were widespread, experts estimate.

One pitfall of detecting every potential tumor early: Creating anguish over tumors that are actually harmless. That may even lead to unnecessary surgeries. But even that problem may soon be solved.

Researchers at Dana-Farber, in preliminary studies, are having some success at finding genetic markers to predict those tumors that will spread and those that will harmlessly stay put. Last week, scientists at Stanford University and the University of Miami reported the discovery of six DNA markers for aggressive lymphomas -- a quick DNA test could soon help doctors shepherd these patients into the most aggressive treatment regimens. And researchers are currently trying to match tens of thousands of Swedish cancer patients with data on how their tumors fared over time, seeking DNA clues that signal which ones are nasty and which benign.These scientists have taken advantage of the enormous amount of data streaming from the Human Genome Project, as well as new computerized methods for rapid analysis of genetic information.Many researchers predict that in the not-too-distant future, doctors will use these technologies to quickly spot early-stage tumors, accurately predict how they'll behave, then treat them, if necessary, with genetically attuned smart drugs. Today's technological advances, they say, are tomorrow's cures.

"The barriers to doing all this," Lenkinski said, "have really been diminished."
