As profits slip, inns on Nantucket are converted to homes

By Chris Reidy, Globe Staff  |  May 24, 2004

NANTUCKET -- Slowly a bit of Nantucket is slipping away.

ADVERTISEMENT

Its quaint inns and bed-and-breakfasts have long been part of the island's charm. On an island legendary for high prices, many B&Bs were ideal for vacationers on a budget.

But there are fewer inns and fewer rooms today, and one culprit is Nantucket's red-hot real estate market. With home prices soaring, inns aren't just being purchased by the next generation of innkeepers, they're also being snapped up by millionaires who want to turn them into private homes.

Earlier this month, developer Stephen Karp sold the Breakers for $11 million to owners who intend to convert the waterfront inn with about 25 rooms into one or more homes, according to Karp's island broker, Clarence Doucette of The Maury People Inc..

Over the last decade, the island has lost 300 rooms, or nearly a quarter of its inventory, according to the Nantucket Lodging Association. Today there are just under 60 lodging establishments with about 1,000 rooms.

The association, a volunteer group, doesn't track the number of inns that have been turned into homes. But Richard Ray, a longtime town official who oversees health inspections of inns, said that over the last three years, ''I can count six off the top of my head. It's not a phenomenon we've witnessed before."

Currently, there are two inns for sale, according to the island's listing service. One is being marketed both to innkeepers and to developers who might turn it into private homes. The second, which hasn't been operational in several years, is on the market as a private home.

According to brokers, the value of an inn's real estate can be worth more than the value of its business. At today's prices, an inn that rents out eight rooms for $110 to $310 a night can't always cover its expenses. Throw in the high costs of doing business on the island, and the fact that many inns operate only part of the year, and profits can be out of reach for someone carrying a $2 million mortgage.

''From a business standpoint, the numbers don't work," said Douglas Foregger, chief executive of Country Village Rentals & Real Estate.

For many Nantucket house-hunters these days, money seems to be no object. According to Flint Ranney at Denby Real Estate Inc., the median home price on Nantucket rose from $290,000 in 1995 to $917,000 in 2003.

Money is in such ''excessive supply" on the island that ''we have an artificial economy," Ray said. ''It ain't like America. It's a different world out here."

Given all that money, selling a B&B to a home buyer can be a great exit strategy for a small innkeeper who is ready to retire after years of hard work and making other people's beds. Thus, a B&B that started life as the mansion of a 19th-century whaling captain will probably become the mansion of a 21st-century millionaire.

Pam Horovitz, president of the Professional Association of Innkeepers International, cited anecdotal evidence of similar instances of the B&B reversion in other ''high-end resort areas" such as Park City, Utah, and Santa Barbara, Calif.

Earlier this year on Martha's Vineyard, John Chirgwin, 68, and his brother, Jim, sold a historic inn in Edgartown for $7 million. The buyer plans to turn the Daggett House into two private residences, John Chirgwin said. ''The property is worth a fortune, but the amount of income you can make from it is limited," he said.

In some ways, Gail and David More have lived the B&B fantasy. Dissatisfied with their jobs in New York, they left it all behind 18 years ago to operate the Nantucket Landfall, a guesthouse with eight bedrooms. Ready to retire, the Mores put the Landfall up for sale for $3 million.

''It doesn't make sense to run it as an inn unless that's your thing," said the Mores' listing broker, Mary Taaffe of Denby Real Estate.

Earlier this month, the Mores accepted an offer from a buyer who plans to turn the Landfall, with its harbor views and easy walk to Nantucket town, into a home, Taaffe said.

A few years ago, Ian MacKenzie bought the India House, an inn with a restaurant in the residential neighborhood where he has a home. Nervous that a new owner might expand, the retired investor said, ''We stepped in and bought it." He recently put the place on the market as a home with an asking price of $1.65 million.

Not all inns are destined to become the island addresses of the wealthy. Philip Read, 67, co-owner of the landmark Jared Coffin House, said he plans to sell to another inn operator.

One nationwide trend in the B&B business, which is also playing out on Nantucket, is that accommodations are becoming more upscale.

''Twenty years ago, the discussion was, 'Should we have a private bath in every room?' " said Horovitz of the innkeepers' group. ''Now it's, 'Should we have spa features in every bathroom?' "

At the White Elephant, one of the island's most luxurious inns, room rates for a weekend summer night start at $520. Most rooms have working fireplaces and harbor views. Guests also enjoy access to a library, a fitness room, and a business lounge.

In February, Danielle deBenedictis and her husband, Peter Karlson, purchased the Pineapple Inn for $2.6 million and will continue to operate it as an inn, their fourth on the island. A key to their business success is spreading costs over four properties with 50 rooms, and offering luxurious amenities, deBenedictis said. At the Pineapple, room rates range from $250 to $450 a night at peak season.

In 1996, Tim Madden bought the 18 Gardner Street Inn for $1.2 million. It was old and needed work. Meanwhile, the market was evolving. Customers wanted amenities such as hot tubs and room service. Big upgrades were ''cost-prohibitive" for an inn barely making money, he said. In 2000, he sold it for $2 million, he said, and today it's a private home.

If real estate prices stay high, the number of Nantucket inns could continue to erode. As Madden sees it, most people who can afford a down payment on a Nantucket inn at today's prices have little interest in being innkeepers.

''There's not a lot of folks walking around with $500,000 in their pockets who want to make beds all morning," he said.
