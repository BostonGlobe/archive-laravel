BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Bill aims at small business health costs

May 20, 2004

THE REGION

Premiums for employee health coverage for small businesses could decline between 9 and 25 percent if a bill that permits professional associations to pool together to buy coverage passes the state Legislature, said a report for the Massachusetts Dental Society by GBG Consulting Services. The bill awaits a House vote. Currently, small businesses are forced to buy insurance in the state's regulated small-group market. Bill supporters say pooling would give individual associations -- dentists, chambers of commerce, or retailers -- leverage to negotiate better rates with insurers. Opponents say it would undercut an insurance system designed to help small business. Pooling, said GBG consultant George Pollino, would allow associations to "expand the insurance to people who can't afford coverage." (Kim Blanton)

Constitution Quarters to be renamed

The new owners of Constitution Quarters in Charlestown are renaming it Parris Landing at the Navy Yard in honor of 19th-century architect Alexander Parris. He not only worked in the Navy Yard but also designed lighthouses along the East Coast. Plans call for a waterfront property of 367 rental units to be converted to condos that will be priced from about $250,000 to almost $700,000, said the development team of the Carlyle Group and Draper and Kramer Inc. They hired Yoo, a London firm headed by fashion icon Philippe Starck, to redesign the building's common areas, entrances, and massive atrium. (Chris Reidy)

Bush reaffirms position on base closings

The Bush administration issued a policy statement threatening to veto any bill that "weakens, delays, or repeals" next year's military base realignment and closing process, which could target Hanscom Air Force Base in Bedford and the Army Soldier Systems Center in Natick. The veto threat came as the House of Representatives debated a defense authorization bill that included a two-year delay in the process. The Senate narrowly rejected a similar measure on Tuesday. (Robert Weisman)

Defense contractor wins Army contract

Raytheon Co., the fourth-largest US defense contractor, got three US Army orders valued at more than $165 million to more than double production of thermal weapon sights for use by troops in the Middle East. The Army asked Raytheon to raise monthly output of the sights to more than 1,000 units from 400, and to deliver 9,956 of the systems, which use infrared technology to let soldiers see at night or through fog, smoke, dust, and sandstorms, the Waltham company said. The US plans to keep at least 138,000 troops in Iraq through 2005, and will shift as many as 3,600 soldiers into the area from South Korea by August, the Defense Department said this week. (Bloomberg)

Friendly's cofounder offers $50m loan

The 89-year-old cofounder of Friendly Ice Cream came to the company's annual shareholder meeting to offer the board of directors a sweet deal -- with a catch. S. Prestley Blake, who sold the ice cream empire in 1979, will give the company a $50 million, low-interest loan out of his own pocket, contingent on the board forcing chairman Donald Smith to repay the $3.5 million that Blake says Smith took from Friendly's and funneled into another restaurant company. Blake says his offer, which calls for an interest rate two percentage points above prime, could save the company about $1.25 million a year in interest. Blake dealt himself back into the company three years ago, spending more than $2 million to become the largest individual shareholder. He currently owns 9.8 percent of the stock. Blake claims Smith used a joint operating agreement for a corporate jet to funnel the $3.5 million to The Restaurant Co., in which Smith owns a 70 percent interest. (AP)

THE NATION
Airlines follow suit on fuel surcharge

Several US airlines unveiled fare hikes to offset soaring jet fuel prices, following Continental Airlines' action a day earlier. Continental raised fares worldwide to counter fuel costs, which have risen 50 percent from a year earlier on the back of soaring crude oil prices. United Airlines, which matched Continental's price increase of $10 each way for flights up to 1,000 miles and $20 each way for flights over 1,000 miles, said high jet fuel prices were keeping the bankrupt airline from reaching profitability. American Airlines, the world's largest carrier, said it matched the increase in European markets. Delta Air Lines, the number three US carrier, said it has also matched Continental's hike. Northwest Airlines, the number four US airline, said it has selectively matched the increase. JetBlue Airways said it did not plan to surcharge. (Reuters)

Sexually explicit spam must be labeled

"Sexually explicit": A federal rule now requires that two-word warning in the subject line of all sexually oriented unsolicited commercial e-mail. The rule also bars graphic images from appearing in the opening body of the message. Instead, the recipient must take some action in order to see the objectionable material, either by scrolling down in the e-mail or by clicking on a provided link. The government says the label will help Internet users screen out raunchy stuff they don't want. (AP)

Minority homeownership on increase

Homeownership among US minorities in the first three months of this year jumped to levels not seen since records were first started in 1975, mortgage finance company Fannie Mae said. Citing US Census Bureau Data, Fannie Mae said homeownership nationally remained at an all-time high of 68.6 percent in the first quarter of 2004, unchanged from 2003's fourth quarter. "For minority households, the rate rose by 6.6 percent, compared with 5.1 percent for nonminority households," according to the report. Fannie Mae also said the homeownership gap between minority and nonminority households narrowed to a record low of 24.6 percent in the first quarter of this year. (Reuters)

. . .Etc.

Fairchild Semiconductor International Inc. of South Portland, Maine, said Alcatel Canada has sued for damages allegedly stemming from a defective mold compound, the plastic resin used to encapsulate semiconductor chips. The phosphorus-containing mold compound at issue caused some chips to short in some situations, resulting in chip failure, Fairchild said in its annual report filed March 12 with the Securities and Exchange Commission. . . . Shares in Acambis PLC rose 9 percent to $11.80 after the Cambridge vaccine maker said it will receive $19 million from Baxter Healthcare Corp. in exchange for terminating a manufacturing agreement. Acambis had previously agreed to produce an unspecified amount of Baxter products in a facility in Canton, but the space has been unused since the deal was struck in 2000. . . . Molecular Insight Pharmaceuticals Inc. of Cambridge plans to disclose today it has raised $7 million to fund clinical trials of a new molecule to be used in medical imaging, a spokesman said. Closely held Molecular Insight had previously raised $16 million since it was founded in 1998 and has received more than $3 million in federal grants. . . . Massachusetts Mutual Life Insurance Co.'s MML Growth Equity fund will replace its subadviser, Massachusetts Financial Services Co., with Grantham, Mayo, Van Otterloo & Co., the fund said in a regulatory filing. MML Growth Equity fund didn't give a reason for the change. . . . EMC Corp. and IBM extended an agreement to let IBM resell some EMC programs for another three years. IBM signed the original contract in 2002 with VMware, a closely held company EMC bought in January. The software lets clients move data between server computers and run operating systems such as Windows and Linux on the same machine. (Globe staff and wire services)
