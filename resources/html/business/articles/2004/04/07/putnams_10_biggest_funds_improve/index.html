BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Putnam's 10 biggest funds improve

April 7, 2004

Putnam Investments, the first mutual fund company sued for allowing alleged improper trading, has improved the performance of its largest funds since overhauling its investment staff at the end of 2002. Six of Putnam's 10 biggest funds outperformed similarly managed funds during the past year, according to data compiled by Bloomberg News. The $19.7 billion Putnam Fund for Growth and Income, the Boston-based firm's biggest fund, gained 37 percent, ranking sixth of 40 competing funds. Chief executive Charles ''Ed" Haldeman joined Putnam in October 2002 as co-head of investments to try to fix equity fund performance that was among the worst in the industry during the three-year bear market. Putnam's stock funds lost 57 percent of their value from March 2000 to October 2002, the fourth-worst performance among the 25 biggest US mutual fund companies, according to Kanon Bloch Carre, a consulting firm in Boston. In the past year, Putnam's 10 biggest funds, with total assets of more than $81 billion, beat 52 percent of their peers on average, according to Bloomberg News data. Investors have yet to notice the improvement, yanking $5.6 billion from Putnam funds in the first two months of this year. (Bloomberg)

Tweeter expects wider loss in 2d quarter

Tweeter Home Entertainment Group Inc. said its second-quarter loss will be wider than expected due to weak sales of televisions. The Canton-based electronics retailer said it now expects to report a loss of 5 to 8 cents a share for the second quarter ended March 31. Revenue is expected to grow 4 percent to $189 million, the company said. Analysts surveyed by Thomson First Call had been expecting a second-quarter loss of 6 cents a share on revenue of $186 million. Tweeter specializes in mid- to high-priced audio and video equipment. Half of its sales come from TVs, VCRs, and DVD players. In the year-ago second quarter Tweeter reported a loss of $2.5 million, or 10 cents a share, on revenue of $182 million. (Dow Jones/AP)

Zoll Medical profit falls below estimate

Zoll Medical Corp., which makes cardiac resuscitation devices, said fiscal second-quarter earnings unexpectedly fell to as low as 16 cents a share because sales were lower than forecast. Shares fell $11.78, or 27.6 percent, to $30.84. The company said it earned 16 to 20 cents a share in the period ended April 4. Chelmsford-based Zoll had net income of 30 cents in the year-earlier quarter and was expected to earn 35 cents, the average estimate of eight analysts polled by Thomson Financial. Revenue for the quarter rose to $50 million to $51 million, from $46.6 million in the year-earlier period. Zoll said international sales were little changed from a year ago and North American orders for emergency medical equipment were weaker than expected. (Bloomberg)

Sonus Networks transfers finance chief

Sonus Networks Inc., a maker of telephone-network equipment that expects to restate results for almost the past two years, said it's replacing chief financial officer Stephen Nill. The Chelmsford company will also review how it booked sales before 2002. Nill will become vice president of business operations and Albert Notini, hired yesterday as president and chief operating officer, will oversee financial operations until a new CFO is named, Sonus said. Sonus reiterated that it's assessing when, not whether, certain sales should be recognized. Sonus said last week it expected to restate results for 2002 and the first three quarters of 2003 and was considering examining earlier periods. . Nill's transfer has nothing to do with the planned restatement, said Michael O'Hara, Sonus's vice president of marketing. (Bloomberg)

Brooktrout shares plummet 40.8%

Brooktrout Inc. shares fell 40.8 percent, the day after the maker of communications hardware and software said first-quarter sales fell short of its forecast. Shares fell $9.12 to $13.25, their lowest close in more than three months, after earlier trading as low as $13.20. Revenue was $18.1 million to $18.3 million, compared with a Feb. 17 estimate of $21 million to $22.5 million, Needham-based Brooktrout said in a preliminary earnings statement. Brooktrout sells fax, data, and voice products to equipment makers who incorporate them into their telecommunications and computer systems. The company expects second-quarter revenue of $18.5 million to $19.5 million. The average estimate of four analysts surveyed by Thomson Financial was for $22.9 million. (Bloomberg)

FTC backs attempt by Jones Apparel

The Federal Trade Commission cleared Jones Apparel Group Inc.'s acquisition of Maxwell Shoe Co. A waiting period for objections to be filed expired under federal antitrust laws, the FTC said. Shoe designer Maxwell, of Hyde Park, has asked a federal court to block a $300 million takeover attempt by Jones. Maxwell claimed Jones Apparel misled the public when it called its $20 a share offer for Maxwell an ''attractive premium." (Dow Jones/AP)

THE NATION
Ruling goes against ex-Grace unit official

A former controller at National Medical Care Inc., a onetime subsidiary of W.R. Grace & Co., committed securities fraud in connection with earnings manipulation at the chemical producer during the 1990s, a Securities and Exchange Commission judge ruled. Robert Armstrong III ''cooperated, albeit with misgivings" in the earnings manipulation, SEC judge Carol Fox Foelak said in an order issued yesterday. Armstrong was punished with a cease-and-desist order that opens him up to more severe punishment if he violates securities laws again. The ruling is among the final pieces of a case filed against seven former Grace executives in 1998. It was billed as the SEC's first enforcement effort against earnings manipulation, in which companies use accounting gimmicks to bolster quarterly and annual financial results. (Bloomberg)

. . .Etc.

Shares of Boston Scientific Corp. hit a record high of $45.93, then fell back slightly to finish down 36 cents at $45.45, a day after the company said US sales of its newly launched Taxus drug-coated stent had exceeded its targets. Taxus, launched early last month, is the second drug-coated stent to enter the US market, behind Johnson & Johnson's Cypher stent, which was launched in April. . . . Abiomed Inc. founder Dr. David Lederman, whose company is developing the first self-contained artificial heart, is handing over leadership. Danvers-based Abiomed said that Michael Minogue, previously an executive with GE Medical Systems, will become chief executive and president. Lederman will remain as chairman, focusing on technology development and strategic collaborations. . . . State Street Global Advisors, the investment management division of Boston-based State Street Corp., named Peter Leahy to the newly created position of chief operating officer. He will oversee finance, technology, operations, and human resource functions. A 13-year State Street veteran, he also will retain roles including membership on the executive-management team and vice chairman of the investment committee. . . . KB Toys Inc. wants a bankruptcy court to approve rules governing the sale and auction of its websites, according to court papers. In the papers, the Pittsfield company said it wants to sell its Internet business to Toy Acquisition Corp. for at least $6.5 million and license royalties of about $1.5 million over three years. A hearing on the sale and auction rules has been scheduled for April 29 in Wilmington. . . . Sirius Satellite Radio Inc. said its radios will be installed on 11 DaimlerChrysler AG vehicle lines for the 2005 model year. Sirius is issuing warrants to DaimlerChrysler to buy as many as 21.5 million shares at $1.04 each. That's a 65 percent discount to warrants issued earlier when the car maker received 4 million shares at $3 each. (Globe wire services)
