BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Fed OK's Manulife buy of John Hancock

April 6, 2004

The Federal Reserve approved the proposal of John Hancock Financial Services and Canada's Manulife Financial Corp. to merge and create the second-largest life insurance company in North America. The Fed's board of governors found that Manulife's acquisition of Hancock in a stock swap, valued at $10.4 billion when disclosed in September, would not threaten competition or unduly concentrate banking resources. The merger, which has been approved by shareholders of Boston-based Hancock, still needs clearance from Massachusetts' insurance commissioner. At a public hearing before the commissioner last month, no one spoke to oppose it. The merger is expected to be completed this quarter. (AP)

ViaCell files for initial public offering

ViaCell Inc. registered an initial public offering to sell up to $92 million in common stock, according to a Form S-1 filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission. Details about the number of shares and the price of the IPO were not disclosed. The Boston company said it intends to use the proceeds from the offering to fund preclinical research and development activities and clinical trial activities, to expand its manufacturing capacity and its commercial infrastructure in the area of reproductive health, to repay an outstanding promissory note, and for other general corporate purposes. Credit Suisse First Boston LLC, UBS Securities LLC, Lazard Freres & Co. LLC, and Leerink Swann & Co. were listed as the underwriters. ViaCell is a global biotechnology company engaged in sourcing, developing, and commercializing cellular therapies to address cancer, infertility, and certain genetic and cardiac diseases. (Dow Jones)

AG files suit against Agawam nonprofit

Massachusetts Attorney General Thomas F. Reilly filed a complaint against an Agawam credit counseling charity, alleging the nonprofit funneled more than $60 million over 2 years to businesses owned by insiders. Reilly filed suit in Suffolk Superior Court against Cambridge Credit Counseling Corp. and its controlling directors and officers, John Puccio, Richard Puccio, Kurt Meyer, and Chris Viale, for breaches of fiduciary obligations and unfair and deceptive practices. The suit seeks to remove the defendants from the nonprofit, void improper related-party contracts, force the officials to disgorge improper payments, and make the charity pay restitution to consumers. A company spokesman said the charges were without merit and the nonprofit planned to defend itself.

Boston Scientific stent sales beat targets

First-month sales of Boston Scientific's newly approved drug-coated coronary stent handily beat sales targets, the medical device maker said. Boston Scientific, of Natick, which received approval from regulators on March 4 to sell its Taxus Express2 system in the United States, said sales from that date through March 31 totaled $98 million. The company told analysts in February that it expected sales between $54 million and $75 million for March 1 through March 31. The company had estimated total first-quarter worldwide stent sales of $210 million to $240 million, with Taxus accounting for between $150 million and $180 million. Boston Scientific now expects total stent sales of $283 million for the first quarter, with Taxus accounting for $215 million. (AP)

Brooktrout sales fall short in 1st quarter

Brooktrout Inc., a maker of communications hardware and software whose shares have risen more than fourfold in the past year, said first-quarter sales fell short of its forecast. Revenue was $18.1 million to $18.3 million, compared with a Feb. 17 estimate of $21 million to $22.5 million, Needham-based Brooktrout said in a preliminary earnings statement. Shares fell as much as $6.92 to $15.45 in after-hours trading. They had risen $1.15, or 5.4 percent, to $22.37 in the regular session. A spokesman did not immediately return a call.

General Mills loses ruling on Talbots sale

General Mills Inc., the second-biggest US cereal maker, lost a US Supreme Court appeal of a $10.8 million tax assessment by Massachusetts over the company's 1988 sale of clothing retailer Talbots Inc. The court refused to hear arguments that the state can't tax the sale of Talbots, a seller of classically styled clothing, because the sale wasn't linked to the cereal maker's business in Massachusetts. General Mills bought Hingham-based Talbots in 1973. In 1988, General Mills sold Talbots to Jusco Co., now known as Aeon Co., Japan's second-biggest retailer. The sale price was $325 million, General Mills's lawyers said. After an audit, Massachusetts officials assessed $10.8 million in taxes. (Bloomberg)

Ex-broker sentenced on Ohio charges

A former investment broker was sentenced to 4 years in prison in a plea deal that requires his help in an Ohio government corruption investigation. Frank Gruttadauria, 46, pleaded guilty last week to state charges of bribery, racketeering, theft, money laundering, forgery, and election-law violations. His plea deal requires his help in an ongoing grand jury investigation. Gruttadauria used a bribe to seek state investment business. Cuyahoga County Prosecutor William D. Mason declined to name Gruttadauria's bribe target. Common Pleas Judge Carolyn Friedland also fined Gruttadauria $20,000, although he said he is destitute. (AP)

Media seek unsealing of Tyco transcripts

News organizations asked the judge who heard the Tyco International grand-larceny trial to unseal transcripts of meetings that led him to declare a mistrial last week. Lawyer Carolyn K. Foley, representing nine news organizations, including the Associated Press, asked the judge to act ''for the purpose of enforcing the public's right of access to the transcripts of hearings" and other materials from the proceedings that led to the mistrial. New York State Supreme Court Justice Michael Obus, who ended the trial Friday after nearly six months of testimony and on the 12th day of jury deliberations, scheduled a hearing on the matter for tomorrow. Jurors had deliberated the fates of L. Dennis Kozlowski, 57, Tyco's former chief executive, and Mark H. Swartz, 43, the company's former chief financial officer, who were accused of looting Tyco of $600 million. (AP)

High court won't hear Halliburton appeal

The Supreme Court said it will not get involved in a patent dispute concerning a division of Halliburton, the energy and government contracting company led by Dick Cheney before he was vice president of the United States. Lawyers for Halliburton Energy Services Inc. appealed to the high court after a lower court ordered Halliburton to pay $98 million to rival BJ Services Co., which had sued for patent infringement over a particular method of extracting oil and natural gas. (AP)
