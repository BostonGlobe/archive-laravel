Q&A Economy.com's Thorsten Fischer on gas prices

By Keith Reed, Globe Staff  |  April 4, 2004

Gasoline prices are rising sharply, approaching the $2 per gallon mark at most New England filling stations. But while the prospect of sky-high gas costs gets most drivers peeved, especially those planning spring and summer road trips, Thorsten Fischer, senior economist at Economy.com, says we should adjust our thinking and fill our tanks. He talked with Globe staff reporter Keith Reed about how gas prices are still actually pretty cheap.

Q. Why have gasoline prices risen so fast in the last month or so?

A. Actually, when you adjust for inflation, gas prices are very affordable, and they're way below where they were in the early 1980s. They would be almost 70 percent more, about $3 a gallon, if they were at early 1980s levels in real terms, adjusted for inflation.

Q. If that's true, then why do most people get upset about price hikes at the pump?

A. I think it's because gasoline prices are very visible. The psychological impact of gas prices is a lot bigger than the actual impact on household disposable income. Gasoline and heating oil account for only 3 percent of household spending, but people really feel like they can't do anything about it.

They still have to commute into work, they still have to drive kids to soccer practice, so there's very little they can do about gas prices.

Q. So are people starting to feel guilty about buying all those SUVs in the last decade?

A. No, I don't think they feel guilty because the real pain is not that big. Consumers understand that gasoline always becomes more expensive in the spring and in the summer.

It's still hard to believe that gas prices haven't really gone up much when it costs, say, $10 more to fill up your car now than it did last year.

If you look at how much you spent over time and how much your income has gone up over the last three years, it's really not that big an impact. It is true that low-income households are more impacted because they spend a higher level of their incomes on energy. But there are now signs that jobs are being created again, and there's also the effect of the tax cuts and the tax rebates, which have more than covered the rise in gasoline prices.

Q. How high will gas prices go this summer?

A. We will see gas prices increase some more, but the national average will remain below $2 per gallon. Starting in early summer, we expect crude prices to moderate -- they're now about $35 a barrel -- and that will eventually have a moderating effect on gas prices. There are some risks to that outlook. Gas prices might be higher in some regional markets like California, New England, New York state, and the big cities in the Midwest. That's because of the strict environmental regulations, which add about 5 to 10 cents per gallon and make regional shortages likely to last longer if they occur.

Q. How much will the gas price increases cost the average household this year?

A. In 2003, they spent $192.5 billion, and this year you're looking at about $213.5 billion if prices stay where they've been in 2004 so far. There are 109 million households, so for last year that's about $1,800 per household and for this year, spending would be about $1,950, so $200 more per household. That's really not that much if you think about it. There's $7.7 trillion in personal consumer spending in the United States, so that tells you that the share spent on gasoline is really only a small fraction of total consumption.
