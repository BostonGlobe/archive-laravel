Large caps, values fall below 2.8% average rise

Gains trailed 10% increase recorded in prior period

By Bill Deener, The Dallas Morning News  |  April 4, 2004

Most mutual fund investors made a small profit in the first quarter ended Wednesday.

And that's a remarkable feat considering the quarter featured a deadly terrorist bombing in Madrid, assassination attempts of government leaders in Taiwan, sky-high gasoline prices, and a report that Medicare is going broke.

The average US diversified stock fund climbed 2.98 percent, marking the fourth consecutive quarter of gains, according to data from Lipper Inc., which tracks mutual fund performance. But this was a dramatically lower return than the previous quarter's 10 percent romp, and only a rally in the waning days of the quarter saved the average from turning negative.

Money flows into domestic stock funds were a robust $31.5 billion in January and $18.1 billion in February. But the latest estimates show the pace slowed to a trickle in March -- only $400 million -- when the major stock indexes dipped.

Investors apparently have grown skittish of the US stock market after last year's big gains, said Pat Dorsey, director of stock analysis at Morningstar, the mutual fund rating service.

"The economy and corporate earnings have been improving, but the market improved too much," he said. "It got overexcited and projected last year's earnings growth out to this year."

Although the average US stock fund was up almost 3 percent in the quarter, the largest and most popular mutual fund categories -- large-capitalization growth and value funds -- fell below the average returns with gains of 1.07 percent and 2.28 percent, respectively. And Rob Sharps, portfolio manager of the T. Rowe Price Institutional Large Cap Growth fund, reminded investors that both of these categories scored hefty 25 percent-plus gains over the last year.

His fund was up almost 40 percent last year.

"It was natural to have a correction in the market," Sharps said. "We had gone from the second quarter of 2003 through January without even a 5 percent pullback. It was time."

So far the correction has occurred mainly among large-capitalization stocks, such as Intel Corp., which has dropped from about $35 a share in January to about $27 currently. The Standard & Poor's 500 index, which represents many of the nation's largest companies, declined about 3 percent in March.

The smaller-capitalization stocks have held up much better. In fact, the average small-cap value fund increased 6.13 percent in the quarter, making it the best-performing category of all US diversified funds.

Earnings of small companies have gotten a boost from the historically low interest rates, which reduces borrowing costs, said Christine Benz, associate director of fund analysis at Morningstar Mutual Funds.

Low interest rates help big companies too, but the effects of lower expenses and the expanding economy have a larger and more immediate impact on smaller companies. However, Benz also believes that small-caps are attracting money because of last year's average return of more than 50 percent. In other words, investors are "chasing returns."

"Investors are looking in the rear-view mirror and saying that these things did well last year, so they must have the magic touch," she said. "And that's a dangerous way to invest."

Although the average US stock fund struggled to stay positive, some of the so-called sector funds posted impressive results. Sector funds focus on a single industry, such as biotechnology, financial services, or utilities.

The average sector fund gained about 8.38 percent in the quarter, according to Lipper, but some sector funds did even better. Real estate funds were the best-performing sector funds, with an 11.91 percent gain.

Real estate has been on a rampage for the better part of a year, said John Brynjolfsson, portfolio manager of the Pimco Real Estate Real Return fund. The sector has been supercharged by the low interest rates, which gives the owners of hotels, office buildings, shopping malls, and apartment complexes better returns.

Also the expanding US economy has increased demand for real estate and real estate funds, he said.

"We are hitting on all cylinders," Brynjolfsson said. "There are large inflows into this asset class by both large institutional investors and smaller retail investors looking to get exposure to real estate."

Tom Roseen, senior research analyst at Lipper, said he believes the pullback in the stock market has actually helped the real estate sector. Investors are seeking safety, and they believe they have found it in a "hard asset" class like real estate, he said.

"People are using real estate like our parents used utilities," Roseen said. "This category has had four straight years of positive returns."

As good as real estate was, investors who turned outside the United States did even better. Mutual funds with a focus on Japanese companies scorched all other categories, with a 13.4 percent gain.

This continues a trend that began more than a year ago with the re-emergence of the Japanese economy, said Campbell Gunn, manager of the T. Rowe Price Japan fund. The average Japanese fund is up 64 percent over the last year.

"Japan's economy has regained momentum because of a revival in exports," Gunn said. "Trade with Asia is the driving force."

Further, Roseen of Lipper said the normally tight-fisted Japanese consumer is starting to spend more, and businesses are starting to buy more equipment.

"It's still too early to know if Japan has really turned," he said. "Some people might say Japan is just going through a rally in a bear market. But there are a lot of positives there now."

China-focused funds didn't do as well as Japanese funds, but this sector still continues the upward trend that began more than a year ago. The average China fund gained 4.1 percent in the quarter, according to preliminary data, but over the last year this category is up 64 percent.

Tim Halter, managing director of the USX China Index, which tracks Chinese-focused companies, said there has been a little profit-taking in some of these stocks. But the economy, which is gradually becoming more capitalistic, is exploding.

Foreign investment in China topped $50 billion last year, which surpassed foreign investment in the United States, he said.

"You have an economy that is growing 9 percent a year," Halter said. "The country is clearly heading in the direction of capitalism."
