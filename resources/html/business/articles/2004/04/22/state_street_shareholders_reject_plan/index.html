BUSINESS IN BRIEF

State Street shareholders reject plan

April 22, 2004

State Street Corp.'s shareholders rejected a proposal that called for annual voting for members of the money management firm's board of directors with the aim of increasing responsiveness to investors' concerns. The plan received support from holders of 140 million shares, about 42 percent of those outstanding, in preliminary results at State Street's annual meeting in Boston. More than 97 million shares were voted against the proposal. Under state law, the resolution needed two-thirds of outstanding votes to pass. The proposal had drawn support from the California Public Employees' Retirement System, the biggest US public pension fund, which held 1.3 million State Street shares as of December. Last year, stockholders with 108 million shares welcomed the proposal, up from 86 million shares in 2002. State Street opposed the plan, saying staggered elections keep the board focused on the long-term interests of shareholders, a spokeswoman said. Five State Street directors -- Kennett Burnes, David Gruber, Linda Hill, Charles Lamantia, and Robert Weissman -- were each elected yesterday with at least 95 percent of votes cast. (Bloomberg)

Dunkin' Donuts group bids on Bess Eaton

A regional group of Dunkin' Donuts franchisees has placed a bid on the bankrupt Bess Eaton chain, topping an offer by the Canadian company Tim Hortons. The 14 Dunkin' Donuts franchisees offered $36 million for the chain's assets, a lawyer for the group told Projo.com, the website of The Providence Journal. Tim Hortons had struck a tentative deal with Bess Eaton, offering $35.5 million for 42 of the chain's 48 stores in New England, but a bankruptcy judge opened the process to other bidders. Under the deal it proposed, Tim Hortons would pay off the nearly $15 million that Bess Eaton owes to its creditors and another $14 million to cover owner Louis Gencarelli's personal debt. An auction -- as well as the potential sale of Bess Eaton -- is scheduled for tomorrow in US Bankruptcy Court. (AP)

Canton company signs 2 celebrities

Eli Manning and Kellen Winslow Jr., two of the top prospects in this weekend's National Football League draft and the sons of former NFL stars, signed multiyear endorsement contracts with Canton-based Reebok International Ltd. Manning, a quarterback from the University of Mississippi, might be the number one draft pick. Winslow is a tight end from the University of Miami. They will appear in a television commercial airing Saturday, the first day of the draft at New York's Madison Square Garden. Reebok did not disclose terms of the contracts. (Bloomberg)

Granby Telephone sold to N.J. company

Family-owned Granby Telephone and Telegram Co. has been sold to Country Road Communications of Morristown, N.J. The purchase price was not disclosed. George Mugnier, 70, who had served as president of the independent telephone company with 3,154 customers, told the Daily Hampshire Gazette that the family had been looking for a buyer for the past year. Country Road, headed by Harry Bennett, a former AT&T executive vice president, was founded in 1999 to purchase and operate rural local exchange companies. Granby, with 12 employees, is Country Road's third acquisition in New England, Bennett said. It owns Pine Tree Telephone & Telegraph Co. of Gray, Maine, and Saco River Telephone of Bar Mills, Maine. (AP)

THE NATION
US, China reach trade agreements

The United States and China reached agreements on a number of contentious trade issues as the Bush administration sought to demonstrate resolve in attacking America's soaring trade gap. China pledged to take steps ranging from cracking down on rampant copyright piracy of American products, such as movies and computer programs, to opening up the country's complicated goods distribution system to foreign firms. Another set of agreements dealt with promises China made to ensure its adoption of new standards for wireless computer and mobile phone transmissions will be fair to US companies. The agreements were reached during a meeting of the US-China Joint Commission on Commerce and Trade and unveiled at a joint news conference with US and Chinese officials. (AP)

FDA panel backs J&J neck artery stent

A Food and Drug Administration advisory panel voted to support Johnson & Johnson's application to market a new treatment for clearing clogged arteries in the neck, a major cause of strokes. The panel said the Precise stent, a tiny tube to prop arteries open, is safe and effective for use in the carotid arteries in the neck. The FDA usually follows its panels' advice. Inserting the stent is less invasive than the current surgical treatment for clearing blocked neck arteries, Johnson & Johnson told the panel. That procedure requires a two-inch incision in the neck. (Reuters)

FCC rules against AT&T on Web calls

US communications regulators dealt a blow to AT&T Corp., ruling that the telephone company must pay higher fees for connecting long-distance calls that are carried partly over the Internet. A unanimous Federal Communications Commission decided the biggest long-distance carrier improperly treated calls that only briefly traveled the Internet backbone as local calls, and therefore paid local phone companies fees lower than required. ''The carrier has long been obligated to pay access charges for this service and we unanimously confirm that it still is required to do so," FCC chairman Michael Powell said. The FCC did not address the question of whether AT&T owed back fees for the lower connection fees it started paying over a year ago. AT&T sharply criticized the decision, saying the FCC had reneged on past promises to ''resist applying legacy regulatory regimes to Internet telephony." (Reuters)

Judge rejects ex-Rite Aid CEO's plea deal

Sylvia H. Rambo, the federal judge handling the case of former Rite Aid Corp. chief executive Martin L. Grass, rejected a plea deal that had capped his potential prison time at eight years. After the deal is formally rejected at a hearing scheduled for next Thursday, Grass may withdraw his plea, setting the stage for a trial or negotiations on a new plea deal. ''All we can say is that Martin is considering his options and we will advise the court of his decision on April 29," Grass' defense lawyer, William H. Jeffress Jr., said in an e-mail. Assistant US Attorney Martin Carlson declined to comment. Grass pleaded guilty in June to conspiracy to obstruct justice and conspiracy to defraud for his role in an accounting scandal that led Rite Aid, the nation's third-largest pharmacy chain, to restate its earnings by $1.6 billion. (AP)

Mortgage index falls for fifth week

An index of US mortgage applications fell for a fifth straight week as a rise in borrowing costs made it less attractive for homeowners to refinance. The Mortgage Bankers Association's index of applications dropped 5.6 percent last week to 744.5, the lowest since early January, from 788.6. The index of applications to refinance existing loans, a source of extra cash for homeowners, dropped 10.9 percent to 2,550.3 from 2,861.6. The refinancing gauge has fallen 49 percent since reaching an eight-month high on March 19. Average rates on both 30-year and 15-year mortgages increased to their highest since the end of November. The average 30-year fixed mortgage rate rose to 5.84 percent last week from 5.77 percent. The 15-year rate rose to 5.16 percent from 5.08 percent. US mortgage loans this year are expected to decline 33 percent to $2.57 trillion from the record $3.8 trillion in 2003, the Mortgage Bankers Association reported April 14. (Bloomberg)
