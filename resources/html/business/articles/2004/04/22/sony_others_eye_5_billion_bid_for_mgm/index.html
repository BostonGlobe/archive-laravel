Sony, Others Eye $5 Billion Bid for MGM

By Dane Hamilton  |  April 21, 2004

NEW YORK (Reuters) - Legendary film studio Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Inc. <MGM.N> is in advanced talks to be bought by Sony Corp. <6758.T> and two buyout firms in a roughly $5 billion deal putting the owner of the James Bond movies back in play on Wall Street, sources said on Wednesday.

The studio, 74 percent-owned by billionaire Kirk Kerkorian and his Tracinda Corp investment firm, has been said to be either up for sale or a potential acquirer of media companies ever since the 86-year-old investor acquired it in 1996 for a third time.

The buyout firms joining Sony are Texas Pacific Group and Providence Equity Partners, sources said. The three are seen splitting a cash investment of $1.5 billion and borrowing the balance. MGM has hired investment bankers at Goldman Sachs to represent it, said sources who knew of the deal.

Key to an agreement would be whether MGM continues to pursue an expected one-time cash dividend of $8 a share. MGM said late Wednesday it would put the plan to a board vote when, as expected, it completes a refinancing package next week.

"They had talked to Sony (in the past). We knew that," said Hal Vogel, a New York-based fund manager. "They had talked to many people. This is one that seems to be serious."

MGM, Sony, Texas Pacific and Goldman Sachs all declined to comment. Providence Equity could not be reached. Other firms involved in talks have included Apax Partners, sources said.

People familiar with the deal said Sony initiated talks because it wants access to MGM's 4,000-plus film library that includes the James Bond and Pink Panther films and numerous Oscar winners. Libraries provide steady revenues from sales of DVDs and videos and TV licensing.

THE SONY FIT

"MGM has a wonderful library.... The problem has always been price," said Mario Gabelli, chief investment officer of Gabelli Asset Management, which holds MGM stock.

Mark Mobius, director of Templeton Asset Management Ltd, which manages stock in Sony, said a deal could make sense, given that Sony's electronics business is seeing greater competition, and owning the library could be lucrative.

"I would have to ask the management how they will integrate this kind of software business -- you know the movie business, MGM and so forth -- into the other parts of their business -- where these all fit," Mobius said.

A deal would allow MGM shareholders to cash out for roughly $21 a share. MGM shares ended up nearly 12 percent at $19.75 on the New York Stock Exchange after hitting a high of $20.25.

Kerkorian and Tracinda bought MGM for $1.3 billion in 1996 from an affiliate of French bank Credit Lyonnais after the studio had suffered years of financial losses. Chief Executive Alex Yemenidjian has engineered a turnaround and added to the library by acquiring other companies.

Some analysts saw the news as an opening round in what could be new bidding for MGM, but most noted that talk of MGM sales or acquisitions has surfaced often over the years.

BEEN THERE BEFORE

"I wouldn't take (the talk of a deal) as gospel," said David Miller, an analyst with Sanders Morris Harris. "This is kind of an opening round in a long, protracted negotiation." He put MGM's share value at close to $22.

Last summer MGM lost a heated battle to acquire Vivendi Universal Entertainment after offering around $11.5 billion for assets that included Universal Studios. General Electric Co.'s <GE.N> NBC television network bought the assets from Vivendi Universal <EAUG.PA><V.N>.

In January 2002, MGM was reported to have hired Goldman Sachs to auction the company for $7 billion, which would have represented a price of around $30 a share at the time.

Kerkorian is said to want to recoup at least part of his investment, and that desire has led to the one-time dividend. Shareholders have seen a volatile stock price in recent years as MGM served up box-office hits like "Legally Blonde" as well as expensive flops such as "Windtalkers."

Talk of a deal surfaced the same day the $2.4 billion loan MGM will use to finance its dividend was allocated, according to Loan Pricing Corp, a unit of Reuters Group Plc. MGM would be essentially debt free if it did not borrow to fund the payout.

"It doesn't make any sense to declare the distribution and then sell," Miller said, adding that either way, "Kirk is going to make his money back." (Additional reporting by Jeffrey Goldfarb, Julie MacIntosh and Bob Tourtellotte)
