When big banks merge, small banks see a chance to grow

By Davis Bushnell, Globe Correspondent  |  April 22, 2004

Chief executives of smaller banks in this region say they are eagerly awaiting a customer fallout from the recent merger of FleetBoston Financial Corp. and Bank of America.

At the same time, several of these community banks are on the move, their senior executives noted, opening new branches to try to lure customers of other mega-banks such as Sovereign, BankNorth, and Citizens.

Among the expanding community banks are Lowell-based Enterprise Bank & Trust Co., Lowell Co-operative Bank, and North Middlesex Savings Bank, headquartered in Ayer.

Also, Eastern Bank, which has corporate offices in Boston, is aggressively scouting new branch locations in an area extending from Woburn to Framingham, as part of a five-year expansion plan, its spokesman said.

Although competition is considerable among the scores of smaller banks in the region, there is plenty of business to go around, according to chief executives like George Duncan of Enterprise Bank, formed 15 years ago. It now has 12 branches.

"We don't have to steal customers from other small banks; rather, they come to us from large banks that don't deliver on personal, face-to-face service, which is the smaller banks' appeal," Duncan said.

Indeed, the big bank consolidations continue to be "very beneficial to smaller banks -- business is coming in their doors" all the time, said James Moynihan, a senior vice president-banking analyst in the Boston office of Advest Inc., an investment firm.

Enterprise Bank, through its expansions, is adding from 3,000 to 5,000 retail and commercial customers a year, Duncan said, adding that the total number currently is 38,000.

As a result, the bank's assets are growing at an annual clip of 15 percent, to $775 million, said Duncan and Jack Clancy, president of the holding company, Enterprise Bancorp Inc., in a conference call this week. The bank's trust and investment management business is also increasing annually, to some $350 million today, they said.

Last month, the bank, which has 260 full- and part-time employees, opened a branch in downtown Andover. By early next year, it will open a second office in Tewksbury, this one in the center of town, Duncan said. The existing branch is in North Tewksbury.

Other branches are in Lowell (two), Chelmsford (two), Billerica (two), and Westford, Dracut, Leominster, and Fitchburg.

At all locations, bank managers deal personally each business day with stockholders and other customers wanting updates on real estate and commercial loans or services for professionals such as lawyers, doctors, and accountants, Duncan said, emphasizing that those all are strong niches for the bank.

Enterprise's much smaller, but much older, competitor in the city, Lowell Co-op, recently opened its first branch at 691 Rogers St. in Lowell, in an effort "to get out into our community more," said Robert Houde, vice president of lending.

The 119-year-old bank, which has 31 employees, grew 15 percent last year, boosting assets to $94 million, Houde said. New customers, in undisclosed numbers, are also coming from large banks, adding to a base of longtime depositors. "I'm seeing the grandkids of people I first saw years and years ago," he said. The total number of customers is between 5,000 and 6,000, he added.

On the edges of Middlesex and Worcester counties, North Middlesex Savings opened a branch in Lunenburg last June and is now hammering away on a new office at Front and Frost streets in Shirley, which is expected to open in early September, treasurer Warren Chase said. Other branches are at the former Fort Devens complex and in Pepperell and Littleton.

Due to demand for services across the board, from real estate to commercial loans, in an area where the population is rapidly increasing, the bank's assets rose to $295 million in 2003 from $278 million the previous year, Chase said. In the last six years, the number of employees has increased from 55 to 78, he said.

"We're now trying to court some Fleet customers, and I'm sure that we'll be successful because of personal service," he said. "And, after all, how many people can talk to the treasurer of Bank of America? Well, anyone can come in and talk with me."

The total customer count currently is about 27,000, Chase said.

To the east, the medium-sized, fast-moving Eastern Bank, with assets of $4.7 billion and 1,500 employees in Greater Boston, is fine-tuning plans for opening a branch this fall at 1833 Massachusetts Ave. in Lexington, in what was once a florist shop, spokesman Joseph Bartolotta said. Its other branches in the northwest suburbs are in Burlington, Reading, Medford, and Stoneham. The bank does not disclose customer numbers.

Five years from now, the bank expects to have 18 to 20 new offices in the Boston area, he said. Now, there are 52 branches.

The region northwest of Boston is one of the best for the bank "in terms of density of population and the number of strong businesses," Bartolotta said.

Bank of America's acquisition of Fleet is "causing a marketplace disruption," to the benefit of Eastern and other community banks, he said. "We already have a recruitment plan in place" for employees who will be let go by Fleet "as well as for a number of people from other banks," he said, declining to be specific about recruitment goals.

As Duncan of Enterprise Bank put it: "The large bank mergers are feeding into the smaller banks."
