Q&A Jack Valenti on ways to eradicate movie piracy

April 25, 2004

Illegal Internet file swapping has traumatized the music industry. Is Hollywood next? Not if Jack Valenti can help it. Valenti's about to retire after nearly four decades as chairman of the Motion Picture Association of America (MPAA). But he's as outspoken as ever. Valenti spoke with Globe reporter Hiawatha Bray earlier this month on the right mix of new technologies and new laws that can stamp out the threat of movie piracy.

Are movie companies trying to keep their films off the Internet?

We believe that the Internet is the most astonishing and efficient distribution system ever known to man, and we want to use it. Our aim is to be able to have 20,000 titles available on the Internet, to go back to 1930, to the present, and in the future. Make those available on demand.

In order to do that, however, you need to be able to have some kind of sturdy protection so that people aren't going to abduct that film while it's en route to your home or mine, and then redistribute it on the Internet to 1 billion people around the world.

Are you counting on new security technologies to keep people from making illegal copies of films?

Right now, every one of our companies in the MPAA is talking to MIT, to Cal Tech, to IBM, to Microsoft, to Intel, to everybody in this business. Real Player and Microsoft have digital rights management designs that are helpful. So we're looking everywhere. We want the smartest people in the IT business to tell us what's the best way to protect this property.

But there was quite an uproar when you supported legislation that would have forced computer makers to build anticopying features into desktop PCs.

If you have a noncopyrighted piece of work -- you want to send something to your mother-in-law, pictures of the kids -- that wouldn't stop that. It's only a movie that comes out with something embedded in it, that says, "This is copyrighted; it can only be played if you have the code."

Recent court cases have held that software for using your computer to copy Hollywood DVDs is illegal. Don't people have a right to make backup copies of a movie they've paid for, in case the disc is damaged?

The minute you allow anybody to break the code to make a backup copy, it means they can make a thousand backup copies, because there's no technology I know of that says you can make one copy and then the thing explodes, like "Mission: Impossible."

But if the person really intends to make just one copy, does he have a right to do it?

No. How do you get in somebody's conscience and say, "Did I really just want to have it for my use?" You can't ask somebody to sign a pledge that I'm going to make just one copy, and I'm going to keep it in my room, and I'm not going to let it out.

PCs aren't the only way to copy a movie. Are you worried about people who bring handheld camcorders into theaters to copy the film right off the screen?

We found that some 80 to 85 percent of all the movies that are purloined in Asia and here are camcorded and then go on the Internet. So we've passed, in eight states, anticamcording bills, and there are another 11 or 12 states that are looking at it.

Don't camcorder copies of movies look and sound awful?

We put on a little show for some congressmen, in which we show a purloined camcorder [copy] of "Harry Potter." Very watchable, particularly the sound. And the image you see on the screen's terrific.

Every time you try to close a loophole, people find another one. Do you think the effort to stamp out movie piracy is ultimately hopeless?

No, I don't. It's possible that you can reduce the level of theft by making it so hard that only the hackers can do it. By the time somebody penetrates a thousand algorithms, it'll just be too frustrating.

And by this time, we bring our prices down. We can be on the Internet, and we can be offering these films at very modest prices.
