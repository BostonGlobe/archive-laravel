Mutual disrespect

April 25, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Even so, Columbia and Nations have managed to hold onto investors better than other competitors implicated in the industry's market-timing scandal. Columbia and Nations had modest flows of new money this year and last, according to Boston-based Financial Research Corp., while Putnam, Alliance Capital, Janus Capital Group, and MFS Investment Management have seen money run out the door either this year, or over the last several.

Ironically, the bank funds' pokey performance may have comforted their core customers, who tend to be more conservative investors who stick with mediocre funds through the highs and lows of the market in recent years; firms like Putnam, in addition to having their regulatory troubles paraded earlier than Nations and Columbia, may still be feeling the effects of so-called "hot money" that fled in droves after their go-go funds crashed spectacularly at the end of the late '90s bull market, analysts said.

Meanwhile, Charles Salmans, a spokesman for the new Bank of America asset management business, declined to comment specifically on fund performance. "But we're determined to emerge from this integration process with competitive funds, and a competitive product set," he said, "and we feel are in a position to move forward in a very competitive way."

Spotty performance isn't unusual among mutual fund companies. "It's pretty typical that you're going to have a few pockets of good funds, and then a whole lot of mediocrity," said Phil Edwards, director of fund research at Standard & Poor's. "I don't think Columbia stands out being extremely good or extremely bad. They're extremely typical. Nations funds, I do think, are overall relatively weaker," he said.

Edwards is more downbeat about Nations funds than Morningstar because he said most of that firm's best performers are several large-cap stock funds overseen by investment guru Tom Marsico, whose firm, Marsico Capital Management, is owned by Bank of America. Without Marsico, Edwards said, Nations has few other funds worth bragging about.

The other standout is in foreign investing. Nations' International Value fund is one of the best performers in its category over the past five years, and a second international fund, run by Marsico, has a five-star rating from Morningstar.

"We found foreign funds have their strength," said Judson Gee, a financial planner in Charlotte, N.C., whose firm puts a good portion of its $30 million in mutual fund assets in those Nations funds. But excluding the Marsico funds, Gee said the rest of Bank of America's funds are "middle of the track -- nothing that's going to make me jump up and down and shout: 'Nations is the place to park all our money.' "

The two firms have different long-term records. For example, 30 percent of Nations' mutual funds that have been around for 10 years had annual average returns that ranked them in the bottom fifth of their respective categories, according to Morningstar.

Meanwhile, just 7 percent of Columbia funds scored in that bottom group over the 10-year period, while 25 percent of its funds were in the top fifth of their peer groups over that time.

Fixed-income investing is perhaps Columbia's best strength, analysts said. The Columbia Income fund, for example, is near the top of the list of intermediate bond funds over both five- and 10-year performance periods, and it has some of the best single-state municipal bond funds around.

And a handful of small- and mid-cap stock funds, both growth and value-style funds, also have run up top-level long-term performances and won high ratings, as analysts single out Acorn and Acorn Select funds run by its Liberty Wanger Asset Management LP unit in Chicago.

Though Bank of America officials won't say what their plans are, "if you take their strengths, it suggests there are good synergies" among the two combining fund operations, said Edwards, the S&P analyst: With Nations good in large-cap and international funds, and Columbia strong in mid- and small-cap stock funds and in fixed income, there isn't a lot of overlap. However, that also suggests large areas within each company where funds are vulnerable to elimination.

But interviews with investors reveal that the persistent uncertainty about the future look of the combined company remains Bank of America's most immediate money-management challenge.

"We are not adding money, because of our fear of the future," said Cheryl A. Patterson, partner in Hart & Patterson Financial Services, LLP, a large financial planning firm in Amherst, who noted that the Columbia funds where she has kept clients' money "have been average."
