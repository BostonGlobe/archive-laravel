Racing to stay in place

April 18, 2004

Page 6 of 6 -- LINDSEY CLARKEBack Bay

La vida loca

ADVERTISEMENT

Currently, I work full time as an admissions counselor at the New England Conservatory of Music. I am a part-time master's student in guidance counseling at Boston University. On Saturdays, I teach chamber music at the Longy School of Music, and on Sundays I tutor students in standardized testing for Inspirica Tutors.

My life is pretty crazy! My boyfriend will be the first to attest to this. However, the money that I make at NEC is not enough to pay my rent, bills, and living expenses. I am not bitter about my situation, because I truly love what I do. I just wish that I didn't have to do so much!

ERICA KLEIN

Somerville

The parent trap

I wouldn't say that my wife and I have to make ends meet, but it seems that just to maintain a middle-class lifestyle requires much more work than it did when I grew up.

My wife works part time for the Boston public schools, and I work two different half-time scientific research jobs.

We have twin boys who are 21 months old, and we coparent them to save money. I stay home with them while she works, and vice versa. To put them in day care would cost at least as much as the extra money my wife would make if she worked full time, so it didn't seem worth it to us.

Our mortgage and higher education debt consume a large portion of our income. Although my wife has the summers off, I work a third job teaching a summer class to try to stay ahead of the bills.

HAROLD CHEYNE

Roslindale

Fired up

I am a firefighter in the town of Brookline. Like most firefighters, we don't make too much money with our base salary. There is overtime and fire details, but you can't count on something that isn't guaranteed. So I have to work a second job three to four days a week. That equals two 24-hour shifts at the firehouse a week and three eight-hour shifts at my second job, for a total of 72 hours a week of work, not including commuting time.

Then Ty Law says he is being underpaid at $8 million a year. As much as it is hard to manage, it just makes me sick to hear these athletes complain about how they are being screwed out of money.

Why don't they give some of their salary to people like you and I who have to worry about making the mortgage and car payments?

Sorry to ramble.

OWEN THOMPSON

Melrose

A matter of degree

I am not your typical student. I got out of an abusive marriage with two kids and two suitcases and started from zero. I am a single mom, living in a cheap apartment, and going to college full time for my second degree (legal). The three of us have lived on a $1,400 budget per month for the past 2 years. I did unimaginable jobs for two years to get the tuition together. My kids complained a lot, but they will understand one day.

They wrote in their school that I am their ''hero." I still have my sense of humor. My biggest wish is to live in my own home and not to worry about what I eat tomorrow.

I have three semesters left to get my degree.

GEORGINA WHITTINGHAM

Belmont

Working five jobs

I'm a teacher. I work one full-time job and four part-time jobs. I teach full time in Winthrop, which, according to Boston Magazine, is the 48th lowest-paying town in the state. I teach after school two days a week, teach Saturday school in Roxbury, tutor on Sundays, and work at Fidelity over the majority of school vacations: Christmas, February, April, and seven weeks of summer vacations.

I love to work hard, and I'm proud of my work ethic, but financially I'm still scraping by.

I wake up at 4 a.m. to go to the gym. I clean my house on Sunday mornings and weekdays at 4 a.m. Tomorrow morning we're going shopping at 4 a.m. at the neighborhood 24-hour Stop & Shop. A lot of work gets done between 4-6:30 a.m.

Thankfully, I'm married to a great guy who works long hours and is working on his master's. He was working two jobs, but dropped his part-time job to concentrate on his master's.

As for managing, my husband is in charge of family finance and making lunches. We eat in six days a week and eat out Saturday nights (occasionally Friday nights). We shop for healthy lunch food that's good on the go.

I carry my cellphone with me and make important phone calls during my breaks. I feel like I'm always preparing for my next gig, and, when I'm not working, I'm sleeping or trying hard to relax.

When my husband completes his master's, we might reevaluate moving to a more affordable (and sunnier) region of the country. Most of my family is in Boston, so, in all honesty , I love Boston and feel really attached.

I want to stay, but it's frustrating having a master's and living paycheck to paycheck.

RACHEL WEINSTEIN

Revere
