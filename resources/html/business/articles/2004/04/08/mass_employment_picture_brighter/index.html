BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Mass. employment picture brighter

April 8, 2004

THE REGION

One in three large employers said they planned to hire in the first half of this year, according to a Massachusetts survey. Administrative support firms, which include temporary help agencies, were most optimistic about hiring, a positive sign since temp hiring tends to precede permanent employment, the survey said. Hiring in professional and technical services, a key sector, is also beginning to thaw while healthcare continues its strong demand for workers, after accounting for 23 percent of all job vacancies in the last quarter of 2003, when the survey was conducted by the Division of Unemployment Assistance. But the overall job vacancy rate, which measures the availability of jobs, improved only slightly in that period, to 2.2 percent from 1.7 percent a year earlier. (Robert Gavin)

Plan filed for Nashua Street project

A proposal to build a $140 million tower on Nashua Street next to the FleetCenter was filed with the Boston Redevelopment Authority. Boston Garden Development Corp. plans to build a 37-story tower with 368 condos and rental apartments and 244 parking spaces. Plans call for the project to be completed in 2007. Boston Garden Development is a subsidiary of Delaware North Cos., which owns the FleetCenter and whose chairman and chief executive is Boston Bruins owner Jeremy M. Jacobs. The tower is the first phase of a larger project that envisions offices and shops on the site of the old Boston Garden. (Chris Reidy)

Robertson Stephens fined by NASD

Robertson Stephens Inc. and a former top investment banker at the firm have been fined a total $350,000 by securities regulators for allegedly trying to extort $1 million from a company as a condition for continued research coverage. The National Association of Securities Dealers, the brokerage industry's self-policing organization, disclosed the civil fines of $275,000 against now defunct Robertson Stephens, which was owned by FleetBoston Financial Corp., and $75,000 against Richard Davies, who worked in the firm's New York City office. They neither admitted to nor denied the allegations in their settlements with the NASD. The NASD said that in June 2001 Davies threatened an official of ResMed Inc. with dropping Robertson Stephens' research coverage of the maker of respiratory devices unless it paid $1 million in investment banking fees for work on a ResMed bond offering. At the time, Robertson Stephens' analysts rated ResMed as a "buy." ResMed officials did not pay the money, the NASD said, and Robertson Stephens did not participate in the bond offering. A few weeks later, the investment firm issued a research report changing its rating for ResMed to a "market perform," less desirable than a "buy." (Bloomberg)

Indevus sells rights to bladder drug

Pliva D.D., Eastern Europe's biggest drug maker, said it bought US rights to a treatment for overactive bladder from Indevus Pharmaceuticals Inc. for as much as $215 million. Croatia-based Pliva will pay $30 million now and $120 million if the drug, called Sanctura, gets US approval, which may occur by midyear, the company said. Lexington-based Indevus can also get a $45 million payment linked to development of an extended release version of the product and $20 million in 2013 if the drug reaches commercial goals. (Bloomberg)

Vaso Active will cooperate with SEC

Vaso Active Pharmaceuticals Inc. of Danvers said it is committed to providing full cooperation in addressing Securities and Exchange Commission concerns. "As this matter is in its early stages, it would be inappropriate to provide a more detailed comment at this time," Vaso said. The SEC temporarily suspended Vaso's shares last Thursday, citing questions regarding the accuracy of assertions in company press releases, its annual report, its registration statement, and public statements. This month, an article by TheStreet.com accused Vaso of falsely claiming on its website that a study of its antifungal product was conducted by the New England Medical Center. (Dow Jones)

THE NATION

Dell boosts first-quarter forecasts

Dell Inc., the world's largest personal computer maker, said first-quarter sales will beat its previous estimates after demand for computer servers in Europe and Asia rose faster than expected. Sales will probably be $11.4 billion, exceeding the earlier forecast of $11.2 billion, Dell said. Per-share earnings will be 28 cents, in line with the company's predictions made Feb. 12. Chief executive Michael Dell has boosted sales for nine straight quarters and has said the company is receiving more requests from customers for proposals to supply machines. Demand is rising mainly outside the United States, he said. New products such as printers and imaging are also helping bolster sales. (Bloomberg)

Pension-costs bill said likely to pass

The Senate will pass a bill saving companies as much as $80 billion in pension costs over the next two years, said Senator Charles Grassley, an Iowa Republican, one of the sponsors of the legislation. A vote is scheduled for today. The legislation would enable companies including DuPont Co., Raytheon Co., and Delta Air Lines Inc. to cut required pension contributions almost in half this year. Grassley faces objections from Democrats who say the bill shortchanges so-called multi-employer pension plans, run jointly by companies and unions. Senator Edward M. Kennedy, a Massachusetts Democrat who leads the opposition, said passage by the House on April 2 gave the bill momentum that's hard to stop. If he fails, Kennedy will try to add a provision affecting multi-employer plans to a $145 billion corporate tax bill the Senate is debating, said his spokesman, Jim Manley. Approval would send the bill to President Bush to sign. The legislation would change the formula by which companies calculate pension payments, in time to avoid large deposits that otherwise are due April 15. In addition, steel makers and airlines that have suffered losses due to economic conditions would save an additional $1.6 billion by temporarily delaying required contributions to their plans. (Bloomberg)

FDA accuses J & J of mishandling report

Johnson & Johnson, the world's biggest maker of medical devices, mishandled reports of blood clots in people implanted with its Cypher heart device and violated US manufacturing rules, regulators said. The Food and Drug Administration sent J&J an eight-page letter detailing flaws in paperwork and testing at plants that make the Cypher stent, a mesh tube used to keep blood flowing in coronary arteries. The FDA has not asked for a recall of Cypher stents, J&J said. The FDA has been monitoring how often Cypher patients develop blood clots. Several hundred 2002 and 2003 Cypher complaints hadn't been fully investigated by late last year when the FDA inspected plants in the United States, Italy, the Netherlands, and Belgium, the agency said. (Bloomberg)

Shares of First Marblehead Corp., which provides student loans, gained 9.7 percent after the Boston company said third-quarter loan volume jumped and fiscal 2004 earnings will increase more than its forecast on rising demand. Net income rose to 14 cents a share from $46.7 million, or 8 cents, a year ago, the company said. Revenue more than doubled to $757.8 million from $292.9 million. (Globe wire services)
