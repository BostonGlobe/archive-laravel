BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Court OK's Genzyme's buyout of Impath

April 1, 2004

Genzyme Corp. won court approval to buy Impath Inc.'s cancer-testing business for $215 million. Impath will use money from the sale to repay its creditors in full under a reorganization plan, said George A. Davis, the company's bankruptcy lawyer. Impath filed for bankruptcy in September after the Securities and Exchange Commission opened a formal investigation into its accounting. The purchase, which is expected to close by the end of April, is Cambridge-based Genzyme's third in six months as the company expands its product portfolio to make up for slowing demand for its two best-selling products, the enzyme-replacement therapy Cerezyme and the dialysis drug Renagel. (Bloomberg)

Mass. again tops tech economy list

Massachusetts remains the state best positioned to take advantage of a high-technology economy, while Mississippi lags the rest of the nation, according to a new study. The Milken Institute, a Los Angeles-area think tank backed by former junk bond king Michael Milken, used 75 separate measurements to come up with its State Technology and Science Index, the second produced by the institute. The same states occupied the top five and bottom five spots as in the last index, though in slightly different order. California rose to number two, while Colorado slipped to three. (Reuters)

R/D Tech signs lease in Waltham

R/D Tech Inc., which makes nondestructive testing systems, has signed a five-year, 56,826-square-foot lease at 48 Woerd Ave. in Waltham, a building bought this year by Intercontinental Real Estate Corp. for $18.8 million. R/D Tech is subleasing from Cisco Systems Inc. and will put its Panametrics NDT division, acquired in January from General Electric Co., in the space. R/D Tech was represented by Richards Barry Joyce & Partners, which also brokered a 45,000-square-foot lease for the accounting firm Carlin, Charron & Rosen, which is consolidating its Newton and Worcester offices in new corporate headquarters at 1400 Computer Drive, Westborough. The building is owned by Massachusetts Laborers' Pension Fund and Westborough Investment Corp. (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

Ilex seeks FDA approval for drug

Ilex Oncology Inc., maker of the Campath cancer medicine, applied for US approval of its clofarabine drug for children at risk of dying from leukemia that has resisted other treatments. Ilex said it completed a Food and Drug Administration application for approval of clofarabine, which would be the company's second product. Genzyme Corp. last month agreed to buy San Antonio's Ilex for about $1 billion in stock. Peak clofarabine sales might reach $100 million a year, said Piper Jaffray & Co. analyst Sandeep Bhatia in a report. (Bloomberg)

Jones files complaint against Maxwell

Jones Apparel Group Inc. filed a complaint against Maxwell Shoe Co., alleging it ''improperly" tried to stop Jones's $297 million hostile takeover of the Hyde Park company. Maxwell had said shareholders of record on March 25 were eligible to participate in Jones's consent solicitation, in which they would be asked to support Jones's move to replace Maxwell's board. The company's setting of the record date is ''invalid" under Delaware law because Jones hasn't begun the solicitation process, the Bristol, Pa.-based Jones said. ''The company believes the lawsuit is without merit," Maxwell spokesman Dan Katcher said. ''We intend to vigorously defend against it." (Bloomberg)

Analog Devices wins telecom award

The Massachusetts Telecommunications Council, an industry trade group, last night named semiconductor maker Analog Devices Inc. of Norwood state telecom company of the year. Maria Cirino, former chief executive of network-security systems maker Guardent Inc. of Waltham, acquired by VeriSign Inc. in December, was named CEO of the year. Council members also voted to pick Comcast Corp.'s video-on-demand service as product of the year and inducted Rubin Gruber, founder and chairman of Sonus Networks Inc. of Chelmsford, to its telecom hall of fame in recognition of his founding four companies and investing in 10 start-ups that went public. (Peter J. Howe)

Benson tries out online pharmacy

While researching the safety of prescription drugs, New Hampshire Governor Craig Benson ordered brand-name medicines from a Canadian online pharmacy for about half the cost of purchasing the same drugs in the United States. Benson used his credit card to buy the drugs from the website of CanadaDrugs.com and had the medicine mailed to his home. He hopes his findings encourage the federal government to give New Hampshire a waiver to order drugs from Canadian pharmacies for Medicaid patients and state prisoners. The Food and Drug Administration issued a condemnation of Benson's actions. (AP)

THE NATION
Novoste to cut 86 as business slows

Novoste Corp., which repairs stents that keep heart arteries open, is firing 86 workers, or 40 percent of its workforce, because the introduction of drug-coated stents has reduced demand for its services. Most of the jobs cuts are in Georgia. ''We're seeing a decline in our business because these stents fail at about half the rate as stents that don't have medicine on them," chief executive Alfred Novak said in an interview. Johnson & Johnson introduced drug-coated stents in April 2003 and Natick's Boston Scientific Corp. began selling them last month. Most of the job cuts, which involved workers in operations, quality assurance and engineering, occurred at two facilities in Norcross, Ga., where Novoste is based, Novak said. The company didn't cut any sales jobs. In August, Novoste cut 50 jobs, most of them at Norcross. (Bloomberg)

THE WORLD
Japan OK's drug-coated stent, J&J says

Johnson & Johnson said regulators in Japan approved its Cypher drug-coated stent, a device to prop open cleared arteries that has already become a blockbuster product in the United States. J&J said the Japanese Ministry of Health, Labor and Welfare had approved the stent, which is coated with a drug that prevents it from clogging with scar tissue once inserted into coronary arteries that have been cleared of fatty plaque. Japan is the world's second-biggest market for drugs and stents. J&J, based in New Brunswick, N.J., said it aims to launch Cypher in Japan by the middle of the year, making it the first drug-coated stent available in the country. (Reuters)

Raytheon, of Waltham, said if it wins the work for ultra high-frequency long-range early warning radar systems it will send the work to facilities in Alabama, Massachusetts, and Maryland . . . M-Qube Inc., a Boston start-up that provides services for downloading games, songs, and other content to wireless phones, has bought Sharp Robot, a 17-person Toronto company with a similar market focus whose customers include big Canadian carrier Telus Mobility. Terms are not being disclosed. . . . Internet search engine Google Inc. said it began testing a preview of a new, free and searchable e-mail, called Gmail. The e-mail service will allow users to search every message they've sent or received, Google said. (Globe staff and wire services)
