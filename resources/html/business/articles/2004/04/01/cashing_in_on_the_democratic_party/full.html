Cashing in on the Democratic party

Area homeowners see dollar signs when delegates come to Boston

By Mary Mulkerin Donius, Globe Correspondent  |  April 1, 2004

Ron Tashjian, a single father of grown children, is really looking forward to the Democratic National Convention in Boston this summer. Not that he plans to attend; he isn't even a Democrat.

ADVERTISEMENT

But Tashjian, who is retired, is hoping his 1,600-square-foot, three-bedroom, 1 1/2-bath Garrison Colonial in Melrose will get snatched up by delegates in town for the weeklong party at the end of July.

The fact that the house is 12 miles from the FleetCenter, and that Interstate 93 and North Station may be closed for security purposes, has not dampened this enthusiasm, or his expectations.

He's asking $6,250. For a week.

"It's not outrageous," he says of the steep asking price. "The nice hotels get at least $300 per night per couple, so if I get two couples, it's in the ballpark," he says.

Never mind that with only one shower, his deep-pocketed renters might have to wait in line for the bathroom.

Tashjian is not alone. Homeowners from Melrose to Quincy and as far west as Hopkinton are seeing dollar signs as talk of the convention and all of its potential windfalls and logistical nightmares begin to intensify. With four months to go, many are using the Internet and their personal connections to get the word out that

their homes are available for a price. And you don't need a brownstone in Louisburg Square or a fab penthouse at The Four Seasons to dream about the possibilities. Tashjian says his home -- with laundry facilities and an Internet hookup, plus his willingness to come in and pick up and change the sheets every two days -- "affords more than the average hotel room."

Ann Collard is hopeful, too. If things go her way, she and her husband, David, will bunk with friends while convention-goers take over their Wellesley home during the last week in July. The convention runs July 26-29.

Both Tashjian and Collard posted their homes on craigslist.org, a free website specializing in classified advertising, and are hoping to lure a couple of well-heeled short-term renters when an estimated 35,000 out-of-towners pour into Boston for the Convention.

Other would-be landlords are using personal connections and home-swapping services to try to work out a temporary real estate deal that most agents want no part of because of the short-term nature of the leases and the difficulty of qualifying out-of-town renters, sight unseen.

It wouldn't be the first time Collard voluntarily hit the streets. In 1999 when "on a lark," she listed her family's smartly decorated four-bedroom Tudor

with a bed-and-breakfast agency, hoping to get in on the frenzy surrounding the Ryder Cup at The Country Club in Brookline. Houses on the famed golf course were claiming upward of $50,000 from the golf world and corporate entities, and Collard thought she'd try to get a piece of the action. It worked. Collard landed former professional golfer and British television broadcaster Tony Jacklin and two of his business associates and their wives for a week, pocketing $9,000 for her trouble. She also arranged for a local caterer to see to Jacklin's entertaining needs and hired a cleaning service to come in daily. Her tenants cheerfully paid for it all.

"I did it on a fluke and it worked out really, really well," says Collard, a health policy researcher at the Schneider Institute for Health Policy at the Heller School at Brandies University. She shrugged off warnings from friends that strangers could damage her home.

"This isn't a fussy house," she says. "I raised three kids here. If someone spills wine on the carpet, we'll get it cleaned."

The only person who wasn't delighted with the arrangement, she acknowledges, was her son Tim, then 18, who complained about the family's having to move in with neighbors for a week. "But I gave him $100 and never heard another word about it."

Daniela Silver, a 26-year-old event planner, also sees an opportunity for a cash windfall. Her father, Richard Silver, says his daughter has always been entrepreneurial and that it was her idea to list her parents' spectacular seven-level, three-bedroom, three-bath home near Harvard Square on craigslist.org for $30,000 for the convention week. The eye-popping asking price, she thinks, is justified.

"Compared to what else is out there, we have such a unique house and such a great location," Silver says, "I'm feeling confident."

Daniela grew up in the house but no longer lives there. She adds, "After we put all the effort in it, I'll be disappointed if it doesn't work out."

That's understandable, especially since her father promised her a big chunk of the fee if they get a renter.

Richard Silver says that the contemporary, spacious home with an indoor hammock in the living area would be ideal for party bigwigs who would be interested in luxury accommodations and the ability to entertain in a private yard. Daniela says her father was on board with the idea from the beginning. Her mother, she says, took a bit more convincing and is still a little uncomfortable about turning her home over to strangers.

Perhaps she'd be more comfortable with the deal that Cindy Rehm has brokered with a member of John Kerry's staff. Rehm, a 40-year-old financial consultant, is trading her two-bedroom Beacon Hill condo during the Convention for a week at a staff member's four-bedroom house in Sag Harbor in the Hamptons with a pool that's just one block from the beach.

Rehm, who got the lead from a friend, has discussed ground rules regarding pets, parties, and smoking with her house-exchange partner, but they've never met. She says she never would have rented to a stranger but feels comfortable swapping.

"We've spoken on the phone, we've set up the rules, I feel comfortable," says Rehm. She thinks she's getting a great deal and doesn't feel that she's leaving any money on the table when other Back Bay and Beacon Hill dwellers are asking in the tens of thousands for that week.

Boston has always been a popular destination for home exchangers, expecially during the summer. Exchanging homes is a popular way for people to vacation in places they couldn't ordinarily afford. The Democratic National Convention in Boston is likely to lead to a spike in home swapping in the area, according to Ed Kushins, president of HomeExchange.com, a leading home swap website. Mary Cullen of Amesbury and her family are devotees of home exchanging, an economical way to travel in which individuals, couples, or families decide to swap homes for vacation. But money never changes hands. They've made 10 major trips in the past three years.

"People either think we're really resourceful or really crazy," she says.

Cullen, her husband, and their two children say they've never had a bad experience exchanging

homes with people in Ireland, Scotland, New York, California, and Canada. Home exchange has grown in popularity with the Internet, but it wasn't invented there. Judson Hale, editor-in chief of Yankee Publishing Inc., says home swaps have been a staple of Yankee magazine's "Swopper's Column" since it began in 1935.

The magazine receives many more swaps -- or swops, to use its folksy spelling -- than it can publish, most from people seeking a week in a New England home in exchange for a week in another country or another part of the United States. "This month's issue of Yankee contains the following offer: "Will swop paranormal research and investigation of your B&B or inn for a 1- to 2-night stay during off-season weekdays."

Mary Kay Donovan, a real estate broker in Charlestown, says she's been approached by local residents to act as broker for renting their home or condo during the convention.

She has declined, saying the rental is so short-term that she doesn't have the ability to do the background and credit checks that she would normally do.

"I don't want the liability," she says.

Yet Donovan spent $175 to list her own home on www.2004dnc.com, a website that she erroneously thought was an official Democratic Party website. So far she's had no response.

The website she used, like many others, is set up to look like it's sanctioned by the DNC, but instead is a for-profit venture by a private company and has no DNC affiliation.

Unlike Collard, Donovan doesn't see many parallels between the Ryder Cup in 1999 and this summer's convention.

"I think a lot of people are thinking of these events in the same way and they're totally different things. (At the Ryder Cup) there were mansions on the golf course that people were getting tons of money for," she says. "The convention is going to be a different kind of event. The hotels are booked, traffic is going to be horrendous, they're going to be looking for convenience."

Ken Stone, another Charlestown broker, says he doesn't think he'd be "doing any favors" if he helped arrange short-term rentals for his customers during the Convention.

"People think they're going to rent for big bucks," he says. "But it isn't going to happen."
