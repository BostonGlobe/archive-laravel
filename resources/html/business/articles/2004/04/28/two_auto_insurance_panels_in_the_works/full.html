Two auto insurance panels in the works

April 28, 2004

The auto insurance overhaul effort is getting crowded: Governor Mitt Romney is planning to unveil a task force to study the issue, and the House has approved a budget amendment that would set up a $400,000 commission financed by a 10-cent assessment on each auto insurance policy. The amendment, proposed by Democratic Representative Anne M. Paulsen of Belmont, calls for ''nine truly consumer-oriented individuals with no affiliations with either the insurance industry or the trial bar." The panel would draft legislation and complete a report by July 2005. Representative Ronald Mariano of Quincy, Democratic chairman of the Legislature's Insurance Committee and a likely member of the governor's task force, opposes Paulsen's amendment. (Bruce Mohl)

Firms face securities-fraud charges

Massachusetts Secretary of State William F. Galvin charged two companies with securities-related fraud yesterday, saying they set up phony firms to collect fees on stock trades they never completed. His office issued administrative complaints against RJL International Limited and Phoenix Capital Services Inc., seeking cease and desist orders and the return of money. RJL and Phoenix Capital allegedly would contact investors in small public companies whose stock trades over the counter and claim to represent anonymous buyers who would pay as much as 200 times the trading price. The firms required the investor to pay 7 to 40 percent of the purchase price upfront until the transaction was complete, which never happened, Galvin's office said. One investor allegedly lost $19,000 to RJL. (AP)

Medicaid claims system to be overhauled

The state is seeking bids to replace its 20-year-old Medicaid claims system. The health insurance program for the poor accounts for 70 percent of the $11 billion annual budget of the Executive Office of Health and Human Services. Bids are due July 7. The contract, to be funded partly by the federal government, is worth up to $77 million. State officials hope to implement the new system, to handle 75 million claims per year, by mid-2006. (Beth Healy)

Canadian Sears to get Big and Tall stores

Casual Male Retail Group Inc. said it struck a deal with Sears Canada Inc. to open Casual Male Big and Tall stores inside a dozen Sears locations this fall, including Toronto and Ottawa. If the venture is successful, the Canton company plans to open another 68 Big and Tall operations in Sears Canada stores next year. The companies declined to comment on finances. Casual Male said the move is aimed at increasing sales and raising brand awareness worldwide. Its apparel is expected to be sold in Sears Canada's catalog and on its website in 2005. (Naomi Aoki)

Phone-tower firm gets $10m in funding

Optasite Inc., a Worcester cellphone-tower development company whose chief executive cofounded American Tower Corp., has received a $10 million round of venture capital funding from Highland Capital Partners of Lexington and several other investors. Jim Eisenstein, Optasite's CEO, who formed industry leader American Tower with Steve Dodge in 1995, said the funding could support siting and construction of 50 Optasite-owned towers as larger companies like American Tower and Crown Castle International Corp. focus on improving cash flow and profitability. Optasite, founded in 1996, plans to focus on the Northeast and look for opportunities to buy smaller tower owners. (Peter J. Howe)

7-Eleven offering prepaid wireless plan

About 200 7-Eleven Inc. stores in New England are beginning to sell a prepaid wireless phone plan that offers local and long-distance calls for 20 cents a minute, regardless of time or day. The Dallas-based chain's Speak Out service uses the Cingular Wireless network and allows customers to extend unused minutes for up to 120 days when they buy more calling time in blocks of at least $25. The service requires buying one of two Nokia phone models that sell for $90 or $50 after a $50 rebate, and includes text messaging for 10 cents per message. (Peter J. Howe)

Sonus to restate 2002 financial results

Sonus Networks Inc., which makes telephone-network equipment, expects to restate results for 2002 and the first three quarters of 2003. All of the transactions under review are valid sales, the Chelmsford company said. Sonus has said it is assessing when, not whether, certain sales should be recognized as revenue. ''We take this situation very seriously and we have built strong partnerships with our customers," CEO Hassan Ahmed said. The company will also review how it booked sales before 2002. Sonus had reported a 2002 net loss of $68.5 million on sales of $62.6 million. For the first three quarters of 2003, it reported a net loss of $6.38 million and sales of $66 million. (Bloomberg)

Ex-Firepond executive accused of fraud

A former software executive has been charged with fraud for allegedly faking sales. James E. Reid, 56, of Waterloo, Ontario, Canada, was arrested as he attempted to enter the United States in Niagara Falls, N.Y., the US attorney's office in Boston said. Firepond, formerly of Waltham but now in Minneapolis, develops software for manufacturers of complex machinery. Reid was a sales executive for Firepond beginning in March 2001, then acting vice president for North American sales, according to the indictment. Prosecutors said he faked more than $4.8 million in three contracts and received $156,000 in commissions on bogus sales. He is charged with mail and wire fraud. The Securities and Exchange Commission also filed a civil fraud action. (AP)

Quattrone denies he obstructed justice

Former star technology banker Frank Quattrone took the witness stand at his retrial and denied he obstructed justice by sending an e-mail in 2000 encouraging the destruction of files. He testified his e-mail had ''nothing to do" with a warning he received two days earlier that his bank, Credit Suisse First Boston, was the subject of a criminal probe into stock allocation. He is charged with obstruction of justice and witness tampering. His first trial ended in a hung jury last fall. Yesterday, he recounted a warning from CSFB lawyer David Brodsky on Dec. 3, 2000, that a federal grand jury was looking into allocations of initial public offerings by the bank. But Quattrone said such allocations were handled by a different part of his bank. (AP)

Wyeth to fight $1b fen-phen award

A Texas jury awarded over $1 billion to the family of a woman who died after using one of the drugs in the fen-phen diet drug regimen, said Wyeth, maker of the drug. The company, though, said there is a strong basis to overturn the ruling based on ''legal errors." Also, Texas caps punitive damages, which could substantially shrink the award to the family of Cynthia Cappel-Coffey, who used Pondimin during a three-month period. An attorney for Wyeth said he expects the verdict to be overturned, or the award reduced to two times actual damages, or about $5 million. Wyeth has already taken $16.6 billion in charges for past and future payments to the 6 million Americans who took fen-phen. Some patients developed heart valve problems. (Reuters)

Global Crossing to redo results

Telecommunications carrier Global Crossing Ltd. said it expects to restate last year's results since it underestimated some costs by $50 million to $80 million. The Florham Park, N.J., company's shares dropped $5, or 27 percent, to $13.20. The costs in question are charges paid to other carriers for, among other things, originating and terminating calls. (Dow Jones/AP)
