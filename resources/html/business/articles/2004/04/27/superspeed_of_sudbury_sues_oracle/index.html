BUSINESS IN BRIEF

SuperSpeed of Sudbury sues Oracle

April 27, 2004

THE REGION

SuperSpeed of Sudbury sues Oracle

Oracle Corp. was sued by SuperSpeed Software Inc., which claims patent infringement for a method of increasing data-processing speed. SuperSpeed, of Sudbury, claimed Oracle infringed on three patents related to caching, which stores a copy of frequently accessed data in the memory of the individual computer that's using the data. The lawsuit, seeking a court order blocking any further infringement and cash compensation, was filed April 15 in US District Court in Corpus Christi, Texas. "We are aware that SuperSpeed Software has filed a suit in court without ever contacting Oracle," said Deborah Lilienthal, spokeswoman for Redwood City, Calif.-based Oracle. "Once we have had time to review the complaint, we will respond appropriately in court." (Bloomberg)

SEC WorldCom counsel promoted

The Securities and Exchange Commission promoted Peter H. Bresnan, 49, who served as the agency's lead counsel in the WorldCom Inc. case, to associate director in the enforcement division. He recently served as an acting district administrator in Boston after Juan Marcelino resigned amid reports the SEC was slow to respond to a whistle-blower at Putnam Investments. Since November 2001, Bresnan has also served as deputy chief litigation counsel. In his new position, Bresnan will assist in planning and directing investigations and other enforcement efforts, the agency said. (Bloomberg)

THE NATION

Janus close to settling with regulators

Janus Capital Group is close to settling alleged trading violations, people close to the talks said, with one saying the mutual fund firm will pay more than $200 million in fines and disgorgement of profits. The company, which posts first-quarter earnings tomorrow, announced the resignation of CEO Mark Whiston last week. A settlement is expected with New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer, the Securities and Exchange Commission, and Colorado Attorney General Ken Salazar. One source said the agreement would require Janus to cut fees. Janus has already said it would reimburse shareholders $31.5 million for abusive trading. Janus was one of four companies named in a $40 million settlement between Spitzer and hedge fund Canary Capital Partners LLC in September. Janus allegedly let Canary make trades not allowed for smaller investors, in return for investments with big fees. (Reuters)

Kmart, Martha Stewart end dispute

Kmart Holding Corp. strengthened its partnership with Martha Stewart's company yesterday, dropping a lawsuit about royalty payments. In addition, the two companies are extending their licensing agreement by two years, to 2009. Stewart's March 5 conviction of obstructing justice and lying about a stock sale does not appear to have hurt the partnership. "We are pleased to have extended and deepened our relationship with Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia," Kmart chief executive Julian Day said. Troy, Mich.-based Kmart, which emerged from bankruptcy in May, has staked its recovery in large part on the success of the Martha Stewart Everyday brand. It had accused Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia Inc. of overcharging for exclusive rights to sell housewares and other products. (AP)

Pritzker cousins refile lawsuit

Hyatt Corp. chairman Thomas Pritzker and other family members were sued by two cousins who renewed allegations they were cheated out of inheritances. They are seeking $2 billion. Matthew and Liesel Pritzker, 21 and 20, filed the new lawsuit in state court in Chicago, a month after their original case was dismissed, said their spokesman. The judge allowed them to amend their complaint. The Pritzker fortune includes the 209-hotel Hyatt chain, commercial real estate, industrial businesses, and a 25 percent stake in Royal Caribbean Cruises Ltd. The plaintiffs say trust fund assets were transferred to other family members and the Pritzker Foundation. Among those sued is their father, Marmon Group Inc. founder Robert Pritzker, 77. (Bloomberg)

New rules would tighten sale of annuities

The National Association of Securities Dealers proposed rules to better govern the sale of variable annuities. The NASD would mandate the enforcement of previously issued best-practice guidelines and require firms to provide clients with risk disclosure documents in "plain English" that detail liquidity issues, sales charges, and fees. Variable annuities let investors put savings into stock, bond, or money market funds. New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer is investigating whether fees paid to brokers create a conflict of interest and breach of fiduciary duty. (Reuters)

THE WORLD

Brazil wins WTO ruling against US

In a landmark decision, the World Trade Organization has sided with a complaint from Brazil claiming US subsidies for cotton farmers unfairly boost US production and exports while lowering world cotton prices. Details of the decision were not made public, but top Brazilian trade officials said they were pleased with a long-awaited report by three WTO officials. US trade envoys plan an appeal if the report isn't changed. Brazil, the world's fifth-largest cotton producer, says the United States has managed to keep its spot as the planet's second-largest producer only because the US government paid $12.47 billion in subsidies to farmers between August 1999 and July 2003. The ruling is the first time a country has been challenged over its domestic agricultural subsidies, and the first case that looks at the effect of export subsidies on agricultural products. (AP)

Battle for Aventis ends, riling some

Investors reacted with disappointment to a takeover deal between France's two biggest drug makers after governmental pressure helped to eliminate Swiss rival Novartis AG from the battle for Aventis SA. Shares of Sanofi-Synthelabo plunged 7 percent in Paris, after chairman and CEO Jean-Francois Dehecq announced a $63.2 billion cash-and-stock buyout of Strasbourg-based Aventis, ending a bitter three-month struggle and creating the world's number three drug company. Aventis shares closed 5.5 percent lower. A French government official said there was heavy pressure on Sanofi to raise its offer. On Saturday, Sanofi's board gave the green light to raise the cash portion of the offer to $19 billion, from $11 billion. A day later, 13 of Aventis's 16 board members voted to accept it. Yesterday, some Aventis shareholders said they were shortchanged by government meddling. (AP)

Reebok International Ltd. of Canton plans to sell up to $300 million of 20-year convertible securities to help finance its Hockey Company Holdings Inc. purchase. Proceeds may also be used to refinance existing debt or repurchase shares. Reebok also plans to redeem $250 million of its 4.25 percent convertible securities due in 2021. . . . The Boston Stock Exchange appointed Mike Curran chief executive, completing the separation of its regulatory and business operations. Kenneth Leibler, previously chairman and CEO, will remain chairman and be responsible for the regulatory function, strategic direction, and Boston Options Exchange. Curran was previously chief operating officer. . . . State Street Corp. of Boston named Robert Sisti head of operations for State Street Global Markets, its investment research and trading division. . . . Andover-based MKS Instruments Inc. named vice president Leo Berlinghieri president. He continues as chief operating officer and reports to John R. Bertucci, who resigned as president but continues as chairman and CEO. (Globe wires)
