Need milk? Get ready to pay more

Regulators OK 52-cent-a-gallon increase for farmers; supermarkets' plans are not yet clear

By Bruce Mohl, Globe Staff  |  April 24, 2004

Those "Got Milk?" mustaches will soon cost a lot more, as federal regulators yesterday authorized a 52-cent-a-gallon increase in the price farmers receive for their milk. The increase, which takes effect May 1, brings the farm price to a record high of $1.99 a gallon, which is about as much as some low-priced retailers like Costco have been charging consumers for milk in the Boston area.

Dairy processors are expected to pass the price increase on to supermarket retailers, who will have to decide how much to raise their prices. Supermarkets, some of which are already charging $3.19 for their store-brand milk, didn't announce their pricing plans yesterday. But analysts expect retailers to absorb at least part of the 52-cent increase to keep consumers from shifting to other beverages.

"There's clearly a strong incentive for prices to increase at the retail level," said William Gillmeister, an agricultural economist at the state Department of Agricultural Resources. "But I expect fluid milk prices to rise only modestly," he added.

Christopher Galen, vice president of communications for the National Milk Producers Federation in Arlington, Va., said supermarkets can afford to absorb some of the price increase because they earned a large profit -- some analysts say as much as $1 a gallon -- when farm prices for milk reached all-time lows last year.

"Retailers have made a lot of money in the dairy case the last couple years, so hopefully they'll be circumspect about what they do," Galen said.

Faith Weiner, a Stop & Shop spokeswoman, hinted the chain would raise its milk prices, but not by the full 52 cents. "We're facing an unprecedented increase in milk prices," she said. "We haven't made any firm decisions yet. We're evaluating the increase and we'll continue to do our best for our customers and be competitive."

Shoppers outside Star Market in Dorchester yesterday were dismayed at the prospect of paying more for a staple like milk. "You still need it whether it's $2 or $12," said Elaine Donnelly, 55, of South Boston. "It will hurt a lot of young kids for parents that can't afford it."

Paula Casper, 33, of South Boston said, "I'm not too happy about it. I have two babies and they go through a gallon a day."

The rapid run-up in raw milk prices is attributed to a slight downturn in supplies that has coincided with a pickup in demand. The US Department of Agriculture says milk production during the first three months of this year was off 0.9 percent from the same period a year earlier, while demand for milk has picked up. The latest USDA figures, from November to January, indicate milk consumption rose approximately 2.5 percent, a trend that appeared to continue through February and March.

The higher prices are good news for dairy farmers, who have struggled with high feed costs and low milk prices. Last spring, the prices dairy farmers were paid hit a 25-year low.

At about that time, the nation's dairy cooperatives launched a voluntary program to reduce the size of dairy herds to curb milk production and bring milk supplies more in line with demand. Industry officials estimate the program thinned the nation's dairy herd by about 30,000 cows.

Three other factors have contributed to the downturn in milk supplies. An artificial growth hormone that induces a third of the nation's dairy herd to produce more milk is in short supply. Milk for import is scarce, in part because of a drought in Australia. And importing dairy cows has been nearly impossible. The United States banned cattle imports from Canada because of a case of mad cow disease there in May.

Galen, of the National Milk Producers Federation, which represents dairy cooperatives, including Agri-Mark in Massachusetts, said the organization will continue efforts at thinning the nation's dairy herd because the current supply-demand imbalance is unlikely to hold for long. Galen said futures markets indicate the high price of raw milk is likely to last through the summer, or about four to six months. Then, he said, the price farmers are paid is expected to start falling. "Our goal is to prevent a crash," he said.

In the past, retail milk prices have been quick to respond to a run-up in the farm price but very slow to react to a downturn. Retail prices in Massachusetts, as tracked by the Department of Agricultural Resources, have not fallen below $2.99 a gallon since October 1999, even though the farm price hit a 25-year low last spring. Retail prices, as tracked by the US Department of Agriculture, have dipped lower than $2.99 over that time period.

Farm prices rose last fall, and Stop & Shop and Shaw's Supermarkets, the two biggest chains in Massachusetts, responded by raising the price of their store-brand milk to $3.09. Both chains held their prices steady when the farm price dipped down over the winter and then increased their prevailing prices last month to $3.19 a gallon when farm prices jumped 15 cents a gallon.

Supermarkets like Market Basket have traditionally sold milk for far less than their bigger competitors, often for $2.59 or $2.49 a gallon. The recent price run-up is likely to force lower-priced chains to increase their milk prices, as well.
