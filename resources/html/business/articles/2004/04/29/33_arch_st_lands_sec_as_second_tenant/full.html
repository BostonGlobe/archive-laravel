BUSINESS IN BRIEF

33 Arch St. lands SEC as second tenant

April 29, 2004

THE REGION

In the second piece of good news this week for developer Dean F. Stratouly, the Securities and Exchange Commission has leased 65,000 square feet in his 33 Arch St. tower in Boston. After three years of a down market with no tenants, Stratouly signed up Digitas Inc. on Monday, for 200,000 square feet. Another tenant is expected to take about 60,000 square feet next week. The blue glass building with curved facades near Downtown Crossing, not yet officially open, is now about half leased. "I'm back from the dead," said Stratouly, whose own company, Congress Group Ventures, will occupy half the 21st floor. "Everybody was burying me six months ago." (Thomas C. Palmer Jr.)

Candela tumbles on earnings news

Shares of Candela Corp. plummeted after the maker of medical lasers reported third-quarter earnings well below Wall Street's expectations. The Wayland company's shares fell $5.34, or 32 percent, to $11.55. After the markets closed Tuesday, Candela reported earnings of 9 cents per share on net income of $1.96 million for its latest quarter. The average Wall Street estimate was 13 cents, leading Maxim Group to downgrade Candela shares to "hold," from "strong buy." Revenue rose 29 percent to $27.7 million, above analysts' expectations. (Dow Jones/AP)

Schering-Plough is subpoenaed again

Schering-Plough Corp. has received another subpoena in a grand jury investigation into its sales and marketing activities, according to a Securities and Exchange Commission filing. The pharmaceutical company said it was subpoenaed this month by the US attorney's office in Massachusetts, which demanded documents regarding the drugs Proventil, Vanceril, Vancenase, Nitro-Dur, Imdur, K-Dur, and Claritin. Schering-Plough said the subpoena seeks documents related to managed-care entities; all contracts where the price of one drug is dependent on the purchase of another drug; outside audits in the Medicaid best price area; and Warrick, the company's generic subsidiary. (Dow Jones)

Praecis, Schering in drug marketing deal

Praecis Pharmaceuticals Inc., of Waltham, said it could receive up to $90 million from Schering AG under a drug marketing deal. Praecis would not say over what time period it might receive the payments, to be made in return for giving the German drug company the right to market its Plenaxis drug for prostate cancer in Europe, Russia, the Middle East, South Africa, Australia, and New Zealand. (Ross Kerber)

THE NATION
The defense rests in Quattrone trial

Lawyers for Frank Quattrone rested their case yesterday after the former banking star endured a grueling six-hour cross-examination focusing on his involvement in Credit Suisse First Boston's allocation of hot new stocks. Closing arguments in the trial, underway in New York City, are tomorrow. Quattrone is charged with obstruction of justice and witness tampering for sending a Dec. 5, 2000, e-mail urging bank employees to "clean up" their files as the government was looking into whether some clients had paid kickbacks to get a piece of hot new issues. Prosecutors presented e-mails showing people from the bank asked Quattrone's advice on who should get stock shares. In one case, Quattrone answered, "We can't do something where it looks like we are spinning," referring to a frowned-upon process for wooing investment banking business. Quattrone insists he never made any final decisions on allocations. (AP)

RealNetworks chief quits following loss

Lawrence Jacobson, president of Seattle-based RealNetworks Inc., resigned after the digital media company posted a $10.4 million quarterly loss. The 6-cents-per-share loss was double what it lost in first-quarter 2003. Jacobson, who had been on the job for three years, will continue to work temporarily for the company as a strategic adviser. RealNetworks' shares ended the regular session down 51 cents, or 7.7 percent, at $6.09 on the Nasdaq Stock Market. They were up 6 cents in extended trading. (AP)

Toyota again leads satisfaction survery

Toyota Motor Corp. led an auto industry quality study for the seventh straight year, J.D. Power & Associates reported, and consumer complaints in the first 90 days of owning a new car or truck fell 11 percent, to 119 per 100 vehicles. Even with gains this year, "Ford and DaimlerChrysler must raise quality if they intend to compete without the bottom-line-draining rebates," said Brian Bruce, of PanAgora Asset Management in Boston. US brands averaged 123 complaints per 100 vehicles. Japanese brands led at 111, followed by Korean at 117 and European at 122. Toyota's score of 101 was lowest and improved from 115 last year. Honda was next at 102, down from 126, followed by General Motors, which improved to 120 from 134. DaimlerChrysler was down to 123 from 139; Ford fell to 127 from 136; Volkswagen AG was unchanged at 141; Nissan Motor Co. worsened to 147 from 135. Toyota's Lexus was best among all brands with 87 complaints per 100 vehicles. GM's Hummer had the worst score, at 173 problems per 100 vehicles. (Bloomberg)

Viacom chief to boost stake in Midway

Viacom Inc. chairman Sumner Redstone intends to boost his personal stake in video game publisher Midway Games Inc. to at least 60 percent of its stock, he said in a filing. Midway shares fell nearly 10 percent as investors concluded Redstone would not pay a premium to take the company private. This month, Redstone said he was considering acquiring Midway. Redstone, individually and through his company National Amusements, owns 49.3 percent of Midway, which has not posted a quarterly profit in over four years. He said the difference between Midway and larger games publishers is the amount of creative talent on staff. "You will see very soon an enormous infusion of great creative talent to Midway," he said. For the first quarter, Chicago-based Midway reported a net loss of $15 million, or 27 cents per share, vs. a year-earlier loss of $9.6 million, or 21 cents. Revenue fell to $20.1 million from $45.8 million. For the full year, the company now expects a net loss of $18 million on revenue of $148 million. (Reuters)

Bankrupt Enron settles gas price case

Enron Corp. has agreed to pay $35 million in a settlement with the Commodity Futures Trading Commission over allegations it sought to illegally manipulate natural gas prices. But the penalty will go to the back of the line, behind thousands of the bankrupt company's other creditors -- meaning the agency won't be paid. The CFTC's pursuit of Enron began when the agency filed a civil complaint in March last year accusing the company and a former vice president, Hunter Shively, of operating the Enron Online Internet trading platform as "an illegal futures exchange" and cited alleged attempts to manipulate the natural gas market. Enron imploded in 2001 amid revelations of hidden debt and inflated profits that sent investors fleeing. Twenty-eight people have been indicted. (AP)

THE WORLD
Japan's GDP growth forecast to be 3.1%

Japan's central bank has lifted its economic growth forecast, saying a rebound in the United States and Asia would fuel strong exports and company profits. The Bank of Japan said gross domestic product is likely to grow 3.1 percent in the fiscal year that began April 1, up from the previous forecast of 2.5 percent it made in October. Japan's GDP grew at an annual rate of 6.4 percent in the final quarter of 2003. The bank said the economy will continue to recover as pressures of continuously declining prices, or deflation, ease. (AP)
