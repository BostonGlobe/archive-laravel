Hale and Dorr OKs merger deal

By Ross Kerber, Globe Staff  |  April 20, 2004

Hale and Dorr LLP, one of Boston's oldest and largest corporate law firms, will merge with the Washington law firm of Wilmer Cutler Pickering LLP, senior partners said yesterday, marking the most significant combination to date in an ongoing round of consolidation and geographic expansion that is remaking Boston's legal community.

The two firms, which each had revenue of more than $300 million last year, said the deal has been approved by partners on both sides. It will create an organization with about 1,000 lawyers and a structure splitting power equally between Hale and Dorr, whose client roster includes many of the region's top technology and pharmaceutical companies, and Wilmer Cutler, one of Washington's highest-profile firms. Wilmer Cutler is noted for its securities practice; it represents many major corporations and brokerages, including Bear Stearns & Co. and Credit Suisse First Boston.

Last year, its attorneys also successfully defended the University of Michigan's affirmative-action program before the US Supreme Court.

Hale and Dorr managing partner William F. Lee, who will be co-managing partner of the new firm, said the deal set to close May 31 will double the number of specialized practice areas both firms now offer.

''What we collectively believe is that by growing we'll have more preeminent practice areas, so we can supply clients with the expertise that they demand in different situations," Lee said. ''We were doing fine apart, but I think we'll be even more effective together."

Other law firms have followed a similar strategy of merging to achieve scale and geographic spread, notably Bingham McCutchen LLP, formed when Boston institution Bingham Dana merged with a San Francisco firm in 2002. Lee and others said the deal follows the demands of corporate clients looking for the best advice money can buy.

''The nature of the clients who can afford the fees for these major firms is that they're the ones with the operations spread out across the country and around the world," said Renee M. Landers, president of the Boston Bar Association and a law professor at Suffolk University. ''They also have legal problems that require a lot of bench strength, and size can get you bench strength."

The deal also suggests a widening gap between large and midsize firms as Boston's once-insular legal world opens to out-of-town competitors. In recent years some of the biggest-name local firms have laid off attorneys as the technology sector cooled off and companies stopped filing initial public offerings of stock, which required extensive legal vetting. Some well-known firms are roughly the same size they were in 2000, including Choate, Hall & Stewart, with 170 attorneys, or smaller. Palmer & Dodge LLP, for instance, now has about 170 attorneys, according to its website, down from 201 in 2000.

Hale and Dorr managed its way through the tech downturn without layoffs, Lee said, because of its growing intellectual property practice and litigation work for clients such as chipmaker Intel Corp., data-storage company EMC Corp., and drug maker Pfizer Inc. Over the same period, large outside firms opened local offices to snap up commerce from local companies, or expanded existing shops.

''This is a trend that has been around for some time, but it has more recently accelerated in the Boston market," said Doron Ezickson, managing partner of the Boston office of McDermott Will & Emery, with 80 attorneys. His firm and others are aiming at clients in areas such as life sciences, financial services, and the healthcare sector.

The new firm will be known as Wilmer Cutler Pickering Hale and Dorr LLP.

No attorneys will leave the firm because of the merger, said Lee and William J. Perlstein, Wilmer Cutler managing partner who will be co-managing partner with Lee of the new entity, in contrast to mergers in other industries such as banking that are mainly designed to cut costs.

Hale and Dorr said its 2003 revenue of $312 million was its highest ever, and a $6 million increase from 2002. Profits per partner, a rough measure of the firm's income after paying the salaries of other lawyers, were flat at $810,000 both years. Wilmer Cutler said its revenue was up to $337 million last year from $304 million the year before, and that profit per partner rose to $776,000 from $764,000 in 2002.

Hale and Dorr traces its roots as far back as an 1810 law office and took its current name from a partnership struck in 1918 between Richard Hale and Dudley Dorr. It first attracted national attention in 1954 when its partner Joseph N. Welch donated his time to represent the US Army in televised hearings over charges by US Senator Joseph R. McCarthy, Republican of Wisconsin, that the service was infiltrated by Communists. Twenty years later partner James D. St. Clair represented President Richard M. Nixon during the Watergate scandal. Also Hale and Dorr attorney Jerome P. Facher achieved some fame when he was profiled defending Beatrice Foods Inc. in the 1995 book, ''A Civil Action."

Under Lee, the firm moved aggressively to court technology start-ups in the 1990s and lately, life sciences companies such as Boston Scientific Corp. But he noted that the company's 150 litigators are mainly involved in cases outside New England. Wilmer Cutler was founded in 1962 and has a roster of politically experienced partners, including former Clinton administration officials Jamie S. Gorelick, now a member of the 9/11 Commission, and Seth P. Waxman, who argued more cases before the Supreme Court last year than any other private-sector lawyer.

According to Lee and Perlstein, the two firms have rebuffed scores of suitors, but found themselves talking about a merger last year after they worked together on an intellectual-property case for a client they wouldn't name. All of each firms' roughly 150 partners favored the deal, partly because the firms wouldn't have to shed any clients as a result and because the two firms seemed to promote similar working cultures.

To preserve secrecy during the 10 months of talks, the firms used code names on internal memos -- Adams and Jefferson -- after the two founding fathers, one from Massachusetts, one from Virginia. The negotiations stayed out of the public eye until they were reported by the Globe on Friday.
