AUTOMOBILIA

Land Rover models prove their mettle in the mud

By Royal Ford, Globe Staff  |  April 3, 2004

WHITE SULPHUR SPRINGS, W.Va. -- One of the best days I've ever had testing cars was driving a Porsche lineup that climbed, in hundreds of horsepower, from basic Boxster to all-wheel drive Turbo on the straightaway and road course at the NASCAR track in Homestead, Fla.

Driving a company's entire lineup, from cheapest to most expensive, most sedate to most powerful, tamest to toughest, is a true test of what the automaker builds. As with the Porsche, you drive each car the same, only you go faster, more confidently, with greater brutal grace as the ladder is climbed. It can also happen off-road.

That is why it was exciting to drive Land Rovers deep into the Southern hills here, where winter-thaw mud was slick, and icy water slid over gray shale chips that litter the trails like tiny razor blades.

I was at Land Rover Experience, the company's off-road driving school, crosshatching some of the 6,500 acres of the Greenbrier Resort.

I found that it is a test filled with precarious momentary challenges.

For instance: Our front left wheel is 2 feet off the ground even as the right rear is deep in a muddy ditch. The right front has crept into a puddle, and has pushed against the side of a ditch, even as the left rear articulates inward like a busted ankle. Three wheels drive us ahead in a steady creep. The air-grabbing wheel does not spin because a computer knows it has no purchase and therefore brakes it, robbing it of power and sending the rescued torque to the driving wheels. Only when the stopped wheel finds ground and is useful again will it be fed.

And more: Sometimes, on a long downhill grade, even with automatic hill descent control or gentle manual braking, the SUV becomes a sliding sled.

Or: When climbing a very steep, slick slope -- where all you can see out of the windshield is sky -- the car starts to slide backward. Shift into reverse, overtake the slide with extra gas, resettle at the base of the slope, and try again with more aggression, clinging to what Land Rover off-road instructor Jim Swett says is the tenet of serious off-road driving: "As slow as possible, as fast as necessary."

And it is a fragile balance that must be struck between the two as off-road travel tosses its challenges: slick climbs and descents; stumps, rocks, mud holes, and deep water; the danger of scraping the upper edge of the roof on a tree as the car tilts precariously on a side slope.

Fixes can feel counterintuitive: Use brakes while giving gas, one for power, the other to ease the drop of wheels off obstacles even as other wheels push to climb obstacles of their own. When sliding forward downhill, override hill descent's automatic braking by applying gas (disconcerting when you are descending so steeply that you are already thrust forward into your seatbelt) and steering into the direction in which a rear quarter of the car is trying to pass you. Drive over rocks with tires, don't try to straddle them. Don't approach logs, rocks, or ditches straight on: Go at an angle. This allows one wheel at a time to deal with the approach as others do their digging.

It can seem a tough way to treat the terrain -- or, if the terrain wins, vehicles that can cost $72,000 and more. Yet Land Rover, perhaps the world's best-known off-road vehicle manufacturer, is not afraid to combine exquisite luxury with superb, brutish off-road capabilities.

In fact, the folks at Land Rover say "off-highway" is a better description because, even as rough as they can be, the nearly half million miles of unpaved trails, gravel paths, cliff-clinging slabs, rocky ribbons, and stump-pocked swaths that crosshatch the country are still, indeed, "roads" for four-wheeling enthusiasts.

All three Land Rover models are capable of going where most SUV poseurs built today cannot go, though the Freelander, least expensive and newest of Land Rover products, is obviously aimed more at the set for whom off-road might mean a spring-washed gravel trail leading to the fishing camp or a hard winter's night approach to the ski chalet.

The Discovery and Range Rover? How rough do you want to get?

The Discovery and Range Rover have two-speed transfer cases (high- and low-range) that, by my definition, separate off-road beasts from very dependable bad-weather transportation. The Freelander, instead of a locking center differential, uses viscous coupling to provide its permanent all-wheel drive.

While the interior levels of the Freelander are most comparable in my mind to, say, Toyota's 4Runner, the Discovery, and, particularly, the Range Rover, dance the floor with some of the most wood-trimmed, leather-swathed luxury sedans on the road.

What might compare? Possibly the Porsche Cayenne, the Mercedes-Benz G-Class, the V-8 Volkswagen Touareg, the Toyota Land Cruiser.

Other cars with which to compare all three, in various models and with varying features? The Cadillac SRX, BMW X3 and X5, Acura MDX, Honda Pilot. These are cars that run in the $30,000 to $90,000 range -- a niche that Land Rover fits into with its Freelander, at $27,000 to $32,000; Discovery, $36,000 to $45,000; and Range Rover, $72,000 and up.

The engines in the three include a 2.5-liter V-6 in the Freelander that produces 174 horsepower and 177 lb.-ft. of torque; a 4.6-liter V-8 with 217 horsepower and 300 lb.-ft. of torque in the Discovery; and a BMW-developed 4.6-liter aluminum V-8 in the Range Rover with 282 horsepower and 325 lb.-ft. of torque. The Freelander has a five-speed automatic transmission, the Discovery a four-speed, and the Ranger Rover a five-speed. All have manual options.

All did very well in the moderate conditions of our test trails, though if I were going deep into the heart of get-stuck danger, I'd probably go with the Discovery just because its size (more than a foot shorter than the Range Rover at 182.5 inches, with a similar disparity in wheelbase at 100 inches, and about an inch narrower) can be an advantage in tight maneuvering.

There's a new Discovery model debuting next week at the New York International Auto Show and I hear it will have a more powerful engine, so I'd wait for that one.
