Judge declares mistrial in Tyco case

By Jeffrey Krasner and Tatsha Robertson, Globe Staff  |  April 3, 2004

NEW YORK -- The roller-coaster corporate corruption trial of former Tyco International Ltd. executives L. Dennis Kozlowski and Mark H. Swartz ended with yet another wrenching turn yesterday when the judge unexpectedly declared a mistrial.

A suddenly famous juror, who last week made a gesture to defense lawyers that some observed as an "OK" signal, told the judge she received a threatening letter at home.

Breaking journalistic convention, The Wall Street Journal and the New York Post both named the juror, Ruth Jordan, a 79-year-old former schoolteacher, after she made the gesture. Most news organizations do not name jurors until a trial ends. New York state Supreme Court Justice Michael J. Obus faulted the media in part for the mistrial and said disclosing the juror's name subjected her to "outside pressure."

"It is certainly a shame that this has to be done at this time," Obus told the jurors in a quaking voice as he declared a mistrial. "There has been no finding that this juror has done anything wrong. A great disservice may have been done to her and her family."

Courtroom observers gasped and several jurors grimaced when Obus announced the mistrial. Another juror, Peter McEntegart, speaking on CNN, said, "We literally were within minutes" of reaching a verdict when deliberations ended. McEntegart said the jury would have returned a mixed verdict, convicting Kozlowski and Swartz on some charges and acquitting them on others.

Jordan, in a statement released last night, said, "My greatest interest is, and always has been, to do the right thing in my role as a juror in this case."

The declaration by the judge ended the six-month trial and opened the door for New York prosecutors to try the case again at enormous expense to taxpayers. Kozlowski and Swartz were accused of looting Tyco of $600 million and faced 32 counts that included grand larceny, falsifying business records, conspiracy, and securities fraud. They faced a maximum penalty of 25 years on the grand larceny charges, with multiple convictions possibly leading to an extremely long jail sentence, if the judge ruled they serve the sentences consecutively. Manhattan District Attorney Robert M. Morgenthau, for whom the trial might have capped a lengthy career taking on rich and powerful defendants, said his office would seek a retrial "at the earliest opportunity."

Kozlowski's lawyer, Stephen Kaufman, told reporters as he rushed outside the courtroom that he was disappointed about the results and that defense and the prosecution will return to court May 7 to set a date for a new trial.

"Because of the events that occurred outside of the courtroom we are disappointed the case did not reach a verdict," Kaufman said. "My client went in this case with courage and determination, and he still has those qualities."

Charles Stillman, who represents Swartz, said: "I've been doing this 40 years. . . . I ain't seen nothing like this."

The judge's ruling, and other recent decisions, may change defense strategies in other corporate corruption trials that are underway or pending. In October, a hung jury resulted in a mistrial for Frank Quattrone, a former star analyst at Credit Suisse First Boston, who was charged with obstruction of justice. That case is slated to be retried next month. Other trials in the offing include Enron Corp. and WorldCom.

"You have to wonder what impact this ruling will have on the government's battle against corporate crime," said Stanley A. Twardy Jr., a lawyer and former prosecutor with Day, Berry & Howard LLP in Stamford, Conn. "This may embolden more white-collar defendants to go to trial, and it may make prosecutors rethink whether they bring charges in the first place."

For now, the mistrial is a triumph, albeit temporary, for Kozlowski, Swartz, and their lawyers.

"This really is a big win for the defense," said Michael DeMarco, a partner specializing in white-collar crime at Kirkpatrick & Lockhart LLP, a law firm in Boston. "They made the government work very hard, investing all the resources and all the time and all the money, for a big letdown."

Moreover, at least one juror -- likely Jordan, according to notes sent to the judge last week -- seemed intent on acquitting the men. "The defense knows that at least they reached one juror out of 12," DeMarco said.

The retrial also gives the defense a big advantage because it already has seen the government's entire case. "Trying cases for a second time is very, very difficult, and the chances for success for the defendants have increased exponentially," DeMarco said.

Another difficulty, according to lawyers, is that the government runs the risk of undermining its case if testimony from witnesses does not precisely agree with their statements during the first trial. Several attorneys predicted that the government would work hard to simplify and streamline its case. Over 5 1/2 months of testimony, the government presented 700 documents and called 48 witnesses.

Although Morgenthau said he would retry, several criminal defense lawyers said the mistrial also raises the possibility the two sides would reach an out-of-court settlement. "If I were Kozlowski, I'd certainly be thinking of a possible deal," said Ted Poretz, a former assistant district attorney who worked for Morgenthau who is now a lawyer with Bingham McCutchen in New York.

Regardless of the outcome of the government's case, the defendants' former employer, Tyco, said in a statement that it was moving forward with civil cases it filed against Kozlowski and Swartz.

The government case against Kozlowski and Swartz was a dense 90-page document that went so far as to describe Tyco as a "criminal enterprise," a legal term for a fictitious business without real operations used in cases against organized crime operations. That charge was ultimately thrown out, but the rest of the charges, outlining dozens of ways in which the two executives allegedly used Tyco and its many dealings to unfairly enrich themselves, painted a picture of greed run amok.

The government said the pair "looted" the firm by securing $170 million in illegal compensation exceeding their enormous pay packages. The pair also allegedly netted $430 million by promoting Tyco's growth while they were secretly selling shares whose value they had helped to inflate.

During the trial, the nine-man, three-woman jury saw a video of a $6,000 shower curtain that was meant to symbolize Kozlowski's alleged excesses. Jurors also saw a video of a $2 million party that Kozlowski threw in Sardinia in 2001 to celebrate the 40th birthday of his wife, Karen. The Roman-themed party featured toga-clad women for which prosecutors say Tyco picked up half the tab.

The ruling yesterday shocked many observers because, a week earlier, it had seemed as though Obus had saved the jury from self-destruction.

On March 25, the sixth day of deliberations, jurors sent a series of notes to the judge telling him that "the atmosphere in the jury room had turned poisonous."

Jurors were harshly attacking one another, they wrote in the notes. In one note, a juror charged that some members of the panel were dead-set on conviction and did not "have an open mind as to the possibility of the defendants' innocence."

The next day, the jurors sent what sounded like a death knell for the trial. "Let us be clear," they wrote to the judge. "This is not a hung jury based on a lack of unanimity. This is a jury that has ceased to conduct respectful, open-minded, good-faith deliberations."

More doubts were raised about the jury's ability to reach a verdict that would stand when Jordan, juror number four, who holds a law degree, gave a signal to defense attorneys when she entered the courtroom during deliberations. Some interpreted the gesture as a thumb-and-finger circle indicating "OK," prompting many observers to conclude the woman was supporting an acquittal. Throughout the uproar, Obus responded with calm, reasoned counsel. He spoke privately with Jordan and urged the jurors to respect one another regardless of their disagreements. He rejected repeated defense motions asking him to declare a mistrial based on the news coverage. Yesterday, the judge said a threatening letter sent to Jordan forced him to call a mistrial.
