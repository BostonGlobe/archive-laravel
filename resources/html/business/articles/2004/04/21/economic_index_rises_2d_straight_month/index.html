BUSINESS IN BRIEF

Economic index rises 2d straight month

April 21, 2004

Four of five of the Boston area's leading industries added jobs in March, hinting that the region's economy is starting to revive, according to the Greater Boston Chamber of Commerce. The chamber's leading industries index, which tracks employment in the five sectors that provide more than half the metropolitan area's jobs, rose for the second consecutive month in March, to 105.3 from 105.2 in February and 104.9 in January. The index, however is slighty weaker than in March 2003, when it stood at 105.5. According to the chamber, the five sectors added a net 2,800 jobs in March, led by healthcare, which gained 2,100. Tourism added 800 jobs; education and consulting 200 jobs; and technology 100. Financial services was the only sector to lose jobs over the month, shedding 400. (Robert Gavin)

Manulife-Hancock deal gets Canadian OK

Canadian regulators approved Manulife Financial Corp.'s proposed $10.9 billion takeover of John Hancock Financial Services Inc., creating the second-biggest North American life insurer by market value. Canada's Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions and minister of finance approved the purchase, Toronto-based Manulife said. The acquisition has already been approved by John Hancock shareholders and US regulators. Manulife chief executive Dominic D'Alessandro is buying John Hancock to expand in the United States after falling behind Sun Life Financial Inc., the largest Canadian insurer by assets. The acquisition is scheduled to close April 28, Manulife said. (Bloomberg)

Maxwell Shoes says Jones drops 3 claims

Maxwell Shoe Co. said Jones Apparel Group Inc. withdrew three of the four claims it made last month, when it told a court that Maxwell improperly tried to stop its $297 million hostile takeover of the shoe company. Jones and its MSC Acquisition Corp. unit withdrew a claim that Maxwell's board breached its fiduciary duties by setting a March 25 record date for consent solicitations. Hyde Park-based Maxwell said Jones's remaining claim, that Maxwell's certificate of incorporation didn't permit its board to set a record date, is without merit. MSC also dropped a claim that Maxwell should provide financial records relating to the March 25 record date. Maxwell's claims that Jones and MSC made "materially false and misleading statements and omissions" which violate federal securities laws is pending. A Maxwell spokesman declined to comment further. (Bloomberg)

Group raises offer for Lincoln Park owner

A US-based investor consortium has increased its offer for the parent of Rhode Island's Lincoln Park gambling facility, outbidding a previous offer by MGM Mirage. London-based Wembley PLC recommended its shareholders accept the increased offer of $51.20 a share, for a total offer of about $555 million, according to the current exchange rate. MGM had offered $50 a share on April 8. BLB Investors LLC's new offer also matches MGM's offer to pay back to shareholders any funds left over from Wembley's $8 million legal defense fund -- put aside for its battle in US District Court in Rhode Island. Wembley began taking bids after former chief executive Nigel Potter and Lincoln Park chief executive Daniel Bucci were indicted on federal conspiracy and wire fraud charges. Both have pleaded innocent. (Dow Jones/AP)

THE NATION
DirecTV sells PanAmSat for $3.53b

Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., the world's biggest buyout firm, agreed to acquire satellite operator PanAmSat Corp. from Rupert Murdoch's DirecTV Group Inc. for $3.53 billion. KKR also will assume PanAmSat's $750 million in net debt. KKR is buying PanAmSat from DirecTV and other investors for $23.50 a share, the companies said. DirecTV chief executive Chase Carey said in February that he might sell PanAmSat to focus on DirecTV's consumer pay-television business. PanAmSat mainly leases satellites to TV networks that use them to deliver programming to local cable-TV systems and broadcast stations. Shares of Wilton, Conn.-based PanAmSat fell $1.49, or 6 percent, to $23.30. (Bloomberg)

WorldCom exits Chap. 11; now called MCI

WorldCom Inc. emerged from Chapter 11 bankruptcy, shedding $35 billion in debt and a name that had become synonymous with corporate scandal. WorldCom is now officially known as MCI, the name of the long-distance phone business it acquired in 1998. The company had been doing business as MCI for roughly a year but could not make the official switch until it left bankruptcy protection. WorldCom filed for Chapter 11 protection after an accounting scandal revealed up to $11 billion in inflated profits. The bankruptcy process has allowed the company to slash its debt from $41 billion to about $6 billion. That will shave $2.1 billion a year off interest payments for a company producing about $21 billion a year in revenue. MCI issued roughly 320 million shares to creditors, including $250 million worth to the Securities and Exchange Commission, part of a $750 million fine it paid to settle the fraud allegations. An MCI spokesman said the company would probably be listed on the Nasdaq exchange within the next few weeks. (Bloomberg)

Internet traffic technology flaw revealed

Researchers uncovered a serious flaw in the underlying technology for nearly all Internet traffic, a discovery that led to an urgent and secretive international effort to prevent global disruptions of Web surfing, e-mails, and instant messages. The British government said the vulnerability was in core Internet technology. Left unaddressed, experts said, it could allow hackers to knock computers offline and broadly disrupt vital traffic-directing devices, called routers, that coordinate the flow of data among distant groups of computers. The Homeland Security Department issued its own cyberalert hours later that attacks "could affect a large segment of the Internet community." It said normal Internet operations probably would resume after such attacks stopped. Experts said there were no reports of attacks exploiting this flaw. (AP)

Citigroup chairman survives challenge

Citigroup chairman Sanford I. Weill easily survived an effort by the California Public Employees' Retirement System to unseat him at the bank's annual meeting. Weill and the other 14 directors on the Citigroup board were reelected "by not less than 94 percent of those [votes] cast," said Michael Helfer, the Citigroup general counsel who served as secretary of the meeting. Helfer declined to give the total for each director, saying the figures would be included in the next filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission. Calpers, the nation's largest pension fund, withheld its votes for more than half of Citigroup's directors, including Weill, saying he should be replaced by an independent director because he played "a significant role in several scandals to negatively impact the company" during his tenure as chief executive, which was from 1998 to last October. (AP)

Cingular reports customer increase

Cingular Wireless LLC got a boost of 554,000 subscribers in the first quarter, while the rival it has agreed to buy, AT&T Wireless Services Inc., saw a decline of 367,000. AT&T Wireless is suffering more than other companies from number portability, which allows customers to keep their cellphone numbers when they switch providers, Guzman & Co. analyst Patrick Comack said. He said many of the company's lost subscribers, especially business customers in the Northeast, are being gobbled up by Verizon Wireless, which has a strong presence in the region. Comack said he does not believe the subscriber numbers will affect the $41 billion deal Cingular and AT&T Wireless disclosed in February, since an expected loss of customers at AT&T Wireless was factored in. If the acquisition of AT&T Wireless goes through, Cingular would be the nation's largest cellphone provider. (AP)
