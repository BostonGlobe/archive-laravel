High-risk driver insurance plan sought

April 30, 2004

THE REGION

State Insurance Commissioner Julianne M. Bowler told an auto insurance industry group to come up with a new way of apportioning high-risk drivers within 30 days and threatened to implement a plan unilaterally if she doesn't like the industry's proposal. In a letter to the Commonwealth Automobile Reinsurers, which manages the state's system for distributing high-risk drivers, Bowler said she wants to phase in an assigned-risk plan and asked for the sharing of losses and premiums this year of certain agents representing drivers who generate unusually high losses. Bowler also wants high-risk drivers assigned to carriers based on market share and individual carriers to be responsible for losses associated with those drivers. The move could mean hundreds of thousands of Massachusetts drivers will face higher coverage rates and lose the ability to choose their auto insurer. Bowler's letter comes as Governor Mitt Romney seeks to revamp the auto insurance system. (Bruce Mohl)

Romney reiterates stand on drug imports

Massachusetts Governor Mitt Romney said he strongly opposed efforts to import prescription drugs, raising the bar that such measures face on Beacon Hill. ''I believe drug importation is not the solution to America's drug problems," said Romney, who cited the threat of counterfeit pills from foreign suppliers. He made his remarks at the annual meeting of the Massachusetts Biotechnology Council, which opposes drug importation. Although a spokeswoman said Romney has mentioned his opposition before, company executives took his remarks as his clearest stand so far against importation, which they worry would lower US drug-company profits and in turn reduce investment in their growing industry. Beacon Hill lawmakers are struggling to craft proposals that would help residents cope with the rising cost of medications without angering life-sciences companies, which state leaders hope will create tens of thousands of jobs in Massachusetts. (Ross Kerber)

Rent deal reached for Hancock garage

A deal approved by the the Massachusetts Turnpike Authority Board will allow the turnpike to reap millions more in rent for the John Hancock parking garage straddling the highway between Clarendon and Dartmouth streets. The turnpike authority had a 65-year air-rights lease with John Hancock Life Insurance Co. that was transferred to Beacon Capital Partners, the new owners of the John Hancock tower and the parking garage. The annual rent will increase from the current $111,880 to $350,000 and then to $650,000 through 2009, and then jump to $1.25 million and finally $1.5 million through 2024. The new deal fairly reflects the value of the property and will produce much-needed nontoll revenue, said turnpike chairman Matthew J. Amorello. In other business, the board approved an extra $4 million for Mass. Electric Construction Co. for its $94 million contract to install wiring for electrical devices in the Big Dig. (Anthony Flint)

Group expected to choose Courier CEO

Associated Industries of Massachusetts, the state's largest business lobbying organization, is expected to name Courier Corp.'s chief executive James F. Conway III chairman of the group's board today. Since 1992, Conway has been CEO of Chelmsford-based Courier, a book manufacturer and specialty publisher that has revenues approaching $200 million annually and 1,500 employees nationwide. Conway succeeds Samuel Cabot III, chairman of Newburyport wood-stain manufacturer Samuel Cabot Inc., who has served as AIM chairman for three years. AIM represents 7,500 companies across the state. (Peter J. Howe)

US Airways switches to three-tier fares

US Airways said it will switch to a three-tier fare system at its Philadelphia hub, a move aimed at lowering ticket prices and competing with low-fare stalwart Southwest Airlines, which will begin service at Philadelphia International Airport May 9. Passengers booking flights 14 days in advance will pay the minimum price, as low as $29 each way. Walk-up fares will be capped at $499 each way. First-class fares are also being trimmed. The change will apply to flights between Philadelphia and 13 cities, including Providence and Manchester, N.H. Advance coach fares between Philadelphia and Providence and Manchester will start at $29 each way, with walk-up fares set at $239 each way, and first-class seats at $299 each way. (Keith Reed)

THE NATION

30-year mortgage rate rises to 6.01%

The weekly average for a 30-year, fixed-rate mortgage rose above 6 percent for the first time this year, said Freddie Mac, the second-biggest purchaser of US mortgages. The weekly rate hit 6.01 percent, and the average has risen six straight weeks since a nine-month low of 5.38 percent on March 18. Signs of a strengthening economy have pushed up borrowing costs and caused concern about inflation. In the short term, rising rates could prompt an increase in home sales as potential buyers take action out of fear that rates will rise more. (Chris Reidy)

FTC sues to stop Australian spammer

The Federal Trade Commission filed suit against an Australian firm accused of sending millions of unsolicited e-mails to Americans, advertising questionable weight loss drugs and growth hormone treatments. The lawsuit against Global Web Promotions Pty Ltd. was disclosed one day after the commission and the US Justice Department filed criminal and civil complaints against four Detroit-area residents for violating the federal CAN SPAM Act, as well as mail fraud and consumer protection laws. Commission officials said Global Web Promotions produces and ships its products from within the United States. Therefore, federal courts can halt the company's activities and levy financial penalties. Despite this, Global Web Promotions could continue sending millions of e-mails, but will probably stop because of the financial penalties, the officials said. (Hiawatha Bray)

Teamsters anticorruption official resigns

The former federal prosecutor who heads the Teamsters' anticorruption program and 20 other investigators and lawyers involved in that effort resigned, saying the union's president was not committed to fighting corruption, according to The New York Times. The former prosecutor, Edwin H. Stier, sent a letter accusing James P. Hoffa, the Teamsters president, of blocking a probe into possible corruption in Chicago and of dragging his feet in a case of alleged embezzlement by a Teamsters leader in Houston. The anticorruption program was created five years ago by Hoffa to persuade the federal government to drop its longtime oversight of the union. The Teamsters had agreed to far-reaching federal supervision in 1989 to settle a federal racketeering lawsuit charging the union with being controlled by organized crime. The resignations could jeopardize the union's push to end federal oversight of the union.

Forstmann Little paid $497 million in debt and equity for a 90 percent stake in Yankee Candle in 1998 and sold the last of its shares last year for $830 million total. (Globe wire services)
