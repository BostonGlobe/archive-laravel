Earnings Preview: Continental Airlines

January 27, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

DALLAS—Continental Airlines Inc. reports fourth-quarter and full-year 2008 results on Thursday. The following is a summary of key developments and analyst opinion related to the period.

OVERVIEW: The Houston-based airline is expected to follow the lead of rivals Delta, American, United and Southwest by posting a loss for the October-December quarter and for all of last year.

After getting pummeled by high fuel prices in the first half of 2008, Continental and other airlines faced headwinds in the second half from a recession that cut into demand for air travel. Even some of the benefit of falling fuel prices was offset by setbacks in fuel-hedging transactions, which were essentially bets that oil prices would remain high.

Continental cut capacity in the fourth quarter compared with a year earlier, which turned out to be a fortunate move because traffic fell sharply too.

BY THE NUMBERS: Analysts surveyed by Thomson Reuters expected Continental to report a loss of 89 cents per share, or about $98 million, for the fourth quarter, excluding any one-time gains and losses. A year ago, Continental lost $32 million, or 33 cents per share.

Continental said last week it would take $170 million in net charges for the fourth quarter. The biggest, $125 million, would cover losses on a fuel-hedging contract with a Lehman Brothers unit that later filed for bankruptcy.

ANALYST TAKE: Several analysts have "overweight" or similar ratings on Continental stock, saying the company will benefit from lower fuel prices.

Goldman Sachs analyst Christopher Cuomo upgraded the shares to "buy" from "neutral" this month, saying lower fuel prices would offset a bleak revenue picture.

Calyon Securities analyst Ray Neidl said the company is expected to report a "comfortable" amount of cash and should build that reserve even in a tough year because of low debt and capital lease obligations due in 2009.

WHAT'S AHEAD: Airlines are now in the midst of the traditionally weakest part of the year for travel. Officials at other carriers say February and March bookings look especially weak.

Airlines are responding by cutting more flights besides the reductions made last fall. United and American have announced cutbacks for 2009, and Delta officials said this week they are ready to shed more capacity even more.

To fill seats, airlines are launching fare sales. Continental and other major carriers matched reductions that Southwest put into play last week for travel in February and March. If the economy continues to sink, it could spell bad news for the peak summer travel season.

Continental is also in the midst of labor negotiations with its unions. Leaders of the pilots' union said recently they have reached tentative agreements on some items but not pay and benefits.

STOCK PERFORMANCE: Continental shares rose 8 percent in the fourth quarter but fell 19 percent for all of 2008, ending the year at $18.06. On Tuesday, they closed at $14.40, down $2.38, or 14.2 percent.
