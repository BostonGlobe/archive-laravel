Earnings roundup

January 23, 2009

Google exceeds analysts' estimates

YESTERDAY
Close$306.50
Change+$3.42
52-WEEK
High$602.45
Low$247.30

Google Inc., owner of the world's most-used search engine, reported fourth-quarter profit that beat analysts estimates after more companies used the Internet to promote their products in the midst of a recession.

Net income was $382.4 million, or $1.21 a share, the company said. Last year, profit was $1.21 billion, or $3.79 a share. Leaving out some costs, the most recent profit was $5.10 a share, beating the $4.96 estimated by analysts in a Bloomberg survey. The company wrote down its investments in Time Warner Inc.'s AOL and Clearwire Corp. by $1.09 billion, causing a decline in profit from a year earlier.

Chief executive Eric Schmidt, grappling with Google's first recession as a public company, has trimmed costs and jettisoned underperforming products to maintain profit growth. Google controls almost two-thirds of the US market for ads linked to Web searches, a market that is still expanding as companies move more of their ad budgets to the Net.

"This was a pretty decent quarter considering all the fear and loathing out there in the market," said Christa Quarles, an analyst with Thomas Weisel Partners in San Francisco. "It seems that retailers had pulled out all the stops to drive revenue."

Excluding revenue passed on to partner sites, sales climbed to $4.22 billion. Analysts predicted $4.13 billion. At least a dozen analysts had reduced their fourth-quarter earnings and sales estimates since November.

Spending in the United States on ads linked to Web-search results increased 21 percent in 2008, compared with a growth rate of 30 percent in 2007, according to research firm EMarketer Inc. in New York. The firm estimates growth of 15 percent in 2009. (Bloomberg)

Auto loan unit pulls Capital One into red

Change-$1.02
52-WEEK
High$63.50
Low$20.15

Capital One Financial Corp., the McLean, Va.-based credit-card firm, reported a $1.42 billion loss in the fourth quarter on impaired-goodwill charges from its auto lender and a $1 billion boost to reserves for soured loans.

The loss was $3.74 a share, compared with net income of $226.6 million, or 60 cents a share, a year earlier, the company said. The loss from continuing operations was $3.67 a share, missing the 29-cent average profit estimate of 20 analysts surveyed by Bloomberg. Capital One booked an $810.9 million noncash impairment on its auto finance unit and said it expected $8.6 billion in loan defaults in 2009.

Capital One has slumped 45 percent in the past year in New York trading as late payments and defaults rose. Consumers are struggling to repay debt of all kinds as unemployment climbed to 7.2 percent in December, the highest level in almost 16 years, according to the Labor Department.

The goodwill charge is "an acknowledgement that the company overpaid for past acquisitions and no longer expects to realize the future benefits it once did," Bill Carcache of Fox-Pitt Kelton Cochran Caronia Waller said in a research note.

The lender had $12.8 billion in goodwill, or value booked if the price of an acquisition exceeds the fair value of assets, that may eventually be wiped out, Carcache wrote. (Bloomberg)

AMD racks up 9th straight loss

Change-$0.23
52-WEEK
High$8.08
Low$1.62

Advanced Micro Devices Inc., the second-largest maker of personal computer processors, reported its ninth consecutive loss after plummeting PC demand forced customers to slash orders.

The fourth-quarter net loss was $1.42 billion, or $2.34 a share, compared with a loss of $1.77 billion, or $3.06, a year earlier, the Sunnyvale, Calif.-based company said. Sales, excluding some items, were $1.16 billion.

Consumers and business are holding off on computer purchases in the industry's worst slump since at least 2001. AMD and larger rival Intel Corp. are coping with the slowdown by cutting production and staff.

"I don't see any snap back," said Betsy Van Hees at Caris & Co. The San Francisco-based analyst, an Intel shareholder, advises selling AMD stock. "Demand continues to deteriorate."

AMD had said in December that sales would drop about 25 percent from the $1.59 billion in the third quarter, excluding some revenue from licensing its technology. That would have been about $1.19 billion.

Analysts had predicted a loss of 60 cents a share on sales of $1.2 billion, the average of estimates in a Bloomberg survey. For the current period, they project a loss of 59 cents on sales of $1.08 billion. (Bloomberg)
