Limiting risk can be risky, too

Mutual funds designed to deliver returns in any market have a mixed record

By Ross Kerber
Globe Staff / January 25, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

With their investment records in tatters, some mutual fund companies are pinning their hopes on products with an old-fashioned ambition: delivering steady returns. Many of these funds are doing just that, while others are falling short of the mark.

Earlier this month Putnam Investments in Boston introduced four "absolute return" funds that use strategies like buying foreign securities or bonds to smooth out their performance and aim to earn investors 1 to 7 percent a year above inflation. Other companies have launched "long-short" funds, which count on stock declines as part of their strategy to boost returns. And some "market-neutral" funds are designed to perform independently of how stocks are doing.

Many funds use these strategies, in the past only available to ultrawealthy and institutional investors, to make their performance more predictable - like bank deposits - while others chase big returns. But a review of the performance of long-short and market-neutral funds by Chicago data firm Morningstar Inc. found big variations in how well these funds managed to insulate investors from the market's worst days.

While the Dow Jones industrial average fell 34 percent last year - its worst annual showing since 1931 - some funds performed even worse: Franklin Templeton Investments' Global Long-Short fund, for instance, was down 37.6 percent in 2008 and Aberdeen Asset Management's Hedged Core Equity fund, another long-short fund, was down 39.5 percent.

At the same time, most of the long-short funds lost much less than the markets overall and a few even made money in 2008. For instance, Rydex Investments' Managed Futures fund was up 8.5 percent and Deutsche Bank's DWS Investments' Disciplined Market Neutral fund was up 6.8 percent.

John Bonnanzio, who edits a newsletter for Fidelity Investments investors, said plenty of investors would be pleased to have lost only 10 percent of their money last year. But taken together, the showing is disappointing, said Morningstar analyst Harry Milling: In theory, many of the funds shouldn't have lost anything, and their fees tend to be higher than those of other equity funds, as well.

Technically, a long-short fund - the most prevalent type of these kinds of funds - sells some stocks short, betting that they will decline in value, and uses the proceeds to buy extra shares of stocks in which it holds tradi tional long-term investments. Not all such funds promise to hedge against market downturns or big losses, however: When Fidelity introduced its first long-short fund last year, for instance, it mentioned in a press release how its short sales can lead to big losses.

Treasury bonds or certificates of deposit can be a much more reliable way to protect against losses, Milling said, and retail investors drawn to the notion of short-selling should question whether they can handle the inherent risk. "If this is a cheap man's hedge fund, you have to ask why you would want to be in one to begin with," he said.

But some long-short funds do seek to limit losses. Often they adopt a strategy of being "market neutral," or achieving performance unaffected by the stock market's overall direction through a careful balance of their long and short holdings. These are increasingly popular. TrimTabs Investment Research in California found that over the 30 days ended Jan. 21, investors added $577 million to the 34 funds it classifies as "market-neutral" - in a period when equity funds as a whole saw a net outflow of roughly $8 billion.

The firm's chief operating officer, Conrad Gann, said the sector seems especially attractive "given the difficulty that most advisers have had in adding value through any professed edge" by traditional factors such as when they chose to buy or sell stocks.

But long-short funds aren't the only products being marketed as investments that control risk. On Jan. 13, Putnam rolled out four of what it called "absolute return" products, which aim to deliver certain gains in any markets. Its Absolute Return 100 fund seeks to outperform inflation by 1 percent, or 100 basis points, above Treasury bill rates, while three other new funds aim to beat inflation by 3, 5, and 7 percent, respectively. Putnam's management fees increase as the targets get higher, but Putnam will lower its charges if it doesn't deliver the results.

The products are an early strategic move by Robert Reynolds, the former Fidelity leader who last year joined Putnam, part of Canada's Power Corp., charged with turning around its troubled retail funds. Putnam's performance for institutional investors like universities and pension funds was better, however, and in an interview Reynolds said the new absolute return funds aim to give retail investors access to the same strategies as the institutional customers.

Many institutions put 20 to 50 percent of their money into similar strategies, he said, staying in the stock market to beat inflation, but not trying for the highest-possible returns some traditional equities fund managers or hedge fund managers seek. "It's more like an anchor against the wind," he said.

Reynolds said he expects to see more competition in the niche, partly as a way to keep aging baby boomers invested in the stock market with less risk than in traditional funds. "To have a proportion of retirement savings in these vehicles is going to be mandatory" counsel from investment advisers, he said.
