Borders Group says Jones out, Marshall in as CEO

By Michelle Chapman
AP Business Writer / January 5, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

NEW YORK—Borders Group Inc. named Ron Marshall as its new chief executive Monday as part of a management shakeup aimed at turning around the struggling bookseller's balance sheet.

The Ann Arbor, Mich.-based company also warned that its stock is in danger of being delisted from the New York Stock Exchange and reported weak holiday sales -- a key period in which retailers typically record a good portion of their business for the year.

Borders shares added 6 cents, or 13.4 percent, to 51 cents in midday trading. The stock, which traded at $11.60 a year ago and in the $20 range in 2007, has lost nearly all of its value in recent months amid larger-than-expected losses and financing troubles.

Borders appointed Ron Marshall, 54, to replace George Jones as CEO as well as serve as president and a director. Marshall recently served as principal of private equity firm Wildridge Capital Management, which he founded.

Marshall is known for his ability to turn around businesses, having served as CEO of food distributor and retailer Nash Finch Co. during its financial difficulties as well as chief financial officer of Pathmark Stores Inc., now a unit of A&P parent Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Co.

Prior to his tenure at Pathmark, Marshall held senior management roles at Dart Group Corp.'s Crown Books unit and at the college bookstores unit of Barnes & Noble Inc.

But Michael Norris, a senior analyst for Simba Information, a Stamford, Conn.-based media research company, said in an interview that he is a little concerned that Marshall's book retailing experience might be a little dated. He said he thought Jones' approach had been working, but that investors must have thought it wasn't working rapidly enough.

Elsewhere, David Schick of Stifel Nicolaus & Co. thinks Marshall's appointment, coupled with several other personnel changes, may not be enough to get the company back on track.

"We believe such powerful macroeconomic factors are at work that a management change could confuse the key constituents (vendors) and cause incremental friction in the model. Borders has seen too much change in three years, in our view," he wrote in a client note.

The personnel, holiday sales and NYSE listing announcements as well as concerns about Borders' business operations prompted Schick to end coverage of the company with a final "Hold" rating.

In November, after more than a year of evaluating a possible sale of the company as part of a financial turnaround plan, Borders took itself off the block. New management will now help plot the company's next step.

"Progress has been made by Borders Group over recent quarters within the challenging economy to reduce debt, improve cash flow, cut expenses, enhance inventory productivity and improve margins, but it is imperative that the company more aggressively attack these initiatives to address its long-term future," Chairman Larry Pollock said in a statement.

Under terms of Marshall's employment, by Feb. 1 Borders will issue him options to buy 1.8 million shares of company stock. The options vest during certain times over the next three years. He also will receive 200,000 options as part of Borders' long-term incentive program.

In other personnel moves, the company named Mark Bierley as chief financial officer to replace eight-year CFO Ed Wilhelm, who will remain during a transition period. Bierley recently served as Borders' senior vice president of finance.

The company also appointed Dan Smith to the new post of chief administrative officer. Smith will still serve as executive vice president of human resources on top of his new responsibilities, which will include leading the company's information technology group. The group will still be headed by Chief Information Officer Susan Harwood.

Borders also said it was informed by the NYSE that its average closing stock price had fallen below $1 per share over a consecutive 30-day trading period. Borders has 10 days to tell NYSE of its intent to fix the problem and then must show that its stock price and average stock price can trade above $1 for 30 consecutive trading days in the next six months.

Borders also is in jeopardy of not meeting NYSE's market capitalization requirements. The company is hovering near the minimum standard of $25 million over a 30-day trading period. If the company drops below the minimum, its shares may be delisted.

Separately, the company said same-store sales for its Borders superstores fell 14.4 percent for the nine weeks ended Jan. 3. Same-store sales for the company's Waldenbooks segment fell 8 percent.

Same-store sales, or sales at stores open at least a year, is a key indicator of retailer performance since it measures growth at existing stores rather than newly opened ones.

The sales results didn't overly surprise Norris.

"Consumers really cut back on spending heading into the fourth quarter," he said.

Borders, like many retailers, saw its holiday sales drop as shoppers reined in spending due to the ongoing housing downturn, rising food costs, eroding credit and unemployment concerns.

Other booksellers have been squeezed as consumers cut back on their discretionary spending. Powell's Books, one of the nation's largest independent booksellers, said last month that it was asking employees to scale back their hours or take sabbaticals to cope with disappointing sales.

Shares of peer Barnes & Noble Inc. are off 56 percent from their 52-week high of $34.60. Last week business magnate Ron Burkle purchased an 8.3 percent stake in the company through his firm Yucaipa American Management, saying in a regulatory filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission that the company's stock was undervalued.
