Watchdog blasts SEC handling of Madoff case

Warnings were not understood

In a 2005 examination, New York SEC staff found Bernard Madoff agitated and angry at their document requests.
By Beth Healy
Globe Staff / September 3, 2009

Federal securities regulators not only ignored a Boston whistle-blower’s warnings about Bernard Madoff, but could not understand his analysis showing that the swindler’s investment claims were impossible, the inspector general of the US Securities and Exchange Commission concluded in an investigation of the matter.

The inspector general’s findings, released in summary form yesterday by the SEC, found that the agency dropped the ball numerous times after being warned about Madoff, who is serving a 150-year sentence for running the largest Ponzi scheme in history. The investigation found that SEC staff were inexperienced and not knowledgeable about some of the basic parts of the options trading that Madoff allegedly conducted to generate consistent returns.

In May 2000, Harry Markopolos, the now famous whistle-blower, presented the SEC’s Boston office with an eight-page report questioning Madoff’s claims. Markopolos concluded Madoff’s options-trading strategy could not produce the returns he was touting and noted that Madoff seemed to time the market perfectly, always moving out of stocks when the market was falling.

Both Markopolos and an SEC staff accountant testified that it was clear the Boston office’s assistant district administrator at the time “did not understand the information presented,’’ Inspector General David Kotz wrote in a blistering report. As a result, the Boston staff failed to investigate the complaint or, at first, to even refer it to the regional office in New York, according to the inspector general.

“Despite numerous credible and detailed complaints, the SEC never properly examined or investigated Madoff’s trading and never took the necessary, but basic, steps to determine if Madoff was operating a Ponzi scheme,’’ the report said. The SEC also resisted whistle-blowers’ efforts to help, it said.

Between June 1992 and December 2008, when Madoff confessed to a $65 billion Ponzi scheme, the SEC received six substantive complaints that raised significant red flags about Madoff’s hedge fund firm and should have led to questions about whether he was actually engaged in trading, the inspector general found. The full 450-page report is due to be released tomorrow.

In 2001, the Boston office did refer a second complaint from Markopolos to New York, but that office dismissed it almost immediately.

The staff there questioned the whistle-blowers’ motives, the report said, “indicating concerns that ‘he was a competitor of Madoff’s’ who ‘was looking for a bounty.’ ’’

When the SEC finally performed two investigations of Madoff, the report found, the agency failed to follow up on obvious inconsistencies in Madoff’s story, in part due to enforcement staff it called inexperienced.

“Even when Madoff’s answers were seemingly implausible, the SEC examiners accepted them at face value,’’ the report said.

In a 2005 examination, New York SEC staff found Madoff agitated and angry at their document requests. Indeed, one examiner recalled Madoff’s “veins were popping out of his neck,’’ as he grilled the regulators about what they were after. But the examiners’ superiors discouraged them from following up on what they found, the inspector general said.

Kotz said the SEC’s “most egregious’’ lapse was its failure not to verify Madoff’s purported trading with any independent third parties, even after it took testimony from Madoff himself in May 2006.

Madoff later admitted he thought it was “game over’’ after testifying to having cleared his trades through the Depository Trust Co. and provided his account number. He said he was “astonished’’ that the SEC did not follow up.

The SEC’s lapses in dealing with Madoff have prompted the agency to implement reforms, including increased use of subpoenas.

Mary Schapiro, who became SEC chairman after Madoff’s fraud was uncovered in December 2008, said in a statement yesterday that while the reforms should help, “the public deserves a full accounting of why the agency did not detect the Madoff scheme.’’

Chris Dodd, chairman of the Senate Banking Committee, announced that there will be a hearing Sept. 10 on Kotz’s findings. “We will use this report to learn what went wrong and figure out how best to get things right,’’ the Connecticut Democrat said.

Material from Reuters was included in this report.
