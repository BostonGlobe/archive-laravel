In shift, GM to sell majority stake in Opel to Magna

Firms resolve financing issues

A sign outside the Opel plant in Bochum, Germany, heralded the sale yesterday. Magna and its Russian partner, OAO Sberbank, will own 55 percent of Opel. GM will retain a 35 percent stake. (Lennart Preiss/AFP/Getty Images)

SOUTHFIELD, Mich. - General Motors Co. agreed yesterday to sell a majority stake in its Opel unit to auto parts maker Magna International Inc., the buyer favored by Germany, after resolving disputes over financing and access to technology.

Magna and its Russian partner, OAO Sberbank, backed by German loan guarantees of $6.6 billion, will own 55 percent of Opel, GM said. GM won assurances on funding and its intellectual property, said a person familiar with the talks who asked not to be identified because details aren’t public.

The accord is the first major strategic move for chairman Ed Whitacre since he joined GM’s revamped board after a US-backed bankruptcy.

Directors surprised Germany last month by signaling they might keep money-losing Opel rather than selling to Magna under terms GM considered unfavorable.

“It’s clearly a new GM,’’ said Gerald Meyers, a former chief executive officer of American Motors Corp. who is now a management professor at the University of Michigan. “This is nothing the old board and management would have done. The idea of selling Opel and selling to this party would never have been considered.’’

GM isn’t getting any money in the deal. Germany’s government offered $2.2 billion in loans to keep Opel afloat earlier this year. That aid is part of the guarantees to back Magna’s bid and save jobs.

Magna and Sberbank, a Moscow-based bank, are putting up $729 million, with $656 million in equity and the rest in a convertible loan. GM retains a 35 percent stake, and Opel workers will get 10 percent.

GM had said earlier that a bid by investment firm RHJ International SA was simpler than a transaction with Magna, because of concern that intellectual property in Europe would be at risk from the supplier’s ties to Russian carmaker OAO GAZ.

Magna’s proposal foresees a linkup with GAZ, which said in May that it wants to produce 180,000 Opel cars at its main Russian site. GAZ probably will take over Sberbank’s Opel share within a year, John Smith, vice president of GM Group, said on a conference call.

The sale should be completed by Nov. 30, and Opel probably will return to profit in 2011, said Smith, who was GM’s lead negotiator. GM plans to keep using technology from Opel vehicles on models sold in the United States.

“GM doesn’t want to give up the expertise in small and midsize cars that Opel has,’’ said Joseph Phillippi, president of AutoTrends Consulting Inc. in Short Hills, N.J. The conditions on the sale will be important because “unless Opel is going to be joined at the hip with GM in product development, they run the risk of severe cost problems,’’ he said.

Smith said GM expects the Opel loan guarantees to be repaid by 2014. Germany’s government, facing Sept. 27 federal elections, had a say in the decision because of the loan guarantees put forward to support Aurora, Ontario-based Magna.
