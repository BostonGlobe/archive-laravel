Senate holds fate of ‘clunkers’ car discounts

Program’s wild success rapidly drained funds

“Cash for clunkers’’ allows consumers to get discounts toward new fuel-efficient cars if they trade in their old gas-guzzlers. (Mike Blake/ Reuters)
By Erin Ailworth and Sean Sposito
Globe Staff | Globe Correspondent / August 4, 2009

The federal government’s “cash for clunkers’’ program gave automakers and dealers a much-needed sales boost in July, but the fate of the promotion remains up in the air and it’s unclear if officials will guarantee any more discounts for trade-ins.

As of last night, the Senate had not scheduled a time to vote on whether it will approve $2 billion in additional funding for the week-old cash for clunkers incentive. The House voted Friday to give more money just a day after officials learned that so many people had traded in their gas-guzzlers for newer, environmentally friendly vehicles that the original $1 billion allocated had probably run out.

Designed to boost auto sales - as the Big Three automakers said yesterday it appears to have done - the program was supposed to last a few months, covering 250,000 vehicle sales. Cash for clunkers allows consumers to get a $3,500 to $4,500 discount toward a new car if their trade-in vehicle is less than 25 years old and gets 18 miles per gallon or less.

Regan LaChappelle, spokeswoman for the Senate majority leader, Harry Reid, said yesterday that legislators were working with the White House on resolving funding but that it was unclear what would happen. The Senate breaks for a monthlong recess on Friday, and if it doesn’t take up the issue the program runs out of money.

“We hope to be able to do something this week,’’ she said. “We’ll need cooperation from the Republicans.’’

Officials said the government would honor discounts on trade-ins through yesterday. Many consumers took advantage of that assurance, flocking to local dealerships.

“It was just a spectacular weekend,’’ said Ernie Boch Jr., head of Boch Automotive in Norwood. Boch’s Toyota dealership moved 97 cars, with about 25 percent of those sales made under the clunkers program. Roughly a quarter of the 80 sales made at Boch’s Honda dealership also were clunker trade-ins.

It was the same at Herb Chambers’ dealerships, said vice president Jim Xaros.

“There were 75 sales this weekend alone that were just through the cash for clunkers program,’’ Xaros said, adding that the Chambers’ dealerships have sold 450 vehicles overall under the program since it began July 24.

Yesterday, Ford Motor Co. credited the government program with helping the company achieve its first US sales increase in nearly two years. With total sales at 158,838 for July, or 2 percent higher than this time last year, Ford executive George Pipas said the government’s clunker program “put us over the top.’’ Ford reported that fuel-efficient vehicles and hybrids set the sales pace.

Officials for both General Motors Co. and Chrysler Group LLC also said the program has given their companies a boost.

“We saw triple-digit increases in showroom traffic, compared to last year,’’ said Chrysler spokeswoman Kathy Graham. “About 70 percent of the traffic that came into our showrooms came in because of cash for clunkers.’’

Dealers’ troubles with submitting sales under the clunker program spurred concerns that the money was running out. The main problem stemmed from computer systems they used, said Rae Tyson, a spokesman for the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, which administers the program.

The glitch created a backlog of unreported deals, and it’s still unclear how many sales have been made under the promotion. With 80,500 transactions logged as of Saturday, Tyson said, Ford, General Motors, and Chrysler vehicles accounted for 47 percent of sales made under the promotion. Trucks accounted for 83 percent of trade-ins, and cars made up 60 percent of the purchases, with the Ford Focus being the most popular.

Still, the program hasn’t worked for everyone.

At Dan’s Jeep Chrysler Inc. in Westborough, Tony Zappella said his dealership hasn’t been able to take as much advantage of the program as much as he would like because it ran out of qualified cars to sell on the first day.

“It was absolutely crazy in here,’’ Zappella said.

Meanwhile, George Leckenby, sales manager at Mazda of Everett, said his dealership has put the program on hold until it’s clear there will be enough government money to reimburse dealers.

“Until the [Senate] vote comes through, we’ve stopped,’’ Leckenby said, adding that sales staff is telling customers “if we can help them, we will. But we don’t want to put ourselves on a limb, either.’’

And at least one dealer questioned whether the program would boost sales in the long run.

Paul Cotrone, general sales manager at Cambridge Chevrolet and Honda, said he worried that the “feeding frenzy’’ he has seen as a result of the clunkers promotion is a flash in the pan that will leave him with fewer sales in the coming months.

“It’s taking future business,’’ he said.
