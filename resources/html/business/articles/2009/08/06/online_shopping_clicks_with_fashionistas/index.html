Clicking with customers

Even in a rough economy, shoppers still crave bargains. Online retailer Rue La La may just have their number.

Fashion stylist Callista Wilson (left) and photographer John Davenport work with model Katelyn Fortes at the Rue La La studio in downtown Boston. (Photos By Barry Chin/Globe Staff)
By Laura Bennett
Globe Correspondent / August 6, 2009

For a website devoted to discount high-end shopping, the home page is strikingly subtle. Simple black script declares, “Most wanted brands. Private sale prices.’’ A cursor blinks invitingly near a box marked “Password.’’ A pink door is hemmed in by velvet rope.

Since its launch in April 2008, members-only sale site Rue La La has taken a bite out of the online retail marketplace. Consumers, skittish about spending, still love a bargain, and Boston-based Rue La La - along with online competitors Gilt Groupe, Ideeli, and HauteLook - are luring shoppers, even as upscale retail stores take a beating.

While some high-end department stores lose revenue and trim staff, Rue La La, which now claims a million users and features over 350 luxury brands, is expanding. The company just partnered with Elle.com, granting visitors to the magazine’s website access to Rue La La designer sales, and launched a mobile component so members can shop from their iPhones. Tomorrow, Rue La La pulls up stakes, moving from its 21,000-square-foot studio downtown to a 43,000-square-foot loft space in South Boston, all bright windows and slick wood.

With so many sales and so many shoppers, they needed the extra room.

“Given the overall state of the economy right now, the private sales site has been the brightest spot both in e-commerce and in the entire consumer retail market,’’ said Dennis Baldwin, managing partner of Rue La La investor Breakaway Ventures.

Here’s how it works: Rue La La acquires slow-selling or overstocked inventory from high-end retailers and offers it at reduced prices to members. Word spreads virally; people must be invited by a friend in order to shop. Featured brands are not little-known names. A recent lineup included designer goods from Dior, Tom Ford, Puma, Corum watches, Judith Leiber, and Antik Denim. E-mail reminders appear chirpily in a member’s inbox every day, announcing the latest sales.

Sales last only 48 hours, and a big blinking clock at the top of the webpage counts down the seconds until a sale is over. Since some items sell out (inventory includes anywhere from two dozen to several hundred of each item, and shoppers aren’t privy to the totals), members are motivated to click and buy quickly. The adrenal charge of fast-paced, exclusive sales and the ease and inconspicuousness of shopping online make for a compelling combination.

“The anonymity of shopping from home makes people much more comfortable these days than walking up and down the fancy shopping streets of America, and that’s definitely contributed to the success of our brand,’’ said Ben Fischman, CEO of Rue La La’s parent company, Boston-based Retail Convergence Inc.

At 38, Fischman - a fast talker who makes fierce eye contact and leans in conspiratorially when he speaks - is a seasoned businessman. He helped launch the headwear chain Lids from a mall kiosk at age 22, and in 2005 became CEO of Smartbargains.com, a bargain retail site offering a range of goods that includes kitchenware, yard supplies, and apparel. Smartbargains.com, which will also move to the new location, is owned by Retail Convergence.

“It becomes a game,’’ Fischman says of Rue La La’s timed sales. “It’s very addictive.’’

Rue La La is not the only website to make inroads with discount-luxury shoppers. Gilt Groupe, launched in 2007, is the behemoth of the sector, and expects to bring in some $400 million in revenue this fiscal year, up from $100 million last year, according to the company. Rue La La projects $125 million to $140 million in revenue this calendar year, up from between $25 million and $30 million last year, Fischman said. The somewhat smaller HauteLook and Ideeli likewise expect their sales to increase four- to five-fold, according to their company CEOs. Rue La La boasts 160 full-time employees - up from 90 at the end of last year - and is currently hiring 30 more.

On a recent morning, Rue La La’s offices at 10 Milk St. are a tangle of electrical wires and piled boxes as employees prepare for their big move across town. But the behind-the-scenes work - photographing models dressed in designer fare and luxe accessories - continues at a breakneck pace.

In a back room, cans of hairspray clutter tabletops. Shelves bristle with stilettos perched beside heaps of chunky necklaces - accessories used to accent the brands for sale. Though racks of soon-to-be-photographed designer goods line the walls, all merchandise is shipped out of a warehouse in Kentucky. Rue La La holds an average of three to four fashion shoots a day, and images are uploaded to the site. A model poses coyly in slinky green Cosabella pajamas, hand on her hip, while a photographer captures her every move.

Rue La La’s creative team has compiled a sheet of instructions for the photographer and stylist. “Do not allow her hair to get too out of control,’’ it reads. “Sexy but classy.’’ In the doorway, Stacey Santo, the company’s vice president of marketing, crosses her arms and looks down at the memo. “We want to make sure the brands are shown in the best possible light,’’ she says, nodding approvingly at the stylist.

Presenting clothing, accessories, even home goods in a way that fits a brand’s profile is key for both Rue La La and the companies whose products they sell.

“These sites are giving brands a chance to sell out during tough times in a way that is appropriate for their image,’’ said Steven Kolb, executive director of the Council of Fashion Designers of America, a nonprofit trade organization. “It’s important that it doesn’t feel secondary and it doesn’t feel cheap.’’

Since private retail sites are password protected, designer brands don’t have to worry about search engines digging up cached evidence of sales or discounts that might cheapen their reputation.

“Designers facing challenging times have been forced to think outside the box and use different distribution channels,’’ said Alexandra Wilkis Wilson, cofounder and chief merchandising officer of Gilt Groupe.

Still, given the litany of sour economic news, who’s buying at these members-only shopping sites? Bargain-hunters who value style - and discretion, said Marshal Cohen, chief retail analyst with the market research firm NPD Group.

“Consumers may be cutting down, but they’re not cutting out,’’ Cohen said. “It’s all about the secret shop, which is more discreet and convenient. Conspicuous consumption has gone through a whole metamorphosis.’’

Rue La La’s customer base, Fischman said, is a combination of aspirational buyers and wealthy ones who’ve been marginally affected by the recession. But even for those who can afford to breeze through Bergdorf with armloads of full-priced merchandise, the private sale site has an increasing allure.

“The wealthiest of consumers still love saving money at this time,’’ Cohen said. “They just don’t want spouses and friends to know that they’re out shopping in this down economy, spending money on what might be considered impulsive or frivolous items.’’

Rue La La ships merchandise in a plain brown box. Gilt Groupe, Ideeli, and HauteLook also favor subtle, simple packaging. The typical trappings of luxury fare are tucked away inside: monogrammed tissue paper, embossed note cards.

“I shop more frequently online than in department stores at this point,’’ said Molly Thalhauber, 26, of Nashville, who’s a member of Rue La La, Gilt, and HauteLook. “If you don’t like something, you can return it without the embarrassment of going back to the store.’’
