ON THE HOT SEAT

Branson's key to staying aloft: filling seats

Virgin chief isn't above price cuts, calling customers

http://link.brightcove.com/services/link/bcpid1185071465http://www.brightcove.com/channel.jsp?channel=16988910
February 22, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

Sir Richard Branson, who established Virgin Group almost 40 years ago, has created about 200 companies worldwide spanning the travel, telecommunications, media, finance, and health industries. Branson spoke with Globe reporter Nicole C. Wong about his new airline endeavors.

As of this month, both Virgin Atlantic Airways and now Virgin America fly to Boston. How does the Hub fit into your airline empire's business strategy?

Boston was one of the first cities that Virgin Atlantic flew to. We started 25 years ago. Bostonians accepted Virgin well and the service that Virgin Atlantic offered.

Boston's got an educated, intelligent group of people who live here. I suspect that's because it's so close to the UK. Great university towns. I suspect the Virgin brand is very well known in Boston. In the East and West coasts, it's particularly well known. In middle America, less well known.

Negotiations are coming up for Phase II of the Open Skies air traffic agreement between the United States and the European Union. What are you hoping will come of that, and how would your airlines take advantage of it?

I'm not sure it's going to survive. Stage I of Open Skies came in. The United States promised to do certain things for Stage II of Open Skies and really try to make it an Open Skies, but there's no indication that's going to happen. It's going to be interesting to see what happens.

At the moment, I can only own 25 percent of an American carrier. I'd like to have no restrictions anywhere in the world, that anyone can fly anywhere. Get governments out of any involvement in this industry.

When you launched the V Australia airline this month, you said those planes will never cross the Pacific Ocean with an empty seat. Instead, you'll slash airfares to fill unsold seats. Will the same be true for Virgin America's planes flying between Boston and California?

Absolutely. Nothing's different. Our philosophy has always been create the best product. It should fill because it's the best product. Be competitive on fares and be very competitive if you've got an empty seat. Why fly an empty seat anywhere in the world if there could be a person in that seat?

This month Virgin America released its first financial results, and they show the company has been gushing losses that are large for a start-up carrier - and are more precarious since we're in a deep recession in which business and leisure travel is plummeting. You invested more money in the carrier last fall to help stem its losses. How long do you plan to pour money into Virgin America to keep it afloat?

The money that we put into Virgin America was money that we planned to put into Virgin America. This year we'll either break even or lose a very small amount of money. And next year we'll start making quite good returns on the money we've invested. As long as we continue to invest, we'll continue to get the load factors that will make us profitable in the future.

The problem with most airlines is they're not willing to invest. If Virgin Atlantic is launching a new international route, it takes about five years for a new route to break even.

The rant by a passenger who complained to you in December about his food on a Virgin flight from Mumbai to London is quite famous now. And you've hired him to make sure your Indian meals appeal to Westerners. Had you seen and tasted those dishes before his complaint?

I've been on the Mumbai route and have eaten on that route. I don't know if it was the same dish. It was a dish that Indians like. It was obvious presentation for Westerners was not good. He's taught us a good lesson and we've done something about it. That's the important thing about complaints. I can't ring everybody personally, but I liked his letter and rang him.

When you flew that route, did you like the food?

Yeah. Virgin generally wins a lot of awards for our food. But the team doesn't always get it right, and on that occasion they didn't.
