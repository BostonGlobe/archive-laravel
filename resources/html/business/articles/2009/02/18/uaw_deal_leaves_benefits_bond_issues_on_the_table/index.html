UAW deal leaves benefits, bond issues on the table

By Kimberly S. Johnson
AP Auto Writer / February 18, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

DETROIT—General Motors and Chrysler kept working Wednesday to hammer out two issues left conspicuously unfinished in their requests for more government aid -- how to fund a union-run health care trust and how to get bondholders to exchange debt for equity.

The companies submitted plans to the government Tuesday spelling out how they plan to become profitable if they get a total of $39 billion in financing.

While the United Auto Workers reached a tentative deal to help General Motors Corp., Chrysler LLC and Ford Motor Co. reduce labor costs, still at issue is whether the union will accept payments to the health care trust as stock instead of cash. Conditions of the government loans ask the union to take up half of what they're owed as an equity position in the companies.

Bondholders, meanwhile, are holding out on a deal to exchange two-thirds of the GM's debt for equity until there's more clarity from the union, which is weighing the risks of taking shares of GM's poor-performing stock to fund retiree benefits starting next year.

"The bondholders would like to see the UAW receive the same treatment," said Kip Penniman, a corporate bonds analyst for KDP Investment Advisors who is not involved in the automakers' negotiations. "I think until those major problems are addressed, there's not going to be a deal."

GM, Chrysler and Ford agreed to fund the trusts, called voluntary employee beneficiary associations, or VEBAs, as part of the landmark 2007 contract reached with the UAW.

The trusts would pay health care bills for about 800,000 UAW retirees, spouses and dependents and move billions in liabilities off the companies' books. GM expects to save about $3 billion a year, while Ford says it will save $1 billion annually.

GM has to pay roughly $20 billion into its health care trust, while Chrysler must pay around $9.9 billion. Ford owes $6.3 billion to its VEBA at the end of this year.

The UAW fears that if it takes too much stock, the trusts won't have enough to pay benefits for hundreds of thousands of retirees and spouses. But if the union refuses to take the stock and GM and Chrysler don't satisfy the government's loan terms, the companies could tumble into bankruptcy, putting those benefits into greater jeopardy.

Penniman said the government must step in so that thousands of retirees can maintain their benefits, adding that the union's biggest mistake in recent years has been "hammering the Detroit Three" to keep programs such as jobs banks that give workers most of their salaries and benefits while they're laid off.

"They should have been focused on health care and trying to solve that problem," he said. "That's the No. 1 sticking point."

But the UAW is conceding some of the benefits its members have enjoyed. Tuesday's tentative agreements limit overtime, change work rules, cut lump-sum cash bonuses and get rid of cost-of-living pay raises to help reduce the companies' labor costs, people briefed on the agreement said Wednesday.

Base wages for UAW workers will remain the same, but the deal limits supplemental pay that laid-off workers receive while they collect unemployment benefits, said the people, who spoke on condition of anonymity because union members have not been told about the terms.

UAW spokeswoman Christine Moroski declined to comment Wednesday. The union said it won't release details until it reaches agreements with the companies on payments into the VEBA.

Everything still has to be ratified by the UAW's members.

"The UAW is in a hard place. They're being asked to unwind all these hard won gains," Penniman said. "How are they going to bring it to their rank and file and ask them to vote on it?"

GM Chief Operating Officer Fritz Henderson declined to give details of the agreement Wednesday but said it takes major steps to bring U.S. automakers' labor costs in line with those at Asian automakers' U.S. plants. Such concessions are a condition of the $17.4 billion in government loans that the automakers have received so far.

Ford, which secured a $23.5 billion line of credit from private sources in 2007 before credit markets tightened, has said it can make it through 2009 without government help and isn't required to make the same changes. But the Dearborn, Mich.-based automaker may need to seek federal aid if industry sales -- already at 26-year lows -- continue to spiral downward.

Ford has said it expects to get the same union concessions so that it isn't left at a disadvantage, and before requesting aid, it may seek to clean up its balance sheet through its own debt exchange.

"I think they would find it desirable to go down that path," said Pete Hastings, senior vice president for Morgan Keegan. "Ford wouldn't want to labor under an amount of debt when GM had relieved itself of that."

Analysts expect Ford to wait until the last possible moment to request aid, most likely in the form of a $9 billion revolving line of credit, as it is currently building its reputation among consumers as the U.S. automaker not taking taxpayer money.

But that's risky, in light of its competitors seeking huge sums of cash now, Hastings said.

"It's hard to know when to pull the trigger," he said. "They need to balance the political will of the taxpayer giving them money with the easier road of following GM and Chrysler and imitating a similar path."
