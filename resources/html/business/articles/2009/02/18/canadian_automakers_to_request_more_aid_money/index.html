Canadian automakers to request more aid money

By Charmaine Noronha
Associated Press Writer / February 18, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

TORONTO—The Canadian subsidiaries of General Motors Corp. and Chrysler LLC are expected to request more aid when they release their restructuring plans later this week, Ontario's provincial premier said Wednesday.

"We know that there are going to be more asks involved. We know that we're going to have to do more to help support the sector," said Dalton McGuinty during a press conference.

The premier of Ontario, the country's heartland of the auto industry, was referring to the Canadian automakers' viability plans that will be submitted to the federal and provincial Ontario government Friday so they can tap into government loans.

The Canadian automakers' strategy plans are expected just days after their struggling parent companies asked the U.S. government for nearly $22 billion more in federal aid. GM and Chrysler have so far received $17.4 billion in loans from the U.S. Treasury. The additional aid request brings the price tag for saving GM and Chrysler to a hefty $39 billion.

GM also announced plans to cut 47,000 jobs and close five more U.S. plants as part of its restructuring strategy outlining how the automakers intend to again become viable and repay government loans.

A total of 26,000 of the GM cuts will come from outside the U.S. It is not yet clear what the impact will be in Canada, where GM employs about 19,000 people. The cuts are expected to be made by the end of 2009. GM Canada did not immediately respond to calls for comment Wednesday.

Chrysler said it will cut 3,000 more jobs and stop producing three vehicle models.

"At first blush, in the grand scheme of things, it seems like good news," McGuinty said of the U.S. automakers' plans, referring specifically to word from Chrysler Chairman and CEO Bob Nardelli that the job cuts will not fall north of the U.S. border.

However, McGuinty called on Prime Minister Stephen Harper to impress upon President Barack Obama during their meeting in Canada's capital on Thursday that the operations of the Detroit Three auto makers are fully integrated in North America.

"Our fates are truly intertwined when it comes to the future of the sector. The only pitch I am making to U.S. decision makers is, 'we are part of the solution. We want to find a way to help you,'" said McGuinty.

Being part of the solution will likely involve Canada offering up 20 percent of the additional $19-billion General Motors and Chrysler are seeking to stay afloat.

In December, Canada's Conservative government and Ontario provincial government offered GM and Chrysler's Canadian subsidiaries CA$4 billion ($3 billion) in aid.

GM Canada recently declined the loan because it said it was able to temporarily support itself through "cost-saving measures." The Canadian automaker said it will still seek money from the Canadian governments to help execute its long-term plan.

The loan offered to Chrysler Canada is expected to be finalized in a few days, said McGuinty.

Along with Canadian governments acknowledging their role in helping the ailing auto sector, the president of the Canadian Auto Workers union has also said that the union will do its part to help the industry survive.

"We know that Chrysler, Ford and General Motors are going to be smaller companies and we know that based on the global financial crisis and based on what the government is going to put on their terms and conditions, the CAW is going to have to make some compromises," said Ken Lewenza.

The Canadian Auto Workers union already agreed to a three-year wage freeze in 2008, saving the industry about CA$900 million ($714 million) in wage costs, on top of agreeing to 13,000 jobs cuts in 2008.

Lewenza said CAW will now begin restructuring talks with the Canadian units of the Detroit Three automakers in the next few weeks and follow up with the Canadian management teams of the U.S. automakers to protect investment in Canada.
