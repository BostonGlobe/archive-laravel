Lee Enterprises restructures debt repayments

By Jordan Robertson
Associated Press Writer / February 19, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

SAN FRANCISCO—Lee Enterprises Inc., publisher of the St. Louis Post-Dispatch and 48 other daily newspapers, said Thursday it has arranged to push out hundreds of millions of dollars in debt repayments until 2012 in a bid to conserve cash.

The changes are designed to help the Davenport, Iowa-based company get some breathing room from a series of big bills that were coming due, as the recession and shift in advertising dollars to the Internet continues to pummel the newspaper business.

There were three parts to Lee's announcement.

The first was that Lee said Thursday it repaid $120 million of $306 million in debt that was coming due in April, and has changed the terms of the contract for the remaining balance of $186 million so that it's not due until April 28, 2012.

Lee assumed that debt when it bought the Post-Dispatch and the rest of the Pulitzer newspaper group in 2005.

In the second prong of the announcement, Lee said it had restructured the payment schedule for the $1.1 billion bank loan the company needed to buy the Pulitzer business.

The publisher struck a deal with its lenders to sharply cut the amount of money it has to fork over each year to pay down the balance of that loan.

Instead of paying $54.9 million for the remainder of 2009, Lee will only be required to pay $22.1 million. For 2010, Lee will only have to pay $77.8 million instead of the $166.3 million that was required under the original terms of the contract. In 2011, Lee is on the hook for just $65 million instead of $261.3 million.

In 2012, Lee will owe $70 million instead of $166.3 million until April of that year, when the rest of the loan comes due.

The arrangement means Lee will owe a lot more when the remainder of the loan needs to be repaid. At that time, Lee will owe $502.5 million, instead of the $83.1 million Lee would have owed then had it stuck to the original repayment plan.

The third part of Lee's announcement was that the publisher redeemed the 5 percent interest that The Herald Publishing Co. held in the Post-Dispatch and STL Distribution Services. Lee said the Herald will be paid 10 percent of the value of those companies at a time of the Herald's choosing between April 28, 2013 and April 28, 2015.

Resolving that deal also buys Lee some time because the Herald company could have redeemed its interest in April 2010 under its original contract.

Lee's chief financial officer, Carl Schmidt, said the rocky economy and the difficult lending environment made the negotiations "complicated," but that the results are in the best interest of Lee's shareholders.

"As a result of these actions, we have significantly improved our liquidity for the foreseeable future," Schmidt said in a statement.

Lee's shares gained 9 cents, or 25.8 percent, to 42 cents in extended trading. The stock had closed up 4 cents, or 13.8 percent, at 33 cents during the regular trading session. The publisher announced the debt changes after the market closed.

Other publishers are doing some financial gymnastics of their own to weather the recession. The New York Times Co. also said Thursday that it is suspending its dividend payments to shareholders.
