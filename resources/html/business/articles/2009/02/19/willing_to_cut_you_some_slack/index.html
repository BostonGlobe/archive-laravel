Willing to cut you some slack

In hard times, businesses go extra mile to keep patrons

By Kathleen Burge
Globe Staff / February 19, 2009

  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

In flush financial times, businesses tend to abide by a strict principle: No money, no service.

But in these times of widespread financial woe, some small-business owners are trying to hang on to patrons who have difficulty with their bills, even if that means carrying them for a while. The hope is that the customers will stick with them and start paying again once the economy revives.

Take Larry Cronin. As the economy sank fast last fall, Cronin started to feel the waves of unease at Fitness First, the small Arlington gym he opened in 1982. Patrons began trickling in to cancel their memberships, saying they had lost their jobs and could no longer afford the monthly dues.

Some of them probably were surprised by what happened next: Cronin offered to let them continue to work out at the gym for free, until they found work again. These days, he said, about 30 people exercise at the facility on "scholarship," free of membership fees.

"They pay nothing, and at some point, when things get better, they might pick up their membership dues again," said Cronin, who is also giving free guest passes to mem bers who donate five items to the Arlington Food Pantry.

No doubt he sees it as a gesture of hope and goodwill. But some entrepreneurs say it's also smart business strategy.

Suzanne Foley, president and founder of A Place to Grow, a child-care company with seven locations in Arlington, Bedford, Belmont, and Concord, has been through several cycles of economic despair since she started her business in 1979.

"The thing I learned the most was the families we helped the most, when they got a job, they came back to A Place to Grow," she said.

The LEAP School, a private program for young children with four locations in the region, including Lexington and Concord, is making plans to double its financial aid budget for next year, helping out as many as 20 families grappling with unemployment.

The Dance Haven in Medford has granted some extra scholarships to families facing sudden hardship. At the Thoreau Club, a Concord fitness club, exercisers can now put their memberships on hold indefinitely, forgoing the club, and membership fees, until they can pay once again.

Robin Shapiro, the executive director and founder of the LEAP School, knows some parents of children at her centers have lost their jobs. So, "we've taken a lot of action in the past year," she said. "We've put together some financial aid options, some scholarship options."

The school has also brought in consultants to train the teachers in handling stress in children whose parents might be struggling financially.

Cronin doesn't advertise the scholarships at his gym, but staffers will offer them when patrons come forward to cancel their memberships citing financial hardship. The company doesn't ask for proof.

Though Cronin has long helped patrons who found themselves unable to pay membership fees, usually only a handful of people are working out for free. But starting last year, he said, that number has increased many times over.

Cronin says gyms can afford to take on some nonpaying customers because they don't significantly drive up the operating costs. But not all businesses decide they can offer free or reduced services.

"We have had some new patients call who said that they're recently out of work," said Chris Pappas, office manager at the Acupuncture Center of Andover. "We kind of stick with what our fees are."

All business owners have limits to what they can offer clients. Foley said that while she wants to keep children at the center even if their parents cannot afford to pay, she also needs to keep her business profitable. "I have 80 teachers I have to keep employed," she said.

Still, one couple she helped recently found themselves unable to pay for after-school care for their daughter, who had been at one of Foley's centers for eight years. "She continues to come two afternoons a week," Foley said. "She's an important part of the school, and she needs to see her friends."

And sometimes, Foley finds ways to be creative. With one family struggling to pay child-care fees, she worked out a barter: The mother is helping her with a project, and Foley is providing free child care.

At the Dance Haven in Medford, owner Rachele Kakles says she has helped out-of-work parents when possible. Now those who get a break in the cost of lessons often try to help out by volunteering, she said.

"If you have a customer that follows you and works with you for years, are you going to turn them away the last two months for dance?" she asked.
