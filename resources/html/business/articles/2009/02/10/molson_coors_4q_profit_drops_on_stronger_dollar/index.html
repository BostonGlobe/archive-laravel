Molson Coors 4Q profit drops on stronger dollar

Coors beers are seen on display at JJ&F Market in in Palo Alto, Calif., Monday, Feb. 9, 2009. Molson Coors Brewing and SABMiller said Tuesday fourth-quarter profit for their joint venture MillerCoors declined 40 percent mainly due to hefty impairment charges related to its Sparks brand and costs to integrate the two companies' beer brands in the U.S. (AP Photo/Paul Sakuma)
By Lauren Shepherd
AP Business Writer / February 10, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

NEW YORK—Higher beer prices weren't enough to safeguard Molson Coors Brewing Co. from the stronger dollar, high commodity costs and declining sales volumes of Miller Lite and other brands, leading the brewer to report a lower profit for its fourth quarter on Tuesday.

Molson, which partially owns the Miller brands in the U.S. through a joint venture with London brewer SABMiller, said its financial results were hurt the most by the strength of the dollar, which turned sales in foreign currencies into less revenue once they were translated into dollars.

But lower sales volumes of Miller Lite and other brands in the U.S., Canada and Britain also took a toll on the results.

Chief Executive Peter Swinburn told The Associated Press that drinkers may have balked at higher prices for Miller Lite in the U.S., where consumers have been hard hit by rising unemployment and a more-than-yearlong recession.

Like other brewers, Molson has raised its prices in the past year to keep up with costs.

Swinburn also said Miller Lite is a popular brand at bars and restaurants and those sales have declined as the economy has softened and fewer people go out for a night on the town.

To boost sales of the brand, Molson plans to unveil a new ad campaign at the end of the month. Swinburn declined to offer details but said it will likely focus on "taste."

The overall decline in sales volumes in the fourth quarter does not necessarily indicate a long-term trend, Swinburn said, since sales volumes in the U.S. have grown slightly since the beginning of January.

"I think we're recession-resistant, not recession-proof," he said. "In economic times like this, this is a good industry to be in."

In the U.K., where sales were also weak despite price increases, Swinburn said the company may be falling victim to the soft economy and a smoking ban that went into effect in 2007.

"The pricing increase we've taken doesn't seem in any way to accelerate the decline we were seeing anyway," he said.

Investors though, still sent shares down $3.20, or 7.9 percent, to to end at $37.30. The drop came as the Dow Jones industrial average tumbled nearly 382 points, or 4.6 percent.

For the quarter ended Dec. 28, profit fell 44 percent to $96.8 million, or 52 cents per share, from $173.2 million, or 94 cents per share, a year earlier.

Once discontinued operations and charges related to the joint venture and other items are stripped out, profit was $105.1 million, or 57 cents per share, down from $133 million, or 73 cents per share.

Analysts surveyed by Thomson Reuters, who typically exclude one-time costs and other special items, expected profit of 71 cents per share.

Swinburn told investors that exchange rates accounted for more than half of the year-over-year decline in profit.

The Denver-based brewer said total revenue fell 49 percent to $1.10 billion but that number does not include sales from the joint venture.

Worldwide beer volume, or the number of barrels of beer sold to customers, slipped about 4 percent.

In the U.S., where the results include the company's 42 percent share of the net income of the joint venture -- called MillerCoors -- a rise in adjusted profit was helped by higher prices.

Excluding hefty charges related to the joint venture and costs to change to its Sparks caffeinated alcoholic beverage, profit at MillerCoors climbed $19 million from a year ago.

Sales to retailers fell about 2 percent, reflecting softer sales of Miller Lite and some "above-premium" brands. Overall, sales in the U.S. rose about 3 percent to $1.74 billion.

Molson said its business in Canada was hurt by the weak Canadian dollar and a slowdown in the nation's beer industry, with sales growing less than 1 percent during the period. The company also blamed bad weather and the soft economy.

Christopher Shanahan, a research analyst with Frost & Sullivan, said sales should jump in all regions during the summer as consumers barbecue and head to the beach.

"There should be a summer spike, especially for the core brands," he said.

But he added that sales might not grow much from the year-ago amount since consumers will likely have less cash in their pockets.

For the full year, net income dropped 22 percent to $388 million, or $2.09 per share, from $497.2 million, or $2.74 per share. Revenue fell 4 percent.
