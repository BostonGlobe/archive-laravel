La. banks have second thoughts about federal funds

By Alan Sayre
AP Business Writer / February 20, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

NEW ORLEANS—Some Louisiana banking companies that took money from the U.S. Treasury are having second thoughts about the resulting strings, including limits on executive cash bonuses and incentives.

The head of Lafayette-based MidSouth Bancorp Inc. said Friday that the government is failing to discern between banks that took the money to avoid collapsing -- under a plan called Troubled Asset Relief Program -- and healthy banks that did so to respond to the call to loan money to stimulate the economy through a program called Capital Purchase Program.

"We thought by accepting the capital, which is money we have to pay back with interest, we were embarking on a plan that would give us even more money to deploy in our communities," said MidSouth Chief Executive Rusty Cloutier.

But Cloutier said the government is trying to put the same restrictions on healthy banks that are being imposed on troubled institutions. He did not specifically cite executive compensation.

"The Treasury needs to stop listening to the special interests of the 'too big to fail' banks that got us into this trouble and break them up," Cloutier said.

MidSouth sold $20 million of preferred stock to the Treasury and said it would use the capital to increase its capacity to support hurricane revitalization projects. Company officials then held 14 meetings in Louisiana and Texas to encourage borrowing.

Two other major Louisiana banking companies also are participating in CPP. Iberiabank Corp. sold preferred stock to the government for $90 million. Whitney Holding Corp. sold $300 million in preferred stock -- the first time that New Orleans-based company has issued such shares.

MidSouth and Iberiabank have reported no major problems with previously issued loans.

Whitney has reported credit problems with residential and commercial real estate loans, primarily in the Tampa, Fla., area. But Whitney has said its regulatory capital levels remain well above those required for the company to be considered well-capitalized.

Beth Ardoin, a spokeswoman for Lafayette-based Iberiabank, which has been expanding its footprint across the South, said Iberiabank continued to analyze the program but would have no further comment.

Whitney officials did not return a call for comment. But Whitney CEO John Hope III told The Times-Picayune of New Orleans that the rules are "now at the point where the cost now far exceeds the benefits."

Contained in the $787 billion economic stimulus package President Barack Obama signed Tuesday are strict rules on bonuses. For example, an executive making $1 million a year in salary could receive only $500,000 in bonus money. The restriction went beyond what Obama wanted, but congressional Democrats said the limits on all banks taking federal money were needed if Obama plans to ask Congress for more money to save the financial sector.

The administration only wanted to apply the restrictions to banks that took money to ward off collapse. The White House warned smaller banks in good financial shape would be dissuaded from taking the money, resulting in slower lending rates.

As of Friday, 416 banking companies had accepted $196 billion in Treasury investments through CPP, said Peter Garuccio, a spokesman for the American Bankers Association. He said he expected at least some to be working on an "exit strategy" from CPP.

"We have consistently expressed a need to recognize that the CPP is designed for healthy banks," Garuccio said. "It's a voluntary program and it is an investment by the government in these institutions designed to promote increased lending."

Program participants will be allowed to opt out immediately, Garuccio said.

"It was sold to us by the feds as a partnership, but it's turning out to be something very different," Cloutier said. "It's looking more and more like the federal government wants to treat this as a needs-based issue, and we didn't need the money."
