Meltdown 101: Consumer prices, by the numbers

By Christopher S. Rugaber
AP Economics Writer / February 20, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

WASHINGTON—A surprising detail can be found in Friday's report on consumer prices: By the Labor Department's reckoning, Americans are paying more for housing than they were a year ago.

How can that be, given that plummeting home prices have wreaked havoc on the economy for almost two years? Aren't we in the middle of the worst housing market in decades?

A closer look at the details of the Labor Department report -- which found that consumer prices, overall, were flat in the 12 months ending Jan. 31 -- offers an explanation for that housing number, as well as a wealth of other interesting tidbits about rising and falling prices.

Starting with the housing figure: It turns out the Consumer Price Index -- the number that's used to measure changes in consumer prices -- doesn't measure changes in the prices of actual homes. Instead, it seeks to gauge the cost of housing by looking at what owners would pay to rent their homes. And rents have increased modestly over the past year despite the glut of homes on the market.

As a result, the report found that homeownership costs rose 2.2 percent in the year that ended Jan. 31.

Steve Reed, an economist at the Labor Department's Bureau of Labor Statistics, says housing is a very complex cost to measure. To begin with, the CPI seeks to gauge consumption costs -- what people actually paid for stuff -- not investments, which is how the department generally classifies real estate.

In addition, when a mortgage is involved, housing costs depend as much on interest rates as home prices.

As a result, the department uses "owners' equivalent rent," or the rent a homeowner could charge a tenant, as a proxy for home ownership costs. The theory is that over the long run, changes in home prices should be reflected in rent levels, Reed said.

So that explains the housing number. What other interesting details are in the CPI report? Here are some highlights, by the numbers.

------

ALMOST NO INFLATION

0.3 percent: January's increase in the Consumer Price Index

0 percent: The change in CPI during the year that ended Jan. 31, the lowest 12-month number since 1955

0.2 percent: January's increase in the "core" CPI, which excludes food and energy

1.7 percent: The increase in "core" CPI during the year ended Jan. 31, the lowest since August 2004

------

ENERGY PRICES HAVE PLUMMETED FOR THE PAST YEAR ...

31.4 percent: Decrease in the CPI energy index since its July 2008 peak

40.4 percent: Decrease in gas prices during the year ending January 31

------

... BUT TURNED AROUND LAST MONTH

6 percent: January's increase in gas prices

1.7 percent: January's increase in the CPI energy index

------

WHAT GOT PRICIER IN JANUARY?

1.6 percent: Increase in men's and boys' clothing prices

0.4 percent: Increase in medical care costs

0.8 percent: Increase in tobacco product prices

0.2 percent: Increase in alcoholic beverage prices

------

AND WHAT GOT CHEAPER?

1.3 percent: Decrease in fruit and vegetable costs

1.1 percent: Decrease in hotel prices and other "lodging away from home"

2.7 percent: Decrease in home fuel oil costs

1.8 percent: Decrease in public transportation costs

0.5 percent: Decrease in televisions, stereos and other audiovisual equipment

------

EAT OUT, OR STAY HOME?

0.1 percent: January's decrease in "food at home" prices

0.3 percent: January's increase in food "away from home" prices

------

THE MILK IN YOUR CORN FLAKES

0.6 percent: January's decrease in the price of dairy and related goods

0 percent: January's change in the price of cereals and bakery products
