Businesses in N.E. say lenders too strict

Terms toughen, despite flow of federal funds

By Ross Kerber
Globe Staff / February 21, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

Even as the federal government pumps hundreds of billions of dollars into the nation's banks, many New England businesses say they are being squeezed by lenders who insist on tougher conditions or higher rates for their loans.

Obama warns about wasting stimulus cash. A2.

In Warren, R.I., shoe store owner Francis Jamiel said he recently tried to renegotiate terms on about $400,000 in loans and lines of credit with Citizens Bank, which had been lending to him for years. The bank, to his surprise, turned him down, and Jamiel got a similar response from other banks, including Sovereign Bancorp. "None of the banks would help us," he said. "I feel like I'm at the bottom of the food chain."

At Metropolitan Moving & Storage in Cambridge, co-owner Neal Mizner said his lender, a large bank he declined to identify, recently told him it would charge $1,000 to renew a line of credit he's had for four years. "The banks are obviously desperate for revenues and scraping them up however they can," Mizner said.

In Boston, commercial mortgage broker Peter Goedecke said major banks like Bank of America and Wells Fargo are pulling back sharply on credit for real estate projects, and "either not lending at all or only on terms so onerous as to not be palatable."

The terms of bank loans are a hot issue in Washington, as federal officials question whether investing $700 billion in taxpayer dollars into the country's largest financial institutions is helping the economy. Bank executives say they're doing their best to keep money flowing, while making conservative loans and caretaking their balance sheets.

Few banks will talk about loan terms in detail. Citizens, Sovereign, and Bank of America, the biggest lender in Massachusetts all declined to do so, or to talk about specific cases like Jamiel, the shoe store owner stuck with the original terms on his business's credit. But there is data showing that commercial lending is getting more restrictive, while at the same time, consumers face higher rates on credit cards and other debt. The Federal Reserve loan officer survey for January found that in the last quarter of 2008, even as companies pulled back on their spending plans and demand for commercial loans weakened, 65 percent of US banks tightened their lending terms to large and midsize companies. Also, a Treasury Department report released Tuesday found that among the country's 20 largest banks receiving public funds, median corporate loan portfolios fell a combined 1 percent.

Figures like those reinforce the common criticism that banks "aren't lending." At hearings in Congress last week, US Representative Michael E. Capuano, a Somerville Democrat, slammed leaders of the country's eight largest banks for not extending more credit after receiving so much capital from the US Treasury, and told them to "get our money out on the street."

In an interview, Capuano said his comment was based on a litany of gripes from constituents, including major developers and business executives, saying that lending terms are much tighter now. That suggests the government's purchases of bank stocks weren't the best way to improve lending, Capuano said. "If they haven't increased their lending, then what are we doing here?" he asked.

One of Capuano's targets was Bank of America, one of the largest recipients of taxpayer funds, $45 billion in all. At the hearing in Washington, Bank of America chief executive Kenneth D. Lewis indicated that the government's investment did boost lending. "We cannot tell you whether the next loan we make is funded by that $45 billion" or by other bank resources, "but the bottom line is that we are lending significantly more with that preferred stock investment than we would be without it," Lewis said.

And according to Bank of America spokeswoman Anne Pace, the bank's credit commitments to commercial and nonprofit organizations in Greater Boston rose 21 percent in 2008, driven by demand from healthcare, high-tech, and government clients.

Robert Smyth, chief of Massachusetts operations for Citizens Bank, the state's second-largest lender, made a similar point, saying his bank is still looking to make loans, but many companies are seeking less credit. "We're looking to support them, but every company is looking at cutting back their capital needs and expansion plans," Smyth said in an interview.

Citizens is owned by Royal Bank of Scotland PLC, which has received billions of dollars worth of support from the British government. The bank says it doesn't face restrictions on the use of this money in the United States.

In addition to the Treasury Department's direct investment in financial institutions, the Federal Reserve has been trying to increase the flow of credit by lowering the interest it charges large banks that borrow funds - effectively offering cheaper money to lenders. The rates are at a record low, but that alone has not freed up the flow of credit to businesses.

Some small business consultants say there's mixed evidence about whether tighter loan terms will hamper economic recovery. Bob Baker, president of the Smaller Business Association of New England in Waltham, which helps arrange loans for 700 members, each with annual sales up to $25 million, said the tougher conditions banks have placed on credit have not had a major impact.

"I haven't seen the terms affect a company's decision on whether they're going to go forward or not" with an acquisition or project, Baker said.

Grafton "Cap" Willey, managing director of CBIZ Tofias in Boston, an accounting firm serving small and midsize companies, noted that although banks are under political pressure to lend more, they also face regulatory pressure to lend less. Agencies like the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. are calling on banks to make more conservative loans. "They're getting hammered" from both sides, Willey said.

The result, he said, is that businesses are finding loans harder to come by. Several small business owners told Willey that recently, and for the first time, banks reviewed not only their businesses, but their personal credit scores while considering requests for commercial loans. Another client had a line of credit reduced to $300,000 from $500,000 by his bank. "There's definitely a credit crunch out there," Willey said.

Executives at Woburn drug maker Advanced Vision Research said they had to shop for a new lender last fall after their previous bank told them they would be charged an interest rate 3 percent higher than they were already paying if they wanted to renew a $3 million line of credit. They switched to Citizens, which didn't insist on the extra interest. "Banks aren't lending to anyone other than those with the most stellar credit," said Steven P. Rodman, head of Advanced Vision's outside accounting firm, who also advises about 200 other companies. "If the banks are making loans, it's with a lot more strings attached."

At telecommunications reseller Granite Telecommunications LLC in Quincy, named to Inc. magazine's list of fastest-growing private companies for the last two years, chief financial officer Ross Weintraub said that in order to get a $30 million line of credit from TD Bank, the company had to agree to an interest rate half a point higher than what it paid for a $15 million line of credit several years ago.

Granite had shopped around with other banks last summer, Weintraub said, but the topsy-turvy markets of the fall made the bank they already knew more attractive. Also, TD Bank, a unit of Canada's Toronto-Dominion Bank, was in good financial health itself.

"Even if someone had offered us a few basis points lower, it wouldn't matter," Weintraub said. "We felt that they were in a very strong position, and their outlook wasn't going to be hindered by toxic assets."
