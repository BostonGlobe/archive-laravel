On the hot seat

How a Westborough battery company attracted HP

(Wendy Maeda/Globe Staff)
March 22, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

Boston-Power Inc. of Westborough recently inked a deal with Hewlett-Packard to provide the computer giant with the Sonata, a quick-charging ecofriendly lithium-ion battery. Boston-Power chief executive Christina Lampe-Onnerud recently sat down with Globe reporter Erin Ailworth to talk about the company she founded, and how she went from working as a chorus director to pay for graduate school to wowing the head honchos at HP.

You did your postdoctorate work at MIT and have a PhD in inorganic chemistry and a bachelor's of science in chemistry and calculus from Uppsala University in Sweden. What drew you to the energy field in school?

I was intrigued by both water and energy because they were both such large questions - this was in the end of the '80s - so people were asking, "What is going to happen? Where are we?" Then I found this effervescent, absolutely delightful professor who headed up this group in lithium-ion power and - I will never forget this - I came in to his office and he said, "Are you applying to my group? Oh, how lovely. Welcome." And he was just so funny and nice and most of the other professors were like, "Oh stand over there and maybe I'll consider your application." And that education was paid for, in part, with money you made on patents. But you also had another job - something to do with being a lyric soprano?

I mean, grad school is tough. You need to make money somehow. So I taught at the local music school, and I became the chorus director for the City of Uppsala, which is 200,000 people. But I was young, and I connect with people, so the kids became really good, and the choruses became very large, and so we went on tour. So I took some of these choruses into international competitions and they won and they were young. You know, it's part of my leadership training

That training, in part, helped you to form Boston-Power a few years ago - a company that now employs 100 people here and 300 overseas. Can you talk about that decision?

I just felt in 2004, which is the year that I made my decision, maybe this is my time. I was very concerned about the direction the Earth was going. And I felt part of my contribution could be to deliver a product that actually differentiates [from other batteries] in performance and in "green." There's also a paradigm in my specific industry where batteries are a constant disappointment. So people have surrendered to the fact that batteries fade. So, if we could change that paradigm and batteries didn't fade, you could make a real impact. And now, four years later, we're pioneering this business. We're referred to as "technology disruptors." We're taken seriously. What a wonderful rep on the street that is.

So, I know the Sonata was built to produce less waste and use fewer, if any, toxic materials. But what makes this battery a "disruptor," as the HP guy called it?

A regular laptop today charges in two to three hours. So, with this battery, you can charge in 30 minutes to 80 percent capacity. And then it's green. The Nordic Ecolabel is going on the box.

What's the Nordic Ecolabel?

It's just very simple. It just says you have to have longevity of product and you have to commit to no toxicity. And you have to have CSR - Corporate Social Responsibility.

The "green" issue is something a lot of people are focusing on right now. Even the economic stimulus package has tons of money in it to promote environmentally-friendly technologies, including advanced batteries. What does that mean for Boston-Power?

For sure, we'll be a part of the new economy. How the stimulus package [will help] and how we are able to have a voice or be able to showcase [ourselves], that is going to be tricky.
