Auto sales plunge in February

US auto sales fell to their lowest point since 1981. General Motors saw the biggest decline, with sales dropping 53 percent. (Bradley C. Bower/Bloomberg News)
Bloomberg News / March 4, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

SOUTHFIELD, Mich. - US auto sales in February slid to the lowest rate since December 1981, led by a 53 percent plunge for General Motors Corp. as the recession scared off customers.

Cars and light trucks sold at a 9.1 million annual pace last month, a drop from 15.4 million a year earlier, according to Autodata Corp. Total deliveries tumbled 41 percent to 688,909, the company said.

Sales at the February level make it more challenging for Detroit-based GM and Chrysler LLC to become profitable and pay back $17.4 billion in US government loans. President Obama's auto task force may approve as much as $21.6 billion more aid for the two companies and support for the thousands of companies that supply automakers with parts.

"It's going to get worse before it gets better," said Joe Barker, an analyst at consulting firm CSM Worldwide Inc. in Michigan. "We do anticipate the sales rate to bottom this quarter. That's not to say that we're anticipating the second quarter to be any sort of real rebound, only slightly better than the first quarter."

Sales tumbled 48 percent for Ford Motor Co., 44 percent for Chrysler, 40 percent for Toyota Motor Corp., 38 percent for Honda Motor Co., and 37 percent for Nissan Motor Co. GM said it plans to build 34 percent fewer vehicles in North America next quarter, and Ford reported a 38 percent reduction from a year earlier.

The February US decline for Toyota, the world's largest automaker after passing GM last year, was its biggest ever. Toyota, forecasting its first loss in 59 years, may ask Japan's government for $2 billion in loans for its credit unit as private financing has become too expensive, public broadcaster NHK reported.

"At this point, it's not as much credit as it is a consumer confidence issue," Al Castignetti, Nissan's vice president of US sales, said. "Even if you can afford the loan, people are not willing to take on the risk right now because of concern about jobs."

Annual US sales of cars and light trucks averaged more than 16 million this decade. In 2008, they totaled 13.2 million, a 16-year low.

"Until we get the housing market corrected, confidence back on Wall Street, and the credit flowing once again I'm afraid we're going to be stuck" near the 9 million annualized rate, CSM's Barker said.

GM's February sales of cars and light trucks fell to 126,170 from 268,737 a year earlier, the automaker said. That included declines of 69 percent for Hummer, 59 percent for Saab, and 57 percent for Saturn, three units the company is seeking to shed.

At Ford, sales of cars and light trucks dropped to 99,050 from 192,178. The results were hurt by a 55 percent decline, to 23,614, for its F-Series pickup trucks. Sales for the Volvo unit, which Ford wants to sell, were also down 55 percent.

Toyota reported a decline to 109,583 from 182,169. The company said sales fell 60 percent for its Tundra large pickup and 51 percent for the Yaris small car.

Honda said deliveries dropped to 71,575 from 115,397, and Nissan said its sales were 54,249, down from 86,219. Chrysler reported a decline to 84,050 from 150,093.

Volkswagen AG said February sales for its namesake brand fell 18 percent to 13,660. Daimler AG said sales of its Mercedes-Benz and Smart vehicles slid 21 percent to 15,614.

Hyundai Motor Co. said it sold 30,621 vehicles last month, 1.5 percent fewer than a year earlier. In January, Seoul-based Hyundai was the sole major automaker to post a gain in US sales.
