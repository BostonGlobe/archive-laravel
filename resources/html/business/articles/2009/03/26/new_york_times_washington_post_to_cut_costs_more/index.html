New York Times, Washington Post to cut costs more

March 26, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

NEW YORK—The New York Times Co. and the Washington Post Co. on Thursday became the latest newspaper publishers to signal the industry's deepening distress, separately announcing new rounds of cost cuts.

The austerity measures, involving a mix of pay cuts, employee buyouts and layoffs, are being driven by a devastating drop in advertising revenue that began in 2007 and now doesn't seem likely to ease until autumn at the earliest.

"We are going through a permanent downsizing of the industry," said newspaper analyst Ken Doctor of Outsell Inc. "Newspapers' cost structure will no longer support the kinds of newsgathering activities that their readers have become accustomed to. Even when there is a recovery, they will not go back to pre-recession staffing levels."

At the very least, The New York Times doesn't expect a turnaround this year, according to a memo from Chairman Arthur Sulzberger Jr. and Chief Executive Janet Robinson.

To cushion the blow, the company plans to at least temporarily reduce the pay of most employees by 5 percent from April through December. In return, the affected employees will get 10 additional days off. About 100 layoffs are also planned in the business operations of the Times newspaper, which employ about 2,000 people.

The pay cut is mandatory for nonunion employees at corporate headquarters, The New York Times and the company's other major daily newspaper, The Boston Globe. Workers represented by labor unions will be asked to accept the pay cuts to avoid further layoffs.

Phone messages left with labor leaders representing the affected Times workers weren't immediately returned Thursday.

Workers employed in other divisions of the Times company, including About.com and the smaller newspapers, will suffer a 2.5 percent drop in pay during the final nine months of the year and get five additional days off.

"This was a very difficult decision to make," Sulzberger and Robinson wrote. "The environment we are in is the toughest we have seen in our years in business."

They promised to restore workers' salaries next year if conditions improve.

Faced with a 13 percent drop in ad revenue last year, the Times company had already been wringing out expenses in a series of moves that has trimmed nearly 900 workers from its payroll in 2008. The cuts included about 100 jobs in the newsroom of the Times paper, which still employs one of the world's largest groups of reporters, editors and photographers.

Meanwhile, the Washington Post Co. is seeking a fourth round of buyouts since 2003 at its flagship newspaper. In a Thursday interview, Washington Post Publisher Katharine Weymouth declined to specify how many jobs the newspaper hopes to eliminate, but she singled out a Springfield, Virginia, production unit in a staff memo.

The Post also will offer buyouts to employees in circulation, the newsroom, advertising and technology. If the buyouts don't attract enough volunteers, Weymouth said the newspaper may resort to layoffs.

Although its newspaper is suffering, the Post Co. is holding up better than most of its brethren because it collects more than half of its revenue from an educational division, Kaplan, that doesn't depend on advertising revenue.

Virtually all newspapers without a financial crutch are hobbling badly.

In other moves outlined Thursday, The Atlanta Journal-Constitution will trim is news staff by about 30 percent, or 90 jobs, and The Bakersfield Californian will shed 26 employees to reduce its work force 12 percent.

Four newspaper publishers, including the owners of the Los Angeles Times, Chicago Tribune, Philadelphia Inquirer and Minneapolis Star-Tribune, have filed for bankruptcy protection within the past four months.

Two large dailies, the Rocky Mountain News in Denver and Seattle Post-Intelligencer, have stopped printing because of mounting losses.

McClatchy Co., the owner of 30 daily newspapers, has eliminated about 4,150 jobs since June, including 1,600 layoffs announced this month. Gannett Co., the largest U.S. publisher, is requiring most of its 41,500 workers to take at least one week of unpaid leave for the second time already this year. The sacrifice translates into a 4 percent pay cut.

The cutbacks by The New York Times and Washington Post are especially telling indications of the newspaper industry's financial decay. Although they're publicly owned, the companies are controlled by families that have traditionally left their newsrooms largely untouched during hard times.

But Doctor said the worst recession since the 1980s, coupled with the torrent of ad revenue shifting to the Internet, seems to have brought both The New York Times and Washington Post to "the crushing realizations" that their businesses are unlikely to see the revenue they once did, even when the economy rebounds.
