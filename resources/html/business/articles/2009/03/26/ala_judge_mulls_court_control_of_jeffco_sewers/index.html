Ala. judge mulls court control of Jeffco sewers

By Jay Reeves
Associated Press Writer / March 26, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

BIRMINGHAM, Ala.—Bond insurers urged a federal judge Thursday to intervene in Jefferson County's massive sewer debt crisis, contending that a toxic mix of corruption and incompetence has pushed it near financial ruin.

Henry Simpson, a lawyer for bond insurers who already have made millions in bond payments the county couldn't afford, asked the judge to name a manager to assume operation of the sewage system in Alabama's most populous county.

Simpson called the $3.9 billion debt debacle -- which has brought the county to the brink of filing the largest municipal bankruptcy in U.S. history -- one of the worst cases of mismanagement in modern times in the United States.

"There could be no greater manifestation of ineptitude, incompetence and corruption ...," Simpson said.

An attorney for the county, Joe Mays, said Wall Street financiers, not local elected leaders, were to blame.

The county's debt payments went up last year because bond insurers' credit ratings fell during the credit crunch, Mays said, and those same insurers are now blaming county officials who came up short of cash to pay the bill.

U.S. District Judge David Proctor didn't immediately rule during a hearing on the request by bond insurers to appoint a receiver to operate the county's sewer system.

The Bank of New York Mellon, the county's bond trustee, filed court papers seeking a receiver along with two companies that insured Jefferson County's debt, Syncora Guarantee Inc. and Financial Guaranty Insurance Co.

County leaders have been at odds for more than a year trying to avoid filing a nearly $4 billion bankruptcy that would eclipse the $1.7 billion case filed by Orange County, Calif., in 1994 as the largest municipal bankruptcy ever.

Jefferson County's debt, which stood at $3.2 billion last year, ballooned earlier this month when JPMorgan Chase & Co. canceled swap contracts with the county worth some $700 million.

With about 660,000 residents and the state's biggest city, Birmingham, Jefferson County got into trouble paying for a massive renovation to bring its sewer system into compliance with federal clean water standards.

Simpson told the judge that Jefferson County has proven it is incapable of operating the system, recounting a string of 21 corruption convictions, bad decisions and waste linked to years of work on sewers.

"The blame may lie on many people who are not here, but we are concerned with the corporate body," he said.

The mayor of Birmingham, Larry Langford, is currently awaiting trial on federal bribery charges linked to the sewer debt and his time on the Jefferson County Commission.

Langford is accused of taking bribes in exchange for telling Wall Street firms they had to do business with a friend's investment banking company if they wanted to handle the county's bond work.

Sewer bills have gone up more than 300 percent since the renovation work began in 1997, with the average customer now paying about $65 a month. Commissioners have balked at more rate hikes or filing bankruptcy as possible solutions.

Alabama Gov. Bob Riley has asked the Obama administration to provide a form of bond insurance so the county could refinance its debt, but the White House has not responded.
