Recovery mirage

By Steven Syre
Globe Columnist / April 21, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

Bank of America Corp. posted quarterly profits that blew away even the most optimistic forecasts on Wall Street early yesterday morning.

By the end of the day, Bank of America shares had plunged nearly 25 percent. The bank stock sunk $2.58 to $8.02 per share. Cancel the parade.

Bank of America had joined a growing line of financial companies that includes Citigroup Inc., JPMorgan Chase & Co., Goldman Sachs Group Inc., and Wells Fargo & Co., all crowing about soaring profits they earned during the first three months of the year.

But those billions of dollars of profits are less impressive when viewed at close range. They are real enough, for the most part, but rely heavily on temporary windfalls and new accounting advantages. They don't signal a banking industry in recovery. They mitigate the damage to a system that appears to have been in accelerated decline during the first three months of the year.

That is important to the entire stock market, not just bank shares. Investors know a stabilized banking system in slow recovery is important to getting the economy back on its feet and restoring business prospects for everyone. It was no coincidence that the recent stock market boom started shortly after an internal memo describing a February profit at Citigroup popped up in public.

Likewise, the broad stock market benchmarks tumbled hard once investors turned thumbs down on the Bank of America news yesterday. The Dow Jones industrial average sunk 289.60 to 7,841.73, a decline of 3.6 percent. Among the stocks that make up the Standard & Poors 500 index, 24 fell for every one that gained ground.

That's not the kind of reaction you would expect when a company like Bank of America reports a quarterly profit of $4.2 billion in the middle of a violent recession. For the record, it's earnings of 44 cents per share ran circles around Wall Street's consensus forecast for a profit of 4 cents per share.

But Bank of America's earnings included several special elements, including a gain of $1.9 billion from the sale of China Construction Bank shares it unloaded during the quarter. Another booster: A $2.2 billion gain from a change in the value of structured notes held by its Merrill Lynch unit. The company's global markets business, not commercial banking, generated most of the operating profits with a gain of $2.4 billion.

Wall Street was a source of huge quarterly profits among some other leading banks. Goldman Sachs made bushels of money trading in fixed-income markets, thanks to huge spreads between the prices paid by buyers and those offered to sellers. JPMorgan Chase also made big money by jumping into the middle of that highly profitable but temporary circumstance.

Like other big institutions that made handsome profits in the first quarter, Bank of America rode a booming residential mortgage business. But the majority of that business comes from customers refinancing their homes, taking advantage of historically low mortgage rates engineered by the Federal Reserve. With many mortgage companies out of the picture, home loans are particularly lucrative for banks, but there is a limited supply of refinance customers.

Meanwhile, credit losses have increased at large banks and reserves for future losses continue to build. At Bank of America, non-performing assets grew by 40 percent in three months. Problems with its consumer mortgages and commercial real estate business grew larger. The bank's credit card operation set aside more than $8 billion to offset losses, nearly twice as much as it provisioned the previous year.

Bank of America chief executive Ken Lewis said credit problems are serious and likely to grow worse in the near future. Lewis said his bank's reserves against loan losses would also grow over the short term.

That caution was consistent with the recent messages of other leading bankers. But the weight of the Bank of America news managed to drag down the price of all the big bank stocks yesterday. Those shares are sensitive to the chance that more bad business news may lead the federal government to inject more money into companies or convert previous investments into common shares, which would dilute the ownership interests of private stockholders.

The message to the market was clear. The bank recovery investors desperately seek is still further off on the horizon.

Steven Syre is a Globe columnist. He can be reached at syre@globe.com.
