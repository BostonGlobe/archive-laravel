For some firms, more caution, more work

By Jenn Abelson
Globe Staff / April 29, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Reprints|
  • |
Text size – +

Face masks were flying off the shelves so fast that Joe Blumenfeld had to make five stops at different pharmacies and shops before finding them yesterday at a Wal Mart in Framingham.

"I literally followed a man who was professionally dressed, making a beeline for the first aid section. We both stocked up on masks," said Blumenfeld, of Natick. "On the off chance that my wife and kids would need face masks, I wanted to make sure that I had them - struck me as a worthwhile investment of less than $10."

Escalating fears over the swine flu outbreak have consumers and businesses across the state scrambling to take precautionary measures as the death toll rose in Mexico and more cases of the illness were reported yesterday in the United States.

Pharmacy chains Walgreens and CVS are reporting a spike in sales for items such as hand sanitizer, antibacterial soap, masks, and gloves, as well as an increase in Tamiflu prescriptions.

"We are working with our wholesaler partners to supply us with Tamiflu in markets where there is increased demand," said Mike DeAngelis, a spokesman for CVS, which is based in Woonsocket, R.I.

Brian Houghton, vice president of the Massachusetts Food Association, which represents the grocery industry, said supermarkets with pharmacies are trying to increase supplies of Tamiflu, disposable respirators, and antibacterial soap.

"They don't want to be behind the 8 ball," Houghton said.

The Shaw's supermarket chain has convened an internal working group to closely monitor the swine flu developments and "take appropriate action as needed," said Judy Chong, a Shaw's spokeswoman.

Some manufacturers of cleaning supplies are also springing into action. The Clorox Co. yesterday said it has increased bleach production to meet growing demand for disinfecting products and has assembled a team of employees from distribution to manufacturing to oversee flu-related efforts, according to Dan Staublin, a Clorox spokesman. The company is restricting all nonessential travel to and from Mexico.

Some airlines are also taking extra precautions, advising crew and passengers on ways to avoid the flu.

"We've gone above and beyond our normal cleaning procedures and have given our crews rubber gloves and hand sanitizers," said Valerie Wunder, a spokeswoman for US Airways. The airline has taken these steps for all flights from Mexico to its three hubs and to Boston. "We're paying special attention to high traffic areas like the bathrooms and using hospital-approved disinfectant."

But some businesses are taking a wait-and-see approach to the crisis.

Epigmenio Guzman, co-owner of Tu Y Yo in Somerville, said it's too soon to tell whether customers will shy away from his restaurant just because it serves Mexican cuisine.

"To be honest, I don't know. We have to wait at least one more week," Guzman said. "The products we have are from California and New York. All the meat is from the United States. We have to very carefully wash our hands, very carefully prepare our meals."

Globe staff Hiawatha Bray contributed to this story. Jenn Abelson can be reached at abelson@globe.com.
