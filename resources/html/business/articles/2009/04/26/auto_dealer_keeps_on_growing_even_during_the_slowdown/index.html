On the Hot Seat

Auto dealer keeps on growing even during the slowdown

(Aram Boghosian for The Boston Globe)
By Clifford Atiyeh
Globe Correspondent / April 26, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

As car dealers across the country face bankruptcies, closings, and sharp blows to new model sales - a 37 percent decline in March year over year - dealer magnate Herb Chambers is walking into his 45th showroom in Westborough. The $6 million smell of fresh carpet and shiny wood paneling lingers among Infiniti luxury cars, one of 27 brands Chambers sells across Massachusetts and Rhode Island. He sat down with Globe reporter Clifford Atiyeh to talk about his 20 percent year-over-year drop in auto sales and how automakers and the economy are affecting his expansion plans and 1,700 employees.

How have you weathered the recession and the drop in car sales? How many layoffs and cuts have you had to make?
We suspected that the car business was going to get difficult and the economy was going to get difficult about August or September of last year. And at that point, we had some layoffs - I'm going to say we had probably 5 percent of our company.

And every company has what I'll call fat. They have an excess amount of employees. That is something that's been going on for probably 50 years, and people don't get fired; it just becomes a lackadaisical business. And when the economy gets tough, all of a sudden you say, wait a minute, we're going to have a cut and you take out these bottom performers. And the truth of the matter is when you do that, you don't miss anything. The company still runs fine. We never wanted to get into a situation where we did anything that would disrupt customer service.

Has customer financing been affected?
In some of the franchises, a little bit. And that's probably contributed to that 20 percent drop in business, but most of the brands that we have, they've got good financing through the manufacturer. There are other brands that I don't want to mention their name, but some of what we'd call second- or third-tier franchises - Japanese franchises, or Korean franchises - that do not have good in-house financing. And those dealerships are more challenged.

If bankruptcy is the only option for General Motors and Chrysler, what will this mean for your dealers?
I really don't think it would impact those dealerships much at all. And the reason I say that is because people don't understand what bankruptcy is. It's not like if the companies went bankrupt, that they're going to disappear; it's more of a legal, financial maneuvering than it is the disappearing of the company. If somebody thought they're going to go in and blow up GM, and say there will no longer be a General Motors, there will no longer be a Chrysler, then [people] would worry about that. But that's not the case, and service is going to get done on these cars, and I think the people understand that.

You're saying GM is probably not going to go away, but we know Saturn will by 2011, and then you have cutbacks at Pontiac and the sales of Saab and Hummer. What is that going to do?
We have never really been a big Hummer dealer, and obviously nobody else has either. That's why the franchise is probably going to go away. They were trying to sell it to somebody; I don't see that as being a buyable product today.

There's a tremendously loyal following with Saab, but it's primarily in the Northeast part of the country, and especially in Boston. Boston is like one of the biggest markets for Saab that there is in the country.

If you look at Pontiac, Buick, GMC, I think there's so many redundant brands there. Do you really need a GMC, and Chevrolet trucks, too?

Now, what's going to happen to [the] Saturn franchise? I think that one of these Chinese companies or someone who is not marketing in the US at this time would come in and buy the Saturn brand, and . . . they would have an in-place dealer network of great dealers, new facilities, and put in the right locations. It would be a quick turnkey deal.

Which of your brands are performing the best - and worst?
I would say that they're all doing pretty much what they're doing before, but they've come down some.

We have seen a drop, for example, in Vespa . . . in Honda Fits, or Smart cars. When gasoline a year ago was $4 a gallon, everybody wanted the Vespa motor scooter, Minis, anything that got exceptionally good gas mileage.

Last summer, were any of your dealers charging above sticker for hybrids?
I don't know that they did. Would we? We may. There are certain cars that we have charged over sticker for, which I don't feel really bad about. The only reason that somebody will pay over sticker is because they can't get one cheaper someplace else.

My feeling about that is that people come in and they don't want to pay sticker for a car, and 99 percent of the cars that I sell, we have to negotiate off of the sticker. On certain cars, we actually lose money.

If The Boston Globe, including Boston.com, were further weakened or failed to exist, what do you think that would do to your business?
We used to be a much larger advertiser with the Globe than we are today. Everybody is into the Internet, and Boston.com is a really big deal, and we're big on Boston.com. The thought of there not being a Boston Globe newspaper makes me sick.
