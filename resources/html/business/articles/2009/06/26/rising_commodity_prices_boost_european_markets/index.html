European, US markets fall on savings rate data

By Louise Watt
Associated Press Writer / June 26, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • |
Text size – +

LONDON—European stocks lost earlier gains Friday, trailing Wall Street lower after data showed U.S. consumers are spending less than they're saving.

In afternoon European trading, Britain's FTSE 100 was down 0.3 percent to 4,239.57, Germany's DAX lost 0.4 percent to 4,782.67 and France's CAC 40 fell 0.7 percent to 3,141.49.

In early trading in New York, the Dow Jones industrial average fell 0.3 percent to 8,446.25 and the broader Standard & Poor's 500 index was 0.4 percent lower at 916.95.

Stocks slipped after the U.S. Commerce Department reported that personal spending, incomes and savings all rose in May. What appeared to trouble investors, though, was that the savings rate soared to 6.9 percent, a 15-year high, while spending rose by only 0.3 percent.

Phil Orlando, chief equity market strategist at Federated Investors, said he expects the savings rate to eventually hit 10 percent before it eases. The savings rate had been 5.6 percent in April, and annual savings rates were below 1 percent from 2005 through 2007.

"If people ramp up savings that aggressively, that is going to result in less GDP recovery than ordinarily would be the case," Orlando said.

But the spending and savings data was not cause for gloom, according to Stephen Pope, chief global market strategist at Cantor Fitzgerald.

"The numbers from the U.S. are excellent and even though saving is increasing, the spending came as expected," said Pope. "Income was good, and if we continue to see a slowing in the month to month changes in the nonfarm payroll and confidence continues to pick up, I see the economic prospects for the U.S. looking good in (the second half)."

The European markets had been up around 1 percent earlier and Asian stocks closed higher, helped by rising commodity prices and Thursday's performance on Wall Street. Strong corporate profits buttressed optimism about the U.S. economy, pushing the Dow up nearly 173 points, or 2.1 percent -- the largest daily gain for the index since June 1.

In Europe, a rise in metal prices helped mining stocks such as XStrata and Antofagasta.

But energy stocks reversed earlier gains as crude oil prices slipped to below $70 a barrel. Benchmark crude for August delivery was down 33 cents to $69.90 in European trading. On Thursday, crude gained $1.56.

Among other losers, UBS AG slipped 2.7 percent after the Swiss bank announced overnight that it expects to raise 3.8 billion Swiss francs ($3.5 billion) through a capital increase and forecasts a loss for the second quarter.

Earlier, in Asia, investors were encouraged after U.S. markets on Thursday snapped a four-day losing streak with the help of better-than-expected earnings from homebuilder Lennar Corp. and home furnishings chains Bed Bath & Beyond Inc.

Despite the gains, some analysts don't expect the rally that began in March and tapered off this month to resume anytime soon.

"I think many investors are waiting for more confirmation that the macro economies are recovering," said Winson Fong, managing director at SG Asset Management in Hong Kong, which oversees about $2 billion in equities in Asia. "Without the fundamentals it's hard to see us moving sharply in one direction or the other right now."

"We have seen the markets quite volatile -- they can't really make their minds up if they are going up or down," added Jane Coffey, head of equities at Royal London Asset Management. "We had a strong rally from the March lows and although we have continued to see reasonable forward-looking economic data ... the big question now is how strong is the recovery going to be when it starts."

In Asia, Japan's Nikkei 225 benchmark inched higher by 81.31 points, or 0.8 percent, to 9,877.39 and Hong Kong's Hang Seng added 325.23 points, or 1.8 percent, to 18,600.25.

Elsewhere, Korea's Kospi gained 0.1 percent, Australia's benchmark rose 1.2 percent and India's Sensex turned higher by 1.5 percent.

Shares of Bridgestone Corp. were among the day's biggest gainers in Tokyo, soaring 8.5 percent.

The tiremaker said it now expects a net loss of 46 billion yen ($479 million) for the January-June half, a big improvement from the 62 billion yen loss it had projected earlier. The company cited cost cuts and lower commodity prices for the upward revision.

------

AP business writers Madlen Read in New York and Jeremiah Marquez in Hong Kong contributed to this report.
