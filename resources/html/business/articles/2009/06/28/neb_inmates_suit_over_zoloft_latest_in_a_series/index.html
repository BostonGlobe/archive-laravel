Neb. inmate's suit over Zoloft latest in a series

By Josh Funk
Associated Press Writer / June 28, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • |
Text size – +

OMAHA, Neb.—A lawsuit by a Nebraska inmate serving time for his girlfriend's murder, blaming a widely prescribed antidepressant for influencing his behavior, echoes similar claims in other cases.

But none of the cases have prevailed against Zoloft's maker, Pfizer Inc., which says the drug is safe and effective.

Randall Robbins II, who pleaded guilty to second-degree murder in the 2002 strangulation of 17-year-old Brittany Eurek, argues that both Pfizer and his doctor should have known that Zoloft might have made him attempt suicide and commit murder.

He says the drug intensified his agitation, suicidal desires, hysterical behavior and hostility and diminished his self-control.

Those arguments are similar to claims made in a few other cases since 2004, when antidepressants gained new warning labels highlighting the risk of suicidal behavior in people under 18.

In 2007, the South Carolina Supreme Court upheld the conviction of Christopher Pittman, who is serving a 30-year sentence for killing his grandparents when he was 12. The court rejected his argument that he was involuntarily intoxicated by taking Zoloft and didn't know what he was doing when he killed his grandparents and burned down their home in 2001.

A year earlier, the North Dakota Supreme Court affirmed the conviction of a man who killed a 19-year-old woman with a shotgun and later tried to blame the 2003 slaying on the antidepressant drug. Zachary Schmidkunz is serving a 35-year prison term.

Zoloft is one of the most widely prescribed antidepressants in the United States. In 2004, the Food and Drug Administration ordered Zoloft and other antidepressants to carry "black box" warnings -- the government's strongest warning short of a ban -- about an increased risk of suicidal behavior in children.

It's not clear what Pfizer or Robbins' doctor would have known about those risks two years before the FDA action, when Eurek was killed.

At the time, authorities found Eurek not breathing and with red marks on her neck in Robbins' bedroom after he summoned help. He says in the lawsuit that he and Eurek started arguing while having sex because she suggested they have a second child.

Police found Robbins in the living room of the duplex he shared with his mother and sister, crying with a cloth over his head and saying, "arrest me, arrest me."

Robbins said in the lawsuit that he had been prescribed Zoloft several months earlier because of depression. While on the drug, he had previously attempted suicide, he said.

Pfizer spokesman Chris Loder said Zoloft has helped millions of people with depression and anxiety, and the company is monitoring available data on the drug to determine if there is any sign of increased behavioral risk.

"Pfizer's evaluation of Zoloft data never has revealed any signal of an increased risk of violence related to either use or discontinuation of use of Zoloft," Loder said.

The FDA also says the underlying mental illnesses that antidepressants are used to treat are the most important causes of suicidal thoughts and actions.

Since the FDA added the stronger warnings, other studies have concluded that the benefits of treatment trump the small risk of increasing some patients' chances of having suicidal thoughts and behaviors. The later studies showed a smaller risk of suicidal behavior than the FDA found.

Robbins, who is acting as his own attorney, is asking for $2 million each from Pfizer and Dr. Richard Wurtz of Lincoln for emotional distress and physical pain, and $1 million from each for every year he spends in prison, according to his lawsuit. He is serving a 20-to-40-year sentence and will be eligible for parole in 2022.

A spokeswoman for Dr. Wurtz declined to comment on the lawsuit, saying his office had not seen it and medical privacy laws might keep them from commenting on a former patient's care.

Robbins filed his lawsuit himself on Tuesday and asked for a lawyer to be appointed to help.

Lancaster County District Judge Jeffre Cheuvront refused that request and ordered Robbins to pay all the fees and costs associated with the case. Robbins must either appeal the ruling within 30 days or use some of his $90-$100 monthly prison income to pay the $79 filing fee.
