To repay US, banks must face markets

Fed says ability to sell shares is a prerequisite

By Craig Torres
Bloomberg News / June 2, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Reprints|
  • |
Text size – +

WASHINGTON - The 19 largest US banks must demonstrate they can sell new shares before they are allowed to repay the government, the Federal Reserve Board said yesterday.

The companies "must successfully demonstrate access to public equity markets," the Fed said. They also need to sell debt without a Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. guarantee and reduce reliance on "government capital" and the FDIC's program.

The conditions reflect an attempt to ensure the largest lenders are strong enough to stand on their own before retiring the government's stakes.

Wall Street firms that took taxpayer assistance in the depth of the crisis last year are now seeking an exit from the Troubled Asset Relief Program - after lawmakers sought conditions, including limits on executive pay.

"Most companies will be able to raise equity in the market," said Paul Miller, an analyst at FBR Capital Markets in Arlington, Va. "I don't think anybody has failed that test per se of the big banks."

JPMorgan Chase & Co., the sole major bank lobbying to repay TARP that has yet to issue new equity this year, said it will raise $5 billion in common equity to satisfy regulators.

JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs Group Inc., and Morgan Stanley have applied to refund a combined $45 billion of government investment, a step that would mark the biggest reimbursement to taxpayers since the $700 billion bank bailout began in October.

An announcement on the "initial set" of redemption approvals will come next week, the central bank said yesterday. Applications to repay TARP money will be evaluated "periodically thereafter."

The 19 largest banks were subjected to regulators' stress tests, and nine of them were judged on May 7 to have no need for additional capital.

Ten were deemed to require a total of $74.6 billion in additional reserves to guard against the risk of a more severe economic downturn.

The list of 10 included Bank of America Corp., with a shortfall of $33.9 billion; Citigroup Inc., $5.5 billion; and Wells Fargo & Co., $13.7 billion.

They were given one month to produce a plan to fill the shortfalls, and six months to execute their proposals.

Since the release of the stress test results, Bank of America, Fifth Third Bancorp, Morgan Stanley, and others with a capital-buffer requirement have announced plans or already raised about 40 percent of the equity for the $74.6 billion, according to data compiled by Bloomberg News on May 18.

The KBW Bank Index has doubled since a March 6 low on signs of possible recovery in the US economy. Still, Fed officials forecast that unemployment will linger at just above 9 percent through the end of next year, showing little improvement from the April rate of 8.9 percent.

The nine banks that have no capital-buffer requirement include Boston-based State Street Corp.

Also on the list are American Express Co., BB&T Corp., Bank of New York Mellon Corp., Capital One Financial Corp., Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, MetLife Inc., and U.S. Bancorp.

These firms have announced or raised a combined $23.3 billion through debt or equity sales to help fund TARP repayments, according to data compiled by Bloomberg News on May 18.

Banks will have to demonstrate how they will meet funding requirements and obligations while reducing reliance on government capital and the debt guarantees, the Fed also said.
