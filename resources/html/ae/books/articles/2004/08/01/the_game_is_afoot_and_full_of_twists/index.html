ON AUDIO

The game is afoot, and full of twists

August 1, 2004

The Game

By Laurie R. King

Recorded Books, unabridged fiction, eight cassettes, 13 hours and 30 minutes, $34.99 (rental $18.50); read by Jenny Sterlin. Also available on 12 CDs; $39.99 (rental $18.50). For rental information, call 800-638-1304 or go to www.recordedbooks.com.

Just One Look

By Harlan Coban

Penguin Audio, unabridged fiction, seven cassettes, 11 hours, $39.95; read by Carrington MacDuffie.

The Statement

By Brian Moore

Audio Editions, unabridged fiction, six cassettes, six hours and 24 minutes, $29.95; read by Andrew Sachs. Also available on six cassettes, $29.95, or as a download from www.audible.com for $19.95.

Laurie R. King has won multiple awards for her novels about Mary Russell and her brilliant and eccentric husband, Sherlock Holmes. ''The Game," like all of her pastiches, has a clever plot and is evocative of a long-gone era (in this case, India in 1924). However, fans of the Baker Street sleuth should be forewarned that Holmes is becoming increasingly diminished in these tales.

The story begins on New Year's Day, when Mycroft, Holmes's older and sickly brother, sends them off an en errand for the Empire. It turns out that Kimball O'Hara, the grown-up hero of Rudyard Kipling's novel ''Kim" and a British spy, has vanished in India. Mycroft shows Holmes and Mary a package of Kim's documents that has the three convinced an international crisis may be brewing.

Holmes and Mary are soon off to India to find the missing agent in a complex and extremely intelligent adventure that is part travelogue and part sociological history. Once the game is afoot, this is great fun, including red herrings, complicated disguises, eccentric characters, and political intrigues. However, the pace is languid, so although the story is well crafted, this is not a mystery for those seeking fast-paced action.

Narrator Jenny Sterlin has a lush, resonant voice and the ability to capture the atmospheric details of this novel, including a variety of accents. Most are flawless, though Yanks may find her American accents the least convincing of her repertoire. But this is a small complaint, for Sterlin effortlessly conveys Mary's wry humor and British aplomb, even in the face of danger or, worse, rude fellow travelers.

In a departure from his Myron Bolitar mystery series, Harlan Coban's ''Just One Look" is a quick-moving whodunit in which a suburban housewife finds herself tracking down her missing husband, leaving her to wonder if she really knows the man she married years earlier.

During an ordinary errand on an ordinary day, Grace Lawson picks up some photos, only to discover an older picture -- at least 20 years old -- mixed in with pictures of her family. A man in this group shot looks like her husband, Jack, but he denies it when she shows him the photo. But he then drives off in the family minivan, and out of her life.

The police are useless, since there are no signs of abduction, so Grace sets out to uncover the truth on her own. Because she has an unusual back story, she also has unusual connections to the Mafia. As a young woman, Grace was involved in a stampede at a rock concert in which dozens were killed or injured. She survived but became a media darling while her struggle to live was publicized daily. This brought many grieving parents into her life, including a well-known Mafioso whose son she watched die.

As she gets closer to the truth, several suspicious characters turn up, and the past and present become a tangle of subplots. In fact, Coban depends far too much on coincidence. But, that said, he does cleverly tie everything up in the end.

Narrator Carrington MacDuffie relies on attitude more than accents, getting across the persona of a too-chipper nurse or a disgruntled bodyguard through superb acting. She changes her voice somewhat for minor characters, adding a little gravel or deepening the pitch.

''Just One Look" is no exception to the rule that mysteries, being dialogue- and character-driven, always work well on audio. Reading this is much more likely to cause one to focus on the tenuous lines holding the story together. But under MacDuffie's lead, the story flies along and absolutely fits the bill for escape during a grueling commute.

''The Statement" is a political-religious thriller following the tired last days of an elderly Nazi who has been hiding in France under the protection of a secret Catholic society.

Pierre Brossard, a 70-year-old Vichy collaborator, has been hiding in French monasteries for 40 years. He suddenly finds himself chased by both gendarmes and assassins, but always believing himself to be in the right for his actions during the war.

Brian Moore presents the story from various points of view, carefully shifting the focus among Brossard, the clerics protecting him, and his pursuers. Aside from being a finely crafted novel, ''The Statement" also delves into such moral dilemmas as racism, the nature of sin, the long arm of justice, and the true meaning of mercy. Moore maintains our suspense until and through an ending that is both stunning and chilling.

Though he has a deep, smooth British accent, narrator Andrew Sachs also brings a slightly sinister air to the characters. That smoothness, unfortunately, occasionally keeps him from conveying the tension inherent in the story. He comes to life when reading dialogue, picking up the pace and slightly changing voices for different characters. Overall this is well worth hearing, as Moore, who died in 1999, was as skillful a storyteller as Graham Greene.

Rochelle O'Gorman is publisher and editor in chief of audiobookcafe.com.
