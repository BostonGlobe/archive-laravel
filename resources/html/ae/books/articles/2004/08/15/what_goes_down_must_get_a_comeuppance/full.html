A READING LIFE

What goes down must get a comeuppance

August 15, 2004

''Sam Patch was a natural born jumper. All morning and afternoon he worked as a cotton spinner. During the lunch hour he found time to jump over four piles of old boxes, and on his way home he made a one-foot hop across a small fifteen-foot creek near the mill." So begins ''Sam Patch, the High, Wide and Handsome Jumper," by Arna Bontemps and Jack Conroy, published in 1951. I still possess my childhood copy of this book, inscribed to me, as it happens, by Bontemps himself. It is a tale of true American gallantry, derring-do, and kindness towards animals; and even now I occasionally read it to remind myself where I got my ideas.

According to Bontemps, a major figure in the Harlem Renaissance, and Conroy, a left-wing writer, Sam Patch was a good son to a poor widow. He joined a traveling show, ''Clary's Congress of Living Wonders," to challenge ''Hurricane Harry, the Kaskaskia Snapping Turtle," a bad egg, whose specialty was jumping from great heights into a tub of water. Soon Sam, touted as ''the Great Descender and Spurner of Heights," and the brutal Harry were pushing the stunt to new and awful extremes: with Sam jumping 215 feet into 2 feet of water, the Snapping Turtle coming back with 225 feet into 6 inches, and Sam preparing himself to jump from a dizzying height onto a wet bathmat . . . When, lo! out came the dirty tricks. Someone -- and I guess we know who -- had wrung out the bathmat. Well.

Suffice it to say that Sam's friends intervened; that Sam, himself, nobly returned good for evil; and that he finally took the stagecoach home to his mother in Paterson, N.J., arriving with ''money rattling in his pockets."

I have just read Paul E. Johnson's ''Sam Patch, the Famous Jumper" (Hill and Wang, paperback, $14), and, I must say, history records a somewhat different story from the one that so inspired me. The real Sam Patch was born in 1799 in North Reading and ended up, thanks to his father's knavery and bibulous improvidence, working as a mill hand in Pawtucket, R.I., at the age of 7. In time he ascended to the highly skilled position of mule spinner, along the way becoming a heavy drinker and an adept at ''falls jumping," which, in Pawtucket, meant leaping nearly a hundred feet down into the river beneath the falls that powered the mills. It was a sport much favored by mill-town youths, each town having its own style. The ''formal Pawtucket style," to which Sam adhered until the end, was to jump feet first, breathe in, stay underwater long enough to convince everyone you were dead, and then pop up in triumph.

This, by the way, is not the form described by Bontemps and Conroy, who have their hero flipping over and jackknifing headfirst into the water, ''as slick as an oiled eel in a tub of hot butter." Still, I see from Johnson's excellent book -- a triumph of archival research and its interpretation -- exactly why the two writers found in Sam Patch a congenial subject: The Great Descender's public leaps began as a form of protest and affirmation of the accomplishments of the common man. He jumped, as he put it, to show that ''somebody besides other folks can do something."

''Other folks" were the likes of Timothy Crane, a Paterson, N.J., mill owner who had bought a piece of forested land on the opposite bank of the Passaic River abutting the falls, a spot traditionally enjoyed by working people as a free, unregulated refuge from town. Crane transformed what had been a scene of nature rambles and family picnics -- as well as drinking, tree climbing, fishing, swimming, and other vulgar spectacles -- into a commercial pleasure ground for the better sort. To turn this wild setting into ''a place of rational amusement," Crane caused the land to be groomed and tidied with paths, and a magnificent bridge, a toll bridge, to be built. The bridge was put in place on Saturday, Sept. 30, 1827, a day of festivities. Sam Patch, now a spinner in a Paterson mill, showed up and declared that while ''Crane had done a great thing . . . he meant to do another." Whereupon he jumped the 70-foot drop into the river just as the bridge was being pulled onto cables to span the river. It ruined Crane's day.

Unfortunately, as Johnson points out, Sam's attitude had an unlovely element of personal resentment and plain begrudgery, for he, himself, had failed in business twice and was an out-and-out alcoholic. Be that as it may, his next few jumps were performance protests, gestures directed against the high-handed maneuvers and genteel accomplishments of the rich. Within months of his first, momentous jump, he became a professional showman, presenting himself as specialist in a workingman's art. ''Some things," he was fond of saying in his gnomic way, ''can be done as well as others."

His daring leaps from increasingly high and dangerous perches included two at Niagara Falls, the second a 120-foot drop. This kind of thing did not sit well with those of refined sensibilities in whose bosoms the terrific displays of nature -- Niagara being exemplary -- awoke a sense of the poetic sublime. Sam Patch and his stunts made a mockery of both art and nature, and of progress too. ''Rational, scientific, and even daring exploits for rational objects, may be encouraged," pronounced one newspaper, ''but what is there in a leap over a cataract of 90 feet? Danger and desperation."

True enough, but who doesn't feel like jumping off a cliff when confronted with the taming, homogenizing, market-oriented apparatus of modern life? Sam Patch's last jump, in Rochester, N.Y., on Nov. 6, 1829, at Genesee Falls, killed him. Drunk, he was unable to maintain the Pawtucket form, and that was that -- or as his wooden grave marker put it: ''Sam Patch. Such is Fame."
