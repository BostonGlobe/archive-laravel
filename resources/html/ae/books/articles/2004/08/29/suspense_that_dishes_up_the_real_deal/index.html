ON CRIME

Suspense that dishes up the real deal

By Hallie Ephron  |  August 29, 2004

Suspension of disbelief -- that's the essential, look-Ma-no-hands moment in a mystery/thriller when the reader realizes: This could really happen. Laura Lippman achieves it from the outset in "By a Spider's Thread."

Natalie Rubin, a knockout brunette who's a ringer for Natalie Wood, vanishes with her three children from their affluent Baltimore home. "I came home one day and they were gone," her husband, wealthy Orthodox Jewish furrier Mark Rubin, tells PI Tess Monaghan. He's convinced his family has been abducted or worse.

When Tess gently points out that everything about this disappearance suggests a planned, voluntary departure, he seems stunned. But he's by no means as clueless as he seems, Tess later discovers, when he grudgingly discloses information that he's either been unwilling to share or unable to admit even to himself.

I've long been a fan of Lippman's breezy style and compelling storytelling (I asked her to blurb one of my books). One of the pleasures of this eighth Tess Monaghan novel is the cast of characters that Lippman draws, neatly avoiding stereotypes. There's the rigidly religious, controlling, hard-as-nails businessman husband who's also a nurturing father with a gentle sense of humor. His wife is cold and passionate, self-obsessed and at the same time devoted to her children. And Isaac Rubin, a 9-year-old with grown-up smarts, on the run with his mother, works tenaciously from within while Tess and her network of women PI "Snoop Sisters" work from without to make this family whole again.

Lippman explores her heroine's dual heritage. On her father's side, Tess is Irish Catholic. Her mother's family are Jews who "went out for Chinese and held backyard crab feasts, rationalizing that anything eaten outside wasn't really treyf." The Orthodox Rubin makes her feel "100 percent goyish."

Artfully shifting point of view, Lippman keeps us dangling through a hair-raising ride. The final switchback is a gutsy, thoroughly believable choice.

A grittier realism pervades Chuck Hogan's "Prince of Thieves." Charlestown's Bunker Hill Monument pokes like a needle up through the middle of this multilayered, richly textured novel about loyalty and belonging. Down one side of the hill from the monument are townies and a hoodlum subculture that lives by a code of silence. Down the other side, affluent yuppies have settled, pricing local residents out of their childhood homes.

The battle lines are drawn, and two antiheroes -- townie Doug MacRay, the book's "prince of thieves," and FBI agent Adam Frawley -- square off. Between them is a woman, bank manager Claire Keesey, one of the new wave of yuppie Charlestown residents. Claire is taken hostage during a bank robbery that Doug and three buddies pull off. He lets her go, and then can't get her out of his mind. Adam is on a mission to track down Doug and put him and his gang behind bars. Adam questions Claire, and his interest in her quickly moves beyond professional.

As much literary novel as thriller, this book finds a place in my list of favorite books ever. Hogan excels at creating the over-the-top adrenaline rush of heists, heart-stopping chases, and gun battles, and gives us a blood-chilling portrayal of a wacked-out buddy you know is going to self-immolate before the story is out. At the same time, the novel is full of nuance, with richly drawn Charlestown settings and three-dimensional characters such as Claire, her life forever changed from that moment when she's pushed blindfolded from the getaway van and left on the beach with instructions: "Walk to the water until you can feel it on your toes. Then take off the blindfold."

The book explores the permeable boundaries between extremes -- humanity and depravity, love and hate, family and outsiders, victims and perpetrators. "I'm half cop half criminal," says Adam.

For fans of Dennis Lehane, this book will feel like coming home.

Some will find Tess Gerritsen's newest thriller, "Body Double," a bit too real. I confess, I kept my eyes half closed and skimmed graphic autopsy scenes. But there are rewards aplenty in this book, which I inhaled more than read.

Gerritsen gives a new twist to one of the oldest clichs in crime fiction -- the identical twin. Boston medical examiner Dr. Maura Isles, known in Boston's forensic circles as Queen of the Dead, returns from a trip to Paris to find police investigating a fatal shooting in front of her Brookline home. The victim, Anna Jessop, who has been shot in the head while parked in her car, is a dead ringer (pardon the pun) for Maura. Could this woman be her sister? wonders Maura, who was adopted as an infant and knows nothing about her birth parents.

More questions follow. What was Anna doing in front of Maura's home? Was Anna the intended victim, or was it a case of mistaken identity, with the killer really aiming for Maura? Maura's quest for answers takes her and her friend and colleague, nine-months-pregnant Detective Jane Rizzoli, on a journey to discover who this doppelganger is, and what brought her to a tragic end at Maura's doorstep.

In the first 70 pages, Gerritsen sets this and two other apparently unrelated plot strands spinning, and you're wondering how on earth they're all going to come together. Fear not; you're in the hands of a master plotter. "Body Double" is suspense at its best with surprises right up to the final pages.

Hallie Ephron is co-author of the Dr. Peter Zak series of psychological mystery thrillers by G. H. Ephron.
