A READING LIFE

Bygone seasons of bombast and buffoonery

By Katherin A. Powers  |  August 29, 2004

For sheer dreadfulness few events can match national political conventions. They were once so enjoyably grotesque, so much the peerless sideshow, that my parents, who abhorred television, procured a set that they might regale themselves with the spectacle of the Democratic and Republican conventions of 1956; and they did so again in 1960. Three years later, we all left the country, but returned for two months in 1968 to witness the storied contests of that summer. Today, however, thanks to what might be called enhanced production values, I wouldn't walk across the room to watch a political convention. Gone is the old carny spirit that made this nation great. Gone, too, are the flamboyant descriptive powers that once brought home the baroque awfulness of those events, exhibitions of bombast and buffoonery that H. L. Mencken extolled as being "as fascinating as a revival or a hanging." Try as I might, I cannot imagine anyone writing of the Republican National Convention, which begins tomorrow, in terms, however serviceable they might be, employed by Mencken in describing its predecessor of 80 years ago.

The event, he observed, promised to be regrettably dull as the incumbent, Calvin Coolidge, was a shoo-in for the nomination; still, it would not be "without its charms to connoisseurs of the obscene." "Here," he predicted, "will be assembled all the great heroes and masterminds of the majority party in the greatest free nation ever seen on earth, and the job before them will be the austere and solemn one of choosing the head of the state, the heir of Lincoln and Washington, the peer of Caesar and Charlemagne. And here, after three or four days of bombarding the welkin and calling upon God for help, they will choose unanimously a man whom they regard unanimously as a cheap and puerile fellow."

You can turn from the oleaginous products of your screen and fortify yourself with the likes of that and much more that would never adorn the pages of a respectable, family newspaper in "On Politics: A Carnival of Buncombe," a collection of the master's exuberant political reportage edited by Malcolm Moos (Johns Hopkins University, paperback, $19.95). It is Mencken's festive air and the unchained delight he takes in balderdash and bamboozle that are so lacking in today's political commentators, who are, to my taste anyway, unendurably euphemistic and solemn.

A fine appreciation for bad things done well informs Broadway Books' recently inaugurated Library of Larceny, a series of republished classics of con artistry and theft. According to its editor, Luc Sante, the series proposes "to restore property crime to its full, glorious stature. It is, after all, a portion of the inheritance of this Land of Opportunity." The series now runs to five books, of which the two latest are "Con Man: A Master Swindler's Own Story," by J. R. "Yellow Kid" Weil and W. T. Brannon (paperback, $14), and "The Telephone Booth Indian" (paperback, $12), a collection of 10 pieces written by A. J. Liebling.

Appearing in The New Yorker from 1936 to 1939, Liebling's essays celebrate the small-time showmen and scam artists -- or "promoters" -- who set up shop along the gaudy stretches of Broadway. The book's title comes from the characters whose offices are nothing more than the telephone booths in "the Jollity Building," a fictional composite of some dozen or so actual buildings that provide rental space of varying degrees of materiality to fly-by-night operators. This teeming ecosystem and its arcane arrangements are presided over by the building's renting agent, Morty Ormont, "whose expression," Liebling tells us, "has been compared, a little unfairly, to that of a dead robin."

Ormont -- whose name, we are told, is a translation effected by himself of "Goldberg" -- is a connoisseur of scams, and he reserves his highest praise for the man called Maxwell C. Bimberg. Also known as "Count de Pennies," Bimberg was fearless, and his stratagems for filleting other men's wallets were those of a genius.

As the consummate professional con man, he makes a point of giving no value for money. "After all," observes Ormont, "what is there smart about selling a guy a piece of something that might make money? Smart is to sell a guy for a good price a piece of a sure loser." Some ideas, as certain financial analysts could tell you, never lose their luster.

Liebling's infectious elation over the small and busy doings of New York's demimonde, his fascination with the linguistic tide pools of its esoteric rackets, and his generosity of spirit are so wonderful that I hesitate to mention that I'm not sure I trust him. I wish, for instance, that the Jollity Building were real and not a composite, and I wish that his characters weren't pseudonymous (as we are told they are). What are we to make of this "Ormont" name-change caper, to say nothing of "Count de Pennies," if there was no one called either? One feels that the pleasure Liebling took in his subjects' ingenious invention led him down the path of emulation.

And now, to return to our own euphemistic time: I don't see why Larry Beinhart settled for the names he did in his new novel, "The Librarian" (Nation, paperback, $15.95). The story revolves around a plot to steal the 2004 presidential election by a small group of people supporting the incumbent, Augustus Winthrop Scott, who achieved office in 2000 through some shady vote-counting maneuvers in Florida and the connivance of a malleable Supreme Court. Naming names does not seem to be too much to ask from this writer. Beinhart's 1993 novel, "American Hero," was a brilliant satire that began with a dying Lee Atwater gasping out that "George . . . is going to blow it" and leaving instructions for staging a well-produced war to ensure Bush's reelection. (This is the novel -- or rather the concept -- upon which the movie "Wag the Dog" was based, which, as luck would have it, was thought to be prescient of Clinton's difficulties and expedients.)

"The Librarian," though not a patch on "American Hero," is exciting and filled with devastating characterizations of media flaccidity. Moreover, its hero is a librarian, of which there are all too few in political action adventure. And it is funny -- which brings us back to Mencken. "Has the art and mystery of politics no apparent utility?" he asked. "Does it appear to be unqualifiedly ratty, raffish, sordid, obscene and low down, and its salient virtuosi a gang of unmitigated scoundrels? Then let us not forget . . . its incomparable services as a maker of entertainment."

Katherine A. Powers, a writer and critic, lives in Cambridge. Her column appears on alternate Sundays. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@earthlink.net.
