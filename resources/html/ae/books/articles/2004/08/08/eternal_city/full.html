Eternal city

Roman town, hub of empire, racial and religious kaleidoscope -- there will always be a London

By James Buchan  |  August 8, 2004

London: A History
By A. N. Wilson
Random House, 221 pp., $21.95

ADVERTISEMENT

London is not a city that captures the heart at once. It doesn't have the spectacular setting of San Francisco and Istanbul, or the architectural unity of St. Petersburg, or the thrilling modernity of Shanghai. Nor, like Rome and Isfahan, does it preserve in romantic disrepair the remains of a lost civilization.

A chaotic collection of engulfed villages, odd scraps of stranded countryside, defunct nationalities, restless immigrants, religions, motor traffic, and some shocking bad building, London will always seem to some visitors to cover just too much -- about 1,000 square miles -- of God's precious earth. Non-Londoner Britishers (who exist, they really do) resent the gluttony with which London sucks the capital and talent of the country within the fence of its hellish orbital highway, the notorious M25.

Yet the truth is London has made just so much history. No other town on earth, not even Paris or Jerusalem, has done as much as London to make the world as it is today. On the banks of the Thames, monarchs and priests were brought down to earth, democracy tried and tested, women and children emancipated, slaves freed. London was the backdrop and the inspiration for the greatest masters of writing in English, from Chaucer by way of Shakespeare to Dickens. Colonial trade, mercantilism, banking and finance, and imperialism as well as every sort of crime, perversion, and murder all have their London memorials in groups of buildings or the conjunction of streets.

"London: A History," by the British novelist and biographer A. N. Wilson, is a brief account of London's history since Roman legions crossed the Thames in AD 43. In a whir of short chapters, Wilson describes the Roman town (which is now the financial district, or "the City"), the building of a royal town upriver at Westminster, and the London of Shakespeare.

Reform-minded in religion but too fond of their privileges to be truly radical, the merchants of the City and the House of Commons won out over the absolutist tendencies of the Stuart kings in the 17th century. One king, Charles I, had his head cut off in 1649. The new liberties brought in train the expansion of commerce and the public debt with the founding of the Bank of England in 1694 and the great stock speculation of 1719-20 known as the South Sea Bubble.

As Britain grew to be a world power, so London turned into the greatest commercial and administrative city ever seen. London's population grew to a million people by 1800 and then doubled and doubled and doubled again to a peak of 8.7 million souls on the eve of World War II. With new immigrants from central Europe and the Mediterranean periphery, London is heading back up toward that number.

Wilson gives vivid little accounts of such great events as the Great Fire, which destroyed most of the city in 1666, and the anti-Roman Catholic Gordon riots of 1780 described in Dickens's "Barnaby Rudge." He devotes a fine chapter to the Blitz of World War II, when Londoners bedded down in underground railway stations while the German Air Force dropped thousands of tons of high explosive on the city.

Wilson does well to find new things to say and quote that are not in Peter Ackroyd's 800-page love letter to the city, "London: The Biography." In fact, Ackroyd, in his gorgeous parade of oddities and lowlifes, had little space for the ordinary hardworking Londoner, and Wilson shows a better grasp of how London made and makes its living.

The fault of "London: A History" is that Wilson sometimes forgets he is writing for North American readers. Nobody is more provincial than the Londoner, but Wilson's tirades against the mayor of London, the leftist Ken Livingstone, or the real estate speculators after World War II look out of scale against such wide historical horizons. Wilson forgets that modern London, as the oversize metropolitan remnant of a vanished world empire, was bound to have some trouble deciding what sort of town it was going to be.

He gives too much space to those who complain that there is something un-British or un-English about the racial, religious, and commercial kaleidoscope that is London. Tories have been saying that since at least the 17th century with absolutely no effect whatsoever on the fate of the city or the domestication of its immigrants. At one point, Wilson says that half the population of London "will in future be illiterate." Why he thinks that is a mystery. It doesn't sound very likely. As Wilson is the first to admit, London has been immeasurably enriched by such immigrants as the Protestant weavers of France in the 17th century or such Jewish refugees from National Socialism as the father of psychoanalysis, Sigmund Freud, or the art historian Ernst Gombrich.

Finally, in mourning the death of the manufacturing industry in Livingstone's "Silly London," Wilson forgets that London, like all capital cities, whether aristocratic or democratic, was always more a center of consumption than of production. Wilson's pessimism gives the book in its last chapters a dated and rather small-town air.

James Buchan's most recent book is "Crowded With Genius," a history of the Scottish city of Edinburgh.
