Golden trove or red herring?

By Antony Shugaar  |  September 5, 2004

Valverde's Gold: In Search of the Last Great Inca Treasure
By Mark Honigsbaum
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 348 pp., illustrated, $25

ADVERTISEMENT

A young Jules Verne once planned a daylong boat excursion in such detail that when it came time for the trip, he stayed home, bored.

In a similar but unrelated case, a pioneering New Journalist, Gay Talese, once wrote an article entitled ''Frank Sinatra Has a Cold." He tracked Sinatra from a distance, never obtained an interview, but still wrote a cover story for Esquire.

''Valverde's Gold," the account of one author's ambivalent pursuit of a vast Inca treasure, has more than a passing resemblance to each of these episodes. Not only does the writer-explorer, Mark Honigsbaum, never come close to finding the treasure, but he persistently questions its existence throughout the book. In the final episode, when the author finally makes a 20-day trek into mountains thought to conceal the treasure, he confesses that he is ''heartily sick" of those mountains, the maps, and the treasure hunters themselves.

It's like ''The Treasure of the Sierra Madre" crossed with a 12-step program. Honigsbaum spends as much time musing on the distorting effect that the myth of a treasure seems to have on those obsessed with it as he does spinning the tale of its origins and seekers. By the end, he begins to diagnose symptoms of the obsession in himself, and decides that he does not want to become a victim of the legend.

To be fair, the legend is a corker. In 1533, the conquistador Francisco Pizarro, holding the great Inca Atahualpa prisoner, decides to execute his hostage instead of waiting for the third and supposedly largest installment of the ransom, then arriving via llama train from the north. When the Inca's half brother Rumiahui learns of Atahualpa's execution, he orders the llama train to turn east into the mountains, and the immense treasure vanishes.

Although the Spaniards tortured and terrorized the Andean natives in their quest for this lost hoard, they learned nothing. One story runs that a group of Indians, taunting the conquistadors, removed a single kernel from a heap of maize, saying that the kernel was what the Spaniards had obtained, and the rest of the heap was the treasure they had lost.

Understandably, people have hunted for the trove ever since. In the Llanganati Mountains, where the gold was said to have been hidden, either in a cave or at the bottom of a man-made lake, servicing those on a treasure quest has become a local cottage industry.

So when Honigsbaum tells the story of his pursuit of the treasure, his first descriptions of the nearest scenery necessarily focus on those who started looking before him. And there are plenty of them, beginning with a ex-Mormon former detective, proceeding to a German geologist, an aging Ecuadorean mineral-water millionaire and muscleman, a Jesuit historian, a Scottish adventurer and alcoholic, and culminating in a legendary explorer and aviator. In time, the author's investigations creep further back into the past, yielding sailors, priests, Inca princesses, and naturalists, and Valverde himself, a perhaps fictional conquistador who is said to have married an Inca princess who brought access to the treasure as her dowry.

It's all a ripping yarn, as the English say (and Honigsbaum is English as can be, referring for instance to the ''central reservation" of a road when describing a median). But, because the author wants to lead us through his own experience of pursuing leads, it is also bafflingly dense. Since so much of it hangs on geographic detail -- does this mountain lie to the east or west of that dry lake? how far is this river from that gorge? -- it is maddening, absolutely maddening, that no usable map is included. When the author wonders, in one of his many bemused soliloquies, ''Why couldn't I be more like the blind Boston historian William Prescott . . . who described the scene without ever leaving home?" several tart responses came to this reader's mind.

Adding to the confusion is either sloppy exposition or, perhaps, a desire to make the tangled treasure trail as confusing as possible (to heighten the ''you are there" effect). One disconcerting detail is that, when Honigsbaum calculates the size of the treasure, a crucial element in evaluating plausibility, it becomes apparent that he believes there are 1,000 pounds to a ton (2,000, last I checked).

In a sense, ''Valverde's Gold" is a tale of disillusionment. Honigsbaum first learns about the treasure while doing research on his previous book, ''The Fever Trail: In Search of the Cure for Malaria." He quotes Robert Louis Stevenson on the importance of treasure hunts to a child's imagination. He plunges into the quest enthusiastically. At each roadblock, however, he grows a little more frustrated, a little more bitter, until he suddenly glimpses the immense wild goose chase on which he has embarked -- a realization not unlike the closing scene of ''Raiders of the Lost Ark," in which we see a treasure buried so deep that there is no hope of finding it.

The problem is that Honigsbaum was determined to salvage something from the ruins. And what he saves may glitter, but it doesn't really hold up. Six full pages are devoted to him typing at a library keyboard in the Archive of the Indies in Seville, Spain. The information he recovers fits in a paragraph, but (he seems to be thinking) what a great scene for a movie! Questions are constantly racing through his mind, and he poses them in a breathy, confidential monologue. It's a shame, because here and there, like the moon piercing the Ecuadorean neblina, or ground-hugging fog, there are glimpses of the pure, bracing thrill of the quest for lost treasure.

Antony Shugaar is the co-author of ''Latitude Zero: Tales of the Equator."
