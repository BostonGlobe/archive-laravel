City on a hill

Finding innovation and contradiction, writers chart Boston's neighborhoods, people, and purpose

By Dan Wakefield  |  September 5, 2004

The Good City: Writers Explore 21st-Century Boston
Edited by Emily Hiestand and Ande Zellman
Beacon, 175 pp., paperback, $14

ADVERTISEMENT

Not only its current residents but all of us who ever lived in the city have our own Boston, each version defined by where and when we were there. My own Boston is Beacon Hill in the '60s and '70s, and again in the '80s on returning from Hollywood exile. It's the first years of the University of Massachusetts at Boston when classes were held in the old Gas Company Building and we hung out at Flash's Diner, the Captain's Bar of the Statler, and the downstairs bar of the Hampshire House before it became immortalized as Cheers. Each fall began with the Charles Street Fair; The Atlantic Monthly was still in its historic building at 8 Arlington St., and out of his one-room office across from the Public Garden, Seymour Lawrence published a hot list of authors headed by Kurt Vonnegut.

When I crawled back from Hollywood in the '80s, seeking and finding succor, my Boston was centered in the King's Chapel Parish House at Beacon and Charles, and the Writers' Room (then in the Transportation Building), as well as old standbys on the Hill like Gary Drugs and DeLuca's Market. In that incarnation I switched from drinks at the Sevens to coffee at Il Dolce Momento, and traded the burgers and fries at the Paramount for a natural foods cooking class at the Boston Center for Adult Education. The city has just what you're looking for, no matter what mood or phase of life you may be in.

The depth of my feelings for the place is best explained by Lynda Morgenroth's lovely essay ''An Intimate Geography: Boston Neighborhoods." She writes that the city, ''a place rich in history, architecture, and human endeavor, can impart meaning and dignity to our efforts, and soothe the small but continual humiliations that come to every life. . . . Through all our seasons of failure, foolishness, and blunted hopes, there is, in the community of the great city, the balm of respectable association and the consolation of beauty."

Morgenroth's essay is part of ''The Good City: Writers Explore 21st-Century Boston," a collection commissioned by The Boston Foundation ''to be published on the occasion of the 2004 Democratic National Convention in Boston." Most of the contents emphasize the informational aspects of the subjects, sometimes clogged with bureaucratic jargon, as in a complaint of ''jurisdictional ambiguity and conflict between city and state government and their disputatious progeny." Most of the prose is more graceful, though, and personal experience often peps up the authors' accounts of the city's politics, architecture, sports, art, religion, and business.

Amateur Hub history buffs will discover nuggets as they cull these pages, learning that Boston not only had the first public school, public library, paid police force, use of general anesthesia, Polaroid camera, and residential phone line, but also the first microwave oven, spreadsheet program, and iron lung, while Cambridge can boast of ''the nation's first openly gay African American mayor."

On the other side of the ledger of proud accomplishment, the authors do not shirk from showing the shameful side of what has been historically a two-faced city. It is ''famous, equally," as sportswriter Howard Bryant points out, ''for abolition and racism, for social justice and busing riots, for vision and inflexibility -- interconnected strains that can be felt while walking around town, or in the box seats at Fenway. . . . The city is famous for its oft-stated desire to confront these stubborn contradictions, and for its only limited success in doing so."

In every period and aspect of the city's history, there are reasons for civic pride and a flip side of shame.

Those Beacon Hill Brahmin ladies ''so proper they blacked out the erotic lines in Byron and Shelley" were also the ones to start ''a boycott of hats bearing the feathers of wild birds," to save the birds being slaughtered for the purpose, a movement that led to the creation of the Audubon Society, and later the Migratory Bird Treaty Act, which prohibited trade in feathers.

It was also the 19th-century Brahmins who formed a secret all-Protestant society that became the nationwide Know-Nothing movement (members answered all outsiders' questions with ''I know nothing"). Atlantic editor Jack Beatty tells us the Know-Nothings passed a constitutional amendment ''forbidding Catholics from holding state jobs" and rounded up ''thirteen hundred Irish paupers from the state's almshouses and shipped them to Liverpool."

At least these days ''Boston is a minority majority city, meaning no one group can claim it as their city," Beatty writes in one of the book's most valuable and entertaining essays, ''Whose City, Whose Hill?: The Tradition of Exclusivity in Boston Politics."

Beatty is also a biographer of James Michael Curley, who was elected Boston's mayor while in jail ''for taking a postal exam for a constituent who, Curley said, 'could not spell Constantinople but had the feet for a mailman.' "

Famous for living on graft, Curley gave out $100 tips during the Depression, instructing his eldest son: ''Don't ask me to explain it, but never open a checking account."

Sins long past provide present amusement, but Jane Holtz Kay, The Nation's architecture critic, jolts us up to date on urban problems: ''The close-packed plan that made Boston a walkable gem of a city is assaulted by today's developers. . . . As budgets shrink, the search for affordable housing loses allies and Boston's splendid park system lacks a commissioner and custodial care."

Despite its problems, the city still charms. A perceptive writer like Susan Orlean has titled her essay ''Hooked on Boston," feeling the city has outgrown its image as ''a crabby librarian" to become ''the brainiest American city" instead of ''the squarest."

This variety pack of Bostonia is notable not only for what it contains, but deserves commendation for a phrase it omits; nowhere in its pages can be found ''The Curse of the Bambino."

Dan Wakefield has written about Boston in his memoir ''Returning: A Spiritual Journey," and his novel ''Starting Over."
