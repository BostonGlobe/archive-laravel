Unfortunate son

Lemony Snicket is the author of darkly comic, wildly popular books filled with terrible happenings

By Joan Anderman, Globe Staff  |  September 23, 2004

Lemony Snicket is a hard man to find. He doesn't do interviews, eschews e-mail, and is rarely photographed. On those infrequent occasions when the elusive writer has been captured on film, he's usually wearing a dark trench coat, with a hat pulled down over his eyes and his back turned, walking purposefully away from the camera into a blizzard or some other dismal circumstance.

Neither does Lemony Snicket show up at his own scheduled appearances. He routinely disappoints his fans -- average age 11 -- who turn out by the hundreds at bookstores, libraries, and schools to meet the author of "A Series of Unfortunate Events," Snicket's wildly popular serialized novels. In his place Snicket sends Daniel Handler, a cherubic, neatly groomed man of 34 who explains to the children in morbid detail the reason Snicket can't be there himself. Once, at a Long Island Barnes & Noble, Handler passed around a box containing an enormous bug that was, he reported, responsible for Snicket's absence, having bitten the author in the armpit at a picnic the previous day.

Despite the author's reclusive nature, the books keep coming. "The Grim Grotto," also known as "Book the Eleventh" in "A Series of Unfortunate Events," arrives in stores today. It picks up where "The Slippery Slope" ("Book the Tenth") left off, with the doomed Baudelaire siblings -- Violet, Klaus, and Sunny -- adrift in a sea of awfulness. Misfortune and despair are the main features of the protagonists' lives.

"If you are interested in stories with happy endings, you would be better off reading some other book," Snicket cautions readers in the opening paragraph of the debut installment, published in 1999. "In this book, not only is there no happy ending, there is no happy beginning and very few happy things in the middle."

Indeed. Mr. and Mrs. Baudelaire die on page 8. Such a disturbing plot development is hardly typical in modern children's literature, and neither is the subsequent string of dire encounters with variously inept, abusive, doomed, and downright evil relations -- chief among them Count Olaf, who may or not be the orphans' long-lost uncle but who clearly will stop at nothing to steal the Baudelaire family fortune.

'A terrible idea'
The first 10 installments have spent a combined 600 weeks on The New York Times's bestseller list and have sold more than 25 million copies worldwide. "Lemony Snicket's A Series of Unfortunate Events," the feature film, starring Jim Carrey and Meryl Streep, will be in theaters this winter. To say that Snicket, who clever readers will have surmised is the alter ego of Handler, was surprised at the warm reception his darkly comic children's novels received would be an understatement.

"I just thought it was a terrible idea, me writing for children," says Handler, on the phone from the home he shares in San Francisco with his wife, a designer, and 10-month-old son.

The idea first surfaced in 1998, when Handler was living in New York. He had recently sold his first novel, called "The Basic Eight," which was set in a high school and chronicled the cutthroat world of adolescent cliques. Impressed with his pitch-perfect teen vernacular, editors at several children's publishing houses approached Handler about the idea of writing something for young people. The only concept Handler had in the mill was a mock-Gothic story he'd been developing, which he believed was far too gruesome for anyone, let alone children, to consume. But he decided, with the encouragement of several rounds of whiskey sidecars, that it was worth a late-night pitch to Susan Rich, a friend who'd recently joined the staff at HarperCollins Children's Books.

"I arranged to meet her in a bar after work, even though I wasn't working, and I figured I would tell her this idea and she would agree that it was terrible," Handler recalls. "I said, `There are three orphans, and their parents die right away, and everything is miserable.' It's a book I would read, but I didn't think it was a book a publishing house would be interested in. She said, `I really like that idea.' Then she called me the next day and said, `I'm in my office, there's no alcohol involved, and I still like the idea.' "

Handler has few like-minded peers in contemporary children's fiction. His name is often mentioned alongside those of Roald Dahl and Edward Gorey, both of whom the author read voraciously as a well-adjusted and popular child (most people assume he was a misfit) growing up in San Francisco. It wouldn't be inaccurate to add Hans Christian Andersen and Lewis Carroll to the list, placing Handler firmly in the tradition of Victorian writers who arranged for terrible things to happen to innocent children. True to his Gen-X roots, Handler frames the entire business with a great serving of irony. His style is arch, almost campy, and not for everyone.

"At first people didn't know what to make of it," says Terri Schmitz, owner of the Children's Book Shop in Brookline. "It's quite a change from 'Goosebumps' and the other popular series books. Some kids find them too upsetting. It takes a kid who gets the humor, which is pretty sophisticated."

A sense of the ridiculous
Handler's appearances are more interactive performances than readings. He urges kids to add sound effects, and to bury their books in the backyard when they get home, and to sing along on "Scream and Run Away," a song written for the "Unfortunate Events" audio books by Stephin Merritt of the indie-pop band Magnetic Fields. Handler, an accomplished accordion player, is an occasional member of the band.

"Daniel grew up as, among other things, a supernumerary in the opera," says Merritt. "He has a finely tuned sense of the ridiculousness of life."

So do Handler's East Coast buddies, several of whom had business cards made up in the name of Lemony Snicket, a pseudonym Handler used while researching right-wing organizations for "The Basic Eight." Lemony Snicket became a familiar feature of Handler's prankish 20s. Pizzas were delivered and letters to editors were written in his name. Lemony Snicket made outrageous acquaintances in bars. When it became clear that the narrator of "A Series of Unfortunate Events" had developed a persona separate from the author's, Snicket's name was resurrected once again. For the most part, Handler -- who enjoys a quiet domestic life -- feels grateful for the anonymity.

"There's a name that's quite well known that's not the name on my credit card, and I only feel like I'm missing out when I'm stuck at the airport," Handler says. "It would be nice if somebody recognized my name and whisked me off into the glamorous room. There's not a lot of glamour in the author business."

Handler is, however, something of a rock star to a significant swath of the preteen nation. He receives massive quantities of e-mails and letters from middle schoolers, ranging from suggestions for subsequent titles to speculation about the identity of Beatrice (to whom every book is dedicated) to unabashed expressions of pleasure over the fresh horrors that befall the orphans. Most surprising for Handler is the number of children he hears from who have themselves lost one or both parents.

"I didn't imagine that audience coming to these books," he says. "I hope that one of the reasons they're responding is that while the Baudelaires get on with their lives, they don't get over the loss of their parents. Often orphans in literature find a substitute, a new mommy or daddy. I think that's probably an abhorrent idea to someone who's lost a parent."

We don't live in a world of happy endings. Kids understand this as well as adults do -- although you wouldn't know it based on the preponderance of children's fiction that insists on tidy conclusions and just resolutions -- and it gets to the heart of what certain children find so compelling about Handler's stories. He presents a view that's intimately familiar to children and, for the most part, is ignored in literature.

Life isn't fair
"When you talk to children about their lives, whether they're in comfortable circumstances or dire circumstances, the notion of unfairness always comes up a lot," Handler says. "It's drilled into their heads how important it is to be fair, and then they watch things that aren't fair at all. If you behave well, you're not neccesarily rewarded. And if you misbehave, you're not necessarily punished. I don't wish to propose that my books are the only ones doing this, but all the best children's literature, all the best literature period, explores the idea that we want the world to go one way and it usually doesn't."

There will be two more installments in "A Series of Unfortunate Events," for an unlucky total of 13. But the final book may not arrive any time soon. First of all, the novels keep getting longer. And Handler takes longer and longer to write them -- partly because he's concerned with not repeating himself, partly due to the fact (he politely points out) that he's on the phone with a reporter instead of working, and partly because, as the series winds down, he wants to make sure everything fits into place.

Perish the thought, however, that Handler is planning to tie up all the loose and mysterious ends with a nice bow.

"I know how most of it will go, and I wouldn't call it a bow," he says. "Maybe scratchy twine."
