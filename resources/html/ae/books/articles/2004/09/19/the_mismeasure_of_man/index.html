The mismeasure of man

Psychology's preoccupation with classifying personality may obscure larger questions about human nature

By Howard Gardner  |  September 19, 2004

Cult of Personality: How Personality Tests Are Leading Us to Miseducate Our Children, Mismanage Our Companies, and Misunderstand Ourselves
By Annie Murphy Paul
Free Press, 320 pp., $26

When we hear about someone, we are likely to say ''What's she like?" or ''Tell me about her." We may expect to hear about the stranger's background, appearance, or intellect. But in all probability we really want to know about the individual's personality -- is she friendly, funny, or fickle? Will she remind us of Hillary Clinton, Julia Roberts, or Martina Navratilova? Is she a cross between Annie Hall and Erin Brockovich? Or is her personality unique?

Taxonomies of personality are scarcely new. In classical times, physician Galen devised a theory of bodily humors that classified people as sanguine, phlegmatic, choleric, or melancholy. In the 19th century, phrenologist Franz Josef Gall attributed differing personalities to varying configurations of skulls. In the 20th century, psychologists tried to place the study of personality on firmer scientific footing.

In an original, absorbing, and provocative book, Annie Murphy Paul relates the stories surrounding the creation of the major tests of personality. She focuses on the four most famous instruments: the Rorschach Test, a set of inkblots that subjects must interpret; the Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory, or MMPI -- several hundred true-false questions about the subject's beliefs and behaviors, including a scale that purportedly ferrets out liars; the Thematic Apperception Test, or TAT, where subjects are asked to make up stories about 20 ambiguous depictions; the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator test, a set of forced choice questions that yields 16 personality types. Paul spices up her account by introducing such current exotica as Emode, a website where one can take almost 200 online personality tests and be matched with one's optimal ''date"; the True Colors approach, where each person can be identified with a particular color; and the race to locate personality through brain imaging. Finally, she describes the most recent vogue: the claim to have discovered the ''big five" factors: extroversion, neuroticism, agreeableness, conscientiousness, and openness.

The popularity of the major tests is extraordinary. The MMPI is used by 86 percent of clinical psychologists and administered to 15 million Americans each year, and drawn on in 94 percent of child custody cases. The Myers-Briggs test is given to 2 million people a year and used by 89 of Fortune's largest 100 companies. The Rorschach test is used by 80 percent of clinical psychologists and administered in almost half of child custody evaluations. Sixty percent of clinical psychologists use the TAT, and its assessment center process is implemented by thousands of American companies. These tests infiltrate schools, the workplace, the therapeutic chamber, and the courts of our land.

Paul provides ample reason to be skeptical about the tests. There are five factions, each with its own approach to interpreting the Rorschach. Expected patterns of response for the MMPI were originally established on white Protestant Minnesotans of Scandinavian descent. Employees have successfully sued companies for misusing the MMPI -- for example, forcing them to participate in rehabilitation if their profiles suggest addiction. The Educational Testing Service dropped the Myers-Briggs test in 1975.

Moreover, none of the aforementioned tests fares well among scientifically trained psychologists. Their use is a matter of faith. More than half the people who take the Myers-Briggs more than once emerge as a different type on subsequent administrations; hence the test is not, technically, reliable. Furthermore, profiles on these tests are rarely compared with diagnoses arrived at independently; hence they are not, technically, valid.

Paul casts a critical spotlight on the test makers. Scott Hathaway, who created the MMPI, doubted in later life that one could in fact measure personality. Henry A. Murray, the co-creator of the TAT, led a World War II effort to select spies and also had an eccentric sex life. Isabel Briggs, the inventor of the Myers-Briggs tests, declared in later life that if she had known her husband's type, she probably would not have married him. Kenneth Clark, key witness in Brown v. Board of Education, Topeka, became depressed about the ways psychological tests were being used to maintain the status quo. Raymond Cattell, author of an influential contemporary personality theory, was a convinced eugenicist. While the gossip makes compelling reading, one has to ask whether it is germane in evaluating the tests. After all, we do not challenge Einstein's contributions just because he turns out to have been a difficult husband and a distant father.

Even those who continue to embrace IQ tests and the SAT are well aware of the many criticisms that have been leveled over the years. Intriguingly, as Paul points out, personality tests have been subjected to much less scrutiny. It is worth considering why these tests have been given a ''pass" by the general public. Paul suggests that they are seen as fun, harmless, and more therapeutic than diagnostic. Yet she emphasizes that consequential decisions about life prospects are increasingly made on the bases of these questionable instruments.

Why such acceptance? More so than other nationals, Americans love tests. As a specialist on learning I am often asked questions like this: ''My 3-year-old does algebra and speaks six languages. Can I have her tested to see if she is gifted?" or ''My 8-year-old has trouble reading; can you test her?" In the first instance, I reply, ''If she doesn't test well, will you conclude that she is not gifted?" In the second instance, I say, ''What will you do differently, depending on what the tests tell you? Since she has trouble reading, find a good tutor and work on her decoding and understanding skills." But my interlocutor is rarely satisfied.

While content for the most part to portray the personality psychologists (and let them hang themselves), Paul offers helpful analytic schemes. For example, she distinguishes between more artistic (gifted interpreter of inkblots) and more objective (electronically scored) instruments, and between those measures that are theoretically motivated (the Myers-Briggs draws on Jung) and those that emerge strictly from statistical correlations (the MMPI). And she reveals her own sympathies with the narrative approach to the study of personality, long favored by social psychologist Gordon Allport, who emerges as just about the only admirable figure in the book.

In the end, what one thinks about personality tests reflects one's view of human nature. If one believes that most of the differences among humans come down to a few basic dimensions, then one is likely to become a fan of current-day tests. My own skepticism derives from two facts. First, the way in which personality has been construed by experts will over time come to affect how people think of themselves; a conceptualization can in turn become either a self-fulfilling prophecy or a characterization to be undermined. Second, one of the most powerful findings from psychology -- described by Paul -- is the extent to which individual behavior changes significantly across context or culture. To the extent that this is true, any search for the basic underlying personality -- in one of the four humors or one of the big five factors -- is likely to be futile. I'd rather derive my understanding of human personality from George Eliot than from Raymond Cattell.
