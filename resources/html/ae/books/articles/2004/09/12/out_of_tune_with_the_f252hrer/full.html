Out of tune with the Führer

A musician's path from protégé to Allied informer

By Jane V. Curran  |  September 12, 2004

Hitler's Piano Player: The Rise and Fall of Ernst Hanfstaengl, Confidant of Hitler, Ally of FDR
By Peter Conradi
Carroll & Graf, 352 pp., illustrated, $26

ADVERTISEMENT

If only in his days as an aspiring painter Adolf Hitler had been welcomed into the Art Academy in Vienna instead of twice having his application rejected, someone once lamented, what a difference it might have made to the history of the world. One of Hitler's more incongruous characteristics was his sentimental enthusiasm for art and artists; we tend to associate appreciation for culture with nobility of the soul. In addition to his interest in painting and his zealous attendance at the Wagner festival in Bayreuth, Hitler counted the photographer Heinrich Hoffmann, the film director Leni Riefenstahl, and the pianist Ernst Hanfstaengl among his protgs.

Peter Conradi traces the fortunes of Hanfstaengl (1887-1975) in ''Hitler's Piano Player," a biography that reads as grippingly as any John le Carr thriller. This larger-than- life, Harvard-educated character known since childhood as ''Putzi" possessed enough charisma, energy, and expensive tastes to attract prominent and well-placed supporters and friends on both sides of the Atlantic, including the likes of William Randolph Hearst, Teddy Roosevelt, Djuna Barnes, and the Mitford sisters. And when, at the end of the war, the Gestapo death list was made public, his name stood alongside those of Winston Churchill, Charles de Gaulle, novelist Stefan Zweig, and Sigmund Freud.

Naturally the chief interest for the reader lies in Putzi's dealings with Hitler and in the way he was drawn into the Fhrer's orbit. Although a great admirer of Hitler's rhetorical skill, which he compared to that of an accomplished violinist, he said he was never completely in Hitler's thrall but remained distanced -- at times disbelieving, at others disapproving of reported and planned acts of violence. Moral scruples stood between him and a complete commitment to the Nazi stance.

Like some modern-day public relations consultant, Putzi viewed Hitler the man as someone badly in need of civilizing: He was ill educated, had no dress sense and few social graces. His political success would be enhanced by attention to such details. By playing the piano for him, Putzi was able to affect his mood. He even composed pieces at Hitler's request, marches and songs for party rallies, and ''Deutsches Largo," which Riefenstahl incorporated into the soundtrack of her film ''Triumph of the Will."

For too long, Putzi maintained the hope that Hitler could be educated, that he would submit to civilizing influences and eventually adopt a more moderate tone. Subsequently appointed Hitler's press chief, Putzi earned his keep by organizing interviews with foreign journalists and acting as party apologist at embassy events. Having begun by being mesmerized by Hitler, Putzi ended in a state of financial dependence on the Nazi regime. This made it all the more difficult for him to make a break, which he finally did, in 1937, fleeing to England via Switzerland.

Of crucial importance, as things turned out, was Putzi's mixed German and American heritage, although his attendance at successive Harvard reunions provoked enormous controversy. His relations with American journalists and former Harvard students while he was still living in Germany in the early '30s were important to his survival. And his intimate knowledge of the members of Hitler's entourage and of their political intrigues proved especially helpful to the American government. The inevitable downside was that he fell under suspicion from both sides. The freedom of movement he had enjoyed in his youth was irrevocably withdrawn.

Having escaped from Nazi Germany, and having endured internment in the British Isles from 1939 to 1940, Putzi was dogged by financial hardship.The alliance with President Roosevelt mentioned in the book's subtitle provided none of the excitement and glamour of his earlier life, but none of the hazards either. Putzi supplied the American government with anecdotal information based on his experiences before the outbreak of war. He did so over a number of years, until his reports became too dated to be useful, all the while living essentially in a state of internment, under military guard. The president did not meet his informant face to face, though they had been nodding acquaintances in the Harvard Club around 1912.

Putzi's naivete and eccentricities emerge only gradually in Conradi's skillful account. The attractive, adventurous socialite slowly reveals his impressionability and uncertainty of judgment. When, in October 1939, news of his internment in Britain reached The New York Times, an article in that paper accurately described him as ''the best of companions . . . a naively amiable man, weak enough to be swept off his feet by authority and power." By the end of his life, we find him back in Germany and officially ''denazified" but still virulently anti-Communist and holding racist and anti-Semitic views.

While it is certainly true that Putzi lived in interesting times, it is equally true that Conradi has made the most of his material. He has mastered the art of presenting biography as a story of suspense and action. Dramatic moments on the world stage are carefully prepared with an appealing attention to everyday details such as clothing and meals. Best of all, many of the chapters end with a cliffhanger, with sensation hovering in the atmosphere and momentum gathering like clouds.

Jane V. Curran is a professor of German at Dalhousie University in Halifax, Nova Scotia.
