BETWEEN THE LINES

Christianity's great fault line

By Robin Dougherty  |  September 12, 2004

Diarmaid MacCulloch says that one reason he wanted to write an 800-page book about the Protestant Reformation was to ''put a spotlight on the details." One detail the attentive reader learns is that there are more Presbyterians in South Korea than anywhere else on earth, and that they worship in buildings modeled after those in the American Midwest, an observation there's seldom room for in MacCulloch's lectures to undergraduates at Oxford University.

MacCulloch also had another, more serious mission. ''I think it bothered me more and more after 9/11 and with the Bush presidency. Here was something enormously urgent and relevant to talk to the world about." That something is the religious rhetoric of American political discourse, among other Reformation-derived elements of modern life.

''The Reformation: A History" (Viking, $34.95) looks at the effects of this massive social change on such disparate fields as family life, Catholic missions to Africa, homosexuality, anti-Semitism, and, of course, church doctrine. MacCulloch's discussion embraces far more than the conventional analysis of the social and economic forces of the era, and reaches to explain the ideas for which ''Europeans burned each other at the stake."

MacCulloch, a professor of history of the church at Oxford's St. Cross College, has recently finished a residency at the Folger Shakespeare Library in Washington, D.C.

Q. Why do Americans need to know more about 16th-century Europe?

A. The US is a Protestant Reformation power, and its public rhetoric comes out of the Protestant Reformation. It seemed to me that there wasn't much understanding of where that rhetoric was coming from. . . . What struck me more and more was the [biblical] theme of the ''Last Days" [described in the Book of Revelations]. Twenty years ago it was fairly marginal. . . . It became mainstream. It's something that Europe has really lost. In the States, there's a big [religious] constituency that interacts with the government.

Q. The religious right?

A. Yes. The big issue is the place of Israel. This was one of the big issues in 17th-century England. Oliver Cromwell let the Jews back into England, but not because he was a 20th-century liberal. He believed in the Last Days and . . . needed the Jews to bring on the Last Days.

Q. You say that Europeans cannot read American politics because they have lost the Reformation.

A. What they can't read is the accentuated religious rhetoric of the Protestant president. No politician in Europe would give five minutes to the religious rhetoric of Protestant America. It would be the kiss of death. This God talk is something you just don't do in public [in Europe].

Q. What light does the Reformation shed on the current upsurge in spirituality, which ranges from new age beliefs and yoga and interest in Buddhism all the way to Christian fundamentalism?

A. I think [all] are reactions to the 19th-century shift by Westerners that there were other ways of being religious that have as much validity as Christianity. Fundamentalists go back to old certainties, and the other reaction, which is to explore, comes from the Enlightenment. Both come from the Enlightenment. You can't have fundamentalism without the Enlightenment. The very literal interpretation of the Bible was very uncommon before the Reformation.

Q. What did you think of Mel Gibson's ''The Passion of the Christ"?

A. I thought it was a very interesting reworking of medieval tradition, full of nonbiblical material. Veronica wiping the face of Christ on the towel -- a dead give-away that it's a medieval spectacle. . . . It's curious in the ways in which it is unfaithful to the Bible. It doesn't use Greek, the language of the powerful at that time. . . . And the anti-Semitism. . . . There's a medieval tradition of doing that. It's very medieval in its depiction of the suffering of Jesus.

The predominant schoolchild image we have of the Reformation is of Martin Luther nailing his theses to the church door. In expanding our idea of the period, what image would you like readers to take away from the book?

I think one [image] that always appeals is that of men with beards. The big Protestant reformers who wore beards. Sixteenth-century clergy were allowed to get married. In the medieval church they had to stick with celibacy. . . . Medieval clergy were clean-shaven and tonsured and made sexless by this. A man with a beard being a prophet but also being a husband and father, just being a heterosexual man.

Q. Talk about the legacy of religious tolerance, which is what we're traditionally taught came out of the Reformation. Wasn't this only for Christians? For Jews, the result of the Reformation was not tolerance. Does the West have a Christian bias in this sense?

A. Yes. There's a poor record in Christianity. Christianity is historically not a tolerant religion. . . . It only comes in with the 18th-century Enlightenment, the first time Christianity showed much of an inclination to be tolerant.

Q. Do you think most churchgoers know much about the anti-Semitic venom of Martin Luther?

A. No, it comes as a complete surprise. They need to be told. It's very dangerous to have saints. Human beings are not saints. The way you make them into icons . . . you should assimilate the bad and good. It doesn't help to sweep things under the carpet.

Q. Are you an atheist?

A. I wouldn't say that. A friend of Christianity who has abandoned any dogmatic creed. I can't take the claim of any sacred book seriously. . . . At the same time, I love churchgoing, frankly. I go regularly to Anglican evensong. I play the organ. It's a complicated, ambiguous relationship.

Robin Dougherty, a writer and critic, lives in Washington, D.C. She can be reached at inkrd@aol.com.
