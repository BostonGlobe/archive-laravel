Sense and cinematography

The film industry's embrace of the blockbuster - heavy on hype, light on content and acclaim - is weighed in two new books

By Thomas Doherty  |  December 12, 2004

Blockbuster: How Hollywood

Learned to Stop Worrying

ADVERTISEMENT

and Love the Summer

By Tom ShoneFree Press, 339 pp., illustrated, $26

Open Wide: How Hollywood Box

Office Became a National Obsession

By Dade Hayes and Jonathan Bing

Miramax, 432 pp., illustrated, $23.95

Since the mid-1970s, the lumbering, slow-witted blockbuster has been Hollywood's flagship product. Whether in service to a bonanza or a boondoggle, the high concept is pure formula: Affix name brand, add subtitle -- then repeat. "Jurassic Park," "The Lost World: Jurassic Park," "Jurassic Park III" . . .

Like two franchise films going head to head on a summer weekend, Tom Shone's "Blockbuster" and Dade Hayes and Jonathan Bing's "Open Wide" compose a double bill on the marquee topic. Shone's is a sweeping cultural history surveying 30 years of blockbuster domination; Hayes and Bing's is a close study of three blockbuster wannabes released over the July Fourth weekend in 2003. More complementary than competitive, both books are smart, trade-wise inquiries into the megabuck films on the megaplex screens, the must-see spectacles typically forgotten before your feet hit the pavement of the mall parking lot.

Shone, a former film critic for the London Sunday Times, defines the blockbuster as "not quite a genre, but almost; often science fiction but not necessarily; something to do with action movies although not always. Most definitely not Kramer vs. Kramer, though." You know it when you are engulfed by it. A seasonal creature, it inhabits "one long canyon run, stretching between Memorial Day and Labor Day, into which the studios loose one $150 million behemoth after another, in the dim hopes of finding public favor." The species may have reached a self-referential tipping point with the pyrotechnics attending "Independence Day" (1996), a blockbuster actually named after its release date.

In zesty prose fueled by the moral outrage of a fan betrayed, Shone digs through the generic roots of the blockbuster, back before CGI dinosaurs ruled the earth: the perfect thrilling machine that was "Jaws" (1975), the forceful hokum that is still "Star Wars" (1977), and the high renaissance arc that stretched from "Alien" (1979) to "Terminator 2: Judgment Day" (1991). Then, mournfully, he relates the sorry legacy, artistic if not financial, of the bloated leviathans and knockoff sequels of the 1990s and beyond, when being entertaining is less important than being "toyetic" (that is, easily turned into toys. "Batman" is toyetic; "Driving Miss Daisy" is not).

Shone's outlook on the blockbuster veers radically from the standard film-critic party line, specifically the legend printed in Peter Biskind's influential 1998 book "Easy Riders, Raging Bulls." In Biskind's view, Hollywood in the 1970s was an oasis for hip auteurs and high ambitions until aliens -- some cuddly, some acidic -- invaded the noble artists' colony. Nonsense, says Shone, who would rather munch popcorn watching Steven Spielberg or George Lucas than be force-fed a diet of Martin Scorsese or Robert Altman. Such unabashed pop-cult affinities can be provocative ("Die Hard" is rated "one of the best films of the eighties . . . a national treasure of coherence, sophistication, reasoning, etc."), but Shone's rhetorical exuberance will have dissenters scrawling comebacks in the margins. "Is there a more exquisite piece of narrative clockwork in modern cinema than Back to the Future?" he asks breathlessly. Uh, "Memento"?

Like Biskind, Shone is afflicted with cinematic nostalgia, but his is for the good old-fashioned delights of the all-American blockbusters of the 1970s and 1980s. As a British lad with a serious Jedi Knight fixation, he was happy to have his consciousness colonized by Hollywood. However, once overseas grosses from "Jurassic Park" (1993) surpassed domestic box-office returns, the blockbuster lost its Yankee ingenuity and cowboy swagger. "One of the problems with the American blockbuster is not how insufferably American it is these days, but how disappointingly European it turned out to be," he complains. Too much planet, too little Hollywood.

Where Shone takes the big-picture perspective on the big pictures, Variety editors Hayes and Bing scrutinize just three blockbusters with the forensic zeal of a CSI tag team picking over the remains of a hit-and-run accident: "Terminator 3: Rise of the Machines," the third installment in the killer cyborg series and serendipitous gubernatorial product placement; "Legally Blonde 2: Red, White, and Blonde," a dose of estrogen counterprogramming starring Reese Witherspoon; and "Sinbad: Legend of the Seven Seas," a pet project of DreamWorks president Jeffrey Katzenberg, who hoped to lure youths weaned on computer-generated animation back to hand-drawn cell animation. Flashing their press credentials, Hayes and Bing gained fly-on-the-wall access to studio development meetings and marketing sessions. Only the spoilsports at DreamWorks barred the doors. No matter. Saddled with an archaic animation format and a Baghdad-born hero, "Sinbad" was dead on arrival, "the 'Gigli' of animation," crack the authors by way of payback.

Following the three contestants from preproduction to postmortem, Hayes and Bing endure a grueling gauntlet of test screenings, press junkets, exhibitor confabs, sci-fi fan fests, Internet geek chat rooms, and bonehead celebrity-on-celebrity interviews ("You're, like, one of my favorite actresses!" gushes Kelly Clarkson to Witherspoon). They take careful inventory of stockrooms full of cheesy "swag" (free goodies given out by the studios to sway impoverished film critics, who hawk the loot on eBay) and provide useful vocabulary lessons in the arcane lingo of the locals: "Frankensteining" (the process of stitching together a single screen trailer from the work of several different companies), "tradigital" (a seamless blend of two-dimensional and three-dimensional animation techniques), and "franchise fatigue" ("Alien Vs. Predator"). Though "Open Wide" delves perhaps too deeply into the concession-stand business at the 52-screen mega-megaplex at the Ontario Mills Mall in Orange County, Calif., readers will be hard-pressed to find a more informative and fair-minded dissection of the cybernetic organism that is contemporary Hollywood. (Speaking of which: Hayes and Bing are curiously silent about the controversial casting decision to replace Edward Furlong, who played savior of mankind John Connor in "Terminator 2" with Nick Stahl in "Terminator 3.")

Though every blockbuster aspires to be a " 'four-quadrant' movie" (a film that gets a thumbs-way-up from all four sides of a demographic rectangle made up of men and women over and under the age of 25), the prime target audience lives in the vicinity of PG-13. As much as the director, the coveted juvenile customers can exert a kind of final cut. If teenybopper focus groups demand more scenes featuring the mutt Spike in "Sinbad," then it will be so. "We need more dog," say the animators at DreamWorks after one such session, unaware that they already have one on their hands.

As befits their title, Hayes and Bing keep a close eye on exhibition practices, an unheralded but crucial component of the motion-picture business. During Hollywood's Golden Age, the major studios released a limited number of prints to movie palaces in big cities, after which the films trickled down into the hinterlands. Television transformed that leisurely pace into a full-on blitzkrieg riding a tidal wave of saturation exploitation. On June 20, 1975, the blockbuster prototype "Jaws" was released on just 409 screens in the United States. On Nov. 5, 2003, at 1400 Greenwich mean time, "The Matrix Revolutions" opened simultaneously, worldwide, on more than 18,000 screens. "Opening movies is a smash-and-grab business for the studios," write the authors. "They shatter glass, set off alarms and try to escape with the loot before somebody calls the cops."

Both books decry the fact that the modern blockbuster is so fortified by ancillary revenue streams and greased by industrial-strength publicity that even the most gargantuan turkeys -- "Hulk" comes to mind -- can turn a profit and, God forbid, spawn a sequel. Some films have always been critic-proof, but the modern blockbuster is virtually audience-proof. An unsettling epiphany hit the industry around the time of "Batman" (1989): Marketing really can trump aesthetics. No matter how bad the next episode of "Stars Wars," it will make a fortune.

But just when it seems like the game is fixed and the house always wins, the audiences -- and the artists -- strike back. Maybe Shone and Hayes and Bing have all seen too many Hollywood blockbusters to end on a total downer, but out-of-nowhere hits like "The Passion of the Christ" and "Fahrenheit 9/11" prove that the motion-picture racket is not quite yet down to a science. Likewise, "The Lord of the Rings" trilogy managed to breathe life into a flatlined genre with a blockbuster series that was marvelously inventive, hugely popular, and authentically beloved. Still, in the final tally of the non-box-office variety, the return on so much effort and the expenditure of so much cash seems rather paltry. "The dollar value of our butts in the seats has never been greater, but what it signifies has never meant less," laments Shone. Unlike that supersized T. rex glimpsed in the side-view mirror in "Jurassic Park," the blockbuster seems a motion-picture object that is smaller than it appears.
