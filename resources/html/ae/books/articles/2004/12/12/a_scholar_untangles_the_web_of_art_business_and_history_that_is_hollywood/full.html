A scholar untangles the web of art, business, and history that is Hollywood

By David Freeman  |  December 12, 2004

The Whole Equation:

A History of Hollywood

ADVERTISEMENT

By David Thomson

Knopf, 402 pp., illustrated, $27.95

Hollywood movies, like fiction and the theater, are regularly declared to be at death's door. Novels and plays may be all but comatose, but Hollywood isn't. It is, however, in a state of accelerated change and not for the better. It has always been changing; that's its nature. David Thomson takes a look at just why that might be in "The Whole Equation."

The title is from F. Scott Fitzgerald's "The Last Tycoon": "Not half a dozen men have ever been able to keep the whole equation of pictures in their heads." It's a rare person who can know all the secrets of the art, the business, and the glorious calculations of movies. Thomson uses the remark to examine important figures, ideas, and the twists of history from the silent era to the present. He's well equipped to take on this daunting challenge. His "Biographical Dictionary of Film," a volume written in the one-man-band spirit of Dr. Johnson, is idiosyncratic and indispensable.

Much like Thomson's "Dictionary," "The Whole Equation" is a big-hearted and occasionally ornery ramble through the decades. He examines a gallery of personalities including D. W. Griffith, who invented some of the grammar of the cinema in "The Birth of a Nation" (and marked himself a racist ever after); Erich von Stroheim, who made "Greed"; and Louis B. Mayer, the last M in MGM, once the most powerful of all the moguls.

In the modern era, Thomson writes about the screenwriter Robert Towne and "Chinatown." He thinks Towne might have been better served if he had made a novel of his story of water and power in Los Angeles rather than a movie. That might be so, but despite the compromises of production, the screenplay seems to me a masterpiece that just gets better as the years go on. Thomson also examines the work of Steven Spielberg, whom he sees as more of a producer than a director, but one of the few who can be said to hold the whole equation in their heads.

Like Thomson himself, the book is hard to encapsulate. His dukes are often up, and as a result I found that when I agreed with him, he seemed a sensible man and when I didn't, he seemed cranky. His book is a history and contains plenty of facts, some of which were new to me. The opinions are what kept me engaged. There are hundreds, and he tosses them around like a boy with firecrackers. There are far more incisive opinions and chapters than not. I'll report on two, one from each side of my ledger.

Film critics and historians often eulogize silent movies as balletic and pure and say that dialogue tainted the form. This view was held by directors who worked well in both the silent and sound eras -- Alfred Hitchcock, for example. Thomson argues, "If there was an 'art' in silent film, it was too reliant on stilted dance [and] Victorian theatricality." He certainly appreciates the great late-silent-era films, but he says, "With the greatest of silent films, I feel only the prospect of liberation, in that a half-made medium was about to become complete." It's an argument that blows the dust off generations of received wisdom.

That very originality can sometimes be a little baffling. There's a famous story that Thomson tells about Marlon Brando and "On the Waterfront." Brando's contract allowed him to leave the set each day to keep a standing appointment with his psychiatrist. It led to Brando behaving badly in the taxi scene with Rod Steiger ("I could have been a contender.") It's traditional that while close-ups are shot, the actor off camera feeds lines to the other player. Steiger held up his end of the bargain, but when it was Brando's turn he left to see his shrink. Rudeness, certainly. Thomson sees it as part of the "cult of incoherent earnestness that overtook American acting," some of which he traces to psychiatry. It's a principle of method acting that an actor use his own emotional pain to find the nature of the character he's portraying. Psychotherapy of course is dedicated to finding and alleviating such pain. Thomson is hard on both psychiatry and the method: It's "a nonsense that could yet destroy a society, as well as an approach that drags down art or storytelling." Self-absorption, yes, but the end of the republic? Seems a stretch.

Thomson tries to be optimistic about the future of Hollywood movies. He knows that "confidence and . . . energy" are the watchwords for America as well as its movies. He says, "The confidence cracked after World War II." It was the unfortunate confluence of the consent decrees -- by which the major studios agreed to sell off their theaters and consequently said goodbye to the vertical integration that was key to their commercial success -- and the blacklist, and the coming of television. The movie business still makes vast sums of money, but the pictures have yet to fully recover from those blows.

"The Whole Equation," like Hollywood movies, can be uneven, but it can be astonishing, too. It is astute and often fascinating. In combination with his "Dictionary," Thomson has produced something very like the whole equation.

David Freeman is a screenwriter and the author of six books. Among them are "A Hollywood Education," "One of Us," and, most recently, "It's All True."
