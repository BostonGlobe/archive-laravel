Shreve's winter of discontent and discovery

By Judy Budz  |  December 12, 2004

Light on Snow

By Anita Shreve

ADVERTISEMENT

Little, Brown, 304 pp., $24.95

Anita Shreve's new novel, "Light on Snow," tells the story of a father and daughter who find an abandoned infant left to die. If this premise sounds familiar, it may be because stories about lost and found infants seem to be a fast-growing subgenre of parenting literature. Not that the plot is a new one; baby boomers are likely to remember their ninth-grade exposure to one of these classic plots. But current versions do not feature Silas Marner and his "daughter," Eppie. These days, fictional baby finders are often crazy or criminal, as in Margot Livesey's "Criminals." At best, they are loving ex-con gardeners drawn into surrogate parenting, as Anna Quindlen shows us in "Blessings."

Shreve's novel is much closer to Quindlen's than to Livesey's, and it is far, indeed, from George Eliot's, even though Shreve also focuses on the baby's finder. In "Light on Snow," the baby, alive though frostbitten, actually exits the scene within a few pages. We hear reports of Baby Doris, but her finders have merely a single passing glimpse of her very late in the novel, and only through a window. Shreve is not interested in this part of the story or the details of the baby's future.

Still, her plot is echoingly familiar. The main characters, Nicky Dillon and her father, Robert, have lost their mother and wife in a devastating Christmastime car accident that also claimed 1-year-old Clara. Overwhelmed by grief, Robert abruptly decides to leave his Westchester, N.Y., home and his Manhattan architectural career to start over somewhere "north." Purely by chance, the pair ends up in small-town New Hampshire, where Robert buys an old house at the far end of a long, rutted road. His aim is essentially to hide from his past while indulging his grief.

Meanwhile, 12-year-old Nicky, yanked from her home in this confounding act of upheaval, shares Robert's isolation. "Ragged and raw and prone to sudden fits of weeping," she knows that her father is also barely holding himself together. The Dillons each mark the anniversary of the accident, but neither speaks of it directly or comforts the other. While Nicky loves her father, she is on the cusp of becoming a woman, and she yearns for a mother to ease her initiation into adulthood.

Then, on a late afternoon walk in the snowy woods, Robert and Nicky find the baby. Naturally preoccupied with saving the newborn, neither pauses to articulate the most obvious question: Who could have left the child to die? The reader does ask this question, although we expect to be told the answer since, in these stories, the absent/erring/evil parent will likely turn up. Thus we are not surprised when a few days before Christmas, Charlotte, the baby's 18-year-old mother, makes her way to the Dillon household to thank her baby's saviors. Nor are we surprised when a monster northeaster locks the threesome together for several days. The novel still has more than 160 pages to go.

Shreve prefers her readers to ponder moral issues, not plot twists. Clearly Robert, although slovenly and unshaven, does not deserve the grilling he receives from Detective George Warren; and just because Nicky lives on whoopie pies and lacks a TV or even a cassette player, she is not abused. Also, now that Charlotte is in the house, Nicky is free to fantasize about a loving older sister who will braid her hair and provide romantic descriptions of pregnancy. Charlotte even cooks French toast one morning; she would have made pancakes, she explains, but she could not find the mix. Pages later, when Nicky whips up pancakes from scratch and even adds blueberries, we know that both her kitchen skills and her moral compass are far superior to Charlotte's.

Nicky, an innocent yet reliable narrator, frames the novel's central ethical issues: How might they "save Charlotte"? What is "the right thing" to do? Good Samaritans twice over, how responsible are the Dillons for those they save? How much are our lives affected by fate and chance? Nicky says, "I want to believe that my father and I were meant to stumble across Baby Doris and give her a chance at life. But I'm not sure. I think about accidents and intersecting footsteps."

In addition to these questions, Shreve explores how the Dillons will be changed by their lucky or fated discovery. Will Charlotte's arrival be a catalyst for the family's rebirth? Will they, like Quindlen's gardener and Silas Marner, be reborn into emotional life? By novel's end, will father and daughter push a table into the kitchen instead of eating sandwiches on trays in the front room, the workshop, or their bedrooms? When will the lights come back on after the northeaster? Will Nicky be initiated into the painful truths about childbirth as well as the romance of pregnancy?

This novel is much more readable than Shreve's last, "All He Ever Wanted." Her fans will be relieved to settle down on a snowy day with an appealing narrator caught in challenging circumstances. There is comfort, too, in a world where all the loose ends are neatly resolved in the last few pages. Shreve may reach for the predictable in her novels, but she still gives us a good read. For the reader who wants more, there is always "Silas Marner."

Judy Budz is a professor of English at Fitchburg State College.
