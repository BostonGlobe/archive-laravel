From Jackie O. to hippies, a bumper crop of gift books

By Richard Dyer  |  December 5, 2004

There is a smaller selection of gift books this season, those elegant extravaganzas of slick paper, glamorous photographs, and sheer poundage -- whole forests have been saved from the saw. Perhaps this phenomenon is another index to a faltering economy; perhaps the publishing industry is coming to its senses, although it would be best not to count on that. If the quantity is down, however, the quality seems up, and interesting books have appeared in just about every category.

LOCAL INTEREST

''Jackie: A Life in Pictures," edited by Pierre-Henri Verlhac and Yann-Brice Dherbier (powerHouse, $60), is a companion to last year's ''John Fitzgerald Kennedy: A Life in Pictures," which simultaneously reappears in a paperback (Phaidon, $19.95). Both volumes reproduce famous and unfamiliar photographs, posed images and spontaneous ones. In the newer book, we see Jackie seated in the background during a fashion show at the Hotel Pierre, immaculately turned out and looking more glorious than the foreground model on the runway. Most fascinating of all are the non-iconic photos in both books -- Jackie in a peasant dress, headed toward a family gathering; the young senator and Mrs. Kennedy in a quarter photo booth in Boston in 1956, her gloved hand on his shoulder, he already himself, she still unformed by life. One of the poignant aspects of the Jackie volume is our ability to follow the process of her aging; her husband didn't get the chance to do that.

''The Rarest of the Rare: Stories Behind the Treasures at the Harvard Museum of Natural History," text by Nancy Pick, photographs by Mark Sloan (HarperCollins, $22.95), rescues a valuable resource from the hazards of the school trip or the family visit. ''Don't bother to go to see the glass flowers," a friend sagely advised when I arrived in Boston as a student. ''Sooner or later your mother is going to come to see you, and that is the first place she's going to want to go."

There are some interesting paragraphs on the glass flowers, but the book concentrates on some of the museum's natural treasures, especially those with curious provenances -- Charles Darwin's sand dollar, Henry David Thoreau's dried, pressed cranberry plant, a woodpecker preserved by Meriwether Lewis. There is a charming photograph of a composite dodo skeleton next to a Victorian dodo model, made of ''chicken feathers, duck wings, and -- for the tail -- the curled feathers of egrets." The book is charming and curious, and it does its job -- it creates an irresistible urge to return to the museum.

ENTERTAINMENT ''Broadway: The American Musical," by Michael Kantor and Laurence Maslon (Bulfinch, $60), is the companion volume to the recent PBS series. The book puts 510 photographs onto 470 heavyweight pages that survey musical theater on the Great White Way between the Ziegfeld Follies of 1919 and ''Wicked" from 2004. There are essays on composers from Irving Berlin to Stephen Sondheim, on stars from Fanny Brice through Audra McDonald, and commentaries by Sondheim, Tommy Tune, Mel Brooks, and others, lyrics from great songs -- the whole works.

There's yet another Beatles book, ''The Beatles: 10 Years That Shook the World," edited by Paul Trynka (DK, $39.95) and drawing on the resources of Mojo magazine; Brian Wilson contributes a generous foreword: ''I was jealous. I was jealous of their electricity . . . the magnetism they had. . . . Paul is the most versatile singer ever. He can rock like Little Richard and sing a ballad as sweet as anybody."

Next on the sagging shelf is ''Paul McCartney: On Stage, Off Stage, and Backstage" (Chronicle, $35), announced as a ''revealing, access-all-areas account of life on the road with McCartney," a chronicle of McCartney's 2002-03 world tour. The photographs are fun; the transcribed tape recordings of conversations about, and sometimes even with, McCartney are of an almost indescribable triviality and tedium.

There are two books about an icon of an earlier generation. ''Sinatra," by Richard Havers (DK, $40), is a big, photo-reference book about the singer's six-decade career; there can't be many questions about Sinatra that aren't answered somewhere in this tome. A more personal volume is ''The Sinatra Treasures," by Charles Pignone in collaboration with the Sinatra estate (Bulfinch, $45). Like predecessor volumes on John Lennon and Elvis Presley, this contains pull-out reproductions of Sinatra memorabilia, including the lead sheet to ''My Way," a copy of a mimeographed 1943 fanzine ''Frank Fare," a poster for ''Ocean's Eleven," even a backstage pass for a Sinatra concert.

From another dimension of entertainment comes ''Burlesque: Legendary Stars of the Stage," by Jane Briggeman (Collectors, $39.95). The author is the founder of the Golden Days of Burlesque Historical Society and lives, of all places, in Salt Lake City. For years she has sought out retired burlesque queens, interviewed them, and collected memorabilia. Most of the peelers say similar things (Jennie Lee: ''I was always sexy, but never vulgar"), and Briggeman doesn't ask tough questions. At the end, she wonders what happened to a number of ''lost" performers -- Sheena Savage, Cupcake O'Mason, Busty O'Shea, Blaza Glory. Evidently national telephone directories won't be much help.

FOR THE KIDS (AND YOURSELF) The National Geographic has created a new edition of ''The New How Things Work: Everyday Technology Explained" ($35). Author John Langone begins with the wheel and the pulley and ends with ATMs and the Tomahawk land-attack missile. Homely articles like the vacuum cleaner, the refrigerator, and synthetic fabrics alternate with digital cameras and video games, laser surgery and fiber optic cables. There are even a couple of pages on roller coasters -- and that's the experience this book delivers to its readers.

SHIFTING PERSPECTIVES

The cartoonists of The New Yorker certainly helped define the human comedy as it has played out in America over the last 79 years. Now Black Dog & Leventhal has published ''The Complete Cartoons of The New Yorker" ($60) -- more than 2,000 of them appear in the book, surrounded by essays by such New Yorker writers as Roger Angell, Lillian Ross, John Updike, and Calvin Trillin. The book also includes 2 CDs that contain all 68,647 cartoons ever published in the magazine.

And another volume that provides perspective on the human condition, ''Earth From Space," by Andrew K. Johnson, published for the Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum by Firefly Books ($49.95). The satellite photos of our land- and seascapes, weather patterns, and vegetation, of seasons and organic rhythms, are breathtaking. Man is absent, but we can see his handiwork on the beleaguered planet. There are wonderful images of cities (Boston, the hub of the universe, is not pictured) and of monuments like the Great Wall of China. But we also see deforestation and pollution and fires around Baghdad.

CURIOSITIES

''The New Annotated Sherlock Holmes" (Norton, $75), edited by Leslie S. Klinger, succeeds its famous predecessor from 1967, edited by William S. Baring-Gould. The new volumes collect Sir Arthur Conan Doyle's stories about the most famous resident of Baker Street; a third volume, to be published next year, will have the four novels. Klinger's is a vast work of entertaining ingenuity and erudition, annotating many obscure aspects of the Victorian era and drawing on a vast library of sources -- the ''selected sources" run to 86 pages, and there is a 32-page, double-columned list of active Sherlockian Societies, including four in the Boston area. ''Hippie," by Barry Miles (Sterling, $24.95), chronicles a period between 1965 and 1971 that has not lost its fascination because we are still living within its legacy. Here are ''Easy Rider," Sonny and Cher, Pink Floyd, Sergeant Pepper, the Weathermen, Woodstock, Altamont, Charles Manson, Alice Cooper, Dr. Timothy Leary, Allen Ginsburg, and lots else that certainly changed the planet if they failed to save it.

''The Comic Book Encyclopedia," by Ron Goulart (HarperCollins, $49.95), is basically an encyclopedia of the childhood of most Americans of the last seven decades, and most of the famous titles from ''A-1 Comics" through ''Zorro" are here, as well as many rarely mentioned, like ''Captain Tootsie," the hero who passed out Tootsie Rolls. Specialists will turn to other sources for advanced study of Donald Duck, Mickey Mouse, and the superheroes (Superman, Batman, Captain Marvel, and Wonder Woman), but most people will enjoy a few nostalgic moments with this book, and wish, again, that they hadn't thrown away so much of their personal history.
