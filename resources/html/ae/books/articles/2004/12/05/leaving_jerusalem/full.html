Leaving Jerusalem

A farewell essay by a veteran journalist has many secular Jews in the Israeli capital asking, Should I stay or should I go?

By David B. Green  |  December 5, 2004

Writing in his diary in April of 1948, when Jewish Jerusalem was under siege by the Jordanian Arab Legion, David Ben-Gurion described "the element in Jerusalem" as "20% normal people, 20% privileged (university, etc.), 60% weird (provincial, medieval, etc.)."

In his new memoir, "A Tale of Love and Darkness," the novelist Amos Oz quotes this droll characterization of his birthplace by the man who was shortly to become Israel's first prime minister, adding that "it is hard to say whether Ben-Gurion smiled when he wrote this entry in his diary."

Amos Klausner, as Oz was known before he Hebraized his name as an adolescent, grew up in the shabby central quarter of Kerem Avraham, the son of two largely secular, highly educated European-born parents. Today, the neighborhood is 100 percent ultra-Orthodox (undoubtedly "weird," by Ben-Gurion's standards), but in the years before and after Israel's independence, it had a far more varied population: merchants and craftspeople, intellectuals and civil servants, all Jews, but of varying degrees of religious observance. They were Holocaust survivors, ideologically driven immigrants, or people who'd come to Israel out of religious conviction -- sometimes all three.

Today, Oz himself famously lives in the small desert city of Arad, while the center of Israel's artistic and literary community is Tel Aviv, some 35 miles to the west. The distance between that modern city on the coast and Israel's ancient capital often seems much greater, however. To many secular Israelis, who make up the majority of the country's Jewish population, Jerusalem is gloomy, unsafe, backward, and most of its inhabitants "weird." In short, it is neither a nice place to visit nor to live.

Four years of intifada, a lengthy recession, and a declining tax base (thanks to its poor population and high number of religious, academic, and government institutions) have all left Jerusalem's downtown feeling like something of a ghost town. A popular image of the main highway heading out of town is of a one-way channel taking all the city's secular, middle-class population anywhere but here.

A recent article in the weekend magazine of the Ha'aretz daily by Neri Livneh, a veteran reporter for the paper, offered a dramatic expression of the feelings of one of those who deliberated lengthily before finally leaving Jerusalem, and sparked an exchange that went to the heart of how secular Israelis perceive their nation's largest city and capital -- whether as an increasingly closed, dangerous, and economically depressed town, or as one of the world's most vibrant caldrons of multicultural coexistence.

Livneh described her recent decision to move from the capital to Tel Aviv as part of an inevitable process that is emptying the city of all of its enlightened class. "Almost everyone I know from Jerusalem has left the city or plans to leave it," she wrote, adding that "the others stare sadly at the backs of those who are leaving, in a kind of collective fear of abandonment." Thirty years after she had arrived in Jerusalem as a university student, few of the things that had originally attracted her -- a bohemian community, lively cafes and night-life, a bustling university community at its heart -- exist here anymore.

Others interviewed by Livneh agreed. "There's no reason to stay in Jerusalem," said one woman. "From every point of view you can think of, you have to leave." Says another, "Already now, there's a feeling that the liberal residents that have remained in the city are under a kind of siege, that they're in the minority and on the losing side. . .."

True, Livneh's elegy isn't quite matched by the facts. Though more Jews are leaving the city than are arriving, thanks to a high birthrate the Jewish population (which currently makes up 67 percent of the total) is still growing, if not as fast as the Arab population. More of those Jews who are migrating are ultra-Orthodox than are secular, and in both cases the vast majority cite lack of jobs and of affordable housing, not a culture war. Still, perceptions can count for more than facts, and many secular Jews in Jerusalem are certain that if they don't go the way of Neri Livneh, they will become the Last of the Mohicans.

. . .

Livneh's essay was the talk of the town throughout much of the fall among the city's chattering classes, many of whom live in such upscale southern Jerusalem neighborhoods as Baka, where Livneh and I were, until recently, neighbors. But not everyone agreed with her grim assessment.

In the following week's paper, the Hebrew University geographer and historian Ronnie Ellenblum wondered why it was that self-exiled Jerusalemites seemed to feel a need, "like converts to a new religion,. . . to stand before the public and deliver a speech explaining in detail their road to the light." Ellenblum (who also lives in Baka) was not unsympathetic to Livneh's feelings of being in a spiritual and political minority. Nonetheless, he argued, "Jerusalem remains the only place in Israel that offers a true multicultural alternative," with "more types of Christians, Jews and Muslims than any other place under the sun." Tel Aviv may offer liberals the opportunity to live among like-minded people fighting for the rights of "the Other." But those who stuck it out in Jerusalem, he suggested, were faced with the challenge of actually living with the Other.

When I spoke recently with Neri Livneh, she dismissed Ellenblum's attitude as sentimental, if not condescending, and described Baka as the haven of "a small elite who have a different set of values than most of the rest of the city. . ., moral and enlightened, but heaven forbid there should be any mixing with other groups."

My perception has been different. I agree that this neighborhood of stone houses, founded and built in the early decades of the 20th century by Palestinian Arabs (who were in turn replaced after the city was divided in 1948 with Jewish refugees forced to flee their homes in nearby Muslim states), is desirable for its beauty and central location. But I love it mainly for the diversity of its population. Its residents are all Jewish (with few exceptions, Jews and Arabs live in separate districts here), but by no means are they all secular, left-wing, or Ashkenazi. In the modest Baka building where I live, for example, there are families from Morocco, Canada, the United States, Algeria, Russia, England, and Israel, and despite as wide a range of political and theological outlooks as can be imagined, we're all talking to one another (most of the time).

More to the point, for those who are open to it, Jerusalem truly is a living laboratory of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. The city's political status may be the final issue that Palestinians and Israelis will be left struggling with -- if they ever begin talking again, something that seems to be more imaginable in the changed political climate following the death of Yasser Arafat. But while the larger Israeli-Palestinian conflict intrudes on our lives through suicide bombings and other acts of violence, I would submit that relations between the city's different ethnic and religious groups are overwhelmingly peaceful.

I discussed this observation with J.L. Barnett, a British-born amateur anthropologist and a columnist on religious communities for The Jerusalem Report (my employer). Barnett, who arrived here three years ago, notes that the misguided development of the city over the past half-century -- as characterized by the large, cold Jewish neighborhoods thrown up on the city's periphery in order to shore up the capital's Jewish majority and form a physical bulwark against adjacent Palestinian villages -- has detracted from the city's physical beauty. But the "glory of Jerusalem," Barnett insists, "is not its stones or walls, it is its people. A majority of the people here are living according to a concept. You can see it in their faces. And if every building was destroyed and the people survived, I believe it would stay the same."

I recently spent an afternoon walking with Barnett in the Old City -- the walled, densely inhabited section of Jerusalem that contains the most important shrines of the three faiths (though most of the residents and merchants are Palestinian). After four years of intermittent terror, tourists have slowly begun to return, but most of my Jewish friends are still convinced that the Old City is unsafe.

It was a Saturday afternoon during Ramadan, and the streets of the Muslim Quarter were so crowded that just moving was a challenge. We walked along the Via Dolorosa, passed the perfume shop of the former Satmar Hasid from Brooklyn who today is a Shi'ite Muslim, and headed up a narrow street to a building whose staircase ascended onto a rooftop neighborhood called the Galician Compound, a small community of ultra-Orthodox Jews whose antecedents came to the city in the late 18th century. The surrounding Muslim neighborhood is overwhelmingly Sunni, but the second floor of the building below us functions as a Shi'ite mosque. From the roof one looks into the Christian Quarter and the Jewish Quarter, and through the skylights you can see the meat market of the Muslim Quarter. Different worlds, different beliefs, but a peaceful (if not always easy) day-to-day coexistence.

Barnett's explanation: "Everyone understands that the things they disagree about could fill a Pandora's box, and if that were to be opened it could turn into a bloodbath. There is ignorance, but interdenominational violence is the last taboo."

. . .

Nonetheless, among journalists, it is Neri Livneh's perception of Jerusalem as a battleground between religious and secular Jews that seems to carry the day. Last month, a panel on the theme "Is the Media Hostile to Jerusalem?" at the Jerusalem Institute for Israel Studies drew a large crowd. For the most part, the panel of mainly secular journalists spoke about the city as if it were the arena for an ongoing war between the sons of darkness and the sons of light.

When members of the audience were allowed to speak, however, it became clear that they didn't share this view. As one young man, who said his name was Chaim, commented: "I'm not really interested in the fight between secular and ultra-Orthodox. When I think about where to live, I want to know: Does the city let me live in dignity, work, buy an apartment? And by these criteria, Jerusalem is just not in the competition."

For those who choose to be here, the difficulty of Jerusalem "is not in Heaven," in the words of Deuteronomy, but very much on the ground.

David B. Green is deputy editor of The Jerusalem Report.
