A religious experience

Writer Neal Karlen explores his unconventional journey back to Judaism

By Mark Feeney, Globe Staff  |  December 13, 2004

Neal Karlen has decided to order a pastrami sandwich. He's having lunch at Les Zygomates, and French bistros are not known for their deli. So to be on the safe side he asks, ''Is there mayo on that?" The waiter says there is but he can ''eliminate" it. Karlen nods. ''Could you eliminate it with extreme prejudice?" Then he mutters to himself, ''I'm not that assimilated."

Assimilation is a matter of no small concern to Karlen, the author of ''Shanda: The Making and Breaking of a Self-Loathing Jew."

''Shanda" is a Yiddish word meaning ''disgrace" or ''scandal." Its best-known use, perhaps, is in the phrase ''Shanda fur di goyim," meaning ''an embarrassment in front of non-Jews." That's how Karlen came to see himself, but that's getting ahead of the story.

Karlen, 45, grew up Jewish in a Minneapolis suburb -- so Jewish he went to Brown to study with Jacob Neusner, the renowned Judaic scholar, and intended to become a rabbi. Yet he was so Minneapolis suburb that he worshiped the Twins and inhaled popular culture.

''Neal was always six months ahead of the rest of us," says Art Simon, a visiting professor of film at New York University who's known Karlen since they were in second grade together. ''He was always sniffing out new things, a kind of intellectual and literary guide. He's still that way."

For a while, popular culture won out. Karlen worked as a reporter at Newsweek, then Rolling Stone, where he became the magazine's pipeline to Prince.

''For some reason, I was the only person he'd talk to," Karlen says, with equal parts amusement and bafflement. ''The Minneapolis connection, I guess. It got pretty ridiculous. I was paged once at the Dallas-Fort Worth airport and told to call my mother. She was puzzled. 'Rolling Stone telephoned, and they said, ''The Prince wants you?" ' "

Karlen's rock associations led to a book on the band Babes in Toyland. His devotion to baseball led to a book on the independent Northern League, ''Slouching Toward Fargo." He also collaborated on memoirs by actress Jenny McCarthy and comedian Henny Youngman.

The Youngman assignment was a match made in manic-impressive heaven. Karlen's liquid eyes and curly hair give him an elfin quality. But inside him lurks the soul of a stand-up comic. His velvety baritone and polite manner make the one-liners sound more like aphorisms than shtick. Make no mistake, though, they're one-liners, often very funny. The water Karlen drinks with his pastrami isn't carbonated. It doesn't have to be. He supplies his own spritz.

Youngman's milieu, the world of borscht belt comedians, was thoroughly Jewish. It was very different from the Minneapolis Karlen moved back to in the early '90s.

''The sense of other was so strong there," Karlen says. ''You didn't get that in New York or LA. There you were just another schmuck with a typewriter. In Minnesota, I was the brother from another planet, and I loved that."

Karlen's return to Minnesota increased his alienation from Judaism. Briefly married to an Irish-Catholic woman, he more and more mocked his Jewishness. Yet even as he embraced the role of shanda fur di goyim, turning himself into a self-styled ''Jewish Uncle Tom," he found himself more and more unhappy. He was 40: divorced, childless, back in his hometown but spiritually homeless.

That's where ''Shanda" begins. The book takes off, literally, when Karlen encounters Rabbi Manis Friedman on a flight from Los Angeles to Minneapolis. It was a sign. A famed Hasidic scholar, Friedman spoke to Karlen's synagogue youth group 30 years before. If that wasn't recommendation enough, it was Friedman who'd converted Bob Dylan back to Judaism after his born-again phase in the late '70s.

That chance meeting led to Karlen's taking instruction from Friedman. The rest of ''Shanda" focuses on those meetings and Karlen's reconciling with his Jewish roots.

Of course, there are Jewish roots and there are Jewish roots. Karlen prays daily and he wears tefillin, the pair of small black boxes containing biblical verses worn by men as they say their weekday prayers. But he doesn't keep kosher or go to temple every week. Yiddishkeit, Jewish lore and culture, matters to him as much as ritual does.

''To me," Karlen argues, '' 'The Big Lebowski' and Sandy Koufax are important parts of Jewish lore. I really do feel that what Koufax did in the 1965 World Series, missing a start because it was Yom Kippur, is as important as anything in [the writings of the Jewish philosopher] Maimonides."

Karlen put his unconventional views on Yiddishkeit to the test when he tutored a friend's daughter for her bat mitzvah. Her mother, Lu Lippold, recalls being at a party a few years ago and hearing Karlen describe his work on the manuscript that would become ''Shanda."

''The light bulb went on, Lippold says, laughing; Lippold is the producer of a new documentary on Paul Wellstone, the late Democratic senator from Minnesota, and his wife, Sheila. She says that having Karlen tutor her daughter ''was worth a try, though I didn't know it would end up in the book.

''Neal always had some shtick going, but he really knew his stuff. As my daughter said in her bat mitzvah speech, being prepared by Neal was as much of an experience as actually being bat mitzvahed was."

Karlen hasn't lost a sense of humor about his religion. ''I should have ordered bacon," he deadpans when a photographer arrives.

Karlen grows quite serious, though, when asked the simplest of all religious questions, if also the most difficult. Does he believe in God?

There's a long silence. Finally, Karlen says, ''I only pause because I don't know what name to put on it. By 'it' I mean God. I don't know if God is a guy with a long beard and the Ten Commandments, Charlton Heston, and Cecil B. De Mille, but, well, if I had to find some way to describe what I believe in, it would be seeing everyone with an individual face: That's God."
