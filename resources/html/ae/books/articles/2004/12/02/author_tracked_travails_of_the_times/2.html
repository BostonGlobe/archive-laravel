Author tracked travails of the Times

December 2, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Working on the book also opened Mnookin's eyes to the passion readers have about the Times.

"The thing I learned on a much more visceral level is the place this institution has in people's lives," he said. "When I told people I was working on a book about The New York Times, everyone had a strong view on that paper."

Changes prompted by the Blair scandal, such as the promotion of several editors who had been critical of Raines and the addition of an ombudsman, have made the Times better equipped to deal with future crises, Mnookin said.

"The editorial team in charge now is much more responsive to taking criticism and not taking it as a personal affront," he said. "The paper as a whole is much more responsive in dealing with issues of credibility."

Mnookin grew up between Newton Centre and Chestnut Hill, attending Newton North High School from 1986 to 1990. His parents, Wendy, a poet, and James, who works at a local investment firm, still live in the house in which he grew up.

"I really loved it at North," he said, describing himself as a cocky punk rocker. "I was in all honors classes; at the same time I had ratty bleached blond hair and did a large amount of drugs. I was probably one of the few punk kids working on the student newspaper and acting in plays."

Mnookin discovered his calling as an editor and reporter for the Newtonite, the school's award-winning student newspaper.

"Almost immediately it was all I wanted to do professionally," said Mnookin, who praised the influence and guidance of the paper's longtime adviser, Helen Smith. She's "a rabid advocate for high school journalism," he said.

But Mnookin's dabbling with alcohol and marijuana as a freshman quickly turned into getting high every day and binge drinking. It wasn't long before he was selling pot and using cocaine, LSD, speed, and prescription painkillers.

In a 1999 essay for the online magazine Salon.com, Mnookin wrote: "I had lots of friends and people thought I was cool. I was the smart, witty kid who smokes pot all the time and somehow still gets straight A's."

Mnookin wrote that he enjoyed being perceived by classmates as edgy and rebellious, rather than as an overachieving honors student.

Mnookin's drug habit did not prevent him from going on to Harvard, where he studied the history of science and worked on the Harvard Crimson. By the time he graduated with honors in 1994, Mnookin already had tried to get clean once, with little success, in a detoxification program at McLean Hospital in Belmont.

Shortly after college, Mnookin moved to New York with romantic visions of being like Beat Generation writer William Burroughs. Instead, he wound up with a four-times-daily heroin habit, an apartment filled with blood-stained rags and worn-out needles, and a series of dead-end jobs. The years became a revolving door of near overdoses, hospitalizations, and then using all over again. He almost severed his relationship with his family for good.

Moonkin entered a boot-camp-style rehabilitation facility in Florida in 1997. He was kicked out before he completed the three-month program, but he said he has remained sober since.

Even now, Mnookin is not sure what turned things around, whether it was finally getting drugs out of his system for more than a week or whether it was his stubborn refusal to die from an overdose, which his mother feared.

Shortly after getting clean, Mnookin's journalism career took off. "In some ways," he said, "it was easier for me to start fresh because it didn't feel like anything was beneath me."

After stints at the Palm Beach Post and the now-defunct Brill's Content magazine, Mnookin became a media critic for Newsweek in 2001, covering the industry's power brokers.

He left Newsweek last year to work on "Hard News" as a fellow at Harvard's Kennedy School of Government.

Now, when he's not on a cable news show promoting the book, Mnookin is working on a piece for Vanity Fair about the 2004 Red Sox team.

A lifelong Sox fan, Mnookin said he pitched the story so that he could soak up the clubhouse atmosphere under the guise of research.

He even has little mounds of dirt he collected from Yankee Stadium and Busch Stadium sitting on his dresser.

Mnookin said that being on the field after the Red Sox clinched the World Series was "one of the most fun experiences of my life."
