FOR CHILDREN

As the year wanes, rites and remembrances

By Liz Rosenberg  |  December 19, 2004

Christmas in the Barn
Written by Margaret Wise Brown
Illustrated by Diane Goode
HarperCollins, 32 pp., ages 3-7, $15.99

B Is for Bethlehem: A
Christmas Alphabet
Written by Isabel Wilner
Illustrated by Elisa Kleven
Dutton, 24 pp., board book, all ages, $8.99

One Candle
Written by Eve Bunting
Illustrated by K. Wendy Popp
HarperTrophy, 40 pp., paperback, ages 4-9, $5.99

Snow
Written and illustrated by Uri Shulevitz
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 32 pp., paperback, all ages, $5.95

Miss Bridie Chose a Shovel
Written by Leslie Connor
Illustrated by Mary Azarian
Houghton Mifflin, 32 pp., ages 4-9, $16

Now is the season when the steady stream of new children's books becomes a rushing river. Many are holiday books, covered in red and green, and silver, some more wrapping than substance. This is peak season for bookmakers and booksellers, looking to enliven familiar themes or deliver Christmas classics with a new twist. Not so long ago Chris Van Allsburg's "The Polar Express" was such a book, dazzlingly beautiful, mysterious, and fresh, the story of a child's nighttime journey by train to the North Pole -- now playing, as they say, in your local movie theaters. It remains, between the covers, as strangely wonderful as ever. I did not discover a new "Polar Express" this season, but found enough other beauties to bring the year to a happy conclusion.

Margaret Wise Brown's "Christmas in the Barn" was first published in l952, and more than half a century later, her text stands firm in what may be its ultimate edition, with new color illustrations by Caldecott Honor artist Diane Goode. Brown's gently tumbling rhymes are simultaneously whimsical and reverent as she tells the story of the animals gathering around Baby Jesus on the night of his birth. Perhaps her greatest gift was knowing how to pay attention. Here her attention is on the animals: "The oxen lowed, the donkey squealed,/The horses stomped, the cattle sighed,/And quietly the daylight died in the sunset of the west." It was Brown's secret desire to be a serious poet. Her blessing and curse is to be remembered as a children's writer, the great lyric poet of American picture books. Goode's art matches Brown measure for measure; a touch of the cartoon, a touch of the poet, all lit with gold shimmering from barn, star, hay, and sun.

"B Is for Bethlehem," by Isabel Wilner, tells the Christmas birth story in a rhyming alphabet. Here the language is more elaborate and stiffly adult: "A's for Augustus, emperor of Rome,/ Who decreed, 'To be counted, let each man go home,' " and one might have passed over it in silence, but for the brilliant, jewel-hued collage illustrations by Elisa Kleven. Kleven's images are childlike, detailed, ornate -- like dozens of lacquered Russian boxes or Ukrainian Easter eggs. Wilner's verse has its highlights, too, as in "N is for Night, so quiet, so still./Peace in the stable. Peace on the hill." Adults and children may squabble over rights to "B Is for Bethlehem" for coffee table or nursery nightstand; the new board-book format suggests this version is intended for the latter.

The premise of "One Candle," a Hanukkah book by Eve Bunting, may seem dauntingly sad -- two elderly sisters recall their Hanukkah interred in Buchenwald. The story is told in the midst of merriment, a memory of starving in the midst of plenty, how two sisters contrived a menorah hollowed from a single precious potato, a tradition they honor each year. Such remembering "has to do with being strong in the bad time and remembering it in the good time." K. Wendy Popp's illustrations emit the feel of sepia photographs, lightly touched with color, as the past touches the present, and the present colors the past. "One Candle" is also a story of family togetherness, and images of family happily crowded around the Hanukkah table help offer a joyful conclusion. Grandma Rose remembers how the single light "lifted us up to the stars," and the young narrator -- a girl just now old enough to understand the meaning of this yearly ritual -- sees "the one, steady flame" as the family offers its Hanukkah cheer: " 'To life!' " It is a beautiful, heartbreaking, uplifting book in words and images, worth reading even though the celebration is just past.

Winter would not be the same without Uri Shulevitz's Caldecott Honor Book, "Snow," now available in a large, square, handsome paperback, surely the ideal gift book for snow lovers of any age. Shulevitz is a master of concision: "The skies are gray. The rooftops are gray. The whole city is gray." So begins "Snow," but one small boy spots one snowflake here, two snowflakes there. "It's snowing," he declares to the dismissive grown-ups. Luckily for him, "snowflakes don't listen to radio, snowflakes don't watch television." They just keep piling up, and as the boy drifts past a children's bookstore, he is briefly joined in an ecstatic, dreamy, nursery-rhyme dance. Perhaps no other illustrator-author touches my heart the way Shulevitz does. "Snow" is a child's victory over cynicism, an urban snow-dance, a miniature theater of perfect snow.

Granted, "Miss Bridie Chose a Shovel," by Leslie Connor, with exquisite woodcuts by Mary Azarian, is not a holiday book, strictly speaking, though it begins with Miss Bridie choosing a shovel for a gift. Nor is it just a winter book, although it ends with Miss Bridie "looking out at the quiet, frosted farm," sweetly illuminated by Azarian. It is a story about one young woman's eccentricity and "divine stubbornness," as John Gardner named it, choosing "a shovel back in l856," sailing across the sea, and beginning a life she forges for herself. She digs a garden, scrapes snow from a "frozen, moonlit river," where she meets her husband; she shovels coal into a black iron stove, digs fence holes; survives a fire, and begins again, shovel in hand. "Miss Bridie Chose a Shovel," Connor's first children's book, moves in large cycles and seasons, celebrating a life of bravery and of choice. This seems a good way to conclude a year.

Liz Rosenberg reviews children's books monthly for the Globe.
