Humoring himself

July 27, 2004

Page 3 of 3 -- His Atlantic work -- following around Bill Clinton, eavesdropping on a Howard Dean house party -- has been pretty darned serious. In the September issue, he is publishing a long interview with Secretary of State Colin Powell, during which the two men mull over Powell's notion of a "non-zero-sum" foreign policy. "I've always had an interest in serious topics," O'Rourke says. "Just because a subject is serious doesn't mean it doesn't have plenty of absurdities. I think all these subjects are funny, even Colin Powell's foreign policy."

His longtime book editor, Morgan Entrekin, feels that O'Rourke has been addressing serious questions for at least the past 15 years. "If you read some of the foreign reporting he did for Rolling Stone, you could learn a lot more about the places he visited than from the correspondents of The Washington Post and The New York Times. He's been evolving, and I think the gonzo edge shifted a little as he got older."

In person, O'Rourke is witty and intelligent, and an unapologetic non-yukmeister. Policy handbooks and military histories lie strewn around his office. On a recent night he dined with Harvard professor emeritus Richard Pipes, perhaps America's most eminent historian of the USSR, who summers nearby. "Look, if you scratch Sancho Panza, you're always going to find Don Quixote," O'Rourke says. "As my wife would be the first to point out, I'm not very funny around the house."

Outside the house, O'Rourke pursues his shooting and gaming habits with dead aim. From April to September, he and a group of three or four pals spend about a day each week running and training their hunting dogs, and blasting small winged objects out of the sky. "We all like guns," testifies Alex Bass, a construction supplies executive from Jaffrey (and the congressman's brother). "Basically he's running a game management preserve up there. We let the birds out, we run the dogs, and we drink beer, not necessarily in that order." Bass says the season culminates in a hunting trip to New Brunswick. "Then we run our dogs up there and drink more beer."

Alex Bass has an alert sense of humor but warns a reporter to be careful when writing about O'Rourke's dog-training acumen: "He probably wouldn't think it was funny if you implied that he wasn't successful in training that dog." Bass's brother seems more candid on this point. "Both P.J. and my brother are irrational when it comes to dogs," the congressman comments from the safety of his Washington office. "There has been too much inbreeding -- both of the dogs are clinically insane. They scare off everything within five miles, but those guys think the dogs are perfectly trained."

"He's just jealous," O'Rourke retorts. "His dogs are very short."

O'Rourke has mocked his hunting exploits in articles for outdoor magazines, and he was asked if he might consider turning the humorous Forbes articles about the country squire of Breakwind Oaks into a book, like the classic "Mr. Blandings Builds His Dream House."

He seems reluctant. After writing four seasonal articles for Forbes, he says, "I ran out of jokes. The septic disaster; the ornery neighbor; the goat got loose -- that's a rich but narrow vein of humor." Entrekin says the two men have joked about a proposed tome titled "The Drinking Man's Guide to Raising Children." This turns out to be a subject that P.J. warms to. "You can't have a resource like the kids and not use them," he says, his eyes twinkling with anticipatory delight.
