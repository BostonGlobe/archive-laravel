Political primers

July 25, 2004

Forty years ago, historian Richard Hofstadter wrote a famous essay titled ''The Paranoid Style in American Politics." The flood of new political books this season would have given him even more material for his thesis than he had in the 1960s.

Practitioners of the ''paranoid style," he said, often rail against their political opponents as part of ''a vast and sinister conspiracy" that seeks to undermine and destroy a way of life. Paranoids in politics are not irrational, but they become carried away with exaggerations and distortions. At the time he penned his essay, Hofstadter specifically had in mind the extreme right-wing movement of the 1950s and '60s. But he said the paranoid style is by no means limited to the right, and his essay contains historical examples from a range of minority American political movements.

Not everyone, however, is caught up in imputing dark motives to the political opposition and in limiting themselves to a narrow dialogue that bolsters their own preconceived views. The books discussed below concern themselves with the venom in today's politics, but rather than adopt the paranoid style, they seek to understand with less shrillness and fear-mongering, albeit no less point of view, how we got where we are and, in many cases, offer ways out of the current political deadlock.

Fat Man Fed Up: How American Politics Went Bad
By Jack W. Germond
Random House,240 pp., $24.95

As Jack W. Germond makes clear, much of the public is just plain tired of politics. In his aptly titled book, ''Fat Man Fed Up," Germond believes there is enough blame for everyone. That includes the voters (we get what we deserve), the politicians (they should talk about something that affects people's everyday lives) and the media (especially television). There are some inconsistencies in his book -- he suggests it doesn't matter that most reporters may be liberal, though it does matter that Brit Hume of Fox News is conservative -- but on the whole Germond's rant is straightforward, fun, and clean. The most compelling part of the book is his criticism of the media and how it has contributed to the state of campaigns today through its evolution of focusing more on ''gotcha" moments and breaking a story above really getting to know the candidates and telling the voters what matters.

In his chapter about being a liberal, he repeats a standard refrain: ''It isn't difficult to see why conservatives dominate the talk shows and the little magazines -- it's called moral certitude. . . . On the other hand, liberals by definition seem less positive about the rightness of their positions." As a result, he does not believe liberals can ever represent their views with the same strength.

Stand Up Fight Back: Republican Toughs, Democratic Wimps, and the Politics of Revenge
By E. J. Dionne Jr.
Simon & Schuster, 256 pp., $24

E. J. Dionne Jr., a liberal columnist for The Washington Post, disagrees with Germond. Politics weren't always this divisive and don't have to be so now, he says in his new book, ''Stand Up Fight Back." Dionne says President George W. Bush squandered a real opportunity to unite the country and the Democrats are equally to blame for the enactment of Bush's agenda. Although a Republican would be unlikely to agree and might even say there is a bit of the paranoid style in some of Dionne's points, Dionne is forceful in his Bush arguments. But, as in Germond's work, the best part of Dionne's book is when he examines his own group -- the Democrats.

''It is hard to express your own beliefs if you are forced to speak in the tongue of your opponents," Dionne writes. ''Arguments are lost before they begin" because they are made through the language of the market, on the assumption that government is bad, and on whether a policy is tough or soft -- all of which favors conservatives. The Democrats are always on the defensive, rather than asserting their views, he argues.

What's the Matter with Kansas?: How Conservatives Won the Heart of America
By Thomas Frank
Metropolitan, 320 pp., $24

Another book that looks at how the political climate has changed to where liberalism is on the defensive is Thomas Frank's ''What's the Matter with Kansas?" The book explains why, in Frank's view, Kansans are not voting their economic interests. His thesis is that Republicans have courted voters on culture issues, from religion to railing against the so-called elites of New York City, in a strategy that Frank dubs the Great Backlash. How else to explain why they would vote for the pro-business GOP?

Washington Post conservative columnist George Will has criticized the book for having a paranoid style, and Frank's cynicism may lend some truth to that, but Frank presents an in-depth look at a part of the country with which he is familiar -- he grew up in Kansas -- and in the process tears into conventional wisdoms, such as the idea of a red-blue divided America promulgated by political pundits.

The Two Americas: Our Current Political Deadlock and How to Break It
By Stanley B. Greenberg
Thomas Dunne, 288 pp., $25.95

While not seeing America in a strict red-state versus blue-state divide either, Stanley B. Greenberg addresses the politics of America's current parity in ''The Two Americas." Greenberg, who was a political strategist for President Clinton, takes a balanced and nuanced view of the political climate. He offers a new and intriguing twist on the political picture of the last half-century.

This unconventional view says that for the first time in American history neither party has been dominant. As a result, the 2000 election was not an aberration, nor should it have been a surprise. While Greenberg believes the nation's trends favor the Democrats, he provides two distinct roadmaps of how both the Republicans and Democrats can, through tactics and strategy, reshape the political map and claim hegemony.

The Buying of the President 2004: Who's Really Bankrolling Bush and His Democratic Challengers -- and What They Expect in Return
By Charles Lewis
Perennial, 528 pp., $14.95

Charles Lewis and the Center for Public Integrity take a different look at the political scene in ''The Buying of the President 2004."

The book is not completely accurate. Thanks to Howard Dean, it is no longer true that since 1976 every candidate who has raised the most money in the year preceding the election won his party's nomination. But it still presents a comprehensive portrait of the fund-raising histories of all 10 candidates for the Democratic nomination this past spring, as well as a thorough look at Bush's fund-raising history.

Michael Horn is David Gergen's research assistant and enters Harvard Business School in the fall.
