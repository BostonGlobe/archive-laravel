What makes George run?

July 25, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- These moments, while dramatic, are not isolated. Richard Clarke, in his book, ''Against All Enemies" (Free Press, $27), also portrays a president who tends to jump toward policy conclusions. So did David Frum, a White House speechwriter who wrote one of the earliest, inside accounts, ''The Right Man" (Random House, $25.95). In an otherwise admiring work, Frum writes that Bush is ''impatient and quick to anger; sometimes glib, even dogmatic; often uncurious and as a result ill informed." Even Karen Hughes, in her book ''Ten Minutes From Normal" (Viking, $25.95), shows a White House where clear, sharp marching orders are valued over careful, drawn-out deliberation. Hughes has written an appealing and highly readable memoir of her days as a close Bush confidante; his fundamental decency and courage as a leader come through clearly. But only on one issue, stem cell research, does he engage in deep reflection before setting a course.

How does one account for the way Bush approaches decision-making? A variety of theories have been advanced, none wholly convincing. Many have written that he is driven mostly by ideology. But Bush also often makes quick judgments in a variety of other areas where ideology isn't at issue. (Example: Immediately after 9/11, he decided that the United States should change its long-held policy toward terrorists, no longer treating them as isolated forces requiring targeted responses but as a worldwide movement requiring an all-out war; Bush was right, but most presidents would have had lengthy debates first.)

Another theory that has taken hold among the anti-Bush contingent is that the president is under the thumb of his vice president. In recent Bush books, Dick Cheney is indeed the most powerful influence on the president. Still, all of the serious evidence points to the fact that Bush, not Cheney, is the man who makes the ultimate calls.

Craig Unger advances an even darker theory in his book ''House of Bush, House of Saud" (Scribner, $26): that the Bush family had become so financially indebted to the Saudi royal family over the years that both the president and his father knuckled under to Saudi hatreds of Saddam Hussein. Michael Moore makes use of both Unger and his ideas in ''Fahrenheit 9/11." There is only one little problem: Unger's conclusions are so far-fetched and so critical of a Bush family that has honorably served and sacrificed for the country over three generations that no one of serious reputation takes them seriously.

Finally, one reads in some accounts that Bush thinks he is taking orders from God. There is no doubt that Bush runs one of the most overtly Christianized administrations in modern history. In his interesting new book, ''A Man of Faith" (W, $21.99), former Time correspondent David Aikman describes a president spending more time on his knees than anybody; he also quotes a senior member of the staff that there were seven Bible-study or prayer fellowship groups meeting every week around the White House. Many may find the degree of religiosity uncomfortable, but the insider accounts are reassuring on one point: Bush does not appear in any of them as a fanatic on a mission from God; rather, it seems that he tends to look at reality through a theological lens, as other presidents like Woodrow Wilson have. The difference is profoundly important.

We are thus thrown back to the original question: How does one explain the snappish decision-making by Bush, and what difference does it make? Boiling down the various accounts, the answer appears to be that amid all of his virtues -- and they are numerous -- this president often lacks an open, inquiring mind. As someone accustomed to decision-making under Presidents Nixon and Ford, O'Neill was shocked that he never heard Bush carefully analyze a complex issue, weigh opposing positions, and then reach a judicious decision. O'Neill told Suskind that Bush was living in an echo chamber of his own making, listening mostly to a tiny circle of aides and cut off from opposing perspectives. Other accounts are less harsh but point in the same direction.

To those who have observed presidential decision-making over the past decades, the Bush approach is disconcerting at best. One remembers how much trouble John F. Kennedy invited when he made a snap decision to go forward with the Bay of Pigs invasion -- and how, in the following year, he saved the world from a nuclear disaster when he and his advisers engaged in extraordinarily careful deliberations during the Cuban missile crisis. Had Kennedy chosen to follow the quick consensus that developed in favor of air strikes, history shows that the United States would have soon been hit by nuclear missiles. By sifting through alternatives a few more days, Kennedy allowed a different consensus to develop in favor of a quarantine -- and that worked! It was a model of presidential decision-making that has guided most administrations since.

Perhaps Bush will one day be vindicated by events. Perhaps, as Cheney told O'Neill, huge federal budget deficits won't matter. Perhaps Iraq will calm down and become a stable, modestly democratic country. But in the meantime, these books show that Bush has engaged in a process of decision-making that is virtually without precedent in modern White Houses -- one that not only puts his own presidency at risk but the rest of us, too.
