ON MEMOIRS

Cats, confessions, emotional cargo

By Kate Bolick  |  July 18, 2004

If you haven't read any of Kathryn Harrison's previous nine books, chances are you've heard about one in particular: her memoir about her incestuous relationship with her father. When it came out in 1997, "The Kiss" scandalized and polarized readers, for many signaling all that was wrong with memoirs in general, and society itself, for that matter (and became an instant bestseller in the process). First we were derided as a nation of talk-show narcissists addicted to purging our sins in public. Then came reality television. Then came "The Craggy Hole in My Heart and the Cat Who Fixed It," which we'll get to later.

Until the day we find covert ties between the late-1990s memoir boom and this year's makeover TV show "The Swan," however, "The Kiss" hardly warrants burning. Without a doubt it is a confessional piece of work, but it's too smart and well crafted to be self-indulgent. The truth is, "The Kiss" is a good book -- spare, lyrical, emotionally honest. As one critic put it, "It is an ugly tale, beautifully told." So how are we to account, then, for Harrison's newest memoir?

Viewed at a distance, "The Mother Knot" could double as some sly joke. Memoirists are all the time digging up the dead -- metaphorically, that is. But yet again, Harrison outpaced the pack: 20 years after her mother's burial she called up a funeral home in California and requested that they disinter her mother's body, cremate the remains, and ship the ashes across the country so that she could really bury her once and for all. (And, one wonders, write about it?)

It's a suspicious premise -- though nothing compared with incest, and nothing that a writer as sophisticated as Harrison couldn't tackle. But the reason Harrison's first memoir worked was because as it trounced decorum and upped the ante on self-revelation it also adhered to a quieter tradition: the memoir-as-novel. Harrison had previously addressed similar themes in her fiction; by turning the tables and applying a novelistic sensibility to autobiographical material she was able to transcend its shock value.

"The Mother Knot," however, is the anti-novel. It has all the narrative urgency, character development, and reliability of a diary entry. Even its alleged cause-and-effect strains credulity: Harrison stops breast-feeding her daughter, which prompts her son to have an asthma attack, which leads him to wear an oxygen mask, which causes Harrison to remember her mother's last days, thereby inspiring the revelation that she'd never truly resolved their tortured relationship, hence the disinterment.

Meanwhile, as Harrison struggles to come to terms with the mother she had and the mother she herself is, an old eating disorder reemerges, and she tests the patience of her husband, her doctors, herself.

I don't doubt the psychological plausibility of Harrison's train of associations. As her own analyst reminds her (excerpts from their conversations are interspersed throughout): "In therapy, the feelings are the facts." But useful as such logic is on the analyst's couch, it can make for claustrophobic and arid reading. Dare I say -- even boring. At times I was intrigued by the pitch of Harrison's single-minded intensity; often, I admired the clarity of her prose; not once, much to my surprise, was I moved.

What happened to Harrison's previous regard for us, her readers? Did her infatuation with her situation blind her into thinking she no longer had to work to make us care? Or did she trick herself into believing we already do? Or is "The Mother Knot" the thinking woman's self-help -- one woman's therapeutic journey distilled, abridged, and handed down to the reader without all that dreary "self-realization" pap and 12-step bother?

The antithesis, that is, of Geneen Roth's "The Craggy Hole in My Heart and the Cat Who Fixed It." If ever there were two books destined for different sections of the bookstore, surely these are they -- a fate that will forever rob idle browsers of the amusing irony that each contends with the exact same terrain: love, loss, mothering, death, eating, unabashed emotional histrionics.

Roth has made a career out of "emotional eating," leading retreats and workshops on the subject, and writing advice books, including the best-selling "When Food Is Love." In this, her seventh book, Roth describes in breezy snippets how she fell head over heels for a cat named Blanche and learned how to love. Crazy love: When Blanche sheds whiskers, Roth saves them in a box; when she can no longer shake the feeling that Blanche is dying, she consults animal psychics; Blanche's diet of organic chicken and cooked sweet potatoes is healthier than my own. Along the way she gets married (to a man, Matt), endures her father's death, learns how to accept loss, lets go of Blanche . . . It's a simple tale, simply told, a little nutty, though no more so than Harrison's book, and unabashedly of the inspirational and not literary tradition. But in it Roth makes such a likable and unobtrusively intelligent and nearly childlike play for her reader's affections that in the end I felt more for Roth and her cat than I ever did for Harrison. (And I'm not a cat person.)

There are probably various conclusions to be drawn, some reflecting poorly on me (already, in conversation with a friend, I've weathered the charge of "anti-elitism"), others pointing to the dubiousness of memoir, and, who knows, even the state of the memoir, so for now, I'll leave it at that: Against all odds, I fell for a cat.

Kate Bolick is the deputy features editor of The New York Sun. Her column appears every other month.
