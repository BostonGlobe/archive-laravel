Sweet obsession

July 13, 2004

Page 3 of 3 -- "I'd get hung up on, `Why is this lawyer self-destructing? What's going on inside of him?' So I finally decided to go to grad school in North Carolina for an MFA. Sometimes you listen to your gut. Sometimes you don't. I listened to my gut in writing this book. I almost didn't do it because I didn't think I could work on something that sounds on the face of it as silly and gimmicky as writing a book about candy. It was a close call."

Almond, who moved to Boston in 1997, no longer counts or collates or stages elaborate little battles with his candy. He does, however, notice a pattern to his candy consumption -- basically, he eats it when he's feeling really bad or really good -- and it was during a particularly pronounced episode of the former that the seeds for "Candyfreak" were planted.

"I had started a novel right after `Heavy Metal' came out and spent a year and a half working on it, and it failed," Almond says. "I was very down about that. `Heavy Metal' had been a commercial failure, even if it was an artistic success, my agent had fired me and I couldn't find a new one, and nobody wants any more stories anyway. I was bummed and I started eating a lot of candy, and I started returning to the feelings I had as a child when I felt out of sorts and like a failure. I started ideating about candy. I became insufferable at parties, and my friends started saying, `You should write a book about this. You really love it. You really know it.' And I said no, no, no, it's not a serious topic. It's pop. I'm a writer. But I started visiting factories [for a Boston Phoenix article about Boston's candy legacy] and once I started seeing the production I just stopped trying to fight it."

"Candyfreak" began as straightforward history of the candy industry. Almond spent many months writing impassioned letters to candy company presidents, flying to places like Merriam, Kan., to watch machines plink liquid chocolate and marshmallow syrup into glazed paper cups at the Sifers factory where against all odds the Valomilk (b. 1931) is still made, and throwing candy-tasting parties for his friends in the hopes of collecting witticisms to use in his book. But in the midst of all this exhaustive research and meticulous reporting Almond sensed a more personal story emerging.

"I started asking myself, `What does this book have to say other than the history of candy is really interesting?' " Almond says. "It's my part in it. I shook myself down and really let myself think about the cogent memories of my youth, and so many of them radiated around candy. My relationship to my dad and my own loneliness and making candy a kind of companion. I love my family and they know it, but there was this unhappiness, and one of my responses was to latch myself on to candy. My dad's reaction to the book was, `I didn't realize your childhood was that unhappy,' and my response to that was, `Where the hell were you?' "

Almond is inundated daily with letters and care packages and e-mails from people who can relate. He doesn't feign delighted shock at the success of his quirky book or the zealous community of candy lovers that's sprouting in its wake, because in his heart of hearts he knew that there was a nerve waiting to be hit and that he was the freak to do it. Almond is reveling in the attention, the readings, the travel, and the delicious opportunity "Candyfreak" has given him to avoid working. But there's a book of short stories due out in the spring. And a collaboration on a novel with the author Julianna Baggott. So amid the accolades and invitations, Almond is freaking on a new idea: how to force himself back into the cage, reacquaint himself with isolation and self-doubt, and write.

"It's wonderful when people like your stuff," Almond says. "But who you really are is at home with this keyboard having a chance to get to the deepest part of whatever your weird, mixed-up feelings are about life. I could keep milking the candy thing. But my job now is to find what I'm obsessive about. Otherwise it's like trying to put fake chocolate in something. It never works."
