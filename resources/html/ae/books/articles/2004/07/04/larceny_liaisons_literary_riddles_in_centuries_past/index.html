ON AUDIO / ROCHELLE O'GORMAN

Larceny, liaisons, literary riddles in centuries past

July 4, 2004

A trip into the past can make for good listening, and these three audiobooks explore some unusual territory.

''The Rule of Four," by Ian Caldwell and Dustin Thomason, reaches back the farthest in time. At the core of this novel, written by two young men, best friends for most of their lives, is a Renaissance riddle. The ''Hypnerotomachia Poliphili" is a real if most curious book published in 1499 by an anonymous author. An acrostic formed by the first, elaborately decorated letter in each chapter in the original Italian reads, in translation, ''Brother Francesco Colonna Dearly Loved Polia." But that acrostic is just the beginning, for the book is filled with clues leading to a treasure trove of art and artifacts -- if only someone can figure them out.

Princeton classmates Tom Sullivan and Paul Harris not only attempt to solve the riddles in this ancient tome, but they become completely obsessed by them. This is hardly an academic work, offering romance, murder, mayhem, and an excellent description of Ivy League life. The story is told through the eyes of Sullivan, whose father, a Renaissance scholar, introduced him to the ''Hypnerotomachia" at a young age.

Comparisons with ''The Da Vinci Code" are unavoidable, and this is better written. For that reason alone, it should not have been abridged. The nuances of the relationships in the unexpurgated book are almost completely lost. While most of the mental detective work is included, the book plays out like a thriller and not the greatly imaginative and complex novel it is when presented in full.

Josh Hamilton sounds just right -- young and educated. His narration is natural and easy. Though he does attempt accents a few times, it would have been better had he not. His delivery is unclear and muddy when attempting an older character, and his pale version of an Italian accent is startlingly bad. Thankfully, it is a very small portion of the audiobook. This does not mean you should avoid the audio version. Hamilton, for the most part, is quite enjoyable, as is the story. It is just not what it could -- and should -- be.

Move ahead to 18th-century Venice for a touching true romance in ''A Venetian Affair,'' by Andrea Di Robilant. The story began when Italian journalist Di Robilant found a cache of letters in the attic of his family palazzo. He used them, along with painstaking research of the era, to reconstruct the hypnotic story of a forbidden love affair between his patrician ancestor Andrea Memmo and the beautiful, illegitimate Anglo-Venetian Giustiniana Wynne.

The details of Venice and London are bewitching. Casanova was a friend of the couple, and such contemporaries as Madame Pompadour routinely make their way into the tale. Aside from information about contemporary mores, and in one instance medical practices, we have a heart-breaking tale of two people kept apart by family and society. The pair's letters reveal conspiracies, complications, and confidences that would rival any modern soap opera. Just as fascinating, however, is the history of their later lives. Memmo went on to a distinguished diplomatic career, and Wynne lived a life that was truly ahead of her time.

The problem with this production is that the narrators do not match the passion of the authors' words. Paul Hecht is pleasant enough technically; his diction and voice are both clear. Though not terribly indistinguishable from dozens of other narrators, he is perfectly serviceable. However, he completely lacks passion. The same can be said of Lisette Lecat, who reads Wynne's letters. She has a lovely voice, lovely diction, and sounds like she is reading an important literary tract, not letters oozing with the heat of first love. Jeff Woodman, who reads Memmo's letters, is more passionate, but he sounds flatly American, and his voice is too high for the lothario Memmo.

And now we jump ahead to London, 1875, and ''Montmorency," a stylish thriller by Eleanor Updale, aimed at grades 6 through 10, but fascinating enough to keep this listener glued to her stereo. In fact, it is hard to believe that this novel is aimed at teenagers, as it is both sophisticated and earthy in ways that seem terribly grown-up.

Montmorency is the name adopted by a criminal mastermind who learns the details of the then modern London sewage system during his service as a medical guinea pig while a ''guest" of Her Majesty. Upon his release from prison, he uses the underground tunnels as a means of gaining entry to the homes of wealthy citizens.

To help line his pockets, Montmorency adopts an alter ego named Scarper, who commits his crimes and acts as his servant -- at least to prying eyes. Montmorency has finer things in mind. Educating himself about culture and clothing, he takes rooms in a fancy hotel and moves into a higher realm of society, financed by Scarper's burglaries. As he learns compassion and duty while living as Montmorency, Scarper takes more and more of a back seat as this device of hidden identity works itself out.

British actor Stephen Fry's narration is nothing short of masterful. His voice has presence. He sounds urbane and composed as Montmorency, working-class and slightly excitable as Scarper. Fry gives every character in the production a unique and interesting accent, and his acting skills, so finely honed from years of experience in film, theater, television, and radio, grace this production with both subtlety and depth. Jolly good work.

Rochelle O'Gorman is publisher and editor-in-chief of audiobookcafe.com, an online magazine featuring daily reviews, interviews, and articles about the audiobook industry.
