POP LIT

A born-again's battle; a family's eventful century

By Diane White  |  July 11, 2004

It's a pleasure to open a book and find an authentic and appealing voice. These two novels are very different, but they have in common narrators whose voices ring true. The stories they tell are funny, poignant, and completely human. They'd make good vacation reading, or simply good reading anytime.

Sarah Dunn's first novel, ''The Big Love," isn't another run-of-the-mill romance. The dust jacket is pink, and the theme is familiar -- boy dumps girl, girl finds self -- but Dunn's story is fresh, her writing clever, her characters genuine.

Alison Hopkins's boyfriend of four years, Tom, pops out to fetch some Dijon mustard just before guests are due to arrive for dinner. An hour later he calls from a pay phone to say that he isn't coming back, that he doesn't have the mustard, and that he is in love with somebody else. ''And we had company!" is Alison's first reaction. ''I was raised in such a way that you didn't do anything weird or impolite or even remotely human when you had company."

The way she was raised is at the heart of this novel. Alison, who narrates her story, grew up wanting to be Nora Ephron, a goal difficult to achieve because, she confesses, not only is she not Jewish, she is ''the opposite of Jewish. I was raised as an evangelical Christian, a real born-again tribe which completely lacks a comedic tradition and is almost entirely missing an intellectual one."

Alison tries to overcome her comedy-deprived origins in a column she writes for the Philadelphia Times, an alternative weekly, but she can't completely shed her ''uptight bourgeois suburban normalness" along with ''some of the more glaring aftereffects" of her Christian upbringing. She was a virgin until she was 25, when after months of chaste dating she managed to seduce her reluctant boyfriend, who soon after announced that he was gay. And even after 11 years of cut-rate psychotherapy, she still feels guilty because she failed to keep a church-camp pledge she made at 13 not to engage in sex before marriage.

At 32, Alison remains inexperienced, compared with her friends. When Tom leaves her for his beautiful former girlfriend Kate, Alison takes to her bed, alone, brooding over his perfidy. ''She's like a drug," Kate's ex-lover tells Alison, plunging her further into gloom. Then a thought occurs: ''I might finally get to have sex with somebody who a) wasn't Tom, and b) isn't gay. And the prospect didn't entirely lack appeal."

Alison takes advantage of her new freedom and explores the possibilities of being single, including a dalliance with her good-looking new editor. Dunn is a seasoned sitcom writer. Her wit would be enough to set ''The Big Love" apart from the chick-lit pack, but it is her unsparing, hilarious, touching account of Alison's struggle with her faith that make this novel a special pleasure.

David Haynes's ''The Full Matilda" is an engaging novel that follows four generations of an African-American family through profound social and personal changes. Haynes, the author of several novels and short story collections, is a graceful writer who clearly has great affection for the characters. The story spans a century, but the narrative never seems hasty or forced. Each of his characters has a unique and unmistakable voice. ''The Full Matilda" isn't one of those seemingly endless, leaden, multigenerational sagas. It's a sprightly, amiable story that comes alive in countless precise, well-chosen details.

Matilda Housewright was born early in the last century, and grew up ''backstairs" in a grand Washington, D.C., mansion in which her father, Jacob, served as majordomo to one of the capital's most powerful senators. Under their father's direction, Matilda and her brother, Martin, learn the myriad details of managing and maintaining a gracious residence. The nature of the family business, Matilda notes at the outset of her story, has always been about being ''mostly invisible." It was a lesson her father drummed into the young Housewrights, as much to protect them from being hurt as to hone their skills.

Invisibility doesn't come easily to Matilda. She is willful and imperious, a perfectionist. But she can bend, if she must. She is a pragmatist above all. ''I have always believed in doing what works," she says at one point. It's a variation on her father's maxim: ''You do what you have to do."

After their father's death, Matilda encourages Martin to open a catering business. They work together briefly, but Matilda's uncompromising ways are more than good-natured Martin and his much-put-upon (by Matilda) fiance, Evie, can endure. Martin cuts Matilda out of the business, although he continues to support her financially.

''The Full Matilda" is told in several voices: Matilda's, Martin's, and those of Matilda's nephews and grand-nephews. Their different points of view compose a vibrant portrait of a remarkable family living in challenging times. Matilda, though, is always the focus, the proper maiden aunt, maintaining her standards through civil rights marches and black power and interracial marriage, as the family fortunes prosper beyond Martin's imagining.

Toward the end of the novel, Matilda reveals just how much of a pragmatist she is, how far she was willing to go, once, to make things work. The revelation is disturbing, and it's shocking because it seems so out of character. However, given the racial climate of the time and place, and what was at stake, the choice she made is understandable.

Diane White writes every month about new light and popular fiction.
