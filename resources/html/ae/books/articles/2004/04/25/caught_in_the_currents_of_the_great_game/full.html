A READING LIFE

Caught in the currents of the Great Game

By Katherine A. Powers  |  April 25, 2004

Everyone who reads has a few books, and even whole writers, for which and whom they have an ungovernable enthusiasm. One person I know, for instance, rarely fails to bring up the book "Nansen," by E. E. Reynolds (1932). I have a copy of this, once urgently pressed upon me, but, in my shabby, ungrateful way, I have never got much beyond the first sentence, which warns, "So varied in their nature and widespread in their range were Nansen's interests, it would tax the abilities of man to deal adequately with all of them."

It would be taxing, too, to deal adequately with my own enthusiasms, but one of them is Ben Macintyre. Here is a writer who seems as taken as I am with crackpottery, delusion, grandiosity, chicanery, and impersonation, but who manages to write about it all with amused restraint, without, that is, the air of the ogler. I became a devotee in 1992 with his first book, "Forgotten Fatherland: The Search for Elisabeth Nietzsche." This is an account of Friedrich Nietzsche's unsavory sister's attempt, with her equally unsavory husband, to set up an Aryan colony in Paraguay and her later manufacture of a proto-Nazi philosophy out of her brother's work. Macintyre's next book, "The Napoleon of Crime: The Life and Times of Adam Worth, Master Thief" (1997), is a biography of a man of many identities, prodigious forgeries, and spectacular heists who fell in love with a stolen portrait. His third, "The Englishman's Daughter: A True Story of Love and Betrayal in World War I" (2001), relates the fate of four English soldiers who were hidden for two years by French villagers from their town's German occupiers. Now, published this month -- and how timely it seems -- is Macintyre's fourth book, "The Man Who Would Be King: The First American in Afghanistan" (Farrar, Straus & Giroux, $25).

The man in question was Josiah Harlan, the probable inspiration for Rudyard Kipling's short story upon which John Huston's famous movie, starring Sean Connery, is based. In reality -- as it might be termed -- he was "Prince of Ghor," "Paramount Chief of the Hazarajat," "the Highly Stationed One Equipped with Ardour and Might," "Paragon of the Magnificent Grandees," "Companion of the Imperial Stirrup," "Nearest Friend of Shah Shoojah al-Moolk, King of Afghanistan," and a Quaker, a Freemason, and a self-styled doctor from Pennsylvania who found inspiration in the career of Alexander the Great. Macintyre has been able to piece together this never-before-told story by a great archival find: Harlan's own unpublished autobiography, letters, and other papers, which had been lying dormant in a little Pennsylvania museum.

Harlan's adventures were prompted by a maverick spirit, a good deal of reading, and a thwarted romance. After service with the East India Company, he inveigled his way into the good graces of Shah Shujah al-Moolkan, an exiled Afghan potentate, and, in 1827, set off with his dog Dash to arrange for the shah's restoration. Reflecting upon this later, he wrote, "I had just stepped within the threshold of active life. . . . Completely alone, companionless and solitary, I plunged into the indistinct expanse of futurity . . . which like the obscurity of fate is invoked in the deep darkness of time."

Out of that darkness, what became known as the Great Game began to coalesce, with Harlan at its center, though he was more ignored than otherwise by its European players, Britain and Russia. In the course of 18 years of migrating alliances and intrepid deeds, of passing through territories "that valued hospitality and revenge above all else," Harlan became transformed from adventurer to astute player in Afghan affairs and, finally, to Prince of Ghor. In tracing Harlan's Afghan career, Macintyre summons up a brilliant cast of characters, each fleshed out with a novelist's adeptness and his own panache. There are princes of great personal complexity and advisers of luxuriant idiosyncrasy, including an alchemist. There is an excellent madman ("a fizzing bundle of pure polemic" and "a ranting little blob of a man"), as well as a marmot -- and, as we move farther into the book toward the British disaster of the first Afghan campaign of 1839 to 1842, there are Englishmen and Scotsmen of such obliviousness that it has taken Americans more than 150 years to surpass it.

"I have seen this country," Harlan wrote later of his beloved Afghanistan, "sacred to the harmony of hallowed solitude, desecrated by the rude intrusion of senseless stranger boors, vile in habits, infamous in vulgar tastes, the prompt and apathetic instruments of master minds, callous leaders in the sanguinary march of heedless conquests, who crushed the feeble heart and hushed the merry voice of mirth, hilarity and joy." He watched with disgust as the arrogant, deluded British assumed control through a puppet king -- none other than Harlan's erstwhile nearest friend, Shah Shujah. Utterly insensible of the temper of the Afghan populace, the British envoy boasted that the restored shah "was greeted with 'feelings very nearly mounting to adoration.' " In reality, as Macintyre points out, the attitude "ranged from apathy to extreme hostility." By the time the British had made it to Kabul -- with their bales of cigars, pipes of port, hunting dogs, and other requirements -- and had placed Shujah on the throne, they were even more thoroughly despised; though as before, they were not remotely aware of it.

Model though he may have been for Kipling's Daniel Dravot, Harlan did not finish up dead with his head in a bag, nor was he one of the 15,000 English men and women who were expelled from Kabul in January 1842 to die in the snows of the mountain passes. Rather, he landed back in the United States, holding for a short time the position of "camel consultant to the U.S. government." In 1861, he recruited and trained his own Union regiment, Harlan's Light Cavalry, the story of which Macintyre also tells with unflagging lan.

There is so much tragedy, cruelty, and general badness afoot in this book that I wonder how to explain how really funny it is. But it is: sometimes bleakly so, as when the British move into Kabul, bringing "their wives, the formidable memsahibs, the better to pretend that Afghanistan was really part of India," and, at other times, absurdly, as when Harlan fondly believes he is fooling everyone, traipsing over mountain and through defile, disguised as a holy man.

Katherine A. Powers, a writer and critic, lives in Cambridge. Her column appears on alternate Sundays. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@earthlink.net.
