For children

To the blues' true beat; a gift for invention

By Liz Rosenberg  |  April 25, 2004

Blues Journey
Written by Walter Dean Myers
Illustrated by Christopher Myers
Holiday House, 48 pp., $18.95

The Wishing Bone and Other Poems
Written by Stephen Mitchell
Illustrated by Tom Pohrt
Candlewick, 51 pp., $16.99

Walter Dean Myers's book-length poem "Blues Journey'' -- exquisitely illustrated by his son, Christopher Myers -- is a profound tribute to the blues, from the midnight-colored endpapers, the fine introductory note offering a history of the blues, and the shadowy blues that alternately deepen and brighten each picture to a useful "blues glossary" at the back, and, of course, the poem itself. "Blues Journey" is a marvelous, sad, unflinching contemporary blues poem without a false note anywhere, and Myers's love of the blues sings in every line: "Blues, blues, blues,/ blues, what you mean to me?/Are you my pain and misery,/or my sweet, sweet company?"

This is no watered-down lyric aimed at children, but the real thing -- "Said I hollered to my woman,/she was across the way/I said I loved her truly,/she said, 'It got to be that way.' " "Blues Journey" is wonderfully well suited to teenagers, who can relate to the edginess, the sadness, and the long complicated history here evoked in words and images.

There's deep sorrow at work in this poem -- "Blues, won't you free me,/let all this suffering cease?" -- and the distinct rasping bass of scraping bottom: "Pain will push and poke you,/despair will scrape the bone/Misery loves company,/blues can live alone." There's the horror of a "strange fruit" lynching, and the jubilation of singing -- singing the blues. Myers echoes the best of old blues classics, brings it on this journey, and gives it his own twist and turn. This is how the blues work, looking back and forward at the same time, a call and answer to the "call and response" of the African slave songs, borrowing from European, African, and American forms. In his introductory note, Myers writes, "I've heard the blues all my life and I love this rich music. I also love the lyrics as a poetry form, which is what ['Blues Journey'] is all about, invoking the heartfelt feelings of those who sing and of those who take the journey just by listening."

"Blues Journey" is the kind of book that merits reading, close reading, and rereading. It sounds beautiful out loud, and it is beautiful read in silence, alone. It's a remarkable step along the great blues journey, not a strained note, not one wrong stroke of the brush.

Author Stephen Mitchell has had a long and illustrious career in adult literature. He has translated, among other major important works, "The Bhagavad Gita," the poems of Rilke, and the "Tao Te Ching."

In the very first poem of "The Wishing Bone and Other Poems," Mitchell throws down the gauntlet toward perhaps the most charming poet who ever wrote for children, the equally versatile Robert Louis Stevenson. Here's Mitchell's opening shot from "When I Grow Up":

When I grow up and I am wise, I'll know if needles shut their eyes, If shadows dance, if worms have knees, If bears say "Bless you" when they sneeze. When I grow up and I am old, I'll know where secret tales are told, Where dreams are born, where dragons fly, Where ladders lean against the sky. And when they think that I am dead, I'll know who puts the moon to bed, Who lights the stars, who lifts the sun, Who leads the planets, one by one.

There are echoes here of Stevenson's famous "Envoi" from "A Child's Garden of Verses," but Mitchell stakes out this classical poetic territory as his own. His "when they think that I am dead,/I'll know who puts the moon to bed" shows what he does so brilliantly -- balancing between sorrow and merriment, lightness and weight. In doing so he creates what may be a true new classic in children's poetry. "The Wishing Bone and Other Poems" -- which has already won this year's Lee Bennett Hopkins Prize, and is headed for who knows what other honors -- is charmingly illustrated by Tom Pohrt, who mingles a fresh, soft, classical-looking English countryside palette with a strong dash of wit, especially in the longer narrative poems. Pictures are liberally sprinkled through the book, some occupying a full page, others a half, a quarter, or less. The visual images waltz alongside the poems, creating a rhythm that enchants both the eye and the ear.

In an era of tone-deaf poetry, Mitchell possesses a golden ear for sound. He plays joyfully, inventively with words, with rhythms and rhyme. Like all really good poets, Mitchell stretches the language, inventing new words, cleverly interposing long, elaborate ones, clipping some phrases, attenuating others. Many poems possess the sly weight of a Zen story, seemingly light but rich with possibilities, as in this triplet from the poem "Questions" : "What language is the letter Y?/Who taught the hummingbird to fly?/When you are dreaming, where am I?"

Others end with a touch of melancholy. Mitchell has the clear genius for comic metaphor -- a light leap into the ozone in the midst of a stanza, "My mind lay flat before me like/A piece of buttered toast." And there are moments of paintbox-bright beauty: "I see her purple smile among/The lemon-yellow sheep."

All of the poems in "The Wishing Bone" are musical, playful, dramatic, funny, and slyly philosophical -- "The future is more flexible,/More marvelous and vast,/Than we can ever really know./(We only know the past.)" It is the kind of book that comes along once or twice in a century -- if we are very lucky.

Liz Rosenberg has edited four award-winning anthologies of poems for young readers and teenagers. A fifth collection is scheduled next year from Houghton Mifflin. She teaches English and creative writing at the State University of New York at Binghamton.
