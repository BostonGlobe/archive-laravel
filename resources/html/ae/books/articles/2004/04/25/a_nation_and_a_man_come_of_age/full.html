A nation, and a man, come of age

War, betrayal, and torture in a besieged 1942 Singapore

By Judy Budz  |  April 25, 2004

Breaking the Tongue
By Vyvyane Loh
Norton, 405 pp., $24.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Framed in tortured silence, Vyvyane Loh's "Breaking the Tongue" tells the story of Singapore's fall to the Japanese in 1942. The British surrendered the city to the invaders after an intensive air campaign destroyed the British naval base at Sembawang, the Gibraltar of the East, thereby demoralizing the garrison. These troops, like the expatriate British businessmen running Singapore's economy, were made up of "expendable" men too incompetent to succeed at home.

The novel describes literal torture, for the plot is framed by the brutal Japanese interrogation of Claude Lim, the teenage son of an ethnic Chinese clerk at the British bank. Claude's captors carve a map of their invasion on his face, but his "body" struggles to remain silent. To avoid speech, Claude sends his mind into retreat, focusing on the years preceding Singapore's fall when he and his family felt protected by the British colonial government.

Yet "the mind's" memories make it clear that Claude has undergone a metaphorical torture more painful than his physical suffering. His ambitious father has raised him in a limbo of disconnection between his Chinese body and his British manners. Humphrey Lim insists that the family take tea in intense tropical heat. Only Grandma Siok actually speaks Chinese. Claude attends an English-speaking school where his classmates are unable to organize their firefighting brigade as efficiently as their Chinese counterparts. Claude's mother openly pursues white men, whether clerks or managers, but Claude is deeply aroused by the naked body of a beautiful Chinese girl whose family is squatting outside their fence. Peeking, he blushes, but the girl stares back defiantly.

Clearly, Loh intends Claude's "deculturalization" to be a metaphor for the "deculturalization" of Singapore itself. For a century, the port city had been controlled by British interests, but its population was a melting pot of ethnic Chinese, Indians, Thais, and Malaysians. And by the 1930s, this tropical pot was boiling. Upwardly mobile Chinese bureaucrats were in love with British "tradition, ceremony, flourishes, titles, and aristocracy." They scorned the other overseas Chinese for their "flag touting" and "Communist leaning." Meanwhile, the servants were deserting, the squatters were gathering, and the London government ignored the Japanese threat.

The novel is built around Claude, but his opposite is the beautiful young nurse Han Ling-li, born in China and raised in Singapore. Ling-li has no doubts about her own culture; she is Chinese. Her hero is General Yue Fei, a Southern Song Dynasty warrior who traveled to battle with the words "Serve Your Country" carved in his back. Ling-li's tongue is decidedly not broken. She speaks Hokkien and Mandarin, Malay and English, and these language skills cause her to be recruited as a spy for the British. Sadly, it is not possible to be a woman warrior in Singapore, however, and Ling-li is targeted by a female Fifth Columnist who envies the attention paid Ling-li by a young man.

Claude meets Ling-li during the most chaotic days of the invasion, when Japanese bombs are destroying the city. Drunken British troops are roaming the streets. Most of the upper-class British and Chinese have left the city, some for Australia and others for the countryside. Claude's family has withdrawn to a country cousin's. Claude, left alone in the house, finds himself nursing a family acquaintance, Jack Winchester. A lowly bank clerk, Winchester enjoyed the good life in Singapore too much to catch the boat back to England with his colleagues. Now he is ill, and Claude's English manners demand that he risk his own life by nursing the white man.

Loh leaves no doubt that the British were incredibly arrogant in their assessment of Singapore's impregnability and Japanese force. They misread the competence of their own troops and misled the citizens in daily newspaper stories. These civilians blindly trusted their news sources and ignored street rumors, while traitors and spies manipulated events.

But "Breaking the Tongue" is not a war novel. It contains little detail about bombing or spying, and even the Fifth Columnist is motivated by misguided love rather than patriotic zeal. There is no discussion of battle strategy, despite the quotes from the Chinese strategist Sun Tzu. Nor is this a novel about the home front, even though Loh describes the Lim family and, sometimes, Ling-li's activist uncle.

Instead, Loh has a more modern, perhaps more philosophic interest. She is writing about the collision of Western manners and Eastern values, the intersection of language and identity. This emphasis is made clear early in the book, when Claude's grandmother takes him to a park. She shows him the Ten Courts of Hell, a series of tableaux that make flesh what the old woman calls the "real Chinese" morality. Rob and cause pain and be flung into a volcano; evade taxes and have your body "ground to dust between two boulders." Claude is stunned by the violence of these scenes. The most unbearable, however, is the image of a man whose tongue is being torn from his mouth. Unable to bear the "sheer familiarity" of the scene, thinking of his own "disintegration, decomposition," Claude faints. His own tongue, after all, feels similarly broken.

Under torture, Claude thinks about denotation and connotation; he remembers his father's mailbox, name written in gold. He wants to puzzle out Chinese calligraphy. This concern with language is what we expect from a novelist, and interestingly, some of the book's last pages contain passages written, literally, in Chinese. Loh expects that we, like Claude, will understand these symbols without translation. Even readers who prefer translations will find much to admire in this novel.

Judy Budz is a professor of English at Fitchburg State College.
