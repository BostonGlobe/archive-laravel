Split decision

By Eric Arnesen, Globe Staff  |  April 25, 2004

All Deliberate Speed:Reflections on the First Half Century of Brown v. Board of Education
By Charles J. Ogletree Jr.
Norton, 365 pp., illustrated, $25.95

Silent Covenants: Brown v. Board of Education and the Unfulfilled Hopes for Racial Reform
By Derrick Bell
Oxford University, 230 pp., $25

Fifty years ago next month, the US Supreme Court shocked the nation with its unanimous decision in Brown v. Board of Education. Racial segregation and the doctrine of "separate but equal" had long been the law of the land, sanctioned by an earlier Supreme Court in its 1896 case of Plessy v. Ferguson. To many Americans, Southern segregation had taken on the aura of permanent custom, something so ingrained in the nation's history and its people's psyche as to be timeless, a fundamental part of the American way of life.

Determined and dedicated civil rights attorneys and activists had long dissented from this view, and in 1954 the Supreme Court finally agreed with them. Separate was tantamount to unequal, the justices concluded; because it deprived black children of equal educational opportunities, it had no place in public education. The historic decision marked the beginning of the end of legalized segregation in the United States.

In their deeply pessimistic books, legal scholars Charles Ogletree Jr. and Derrick Bell take stock of the changes in American race relations since 1954 and find the legacy of Brown troubling. Brown was "perhaps the most significant case on race in American history," Ogletree concludes in "All Deliberate Speed," but severe racial segregation is "still with us." Brown was, Bell writes in "Silent Covenants," a "magnificent mirage" that fell "far short of achieving its goals."

Ogletree and Bell revisit the failure of public school integration. The Supreme Court's 1955 follow-up decision rejected immediate integration and allowed Southern school districts to make only a "reasonable start" and to proceed with "all deliberate speed." Anti-integrationists interpreted these phrases as approval to go slowly or resist integration altogether. Many districts closed their public schools; white mobs attacked black children and their families, who attempted to make Brown a reality. Only a few districts engaged in token desegregation.

It wasn't until the late 1960s that the Justice Department and the courts aggressively enforced Southern school integration, but those real gains proved temporary. Since then, a political and judicial backlash has allowed public education across the nation to revert to its segregated norm. White parents enrolled their children in private schools or moved to the suburbs, leaving urban schools the domain of poorer minority students. The Supreme Court, in its 1974 Milliken v. Bradley decision, blocked busing across district lines and, in Ogletree's words, allowed white suburban communities to "escape the mandate of Brown." Fifty years after Brown, large numbers of white and minority students attend racially exclusive schools.

This much is familiar, as is the often schematic (and at times simplistic) history of racial oppression that both Ogletree and Bell provide. But what makes their contributions distinctive is their combination of autobiography and political analysis. Ogletree, for instance, movingly recounts his schooling in a poor and largely minority community in Merced, Calif., in the years immediately after Brown, his years at Stanford University as an early affirmative-action recruit, and his attendance at Harvard Law School during the years of the South Boston riots against busing. Interspersed with his careful explorations of the Bakke so-called reverse-discrimination case and the recent University of Michigan affirmative-action cases are lengthy digressions on his work as a public defender, his involvement in preparing Anita Hill for her testimony during the Clarence Thomas confirmation hearings, his admiration for Thurgood Marshall, and his intense dislike of Thomas's legal philosophy. Although interesting, these chapters serve more to illuminate Ogletree's political evolution than they do to cast light on the legacy of Brown.

Bell, always a self-consciously provocative writer, remains true to form in "Silent Covenants." Decades ago as an attorney on the front lines of educational desegregation litigation, he, "like most civil rights professionals, believed with an almost religious passion that the Brown decision was the equivalent of the Holy Grail of racial justice." Today, he questions the value of his efforts.

In his most creative chapter, Bell imagines an alternative Brown decision that would have upheld segregation but insisted on the equalization of resources between blacks and whites. Had that road been followed, he suggests, black children might have gotten the education they needed and deserved. Bell intends to challenge established modes of thinking, and he succeeds. But his own perspective suggests, rightly, that separate but genuinely equal would have proved to be as elusive as integration.

Much of American history, he argues, can be reduced to two themes: "silent covenants," in which whites always settle their differences with one another by sacrificing black rights, and "interest convergence theory," according to which black rights are "recognized and protected when and only so long as policy makers perceive that such advances will further interests that are their primary concern." These formulations oversimplify the complex history of racial oppression and whatever imperfect progress has been made toward racial equality. There are, of course, elements of truth in this reductionist model. But while it has the advantage of clarity, Bell's judgments are sweeping and categorical, grounded in his profound pessimism and a selective reading of American history.

Concluding that racism is "permanent in this country," Bell cautions today's advocates of civil rights to "pause before seeking judicial help in the resolution of serious racial issues." He and Ogletree draw inspiration from small-scale community efforts to build charter schools and after-school programs for black children. Ogletree also endorses the movement for reparations as a legitimate "response to the failure to realize fully the promise of Brown." Absent in both works is any serious consideration of an alternative public policy designed to reallocate resources and address discrimination and pervasive poverty. Indeed, both books are, in their different ways, testaments to their authors' loss of faith in public policy and the integrationist ideal. Given the sketchy alternatives they lay out, perhaps there is reason for even more pessimism.
