BOOK REVIEW

Spillane rises to the occasion in 'Down There'

By Richard Dyer, Globe Staff  |  April 6, 2004

Something’s Down There, By Mickey Spillane, Simon & Schuster, 273 pp., $24

Every so often Mickey Spillane, now 85, knocks off fishing in Murrells Inlet, S. C., long enough to write another novel. "Something's Down There" is his 53d book since "I, the Jury" changed the detective story forever back in 1947.

This writer has been reading Spillane since the time Miss Jean Harrington sent him home from the Carnegie Library to get a note from his mother before the librarian would permit him to check out "Kiss Me, Deadly." Spillane's most popular novels center on Mike Hammer, private eye, but the novels outside the series are not very different in character.

The hero of Spillane's latest, Mako Hooker, is a government operative living in retirement on the Caribbean island of Peolle, a place that is not on the map, as it happens, although it figures in Spillane's novel for young readers, "The Ship That Never Was," and it lies in the Bermuda Triangle. Like his creator in a series of commercials, Mako drinks Miller Lite, and here is his psychological profile: "He was alone, but he wasn't lonely. He would hurt, but he wouldn't complain. He had a shell about him you couldn't see yet knew was there, an invisible armor he didn't want but had to have to protect others from becoming like him." Sound familiar?

There are strange happenings down in the Bermuda Triangle. Something is taking big bites out of fishing boats -- an "eater," as the natives think, a giant prehistoric supershark that has somehow survived old mines from World War II floating to the surface? Nobody knows, but the situation is threatening, and a lot of people are curious, including US agents, wealthy tourists, an opportunistic film company, and a former gangster.

Of course there are a couple of gorgeous women around, and Mako can't be sure whether either of them is on his side.

Chana Sterling, who works for the government, once put a bullet in him. "He simply let his eyes slide up to the dirty old black bar mirror and he saw Chana at the same time she saw him and their eyes met and for a second he wished he had been packing his .45 and the piece was in his hand with the hammer back so he could turn and shoot her guts right out of her beautiful belly and it would finally be all over with for all time. . . . She was almost ready to move before she remembered there was no way she could take him the way she could almost anybody else."

And then there's the wealthy Judy Durant. "The light from behind silhouetted her body under the yellow silk sarong, and when Mako turned to look at her he felt as if he had been punched in the gut. It had happened before, so he realized what it was and let the old armor plate settle over the part of his mind that was being targeted."

What's happening is all a big jigsaw puzzle, as Mako realizes. "If you can separate out the parts that make four ninety-degree angles, you have the corners and everything else fits inside them. They were the easiest parts. The real work is sorting out the bits and pieces that make up the body of the picture. Mako could mentally visualize the corners, now he had to start arranging all the loose parts into reasonable order."

That process takes him the better part of 300 pages, and it is not the most interesting aspect of the book. Spillane goes into mechanistic Tom Clancy mode to work out the details, and we prefer him in Spillane mode. Fortunately much of the book couldn't have been written by anyone other than a mellowed Spillane -- but then the passage of time has mellowed Spillane's earliest and darkest books; in the world of Dr. Hannibal Lecter, Mike Hammer sounds almost wholesome.

"Something's Down There" boasts a fair amount of action, double-dealing, tough talk, and there are some satisfyingly nasty characters who get what they deserve as the plot unpredictably swerves. But there's also reassuringly a sense of place, a love and respect for the sea and for the men who go down to it in fishing boats. The sex doesn't go much further than an arm squeeze, a kiss, and, as Mako dances with a "doll," "muscles in her stomach rippled against his hands, saying quiet things that he could hardly believe." Spillane has always said he's a writer, not an author; he likes to quip that more people like salted peanuts than caviar. But the man who wrote that dancing sentence is a damn good writer who knows his salted peanuts.
