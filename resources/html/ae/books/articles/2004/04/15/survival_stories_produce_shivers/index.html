BOOK REVIEWS

Survival stories produce shivers

By Jerry Ackerman, Globe Correspondent  |  April 15, 2004

Four Against the Arctic: Shipwrecked for Six Years at the Top of the World, By David Roberts, Simon & Schuster, 320 pp., $25

Ada Blackjack: A True Story of Survival in the Arctic, By Jennifer Niven, Hyperion, 448 pp., illustrated, $24.95

David Roberts is an experienced adventure writer with a penchant for cold-weather survival tales. But even he was caught short when he met up with the one he tells in "Four Against the Arctic: Shipwrecked for Six Years at the Top of the World," which he calls "the most astounding survival story I had ever read or heard about."

The story begins in 1743, when four Russian walrus hunters are stranded on remote and barren Edge Island, one of the cluster of icy islands known as Svalbard (the best known of which is Spitsbergen), lying just 600 miles or so below the North Pole.

An 11-month-long winter was setting in, and their small boat had been blown off course and threatened by pack ice. Going ashore to seek shelter, the four found a crude wood hut built by earlier travelers. They went to tell their shipmates, only to find that the boat was gone -- vanished, without a trace.

Except for the polar bears, foxes, and reindeer that abound on Edge Island, they were alone. For how long they couldn't know. And yet they survived, three of them at least, not for just that winter but for six more.

They carried the barest of armaments (a musket, 12 balls and 12 charges of powder, a knife, and an ax), a tinderbox, and 20 pounds of flour. They killed 12 reindeer.

Their powder gone, they used driftwood to fashion lances, arrows, and bows, stringing the bows with reindeer tendon. For almost 2,300 days, they ate only venison, fox, and bear meat. When their clothes wore out, they made new ones from reindeer skins.

The one man lost apparently died of scurvy. Then, only by chance, a passing boat spotted the survivors as they were facing a seventh winter in isolation. The captain took them aboard and back to their native northern Russia, but charged them for their passage.

These are the bare bones of the story Roberts stumbled across in 1997, leading to what he calls an "obsession" to learn more. And what makes this book work is his decision to embellish this story with the details of a personal search.

Following the scent, Roberts takes us along as he deals with personable bureaucrats who rule over Russian academia, then moves on to the cold, coastal village of Mezen, and a poignant meeting with two elderly widows who represent the last in the line of two of the survivors. Finally, we go with Roberts to a small island just off the larger Edge Island, where his diligence determines the sailors actually lived out their ordeal.

Roberts admits his quest added little to the story he first heard, apart from his apparent discovery of the place where the men survived. But faced with what he calls "the damnable ambiguity of truth," he also resists the siren lure of speculation, preserving the original story while adding his own.

On a parallel course, Jennifer Niven's "Ada Blackjack: A True Story of Survival in the Arctic" follows five adventurers in 1921 to another remote land, barren Wrangell Island, 200 miles northeast of Siberia, and surely as stark a place as Edge Island.

Blackjack, an Eskimo hired for $50 a month to sew and cook for the expedition, was the only survivor. Three of the other four were lost after setting off across pack ice to seek help. The fourth, too weakened by scurvy to make that trip, died on the island.

Along with bravado and fear, Niven's story has a dark, mean underside. It's the bitter truth that this expedition should never have taken place. It was the brainchild of an erstwhile Arctic adventurer, Vilhjalmur Stefansson, a native Canadian who thought that Wrangell Island, while ostensibly held by Russia, should be part of his homeland. When Ottawa turned him down he decided to stake a claim for Britain.

Of course, Stefansson didn't intend to do this himself. He made his living lecturing about "the friendly Arctic," promoting the notion that polar regions were ripe for settlement. Meeting three young men seeking adventure in their own lives, he signed them on to do the deed, along with a 20-year-old student from Toronto whose citizenship might help legitimize the claim.

In hindsight their fate seems almost inevitable, and Blackjack's survival just short of a miracle. Niven is lavish with the details, working with diaries, letters, and memorabilia kept by the families of the lost men, as well as news accounts, official documents, and the words of Blackjack's son, eager in his old age to tell his mother's story.

But alongside Roberts's story, this one pales. Perhaps it's because Niven's style is more journalistic and less personal. Or maybe it's because, in the spirit of journalistic narrative, she holds back, failing to voice proper rage toward Stefansson, a snake-oil salesman to the core. His actions today would be an indictable offense.

Jerry Ackerman is a freelance writer and former Globe reporter and editor.
