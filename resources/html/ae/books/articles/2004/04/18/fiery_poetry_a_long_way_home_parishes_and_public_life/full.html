ON NEW ENGLAND

Fiery poetry; a long way home; parishes and public life

By Michael Kenney  |  April 18, 2004

John Greenleaf Whittier: Selected Poems
Edited by Brenda Wineapple
Library of America, 187 pp., $20

ADVERTISEMENT

Walking to Vermont: From Times Square Into the Green Mountains — A Homeward Adventure
By Christopher S. Wren
Simon & Schuster, 273 pp., $24

Ballots and Bibles: Ethnic Politics and the Catholic Church in Providence
By Evelyn Savidge Sterne
Cornell University, 294 pp., illustrated,$34.95

In her introduction to "John Greenleaf Whittier: Selected Poems," literary biographer Brenda Wineapple writes that for her, growing up in Haverhill -- and in eighth grade at the John Greenleaf Whittier School and being "force-fed" his winter-idyll "Snowbound" -- the poet "seemed so, well, local."

Nothing the matter with "local" in a review of books of local and regional interest. But as Wineapple has discovered, there is a freshness and honesty to be found in a revisited Whittier.

Among New England's academic (i.e., Harvard) poets, Whittier stands out as a hardscrabble farmer's son who discovered poetry pretty much on his own. And he was, also, "a crack political operator," helping to found the pre-GOP Liberty Party and running for Congress on its anti-slavery ticket.

His poems, Wineapple comments, "were meant to be read, sung, shouted or printed on broadsides." And it is hard not to shout aloud some of his anti-slavery poetry -- like the lines from "Massachusetts to Virginia" -- written in protest at a Virginia planter's attempt to claim his escaped slave, George Latimer, in Boston in 1842 : "No slave-hunt in our borders -- no pirate on our strand!/No fetters in the Bay State -- no slave upon our land!"

There is probably no great Whittier revival looming, but there should be a place for a master of the poetic narrative who captured the events of Connecticut's "Dark Day," May 19, 1780, when "over the fresh earth and the heaven of noon, a horror of great darkness" fell, perhaps caused by smoke from forest fires: "Men prayed, and women wept: all ears grew sharp/To hear the doom-blast of the trumpet shatter/The black sky, that the dreadful face of Christ/Might look from the rent clouds."

The General Assembly was in session, and many of its members, "dim as ghosts," wanted to adjourn. But the councilor and wartime leader Abraham Davenport "rose, slow cleaving with his steady voice/The intolerable hush. 'This may well be/The Day of Judgment which the world awaits;/But be it so or not, I only know/My present duty.' "

He called for candles to be lighted, and the Assembly proceeded to consider an act to regulate the shad fisheries -- as important a matter in the Connecticut of today as it was back then.

When Christopher S. Wren retired as a foreign correspondent for The New York Times, he began by walking from his Times Square office to his home in Fairlee, Vt.

In one of the wry observations in "Walking to Vermont," Wren recalls that bit of Chinese sagery that a journey of 10,000 miles begins with a single step. "Two weeks of walking," he comments, "had enlightened me to a painful corollary of this ancient wisdom, which was that a single step should never begin without expensive socks."

Including an emergency-room visit in North Adams for an infected tick bite, his walk turned out more slog than ramble, much of it along the Appalachian Trail, which provides delightful vignettes of fellow "trail travelers." Each had adopted a "trail name" to preserve anonymity (Wren was "Super Tortoise, conveying a perverse pride in my comparatively slow pace").

There are also frequent notices of imminent geezerhood. The Housatonic River "looked so inviting" one day that Wren stripped and jumped in. Hearing "younger, shriller voices," he swam for shore and was pulling his hiking shorts back on when rafts from a local girls' camp came around the bend.

"I reacted," Wren writes, "as any mature male instinctively does when caught in a state of undress by a bevy of young females. I sucked in my gut. I would like to report they whistled, but a few only giggled."

As the Boston Archdiocese prepares to close dozens of parishes, Evelyn Savidge Sterne's "Ballots and Bibles" is a reminder that from the mid-1800s on, "parishes [were] grassroots political vehicles that incorporated Catholic immigrants into American public life."

Sterne, a history professor at the University of Rhode Island, notes that Little Rhody, "the first state to industrialize and attract a foreign-born proletariat," abolished its property qualification for voting only in 1888.

During the half-century when immigrant Catholics lacked access to electoral politics, she writes, their parishes "served as alternative political vehicles," and out of them came leaders such as Patrick J. McCarthy and Isabelle Ahearn O'Neill.

McCarthy, who "received his first taste of government" in parish societies, was elected as the first foreign-born mayor in 1906. O'Neill, daughter of immigrants, had performed in vaudeville and summer stock, but "used religious networks to affirm her respectability and build her constituency" and was the first woman in the nation to serve as a state senate's floor leader.

They and others, Sterne writes, "learned to be political through their parishes," turning "their lay societies into activist vehicles [and] used parish networks to forge political careers."
