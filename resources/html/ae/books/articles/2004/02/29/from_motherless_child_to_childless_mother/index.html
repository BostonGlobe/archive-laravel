From motherless child to childless mother

By Kerry Hardie  |  February 29, 2004

Mourning Ruby

By Helen Dunmore

Putnam, 278 pp., $23.95

The Russian writer Osip Mandelstam was one of the finest poets of the 20th century. He was last seen in 1938 in a transit camp at Vladivostok after his second arrest. He was 47.

In ''Mourning Ruby" Helen Dunmore, herself an accomplished poet and a translator of Mandelstam, has written a novel that owes its form to an idea: ''Someone asked Mandelstam what poetry was like, and he said that it was like an aeroplane flying along which gives birth to a baby aeroplane which immediately begins to fly with its full strength and its own life, and gives birth to its own baby aeroplane. . . . All this happens within one poem."

Dunmore's novel begins with the main character, Rebecca, dreaming of her dead daughter, Ruby. Throughout, Rebecca speaks in the first person, telling her tale of birth and loss with such passionate clarity that the reader can be forgiven for assuming that this is what the novel is ultimately about. Not so -- the Rebecca-Ruby strand is merely Dunmore's first airplane, the one that spawns all the others.

From the start, this novel moves fast; it establishes its themes, then switchbacks between them, revealing, concealing, drawing us into its web. Rebecca is a woman without a family history: She was put into a shoebox at birth and left in the backyard of an Italian restaurant, echoing the circumstances of Oscar Wilde's ''The Importance of Being Earnest," only Ernest came out of a handbag in Victoria Station. The book is as full of deliberate echoes as a fairy tale; Dunmore relishes the way they enrich her text and vibrate in the reader's psyche.

Rebecca is adopted by a couple so featureless that they leave no mark on her, not even that of sensual deprivation, which is surprising, since the only food she eats as a child comes out of tins. But these adoptive parents are not important and are alluded to only in passing; the formative character for Rebecca is her flatmate Joe, whose father and grandfather were pilots, or ''flyers," in two world wars. When we meet him he is writing a biography of Stalin's second wife, Nadezhda Alliluyeva.

Joe, who is in love with Rebecca, introduces her to good food, good living, the story of Mandelstam, and ultimately to her husband, Adam. Joe soon sees which way the wind is blowing and tactfully removes himself, clearing the way for marriage and the birth of the spotless and shining Ruby, who floods Rebecca's life with love and meaning; maternity is a recurring theme in Dunmore's novels and poems. After Ruby's death at the age of 5, Adam, who is a neonatologist (more babies), concentrates on saving the lives of other people's newborns. Rebecca leaves to walk alone through the Valley of the Shadow.

Enter Mr. Damiano, Dunmore's next airplane. In his youth Damiano was also a flyer, but this time an acrobat, the sort who swings through the upper air in a circus tent. By the time we meet him he has become a hotelier, employing Rebecca to help him create hotels of such luxury that when you enter them you enter the lost land of your childhood imagination: a real fire burns in the grate of your room, and beside your bed, in the glow of the lamp, your book lies open.

And all the time there is Joe, who has finished his book on Stalin's wife and is now trying to write about Stalin himself and Hitler's invasion of Russia. Joe has moved to Moscow, acquired a Russian girlfriend called Olya, and been visited there by Rebecca, Adam, and Ruby. Joe is a gifted talker who likes to discuss what he's trying to write, so we learn a lot more about Stalin. Along the way we've grasped that the Joe/Stalin/Russia motif is another baby airplane.

Time passes; Joe abandons his book, Moscow, and Olya, and goes to live in Canada, supporting Rebecca faithfully with letters and e-mails and the odd meeting. Rebecca leaves Damiano and takes a job in the Cornish village where Ruby is buried. She is visited there by Joe, now writing a personal fictional history for her in the form of a novel about a First World War flyer and a woman who might conceivably have been Rebecca's grandmother. The airplanes are still spawning.

Confused? Don't worry, Dunmore is very skillful, all the balls are up there, whizzing about, and though I don't believe a word I don't think I'm meant to, I have loved this walk through her enchanted garden. Now I want to see her close down the act, switch off the lights, see us out, never once breaking the illusion.

But that's not what she has in mind. Instead she stops the act in mid-juggle, pulls back the curtain, shows us the hidden scaffolding, insists that we stand beside her taking it in. It's like a film being stopped in its flow to demonstrate how the special effects are done.

Perhaps it isn't really that abrupt, perhaps the book has been deliberately unraveling for some time, the unfinished extract from Joe's novel signaling yet another deviation that carries the whiff of deterioration. This is hard judgment because Dunmore writes quite beautifully and the novel is mischievously but immaculately constructed, its form perfectly reflecting its subject.

Nevertheless what Dunmore is doing is ultimately self-indulgent. She is a writer writing about writing while pretending not to, sacrificing her storyline to Mandelstam's idea -- an idea that he used to describe what happens inside a poem, but was never ever the sum of the poem itself.

Kerry Hardie is the author of ''A Winter Marriage," recently out in paperback from Back Bay.
