Short Takes

By Amanda Heller  |  February 29, 2004

The Bront Myth

By Lucasta Miller

Knopf, 351 pp., illustrated, $26.95

Biography, being an art, not a science, is inevitably susceptible to cultural trends and personal agendas. Biographies of the Bront sisters, as Lucasta Miller shows in this insightful and entertaining study, have carried this tendency to an extreme, often hovering in a realm of make-believe totally disconnected from verifiable fact.

The godmother of the enduring "Bront myth" was Elizabeth Gaskell, whose sentimentalized and hugely popular 1857 "Life of Charlotte Bront" was facilitated by Charlotte's premature death just as Gaskell was getting under way, leaving her free to improve on reality. In order to depict the author of "Jane Eyre" as a demure Victorian homebody, Gaskell had to hide in plain sight the facts of Charlotte's life -- her literary sophistication, her experiences abroad, her determined courting of fame. Charlotte herself, says Miller, had performed a similar posthumous lobotomy on her sister Emily, reducing the creator of the turbulent masterpiece "Wuthering Heights" to an ethereal idiot savant, and incidentally initiating the Yorkshire Gothic school of Bront criticism.

The transformation of their family home into a maudlin tourist trap foreshadowed what would become of the Bronts at the mercy of Freudians, feminists, and Hollywood screenwriters, a long, strange trip to which Miller provides a shrewd and witty guidebook.

Love and Hydrogen

By Jim Shepard

Vintage, 340 pp., paperback, $13

Men playing with fire: That is the recurring theme of this superb collection of short stories by Jim Shepard, released in parallel with his novel "Project X."

Tales of sublimely foolhardy heroism commingle here with dark scenes from suburban life, snapshots of families whose machinery has developed an ominous rattle. The breathtaking title story foregrounds two behind-the-scenes heroes, daredevil airship mechanics in love with danger and with each other, floating high above the earth aboard the doomed Hindenburg. Other protagonists tempt fate with less romantic grandeur: a young husband and father who can't shake his addiction to self-destructive stunts; a schoolboy gripped by an opaque tragicomic rage. In "Won't Get Fooled Again," a dour John Entwhistle looks back on the guitar-smashing, hotel-trashing glory days of The Who and wonders what it was all about. One truly oddball story, an imaginative tour de force, conjures a fateful day in the life of the Creature from the Black Lagoon.

Shepard burrows into his protagonists with the ego-erasing intensity of a character actor, building his effects not with flashy gestures but through small but telling observations. Situations may recur from one story to another, but the people remain palpably unique.

Occasions of Sin

By Sandra Scofield

Norton, 256 pp., $24.95

Sandra Scofield's mother, Edith, became a parent much too young, at 17, and died much too young, at 33, of chronic kidney disease. A pretty teenager with foolish dreams of stardom, she'd gone off to Hollywood in the waning years of World War II, but before long she returned to Texas and married a boy no older than herself.

While these child-parents squabbled and moved around the state in search of work that would lift them out of near-poverty, Sandra was consigned to a series of Catholic boarding schools. The nuns did little to educate her precocious mind, but they succeeded in weighing her down with an overpowering sense of guilt and inadequacy, which all but crushed her emotionally when she finally came home, at 15, to find her mother slipping away.

Even as she weakened and faded, Edith, deprived of so much she craved in life, sought affirmation of her desirability. More than once, Sandra, suffering her own adolescent confusion, stumbled onto evidence of Edith's desperate sexuality. This searing memoir represents a daughter's attempt to piece together the mystery of a mother lost before her child was old enough to understand her, and before either one had learned her own worth as a woman.

Amanda Heller is a critic and editor who lives in Newton.
