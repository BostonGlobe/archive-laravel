Chaos in Chicago; crooks and books: the Zen mind

February 29, 2004

Like the best of Ross Macdonald's Lew Archer stories, Kris Nelscott's Smokey Dalton novels are infused with an understanding of the social, political, and moral complexities of the community in which they're set. Her characters not only struggle with the contradictions that rule them but are in perpetual conflict with their surroundings. Thus, brutality is no surprise, but then neither is an act of kindness. The most dilapidated building may house a gentle soul or a rabid criminal, and so too may a luxurious home.

This type of subtlety treats readers with a level of respect that makes for a rich, rewarding experience, and so it is with ''Stone Cribs," Nelscott's exceptional new novel. It's 1969, and her African-American private investigator Dalton, who's now using the alias William Grimshaw, is hiding in Chicago with Jimmy, a boy posing as his son. The year before, Jimmy, then 10 years old, witnessed the assassination of the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr. As Nelscott puts it, ''The shooter was not James Earl Ray," and the young boy fears the killer will find him.

Meanwhile, when an acquaintance suffers a botched abortion, Grimshaw is asked to find the abortionist and calm down the injured woman's former husband, a Chicago cop. The investigation leads Grimshaw into territories controlled by gangs who model themselves after the Black Panthers, and soon murder sends the earnest investigator into a confrontation he had hoped to avoid.

Nelscott's hard-boiled style gives an added blast of energy to the captivating story, and because her characters are so nuanced and naturally complex, we don't know whom to trust. Combating racism, the pressures of his home life, and his own moral code, Grimshaw remains a good man throughout the gritty tale. But Nelscott knows good men sometimes must do bad things in the service of justice and their principles, especially in a place as discordant as Chicago in 1969.

Things aren't as they appear in the seemingly genteel world of rare books. In John Dunning's ''The Bookman's Promise," former Denver policeman Cliff Janeway, now a rare-book collector and bookstore owner, finds himself entangled in a murder that involves a lost library of works by 19th-century explorer Richard Burton, who, we learn here, may have sparked the Civil War.

After acquiring a Burton first edition and appearing on a Boston radio station to discuss it, Janeway is suddenly pronounced a Burton expert, and elderly Josephine Gallant turns up to challenge his acquisition's provenance, claiming that it, along with other Burton tomes, was stolen from her family.

Gallant falls ill, and in what turns out to be a deathbed promise, Janeway agrees to look for the missing books. But when one of Gallant's caretakers is seemingly murdered, Janeway also must jump to the defense of the accused, and soon he's hunting a killer who is hunting him. Apparently, one of Janeway's competitors associates with gangsters. Fortunately, Janeway knows one too.

''The Bookman's Promise" is as much a historical novel as it is a crime story, and Dunning allows us to accompany Burton and Gallant's grandfather as they travel through the American South. Not surprisingly, Janeway ends up pretty much where Burton had been, as the story concludes with a confrontation at Fort Sumter and, back in Denver, an unforeseen revelation.

Michael Dibdin's Italy-based Aurelio Zen tales are among the best in today's mystery genre, and in ''Medusa," the ninth novel in the series, the dogged, perspicacious Zen, a detective who works for the interior ministry, is slowly unraveling a crime that puts him in conflict with the remnants of a secret military organization.

A mummified body found in the Italian Alps is stolen from the morgue, and we, unlike Zen, know who it was: a member of a five-man covert paramilitary cell from the 1970s named Operation Medusa. Moving throughout northern Italy, Zen soon learns the dead man's identity and discovers that two other members of the group have been killed, one as recently as the previous week, in a car-bomb explosion.

Zen, who is eminently logical, seeks the other members, and we know where they are. More important, and this is one of Dibdin's great strengths, we know what makes them so subtly dangerous. Of one man whom we follow into hiding, Dibdin writes, ''Imaginary fears exhausted and paralyzed him [but] real and present danger left him cool and collected. . . . Whatever the outcome, it could not be worse than living in a state of perpetual uncertainty and inchoate terror."

The secret to the tale is buried deep in the bureaucracy, and Zen will have to go against entrenched powers in the defense ministry, all the while dodging a very real threat of death. Is there any question Zen will succeed? Perhaps not, but even the criminals know he is a special man, as we know Dibdin is a special writer, one who is a joy to read.

Jim Fusilli is a novelist and critic. His mystery novels include ''A Well-Known Secret" and, most recently, ''Tribeca Blues."
