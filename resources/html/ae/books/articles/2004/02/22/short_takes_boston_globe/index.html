Short Takes

By Barbara Fisher  |  February 22, 2004

The Epicure's Lament

By Kate Christensen

Doubleday, 351 pp., $23.95

In this novel, the epicure of the title, Hugo Whittier, gives voice to his ''thick, pungent, unspeakably reeking private thoughts." It is not unintentional that this description brings to mind a ripe stew. Hugo is not only a richly original narrator but also an inspired cook.

When he is not writing in notebooks or cooking in his obsessively organized kitchen, he is prowling for sexual adventure in inappropriate places, smoking himself to death, reviling the few remaining bits of his family, and generally behaving badly and wishing to behave worse. At the start of his writings, he is ''thriving unmolested" in a tower room at Waverly, an inherited family mansion on the Hudson River. As he struggles to resist the intrusions of his newly separated brother and his estranged wife and purported daughter, and speed toward an untimely death, he alienates everyone he meets. His struggles bring unexpected results and rewards.

But it is Hugo's smug and self-loathing voice that is the greatest treat of this very savory dish of a novel. For example, he refers to culinary mentor M.F.K. Fisher as ''that uppity little slyboots of a voluptuary autodidact, that fresh-faced Irish smartypants." Of himself, he says, ''I'm a career hermit. . . . Moldering is a valid life-style choice, as they say."

Five Men Who Broke My Heart

By Susan Shapiro

Delacorte, 231 pp., $21.95

These sharp, sassy city girls keep on coming. Here's another -- Susan Shapiro. She sets out, in witty one-liners, to describe the five men she has loved and lost from age 13 to 35. While investigating what went wrong with her first five relationships, she demonstrates what's right with her current relationship, with her husband, Aaron.

During a summer when she can neither publish a book nor make a baby, Shapiro tracks down all her past boyfriends. She relives the psychodrama of each relationship, from hopeful beginning to hideous ending. Revisiting her botched romantic past, she reevaluates the heartbreak inflicted by Brad, David, George, Richard, and Tom. This provides distraction, entertainment, and enlightenment for her. Yet for a reader, the meetings prove tedious and the guys interchangeable. More interesting than the men are Shapiro's wacky but warm parents and her loyal look-alike best friend, Claire. The last part of this memoir, which concentrates on family and abandons the search for lost love, is by far the best part. On the last page, she says, ''I realize I hadn't emulated my mother, I'd married her." And she is the best choice of them all. Although her father, her brothers, and her best friend aren't bad either.

Making Love

By Jean-Philippe Toussaint

Translated, from the French, by Linda Coverdale

New Press, 116 pp., $19.95

Only the French bring such intensity of focus to the subject of love. Following in the weepy tradition of Marguerite Duras, Jean-Philippe Toussaint's novel scrutinizes the final days of a seven-year relationship as it disintegrates during a business trip to Japan.

The business is hers. It is fashion so high it is shown at a contemporary art space as well as sold in the Let's Go Go Go boutique. He is her escort on the trip. They stay in a luxury high-rise hotel, smoke cigarettes, dress in black leather, and hide behind dark glasses. Under her black leather coat, she wears a dress of her own design ''of chin wool and organza, and blue silk the color of a starry night." She wears her pink leather mules ''with an aloof elegance, ironic and refined." The lovers sleep, fight, make love, wander the streets, ride the bullet train. An earthquake briefly disturbs their perfect self-absorption. But only briefly. ''Breaking up . . . was more a state of being than an action, more a period of mourning than a death agony." There is little action here, but much careful commentary on the state of being. All the action is elegantly presented; all the commentary is exquisitely detailed. The two lovers act with great delicacy, tact, intelligence, tenderness, and style -- primarily style.

Barbara Fisher is a freelance critic who lives in New York.
