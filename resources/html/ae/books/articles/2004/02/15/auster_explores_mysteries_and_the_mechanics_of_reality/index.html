Auster explores mysteries and the mechanics of reality

By Eric Grunwald, Globe Correspondent  |  February 15, 2004

Oracle Night

By Paul Auster

Holt, 243 pp., $23

Recently I had the occasion, rarer than I'd like, to go to New York -- more specifically to Brooklyn, which is not only where Paul Auster lives but also the setting for his 11th novel, ''Oracle Night," which I was coincidentally reading for this review. Riding the subway into Brooklyn, I looked up from the book at each unfamiliar stop, and just after we'd passed West 4th Street I turned the page to find the narrator, Sidney Orr, boarding the subway at . . . West 4th Street.

This would come as no surprise to Auster, since mysterious coincidences are at the heart of the compulsively readable yet wonderfully complex and unsettling ''Oracle Night." The book is both a babushka doll of stories within stories and a literary Rubik's Cube, the solution of which, if there is one, is the very nature of reality.

Like Auster's best-selling previous novel, ''The Book of Illusions," this one begins just after a brush with death for the narrator, though Sidney faces recuperation, not grief: a 38-year-old novelist stricken by an illness everyone expected him to die from, he is miraculously recovering. But it is no easy convalescence, due not only to his physical debilitation, but also because of, it seems, his random discovery and purchase of a mysterious blue notebook.

''I can't explain why it should have been so," Sidney tells us, ''but . . . when I held the notebook in my hands . . . I felt something akin to physical pleasure, a rush of sudden, incomprehensible well-being." This feeling continues as he takes the notebook home and, for the first time in months, begins to write. A new story flows effortlessly out of him. Hours pass without him moving from his chair. And when he stops he feels none of the exhaustion his post-illness exertions usually bring him.

His joy is short-lived, however, as strange and unpleasant things start happening. His beloved wife, Grace, whom he hadn't heard come home, says she peeked into his small study, where he was writing, but he wasn't there. More disturbing, though, is the way she begins to act that evening: sad, distant, even hostile.

As with ''Book of Illusions," the concentric stories in ''Oracle Night" give the book, paradoxically, a greater feeling of truth. Here they also show the interplay of an author's imagination with real events, ultimately the novel's central issue. For within the story Sidney writes in the notebook there is another novel, called ''Oracle Night," about a man blinded by an explosion during World War I who thereafter sees glimpses of the future. In the end, however, this ''gift" is a curse, destroying him on the very eve of long-awaited bliss.

Eventually Sidney, to this point a rationalist, begins to believe, with his friend and mentor Trause's influence, that words alone can predict -- nay, create -- the future. ''Sometimes we know things before they happen, even if we aren't aware of it," Trause tells him, echoing Rilke. ''We live in the present, but the future is inside us at every moment. Maybe that's what writing is all about, Sid . . . making things happen in the future."

As mystical as Sid's life and stories seem, though, Auster doesn't come down on that side. His goal isn't to pass judgment. ''I'm no longer certain that I know what reality is anymore," Auster tells the Paris Review in its 50th-anniversary issue. ''All I can do is talk about the mechanics of reality, to gather evidence about what goes on in the world and try to record it as faithfully as I can."

Every mysterious event that might be attributed to the blue notebook can ultimately be explained rationally, by the confusion of fear-, denial-, and complexity-blinded minds -- primarily Sidney's. Every event, that is, except one: a dream of Grace's that mirrors almost exactly the story in Sid's notebook. And perhaps the most unbelievable element in ''Oracle Night" is that Sid, an intelligent writer, never addresses this quandary directly.

But this isn't accidental. In the Paris Review interview, Auster also reaffirms his belief in ''the anecdote as a form of knowledge," invoked in his first book, ''The Invention of Solitude": the idea that, according to the interviewer, ''knowledge doesn't have to come in the form of declarations, statements, or explanations. It can come in the form of stories." In addition to Auster's stories ''showing" rather than ''telling," Sid's not taking up the question also evinces his very human inability to face his biggest fear: losing those closest to him, if not his idealized conceptions of them as well. Only for a moment does he let himself consider the worst possibilities; then he discards them. Why should we believe his account of anything? The entire narrative becomes suspect.

If there are a few small things in ''Oracle Night" that seem obvious or pat, we must acknowledge that such is the dangerous tightrope an author traverses when exploring the strangeness of reality, including coincidence, and that Auster walks it bravely and almost flawlessly. Despite my quibbles, the novel is thoroughly enjoyable because Sid -- and Auster's subtly crafted portrayal of ''the mechanics of reality" -- is eminently realistic in his complexity and flaws.

''Contrary to what many people want to believe," Auster tells the Paris Review, ''the novel is . . . as healthy and vigorous as it's ever been." He recites a long list of his peers, but with ''Oracle Night," he proves by example.

Eric Grunwald is managing editor of Agni magazine and is currently writing a novel.
