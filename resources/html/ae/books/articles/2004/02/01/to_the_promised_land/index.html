To the promised land

How 200 freed slaves reinvented themselves in the newly created colony of Liberia

By Debra J. Dickerson  |  February 1, 2004

Mississippi in Africa: The Saga of the Slaves of Prospect Hill Plantation and Their Legacy in Liberia Today

By Alan Huffman

Gotham, 328 pp., $27

History buffs obsessed with American slavery are masochists. An American studying World War II, for example, need not do so with much trepidation or controversial agenda. Given the likelihood that any Nazi sympathizers would have long ago been pruned from our family trees, we can all safely consider ourselves among the good guys. Heroes once removed. Or, at least, that's our story as victors and, since there is no viable victim group credibly claiming otherwise, we're sticking to it.

But slavery. To be American is to be forced to take sides in a battle that no one now defends and for which few will take more than a "mistakes were made," passive-voiced responsibility. No one actually involved still lives. The few remaining quill-penned records molder into illegibility in damp courthouse basements all across America, when not consciously destroyed or absconded with by those with controversial agendas. Record keeping was an intentionally vague affair anyway where the slaves' lives (as opposed to their monetary value) were concerned; they had no last names and, at emancipation, both chose and were assigned surnames often arbitrarily or with great casualness. Slaves were largely kept illiterate, so few got to tell the story of the "peculiar institution" from the business end. That leaves Americans today trying to fill in the gaps in the saga that continues to be America's most lasting trauma: slavery and its effect on modern race relations.

Slaves fought for the Confederacy, yes. But willingly or unwillingly? Valiantly or staying just one step ahead of beatings? Some freed slaves chose to remain in the same old quarters, performing the same old tasks. Had they been happy in slavery, or was choosing all that mattered? Many slaves, like Frederick Douglass, remained in affectionate contact with their former masters in letters that ended in perhaps too nonchalant requests for information on long-sold-away family members. One can be sure of very little where so much time, so many humans, so much profit, so many alternative explanations, and such a need either to escape or firmly locate blame abound. But the buffs just keep on trying, and the history of American slavery becomes more and more . . . untidy. Just like this book, "Mississippi in Africa: The Saga of the Slaves of Prospect Hill Plantation and Their Legacy in Liberia Today."

The book is untidy because the story inescapably is, Huffman's fine storytelling notwithstanding. There is so much we'll never understand, however many facts are on the record. The bare outlines of the story are these: When wealthy plantation owner Isaac Ross died, in 1836, he willed that his 5,000-acre plantation be liquidated after his daughter's death. Its proceeds were to be used to resettle his freed slaves in Liberia, Africa. The fact that this is shocking today conveys how shocking it must have been in 1836. Indeed, contested by several of the heirs, Ross's will, and the fate of nearly 200 slaves, ricocheted through the Mississippi Legislature and court system for more than a decade, with all fully aware that much more was at stake than one estate, however grand. Eventually, the rule of law won out over greed and white supremacy, and most of the Ross slaves left both bondage and America. In Liberia, those freed slaves both faced unmitigated hardship and caused it for the natives they managed to turn quickly into enemies. They saw their African brothers through the eyes of the masters they'd fled and in short order were acting just like them. The bloody, generation-long civil war that rages in Liberia today can be traced directly to this shaky foundation.

How did Ross come to the decision for his stipulation? We can never know for sure, though Huffman digs diligently to understand (as did the Mississippi Legislature and its court system). Was it because Ross was black, as has been credibly claimed? If he was, how could he enslave his brothers? Why did the effectively disinherited grandson, Isaac Ross Wade, who spent a decade fighting to have the will's provisions set aside, "remain in friendly contact with" the colony of slaves he'd lost? How had the slaves, some of whom had set fire to the plantation in hopes of killing the man who kept them enslaved an extra decade, known they could go to him for help? Why did a sizable number of slaves choose to remain enslaved and be sold rather than immigrate to Africa? Those who tout definitive answers to these questions are the reason slavery still traumatizes America. Huffman knows the meaning is to be found in the fundamental question around which all discussions of slavery revolve -- what was slavery really like? -- not in the quest for either conscience-salving or anger-stoking answers.

A lifelong Mississippian clearly besotted with his beleaguered state, Huffman is a patient, confident storyteller who lingers over the details until they come to life. In describing plantation life, he imagines: "Before the Civil War these fields would have been crowded with men and horses, and on any given spring day, the ground would have been laid bare by a hundred plows, with earthworms clamoring in the raw sunlight, attracting great flocks of red-winged blackbirds to swoop down and feast. The birds would rise and fall above the already sweating brows of men for whom another year, another crop of cotton, had just begun. Now and then the breeze might carry a whiff . . . of wood smoke curling in thin tendrils from distant cabin chimneys from the remains of last night's fires, on which the morning's meals of biscuits with cane syrup and fatback were cooked and quickly consumed in darkness. Each day the ringing of the plantation's great cast-iron bells heralded the rising sun." The reader can almost smell the pork sizzle. We should expect nothing less romantic from the author, who bought the remains of the manor house in which these events took place, disassembled it, and reconstructed it on his own nearby property.

Given the constant going-over that slavery gets, it was only a matter of time before one of the obsessed headed for the little new territory that remains, the long-neglected question of those blacks who "returned" to Africa, to Sierra Leone, and, in this case, Liberia. Again, the facts tell us little of what we truly want to know. Why did people fleeing slavery name their new home "Mississippi in Africa"? Build Greek Revival "big houses" just like the master's? Wear bonnets, top hats, and all the stifling finery of the white folks back home? What does it mean that their behavior led the locals to refer to them (as some still do today) as "white"? And most important, did they really enslave the locals? How difficult it must be for modern African-Americans to listen to justifications of the practice on the grounds that these unpaid laborers were wards, rescued orphans, members of the family. What is not in question is that the returned slaves set themselves up as the new elites and set in motion the conflict that has long roiled Liberia, just as the American colonists did with the Native Americans. Huffman quotes historian Mary Louise Clifford's succinct summation: "The Free Negroes who came from America did not regard the Africans as their brothers. They were not returning to their homeland, but rather had left their homeland across the Atlantic because it denied them opportunity. . . . In their eyes, everything attractive and desirable had belonged to the wealthy masters of the plantations on which they had labored." Not exactly what anyone had in mind at the time. Perhaps it should have been discussed with the locals.

With "Mississippi in Africa," as with Edward Ball's "Slaves in the Family," one is reminded that inside every one of us lives all the drama, irony and cataclysm of history, if only we knew the tales. Ball was lucky. He was tracking down his own relatives. Poor Huffman was trying to track down inventory, listed in the will as a sort of horse-and-buggy Schindler's list of people with only one name. The reader can almost hear him sigh as he sat across the table from some elderly black, Liberian or American, who knew nothing of their history but who might well be the missing link that made all the other pieces fit together. If they, and we, only knew how to clean up the mess.
