A spontaneous blaze, or arson?

Reweighing the causes of WWI

By David M. Shribman  |  March 21, 2004

Europe's Last Summer: Who Started the Great War in 1914?

By David Fromkin

Knopf, 349 pp., illustrated, $26.95

I remember the survivors of World War I the way my father remembers the veterans of the Civil War: frail men, enfeebled remnants of noble conflict, reminders of the certainties that made life easier to understand in an earlier time. Time was that the Civil War was easy to comprehend, but no more; the passing of the years has provided it with a complexity it didn't possess only a generation ago. The same is true with World War I. Indeed, it just got more complicated with the publication of this book.

In the old days, by which I mean the time before David Fromkin's new book, "Europe's Last Summer," appeared, the Great War was a comprehensible one -- comprehensible mostly because the most readable accounts of the conflict dealt with an irresistible narrative: The great powers, victims of the alliances that were supposed to preserve the peace, slipped into war almost without thought, the great levers of the international system pushing them inexorably into battle. Some damned foolish thing in the Balkans, as Bismarck had predicted, started it all, and nothing could stop it.

Fromkin, author of the luminous "In the Time of the Americans," a compelling chronicle of the generation that moved the United States out of world isolation, doesn't see it that way. Others have blamed the Germans for the war; that was the whole basis of the Treaty of Versailles, which included a war-guilt clause that would itself provide so much fodder for fevered German minds (Hitler's among them) and quarreling diplomats and historians. But no one has deconstructed the war quite the way Fromkin has.

For Fromkin, who teaches international relations, history, and law at Boston University, sees the war not in its unity but in two parts. The first was the war between Serbia and Austria, prompted by the assassination of Franz Ferdinand and lighting the tinder that had been gathering in the Balkans and in metropolitan Europe for so many years. We know that war, and we think of it as tinder itself for the second world conflagration that followed. But there's more to it than that, according to Fromkin. There is a second war in there, and it is the one that Germany wanted to fight against Russia while it still was sure it would prevail. This second war has almost nothing to do with the first. Again, parts of all this theory are familiar. But the whole is strikingly different, and maybe right.

Here it is in one sentence: "The local war between Austria and Serbia was connected with Franz Ferdinand . . . but the Great War was not; the world war was caused by the struggle for supremacy among the great European powers."

And here, in one additional sentence, is the explanation for why the big conflict unfolded the way it did: "Germany invaded France (through its only feasible invasion route, Belgium) and attacked Russia because Berlin saw France and Russia as mortal enemies that would destroy Germany in a few years unless Germany could defeat them now while it still could." That, in two sentences, is what Fromkin's book is about. All the rest is commentary.

The rest is history, too. Fromkin provides a readable account of Europe's last summer, not as alluring perhaps as Barbara Tuchman did in her 1962 "The Guns of August," but sparer, more disciplined and more pointed. The meat of the book is Chapter 46, a remarkable 6-page argument called "The Key to What Happened," in which Fromkin provides perhaps the most succinct summary of what happened ever written. Many of the rest of the chapters are brief and digestible. Just the facts -- which add up to a continent-wide delusion about what was happening in Europe as war approached or, more precisely, as Europe approached war.

War was a shattering event and a surprise, too. Many writers have quoted Henry James: "The plunge of civilization into this abyss of blood and darkness . . . is a thing that so gives away the whole long age during which we have supposed the world to be, with whatever abatement, gradually bettering, that to have to take it all now for what the treacherous years were all the while really making for and meaning is too tragic for any words."

That's Henry James. Here's David Fromkin: "What Europe was building up toward was not a better world, but a giant smashup, as, in the first twentieth-century war among modern societies, the accumulated explosive power that advanced science had developed was concentrated on the goal of mass destruction."

Through it all are the telling details of diplomatic and military life that make the period so utterly tragic. Serbia's leaders, for example, so agonized over their response to Austria-Hungary's summer ultimatum that the text became almost illegible. Meanwhile, the debate among German leaders was so intense that they "risked strokes or heart attacks in the violence of their fears and rages, and brought one another to tears."

There would be time aplenty for tears, and there would be plenty of tears. And there would be plenty of words written later about how the powers of Europe tumbled into war, their alliances dragging them into the vortex. Fromkin says that the alliances didn't do that; if anything, they were a brake on adventurism, "for each country tended to discourage its allies from running risks on issues in which only one of them had serious interests."

That summer before the war was intoxicating. Emperor Franz Joseph went on holiday. So did Kaiser Wilhelm. It was, as Fromkin said, Europe's last summer -- the last summer, you might say, of the long peace of the 19th century. All good things must come to an end and so, too, must summer. This one did.

This book is called "Europe's Last Summer," but its subtitle is really its subject. It reads as a question: "Who Started the Great War in 1914?" Fromkin doesn't avoid the question he poses so starkly. He answers it in one word: Germany.

David M. Shribman, for a decade the Globe's Washington bureau chief, is executive editor of the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette.
