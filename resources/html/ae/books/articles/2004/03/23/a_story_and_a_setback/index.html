'SOONER OR LATER' CHAPTER FOUR OF A CHILDREN'S STORY IN EIGHT PARTS

A story and a setback

By Patricia McKissack  |  March 23, 2004

Patricia McKissack's novel for young readers, "Sooner or Later," is being published in The Boston Globe on Tuesdays through April 27 (not including April 20). Teachers wishing to receive copies of the paper on those days, as well as curriculum materials developed for the story, should contact the Globe's Newspaper in Education office at 617-929-2639.

There in the quiet of the rented bedroom, Pa told us about what he had been doing the three-and-a-half years he'd been gone.

He was helped by a conductor on the Underground Railroad to make his escape from Tall Pines. We knew there were people who helped runaway slaves reach Canada, which Mas' Wallace made out to be a terrible place. "Up North, you'll freeze to death or die of starvation, and then wild dogs will eat your body," he'd say.

Mama told us not to believe Mas' Wallace. The North meant freedom and nothing was bad about being free. And Pa confirmed it. "Being free was wonderful," he said. "I never felt better in my life than the day I made my first decision without having to get permission from another man!" He went on to tell us how it did get cold up North, and he was hungry lots of times. "But I made it, because I was free."

Anna Lee shrugged and smiled. Both dimples popped into her cheeks. "Pa, were you scared?"

"Plenty scared!" he said. "Out of my mind scared! But I got to the Union line somewhere on the border of Tennessee and Alabama, about a month after leaving here -- hiding in barns, moving at night, almost getting shot."

Pa told the commanding officer that he was good with horses. "So that's what I did. I trained horses for the military up near Chattanooga," he said.

"And you got a medal for being so good," said Anna Lee. "Corporal Young told us all about it."

Pa nodded as he went to his carpetbag and took out a box. Inside was the Congressional Medal of Honor. "I went all the way to Washington, D.C., to get it."

After the war, Pa had stayed on in the military, breaking horses for the cavalry for

good money. "A wild horse bucked me up against a fence and smashed my thigh. Worse still, a rusty nail jabbed me in the calf. I almost lost my whole leg and my life when the poison from that nail went through my body. Army doctors patched me up. And I survived, 'course with a limp. The Army released me, and then I set out for Alabama, riding sometimes, walking most of the time, and floating down rivers and creeks. At last I made it." "Why didn't you write us, tell us where you were?" I asked.

"Never write? I wrote home many times." His voice was strained. "I wrote and wrote. Then sent word by anybody going near Mobile. I never once heard from anybody."

"We never got your letters."

"Never," Anna Lee chimed in. "We thought you were dead."

There was silence after that. We all had much to think about. Before drifting off to sleep, I remembered that we hadn't gotten the package Gramma Dossie gave us out of Corporal Young's saddlebags. Oh, well, I told myself, it was probably bread and smoked meat.

Before day, we headed for Mobile Bay to catch the steamboat that was to take us to New Orleans and then up the Mississippi River to St. Louis. It was good to hear Pa's story, but now I was set to wondering: What had happened to the letters?

Once we reached the docks, Pa found the Sarah Belle. He took Sonny with him and told Anna Lee and me to stay put until he saw about our tickets.

The ship was lit up like a starry sky. Passengers were leaning over the railing, looking like birds lined up on a fence. Below, carriages were arriving with people

hustling and bustling with luggage. There were plenty of colored men willing to help carry bags, just as it had been in slavery days, except for one big difference: Passengers had to pay for the service. Suddenly a carriage pulled up and stopped a few feet away. Out of habit, I rushed over and helped the lady step down. Then I took her luggage down and called for a porter to take it for her.

"Thank you, darling, for being so helpful," she said, smiling. "There is still some civility left in this world." And she placed a coin in my hand. It was the first money I'd ever earned.

I turned around just in time to see a wily looking man siding up to Anna Lee. "Nice looking dog you got there," he said.

"His name is Digger," volunteered Anna Lee. "When he was just a puppy he used to dig, dig, dig up the flowers. Missus thought it was us doing it, but she found out it was Digger."

"The man's not interested in all that," I said through clenched teeth, pulling her behind me as a signal that it was time to be scared.

"Oh, but I am interested in what this pretty little angel has to say," said the man. "Are you children alone here in Mobile?"

"No," I tried to look taller.

The man was insistent. "Are you hungry? If so, I know a place where I can get anything you want to eat and your little dog will be welcome, too."

"No, thank you," I said.

"What about you, little girl?" the man said, flashing a yellow-toothed grin. "You and the little one, come with me. I have something wonderful to show you."

I was just about to take off running, when I heard Pa's voice. "Joe David," he called.

The man fled without looking back.

"There are all kinds of characters down here on the wharf," said Pa. "Never talk to strangers."

Pa looked worried, and it wasn't just about the stranger.

Anna Lee felt it, too. While I always held back, Anna Lee plunged right in with the questions.

"What's wrong, Pa?" she asked.

"I don't know," said Pa, sucking in air, letting it out slowly. I'd noticed that Pa did

that when he was worried about something. He talked to the ship's agent at the gate of the Sarah Belle, showing him the receipt. The agent listened, then shook his head.

"Don't worry. I'm sure Mr. Loganberry is just running a little late," I said, not sounding a bit sure of my words.

The whistle blew to warn all last-minute passengers that the ship was about to leave. Pa was breathing heavy like he was carrying a heavy load.

Pa went wild. "Wait," he shouted. "I paid for tickets. Me and my children are supposed to be on this ship!"

Pa showed another ship's agent the receipt. The agent explained that the receipt meant nothing. Without actual tickets, we couldn't embark.

When Pa insisted and tried to force his way onboard, the agent blew a loud, shrill whistle. We tried to get Pa to come away, but he didn't hear us. He wanted someone to tell him it was all a mistake and make it right.

It took three large men to hold Pa back as the Sarah Belle pushed away from the dock and all hope drained out of Pa's face.

Pa explained to the constable everything that had happened. We all went to where Mr. Loganberry had done business. The constable banged on the door. Nobody came. We tried to see through the curtained windows. The place looked empty.

"I think you've been the victim of a scam. We see it all the time," said the constable. "A man, usually a Northerner, comes into town and tells the coloreds and poor whites that he's a land developer in places like Wisconsin and Iowa"

"Kansas," said Anna Lee.

"Yes, Kansas. Then he gets them to pay him money to make all the necessary arrangements. Then you find out it's all been a hoax. Meanwhile, the perpetrator is long gone."

That is exactly what had happened to Pa down to his last dime.

"Are we going on the big boat?" Sonny asked.

"Not today," I said, all hopes of Kansas fading.
