FOR CHILDREN

Old fables in a new guise

By Liz Rosenberg  |  March 14, 2004

Grandpa Bear Fixes It

Written by Poppy Wells, illustrated by Patti Jennings

Sterling, 10 pp., ages 1-4, $7.95

I Can Fly

Written by Ruth Krauss, ilustrated by Mary Blair

Golden/Random House, 26 pp., ages 2-6, $4.99

The Stories of Hans Christian Andersen

Selected and translated, from the Danish, by Diana Crone Frank and Jeffrey Frank

Houghton Mifflin, 293 pp., all ages, $27

"Grandpa Bear Fixes It" is that pleasant rarity, a sturdy board book with moving pieces. Parents already know the book is not going to last forever -- nor is that the point. Board books with movable pieces are more like toys than literature, but there's a place in a child's library for variety. Here, Grandpa Bear kindly fixes things for his whole family, and that plot provides an ingenious excuse for interesting moving parts.

Very young readers love tools, and "Grandpa Bear Fixes It" gives them a chance to play. There's even a movable up-and-down hammer on the cover. Inside, Grandpa fixes a birdhouse roof; tightens a bike wheel; oils a gate hinge; and glues together a broken flowerpot. Children can "help" by moving the pieces: wrench, oil can, etc. This is not high art, but it is sweet, inviting, and inventive -- and entirely kid-friendly.

Back again is Ruth Krauss's lovely "I Can Fly," with gentle illustrations by Mary Blair. Krauss wrote such classics as "The Carrot Seed" and "A Hole Is to Dig." Her work is accessible and down-to-earth, even when, as in "I Can Fly," she writes in rhyming couplets like "A cow can moo./I can too./I'm merrier/than a terrier." Throughout this picture book, a little girl plays at being a whole menagerie of animals, a game so common to childhood it is the stuff of dreams and myths. It gives Krauss free range in the writing -- "My house is/like a mouse's" -- and free range to Blair's light, bright palette, filled with animals real and imagined. First published in l950, "I Can Fly" remains as fresh as that first breath of spring.

Hans Christian Andersen kept a sign next to his bed each night that read "I only appear to be dead," such was his terror of being buried alive. He need not have worried. More than 100 years later, even his worst critics have not been able to bury "The Little Mermaid," "The Princess and the Pea," "The Ugly Duckling," "Thumbelina," "The Little Match Girl," or a host of others. They are so much a part of our vocabulary, it seems impossible that one mere mortal summoned them all.

Andersen was a complex soul, not the Hollywood Danny Kaye version, or the sweet simpleton often portrayed in his time. He was a great writer -- one of the greatest ever to have written for children. "The Stories of Hans Christian Andersen" proves the point beyond question, in a large, handsome volume of 22 tales, with original 19th-century illustrations and decorations. Here are the old favorites, as well as tales less well known. The translators provide useful footnotes, and a fine biographical introduction, "The Real H. C. Andersen." Here we find him, in his own words, coveting "honor and glory in the same way as the miser covets gold."

Most important, the new translation by Diana Crone Frank and Jeffrey Frank is certainly our best English version yet. It is distinctly less Germanic and more Scandinavian than its predecessors, lighter, more buoyant, conveying the energy and freshness of this most peculiar author: "Meanwhile, Little Claus's old grandmother had died. Sure, she'd been ill-tempered and mean to him, but he was still sad; he put the dead woman in his warm bed to see if she would come back to life. He was going to leave her all night while he slept on a chair in the corner. He'd done that before."

We see in tale after tale those felicitous asides that are the mark of confident genius. In "The Little Mermaid," he writes: "Now don't get the idea that there's only bare white sand at the bottom of the sea. No, you will find the strangest trees and plants whose stems and leaves are so supple that, at the lightest movement of the water, they stir as if they were alive. Fish, large and small, dart among the branches the way that birds up here fly through the air."

Each tale contains an instant springing to life, rendering the comic moment heartbreaking, and the tragic triumphant. Andersen was master of the bittersweet. We see it in "Thumbelina" -- here called "Thumbelisa" -- about a tiny young woman, besieged by dreadful suitors and false friends. Though she marries an exquisite little prince, the story ends with the swallow who saved her, "and sang for them as best he could, but in his heart he was sad, because he was very fond of Thumbelisa and didn't want to be separated from her." In these apparently simple tales -- many, of course, retellings of other, more ancient tales -- Andersen wrote his own life story while keeping it secret. Some critics damned him for this, like fellow Dane Soren Kierkegaard, who called Andersen "a sniveler." Others wished he "would not waste his time writing fairy tales for children." How lucky that he persisted!

"The Stories of Hans Christian Andersen" gives us Andersen pure and clear. In gorgeous fantasies like "The Snow Queen"; tales of humility and purity like "The Red Shoes"; and "The Little Match Girl" -- as enduring and indicting as anything Dickens ever wrote about children -- Andersen created from old scraps and nursery hand-me-downs his works of art. He once wrote to a friend, "I'm beginning to write some fairy tales for children. I want to win the next generation, you see."

Liz Rosenberg teaches creative writing and English at the State University of New York at Binghamton. She reviews children's books for the Globe each month.
