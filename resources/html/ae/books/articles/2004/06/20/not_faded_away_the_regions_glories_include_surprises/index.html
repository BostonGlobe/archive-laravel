ON NEW ENGLAND

Not faded away: The region's glories include surprises

By Michael Kenney  |  June 20, 2004

Think not that all the virgin forests are off in the wilds. In Farmington, Maine, there are 20 acres of ancient forests with trees 350 years old and 4 feet across just a quarter-mile behind the Franklin County Court House.

And think not that all the covered bridges are century-old antiques. In North Hartland, Vt., there is a wooden covered bridge built in 2001 to replace a steel and concrete one.

In New England, there is no shortage of towns and cities that are certified ''historic." But it is also a region where an enterprising explorer can map out a history-filled walk in an unlikely place like Fall River.

And it is a place where you can turn a group of inquisitive high school students loose in well-mapped terrain like the Boston Harbor Islands and have them come back with a guide to ''the cool stuff in the harbor."

That's a sampling from four new books, all ready for a summer off the beaten path.

It is actually not so surprising to find one of Maine's ancient forests so close to the center of town, write foresters Bruce Kershner and Robert T. Leverett in ''The Sierra Club Guide to the Ancient Forests of the Northeast."

In the region as a whole -- including New York (where there is a 25-acre old-growth forest at the tip of Manhattan and reachable by subway) -- there are 400,000 acres of ancient forests. Most, to be sure, are in expected places, the Litchfield Hills, the Berkshires, the Green and White mountains, on Mount Katahdin.

But since ''most of Maine's remote woods are decidedly industrial," they write, ''most of [the] ancient groves are next to villages and cities" -- like Bonney Woods in Farmington and similar groves in Brunswick, Norway, and Waldoboro.

The forests they describe -- complete with explicit directions for visiting them -- are ''the real forests," they write, ''forests with charisma, forests that inspire, forests that can stimulate a sense of time immemorial."

Much like the ancient forests that were once thought to have been virtually wiped out by centuries of land-clearing and logging, covered bridges were thought to be a vanishing part of the New England landscape. Originally built to provide protection from the weather for wooden road surfaces, they survived in rural areas even after road surfaces were paved.

Well into the early years of the last century, write Benjamin D. Evans and June R. Evans in ''New England's Covered Bridges," there were about a thousand such structures in New England. ''The ravages of time, storm, flood, neglect, and the destructive nature of society's vandals" reduced their numbers to about 200.

But, beginning in the 1950s, covered-bridge enthusiasts -- like the Evanses -- began working ''to save, preserve, and rehabilitate these lovely treasures created by artisans long past but not forgotten."

The Evanses provide a clear description of the various styles of covered-bridge design and construction, as well as details of each bridge.

A number of covered bridges have been built in recent years, mainly by enthusiasts on private property. But the North Hartland bridge is on a public road. Originally, there were ''twin" bridges built in the 1870s to span the Ottauquechee River where there is a small island in midstream. The original east bridge survives; its west twin was lost in a flood in 1938 and replaced by a steel and concrete bridge. The 2001 replacement, the Evanses write, ''is truly a twenty-first century covered bridge built in the tradition of the nineteenth century."

In ''Weekend Walks in Historic New England," Robert J. Regalbuto's contributions to the summertime explorer's kit are his introductions to several places not ordinarily visited -- unfortunately, as in the case of Fall River, rather too brief introductions. The better-known destinations like Salem receive well-planned and well-mapped walking tours, but somewhat too much attention is given to places like Boston, already well covered by other guidebooks.

Even reader-explorers who think they know New England well will find among Regalbuto's 45 walks enough destinations to fill summer weekends.

Last summer, a dozen high school students from the Odyssey School at South Boston High School headed out to the Boston Harbor Islands with notebooks and cameras. They came back with ''Adventures on the Boston Harbor Islands," a lively guide to having a good time ''no matter how weird or cool you are."

Michael Kenney writes every other month about new books of regional and local interest.
