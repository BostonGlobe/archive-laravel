BETWEEN THE LINES WITH ANITA SILVEY

Of green eggs and an underage spy

By Robin Dougherty  |  June 27, 2004

Anita Silvey cannot only define good children's literature, but she also knows it when she sees it.

ADVERTISEMENT

Having read at least 125,000 books written for small folk in her 35-year career as an editor for Horn Book magazine and children's book publisher for Houghton Mifflin, she faced a formidable task in compiling "100 Best Books for Children" (Houghton Mifflin, $20), her new volume for children and parents alike, with separate sections for board books, picture books, and books for readers of different levels up to age 12. Narrowing down the list "was the hard part. Everything else was like eating ice cream," she says.

How to choose among the Muggles, the Borrowers, and the hobbits? "Winnie-the-Pooh" or "Pippi Longstocking"? "Misty of Chincoteague" or "The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe"? While putting together the 100, Silvey says, "I talked to cabdrivers and people at the stores, and asked them 'What are the books that sparked [special] memories?' And that started to crystallize the list for me."

It doesn't come as a surprise that the first book in the board book section is "Good Night Moon," by Margaret Wise Brown -- I think the only child who doesn't have this book is the one who hasn't been conceived yet. What is surprising is that although it was published in 1947, it really didn't become the hit it is now until the 1970s. Why?

It really had a slow beginning. I think it did not take off until those children who had known it as children had a chance to use it with their own families. What we call a classic is what moves to the next generation.

"Harriet the Spy," by Louise Fitzhugh, got horrible reviews when it came out. How did it come to be a success?

It was massacred by the reviewers -- it got some of the most vicious reviews in the history [of children's literature].

People weren't ready for a cynical child?

The new realism in children's books dates from "Harriet." People in the publishing houses at the time were appalled by it. She really behaves dramatically differently than the heroines in the books that preceded her. . . . The old guard didn't like it, [but] it was immediately popular with the readership. They started Harriet the Spy fan clubs.

She's kind of a prototypical journalist. She knew that her mission in life was to take notes.

She really was. Another book at the same time that met with resistance was "The Phantom Tollbooth" [by Norton Juster]. When kids found it, it was an immediate success, but it was so different from anything published at the time. Jason Epstein, Random House's brilliant editor for years, had an imprint. He published "Tollbooth" on his imprint. The New Yorker picked it up and had a fantastic review of it, and that got it going.

"The Cricket in Times Square," by George Selden, E. B. White's "Charlotte's Web," and the "Harry Potter" series, by J. K. Rowling, were also met with controversy at first. Does this speak to our uncertainty about what is appropriate for kids to be reading?

I've always believed that great books for children break the mold in a particular way. Whatever that mold is of your time -- those artists who cut through this see a book in a new way. The most interesting thing is that our most censored and challenged books for children tend to be the greatest books for children. That's consistent from the 1930s on. "The Story of Ferdinand" [by Munro Leaf] -- about a bull who would rather eat daisies than fight -- was the most controversial book of its time, coming out as it did at the time of the Spanish Civil War.

The qualities that make books great or different or unusual are going to disturb someone. So "Harry Potter" is no different than all of its predecessors. "Where the Wild Things Are" -- it's sort of hard when you look at them now. If you contrast [Robert] McCluskey's ["Make Way for Ducklings"] with [Maurice] Sendak -- they were creating books at the same time. Sendak took the picture books to a new place.

In your entry on "Harriet the Spy," you tell the back story of how the book was elicited by an editor from Louise Fitzhugh, who had come up with the character. This editorial inspiration seems to be a constant theme in children's publishing.

The editors of children's books have played an enormous role in not only finding [books] but getting them to the final form that they are. . . . "Harriet" is particularly interesting in that way. It really came in as only a set of notes.

Talk about the story behind "The Cat in the Hat," which, among other things, put an end to Sally, Dick and Jane.

Seuss is so famous but he was turned down by everybody. Twenty-seven people had said no. He literally ran into a Dartmouth friend who had just been named a children's editor. He was ready to [give up]. That was "[And to Think That I Saw It on] Mulberry Street." Two decades later, an educator at Houghton, head of the school division, approached Seuss and said if we could put together what you know about entertaining and what we know about education, we could revolutionize reading. What [editor William Spaulding] gave Seuss was 200 words and said, we know these words work with children at the end of first grade and beginning of second. He said it was the hardest book he ever wrote. [Later] he took 50 words and did "Green Eggs and Ham."

Do you think that part of the appeal of children's literature for adults is that it represents a time in which we had a common culture? Our adult reading culture is so fractioned -- but we all remember "Charlotte's Web."

I think children's books come closer to a common culture than anything we have in adult books. More Americans have read "Charlotte's Web" than have read the bestsellers. Books at that time have more impact. There are more people who remember [Virginia Lee Burton's] "Mike Mulligan [and His Steam Shovel]" than anything on the New York Times bestseller list right now. What are those books that tie us together? What do we have in common with each other, that adults can still love and children can love?

Robin Dougherty, a writer and critic, lives in Washington, D.C. Her column appears every other week. She can be reached at inkrd@aol.com.
