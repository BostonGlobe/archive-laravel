ON CRIME

Military maelstrom; corruption on high; framed but good

By Hallie Ephron  |  June 27, 2004

Conspiracies thrive in crime fiction. In Lee Child's "The Enemy," the conspiracy is within the military. It's New Year's Day, 1990. The Berlin Wall is coming down, the Cold War has ended, and the Army is already at war with itself.

In this series prequel, Jack Reacher is a 30-year-old elite military police officer who finds himself transferred stateside. No sooner does he arrive in North Carolina than a two-star general dies of a heart attack, apparently after an assignation at a seedy nearby motel. Mysteriously missing is the general's briefcase, containing the agenda of a top-secret meeting he'd flown in to attend.

Colonel Garber calls from D.C. and orders Reacher to control the situation. Since he's a good soldier, he instinctively salutes and marches. He goes to the general's Virginia home to give the widow a sanitized version of her husband's death and finds she's been murdered. As other senior officers who were supposed to attend the secret meeting die, Reacher's investigation is stymied by Garber's demand that he back off or else.

Reacher knows he's risking his career, but he can't leave it alone. More than a good soldier, this is a guy with his own lawless sense of justice and a vigilante streak. His search for the truth takes him into the Army's highest echelons.

The military setting feels authentic and the dialogue dead-on, though Reacher seems a whole lot crustier and world-weary than your average 30-something. I was riveted as Reacher's painstaking investigation returned, over and over, to the same apparently impossible conclusion.

The "hooker with a heart of gold" may be alive and well somewhere in crime fiction, but she's nowhere to be found in Lynne Heitman's "First Class Killing." In this third Alex Shanahan novel, OrangeAir unwittingly provides a front for a prostitution ring of stewardesses catering to businessmen flying first class. Once a manager for the airline, Alex is now a private investigator who goes undercover as a stewardess to infiltrate the group.

Every stewardess/prostitute in this story is a nasty piece of work, and Angela Velasco, the local madam, is the nastiest. Alex tries to worm her way into Angela's good graces by offering to provide business advice to compete against an encroaching West Coast operation. What Alex is after are computer files, irrefutable proof of the ring's existence.

"You have to find what it is, those deep, dark desires that a man -- or a woman -- has no choice but to give in to," Angela says, confiding her business philosophy. "You find what it is that can hurt them, and then you squeeze until you get what you want."

Things get ugly when Angela insists that Alex turn a trick to show she can be trusted, and Alex finds herself playing a game of Madonna-style truth or dare with this dangerous opponent. Soon the two are locked in a struggle for survival, each having figured out how to destroy what matters most to the other.

Though explicit sex is offstage, this action-filled thriller packs an erotic punch. The novel gathers strength as it goes along, exploring prostitution in the broadest sense of the word, and the lengths to which people and big business will go to protect their bottom line.

If Dave Barry moved to Passaic, N.J., and wrote legal thrillers, they'd probably come out something like David Rosenfelt's Andy Carpenter series. In "Bury the Lead," the third series installment, the millionaire lawyer is defending journalist David Cummings for the murders of three women, all found with their hands severed. Cummings insists he's being framed by the real killer, who's been writing to him, using the reporter as a conduit to get information to the police and the public.

Cummings's defense looks like a lost cause when the police discover gruesome evidence in his home, but Carpenter sticks with it, in part out of loyalty to his good friend newspaper owner Vince Saunders. His client is not only Saunders's star reporter, he's also his son.

Serial killer, severed hands -- it could be pretty gruesome. But it's not. Rosenfelt doesn't dwell on the women whose naked bodies continue to accumulate, or on the details of their deaths. Running counter to the horror is a first-person narrative written in the present tense that reads like an extended joke. Sounds bizarre, but it works.

"As soon as I walk in, the woman gives me the eye," the story begins; only it's not a bar he's walked into, it's a laundromat. A physically unimpressive specimen and self-deprecating to a fault, Carpenter quips, "It's safe to say that over the years I've gotten the finger more than the eye."

The book has a clever plot and breezy style. It's full of colorful, well-drawn characters. There's Carpenter's girlfriend, Laurie, an ex-cop, willing and able to break noses to dispatch Tony Soprano-style bad guys; there's a former death-row inmate who runs an animal shelter and interviews owners before giving away dogs; and there's a very personable golden retriever.

Occasionally the courtroom shenanigans go over the top, but amid the bada-boom and bada-binging, "Bury the Lead" is an absorbing legal thriller.

Hallie Ephron is co-author of the Dr. Peter Zak series of psychological mystery thrillers by G. H. Ephron.
