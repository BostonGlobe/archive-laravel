Learning from 'competitive storytelling'

By Robin Dougherty  |  June 13, 2004

David Sedaris's new collection ranges from his trademark idiosyncratic reminiscences of his childhood in Raleigh, N.C., to musings on the curb appeal of Anne Frank's house in Amsterdam. Fans will recognize his demented humor, which surfaces here in such offhand comments about his childhood neighborhood as: "I hoped that in walking around after dark I might witness a murder, but for the most part our neighbors just sat in their living rooms, watching TV."

"Dress Your Family in Corduroy and Denim" (Little, Brown, $25) -- the title comes from partner Hugh Hamrick's dream image of a book with this name -- is the first collection by the frequent contributor to National Public Radio's "This American Life" and New Yorker essayist since 2000's "Me Talk Pretty One Day." Sedaris's other works include "Holidays on Ice" (1997), "Naked" (also 1997), and "Barrel Fever" (1994), not to mention his radio breakthrough in 1992, "The Santaland Diaries," an essay in which he famously wrote about being a department-store elf. (A Wayne Wang movie adaptation of "Me Talk Pretty One Day" has been shelved.)

Sedaris recently relocated from Paris to London for most of the year. He spoke by phone from Pittsburgh, a stop on his book tour. At the end of the interview, he obliged my request to sing the Oscar Mayer wiener song in the voice of Billie Holiday, something he hadn't done in years, he said.

Q. Are people surprised when they see you in person and you don't look like a department-store elf?

A. The problem is, I do look like one. They're not that surprised.

Q. Your family is remarkably creative, but in your stories about your childhood, you never even mention construction paper, much less art lessons. Did your parents encourage your talents?

A. They encouraged my sister Gretchen. She went to sketching camp and watercolor day school, but I wasn't writing at the time. I wanted to be like my sister Gretchen. I never had a lick of talent, and Amy was always the funniest person in the room. But I don't think my parents thought you could make a living at that.

Q. Did your parents make art?

A. No, my dad was an engineer for IBM. . . . My mother was good at drawing Woody Woodpecker. It was the only thing she could draw, and she would do it as many times as we asked. She couldn't draw anything else. One time she sculpted a little head.

Q. Did you get your sense of humor from your mother?

A. I think so. My sister Lisa had a theory [that] you can tell if someone is from a large family. If someone is a single child, they can tell a long rambling story. In my family you just wanted my mother's attention. If you were telling a story, if it was no good, someone would just interrupt you to tell their own story. You had to beef it up, to exaggerate, to get rid of extra characters. . . .

It was competitive storytelling -- but we were always interested in each other's stories. My sister Lisa's stories are told from a hapless [point of view]. Things happen to her, where my sister Tiffany is an active character in her stories. My sister Amy is not really a storyteller. She comments on the world around her. My brother Paul can imitate anyone.

Q. What about your sister Gretchen?

A. She's boring. [Pause.] She has a more visual approach.

Q. Did you write as a kid?

A. I was the kind of person who wrote his papers the night before they were due. It was a chore, but I think often the way people are taught to write -- the topic sentence and supporting sentences -- makes it seem mathematical and boring. I started keeping a diary when I was 20. It was awful, but I was free from form because no one was going to see it, and when you are free from form, you see "Oh, there's a reason for the structure."

Q. What provoked you to start keeping a journal at 20?

A. I was hitchhiking, I was in my Tom Joad phase. I was writing letters and I didn't have an address. People couldn't write me back. I thought, I'll just write to myself. I just started. I was in a coffee shop and I turned the place mat over. I have no natural talent -- just patience and the ability to do something at the same time every day.

Q. Have you written a piece in your head before you sit down?

A. If . . . something happens and I talk about it, it makes it 10 times harder to write about, because I know where I'm going. . . . If the writer's not surprised, the reader won't be either.

Q. Do you ever worry that you'll run out of stories about your family?

A. The moment I turned my book in, I heard this [new] story. I gave my brother [an obscene wooden] monkey I bought about six years ago. . . . My sister Lisa was visiting and she said, "That's horrible." So he hid it in her bag of espresso, which he thought she'd find the next morning. But instead, she had regular coffee. She picked up her stuff and went to Winston-Salem [N.C.] to visit her in-laws, who were preparing to move into a retirement home. Her father-in-law had early-stage Alzheimer's, but one of the things he could do was make coffee. She came down in the morning and there he was, a retired Baptist minister holding [this] monkey.

Q. You took a huge risk in writing "Possession," your essay about falling in love with the real estate potential of the Anne Frank house, after apartment hunting in Paris.

The worst fear is that you would buy an apartment and then see an apartment that you liked more than your own. It's the same with the Anne Frank house, if you have real estate fever. I don't know how successful [the piece] is, but it was a true fever. I actually felt there was the possibility of buying this apartment. I had that same feeling of viewing an apartment during an open house. "We have to act now." If it had been an open house, I would have bought it impulsively. . . .

A. I think it's OK to laugh. We're not talking about Anne Frank. We're talking about her house. If you're not hiding from the Nazis and you could open the windows, it's adorable. And it's a triplex.

Q. Some of the other pieces also involved taking artistic risks: "The End of the Affair," about saying the wrong thing at the movies with Hugh, and "A Million Bubbles," in which your father throws you out of the house because you're gay. These are more serious pieces, not just observational humor.

A. I [once] thought if people weren't laughing, they weren't listening. You can feel people listening or not listening, and people didn't leave [when I read the serious pieces]. . . . It felt good to be telling a different kind of story. . . . I've proved I can get a laugh with the story, and I thought, "What happens if you say what you really felt?"

Robin Dougherty, a writer and critic, lives in Washington, D.C. She can be reached at inkrd@aol.com.
