Preserving Yiddish and a way of life

Priceless books for future generations

By Judy Bolton-Fasman  |  November 21, 2004

Outwitting History: The Amazing Adventures of a Man Who Rescued a Million Yiddish Books
By Aaron Lansky
Algonquin, 316 pp., $24.95

''The making of books is without limit." Aaron Lansky would have appreciated that aphorism from Ecclesiastes as he stood in front of the dumpster in the freezing rain. Inside, a trove of Yiddish books was about to be ruined. This was the early 1980s, and in Yiddish circles everyone knew that Lansky was the person to call if you had Yiddish books to give away. That previous night, Lansky had answered a call at midnight at home in Western Massachusetts. This time it wasn't an elderly Jew looking to donate books to Lansky's fledgling National Yiddish Book Center. It was a friend who had spotted thousands of Yiddish books about to be thrown away on Manhattan's West Side. A few hours later Lansky was bound for New York. Looking back, it may have been the crowning incident in which he ''decided to save the world's Yiddish books."

At the onset of Lansky's quest, scholars estimated that only 70,000 Yiddish books remained in the world. Twenty-five years later, Lansky and his colleagues --many of them elderly Jews who served as volunteer book collectors -- have amassed more than a million and a half books, many of them unique.

What began as a quixotic journey was also a picaresque romp, a detective story, a profound history lesson, and a poignant evocation of a bygone world. Lansky's extraordinary storytelling conveys the daunting task he set for himself. Books that weren't rescued from dumpsters or abandoned buildings were lovingly wrested from elderly Jews. Emptying their modest bank accounts to rent trucks, Lansky and his friends pulled up in front of countless apartment buildings and spent hours loading boxes of books into a U-Haul.

Lansky was acutely aware of entering a world slowly deteriorating, respectful of how he carried out his mission. He regarded his book pick-ups as engaging ''in a ritual of cultural transmission." Pulling up in front of an elderly man's apartment building in Atlantic City was the beginning of ''a long afternoon. Every book he handed me had its story. . . . He was handing me not merely his books but his world, his 'yerushe,' the inheritance his own children had rejected."

If language is the flame underneath America's melting pot, it is not surprising that Jews who were anxious to be accepted as Americans had no use for Yiddish. Yiddish was like an embarrassing relative from the old country. In America, Yiddish fell victim to its checkered history. It had never been the official language of a country and was initially limited to the spoken word. Scholars and rabbis in Europe wrote and studied in Hebrew and Aramaic, speaking only Yiddish at home. But in Eastern Europe something thrilling happened on the way to the Enlightenment in the late 19th century. Yiddish thrived in newspapers, magazines, theater, politics, art, and a free-wheeling literature that marked ''one of the most concentrated periods of literary creativity in all of Jewish history."

The Yiddish renaissance between 1864 and 1939 produced more than 30,000 volumes. In this golden age of Yiddish literature writers experimented in almost every genre, including erotica and surrealism. This vast literary enterprise came to a brutal end when Hitler invaded Poland in 1939. And it seemed that it was relegated to oblivion on Aug. 12, 1952, when Joseph Stalin ordered Yiddish writers in the Soviet Union murdered.

In light of these horrific events it is no wonder that many, including Yiddish speakers, felt that instilling a love of Yiddish language to the next generation was doomed. By the time Lansky entered the picture his ''work in rescuing Yiddish books" was seen ''not as a triumph but as a sign of their own defeat: If they had been doing their jobs, if they had succeeded in conveying Yiddish culture to their children, then Yiddish books wouldn't need to be rescued. Of course, the historical depredations and upheavals that unseated Yiddish were hardly their fault. But they couldn't see that, and their myopia only compounded the tragedy."

But Lansky transformed tragedy into unprecedented opportunity. During the 1980s books continued to stream into the Amherst warehouse, and the center's operating budget steadily grew. The Jewish establishment began to take notice of his thriving organization. It became clear that Lansky was not creating a ''genizah" -- a mausoleum for sacred Jewish texts -- but instituting ''a clearinghouse through which we could place old books in the hands of new readers." In 1997 the National Yiddish Book Center took up residence in its own state-of-the-art building on the campus of Hampshire College. At the time of its dedication, the center had placed Yiddish books in more than 500 national and university libraries in 26 countries.

Lansky continues to fulfill his vision in the most Jewish of ways -- through a steady accretion of acts of lovingkindness and with an unshakable faith in the future of Jewish life. In epitomizing the Talmudic dictum that ''although one is not required to complete a task, neither is one allowed to desist from trying," he has enriched the world by collecting and reprinting forgotten books ''without limit."

Judy Bolton-Fasman is a research associate at the Hadassah-Brandeis Institute, where she is working on a memoir about saying the Kaddish for her father.
