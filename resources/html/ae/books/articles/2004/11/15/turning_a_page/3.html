Turning a page

November 15, 2004

Page 3 of 3 -- Along with her explorations of the Western canon, she delved deeply into the complex tapestry of Jewish culture, literature, and spirituality. Her writings also include fiery devotion to Israel as a Jewish state, and -- controversially to many -- withering scorn for Palestinian nationalism. Fiction poured out too, novels as well as stories. Short works such as ''Envy" and ''The Shawl" are widely anthologized, and novels include ''The Cannibal Galaxy," ''The Messiah of Stockholm," and the achingly sad and funny ''Puttermesser Papers," a finalist for the National Book Award. She has also published poetry.

Ozick says she was ''burning with writerly power" and ambition in her early years, and sometimes with anger about not being taken quite seriously, as a woman writer, by the literary establishment. She bridles at the memory of the eminent critic who referred to her as ''a lady writer." When ''Trust" was published, it got a generous review in Time magazine, but the reviewer referred to her as a housewife.

''I ghost-wrote a letter from my husband," she said, ''testifying from the condition of the household that I was not a housewife. They published it." If not a housewife, she was a mother (and now twice a grandmother). Rachel Hallote was born in 1965. An archeologist of the Near East, she is now director of Jewish studies at the State University of New York at Purchase.

Unlike Updike, Roth, and others of their cohort, Ozick never led a public literary life, her stature and lengthening shelf of books notwithstanding. She was an Alfred A. Knopf author for 32 years, until moving to Houghton Mifflin for ''Heir to the Glimmering World," and in all that time never had a book tour. Why not? she was asked. She shrugged and said: ''Because they never asked me."

Houghton Mifflin jumped at the chance to add Ozick to its list, though one might not think of her as having obvious appeal to the youthful readers all publishers lust after. Her style is not necessarily easy, her themes are sometimes recondite, and one is wise to keep a dictionary near at hand.

But Janet Silver, Houghton's vice president and publisher, begs to differ about the novel's potential appeal. ''We felt this was a book that was approachable by college-age readers," Silver said. ''I think younger readers are hungry for something of substance."

''Heir to the Glimmering World" is a first-person account of a teenage orphan girl in the Depression, hired to be the amanuensis of a German/Jewish immigrant scholar who specializes in an ancient Jewish heretical sect. The book has echoes of Vladimir Nabokov's ''Lolita," George Eliot's ''Middlemarch," Jane Austen's novels, and the sad story of Christopher Milne, the real-life model for A.A. Milne's Christopher Robin.

Ozick's substance is rooted in the masters. Forster's ''A Passage to India," she said, ''is for me the greatest novel in English of the 20th century." She kept a copy of it on the corner of her desk throughout the writing of ''Heir to the Glimmering World," whose reviews have been mostly favorable, or at least respectful.

Her national book tour, she says, has been an astonishing experience. Despite her literary stature, her privacy had never been much disturbed by actual readers, but now they've been standing before her, telling her about their lives and about what her books have meant to them. It's another echo of the world of Eliot or James, without mediation by television or the Internet, only connection with people who hold her book in their hands.

''I have never felt I had a readership," Ozick said. ''I live a very isolated life. I don't have any sense of my life beyond sitting at my desk [in her Westchester home] with my pen. I have been so startled, absolutely marveling, at these long lines of people with books for me to sign. Each encounter is simply amazing. Where do they come from? How do they know I'm here?"
