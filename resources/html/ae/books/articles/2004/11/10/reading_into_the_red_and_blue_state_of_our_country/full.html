Reading into the red and blue state of our country

By David Mehegan, Globe Staff  |  November 10, 2004

Surely there has never been an election year so freighted with partisan books as 2004 -- books designed to push people one way or the other. Indeed, at times in the last year the nonfiction bestseller lists were so choked with angry or accusing books that it seemed little else was of interest to American readers.

There were books of venom and rage, such as Ann Coulter's ''How to Talk to a Liberal (If You Must)," or, from the opposite wing, Al Franken's ''Lies and the Lying Liars Who Tell Them: A Fair and Balanced Look at the Right." Then came the overt attack books, notably ''Unfit For Command," John O'Neill and Jerome Corsi's attempt to discredit John Kerry's Vietnam service, or ''The Family," Kitty Kelley's many-pronged assault on the whole Bush family.

There were serious policy books, to be sure, such as former Treasury secretary Robert Rubin's ''In an Uncertain World: Tough Choices from Wall Street to Washington," Richard Clarke's ''Against All Enemies: Inside America's War on Terror," and ''The 9/11 Commission Report" (a finalist for the National Book Award). But even these books were mostly viewed through the campaign lens: Would they help or hurt John Kerry or George W. Bush?

With the furious campaign over, that question is moot. But many people are emerging from their storm cellars, looking for books that might answer deeper and different questions: What is going on in our country and society? How do we understand ourselves in the early 21st century? Are we wholly red and blue, two nations with nothing in common? Here are some books that grapple with those questions.

One of the most talked-about books, especially since the election, is Thomas Frank's ''What's the Matter With Kansas? How Conservatives Won the Heart of America" (Metropolitan Books, $24). Journalist Frank, a Kansas native, writes about how the Republican Party won the loyalty of hard-working rural people by appealing to their fears and emotions, causing them to vote against what Frank believes is their own economic and social interest.

Frank writes that ''this species of derangement is the bedrock of our civic order; it is the foundation" of the Republican Party's control of Congress and the White House, and to him the word ''derangement" seems to be the key. Conservatism here is a matter of class: people with little security or wealth latching on to traditional values against those (mainly liberals, but sometimes moderate Republicans) whom they perceive as elites.

A familiar quasi-conservative face is that of David Brooks, New York Times op-ed columnist and panelist on PBS's ''NewsHour With Jim Lehrer." Brooks's new book, quite different in tone from Frank's, is ''On Paradise Drive: How We Live Now (And Always Have) in the Future Tense" (Simon & Schuster, $25). Brooks's mild and usually reasonable conservative manner (unlike Coulter, he clearly does have liberal friends) is evident, and one can't imagine him calling Americans deranged.

A growing heartland of America, Brooks writes, is not on the coasts and not in the cities, but in vast suburban counties, where people are mostly optimistic and content with their lot. He's not writing about the rich or poor, but about ''the moderately affluent strivers." The book is more of a long essay than a study, and one does come away wondering if Brooks's conclusions are, as the social scientists say, either valid or reliable. But they are not nearly so dark as Frank's. Brooks concludes that Americans are still idealistic and hopeful, ''motivated by the Paradise Spell, by the feeling that there is some glorious destiny just ahead."

Everyone's talking about the political impact of conservative Christians as if they were something new in American politics. (Remember the Pilgrims? Prohibition?) Several Democratic operatives quoted recently have said darkly, ''We don't understand those people." Well, here is a book that takes a crack at it: ''Spirit and Flesh: Life in a Fundamentalist Baptist Church," by James M. Ault Jr. (Knopf, $27.95). Ault, a filmmaker, spent three years immersed in the life of the Shawmut River Baptist Church in Worcester, led by pastor Frank Valenti, before writing this book.

In a style reminiscent of William Langewiesche or Tracy Kidder, Ault explores the small community's handling of moral issues such as abortion or divorce, how it functions socially, and how its spiritual underpinnings relate to people's everyday lives. He describes Bible study groups, youth programs, men's and women's activities. Ault does not regard these people as mysterious or nutty; in fact, in the end he has his own spiritual awakening.

Campaign strategist and pollster Stanley Greenberg is a liberal Democrat, and some might doubt the implied balance of his book, ''The Two Americas: Our Current Political Deadlock and How to Break It" (St. Martin's, $25.95). Yet he comes down almost as hard on today's Democrats as on Republicans. In sections called ''The World of Republican Partisans," and ''The World of Partisan Democrats," Greenberg paints pictures of people with good reasons for their views of the world, but with little understanding of their counterparts. He doesn't call anybody deranged but argues that both parties are responding to authentic needs and longings in society, and that a common consent could be within reach if the demonization of the opposition would stop.

Politicians talk much about protecting the middle class, but who are those people? Alan Wolfe, director of the Boisi Center for Religion and American Public Life at Boston College, gives answers in ''One Nation, After All: What Middle-Class Americans Really Think About: God, Country, Family, Racism, Welfare, Immigration, Homosexuality, Work, the Right, the Left, and Each Other" (Penguin Paperback, $11).

This book came out in 1998, but Americans can't have changed that much since then -- even with 9/11. Wolfe's tone is a refreshing switch from the toxic recent rhetoric. A careful researcher and graceful writer, he is never bombastic or judgmental. For this book, he compiled 200 interviews all across what we now call the blue and red states, and he finds a surprising commonality of values.

While more than 80 percent of Americans profess religious belief, for example, Wolfe finds no huge religious revival going on, and finds that most people support a reasonable separation of religion and politics. Americans remain broadly tolerant, though straight America is uneasy about gay America. Even so, Wolfe writes, ''I am persuaded from the results of this study that there is little truth to the charge that middle-class America, divided by a culture war, has split into two hostile camps. Middle-class Americans, in their heart of hearts, are desperate that we once again become one nation." One hopes he's right.
