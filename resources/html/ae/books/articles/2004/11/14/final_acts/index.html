Final acts

A biography's last volume offers a thorough, occasionally tedious portrait of a writer at the height of his fame

By Andrew Biswell  |  November 14, 2004

The Life of Graham Greene: Volume Three -- 1955-1991
By Norman Sherry
Viking, 906 pp., illustrated, $39.95

How many people under the age of 40 are still reading Graham Greene? For my generation, growing up in the 1970s and 1980s, Greene was the great elder statesman of English fiction. In novels like ''Our Man in Havana," ''The Comedians," and ''The Human Factor," he seemed to offer sophisticated responses to the political crises of the time. His nonfiction books, such as ''Ways of Escape" and ''Getting to Know the General," engaged with international conflicts and emergencies. Unlike many English writers, his geographical canvas was truly global. He was regarded by many as the conscience of his generation, and of ours.

Clearly the world has moved on since Greene's death in 1991, and it is worth asking how meaningful his writing is to readers who have come to maturity since the end of the Cold War, which provided the backdrop to much of his later fiction. The works that speak most urgently to the 21st century are perhaps not the ones that used to be thought important. My undergraduate students are on the whole unexcited by the theological paradoxes that Greene puts forward in ''The Power and the Glory" and ''The Heart of the Matter." They prefer his more formally experimental books, such as ''Stamboul Train," ''It's a Battlefield," and ''The End of the Affair." Greene the Catholic novelist seems to have been displaced by Greene the literary modernist.

The question of where the center of Greene's creativity might lie is scarcely touched on in Norman Sherry's three-volume authorized biography. In the course of more than 2,000 pages, Sherry tries his best to avoid such discriminations, with the result that we often feel marooned, without a map or a navigator, in a vast ocean of biographical text. Although documents such as letters, diaries, and poems are quoted from at unusual (and sometimes tedious) length, there is not much in the way of informed literary criticism. If Greene wrote a thing or said it, then Sherry believes in its significance. For a reader who shares the biographer's manic devotion to his subject, this is well and good. But anyone who comes to the book in the expectation that Sherry will build a serious argument for Greene as a major writer -- surely one of a literary biographer's primary tasks -- will be disappointed.

Born in 1904, Greene attended Berkhamsted School, where his father was headmaster. He was persecuted by the other pupils, who believed him to be a spy. He suffered a nervous breakdown and made several suicide attempts before he had turned 20. The theme of betrayal, which Greene returns to in almost every novel, probably arose from these early traumas at school, as he acknowledges in his psychoanalytic autobiography, ''A Sort of Life." After a short career as a poet at Oxford, he published his first novel, ''The Man Within," at the age of 24. It was an immediate success, and he lived entirely by his pen and typewriter thereafter. In the 1930s he reviewed films for the Spectator and Night and Day magazine, although the second of these publications was forced out of business after Shirley Temple, age 7, sued Greene for libel. His passion for film was undiminished by this episode, and in 1949 he wrote the script for ''The Third Man," directed by Carol Reed.

Always a restless traveller -- to Liberia, to Vietnam, to Mexico, to a leper colony in the Congo -- Greene left England for good after the collapse of his marriage (to Vivien Dayrell-Browning, the mother of his children) in 1965. He settled in Antibes, in the south of France, where he continued to produce novels, plays, and short stories at a rate that disconcerted the critics. His works of the 1960s and 1970s are characterized by a suavely ironic tone, which is half a world away from the gloomy hellfire Catholicism of the pre-war ''Brighton Rock." The turning point in his private life occurred in 1959, when he met Yvonne Cloetta, a French woman who remained his constant companion for the last 32 years of his life. His stylistic reinvention as a comedian coincided with the flourishing of his affair with Cloetta.

''I've tried to search out sources of Greene's work," writes Sherry, ''seeking the origins, techniques and models that stand behind his fictional characters." As a methodology, this is hopeless, since it insists on a strictly literal interpretation of the novels. There is little room in Sherry's biographical equation for the alchemy of art. He sees Greene as a pedestrian writer of reportage and documentary, a crude transcriber of the actual. If that were true, then it is unlikely that his life and work would have been worth investigating in the first place.

Sherry is at his best when he writes about the political history of the places that Greene visited. His accounts of the British General Strike, the Malayan Emergency, the war in French Indochina, and Franois ''Papa Doc" Duvalier's reign of terror in Haiti are exemplary.

Curiously, the story of Sherry's first meeting with Greene has altered since he originally recounted it in Volume 1, published in 1989. In the first version, Sherry wrote: ''We were crossing St James's Street in London and narrowly escaped being knocked down by a taxi. . . . He laughed and I knew we had become friends." These days Sherry tells it rather differently: ''He ran across the road . . . black cabs like beetles everywhere, and one of them knocked him down. He was lying in the road -- I remember water running up his trouser leg." It seems to me that to be knocked down by a taxi is not at all the same thing as to narrowly escape the same fate. Which version of the story is true? Bearing in mind that Greene was still alive (and likely to object to inaccuracies) when Volume 1 appeared, I am inclined to think that the most recent account is partly fantasized or hallucinated. An awkward question arises from this: how can a literalist biographer, who makes extravagant claims to be led by the facts, fail to relate his first meeting with Greene consistently? Sherry would have written a better book, and perhaps a more intelligent one, if he had taken the trouble to reflect on such matters.

Andrew Biswell lectures in contemporary English literature at Manchester Metropolitan University, England.
