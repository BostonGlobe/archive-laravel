Shedding light on Franklin's compromises

By Eric Arnesen  |  November 14, 2004

Runaway America: Benjamin Franklin, Slavery, and the American Revolution
By David Waldstreicher
Hill and Wang, 315 pp., $25

ADVERTISEMENT

Americans, at least the book-purchasing public, can't seem to get enough of the Founding Fathers. Treated individually or collectively, the Founders remain the subject of intense fascination; books roll off the scholarly assembly line to satisfy our seemingly insatiable curiosity. Some are feel-good Founder studies, designed to inspire awe, reverence, or at least deep appreciation. Others lay on the moral judgments, excoriating the men who fought for independence from Great Britain and established the new republic for their ostensible hypocrisy (especially with regard to race, slavery, and the fate of Africans in America). Whether celebratory or condemnatory, the Founder fetish shows little sign of abating.

Benjamin Franklin is only the latest of the Founders to command our attention. The subject of several recent biographies by Edmund Morgan and Walter Isaacson, among others, he is now the focus of David Waldstreicher's engaging and provocative ''Runaway America." What Waldstreicher refreshingly offers is less a conventional biography than a critical reconsideration of Franklin's economic and social world and his political thought that focuses on the spectrum of freedom and unfreedom in Colonial America.

The Franklin who appears in his pages is not the ''lovable" Franklin, that devoted and selfless public servant with an ''insatiable curiosity" who is a ''man worth knowing," in the memorable words of the eminent historian Edmund Morgan. Nor is he the antislavery hero that various historians have made him out to be. Rather, in a less hagiographic manner, Waldstreicher offers us a Franklin who was himself a runaway apprentice, an artisan printer-turned-entrepreneur, a shrewd businessman and politician, and an international statesman who found himself inescapably enmeshed in the politics and economics of slavery. His key argument is that ''Franklin's antislavery credentials have been greatly exaggerated," a point he convincingly establishes. His account, however, doesn't aim to ''knock Franklin off his deserved pedestal" but rather to ''restore some measure of reality" and humanity to his life. In this he succeeds well.

The institution of chattel slavery was central to Franklin's Colonial world, Waldstreicher insists. Although slaves forcibly seized and imported from Africa were numerically concentrated in the staple-producing southern colonies in British North America, they also constituted a vital part of the labor force in Massachusetts, New York, and Pennsylvania, especially in their bustling port cities, as household servants, artisans, and common laborers. The middle-class paragon Franklin was no large-scale slaveholder like Thomas Jefferson or George Washington, but over the course of his long life he was ''deeply invested in the system of unfree labor." In his early years as an independent printer, the numerous advertisements from slaveholders seeking the capture of their escaped slaves generated significant revenue for his publishing business; runaways thus captured were brought to his printing shop for retrieval by their legal owners. At times, Franklin owned several slaves. His ''involvement with slavery," Waldstreicher writes, ''was actually quite typical of Pennsylvanians." Yet unlike many other small-scale slaveholders, Franklin was on good terms with early Quaker abolitionists. He was ''at home with theoretical antislavery and slave-ownership."

More important than the economic benefits Franklin derived from slavery were his contributions to the ongoing debates with Great Britain over its relationship with its colonies. As a ''citizen of the empire," Franklin confronted the contradiction that so troubled many of his contemporaries -- namely, white colonists who denounced their political enslavement at the hands of the British were themselves the owners of men and women. Colonists were hardly being disingenuous when they invoked the term ''slavery" to describe their relationship with Britain. ''Precisely because freedom was a relative matter and racial slavery a legal status," Waldstreicher observes, ''slavery and blackness provided potent ways of talking about power: about dependence on others and the ownership of others, and its abuses."

Franklin's solution was to diminish slavery's importance in the colonies. The colonies he represented were populated by ''simple farmers and local tradesmen," not ''slave-driving planters." As the imperial crisis heated up in the 1760s, Franklin made the case ''for North American innocence" by ''underrating" slavery in the mainland colonies and projecting the ''blame for slavery onto Britain and the West Indies." There were few slaves in New England, Franklin inaccurately if plausibly claimed, but he went further, insisting that even in the ''Chesapeake and the Carolinas, slavery is a vestige of privilege for the few," that many sought its abolition, and that slaveholders themselves were kind. At the same time, he disparaged slaves as possessing a ''plotting Disposition, dark, sullen, malicious, revengeful, and cruel in the highest Degree." Franklin's ''anonymous antislavery writings" in the early 1770s, Waldstreicher concludes, must be considered as ''damage control, finesse, or 'spin.' " Only after independence had been secured and his home state had passed a gradual emancipation law did an elderly Franklin accept the figurehead position of president of the Pennsylvania Abolition Society. Franklin publicly assumed an antislavery stance only after it had become ''respectable."

Waldstreicher effectively undermines Franklin's antislavery reputation without falling into the modern habit of extreme moralistic dismissal or outright condemnation. But while he rightly restores unfree labor to the central place it occupied in Colonial and Revolutionary America, slavery was hardly Franklin's only concern, much less his primary focus. If other historians have slighted Franklin's compromises with slavery, critics might argue that Waldstreicher has given them a bit too much weight while slighting other aspects of his political thought and career. Still, if ''Runaway America" is not the definitive Franklin biography -- which it doesn't claim to be -- it is indispensable reading for an understanding of the Founders' generation, for all of its glory and its shortcomings.

Eric Arnesen is professor of history at the University of Illinois at Chicago and the author of ''Black Protest and the Great Migration: A Brief History With Documents."
