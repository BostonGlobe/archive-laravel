Joseph Nye pens a prescient tale

Facts catch up to professor's first fiction

By David Mehegan, Globe Staff  |  November 27, 2004

CAMBRIDGE -- The line between history and fiction is often nebulous, a truth demonstrated anew by current events and a new novel by Joseph S. Nye Jr., former dean of the Kennedy School of Government at Harvard.

"When I started, the plot seemed far-fetched," Nye said in an interview at his Cambridge office. "But as things transpired, I began to think that fact might overtake my fiction."

In "The Power Game," which Nye began in the early 1990s, a Princeton academic joins the US State Department. It soon emerges that Pakistan has made an agreement to sell nuclear weapons to Iran. A fierce battle erupts behind the scenes within the administration over what to do.

Peter Cutler, the academic, hopes to avert a planned commando raid on the Pakistani weapons plant, believing diplomacy would have a better chance of success. As advocates of the military option gain the upper hand, Cutler learns that an old friend from graduate school, a Pakistani scientist sympathetic to the United States, would probably be killed in the raid. Cutler's moral agony and his decisions influence the outcome.

Over the 10 or so years in which Nye's novel, his first, was taking shape, events steadily made its plot less fantastic.

Last year, the United States attacked a predominantly Muslim country, Iraq, in part to prevent it from transferring possible nuclear weapons to Islamic radicals. That policy developed amid an apparent internecine struggle between a US war party (including Vice President Dick Cheney and Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld) and a diplomacy party (headed by Secretary of State Colin Powell).

Earlier this year, Pakistani nuclear scientist A.Q. Kahn's network was found to have sold nuclear technology to North Korea and Libya -- and possibly to Iran -- for which the Pakistani government treated him with notable leniency.

Iran has agreed to halt enrichment of nuclear materials that could be used to build nuclear weapons. US officials have expressed skepticism about Iranian assurances, and speculation is rampant on the practicalities of a US raid on Iran's nuclear facilities. (The December Atlantic Monthly has a story entitled "Is Iran next?," by James Fallows, exploring the risks of such a raid.) Complicating matters further, Israel has reportedly told the Bush administration that it cannot tolerate an Iranian nuclear weapon. Israel carried out just such a raid on Iraq in 1991.

Nye, the Sultan of Oman professor of international relations at Harvard, has spent years in the thick of such debates. He has written hundreds of articles and several books on the uses of power in world affairs. He chaired the National Security Council group on nonproliferation of nuclear weapons in the Carter administration and was assistant secretary of defense for international security affairs under President Clinton.

Framed photographs crowding Nye's small office show him with both presidents Bush as well as Ford, Clinton, and Carter, and with former British prime minister Margaret Thatcher, French president Jacques Chirac, and former vice president Walter Mondale, among others. An enthusiastic fly fisherman, he is also photographed with numerous dead trout and salmon.

"I wrote a book called 'Nuclear Ethics' [1986] about how you lay out the pros and cons and theories behind ethical questions," Nye said, "but there's a certain cut-and-dried nature to that kind of writing that doesn't capture what it's like to go through these situations." That book, he said, entailed "sitting and saying, 'This is OK, and that is not OK, and here's how you make the distinction.' But when you are in the middle of the action, you don't have the luxury of sitting down and figuring these things out. It's like reading the script of a movie as opposed to watching it. When you watch, you have close-ups, dramatic angles, and changing light. It looks so different in motion."

The other theme of "The Power Game" is the seductiveness of power, and the backbiting among insiders who are all sure they are doing what is best for peace and freedom -- and their own careers. Power may not always corrupt, but in Nye's book it is addictive. Even the altruistic Cutler finds power to be a heady drug. But compared to the wily characters in the struggle -- including a woman he's sleeping with behind his wife's back -- he's a babe in arms.

"The game of power can become destructive," Nye said. "The question is, how do you keep from being swept away and using power for immoral purposes? What does the struggle for power do to friendship? What are the obligations of loyalty you have toward friends, and toward people you don't know, and how do you bring those factors into the decisions you make in places like the White House, Pentagon, or State Department?"

The moral struggle is sometimes confused further, Nye says, by external pressure. When you are authorized to use power, and you're limited by time and information, and under the pressure of dangerous events, there's pressure to ignore moral distinctions -- as well as potential risks -- once a call has been made. At one point in the novel, Cutler tries to slow down the momentum of the planning for a military strike. But a colleague says, "Peter, you can't paint vertical stripes on a moving train."

Nye said this tendency was apparent in the march to war in Iraq, "in terms of the original justification, that it was the weapons of mass destruction, that there would not be many deaths, that we would be greeted as liberators. If you tell yourself this will not be a big problem and I'm absolutely certain I'm right, it's not as hard a decision. But if you say, I think it's right in general but if it gets out of hand, then it might not be, you get into tricky moral ground."

Those with power can learn to use it and still sleep at night, even when things do get out of hand. They let historians and journalists sort out the moralities at leisure. Whatever the state of their consciences, however, their blunders have human consequences. Nye points out that power is not the same thing as control: "It's true that men of action make history, but history may not turn out the way they expected."

With all of Nye's direct involvement with history, why would he bother making stuff up? He says he has been asked that question by colleagues, at least implicitly. "Some people were bemused that I would do this," he said. "There is this sort of smile, as if saying, 'Why would you waste your time on something like that?"' His explanation: After he left the State Department in 1980, he longed to capture the moral struggles of power, but even after he finished "Nuclear Ethics" something was missing.

"I felt that I still haven't said what I want to say about this, and I can only do it with fiction," he said. "So I spent a fair amount of time learning how to write fiction -- taking courses and workshops -- because it's a totally different kind of writing. In prose, you're trying to generalize and encompass as much as you can. But in fiction you are particularizing, trying to find a single illustration, that dewdrop on a blade of grass that illuminates something much larger."

It's a paradox: Fiction brings moral issues back from the abstract to the concrete -- makes public events private -- while real-life high-level policymaking tends to do the opposite. Amid the struggles over world power and military strikes, the novel concerns Cutler's affair, his marital problems, his dying father, and lost friendships.

Not that it was all about morality and policy. "I suppose a little of what I was trying to do is draw lessons," said Nye. "But it's not that didactic. It was great fun. It's fun to tell a story."
