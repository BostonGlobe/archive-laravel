The call of the coyote

A sound in the night inspired author Catherine Reidto study -- and admire -- this creature of the woods

By David Mehegan, Globe Staff  |  November 22, 2004

SHELBURNE FALLS -- It's different out here in Franklin County, in the Berkshire hill towns west of Greenfield. Snow and frost come earlier than on the coast, and high promontories with bare tree lines cast deep shadows over the little main streets, making the days seem shorter at one end or the other.

For some, it was once socially different, too. Catherine Reid, 49, returned to these parts in 1998, 25 years after she had left. She and her partner, Holly Iglesias, bought an overgrown farmhouse on a rural road. Soon after they moved in, Reid recalled during an interview in their simple kitchen, they heard in the night "a chorus of raucous, haunting cries. I knew exactly what I was hearing." It was a pack of eastern coyotes.

That call of the wild began the quest narrated in Reid's new book, "Coyote: Seeking the Hunter in Our Midst." She would stalk, and try to see and understand, what she calls a new species of large animal. Not officially a separate species from its western cousin, the eastern coyote's Latin sobriquet is Canis latrans variant, the largest variety of Canis latrans. It has moved southward from eastern Canada since the mid-1950s and now inhabits the entire east as far south as Florida.

Firing Reid's quest was her love-at-first-hearing of a marginal creature, shunned and feared by society, who finds a room of its own. She had first heard of the animal some were calling the "new wolf" when she was in high school in the early 1970s, in Greenfield. It was a time of war and discouragement about the environment, she said, but the appearance of the coyote "was one of the most hopeful signs that life was resilient, that we might brutalize the world around us, yet here in this landscape this new large wild animal had appeared. To me, it was a very exciting response that humans had no control over."

Reid is the second of six children, raised in Greenfield, where her father ran a store. She has three brothers and two sisters, all but one of whom still live in the area, as do her parents. It was a tolerant and loving household, she recalls. But in her early adult years, it became clearer that she was not going to meet conventional expectations of marriage and motherhood. By the time she left for college at Mount Holyoke -- eventually she graduated from Goddard College in Vermont, in 1980 -- she felt out of place in her hometown. "I felt like I had to leave to really be myself," she said. "I didn't fit here."

Except with nature: "I am at heart an introvert, and the woods were quiet and expected nothing of me." In college, she had intended to study wildlife biology but found that most of the nonacademic jobs at that time involved managing animal populations for hunting, trapping, or fishing. At Goddard, she became active in peace and justice causes, which took her far from biology. "I didn't know how to be a humanist in the sciences," she said. With a longing to write, she majored in English, then went on to a master's degree from the University of Maine (1989) and a PhD from Florida State University (1996), both in English.

For a while, Reid stayed on to teach creative writing and women's literature at FSU (where she had met Iglesias). She published poems, short stories, and novellas, mostly in literary journals, but turned increasingly to nonfiction. "The imaginative leaps of fiction were not as easy for me as for others," she said. "Landscape was always such a character in my work." She loved the writings of such naturalists as Edward O. Wilson, Gretel Erlich, and Annie Dillard, and often went to the woods. One summer she hiked the entire Appalachian Trail, and another time canoed the Connecticut River from its source to Long Island Sound.

After leaving Florida in 1997, Reid and Iglesias lived for a time in Washington, D.C., but did not put down roots. "We wanted to be near family, friends, and a Quaker meeting," said Reid, as well as near an airport with frequent flights to Miami, where Iglesias's two married children and one grandchild live. It was Iglesias who proposed Franklin County. Reid was doubtful.

"I didn't think I could live here," she said, "but a lot had changed in my absence. The kinds of things people were interested in, the activities. The more I thought about the colleges and the environment, it made sense. Almost as soon as we arrived, I felt this is where I need to be." After 25 years, "I felt solid in who I am, and that made all the difference." She was welcomed by her family, including her parents (she dedicates her book to them). She currently teaches part time at Smith College, where Iglesias works as an administrator.

After the nighttime cry, Reid listened and looked for coyotes, their signs, and their dens, while immersing herself in the literature and interviewing biologists. More than a book about nature, "Coyotes" is also a narrative about home and family, and about human attitudes toward the wild and the unfamiliar. But it always returns to the woods, filled with wild plants and animals -- foxes, weasels, opossums, and Carolina wrens, and the sign of the coyote.

The eastern coyote is believed to be a hybrid of its sagebrush cousin and a population of gray or red wolves in eastern Canada. Not only is it bigger than the western coyote, but it has somewhat different prey, as well as different social and family habits. Coyotes prey on small rodents or grouse but will also take domestic cats, deer, or unprotected sheep. They will also raid orchards, uncovered garbage, and pet-food dishes left outside.

Their tenacity is remarkable. They can live in a tiny patch of woods or wetland, and studies of coyotes outfitted with radio collars show they routinely travel 10 miles in a night. Though mostly nocturnal, they hunt in the daytime when they have young in the den in spring. They live longer in urban than in rural areas, where they are more likely to be shot. Unlike most other mammals, when food is scarce, they have larger litters -- as many as a dozen pups.

Reid's affection for Canis latrans is not universally shared. She writes that more than 100,000 coyotes east and west are killed each year by hunters and farmers, with no appreciable effect on populations. "I continue to be surprised at the loathing I see toward coyotes," she said. "We are such dog lovers, but people look at a coyote and think, 'Gotta shoot him.' " There is one documented attack on a human in Massachusetts -- a Cape Cod 3-year-old in 1998 -- but similar attacks have been reported in other New England states. Even so, Reid calls the public emergency declared by the town of Hull last spring, after coyotes were spotted near a public school, an overreaction. If people kept trash and garbage inaccessible and pets in at night, she says, the coyotes would probably leave.

It isn't the coyote's dogginess that attracted Reid but its survival skill, as well as its elusive origin and outcast identity. "I welcome uncertainty and the unknown," she said. "In my life, I have run up against too many walls because of rigid thinking. And what I have experienced in being out there on the margins with the other gender outlaws is that there are far more ways of being in the world than we grew up knowing about."

Going into their element

Through the air markedly softer and colder than the day's, I wade into the dark, knowing that this is the element I'll have to frequent more often if I'm going to see the coyotes that traverse this land . . .

In this dark I have to walk by feel, sensing the way as though blindfolded, expecting my skin to see the trees and low limbs and spider webs in front of me. It reminds me of the summer I spent every night outside, when I was hired to be the night watch at a craft school on Deer Isle [Maine], the responsible one who wandered between studios until the last artists went to bed and who stayed awake and watched the lobster boats heading out at dawn. I learned about night then -- about the way foxes talk and raccoons argue, about the pulse and color of the aurora borealis, about the circling of constellations around the North Star, and about the way lovers, convinced of the secrecy of their affairs, give every detail away with the hungry sounds of their bodies.
