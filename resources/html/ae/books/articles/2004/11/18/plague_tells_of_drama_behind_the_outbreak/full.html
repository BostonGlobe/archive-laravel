BOOK REVIEW

'Plague' tells of drama behind the outbreak

By Carol Iaciofano, Globe Correspondent  |  November 18, 2004

Plague and Fire: Battling Black Death and the 1900 Burning of Honolulu’s Chinatown, By James C. Mohr, Oxford University, 235pp, $30

ADVERTISEMENT

James Mohr was sightseeing in the Chinatown section of Honolulu when he passed a modest sign on a building with a remarkable message: No structures in that section of the city were original. The original ones had been destroyed in 1900, in a fire ordered by the Board of Health to stop the spread of bubonic plague. The wind had shifted and strengthened during the project, turning a controlled blaze of a small area into a 38-acre inferno.

Mohr, a Distinguished Professor of History at the University of Oregon, was curious enough to investigate. ''Plague and Fire" is the compelling result.

To give us an idea of the scale of this nearly unknown calamity, Mohr notes that the Chinatown fire ''remains not only the worst civic disaster in Hawaiian history but one of the worst disasters ever initiated in the name of public health by American medical officers anywhere." In one afternoon, 5,000 people became homeless.

The term ''bubonic plague" has a medieval sound to it, conjuring images of rat-infested villages or the storytelling pilgrims of ''The Canterbury Tales." But Mohr puts the disease in stark historical context: The last world epidemic of bubonic plague occurred as recently as the 1890s.

Bubonic plague hit Hong Kong in 1894 and quickly spread around the Pacific region. The Honolulu Board of Health, aware of its citizens' increasing exposure to visitors from around the world, tightened its inspections and restrictions on incoming ships.

For five years Hawaii avoided plague contamination, but its luck finally ran out in 1899. Turn-of-the-century doctors did not know that bubonic plague is carried by rat fleas. Even though ship passengers were closely checked (and, if necessary, quarantined), it did not seem necessary to monitor the rats that ran on and off the ships docked at Honolulu harbor. Once plague started killing rats in an area, and there were fewer live vermin hosts, the infected fleas ultimately jumped to humans.

Medical labwork at the turn of the previous century was primitive, and so was medical sleuthing. Mohr covers the doctors' best efforts like a detective writer, highlighting the clues they used, and the ones they simply could not have been aware of at the time.

Mohr anchors the story around the decisions made by three Honolulu Board of Health doctors: Nathaniel Emerson, Francis Day, and Clifford Wood. They had all graduated from American medical schools, practiced medicine in the islands for many years, and had years of experience as public health officers in Hawaii. These men ended up with ''unprecedented positions of absolute power" during the medical crisis.

Opening the door onto the human strengths and shortcomings of the key players turns what could have been a flat textbook into a near-page-turner. The story seems more real with the many black-and-white photos of the neighborhoods and residents, and the turn-of-the-century portraits of the professionals involved.

By late fall of 1899, residents of Honolulu's Chinatown noticed many dead rats near the docks. Around the same time, many people living closest to the harbor became afflicted with a ''deadly new disease" whose symptoms were unfortunately familiar to Chinatown's older traditional healers.

Plague manifests itself by ''a sudden wave of high fever, headache, chills, and nausea." These symptoms diminish after a day, sometimes giving the ill person the false hope that the worst has passed. But symptoms return in stronger force a few days later, bringing agonizing body aches and flattening fatigue.

Health officials did believe that the newly discovered organism called bacteria, visible only with a microscope, could hide in garbage and human waste. Massive cleanup efforts were undertaken in the crowded, impoverished wharf-area neighborhoods of Chinatown to rid the area of disease.

Public health policy inevitably integrates social and economic concerns along with medical ones, whether it was the polio outbreaks of the 1950s or the initial AIDS epidemic of the 1980s. Mohr recognizes that no medical emergency exists solely in the medical sphere. As a result of this, the narrative is rich with cultural, political, and economic detail.

Even though Chinatown's traditional healers wanted to help their residents, they were also reluctant to inform Honolulu health officials of the plague deaths. A previous epidemic of cholera had generated a culture clash: Residents were forced to submit to repeated medical exams by non-Chinese physicians; patients and doctors did not understand each other's customs or languages, and the perceived insults had not been forgotten.

When plague began to claim the lives of more and more Chinatown residents in November and December 1899, there was much discussion within the community about whether to inform Honolulu health officials. Finally, two Chinese physicians who were educated in Western medicine were able to convince the traditional healers that informing the board of health would help, not hurt, their community.

Efforts to treat the patients and clean out the neighborhoods proceeded without incident until the deadly fire. The conflagration wiped out 5,000 residents' homes and belongings; residents were driven into detention camps, where they stayed for weeks until the medical threat was declared over and new homes could be built.

More than a century later, Mohr's sharp curiosity has helped convey the significance of this remarkable event to a wide audience.
