BOOK REVIEW

Parker's Sunny shines in 'Melancholy Baby'

By Richard Dyer, Globe Staff  |  November 16, 2004

Melancholy Baby, By Robert B. Parker, Penguin, 296 pp., $24.95

''Melancholy Baby" is the fourth of Robert B. Parker's novels about Sunny Randall, the third Parker detective to be giving bad guys a hard time in the Greater Boston area, and the only woman.

Sunny's differences from Parker's most famous gumshoe, Spenser, are primarily biological, but she and her counterpart in the third Parker series, Jesse Stone, share an advantage over Spenser: They haven't been around for so long and haven't appeared in as many books, so they can still surprise us. We want to see Spenser do what Spenser does, and when Parker varies the formula by too much, sends him out of state, or out of character, we don't like it.

Sunny and Jesse are still working toward figuring themselves out, so the reader has the fun of joining them on their personal journey toward self-knowledge as well as on their professional adventures; Spenser is a human gyroscope, never unsure of himself.

In ''Melancholy Baby," Sunny is so unstrung when her ex-husband marries someone else that she goes to see her shrink; she can't live with Richie, but she can't live without him. The shrink has decided to retire and close his practice, and recommends that she go to see a colleague -- a certain Dr. Silverman, whose office is on Linnaean Street in Cambridge. It comes as no surprise to learn that Silverman is good looking and ''subtly well-dressed"; her makeup is ''understated but careful." Parker's readers have met Silverman before -- she is Spenser's beloved Susan, although there is no way for Sunny to know that. Soon Sunny is nearly as smitten with Susan as Spenser is -- she admires Susan's body, her clear nail polish, her ''thick and shiny" black hair, various pieces from her wardrobe, and some of the contents of her jewelry box -- her ''quiet pearl earrings," her ''quiet gold necklace." She begins to speculate on whether Dr. Silverman has a boyfriend, but dismisses the unworthy thought. ''Probably some Harvard geek."

Like Spenser, she begins to discuss the case she is working on with Susan, who provides helpful psychological pointers about the mystery as she steers Sunny toward a fuller understanding of her own situation, and its origins in her childhood and upbringing.

Sunny's case is an interesting one, and with some parallels to her own situation. Sarah Markham, a troubled young woman -- and Parker is always good with troubled youth -- hires her to look into her parentage. Sarah is convinced that she is adopted. She is not like her parents, and her mother appears to despise her, and the feeling has become mutual. Sunny, too, is closer to her father than to her mother. Sarah's parents vehemently insist that Sarah is their own child, but refuse to undergo DNA testing.

Before long, Sunny is in deep trouble, because there is someone out there who does not want her to investigate the question of Sarah's origins -- someone who is willing to kill, and who does. There are some slimy lawyers involved, one of them sleek enough to offer Sunny a martini made with Grey Goose L'Orange vodka in New York's Four Seasons's Grill Room and provide a fleeting romantic interest. There's a conservative TV talk-show host involved too, and before Sunny can sort everything out, she must use all her resources and contacts: her father, the retired policeman; Dr. Silverman; her friend, Spike, the tough-guy gay restaurateur; her ex-husband; several Boston and New York cops familiar to Parker's readers; and the gangster brother of her former father-in-law. Not to mention her beloved bull terrier, Rosie, a major figure in the book.

She travels to the Quad Cities on the Mississippi, twice, and to New York and visits other venues, including Andover, Brighton, and a couple of lightly camouflaged local college campuses. One of the murders takes place in the parking lot behind the Castle at Park Plaza; Sunny seems a little confused about the geography, because she refers to ''half a dozen police cars parked on Huntington Avenue in front of the Castle." The last time we tried to park at the Castle it stood at the corner of Arlington Street and Columbus Avenue.

All is right in the end, but nobody reads Parker to see how deftly he is going to ravel up his plots. Instead local readers turn to him for other reasons -- to enjoy encountering sights and sounds we know captured in a book, for one thing. Readers everywhere revel in Parker's canny handling of dialogue and take pleasure in the well-stocked mind he shares with his more sympathetic characters, like Sunny. New York City ''always made me think of the way Lewis Mumford had defined a city. Something like, 'the most options in the least space.' " Sunny is a likable person to spend a little time with, and she has moments of insight worthy of Spenser. ''The way [my father] loved [my mother] made me think maybe love was irrational. Simply a force that happened to you, like gravity."
