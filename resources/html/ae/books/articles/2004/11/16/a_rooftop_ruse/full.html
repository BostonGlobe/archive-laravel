'SAMMY KEYES AND THE BACKSTAGE CLASH'

A rooftop ruse

Chapter eight of a children?s story in eight parts

By Wendelin Van Draanen  |  November 16, 2004

The story so far: When Marissa and Sammy sneak into the mall to see a band called The Boyz shoot a video, they find the mall security chief bound and gagged by a couple of females he refers to as ''Amazons." In exchange for a promise from the two friends not to tell anyone he was kidnapped by girls, he lets them go backstage. There, they realize that two of The Boyz, Jackson and Toby, are irked at their band mate Ace, who gets all the attention. They also notice a woman named Vanessa, who seems to be in charge of the band. Meanwhile, Sammy keeps smelling a strange odor. Suddenly, two enormous women blast through a door and carry Ace away. In the mayhem that follows, Marissa and Sammy slip through the door the Amazons came through and find themselves in a maze of dark corridors with scarves tied along the way, as if to mark the route. At the end, they open a door and find the security chief trying to interview the two Amazons, whom he has captured. The women escape, however, and Marissa, Sammy, and the security chief head back to the stage, still trying to figure out why anyone would kidnap Ace. Suddenly, Sammy has a thought and races off to go backstage.

Talking to Vanessa is a risk, but at this point I see nothing to lose. Besides, the guard boss hadn't heard any male voices when he was kidnapped, and there is no mistaking hers -- there is nothing feminine about it.

So when I spot her backstage, I immediately go over and drag her aside.

''What are you doing?" she snaps. The Ponytail and his sidekick move in close, so I whisper frantically in her ear. When I step back, her eyes are huge, and I can see the wheels in her head moving faster and faster.

''That makes perfect sense," she says, and her voice is steady, but boy, are her eyes on fire. Then she says, ''And you know what? I know exactly what I'm going to do." She looks around angrily, shouting, ''Evan! Where's Evan?"

Evan appears and says, ''Yes ma'am?"

''You're on! Right now! Toby! Jackson! Get in position. Evan's front and center!"

''Me?" Evan says, pointing at himself. Then he pumps his fist and cries, ''Yes!"

''Evan is?" Toby and Jackson ask together.

''Just do it! Now!" She turns to the soundman. ''Karl, hit it!"

The music starts, and Ace's dad breaks away from a group of reporters, saying, ''Hey, what are you doing?" He sees Evan going onstage and shouts, ''Don't tell me you're putting that runt in for my Ace?"

''He's no runt," she says with a glare. ''And I've seen him do this enough times to know he's going to be great out there."

''I can't believe this," Ace's dad whines over the music. ''My son's been abducted and you're going on with the show?"

The reporters are all around us now, and I think Vanessa is getting worried that she's just made a huge mistake because she turns to me and says, ''You'd better not be wrong," then puts both hands up like, don't ask me . . .! And distances herself from me.

''Wrong about what?" all the reporters ask me. Rather than sputter out an explanation, I shrug and say, ''Follow me."

On my way into the corridor I grab the guard boss and whisper: ''You said you were sneaking out to have a smoke when the Amazons ambushed you, right?"

He nods.

''And you weren't going out to the sidewalk, you were going up stairs, right?"

''Right."

''So you must have been going to the roof."

He nods again.

''Take me there," I say. ''And can you talk in code to have your men change radio frequencies? When they do, tell them to cover the roof exits."

To my surprise, he simply says, ''Ahhhh, brilliant!"

As we scurry through the corridors, I get inundated with questions from reporters: ''Where are you leading us?" ''Do you know where Ace is?" ''Is he all right?" ''Are the Amazons hostile?"

In between their questions, Ace's dad is shouting: ''This is ridiculous!" ''What are we doing?" ''Where are we going?" ''Is this the best you've got? Some smart aleck kid?" ''What could she know?" ''Who is she?"

I don't answer anybody's questions. Marissa has been whispering things to me, too, but I just shake my head and give her a look that says, ''Cross your fingers that I'm right, okay?" Which, of course, makes her whisper, ''You're not sure? Oh, Sammy, if you're wrong, this is going to be so embarrassing."

I pull another face and whisper, ''Tell me about it."

When we get to the roof stairwell, though, I become a lot more sure. Ace's dad basically rears back and says: ''Where does this lead? Why are we going in there?" And while everyone else starts to file into the stairwell, he hangs back and flips open his cellphone.

''Might as well give up calling your son," I say, leaning out the doorway. ''You won't get any reception in here, believe me." I cock my head. ''So? Do you want to lead the way, or should I?"

The reporters cluster around. ''What are you saying? That he abducted his own son?"

I head up the steps. ''Yup."

''I'll sue you for slander!" Ace's dad shouts from the back of the pack.

''Oh, please," I throw over my shoulder.

''But why would he do that?" one of the reporters asks.

''Because he wants his son to be the star, and it makes great press. I mean, what were your headlines going to read? 'Big, Big Fans Abduct Teen Idol!' 'Ace of Hearts Ambushed by Amazons!' Something like that, right?"

''I'm going to sue!" Ace's dad cries, coming up the stairs behind the reporters. ''If anyone wanted my son out of the way, it was those whiny moms. They never wanted my Ace to sing lead! Do you know how many times they tried to push him out? They're insecure because they know that Ace has more talent than both their sons combined!"

Now the truth is, I am not a hundred percent sure it is Ace's dad. And inside, I am kinda sweating bullets because Marissa is right: This has serious embarrassment potential. But I try to act confident, as I say, ''Well, wasn't it just so convenient that the Amazons showed up right after Ace had had his turn at singing? I mean, there's great footage of Ace singing lead, but there's footage of only him singing lead. And it's not like an unknown band without a record contract is going to be able to pull together a publicity stunt like this again, so where's that leave the group? With Ace as lead on a promotional video. What better way of forcing a re-recording of the lead track?"

''Wait a minute," one of the reporters says. ''The Boyz have no record contract?"

We are almost to the roof of the mall and Ace's dad has muscled his way through the reporters. He faces them and announces, ''This is preposterous! You should be --"

And then, in that small space, in that tight stairwell, I get my proof. ''Aaaaaaahhhhhh!" I cry, fanning the air like crazy.

''Ooooooohhhh!" all the reporters wail as the stink bomb envelops them.

''Open the door!" Marissa shouts to the Guard Boss, and we all charge onto the roof, gasping for air.

So now I know for sure that I'm right. Ace's dad is the only person who has been in all the stink-bomb locations.

Well, there is one other person: Marissa.

What a joke that would be on me, huh?

Anyway, it doesn't take long to find Ace. He is sunning himself in a lounger, with a big ice chest of food and cherry colas, a cellphone, and a walkie-talkie radio by his side.

The Amazons are there, too, and although they try to escape, they can't because all the roof exits are guarded. And it's funny how discovering that they are trapped also makes them rediscover English.

''We're from the Large and Lovely Talent Agency!" they say. ''Why are you keeping us here? We didn't do anything illegal."

''Oh, right!" the Guard Boss says, and when the Amazons realize they are going to be arrested for tying him up, they turn to Ace's dad and use their English skills in a very big and colorful way.

Afterward, Vanessa invites Marissa and me to a backstage party, which I stay for so Marissa can do some gushing and get some pictures.

And that's where I get to explain to Marissa that it was mostly motive that pointed me toward Ace's dad, but it was also realizing that the Guard Boss could easily have mistaken Ace's dad's high-pitched voice for a woman's voice.

Plus, there was the cherry cola.

''The cherry cola?" she asks.

''Remember how his dad came in with the soda can saying how he'd finally found a machine? Well, in the first place, he must have been gone for a while because he said finding the cherry cola wasn't easy, right?"

''But the guy from the record company was out in the halls, too."

''I know. But something about the cola can was bugging me, and I finally figured it out: Ace wiped a bunch of water off the top of it."

''So?"

''So a can that's been in a machine isn't wet like that."

Marissa's head goes back like, aaahhh. ''It was in the ice chest."

''Exactly."

During the party, we also find out that the whole scarves-on-the-mike-stands thing came from Toby being way into pirates. But according to Vanessa, anyone could have stolen them; she says there was a big bag of them and nobody would ever have noticed if some were missing.

The best part, though, is finding out that everyone, including the Ponytail, thinks Evan is the cutest thing to ever hit the stage. There's no doubt about it -- he's, uh, aced the other guy right out of the group.

So I guess there's pushing, and then there's pushing too hard. Ace's dad pushed and pushed until his son was pushed right out of The Boyz. And we know that Heather pushed her way to the front of the crowd, but we find out later that she kept on pushing until she got pushed right out of the mall -- by security.

I don't need any pushing myself to leave the mall. I am more than happy to go. But Marissa gets some great backstage photos, and I leave with something even better: the Guard Boss's map of the mall.

I see one of the Amazons ditch it between exhaust fans on the roof, and when I see what it is, I slip it in my pocket.

So now when Marissa drags me to the mall, I'll be ready.

Even if the mall's not ready for me.

Wendelin Van Draanen's novel for young readers, ''Sammy Keyes and the Backstage Clash," has been published in The Boston Globe on eight consecutive Tuesdays. This is the last chapter.
