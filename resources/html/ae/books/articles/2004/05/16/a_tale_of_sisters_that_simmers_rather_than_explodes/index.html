A tale of sisters that simmers rather than explodes

By Mameve Medwed  |  May 16, 2004

Departures
By Elizabeth Oness
Berkley, 341 pp., paperback, $14

Few English majors would ever expect Chekhov's gun-on-the-mantel to go off in the first chapter of a novel. On Page 6 of "Departures," however, that gun fires the minute a hippie mother, now Westchester realtor, mails her three grown daughters a variation of this note: "I'll be out of the country for a while. . . . Don't look for me. Try not to worry. Love, Mom."

Who can resist such a bolt from the starting gate? That Elizabeth Oness is a poet and a prize-winning short story writer is no surprise. Her eye is sharp, her language lovely. Though not a smooth race to the finish line -- the parts don't always add up to the whole -- "Departures" is an accomplished debut.

In the mother's empty house the only things missing (besides her) are her toothbrush and the photo of her three daughters. Who are these sisters, "named for different stages of their mother's spiritual seeking: Pagan, Zhikr, and Prana"? The missing photo offers a clue; it showed "Pagan, the worrying eldest, her hand over her eyes as if looking off to sea, trying to chart the safest course. . . . Zeke . . . seemed caught in forward movement, as if she were going to burst from the group and move forward out of the frame. Prana, delicate and fairy-like, the silent bloom inside her still nascent."

Skip ahead a couple of decades, and their names have become "like clothes out of fashion or pants that were too short." But if the names don't fit, the child is still father to the man -- or, in this case, mother to the woman. Their eccentric pasts mold their current predicaments. Zeke and Pagan are the children of Tom, who died young. Prana is the daughter of Marco, husband on the rebound and commitment-phobe who took off. For a long time, it's been a women's world in Shady Grove. And things are simmering.

Still, the mother's disappearance doesn't bring events to the rolling boil you'd assume. Scenes play out on a lower but steady flame, less driven by plot than by the gentle peeling back of character. Oness serves as fair parent to her trio of protagonists; she bestows equal time and affection on each.

Pagan, the eldest, is struggling with a tough pregnancy. Mother of Maud, wife of Tommy, she has already had one miscarriage and anxiously awaits an amniocentesis. Hunkered down in her domestic nest, she worries that she's conventional; "she often felt that she disappointed people. She had failed to live up to her name."

Zeke, the childhood tomboy, runs the Environmental Center at the local university. "They hire a lesbian . . . it gets them big diversity brownie points. Then they don't have to feel so guilty about going overbudget on the football team," her lover, Marcie, points out. Zeke's renovated carriage house borders her mother's neighborhood, where "the enclaves of suburban modernity were visible mainly to themselves and didn't intrude on the atmosphere of lives lived out quietly, separate from others." Like Pagan, Zeke's life is measured out with idiosyncratic coffee spoons. She instructs her students, takes care of the animals, worries about Marcie's alcoholism, and nourishes an intensifying friendship with Bert the vet.

Prana, the youngest and the family beauty (must all families pick a designated beauty?), lives in Washington, D.C., where she styles food. "Her life consisted of arranging things to look beautiful . . . but she was tired of managing things, tired of trying to make disparate things come together." Prana, who's developed a recreational cocaine habit, has been having a nine-year affair with Stephan, famous photographer and married father of three. In the way of unequal love matches, rapture turns sour; Stephan cancels appointments. He leaves messages on her answering machine to avoid hearing "her forlorn protests." Lonely, set apart from her sisters by having a different father, Prana senses that "her isolation felt large because it didn't seem to matter to the others."

Will the mother's disappearance end such isolation? Will the sisters pledge all for one and one for all? Yes and no. Without their mother, the sisters find it "hard to know how to be with each other. It seemed they had no glue except habit." Yet the small lessons they learn secrete their own glue: how to accept loss, how to recognize love, how to appreciate the compromises their mother made for them.

As the sisters open up, their unnamed mother hides. Even so, bits and pieces float to the surface through her daughters' separate memories. Zeke realizes "their mother watched them with a certain detachment, as if she had scattered seeds without labels and was curious to see what they would become."

What they become propels the novel. By the end, the strongly drawn portraits of the sisters compensate for the mother-vacuum at the center and the structure's occasional choppiness. The reader may have trouble separating Pagan from Prana (note to novelists: Begin characters' names with different letters and vary the number of syllables). The ending feels abrupt, not quite earned; the hints buried in earlier pages strike a gimmicky, "Where's Waldo" note. Nevertheless, if the bombshell of a letter is a familiar literary and cinematic conceit, it's an effective one. We wait for the fallout. We're not disappointed. There are enough pleasures in this first novel to make readers look forward to the talented author's next.

Mameve Medwed's third novel is "The End of an Error."
