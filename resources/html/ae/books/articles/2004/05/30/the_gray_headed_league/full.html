The gray-headed league

May 30, 2004

Page full of 3 -- Picture the classic literary detective and you think of someone impervious to the ravages of age. The Sherlock Holmes at the end of Arthur Conan Doyle's cycle and the Philip Marlowe at the end of the Raymond Chandler stories are much the same characters they were at the beginning.

Many of today's mystery writers continue to keep their detectives frozen in time. Others, though, have mined rich narrative and thematic territory by following their characters into different realms of experience as they passed into middle age and beyond, often mirroring the experiences of their readers.

Television and movies probably spurred the trend. There's no hiding the fact that Peter Falk's Columbo, in his sporadic ABC appearances, is a much older man than in the original NBC series. Colin Dexter's Inspector Morse suffered the ultimate ravage of time --death -- only shortly before his on-screen counterpart, John Thaw, passed away.

If a certain amount of cynicism comes with the territory in modern detective novels, many of today's aging detectives seem less world-weary than bone-weary (or even cholesterol-wary). And as they approach retirement or deal with problems affecting their elderly parents and irrepressible children, they strike chords with readers in the same situation.

These aren't self-help books, unless you think catching serial killers is the best way of dealing with a midlife crisis, but there is a certain satisfaction in watching all-too-human detectives struggle with matters of everyday life as well as matters of life and death.

Much of the charm behind Michael Dibdin's Aurelio Zen novels lies in Zen's impatience with both the creaky Italian body politic and the creakiness of his own body. (Dibdin was born in England and lives in Seattle, but most of his crime novels take place in Italy.) In 2002's ''And Then You Die," he asks himself ''Why couldn't he accept that he was no longer a player and never would be again?" while personally agonizing over ''the normal discomforts of age and decrepitude, telltale signs that the body was reaching the end of its useful life."

Zen, as ever, is committed to showing he's up to the job not out of any special commitment to law and order but out of fears about the ennui of ''a long, tedious enforced retirement." As he ages, Zen increasingly becomes the antithesis of a superhero. He looks for any edge he can get against the villains, including biting one in ''And Then You Die" and even shooting him in the head three times after he's already subdued.

Ruth Rendell's Inspector Wexford and P. D. James's Commander Dalgliesh rarely have to resort to fisticuffs after smoking out their more genteel murder suspects. For all his introspection, Dalgliesh is not that concerned with aging. In fact, Martha Hailey Dubose in ''Women of Mystery: The Lives and Works of Notable Women Crime Novelists" says he stopped aging sometime in the 1980s, although there are not-so-vague references to ''the sad accretion of years" (2001's ''Death in Holy Orders").

Nevertheless, in the latest novel, ''The Murder Room," he is finally emerging from the emotionally paralyzing sadness of losing his wife and son shortly after childbirth and contemplating a second marriage. ''The long years had gradually laid down their merciful cicatrices."

Kate Mattes, owner of Kate's Mystery Books, in Cambridge, says that Robert B. Parker hasn't gotten enough credit for the Spenser books in terms of instituting ''a continuous relationship first with a woman and then with a young boy, and we've watched them age, perhaps the boy more than the adults."

She also mentions Eleanor Taylor Bland, whose policewoman Marti MacAlister has children and is in a relationship with someone else who has children; Elizabeth George, whose working-class character Barbara Havers has to struggle to take care of her ailing mother while her partner, Thomas Lynley, has no money problems; and Tony Hillerman, whose partners, Jim Chee and Joe Leaphorn, get to know each other over time as Leaphorn's wife is dying of cancer.

''That's the way things happen in the real world," says Mattes, ''and I think that's one of the big selling points of mysteries today. I think it's a major shift that's been going on for the past 20 years and is now becoming the norm. We want the characters to do more than solve crimes; we want to get to know them and we look forward to what's going to happen from one year to the next."

These characters are also a welcome relief from the youth-obsessed publishing industry, which favors novels like Jonathan Franzen's ''The Corrections," books that are steeped in ironic humor and that feature little emotional development on the part of the main characters.

Rendell's Wexford has long enjoyed a happy marriage, although she has left open the possibility that he has strayed at least once. Rendell is particularly adept at mirroring the mystery in each novel with Wexford's own domestic issues, often concerning his disapproval over the lifestyles of his two daughters. In her latest, ''The Babes in the Wood," the domestic abuse leveled at his daughter helps Wexford comes to terms with the misogyny at the root of the murder he's investigating.

But there may not be a better writer than Henning Mankell in terms of maintaining a parallel track between a detective's attempt at solving problems in his own life and in his own cases, although the latter sometimes provides cover for the former.

Kurt Wallander, on the surface, is a lot like Zen, in that he has a midlife body that doesn't respond the way it used to as well as a somewhat conventional mind-set that he fears may not be adequate to what he sees as the Americanization of crime (i.e., its increasing grisliness) in Ystad, Sweden, where these crime stories take place.

As he ages, so does his father, an artist who has looked down on his choice of profession. Wallander has struggled with his father's dementia and death in the latest novels while his daughter, Linda, with whom he has also had a problematic relationship, has tried his patience with her bohemian ways. Imagine his surprise when at the end of the latest in the series, ''Firewall," she announces her intention to become a police officer.

Wallander, though hardly a flaming liberal, also feels alienated from a post-welfare-state Sweden that is increasingly vulnerable to violence partly because people have become indifferent to each other. As he contemplates the suicide of a young man who had dressed as an American Indian before committing murders in ''Sidetracked," an earlier novel, he thinks: ''That disturbing mask of paint and blood held little clue as to who the young person locked inside had really been, but it spoke volumes about the violent and largely indifferent society in which he had been formed."

He finds himself envying those who have resigned and found other professions, but he has trouble doing the same, even though his job has cost him one marriage and another loving relationship. In''Firewall," Mankell masterfully ties this situation into the bizarre deaths Wallander is investigating by having the detective place a personal ad. (We can say no more.)

In a 2002 interview in The Guardian, Mankell said, ''[Wallander] feels tired because the work is too much. But if he didn't do the work, he'd feel worse, he would leave a big black hole in himself. . . . I think a lot of people are struggling to manage now -- feeling they are running for a bus they'll never catch. In that sense, he's a very common man. In Sweden, people write to him as if he's alive, and can help them."

Wallander, who like Morse is an opera lover, is also one step up from a Luddite in computer skills, though he understands that his attitude is tantamount to admitting that time is quickly passing him by: ''Wallander realized the futility in asking more questions [of a computer expert]. This new age of electronic developments would eventually require a whole new type of police officer. As usual, criminals were way ahead of the game."

He himself has become vulnerable to his increasingly exhausted body, exhausted because he finds it harder to sleep as he grows older. He sometimes stumbles in pursuit of younger, faster criminals while a rival within the police force thinks it's time for him to replace the aging top dog. At 50, ''he was forced to accept that he had simply become old. An old dog who could no longer be taught new tricks."

Some detectives wear their age like a badge of honor, but Mankell makes Wallander more human by making him fight his self-doubts as well as the criminals. As the crimes he investigates are often unfathomably brutal, Wallander's introspection and Mankell's narrative dispassion make for a combination that is impossible to beat in crime fiction today.

Wallander, like many of his colleagues, is both Everyman and Superman. No wonder they're so popular.
