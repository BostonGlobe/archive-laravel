The darkness at the heart of faith

May 30, 2004

Page full of 2 -- Holy Rollers. Faith healers. Penitents who babble in tongues and saints who see visions. I'm Jewish born and bred, but I've always been drawn to those believers who trust in something outside my own experience. Is it any wonder that I was drawn to two riveting collections of linked stories about faith?

''Dog on the Cross" (Algonquin Books of Chapel Hill, paperback, $14.95), Aaron Gwyn's dazzlingly inventive collection, centers on the denizens of Perser, Okla., a dusty little Pentecostal town caught in the grip of a young charismatic faith healer named Leslie Snodgrass. OK, instantly I'm hooked. This is an unfamiliar setting to me, and Gwyn, who was raised Pentecostal in Oklahoma, is an expert guide, creating a world that's so surprising, so real, that it's exhilarating to read about. What interests me here isn't just the depth of the experience, but the danger. Here, the bliss of believing in the glory of God doesn't necessarily guarantee easy salvation -- in fact there's a price tag, and it's steep.

Belief, it seems, is ''like something that could turn on you." And turn it does. The story ''In Tongues" gives us Reverend Hassler, famous for the way the voices of the angels ripple from his mouth. But when young Snodgrass begins his revival, Hassler is suddenly silenced. Tormented, Hassler tries to fake talking in tongues -- only to discover that instead, a new, more chilling gift has been bestowed on him. In ''Of Falling," a man named George Crider survives a 116-foot fall only to be plagued by nightmares. His wife urges him to visit the revival tents, and when he refuses, she takes his place, falling into the arms of healers with reckless, almost sexual abandon. And then, one day, Snodgrass approaches Crider and, with a touch, makes his fall -- both literal and figurative -- seem almost eerily predestined.

This is a world where characters grapple helplessly with both the flames of hell and the fires of the flesh, searching for ''a cure you can live with, one that doesn't gnaw away at your soul." Redemption is never what you expect. In the marvelous title story, a beagle is found crucified, days before Snodgrass's revival is rumored to close up shop. The prime suspect? Someone outside the flock: Jacob Hollis, transplanted Easterner, beagle owner, and scientist. Surely, gossips say, this is just the thing a man like Hollis might do to cast aspersion on Snodgrass and run him out of town. But did Hollis do it? And why, instead of stopping the revival, is this incident extending its stay? Is there something or someone more sinister at work? In Gwyn's expert hands, nothing,including good or evil, is ever so simple, and that's what makes this collection -- part Flannery O'Connor, part Shirley Jackson, and wholly original -- so brilliantly compelling. Because these stories aren't so much a study of a small group of the faithful as of the rocky path of belief, whether it be the self-delusions of a teenager who refuses to admit that his raging hormones are anything other than damnation, or the sins of a church elder whose only way of dealing with two men in love is with physical violence.

''What to Wear to See the Pope" (Carroll & Graf, $23), by Christine Lehner, also grapples with issues of faith, but it doesn't have the same eerie strangeness of Gwyn's work. Part of that is because it simply isn't as exotic. The terrain is no-surprises suburban, and rather than dealing with a tightly knit religious community, it offers just one heroine: Ursula Codwell, highly intelligent mother, cook, linguist, and conflicted Catholic. Lehner gives Ursula an original voice, captivating, convincing, and yes, at times, wearisomely wry as she veers from tangent to tangent, from saints to salads, from hell to a hell-bent destructive kitchen knife.

Less dark and more genially funny than Gwyn's book, ''What to Wear to See the Pope" is deeply concerned with inner landscapes. Ursula's fixations with saints and linguistics are her attempts to organize her world and to give modern life -- ''our families, our confusion and our hope" -- the kind of mystery and majesty I love. Ursula's the only sort-of-true believer in the bunch (her family snorts at her faith), but even so, just as in ''Dog on the Cross," this framework doesn't always hold together. She sees signs in everything; the name of a little girl's horrifying tumor has its roots in the word ''sin," but how, she wonders, could a child have committed a crime that deserved cancer? The judge at traffic court has the same bushy eyebrows as Saint Teresa, so when he lets her violation go, it makes sense to her, until her husband points out that she was guilty as charged, so what's the meaning in that? The most powerful part of the book centers on her friend Malone, a Christian Scientist whose faith is no help at all. Condemned to a wheelchair by a degenerative disease, he writes her raffish letters, collects porn, and suffers a terrible death, and there's no real way Ursula can ever make sense of that.

Despite the pleasures of both collections, I'm more drawn to Gwyn's. Sure, I may be more attuned to Lehner's savvy questioning than the dark fumblings of the Gwyn characters, and I could navigate Ursula's suburbia without even a hint of a map, while I'm lost in the Oklahoma landscape. But truly, it's the strange, dark, glittering world of the Pentecostals, the dangerous depths of their inarticulated beliefs, that snag my interest. When the very system meant to explain a confusing world ends up being stranger than the world itself, I'm fascinated. Because if mystery is the bedrock of faith, what's more intriguing than the ways in which people grapple with that mystery, fail, and continue to believe?

Caroline Leavitt's most recent novel is "Girls in Trouble." She can be reached at the website www.carolineleavitt.com.
