'SOFT POWER'

By Ken Bode  |  May 9, 2004

The Bushes: Portrait of a Dynasty
By Peter Schweizer and Rochelle Schweizer
Doubleday, 574 pp., illustrated, $27.95

The only thing those bearing Bush bloodlines will not like about this Valentine is that its title heretically suggests a family political dynasty.

The authors' thesis is that the Bushes are now the most successful political family in American history, certainly a defensible proposition. The family is related to 15 former presidents, Princess Diana, and roughly half of the American population. However, ''dynasty" is a dreaded word because it smacks of calculation and entitlement, running contrary to the family ethos, the contrived myth that they are all self-made.

Already there are two Bush books on the bestseller list, Kevin Phillips's ''American Dynasty: Aristocracy, Fortune, and the Politics of Deceit in the House of Bush" and ''House of Saud, House of Bush: The Secret Relationship Between the World's Two Most Powerful Dynasties" by Craig Unger.

With ''The Bushes" by Peter and Rochelle Schweizer you will learn many engaging tidbits about the Bushes, beginning with George Herbert Walker's father, Prescott Bush, who insisted his grandchildren call him ''Senator."

Organized chronologically, the book follows first president Bush from Phillips Academy, Andover, to World War II, where he served heroically and crashed three planes, one of them shot down. Bush wrote home that he hoped none of his sons would ever have to go to war. Thanks to a congenial slot in the Texas Air Guard for his son George W. during the Vietnam War, none ever did.

The authors introduce the concept of ''soft power" to explain the amassing of wealth and position among the legions of Bushes, generation after generation. From Andover to Yale to business to politics, soft power essentially means leveraging upward by trading on relationships, friendships, family money, and contacts. One thing the Schweizers demonstrate conclusively: It works.

After Yale, George and Barbara got their start in Texas with family money, then cashed in on the oil boom before going into politics. George W. got into Yale with miserable board scores, but his great-great-grandfather, his grandfather, father, and seven uncles went there, and there were two Bushes on the board of trustees.

When son Neil, brother Pressy, or other friends needed help establishing business relationships around the globe, former President Bush used his connections to get them private sessions with President Suharto of Indonesia, President Menem of Argentina, the Saudi royal family, and China's President Jiang Zemin. Soft power.

The book is largely a version of modern American history viewed through the prism of the Bush family. For example, ''The family saw the story of Watergate not so much as a story about Nixon's abuses as about the media's efforts to get Nixon." Although a billion dollars in shareholder assets were disappearing in the corruption of the Silverado Savings and Loan scandal, George the father insisted, ''They [the prosecutors] are going after Neil because he is our son."

The reader does learn some important things about the two Bush presidencies and Iraq. Regime change is presented as a family goal well before 9/11. ''In the pantheon of Bush enemies, none was greater than Saddam Hussein."

President Bush 41 solidified his relationship with the Kuwaiti and Saudi royal families with Desert Storm, but stopped before ousting the Iraqi president because he was mistakenly convinced the dictator would soon be overthrown. President Bush 43 saw Saddam Hussein as ''the guy that tried to kill my dad," and decided to finish the job.

The reader sees the elder Bush as nonideological, steady and pragmatic, guided by his long experience. In the aftermath of 9/11 and the Iraq war, family members see in the younger, less experienced president, George W., a commitment tinged with a sense of a divine calling, a religious war.

The book makes interesting reading for its tales about the family. When George and Barbara first landed in Texas, for example, they lived in a duplex, sharing a bathroom with a mother-daughter team of prostitutes. Little George W. used to like to blow up frogs with firecrackers. After losing to Clinton, Bush 41 missed the presidential perks so painfully that he would wake early and grope for the button beside the White House bed to call for his morning coffee.

On inauguration night 2001, after George W. and Laura went to eight balls (and danced an average of 48 seconds at each one), they returned to the White House. The president slipped into the Oval Office and stood alone behind the large mahogany desk. He heard footsteps in the darkness and looked up to see his father in the doorway. ''Mr. President," says 41, with a crack in his voice. ''Mr. President," says the son. And both begin to cry.

It will not be news that the former president called influential Republicans all over the country telling them to ''keep their powder dry" because his son would be running in 2000. Soft power.

It may be news that son Jeb, the governor of Florida, is quietly exploring a presidential bid in 2008. If he does run, say the Schweizers, ''others in the GOP will feel that they are a small merchant shop up against Wal-Mart." No dynasty, right?

Kevin Phillips's book is a deeper, more serious look at the moneyed roots of the Bush political dynasty. The Schweizers provide a chatty tour through family history on which you learn things like the cost of Barbara Bush's inaugural outfit ($6,000 for the white wool coat, $10,000 for the mink jacket).

If Phillips's book is a steak dinner, this is a ladies' lunch.

Ken Bode is the Distinguished Pulliam Professor of Journalism at DePauw University.
