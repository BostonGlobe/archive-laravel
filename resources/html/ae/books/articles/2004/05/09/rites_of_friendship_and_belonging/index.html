FOR CHILDREN

Rites of friendship and belonging

By Liz Rosenberg  |  May 9, 2004

The Cat Who Walked Across France
Written by Kate Banks, illustrated by Georg Hallensleben
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 40 pp., all ages, $16

Why Mole Shouted and Other Stories
Written by Lore Segal, illustrated by Sergio Ruzzier
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 40 pp., ages 5-10, $16

''The Cat Who Walked Across France" -- what an irresistible title. And the book itself begins invitingly, mysterious as a cat: ''For many years the cat had lived in the stone house by the edge of the sea."

The feline hero, and sole proprietor of the story, lives a safe and quiet life. ''At dusk he curled up in the bend of the old woman's arm." Early on, the old woman dies, her belongings are moved, and ''there was no one to scratch the cat's ears or stroke his back. And soon the cat was forgotten." This is not heartless; it is European -- Americans are seldom so unsentimental or matter-of-fact about matters of love, life, and death. Nor are they often this bold. The cat takes off on a solo journey across France from seacoast to seacoast.

He prowls, hunts, begs, watches, lies ''in a pile of stiff hay" or under the ''first rays of the sun." Sometimes he is fed a bowl of milk, other times chased away, up into a tree. ''But when he nestled in its branches, he would remember the tangy smell of lemons ripening on a branch under a window at the stone house by the edge of the sea. And he would move on." This is a cat with character. Author Kate Banks writes compactly and lyrically about his adventures. There is not a false step in all the journey.

Yet what is most striking about this book, certainly, is the breathtaking art. Glorious enough for any museum, luscious enough to grace a coffee table, Georg Hallensleben's pictures always serve the story. Some illustrators are too large, too bold, too self-centered for their text. This is never the case here, where art and prose move hand in hand across luminous landscapes. Hallensleben's paintings are reminiscent of the great French Fauvists -- huge, wavy slashes of color, primitive active shapes, movement and light. One would happily hang any one of them. But they live comfortably within the pages of the book. Banks and Hallensleben have worked together before, in highly prized children's books like ''Close Your Eyes" and ''The Night Worker."

The cat comes through '' weeks . . . then months" to arrive at last at a place that feels like home. Certainly, it's a book for all young cat lovers, armchair travelers, and anyone who longs to see the French landscape at its wildest, brightest, and sweetest.

''Why Mole Shouted and Other Stories," by Lore Segal, runs in the tradition of Arnold Lobel's ''Frog and Toad" stories, or James Marshall's ''George and Martha" tales, those sweet, seemingly simple stories of great and abiding friendships. Children may learn more of friendship from books like these than from years of painful trial and error. Just as important, they learn that love takes all kinds of forms, not merely the predictable or traditional ones.

Here, the friendship is between Mole and his grandmother. The book begins with a wonderful, tender sense of the comic: ''Once there was a Mole. He lived with his Grandmother Mole, and they would have got on well enough if he had only remembered to always put things back where they belonged. Now moles -- you may or may not know this -- can't see until they put their glasses on. In the morning Mole opened his eyes, sat up in bed and patted his pillow on the right side, the left side."

There are several things here worth noting. We're in good hands as soon as we read ''they would have got on well enough if he had only remembered" -- that intimate, confident, and confiding voice that works so well in the tales of Hans Christian Andersen. By the time we reach Mole patting ''his pillow on the right side, the left side," Segal can do pretty well whatever she likes. She clearly knows how people with poor eyesight behave, and that verb '' patted" combined with ''on the right side, the left side" gives us both the search and the mole's paw in an instant. The art of the children's book is often the art of the small, the domestic; one critic noted that Beatrix Potter showed us the hem's-eye view of the world, the view from the bottom of the dining room table.

The title story, ''Why Mole Shouted," works in this tradition. Its two chapters focus on losing, finding, and straightening Mole's glasses, with his increasingly fervent promises never to lose them again. The second story is the breathless ''When Mole Wouldn't Zip His Jacket" -- in other words, it isn't about great epic adventures, but delightful misadventures closer to home. The pleasures here are of listening in at the keyhole, in felicities of speech like ''Well, then, I'll make you a nice, thick, hot cup of soup, so you'll be fortified."

The title story scoots just at the edge of sentiment, when Mole won't stop shouting, and Grandma tries to guess the cause of all the noise.

'' 'I know,' Mole's Grandmother Mole said, 'you mean, ''Notice me!" '

'' 'Aooo . . .' began Mole, but he was thinking about it. 'Ao?' he asked himself. 'Oh!' he told himself, and stopped shouting."

This exchange is all about the pleasure of language, and indeed, ''Why Mole Shouted" takes great joy in making literature of children's literature. The final chapter, while laid out all in prose, rhymes throughout: ''Mole held up both his paws. He said, 'Because.' "

Segal has written versions of fairy tales illustrated by no less a great light than Maurice Sendak. Here she is accompanied by tender watercolors by Sergio Ruzzier, in muted shades of blue, green, tan, brown, and orangey-red, with a dash of lavender thrown in for spice. His pictures are not pretty in any conventional sense; indeed, they are almost deliberately weird, and the creatures are homely, if immensely likable. ''Why Mole Shouted" is quietly stellar: gentle, warm, and witty. It's a great book for reading aloud, and inviting enough for early readers. This is another Frances Foster imprint book, which simply means that the great editor has struck gold yet again.

Liz Rosenberg writes children's book reviews for the Globe each month. A teacher of English and creative writing at the State University of New York at Binghamton, she is at work on an anthology of poems for young readers.
