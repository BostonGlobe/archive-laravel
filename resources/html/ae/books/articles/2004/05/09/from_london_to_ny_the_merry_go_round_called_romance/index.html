From London to NY, the merry-go-round called romance

May 9, 2004

Here are three novels about the complications of love, one of them very good indeed, the others, well, let's say they suffer by comparison.

Dr. Evelyn Rubin of Rachel Pastan's ''This Side of Married" has something in common with Mrs. Bennet of ''Pride and Prejudice." Dr. Rubin is 61, and only one of her three daughters is married, ''and not a single grandchild to buy presents for, or to dandle on her knee, or to show pictures of to the other doctors. What had gone wrong?"

Alice, the eldest at 38, is a lawyer who, despite a series of romantic disappointments, cherishes a vague hope that the right man may someday appear. Tina, 29, the youngest, waits for Mr. Right to come along, meanwhile toning her perfect body and sleeping with dozens of Mr. Wrongs. Isabel, the middle daughter, has been married for 12 years, and lately devotes all her energy to trying to conceive, even giving up her job as a veterinarian. But the harder she works at becoming a mother, the more her husband draws away.

''This Side of Married" is a lovely homage to the spirit of Jane Austen, a domestic comedy about love and betrayal, class and money, misapprehension and misunderstanding. The setting is 21st-century Philadelphia, but the issues are much the same as those that vexed the Bennet family in 18th-century Hertfordshire.

Dr. Rubin -- ''Doc" to her two elder daughters -- is a successful obstetrician/gynecologist and a well-meaning but domineering mother. She and her husband, Judge William Rubin, are celebrating their 40th-wedding anniversary with a big party. Doc has invited Anthony Wolf, a charming, good-looking cardiologist new to her practice. It's clear she intends him for Alice.

However, Anthony may not be the prize he appears to be. Other available men enter the story, complicating matters. Soren Zank, a half-Jewish, half-Swedish distant relation (''a cross between a Viking and a Hassid"), has made a fortune in Silicon Valley and seems to have designs of one sort or another on all three Rubin sisters. And Marco Pena, the Rubins' handsome young gardener, shares a passion for salsa dancing, and perhaps something more, with Alice.

Pastan's prose is low-key and precise, and she gives her characters some witty dialogue. ''Plenty of people seem to manage love just fine outside the confines of marriage," Isabel tells Anthony's friend Simon. ''Confines of marriage," he repeats, ''there's a phrase for you."

Jane Green is a best-selling British chick-lit author. Her sixth novel, ''To Have and to Hold," reads as though she cranked it out as quickly as possible and never once looked back to see if it was working. There may be a story in there, somewhere, but it's buried under a rubbish heap of upscale names, posh settings, plastic characters, loose plot ends, and boring, pointless dialogue.

Alice is a mousy London caterer who dreams of living the simple life in a country cottage, surrounded by children and animals. Instead, she marries Joe, a high-living, free-spending, status-mad control freak who could have any woman he wants but who, inexplicably, wants Alice. He sets about transforming her into the woman of his dreams, a sleek, blond clotheshorse.

But the remade Alice isn't enough for Joe. He's a compulsive philanderer. After Joe is caught in the act with a gorgeous colleague at his place of business, he is transferred to his firm's Manhattan office. He and Alice acquire a city apartment and a country house in a small Connecticut town. The house once belonged to an author named Rachel Danbury, who back in the 1930s wrote a scandalous roman clef based on the adventures of her own womanizing husband as well as some of the town's upstanding citizens. Green goes to some trouble to set up the Danbury connection, and then just leaves it hanging. Will Alice write a similar expos? Does the spirit of Danbury haunt the house? No, and no. Alice lays hands on an old copy of the novel, and reads it, but nothing more comes of this plot line, which is unfortunate because it might have added some interest to this tedious novel. Instead we get more about Joe's endless infidelities, Alice's agonizing, and a hurried happy ending in which Alice finds true love with her best friend's ex-boyfriend.

It would be good to report that the late Olivia Goldsmith (''The First Wives' Club") was back on form with ''Dumping Billy," the novel in production at the time of her death in January. Unfortunately ''Dumping Billy" is a clich-ridden story with characters that are little more than ethnic and sexual stereotypes. The plot is meant to be cute and fresh and charming but is none of the above.

Kate James, born and raised in Brooklyn, has left her working-class roots behind. She's had an expensive education (Brown) and has an enviable job as a staff psychologist at a fancy Manhattan private school. When Bina Horowitz, her dearest friend from the old neighborhood, is rejected by Jack, the man she hoped to marry, Kate and her gay buddy Elliot plot to get Jack back. It seems that there's a babe-magnet Brooklyn bar owner, Billy Nolan, who has a certain reputation: Every woman he dumps marries the next man she dates. Will it work for Bina? You probably can guess the rest.

Diane White writes every month about new light and popular fiction.
