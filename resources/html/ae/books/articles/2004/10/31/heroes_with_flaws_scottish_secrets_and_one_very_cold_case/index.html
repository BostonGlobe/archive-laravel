ON CRIME

Heroes with flaws, Scottish secrets, and one very cold case

By Hallie Ephron  |  October 31, 2004

When I finished S. J. Rozan's ''Absent Friends," I half expected to find my hands coated with white ash. Written in the shadow of New York's collapsed twin towers, this post-apocalyptic story of loss and despair is one of human proportions, played out against a tragedy of historic proportions.

Two fallen heroes are at the center of the novel. Captain James McCaffery, a legendary firefighter, dies trying to rescue victims when the towers collapse. Harry Randall, Pulitzer Prize-winning reporter turned alcoholic hack, jumps from the Verrazano Narrows Bridge after publishing an investigative report that suggests McCaffery had, for decades, been paying off the widow of a childhood friend who took the rap for killing Jack Molloy, McCaffery's childhood friend and small-time gangster. Randall's story hints that McCaffery had connections to organized crime.

The allegations dismay McCaffery's friends,who'd been setting up a charitable fund to memorialize the fallen firefighter. Randall's girlfriend, reporter Laura Stone, is furious when the police assume Randall committed suicide. She believes he was pushed off the bridge to keep him from pursuing his investigation.

This is a dense, ambitious novel, told from multiple points of view and moving back and forth in time, until the truth of Molloy's death decades earlier is revealed and a final tragedy plays itself out. The ending left me stunned and staring into my lap for minutes on end, then paging back to reread the beginning.

Rozan's fiction has been inspired before by iconic events. Her Edgar-winning Bill Smith-Lydia Chin novel, ''Winter and Night," echoed the 1999 Columbine shootings. In ''Absent Friends," Rozan stakes out new high ground. Though I cannot bear to watch video footage of the towers' collapse, I could read this book and grieve as each character remembered 9/11: ''the avalanche roar, the choking black cloud, then the silence, sudden, absolute, and horrifying; then, as in a nightmare, roar and cloud and silence again as the second tower fell."

''Absent Friends" is an eloquent and profoundly sad story about heroes who are flawed in a world that needs all the heroes it can get.

A decades-old crime is also at the heart of ''The Surrogate Thief." Set in Brattleboro, Vt., this is Archer Mayor's 16th novel featuring Joe Gunther, field force commander of the Vermont Bureau of Investigation.

Gunther is thrown off balance when a gun used in a domestic dispute turns out to be the murder weapon used in a notorious cold case. Thirty-two years earlier, a cantankerous local store owner was killed, and he and his wife's $12,000 savings were stolen. It happened back when Gunther was ''a fresh-faced detective on the Brattleboro force."

With the belated discovery of the murder weapon, Gunther has a new chance to investigate the crime he's always felt he gave short shrift; at the time of the original investigation, his wife was dying of cancer.

His colleagues wonder why he's wasting time pursuing this ancient case as Gunther follows the gun, using forensics techniques unavailable decades earlier -- ballistics, fingerprints, DNA -- plus good old-fashioned investigation. Meanwhile, his long-time girlfriend, businesswoman Gail Zigman, decides she's going to run for the state senate. As her campaign gets more and more frantic, Gunther finds himself alone, grieving anew as memories of his wife's death are dredged up by the investigation.

Because of Mayor's crisp writing, and his ability to create thoroughly human, complex, and surprising characters, ''The Surrogate Thief" rises above the average police procedural. For example, when Gunther questions a woman in a nursing home about her daughter's past, we get no caricature of a geriatric or harridan mother. Their conversation veers to why Gunther never had children, and she observes: ''That's the struggle, isn't it? Not to have kids and to mourn their absence, or to have them and be forever concerned about their fate."

From page one, you know you're in the hands of a pro.

''The Reunion," a first novel set in Scotland by Sue Walker, is also about an unsolved crime, but this time it's more of a what-happened than a whodunit. A group of former patients in an adolescent psychiatric unit have made a pact to never reveal the horrific truth about something they did while they were hospitalized.

As the novel opens, the 6-year-old daughter of Simon Calder, one of the former patients, has been kidnapped. Calder is now a successful psychologist. He becomes convinced that the kidnapping is divine retribution for what he and his fellow patients were responsible for years earlier, and he resolves to break their pact of silence and somehow make amends.

The novel zigzags in time, and moves in and out of multiple viewpoints. As the secret threatens to come unraveled, one of the former patients commits suicide and another becomes demented after losing her family in a fire. It becomes clear that someone doesn't want this secret to come to light.

Despite compelling characters, an interesting story, and sharp writing, what's in short supply here is that critical element that makes for a satisfying read: fair play. For example, one chapter ends: ''With an inner weariness taking hold of him, he fetched another package from the old nurses' office." What package? We don't find out for pages. The all-important ''secret" is known to several of the characters narrating the story, but the reader doesn't find out until near the end. Too bad, because the book ends up feeling like a 300-page tease.

Hallie Ephron is co-author of the Dr. Peter Zak series of psychological mystery thrillers by G. H. Ephron.
