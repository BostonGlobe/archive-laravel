Those championship seasons

The fans, plans, and teamwork in Patriots' success

By Aram Goudsouzian  |  October 31, 2004

Patriot Reign: Bill Belichick, the Coaches, and the Players Who Built a Champion
By Michael Holley
Morrow, 240 pp., illustrated, $23.95

ADVERTISEMENT

This Pats Year: A Trek Through a Season as a Football Fan
By Sean Glennon
Taylor, 203 pp., $24.95

For a region steeped in extraordinary devotion to the Red Sox, the New England Patriots had long been a bumbling sideshow, their haplessness snapped only by the occasional, futile title run. Then they won two NFL championships in three years. Only delusional cynics ignore the idea that these Patriots are gifts from the football gods, spiced with the ingredients of professional athletic excellence: a devoted owner, a new stadium, a dynamic defense, a golden-boy quarterback, a freon-veined kicker, a virtuoso coach, a team-first philosophy, and a knack for winning close games.

In ''Patriot Reign," former Globe columnist Michael Holley explores the organizational culture behind such magnificence. He focuses on head coach Bill Belichick, tracing his evolution from ambitious young assistant to defensive mastermind to franchise general. Belichick, we learn, is far more earthy, warm, and thin-skinned than his dour public persona, and he resented the considerable shadow of former boss Bill Parcells. Belichick's success has depended as much on leadership skills as on coaching strategies, Holley writes. He established the current Patriot standards for players of toughness and intelligence that shaped the central drama of the 2001-2002 season, when untested Tom Brady replaced Drew Bledsoe at quarterback. We're told that Belichick made his decision based on scientific breakdowns of their performances, that Bledsoe fumed at his demotion far more than the public realized, and that Brady's mental approach became the organizational gold standard. Holley illuminates these internal dynamics as the Patriots marched to the Super Bowl and reveals the details of the brilliant game plan that delivered the title over the St. Louis Rams.

After that triumph, Holley spent a season with the team, earning backroom access and candid interviews with players, coaches, and executives. Unfortunately, the 2002-2003 season was a forgettable, injury-marred 9-7 campaign. Before the final game, Belichick slumped onto a couch. Someone asked if the Patriots had felt the burden of defending their title. ''I would never admit this publicly," he said. ''But absolutely. There is so much pressure on this team. Every week. Every single week." They failed to make the playoffs.

So Holley kept his nose in the Patriots' business for the 2003-2004 season, and his diligence paid off. He describes meetings where the coaching staff evaluates players with brutal honesty. He explains an elaborate scouting system that quantifies not only physical attributes, but also personal discipline, competitiveness, and mental capacity. He narrates the drama of the ''war room" during the college draft. And he clarifies Belichick's decision to release popular safety Lawyer Milloy just before the season opener. The Patriots, of course, won their final 14 games, culminating with the Super Bowl victory over the Carolina Panthers. When, in a glorious echo of two years earlier, Adam Vinatieri kicked the winning field goal in the waning seconds, it reflected on an organization that values tough, smart, team-oriented players and uses them well.

Holley's top-down approach has its disadvantages. It is Ty Law and Willie McGinest and Brady and Vinatieri who play the game, after all. The author never really delves into the mind-set of the athletes -- what compels them to launch their bodies at each other, why they endure such pain, how they balance personal ego and team sacrifice. But for devoted New England fans or those interested in the makings of a professional football organization, ''Patriot Reign" is required reading.

Sean Glennon's ''This Pats Year" flips the focus, examining the Patriots from the fans' perspective. Based on a series of Glennon's columns from The Boston Phoenix, the book serves up 16 game-day narratives from one season. It's a great idea, one with the potential to explain the fervent zeal of the spectators. Moreover, Glennon writes with crisp humor, such as when he describes a typical New York Jets fan as someone ''completely out of touch with reality, completely unaware that in much of the world outside the city, people still expect each other to maintain a certain degree of decor and civility."

But ''This Pats Year" fails to fulfill its promise. For one thing, it covers the 2002-2003 interregnum; the story lacks a climax, just as the season did. For another, it reads more like memoir than journalism. The author's grappling between faith and ambivalence makes an interesting subplot, but not a main story. Too many chapters are set at sports bars and tailgate parties, and not enough in such unconventional venues as a South End gay bar. The stories never pull together and explain what it means to be a fan.

Why do people devote such energy and passion to a bunch of strangers playing a game? Sports have become our civic religion: They bind us, and we seek larger meanings through the fortunes of the home team. The Red Sox had long taught us a steadfast if irrational faith. The Patriots since revealed have the virtues of discipline, the rewards of sacrifice. They have shown us the possibility of miracles.

Aram Goudsouzian teaches history at the University of Memphis. He is the author of ''Sidney Poitier: Man, Actor, Icon" and ''The Hurricane of 1938."
