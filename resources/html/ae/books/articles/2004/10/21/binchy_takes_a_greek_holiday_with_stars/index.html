BOOK REVIEW

Binchy takes a Greek holiday with 'Stars'

By Julie Hatfield, Globe Correspondent  |  October 21, 2004

Nights of Rain and Stars
By Maeve Binchy, Dutton, 320 pp., $25.95

"Nights of Rain and Stars" is the sort of book you should take with you on a trip to the Greek islands. It sets the scene nicely for all that will surround you there. A group of travelers, strangers to one another, have arrived on the beautiful island of Aghia Anna, and because they happen to witness the horror of a tourist boat catching fire in the harbor of this close-knit town, killing 24 souls, they all stop traveling for a time.

Their lives intermingle and they come to know some of the locals, each of whom has a private Greek tragedy to reveal. This novel, the 13th from the best-selling Irish author who began as a history teacher and went on to write feature columns for The Irish Times, is crying out to be made into one of those plays performed annually by neighborhood theaters, using every would-be actor within 25 miles of the stage. It reads like a Mozart comic opera without the slapstick.

It could all take place in one Greek taverna. Andreas, the anchor of the story, is the 60-something taverna owner and a widower. As in many Greek island towns, this taverna is where everyone meets to catch up on gossip, commune, argue, settle problems, and of course sip a little retsina. Andreas has dysfunction in his own family: He is estranged from his only son, Adoni, who left the island after an argument about putting a nightclub into the taverna and now lives in Chicago.

The first foreign traveler in Scene 1, Thomas, is a teacher from California, on sabbatical and writing a magazine article about his trip. He is divorced, and with bitterness has left his young son at home with his ex-wife and the new man in her life. Elsa is a beautiful blond German television anchor, running away from Dusseldorf and her boss, Dieter. He is in love with her but has problems from a previous relationship, which caused Elsa to flee. David, a Londoner who is more poet than businessman, is traveling to see the world while he is still young, but his parents want him to come home to join his father in the family business.

Fiona is an Irish nurse who has come here with Shane, the annoying and abusive boyfriend who everyone at home, and in the taverna as well, thinks is wrong for her. She discovers this for herself in the second act.

Vonni, also Irish, has transplanted herself permanently to this island. She arrived 30 years ago to be with Stavros, the love of her life. After she married Stavros and had his son, he left her for a much younger Greek woman, taking their son away with him.

Every character has trouble communicating, and at first Andreas gives them all the same "E.T."-inspired advice: "Phone home." They all phone their respective homes, only to be reminded of the family problems that sent them away in the first place. What they really need is a little psychotherapy to iron out their dysfunctions before they leave the island; Vonni turns out to be the group's therapist, if an amateur one.

Possibly she is the best at helping these young travelers, all runaways in one way or another, because she has come through the worst trouble: When her Greek husband left her, she turned to alcohol and went mad before pulling herself together to make a life for herself on her adopted island. Now she runs a craft shop and serves as Mother Earth on the island, taking care of everyone's children, helping in the kitchen after funerals, running for a doctor when a friend suffers a miscarriage.

By the end of the story, Vonni has discouraged enough mismatches, encouraged enough new romances, and patched up enough family squabbles to hang out her own shingle. Author Maeve Binchy, whose novel "Circle of Friends" was made into a film starring Minnie Driver and whose "Tara Road" was chosen as an Oprah Book Club selection, likes happy endings, so you can be sure that sometime soon Vonni's own son, whose father soured him on his mother, will come walking up the hill toward the taverna to be reconciled with her. As the curtain falls on Aghia Anna, the stars will come out after the rain.
