Matters of estate

Legal issues swirl about what Kerouac left behind

By Kathleen Burge, Globe Staff  |  October 21, 2004

The faithful still come to Jack Kerouac's grave, leaving behind small tributes to the writer who embodied the Beat Generation: a single, unsmoked cigarette, empty bottles of cheap booze, a foil-wrapped condom, and a plastic pen stuck like a flagpole in the dirt above a grave marker.

Kerouac's eternal resting place lies in the center of the Edson Cemetery plot belonging to the Sampas family, the Lowell clan Kerouac joined when he married his third and last wife. The Sampas family controls Kerouac's estate, and Kerouac's brother-in-law, John Sampas, has used that power to choose an official biographer, sell manuscripts and journals to the New York Public Library, and allow Kerouac bobblehead dolls to be given away at Lowell Spinners games.

But the Sampas family's stewardship of the author's legacy is under attack. A Kerouac biographer from California has lambasted the family for selling off parts of the Kerouac estate, including the original manuscript of ''On the Road," bought for a record $2.43 million in 2001 by the owner of the Indianapolis Colts. Last week in a Florida courtroom, Kerouac's nephew, the writer's closest living relative, renewed his fight for a piece of the estate.

Kerouac owned little beyond a small house in Florida when he died 35 years ago today at age 47 following a long bout with alcoholism. But his popularity has burgeoned, and with it, the value of his estate, which has been widely reported at $20 million. In recent years, the battle over the writer's legacy has intensified as well.

The nephew, Paul Blake Jr., an occasional carpenter who has often been homeless, argues that a forged will thwarted Kerouac's plans to leave everything to him.

''Paul Blake has been treated horribly by the people who obtained extreme wealth through Jack Kerouac's death," said Bill Wagner, Blake's lawyer. ''He's never gotten one red cent. He's lived in a trailer. He's lived in an old bus."

As for Sampas, the 72-year-old lives in the same Victorian house in Lowell where he and his nine brothers and sisters grew up. He keeps track of Kerouac's legacy in 10 metal filing cabinets, which contain book reviews, newspaper clippings, and letters. Kerouac befriended the family long before he married Sampas's sister, Stella. The writer credited Sampas's older brother, Sebastian, a childhood friend and a poet who was killed in combat during World War II, with inspiring him to become a writer at age 17.

John Sampas argues that his detractors are looking for money.

''What we've got out there is a bunch of swindlers trying to make a grab on the estate of Jack Kerouac," he said.

Kerouac became an overnight celebrity in 1957, when he published ''On the Road," his fictional account of crisscrossing the country in search of often-illicit pleasures. In recent years, Kerouac has seen a rebirth.

Historian Douglas Brinkley, whom the Sampas family gave exclusive access to the Kerouac archive at the New York Public Library, recently edited a book of the author's letters and manuscripts. In three years, on the 50th anniversary of the publication of ''On the Road," Brinkley's biography of Kerouac is to be published. The original manuscript of ''On the Road," now pasted onto a 120-foot scroll, is making a grand tour of museums and libraries across the country.

The writer has attained a cult status, said Cathy Neveux, a guide at the Lowell National Historic Park, who often greets followers who come to the city searching for Kerouac. Many of the seekers are college students, sometimes from overseas, discovering Kerouac for the first time.

''I often think of him as having a following like the Grateful Dead," Neveux said. ''They come from all over the world."

When Kerouac died in 1969, he left everything to his mother, Gabrielle. But just before his death, he wrote a letter to Blake. If Blake outlived his mother, Kerouac wrote, the writer's estate would go to him.

''I just wanted to leave my 'estate' (which is what it really is) to someone directly connected with the last drop of my direct blood line . . . and not to leave a dingblasted [two expletives] thing to my wife's one hundred Greek relatives," Kerouac wrote.

When Gabrielle Kerouac died in 1973, her will left everything to Stella Sampas Kerouac. And when she died in 1990, she left everything to her family. Her brother, John Sampas, became the estate's executor.

The legal battle began a decade ago in New Mexico, when Kerouac's only child, Jan, filed suit against the Sampas family, arguing that the will of Kerouac's mother had been a forgery. After Jan Kerouac died of kidney failure in 1996, Blake got involved. The lawsuit eventually landed in a probate court in St. Petersburg, Fla., the city where Jack Kerouac died.

In March, a judge dismissed the Sampas family from the case under a state law that protected them, because they had inherited the estate through a will that had not been contested, the will of Stella Sampas Kerouac.

''From a legal standpoint, as far as we're concerned, it's over," said Leticia J. Marques, a longtime lawyer for the Sampas family.

But in the Florida court, the case lives on. Last week, a judge announced that since there were no longer any defendants, he would appoint an administrator to represent the interests of the estate of Gabrielle Kerouac.

Wagner, Blake's lawyer, concedes that the chances of his client inheriting the entire Kerouac estate are ''infinitesimally small." But Blake, who now lives in Albuquerque, said he wants to hear the final verdict, even if a victory might not produce any money.

''He feels like he would like to set the record straight so that people know that he should have gotten it," Wagner said.

Sampas said the value of the estate has been greatly inflated. ''If I had $20 million, I wouldn't be living here," said Sampas, who won't disclose his estimate of the estate's worth. ''I'd be living in Paris."

As Sampas waits out the verdict of Blake's lawsuit, he has come under repeated attacks from Gerald Nicosia, author of ''Memory Babe," the 1983 Kerouac biography. As the onetime literary executor of Jan Kerouac's estate, Nicosia helped propel the original lawsuit against the Sampas family.

Nicosia criticizes the family for selling off pieces of the Kerouac legacy, arguing that Kerouac had wanted to keep his papers together. Nicosia was not appeased when Sampas sold a trove of unpublished manuscripts and letters to the New York Public Library in 2001 for an undisclosed price.

''That was the leavings of what they had sold elsewhere," Nicosia said in an interview last week.

Sampas responds that he had to sell off portions of the estate to raise money for taxes and other expenses. Nicosia is obsessed with the case, Sampas said, jealous that he was not chosen as the official Kerouac biographer.
