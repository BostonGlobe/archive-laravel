British fantasy is not just for kids

By Sarah Liebowitz, Globe Correspondent  |  October 25, 2004

LONDON -- British television is full of American sitcoms. British movie theaters show the latest Hollywood blockbusters, and American pop stars monopolize its airwaves. But there's one way in which Britain continues to dominate American culture: children's literature. The homeland of literary heavyweights such as Dickens, Austen, and Woolf is today best known for Hogwarts and Frodo.

From C.S. Lewis to Roald Dahl, from J.R.R. Tolkien to Brian Jacques and J.K. Rowling, these British writers use giant peaches and walking trees to address weighty themes more commonly associated with adult literature. They treat their imaginary worlds seriously.

As a result, children aren't the only ones learning Elvish. The sincerity with which these authors write their fantasies has won them a dedicated -- and sometimes cultish -- adult following.

The latest example of such generational crossover is the celebrated first novel of Susanna Clarke, ''Jonathan Strange and Mr Norrell." Released last month, ''Jonathan Strange" is a history of England told through the eyes of magicians. Though spells and potions are usually the stuff of children's books, the novel's hundreds of footnotes make clear that it's intended for an adult audience. An 800-page erudite fantasy book for grown-ups might seem like a hard sell. But ''Jonathan Strange" hit number five on The New York Times Bestseller List.

The first children's books were written in Britain in the mid-18th century, the result, explains Professor Julia Briggs of De Montfort University, of ''increasing numbers of well-off families with money to spend on children." But this early British children's literature neglected fantasy, focusing instead on the teaching of morality.

Academics date the beginning of fantasy writing for children back to the publication of Lewis Carroll's ''Alice in Wonderland" in 1865. According to Professor Naomi Wood of Kansas State University, fantasy emerged as a reaction ''against the hyper-realism and didacticism in early children's literature."

The arrival of Tolkien's ''The Hobbit" in 1937 and the publication of Lewis's ''The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe" in 1950 took fantasy literature to a whole new level. Confronting the dramatic transformations of the 20th century, the world of fantasy was a way for Tolkien and Lewis ''to go back and capture an England that to them was disappearing," said Wood.

In a 1967 interview with The New York Times Magazine, Tolkien allowed that the Shire was ''rather how England used to be, isn't it?" The reason Tolkien's imaginary world feels so authentic is because to him, it represented something real.

''Fairy tales have always been metaphors for taming the beast of experience, and the fantasy tradition carries that on," said Wood. Fantasy allows us ''to deal with very real and stark issues in a metaphoric manner."

Philip Pullman is the latest example of a children's author using fantasy as a thin veil for questioning reality. The trilogy ''His Dark Materials" is simultaneously a preadolescent adventure novel and a diatribe against organized religion. Pullman portrays the church as mean-spirited and repressive. His books gain further depth from his literary references to Milton's ''Paradise Lost" and Genesis.

But for Pullman, the fantasy always comes first. He never tells his readers how to think. Speaking with the Sunday Times of London in 2001, Pullman said that ''we get our morals from tradition, from art, and from stories which teach the consequences of action. We should just tell [children] the stories and then shut up, not pointing out the moral."

By taking seriously their own imaginary worlds, the best children's authors write without condescension. In 1967, Tolkien told The New York Times Magazine that '' 'The Hobbit' was written in what I should now regard as bad style, as if one were talking to children. There's nothing my children loathed more. They taught me a lesson. . . . Children aren't a class. They are merely human beings at different stages of maturity."

Of course, avoiding didacticism and creating richly textured imaginary worlds is something readers appreciate. The last installment in Pullman's trilogy, ''The Amber Spyglass," won Britain's Whitbread Book of the Year Prize in 2001. While Pullman rejects the label ''children's author," citing his large adult following, his was the first book directed at a children's audience to win the prestigious award. His adult appeal was further evidenced last February, when a six-hour-long theatrical adaptation of ''His Dark Materials" played to sold-out audiences at London's National Theater.

Tolkien's books have also expanded beyond the province of children. Tolkien societies exist around the world, creating atlases of Middle Earth and debating the color of Legolas's hair (blond or brunette?). Harry Potter has gained a similar adult following. The financial windfall of this popularity became apparent last December, when a signed first-edition ''Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire" sold for $49,000.

These demographic breakthroughs have paved the way for such novels as ''Jonathan Strange," which use the familiar elements of children's books -- the English landscape, the epic quest, and the magic spells -- but bypass the young audience. Instead, they target those adults weaned on a diet of Middle Earth and Narnia.

Fantasy, confided Wood, ''has been a secret sin for a lot of us for a long time. . . . I'm absolutely delighted that fantasy is able to come out of the closet."
