Swift boat diaries; death in academe; poetic patterns

October 17, 2004

Page 2 of 2 -- Craig Walley heard about the murder while a student at Mount Hermon from 1957 to 1961. When several planned books about the case failed to appear, Walley, an attorney who now lives in Ohio, undertook the project.

The result, "Murder at Mount Hermon," is a first-rate true-crime story. Examining the evidence, "much of which could not be admitted in court," Walley imagines what happened -- and why.

Walley concludes that the disgruntled Elder was an "obvious suspect" in Speer's murder, and that he had threatened Norton. "We have information that the jury did not have," he writes -- that Elder had been the chief suspect in Speer's murder and that, with the investigation still open, he "may not have known what Norton was telling the police, but he must have felt threatened."

"Real life," Walley writes, "is messier than the world described by mystery novels," and so the murder of Headmaster Speer remains unsolved.

Amy Lowell ranks as a New England poet by her Brahmin heritage if not by her subject matter -- and she stands generationally between Lowell poets James Russell and Robert (who, like Amy, won a Pulitzer Prize for poetry).

As poet Honor Moore writes in her introduction to this collection, "Amy Lowell: Selected Poems," in the admirable American Poets Project series, "To utter the name Amy Lowell is to enter the fraught literary wars of early modernism." Ezra Pound's "vengeful epithet" of "Amygism" (what he dubbed her version of imagism), Moore writes, obscured Lowell's "poetic accomplishment." But through Pound she discovered classic Chinese poetry, and her own disciplined versions are among her finest creations.

Lowell was already in a "Boston marriage," living in Brookline, early in World War I when she wrote her best-known poem, "Patterns," in which the speaker learns of the death of her fianc in battle while admiring the patterns of her garden. It is among the most erotic of antiwar poems:

In Summer and in Winter I shall walk Up and DownThe patterned garden-pathsIn my stiff, brocaded gown . . . And the softness of my body will be guarded from embraceBy each button, hook and lace,For the man who should loose me is dead,Fighting with the Duke in Flan- ders.In a pattern called a war.Christ! What are patterns for?
