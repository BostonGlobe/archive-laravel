A READING LIFE

On baseball, its integration and statistics

By Katherine A. Powers  |  October 24, 2004

Today is the 32d anniversary of the death of Jackie Robinson, a man who is surely among the five most important in the history of baseball, not to mention a player whose career intersected fatefully with the Red Sox's long-blighted expectations. Forced by determined integrationists and an enlightened sector of the press, the team gave Robinson a tryout in April 1945 and that was that -- nothing. This travesty -- an ill omen if ever there was one -- and the Sox's continued disinclination to take on black players, had, unlike the tiresome business with Babe Ruth, truly ruinous, long-term consequences.

Glenn Stout, who champions this view in "Red Sox Century," returns to Robinson and the integration of Major League Baseball in "The Dodgers: 120 Years of Dodgers Baseball" (Houghton Mifflin, $35). The book makes the third in Stout's and Richard Johnson's magisterial team histories, and I shall take this opportunity to say that I hope that they possess the stamina and appetite to get to my first and far-from-forgotten love, the Minnesota Twins (the original Washington Senators). As with the last two books, Johnson has rustled up telling photographs and graphic images, this time of Dodgers baseball from the 1860s to the 2000s. The most absorbing are from the Brooklyn era and include three Willard Mullin cartoons featuring the "Brooklyn Bum." In one, from October 1955, we find the shabby fellow, marked by life's many reversals -- not the least of them being the annual tribulation of being a Dodgers fan -- in the sort of euphoric condition that speaks to the ages: "We dood it! . . . We beat them YANKEES! We spot 'em th' foist two games . . . an' we beat 'em! . . . WOIL' CHAM-PEENS! ME!"

Combining social, cultural, and business history, Stout follows the coupled destinies of Brooklyn and its team, and sets out clearly the real estate machinations, political dealings, demographic realities, and wrong-footed maneuvers of Walter O'Malley that led to their melancholy parting of ways. Naturally, Los Angeles figures large -- the team has been there almost half a century, after all -- but the soul of the book is in Brooklyn.

"History," Stout observes, "is a cumulative process that tries to seize the truth from the irretrievable past." In that spirit he writes with his usual revisionist perspicacity and plausible surmise on matters over which there is much obscurity and disagreement, foremost among them being Branch Rickey's motivation for and method of bringing about the integration of Major League Baseball. Stout has gone into the story in its real complexity, revealing an amalgam of morality, business cunning, and self-aggrandizement on Rickey's part and still astonishing fortitude and grace on Robinson's.

And who else, you may wonder, do I have the temerity to place in this list of five greatest? One is Henry Chadwick, an English transplant to Brooklyn and chief developer of the box score and deviser of essential baseball statistics -- without which baseball could not be said to have a history at all. Eleven and a half pounds of this indefatigable calculator's legacy sit beside me now in the burly shape of the 8th edition of "Total Baseball" (SPORTClassic Books, $59.95) edited by John Thorn, Phil Birnbaum, and Bill Deane. Here, in 2,676 pages, are essays and considerations of various aspects of the game, as well as records for every Major League team and manager, and for every player -- all 16,003 of them -- who ever set active foot on a Major League Baseball playing field from 1871 to 2003.

Lest you balk at the records' terminal date, and think you might as well wait until next year -- don't. This edition has been three years coming and who knows what the future holds for such a massive volume. All its information could be slipped into your pocket in disc form, but where would that leave you? You need a real book at the dinner table to settle debate and for idle tours through its pages during the long months of winter, one search invariably leading to another and on to a cornucopia of serendipitous discoveries. The book is essential, irreplaceable, and independent of electricity -- though block and tackle might come in handy in this instance.

"Total Baseball" was born as a project of the Society for American Baseball Research -- SABR, as it's called, with "sabermetrics" its game. I must confess to being a skeptic when it comes to the longer reaches of this science, which strike me as going an algorithm too far in man's eternal quest for omniscience. Still, it is precisely a love of knowledgeable comparison between teams and players and across the generations that is the great engine of baseball tradition, one which runs upon numbers. The trouble with numbers, however, is that they are pure and nimble where reality is circumstantial and refractory. Among the many fine essays included in this jumbo tome, the greatest is Thorn's "The History of Major League Baseball Statistics," which amounts to being one chapter in the history of humanity's frustrated attempts to create a timeless measure of men.

Once computers were put to the task of harnessing the unwieldy plentitude of baseball statistics and variables, it seemed that scientific comparison of accomplishments could be made, that the fog of context that shrouds performance might be penetrated. But still the problem of baseball itself remained, for the game's rules and circumstances have not only been changing since the game began, but its action has been recorded inconsistently. The more our powers of calculation expanded, the more incommensurability reared its unshapely head.

Beyond that, increased scrutiny of past records began to reveal errors and anomalies that threatened to, and indeed did, topple hallowed numbers: Ruth's home run count rose, Ty Cobb's hit total and batting average tumbled, Honus Wagner's record was all over the map, and so on. It was, in fact, overweening and cloddish attempts to reconcile accuracy and tradition that, to make a long and interesting story short, brought down the once mighty "Baseball Encyclopedia."

"Total Baseball," originally the ruthless iconoclast, and Major League Baseball eventually settled the problem of incompatible and overturned records in the best and only way possible. Averages, for instance, were not altered to make them somehow commensurate with their modern equivalent -- although errors of counting were, in most cases corrected. And tradition was preserved by upholding historic titles and awards, regardless of the continuing revision of data.

Thus is the past granted its integrity and the present restrained from making itself the arbiter of all truth -- which is a good reason to refrain from completing my vexatious list.

Katherine A. Powers, a writer and critic, lives in Cambridge. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@earthlink.net.
