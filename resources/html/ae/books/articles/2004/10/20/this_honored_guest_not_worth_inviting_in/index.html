BOOK REVIEW

This 'Honored Guest' not worth inviting in

By Carol Iaciofano, Globe Correspondent  |  October 20, 2004

Honored Guest: Stories
By Joy Williams, Knopf, 213 pp., $23

The short story might be the perfect literary form for the way we live now. Not enough evenings to finish an entire novel? Too distracted to deconstruct a complex poem? Grab a short story collection and surf the offerings as you would the Net. If you're interrupted by a phone call or haven't replied to the 100th e-mail of the day, you can easily dive back into your book and finish a story the same day you start it.

But even with today's impatient culture, why get to know any fictional character, even briefly, unless it's a memorable encounter?

To make it worth your time, a short story must have some depth of feeling. Without that, the work is simply a sketch. Unfortunately, "Honored Guest," Joy Williams's new collection of short stories, is a set of well-crafted sketches.

Williams is the prolific author of previous short story and essay collections, as well as of four novels (the most recent, "The Quick and the Dead," was a finalist for the 2001 Pulitzer Prize).

There is good physical detail in these stories, and it confidently sets the stage for action. When a museum of oddities is intricately described, you can picture the living rooms and bedrooms, and you know what the men and women inside look like. In a desert that becomes increasingly ominous, the "sun . . . sharpened the smashed bottles and threw itself through the large delicate ears of car-crushed jackrabbits."

But everyone moves in a vague way through these fully realized settings. Though each story is different from the next, they all share a detachment in the telling. As a reader, you're robbed of the chance to see characters react or change or even make a decision to stay the same.

"Congress" portrays a professor of forensic anthropology who is permanently brain-damaged in a gruesome hunting accident. To help him recover, his increasingly disturbed girlfriend and his hunting partner take him on a bizarre road trip. "The Visiting Privilege" shows an obsessive woman who can't stop her daily visits to her friend at a sanitarium, even though many of the patients are openly hostile to her.

"Fortune" shows a cliched set of clueless, comfortable families on vacation in the tropics, oblivious to nature or natives. "Maribou" portrays a mother whose grief for her dead son is interrupted by persistent, unwelcome visits from his many odd friends.

In the title story, a teenage daughter is trapped by her terminally ill mother's knife-edged narcissism. The girl walks through her chores "as though she was hypnotized, as though she were an unwilling yet efficient instrument." Too many characters in this collection share her affliction.

Joy Williams's previous writings have been compared to those of Flannery O'Connor. But in this collection, many stories have brutality but not believable humanity. Instead of Southern Gothic, the resulting abnormality is just bland, like a spice gone stale.
