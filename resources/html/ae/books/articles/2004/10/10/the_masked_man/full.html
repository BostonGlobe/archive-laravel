The masked man

Little is known about the life of William Shakespeare, and the works themselves are an unreliable key to the man. That hasn't stopped scholars from trying to get inside his head.

By Jonathan Bate  |  October 10, 2004

IN THE COURSE OF the 20th century there were nearly 200 biographies of the provincial English boy who became the most famous writer in the history of the world. To judge from the past few years, our own century will have no difficulty in keeping that pace.

In 1998 Park Honan's "Shakespeare: A Life" offered as thorough and reliable a synthesis of the known facts and current speculations as one could hope for. In 2001 Katherine Duncan-Jones's "Ungentle Shakespeare: Scenes from His Life" advanced some striking new angles in a bracingly unreverential tone. Last year, Michael Wood's "In Search of Shakespeare," written to accompany his BBC/PBS television series, gave fresh insight into Shakespeare's friends and neighbors in both Stratford and London. And of course Will was given the ultimate accolade of our celebrity-soaked age, a biopic, "Shakespeare in Love," which won him a fistful of posthumous Oscars.

I know of five more Shakespeare biographies in progress at the moment, while within just the last few weeks two major new treatments have been published. One, ironically enough, is by Harvard humanities professor Stephen Greenblatt, pioneer and leading practitioner of the "new historicism," the approach that dominated academic literary studies in the 1980s and `90s and made biography of an individual author seem a very old-fashioned, not to say redundant, form. The other is Notre Dame professor Peter Holland's essay -- itself the length of a short book -- in Britain's new 60-volume edition of the Dictionary of National Biography.

Indeed, we live in a strange moment of Shakespearean biography, which seems to be experiencing no less than a renaissance. To understand just why it's strange requires some explanation.

In the original Dictionary of National Biography -- begun in the 1880s under the editorship of Leslie Stephen, the father of Virginia Woolf -- the article on the life of Shakespeare was the longest in the entire dictionary, as befitted its subject's status as the greatest genius in English history.

Yet the problem for all Shakespeare biographers, then and now, is how to relate the life to the work. The surviving documentary evidence about Shakespeare's life is not very exciting. Beyond the bare facts of birth, marriage, parenthood, and death recorded in parish registers, most of the surviving papers are legal and financial documents: real estate transactions, records of his shareholding in his theater company, payments for performances at court, a steady stream of minor litigation. Not the sort of thing to reveal the heart and soul of the artist. What we know about the life does not help us to understand the greatness of the work.

At the same time, since plays are plays, in which feelings and opinions belong to the characters and not the author, the works cannot be used as reliable evidence of the nature of the man. Indeed, the thing we most value about Shakespeare is what John Keats called his lack of "personality": his ability to mask his own face, to dissolve himself into his characters, to be now Othello and now Iago, now Prince Hal and at the same time Falstaff.

For these reasons, we will never get inside Shakespeare the man. The best biography would therefore be one that set him in his broad cultural context, moving deftly between the dry documentary evidence and the vibrant intellectual, social, and political life of the age. This is what Stanford professor David Riggs achieves triumphantly for Christopher Marlowe, in his marvellous new biography of Shakespeare's nearest rival.

It is what one had hoped and expected would be achieved by Stephen Greenblatt in "Will in the World: How Shakespeare Became Shakespeare" (Norton). At the heart of new historicism was the idea that literary works were the product less of individual geniuses than of the broader cultural forces of their age. The best new historicists, Greenblatt especially, have an extraordinary gift of going to some obscure corner of Elizabethan culture -- the ritual of exorcism, say, or a strange story of a hermaphrodite -- and using it to illuminate a familiar play, "King Lear" or "Twelfth Night," in an entirely fresh way.

The opening pages of "Will in the World" live up to expectation. When did Shakespeare start hearing poetry, asks Greenblatt? At his mother's knee, he answers, quoting an old nursery rhyme, "Pillycock, pillycock, sat on a hill,/If he's not gone -- he sits there still." Then Greenblatt flashes forward to the adult dramatist at the height of his powers, the depth of his tragic vision: Edgar on the run in "King Lear," disguised as Poor Tom the Bedlam beggar, encountering the mad king on the stormy heath -- he too sings of Pillicock on Pillicock-hill. Insights of a similar kind are repeated on page after page as the narrative moves seamlessly between details in the plays and nuggets of information about Shakespeare's experience and environment.

Yet for all the dazzling insights, the book is curiously old-fashioned in its mapping of the life onto the works. It revives many a Victorian fancy, such as the suggestion that the death of Falstaff is based on the death of rival dramatist Robert Greene. The fundamental problem is that the choice as to which bits of Shakespeare are based on his capacious imagination and which on his actual experience is entirely arbitrary.

Consider some lines in "Henry the Sixth" concerning a soldier who "fought so long, till that his thighs with darts/Were almost like a sharp-quilled porcupine." According to Greenblatt, "Shakespeare himself had in all likelihood not served in the wars and had never seen a soldier's thighs pierced with arrows, but as a country boy, he had almost certainly seen his share of sharp-quilled porcupines."

The current fashion (to which Greenblatt subscribes) is to claim that Shakespeare spent the "lost years" of his early 20s as a tutor in a Catholic household in Lancashire. But back around the time of World War II there was a respectable theory that he spent them as a soldier in the Netherlands. There is absolutely no reason why Greenblatt should not have written the opposite of what he did: "Shakespeare is quite likely to have served in the wars and seen a soldier's thigh pierced with arrows, but as an Englishman he had certainly never seen a porcupine, since this is a beast that only inhabits Asia, the Mediterranean, and the Americas."

Because Shakespeare's "impersonality," his many masks, makes it so difficult to read the life from the plays, biographers are fascinated by Shakespeare's sonnets. Here, at last, Will seems to be speaking in his own voice.

There is, however, no intrinsic reason why a sonnet -- a highly artificial literary form -- should not be a dramatic performance just as a play is. If Shakespeare could imagine Hamlet and Romeo and Viola, he could also have invented the "plot" and "characters" of his sonnets, in which a narrator tells of his tortured feelings for a fair young man and a seductive "dark lady." So it was that Sidney Lee, author of the article on Shakespeare in the original Dictionary of National Biography, could not make up his mind about how to read the sonnets. In the London edition, published in 1897, he argued that they must have been based on Shakespeare's actual experience. But then in the New York edition, published a few months later, he said that they "seem to have been to a large extent undertaken as literary exercises."

So what is the line taken on the sonnets -- and more broadly, on the relation of Shakespeare's life and works -- in the new DNB? The article by Peter Holland could not have been done better. It offers a flawlessly executed synthesis of the current state of biographical knowledge about Shakespeare. Holland scrupulously resists the temptation to read the life into the works. With regard to the sonnets, he lays out the various possibilities: that they are dramatic performances or that they are based on real figures, that the young man to whom the bulk of them are addressed may or may not be synonymous with the mysterious "Mr W.H." named in the collection's dedication, that the Earl of Pembroke and the Earl of Southampton are the leading candidates for this role.

And in our liberated age, Holland can come clean about that which Sidney Lee -- writing contemporaneously with the trial of Oscar Wilde -- remained silent. Dozens and dozens of male Elizabethan poets wrote sonnet sequences, but only Shakespeare and a certain Richard Barnfield addressed their poems explicitly to a man. The "explicit homoeroticism" of the sonnets, Holland writes, "suggests that Shakespeare's sexuality was consciously bisexual in its desires, though the modern concept of bisexuality and one appropriate to Shakespeare's lifetime may be significantly different. Whether Shakespeare's homoerotic desires led to or were connected with sexual acts with the young man or indeed any other man is far from clear." Thus the whole infinitely complex matter is bound within a nutshell.

The most striking feature of Holland's biographical essay is that about one-third of it is devoted not to Shakespeare's life but to what Holland calls his "afterlife" -- the history of his reputation, the editing and translation of the works, the successive reincarnations of the plays on stage and their wider place in Western and ultimately world culture. Holland recognizes that what has made Shakespeare Shakespeare is the power of his works to speak down the ages, to make new meaning in an endless array of different cultural circumstances. In Darwinian terms, Shakespeare's works exemplify the survival of the fittest: Their astonishing capacity for evolutionary adaptation means that they continue to provoke and enthral four centuries after their conception.

This capacity is a direct consequence of that lack of personality identified by Keats. I tell my students that the key to unlock Shakespeare is not the sonnets but the first exchange in "Hamlet":

-- Who's there?

-- Nay, answer me. Stand and unfold yourself.Shakespeare won't tell us who he is. Instead, he makes us -- and our culture

-- reveal ourselves. That is both the source of his endurance and the reason why it is impossible to write a satisfactory biography of him.

Jonathan Bate is professor of Shakespeare and Renaissance literature at the University of Warwick, in England, and author of "The Genius of Shakespeare."
