Anatomy of a biography

Efforts to chronicle the life of novelist Kurt Vonnegut took many surprising turns after he died

By Alex Beam
Globe Columnist / March 11, 2011

Here are my axioms for writing a biography: (1) The deader the better. (2) Few living relatives, good; no living relatives, best. (3) Documents, documents, documents. The lucky biographer has unfettered access to a mountain of records, family memorabilia, and correspondence from the subject’s kindergarten teacher, up to and including the gravedigger who tosses the last pile of earth onto the coffin.

Almost none of these conditions obtained when Charles Shields embarked on his biography of Kurt Vonnegut in 2006. Shields successfully wooed the famous, cranky novelist, who sent Shields an elegant drawing on a postcard with his two-letter agreement: “OK.’’ Shields reproduces this card, and other moments in his biographical journey in his online journal, writingkurtvonnegut.com.

The following year, Vonnegut died. Suddenly, Shields’s world changed. Vonnegut’s literary executors — his son Mark, a pediatrician in Quincy, and his longtime friend and business manager Donald Farber — appointed a young writer named Josh Frank (coauthor of a book about Brookline’s own Peter Ivers, for you 1970s music fans) to write the authorized biography. Shields, who had spent untold hours interviewing Vonnegut, his friends and family members, woke up to see this e-mail: “My name is Josh Frank and I will be writing the biography of Kurt Vonnegut to be published by the Bantam/Dell Division of Random House. I will be working closely with Kurt’s estate and his son Mark on this project . . .’’

Ouch.

Shields stayed the course. His book, “And So It Goes: Kurt Vonnegut, A Life,’’ is coming out this fall. I asked both Shields and Farber how the man whom Vonnegut chose to be his biographer fell out of favor. “Kurt’s family was of several minds about my biography,’’ Shields said. “When you get involved with families, everyone has their own agenda. There are those who loved and admired Dad, and there are those who fault him for not being the father he should have been. One of his family members said to me, ‘You are aware that this is a man who failed at selling foreign cars and couldn’t get hired at the local community college?’ ’’

Before he became one of the country’s most successful and revered novelists, Vonnegut worked at a Saab dealership on Cape Cod.

“We know Kurt Vonnegut as the curly-haired liberal who said riotously funny and clever things,’’ Shields said. “But he was also the tall gawky young man from Indiana who very badly wanted to make good on his promise to himself to become a writer. He was a human being, and not always a great one.’’

Farber said he hasn’t seen Shields’s manuscript, but “I’ve heard there are some things that he attempts to sensationalize. I knew Kurt better than anyone in the world, and there are things about his life that can be sensationalized. He was brilliant, he was lovable, and he was very complicated. Any attempt to write his biography is doomed before it starts.’’

Farber denied Shields permission to quote from Vonnegut’s letters. Pursuant to a famous court case pitting J.D. Salinger against his would-be biographer Ian Hamilton, that means Shields can paraphrase the letters but may not quote them. Farber said that Shields wanted to cite an “outrageous’’ number of Vonnegut’s letters. “I’m a reasonable chap,’’ Farber said, “but he made it awfully easy for me to say no.’’

What became of the authorized Josh Frank biography? “I was Kurt Vonnegut’s official biographer for six months,’’ Frank said. “It was the greatest six months of my life.’’ With a signed contract and a hefty advance, Frank woke up one morning to his own unpleasant surprise: The deal was off. “There were internal issues,’’ he explained. “Part of the family was behind this book and other parts weren’t. Was it more heartbreaking than any other thing that had ever fallen through for me? Yes.’’

(On a less contentious note, former Beacon Hill resident Dan Wakefield, who now lives in Florida, delivered a sheaf of edited and annotated Kurt Vonnegut letters to Bantam/Dell this week, for 2012 publication. “The letters took me a year and a half to edit,’’ said Wakefield, himself a best-selling novelist and friend of Vonnegut’s. “I had no idea what an arduous task that would be.’’)

In his online journal, Shields writes about accompanying Vonnegut to his favorite Manhattan restaurant, Lasagna Ristorante, in late 2006. As they enter, Vonnegut introduces Shields to the owner:

“This is my biographer,’’ Vonnegut said.

“Well, it’s about time,’’ said the restaurateur happily.

“No, it isn’t,’’ Kurt replied with a shrug. “It’s too late.’’

What did he mean? “Kurt was tired of living,’’ Shields replied, pointing me to this 2005 exchange between Vonnegut and PBS interviewer David Brancaccio:

Brancaccio: “How’s life?

Vonnegut: “Well, it’s practically over, thank God.’’

Brancaccio: “For heaven’s sake.’’
