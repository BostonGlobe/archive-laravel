THE ALICE BEHIND WONDERLAND
By Simon Winchester
Oxford University, 128 pp., $16.95

On a summer day in 1858, a young mathematician, writer, and photographer takes a picture of a young girl dressed as a beggar. As Simon Winchester observes when describing the image, the child’s clothing has been “decorously disarranged — her shoulders both visible, her elbows and lower arms, and her chest and her tiny left nipple.’’ She is Alice Pleasance Liddell, age 6, and he is Charles Lutwidge Dodgson, age 26. Four years later, Dodgson, better known by his pen name, Lewis Carroll, will tell a story to Alice and her siblings that will become, in time, “Alice in Wonderland.’’

Dodgson’s works and life have inspired a boisterous biography business, full of intrigue and controversy. After dozens of books accused him of pedophiliac tendencies, using his hundreds of photographs of half-dressed or naked prepubescent girls as evidence, in recent years several scholars have challenged what they call “The Carroll Myth.’’ Winchester for the most part sides with the newer interpretation, that Carroll’s photography was well within the norms of genteel Victorian child-worship. He describes Dodgson as a relatively strange child who made pets of “snails, worms, and toads’’ but who flourished within a family of eccentric siblings. Like all English boys of his class, Dodgson was sent to an all-male boarding school; after that he went to Oxford, where he was forced to take Holy Orders and not permitted to marry — a fate, Winchester reminds us, that meant he could “secure no family for himself, never enjoy the circles of his own innocent children.’’

In this very slim volume — a nice break, for history lovers, from the trend toward doorstop-sized commitments — Winchester sketches both Dodgson’s life and a bit of Alice’s, along with illuminating digressions into the history of photography, and the difference between Oxford and Cambridge universities in the 1850s (Cambridge was devoted to learning, Oxford to “immense drunken parties, outbreaks of wanton vandalism, and a code that dictated disrespect be meted out against authority, architecture, and the church in equal measure’’).

CLEANING NABOKOV’S HOUSE
By Leslie Daniels
Touchstone, 336 pp., $24

Chick lit has been with us for well over a decade now, and the term has lost most of its power. That’s a good thing, since widely accessible fiction about women’s lives shouldn’t be forced into the conventions (shoe-shopping, wise-cracking sidekicks, getting drunk on cosmopolitans) of a narrow genre. In “Cleaning Nabokov’s House,’’ Leslie Daniels accesses some new territory while still giving readers what they want when they want in a light, semi-literary romantic comedy.

Barb Barrett, nearly 40, divorced, and impoverished, is living in a tumbledown house in a college town (a fictionalized version of Ithaca) when she finds a manuscript that may or may not have been written by Nabokov. While trying to finish and then sell the book, Barb conquers a series of challenges, starting with how to make new friends in a small town where “every sparky adolescent runs away, every troubled child is squashed firmly, every artist finds a way out.’’ Barb’s toughest obstacle is regaining custody of her son and daughter, lost in divorce to her control-freak “experson.’’ It’s the authentic, often devastating depictions of a mother missing her children that sometimes raise this book above the rest of it — a standard set of oddball allies, an expressive dog, a suitably goofy big plan that pays off.

BRANCH RICKEY
By Jimmy Breslin
Viking, 160 pp., $19.95

Legendary newspaper columnist Jimmy Breslin isn’t known as a biographer or scholar, which he freely admits in the prologue of his book on legendary baseball executive Branch Rickey. Still, when asked to contribute to the Penguin Lives series of brief biographies, Breslin writes that he had to do it, after hearing from young colleagues that they’d never heard of Rickey, if only to keep the man’s name alive, rescue him — and by extension Breslin, and all of old-time Brooklyn — from modern oblivion.

Rickey was born in 1881 and began playing baseball in 1903. Known mostly for integrating the major leagues by signing Jackie Robinson to the Brooklyn Dodgers, he also invented the farm system of minor league teams feeding the majors. He was a giant of the game when the game was still the most powerful and important sport in the nation, and Breslin’s rat-a-tat-tat sportswriter’s voice is perfect for chronicling that scene; you can almost hear the typewriter keys clacking as he describes Rickey at the bat during his first major league season:

He faced Rube Waddell of the Philadelphia Athletics. Waddell threw three pitches. Rickey wasn’t sure he saw them. The umpire was certain. He said they were strikes.

This is the good stuff, and there’s plenty of it, from tales of Leo Durocher’s biggest brawls to how sports journalism worked in mid-century New York: “Since the Giants were owned by an alcoholic,’’ Breslin writes, “the writers all drank heavily.’’

When it comes to Rickey’s bold, principled actions bringing Robinson (and fellow black pioneers Don Newcombe and Roy Campanella) to the Dodgers, however, Breslin falters. Writing about race and racism, he veers toward melodrama; in multiple scenes black players weep or wish they were white, only to be rescued by white men. Attempting to link Robinson’s achievements to Barack Obama’s only adds to the embarrassment, and when Breslin says that Rickey “could cure the wound of a lifetime’’ with one wave of his cigar (in brushing off doubts about bringing up Robinson), readers might find themselves tiring of this particular old-timer’s game.
