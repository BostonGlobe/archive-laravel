AMERICAN ROSE: A Nation Laid Bare:
The Life and Times of Gypsy Rose Lee
By Karen Abbott
Random House, 448 pp., illustrated, $26

The confusing structure of this biography can’t completely slow down the narrative of the life of Gypsy Rose Lee. Best known now as the eponymous young heroine of the Arthur Laurents and Stephen Sondheim musical “Gypsy,’’ Lee, born Rose Louise Hovick, was a formidable creation.

The less gifted of the two Hovick sisters, Louise grew up on and off stage in vaudeville with her little sister, June, in the spotlight. Rose, their mother, commandeered the young performers, cajoled, threatened, and stole to keep the acts going. When June broke free at 15 to pursue her own Hollywood dreams, Louise reinvented herself as Gypsy Rose Lee and crafted her own singular act — the cerebral strip tease. Along the way, she fell in love with Mike Todd, bore a son by Otto Preminger, consorted with gangsters, and entertained the literati of New York: Noel Coward, George S. Kaufman, William Saroyan, Edmund Wilson, H.L. Mencken. “She could do anything onstage that didn’t involve actual talent,’’ making her long career a series of inventive and desperate acts.

The fiercely beating heart of this story is the relationship among the three women, each one ruthlessly driven and ravenous for fame and fortune. Hating, loving, and needing each other, they were rarely friends. They were accusers and defamers of each other, but never were they completely detached from each other.

AMERICA’S MEDICIS: The Rockefellers
and Their Astonishing Cultural Legacy
By Suzanne Loebl
Harper, 448 pp., illustrated, $34.99

The making, maintaining, and expanding of a vast family fortune are completely beside the point of this history of the Rockefeller contribution to the arts. The original John D. Rockefeller was a devout Baptist who passed on his austere and parsimonious values to his son when he moved the family from Cleveland to New York City. As devout as his father, Junior used the family wealth to build the monumental Riverside Church on Morningside Heights.

Junior married Abby Aldrich, a woman from a wealthy family with a taste for art. She became interested in Japanese prints while Junior started collecting Chinese porcelain. Soon, she discovered modern art, and with the help of 27-year-old Alfred Barr created the Modern Museum of Art. Junior built the Cloisters, Rockefeller Center, and restored Colonial Williamsburg. After Abby’s death, Blanchette Rockefeller, the wife of John III, continued Abby’s work at MoMA. Nelson, the most outgoing of Junior and Abby’s shy sons, worked with other family members to create Lincoln Center and, after the tragic death of his son Michael, established a wing at the Metropolitan Museum for the arts of Africa, Oceania, and the Americas. When Nelson was governor of New York, he created what was judged to be the one Rockefeller artistic failure — the Empire State Mall in Albany.

Several members of the remarkable Rockefeller family, guided by advisers and enabled by great wealth, relied on their own good sense, bold taste, and keen appreciation to enrich their own lives and endow a number of public institutions with art that can be enjoyed by all for all time.

HOW TO BUILD A FIRE: And Other
Handy Things Your Grandfather Knew
By Erin Bried
Ballantine, 304 pp., illustrated, paperback, $15

This is the guy version of Erin Bried’s earlier book, “How to Sew a Button.’’ It offers sound advice on manual jobs — how to mow a lawn, strip furniture, and paint a wall. It also gives lessons on more subtle skills — how to communicate with a mechanic, negotiate a raise, get a haircut, shake hands, be a good sport, wear cuff links, iron a shirt, and change a diaper.

Bried gives instruction on how to introduce people: “Figure out who has the higher rank or level of authority. . . . Name the more distinguished party and present to him the underparty.’’ When buying a suit, “Go for a classic. . . . Buy a lightweight wool . . . above all, avoid poly-blends.’’ When reading animal tracks you should know that “all rodents have four toes on their front feet and five on their back.’’ Bried’s advice on being a gracious guest would help many: “Don’t confuse being a guest with being a king who expects to be waited upon at every turn. Get up off your duff and help out whenever possible.’’
