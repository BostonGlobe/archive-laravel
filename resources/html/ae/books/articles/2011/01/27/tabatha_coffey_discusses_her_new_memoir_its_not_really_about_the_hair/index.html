Of life and hair, she writes from the heart

Bravo’s Tabatha Coffey discusses her new memoir

As a girl, Tabatha Coffey learned about glamour from drag queens. (Pete Tangen/Bravo)
By Christopher Muther
Globe Staff / January 27, 2011

Tabatha Coffey, the platinum haired vixen behind the Bravo show “Tabatha’s Salon Takeover,’’ has been striking terror in the hearts of hair stylists and salon owners for three years, but it seems there’s a tender lady behind the tough-talking reality TV star. In her new memoir, “It’s Not Really About the Hair,’’ Coffey tells of her unusual childhood spent with the drag queens and transsexuals in her parents’ clubs, and her transformation from portly rebel to successful salon owner. Coffey reads from the book tomorrow at 7 p.m. at Brookline Booksmith.

Q. Did you ever consider calling your book “Everything I Need to Know About Hair I Learned from Transvestites’’?

A. No, but that would have been a great title. I may have to steal that line.

Q. It’s all yours. Did those early experiences with the queens ultimately wind up shaping the way that you look at the transformative qualities of hair and glamour?

A. I think it probably shaped how I look at everything. When you’re as young as I was, it definitely shapes who you are and how you look at things. I love glamour. I grew up with drag queens and eyelashes and makeup and wigs and watching Judy Garland. Of course there’s that glamour side to me that I love. On a day-to-day work level, I’m much more practical with my hair than that over-the-top glamour thing. But there’s a part of me that loves that. I wish we could walk around looking like that every day. It would be so much prettier if everybody looked like they stepped out of a 1940s or 1950s movie every day. It would be fabulous.

Q. I can only imagine the things your little ears heard in that dressing room.

A. It was a hell of an education. I think what I took away from it was there was always honesty. Sitting backstage with the girls, they never held anything back. It was total honesty, and I would take it all in, whether some of it went over my head or not. My mother was also incredibly honest. If something had to be said, it was never sugarcoated or hidden. It was always done in an incredibly direct way. I don’t think that’s bad.

Q. “It’s Not About the Hair’’ is an unusual combination of memoir and self-help book. Do you have a lot of people who regularly turn to you for advice?

A. As a hairdresser, a part of your job is confidante. Some people say therapist; I don’t like to say therapist because to be a therapist it takes years of training. Clients sit in a hairdresser’s chair and open up and share things with you that they would not share with anyone else, including their therapist. They tell you everything. Sometimes you give little pieces of advice. For me, what prompted the memoir with advice was the overwhelming amount of e-mail I receive from people that does not pertain to hair. People were writing to me and asking everything from how to yell at their spouse to how to come out of the closet. To be honest, when I thought about telling my story, I didn’t want it to be self-indulgent. I wanted to tell stories that were relevant and could somehow translate back and help other people. I think I have a lot to say, and I’m, as we all know, incredibly opinionated, so I could go on forever. But I don’t think I’m that special that I should write a memoir that’s so indulgent. I truly wanted to give people more of an insight into who I am, to answer their questions, and maybe hopefully they can take something away from it.

Q. Hair is such a fascinating and frustrating topic for so many people. What’s the most common hair question you hear?

A. They fall into certain categories, like “My hair’s incredibly fine and I don’t know what to use on it,’’ or “My hair’s incredibly thick and curly and I don’t know what to do with it,’’ or “How do I find a good hairdresser?’’ What I enjoy about the show, and I’m proud of this, is that it’s helped make hairdressers more accountable, which I think is a good thing. I think it’s also opened up the eyes of consumers a little bit. You’re paying for a service. You should walk out and be happy. You should be taken care of and listened to.
