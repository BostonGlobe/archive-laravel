Old dogs, new tips

Dr. Nicholas Dodman and his veternarian colleagues will be in Wellesley next Sunday to celebrate their new book about dogs.
By Cindy Cantrell
January 23, 2011

Thanks to advances in medical treatment, dogs are living longer than ever. The faculty of the Tufts University Cummings School of Veterinary Medicine in Grafton is helping human caregivers better understand how to handle a range of canine medical conditions through a recently published book, “Good Old Dog: Expert Advice for Keeping Your Aging Dog Happy, Healthy, and Comfortable.’’

According to Dr. Nicholas Dodman, an animal behavior professor at Tufts, Cummings School vets treat more than 8,000 older dogs each year. Their book features common issues such as nutrition, identifying and managing cognitive decline, the cost of caring for an older dog, creating a healthy and comfortable environment, and coping with the loss of a beloved pet.

Dodman edited the book with best-selling author Lawrence Linder. The seven other contributing veterinarians are soft-tissue surgeon Dr. John Berg, cardiologist Dr. Suzanne Cunningham, board-certified nutritionist Dr. Lisa Freeman, orthopedic surgeon Dr. Michael Kowaleski, small-animal internal medicine specialist Dr. Linda Ross, emergency and critical-care medicine specialist Dr. Scott Shaw, and oral surgeon Dr. Jean Joo.

“The goal is to get the information from the experts out there to the people who need it,’’ said Dodson, a Westborough resident who also wrote a chapter in the book, on treatments for Alzheimer’s disease in canines and its symptoms of disorientation, sleep disturbances, and accidents in the house. A founding member of Veterinarians for Equine Welfare, Dodman is a leadership council member of the Humane Society Veterinary Medical Association.

Next Sunday from 2 to 4 p.m., Dodman will join Linder and many of their collaborators at a launch party celebrating the book’s publication at Wellesley Free Library, 530 Washington St. The event, emceed by author and NECN host Vicki Croke, a former Boston Globe columnist who writes about pets and wildlife, will include a panel presentation, question-and-answer period, book signing, and refreshments.

RSVP by calling 508-839-7905, or send an e-mail to vetevents@tufts.edu.

STROKE AWARENESS: Page Minihan was only 4 years old in 1988 when her then-29-year-old father suffered a series of strokes that left him paralyzed on his right side and confined to a wheelchair.

To this day, she said, she hasn’t known anyone who has made her more proud.

After relearning how to speak and walk, Joe Minihan of Holliston continued to coach soccer and basketball throughout the area. In 2005, while continuing to work as a kitchen designer, he earned his master’s degree in education. He is now a special-education teacher at Walsh Middle School in Framingham.

On April 18, Minihan will run in her first Boston Marathon as a tribute to her father.

At 6 p.m. Saturday, she will be hosting a benefit at the Spirit Bar and Restaurant in Porter Square, Cambridge, to boost her fund-raising efforts for Tedy’s Team, a group of more than 35 runners raising awareness and money for stroke research in the name of former New England Patriots player Tedy Bruschi.

Minihan, who grew up in Holliston and now lives in Medford, said she is inspired every day by her father’s strength, determination, and unfailing positive attitude. She is also thankful for his athletic genes, which helped propel her to the role of captain of the Emmanuel College soccer team in her junior and senior years. After graduation, she competed in road races to stay fit, and discovered a passion for long-distance running.

“My father is totally my hero,’’ Minihan said. “Growing up, he never allowed us to quit or give up, and I will take those lessons with me across the finish line.’’

For details, visit www.tedysteam2011.kintera.org/boston/ minihan.

HIGH NOTE: Twelve-year-old cellist Sammy Andonian of Lincoln will be the featured soloist with the Boston Youth Symphony Orchestra’s Repertory Orchestra next Sunday at 3 p.m. in Boston University’s Tsai Performance Center, 685 Commonwealth Ave. in Boston.

Sammy, this year’s winner of the Repertory Orchestra’s Concerto Competition, and his brother Alex Andonian, a 16-year-old violinist, will be among 104 young musicians who will perform in the program, which also features the Sinfonietta and Camerata chamber orchestras.

Tickets, which cost $10 in advance and $15 at the door, can be ordered at 617-353-8725 or www.bu.edu/tsai. For more information, visit www.bysoweb.org.

RUNNING BOND: With her son approaching his ninth birthday in 2007, freelance journalist Nancy Shohet West said, she was keenly aware that Tim was growing older, and away from her.

A longtime recreational runner, West had been inspired by the 32-year running streak of marathoner Ronald Kmiec, a fellow Carlisle resident about whom she had written as a correspondent for the Globe’s regional news sections. Seeking something they could do together, West challenged Tim to run a mile or more with her every day for a year.

She was happily surprised when he accepted.

After getting approval from Tim’s pediatrician and following the guidelines of the US Running Streak Association, they set out to see how many consecutive days they could run. Fortunately, both enjoyed a healthy year that enabled them to endure all weather conditions. They ran on a footpath near their home most days, but also in road races in Massachusetts, Maine, and Pennsylvania.

The streak was threatened several times. Tim told his mother that he was too tired one morning just two weeks in, only to reappear 10 minutes later ready to go. He suffered a bike accident that required a few stitches in his forehead, but felt fine the next day. Then there was the time when the pair had to travel across town to retrieve Tim’s running shoes before it got too dark to run.

Although their streak actually lasted two years, West recently self-published a memoir of their experiences running together from August 2007 to August 2008. “The Mother-Son Running Streak Club’’ includes some of West’s published essays and articles from the time.

“I hoped it would bring us closer together, and for a minimum of 10 minutes a day, it was just the two of us,’’ said West, noting that their runs gradually increased to 5 or 6 miles. “We didn’t suddenly have a different relationship, but this was time I could count on. That was huge.’’

For more information, visit www.nancyshohetwest.com.
