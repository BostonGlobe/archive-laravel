A Reading Life

Years of living dangerously

By Katherine A. Powers
Globe Correspondent / January 23, 2011

Preparing to write his last completed novel, “The Singapore Grip’’ (NYRB Classics, paperback, $17.95), J.G. Farrell spent two months in Southeast Asia, mostly in Singapore and Malaysia. Among the places he visited was the Penang Museum, where he saw a photograph from the early 1900s — when the state was still a British colony — of a group of Europeans, including a couple of ladies sitting in rickshaws. “The padre was there,’’ he wrote in his diary, “looking young and vigorous . . . Nearby one of the Europeans has put his hand on the straw-hat of the rickshaw coolie beside him and forced his head down so that only his hat will be visible in the photograph.’’

Farrell included this in the novel itself, changing the wording slightly to bring out the full force of what was “in the picture’’ from the British point of view. Farrell’s novel and Anthony Burgess’s Malayan trilogy, “The Long Day Wanes,’’ the other great fictional depiction of the region by an Englishman, are both about the dyspeptic end of the British Empire. Not surprisingly, both are more concerned with Europeans than Southeast Asians, but they are also magnificent black comedies about human nature: about vainglory, obliviousness, delusion, and the undertow of despair.

But what of writers who actually spring from this part of the world? I had read none until Tash Aw’s “Map of the Invisible World’’ (Spiegel & Grau, paperback, $15) showed up in the house. Raised in Malaysia, Aw, whose second and most recent novel this is, lives there and in London. His first novel, “The Harmony Silk Factory’’ (Harper Perennial, paperback, $15), won the Whitbread Prize.

Both novels take place in times of crisis, both concern identity and history, personal and national, and both have mysteries at their centers. “The Harmony Silk Factory’’ is, for the most part, set in 1941 in British Malaya in the months leading up to the Japanese occupation. It revolves around Johnny Lim, a ruthless operator, born in poverty, who rose to become not only a powerful communist, but a wealthy businessman. The narrative is pursued from three angles: from Johnny’s son Jasper after his father’s death; from the diary of Johnny’s wife, Snow, who died at Jasper’s birth; and from Johnny’s friend, Peter Wormwood, an aesthete English expatriate.

The novel’s ostensible mystery — later joined by a battalion of subsidiary perplexities — is what sort of person Johnny really was: Was he a villain? A murderer? An arsonist? A betrayer of his comrades’ trust? Yes, he was. Was he a victim? Unjustly served by high-handed British employers? Shunned by an adored wife who had lured him into marriage? Betrayed by a friend, one upon whom he had turned an “innocent, trusting face?’’ Yes, that too. The different perspectives reveal this, but one feels a contrarian impulse at work. Opposite facets appear, but rather than adding dimension, they seem to be in the interest of showing the inadequacy of one point of view — or perhaps any point of view.

Dualities abound: The decent Englishman, Wormwood, is matched by a slimy Brit called Honey. The two of them accompany Johnny and Snow on their honeymoon trip to a little island, an unlikely party that also includes a (noble and sinister) Japanese scholar and spy. After nearly drowning, the group reaches what, for a time, is akin to the Garden of Eden — where, soon enough, a couple of dreadful acts are committed. Taken part by part, the novel is wonderful, completely engrossing in its sense of place and time and mood, as well as in the several stories it tells. But somehow the light that has been shed from its three angles splinters rather than deepens the picture.

In contrast, “Map of the Invisible World’’ has greater coherence, thanks in part at least to its title, which serves as a potent trope for the layers of reality the novel conjures. Set in 1964, chiefly in Indonesia, it begins with 16-year-old Adam, an Indonesian, hiding from the soldiers who have dragged off his adopted father, Karl, originally Dutch, though now committed to Indonesia. The arrest is part of President Sukarno’s attempt to rid the country of the remnants of its Dutch colonial past, a campaign accompanied by a crackdown on the Communist Party and extrication from the machinations of the United States. Adam, who has done some delving into Karl’s past, eventually makes the onerous trip to Jakarta, a city he has invested with promise and hope. Here he hopes to track down Margaret Bates, a university professor, who seems to have been Karl’s lover years ago.

Adam’s own past — his first four years, much spent in an orphanage — are a near blank to him, though memories surface, most searingly of an older brother. Where is he? Though Adam is left in the dark on this score for most of the novel, we learn that the older boy, Johan, was adopted by a rich Malaysian family who refuse to talk to him about his younger brother. He too agonizes over the separation, feeling a sense of guilt for having abandoned Adam, though in reality he was powerless. Both brothers, separated by fate and geography, share a sense of lost coordinates in their lives.

When Adam makes it to Jakarta he discovers a city that is hard and ugly and huge, not at all the place of promise and love of his imaginary atlas. He does find Margaret, but is taken under the wing of one of her students. This is Din, a radical, who had once hoped to write “a secret history of the Indonesian Islands in the Southeast.’’ “To me,’’ Din tells Margaret, “those islands were like a lost world where everything remained true and authentic, away from the gaze of foreigners — a kind of invisible world, almost.’’ No foreign power had imposed its version, its map, of reality on it. Indeed, for Din, the neighboring Malaysia is an affront. Railing against it, he tells Adam: “Malaysia does not exist! . . . Ma-lay-sia is a British construct! It’s a work of pure fiction, created by the old Imperialist countries to destabilize Indonesia and all the newly independent countries of the world.’’

Differing maps of reality impose themselves over each other in this fine novel, creating a picture that is a deftly wrought palimpsest. Beyond that, it is a truly exciting story of a boy’s adventure, some diplomatic hugger-mugger, and a moving tale of several species of love.
