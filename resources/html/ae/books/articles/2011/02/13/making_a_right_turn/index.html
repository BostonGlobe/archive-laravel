Making a right turn

Far from being a joke decade, the ’70s brought new freedoms and national malaise, and sowed the seeds of a conservative era

By Mark Feeney
Globe Staff / February 13, 2011

In the mighty armoire of American history, the 1970s are that leisure suit hanging in the back — far, far in back (if never quite far enough). “Ho, ho,’’ people say, when the period gets mentioned. It’s the decade as punch line. Disco. Gas lines. Gerald Ford. WIN buttons. “Charlie’s Angels.’’ Jimmy Carter. Malaise. The syndicated columnist Joseph Alsop called the ’70s “the very worst years since the history of life began on earth.’’

Except that they weren’t. They were also the most probing and unpredictable days Hollywood has known, bringing to the screen such films as “Chinatown’’ and the first two “Godfather’’ pictures. Women and minorities enjoyed a greater degree of social freedom, political respect, and personal dignity than ever before. More generally, this country loosened up, in ways good as well as bad. The 1950s had been defined by one kind of tightness (the kind that won’t relax), the 1960s by another (the kind that won’t relent). The 1970s, in their wide-necktie, unemphatic way, split the difference.

As Dominic Sandbrook writes at the beginning of his brisk and plenteous account of the second half of the decade, the 1970s were “an extraordinary time in the nation’s history, a period of economic depression, political corruption, military defeat, and cultural introspection, but also one of unexpected popular protest and cultural vitality.’’

“Mad As Hell,’’ which takes its title from Peter Finch’s oft-quoted rant in the 1976 film “Network,’’ begins with Richard Nixon’s resignation and ends with Ronald Reagan’s inauguration. In between Sandbrook presents a rich stew of popular culture (“All in the Family,’’ Bruce Springsteen, “The Deer Hunter’’), politics (court-ordered desegregation in Boston, Proposition 13 in California, two presidential races), and social history (the rise of the Sunbelt, various self-actualization movements, the simultaneous growth of religious evangelicalism and sexual permissiveness).

An Englishman, Sandbrook brings a fresh perspective to this material. He showed in “Eugene McCarthy: The Rise and Fall of Postwar American Liberalism’’ his fluency with US politics. His epic trilogy on Britain from 1956 to 1974 — “Never Had It So Good,’’ “White Heat,’’ and “State of Emergency’’ — demonstrated his knack for blending social, cultural, and political history.

Sandbrook’s being English only occasionally leads him astray. John Updike’s Harry Angstrom isn’t a Toyota salesman but a dealer. Judge W. Arthur Garrity Jr. was no more a “Boston Brahmin’’ than Louise Day Hicks was — even if, being a Kennedy appointee, he tended to associate with a better class of person. More often, Sandbrook’s being an outsider lets him pick up on truths Americans too often overlook. “The real story of 1980,’’ Sandbrook writes, “was not Reagan’s victory but Carter’s defeat.’’ How many people celebrating the centennial of Reagan’s birth last week recalled that he got elected with barely 50 percent of the popular vote?

That’s one example. Sandbrook also notes the crucial role of air conditioning in enabling the emergence of the Sunbelt. He picks up on the affinity between Carter and Nixon: “Both were provincial outsiders, Navy veterans, and obsessive workers; both were deeply pragmatic and suspicious of ideological labels; both were prickly, proud, ambitious men.’’ He points out that making Paul Volcker head of the Federal Reserve was “the most important appointment Carter ever made.’’ It was even more important for Reagan.

Sandbrook has a fondness for informational trees. The cascade of titles, names, and quotations that makes up “Mad As Hell’’ can grow wearying. Yet he rarely neglects the theme-and-concept forests those trees make up. The one he keeps coming back to — it’s there in his subtitle — is the 1970s being for the right what the 1960s were for the left.

At the time, such an assertion would have seemed like heresy — to both sides. Yet the anomaly of Watergate masked liberalism’s decline. And the right’s reflexive respect for tradition and authority made the idea of any affinity with 1960s-style politics anathema. Yet what Sandbrook writes of busing opponents in Boston — “with their contempt for authority, their insistence on their rights, their sense of victimhood, and their appetite for confrontation, Louise Day Hicks and her confederates were genuine heirs to the marchers of the 1960s’’ — can be extended to the populist thrust of so much of 1970s conservatism, and on to the present day.

Reading his account of the fall of the shah in Iran, one inevitably thinks of events in Egypt. Reading “Mad As Hell’’ as a whole, one keeps thinking of the Tea Party movement. “Collective institutions were in retreat; individualism was on the rise,’’ Sandbrook writes of Reagan and the 1970s. “In this respect, as in so many others, the rightward shift of the 1980s was under way long before its greatest architect came to Washington.’’ It continues well after his departure, too. Lest we forget, the two times a Democrat has been elected president since Carter it required Ross Perot splitting the vote, then both the least-necessary war in US history and the worst economic crisis since the Great Depression. If Sandbrook writes a sequel to his book he could call it “Still Mad As Hell.’’

Mark Feeney, a member of the Globe staff, is the author of “Nixon at the Movies.’’
