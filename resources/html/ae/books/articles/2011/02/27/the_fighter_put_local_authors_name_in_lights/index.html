‘The Fighter’ put local author’s name in lights

News anchor and author Bob Halloran. (Jonathan Wiggs/Globe Staff)
By Kathy Shiels Tully
Globe Correspondent / February 27, 2011

Ask Channel 5 news anchor and sportscaster Bob Halloran, a Milton resident for eight years, why he loves living in the town and he’ll tell you: “It’s quiet, family-friendly, and convenient. I love it here. There are great schools. It’s not too busy; it’s safe, and easy to walk around.’’

The middle- to upper middle-class suburban environment contrasts dramatically with Lowell, reputed for years for being “wild, violent, and lawless . . . a town where boxers breed,’’ wrote Halloran, author of “Irish Thunder: The Hard Life and Times of Micky Ward.’’ The 2007 book recounts the legendary story of the boxer’s comeback, which climaxed in a 2002 match broadcast live on HBO dubbed “The Fight of the Year.’’ In that match, “Irish’’ Micky Ward defeated Arturo “Thunder’’ Gatti to capture the world light welterweight title.

“Irish Thunder’’ caught the attention of Holly wood, and Halloran served as technical consultant for “The Fighter,’’ the story of Ward that was nominated this year for seven Academy Awards. Championed by Dorchester-born star Mark Wahlberg, whose passion to tell Ward’s story steered the film through numerous delays, the movie goes up for its shot in the ring tonight, at the 83d Oscar awards ceremony.

The story of how “Irish Thunder’’ came about is a story in itself. Though aware that a movie was in the works, Halloran worked on his book, uncertain it would even be published. After numerous rejections, it was, and an initial 10,000 hardcover copies were sold before the movie was made.

Halloran then got a call from Scott Silver, a screenwriter for “The Fighter.’’

“He asked if he could speak with me,’’ Halloran recalled. “He asked a lot of detail-oriented questions. I sent him all my transcribed interviews. He read my book, and interviews, then went about writing his script.’’

Tonight, Halloran will be rooting for “The Fighter,’’ which has created a resurgence of interest in his book, now out in paperback only. “Sales are really taking off,’’ he said in a recent interview.

Despite his TV career and being married to another television personality, former Channel 4 reporter Eileen Curran, Halloran admits the thrill of seeing his name “in lights’’ — in a movie theater — was unreal.

“For years before the movie was made, I told people my name’s going to be in the credits,’’ he said. “I snapped a quick picture of it at the premiere in Lowell.’’ His children — sons Sean, 20, Dan, 16, and Liam, 13, and daughter, Grace, 9 — are happy for him, “but tough to impress,’’ he said. All except Sean, who has graduated, attend Milton public schools.

The film’s resonating themes of brotherly love, betrayal, loyalty, perseverance, forgiveness, and redemption — set in the world of boxing — are what drew Halloran and 8.5 million moviegoers to Ward’s story. Think “Rocky’’ for the new millennium.

Halloran said he was looking for another subject after his first book, “Destiny Derailed,’’ about the 2003 Red Sox’s failed pennant race against the New York Yankees, was published in 2004, the year of their epic win against their archrivals.

Boxing came to mind.

“It was like a lightning bolt kind of thing,’’ he said. “I knew about Micky for a good 10 years, from when he fought a Providence fighter, Louis Veader, when I was still working in Providence.’’

Searching the Internet for “Micky Ward,’’ many stories popped up, telling Halloran a book was viable. He Googled “boxing gyms’’ and “Lowell,’’ and the West End Gym came up. He cold-called the gym, owned by Arthur Ramalho, and asked whether anyone knew how he could get in touch with Ward.

“Got a pen?’’ was the response.

Halloran called, connected, and the fighter consented to having Halloran write his biography.

“It all happened within 30 minutes,’’ Halloran said.

Beginning his research in August 2005, Halloran finished in September 2006, then began writing. At first he questioned whether he was the right person to write Ward’s story, given their vastly different backgrounds. But, as Wahlberg’s movie ran into delays, Halloran was writing a thoroughly researched story, quelling his own doubts, and weaving hard facts with plentiful quotes from Ward and those close to him.

After numerous rejections, Lyons Press accepted “Irish Thunder’’ for publication.

Moviegoers who read the book are surprised to find “a lot more layers to the story,’’ said Halloran. Such as the two subsequent, legendary rematches, one of boxing’s greatest trilogies, and the surprising, deep friendship formed between the boxers.

Ward’s brother, Dickie Eklund, whom Ward revered, is portrayed more realistically. “The movie had to tone him down,’’ Halloran explained. “In real life, Dickie was much darker.’’

Halloran’s clear admiration for Ward shines throughout. “I hope he comes across as someone who survived in the eye of a hurricane. Micky stayed focused, clean, and sober, when there were so many role models to lead him in the wrong direction. I love that readers recognize the contrast.’’

When not writing, anchoring, or parenting, Halloran is often involved in his community, coaching Milton’s youth baseball and soccer teams, or speaking in schools.

He has talked to the journalism class at Milton High a number of times, and Grace has persuaded him to read to some of her first- and second-grade classes.

He keeps fit running with the Lower Mills Running Club, which he calls “a very informal group, though we do have T-shirts,’’ he said with a laugh. He credits the club with helping him train for the 2010 Boston Marathon, his “first and last,’’ he said. “They took the stress out of it.’’

Halloran has also taught writing at Curry College, where his wife now teaches.

“The college has been very good to me, throwing book parties, he said, one for “Irish Thunder’’ and another for two other books he wrote that came out the same time.

They were “Breakdown: A Season of Gang Warfare, High School Football, and the Coach Who Policed the Streets’’ (Lyons Press, 2010), about Chelsea High School’s 2006 football team and its head coach, James Atkins; and the self-published “The Fellowship of the No Longer Miserable,’’ a fictional story about a boy with leukemia who listens to Boston sports talk radio during 2004 while undergoing a bone marrow transplant.

So what’s his prediction for the Oscars?

“The Fighter’’ is contending for best picture; best director (David O. Russell); best supporting actor (Christian Bale); best supporting actress (Melissa Leo and Amy Adams); best film editing; and best original screenplay (Scott Silver, Paul Tamasy, and Eric Johnson).

“I’d like it to win. It’s an underdog,’’ Halloran said. “But, you know, Micky was an underdog.’’

He added: “I feel it’s a lock for Christian Bale,’’ who plays Eklund in the movie. “I would love Scott Silver to win. He’s a great guy.’’

Asked about his plans for tonight, Halloran laughed. “I’m working,’’ he said, adding he got a taste of Hollywood when he and Curran attended the movie’s premieres in Lowell and Hingham.

Then his cellphone beeped.

“My boss just texted me,’’ he said with a smile. “I can have the night off. Guess I’ll be at the Thirsty Scholar Pub in Somerville with Patrick Cross. He owns it and is a friend of mine.’’

Some scenes for “The Social Network,’’ also nominated for the Academy Awards, were shot in the pub, Halloran said. “We’re betting as to whose movie is going to win.’’
