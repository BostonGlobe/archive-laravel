Flipped lit

Sauron was just misunderstood

By Tom Scocca
February 27, 2011

Can’t anyone let villains be villains? This month brought news of the first full English translation of Russian novel called “The Last Ringbearer,” an inverted version of “The Lord of the Rings,” in which the misunderstood minions of Mordor — a place of “scientific and technological initiative,” as Salon’s Laura Miller put it — are battling to save civilization and reason from being stifled by the magic of the tyrannical elves and wizards. Mordor is the home of “free man . . . living by his own reason,” the novel says; the scheming wizard Gandalf seeks to conquer it, fearing that Mordor’s unchecked progress will eventually allow humans to overthrow their imperial, enchanted overlords.

You can’t really judge the orcs, after all, till you’ve tramped a mile in their armored war-boots. Why shouldn’t they get a turn at being the heroes? It’s the most unsurprising twist in contemporary literature: What if the bad guys are really the good guys?

The desire to tell the other side of the story reaches all the way back to “Beowulf,” the oldest tale in the English canon, thanks to John Gardner’s 1971 novel “Grendel,” in which the strife between the Danes and the monster that plagues them is narrated by the monster, bloodthirsty but mordantly self-aware (“ ‘Grendel!’ they squeak, and I smile like exploding spring. . . . I eat and laugh and eat until I can barely walk”). It’s not an unreasonable reaction to the original poem’s tribal, because-I-said-so account of right and wrong. Even in middle school English class, I found the case against the monster less than persuasive: the Danes were clearly the ones who started the fight; Grendel just wanted some peace and quiet.

But a villain doesn’t have to be ambiguous to get the treatment. The musical “Wicked” broke the Broadway record for weekly gross revenue over New Year’s, bringing in $2.2 million, more than seven years into its run of telling the story of the Wizard of Oz from the perspective of the Wicked Witch of the West. For the youngest readers, there’s “The True Story of the Three Little Pigs,” in which the Big Bad Wolf explains how he was just knocking on doors trying to borrow a cup of sugar, but kept sneezing uncontrollably. Vampires — night-stalking monsters, creatures of dread and bloodlust — are the new unicorns for tween-age girls, and just as scary.

In more grown-up territory, Jane Smiley won the 1992 Pulitzer Prize for fiction for “A Thousand Acres,” which transplanted “King Lear” to rural Iowa and turned Lear’s cruel elder daughters into the heroines. Where Shakespeare had made the daughters the embodiment of a universal ruthlessness — people turned monstrous by acquiring power — Smiley reimagined them as victims of incest, turning against their abuser. (Somehow this was more feminist.)

Before long, the reversals stop seeming clever or challenging and start to seem obtuse. Yes, “The Lord of the Rings” — to say nothing of “The Chronicles of Narnia” — draws in part on some problematic themes of the Anglo-Saxon tradition, going back to the Crusades: fair folk (in all senses of fair) venturing out to battle a treacherous, dusky foreign enemy. In the actual historical Crusades, for sure, civilization and cultivation were not so neatly on the pale people’s side.

Still, we’re talking about Mordor here. Darkness, brutality, and war. If Sauron’s point of view — to recap: conquer and enslave the whole world — is OK, when you look at it the right way, then what isn’t? It’s “Springtime for Hitler,” blooming everywhere.

The effect of the endless revisionism is, in the end, as namby-pamby as the obvious villainies and forced happy endings of the Victorian era. It supposes a world where all conflict is really a matter of different outlooks, where no one is motivated by cruelty or greed or, heaven forfend, evil. Everywhere you look, if you look right, people want to do good.

But if that’s the spirit of the times, so be it. Who is still left waiting for literary redemption, to join the Dark Lord and the Big Bad Wolf in the sunny ranks of the newly acquitted? A few holdouts, with suggestions for finding their better sides:

Simon Legree — Consider (or reconsider) the brutal slave master of “Uncle Tom’s Cabin,” whose coarseness and violence stood out even among the planters of Louisiana. What better cover for a secret abolitionist agent, born in New England? Rather than be above suspicion, Legree put himself beneath it. Only his hatred of the trappings of gentility — stripping the fine clothes from Uncle Tom as soon as he bought him, letting his own plantation lawn lie trampled and littered with debris — betrayed his burning hostility to Southern culture. But his unrelenting public displays of racism guaranteed that no one would ever search the Legree plantation when other people’s slaves went missing — nor would anyone think to wonder whether this Northern interloper might not have a drop or two of African blood in his own veins.

Cruella de Vil — What sort of demoness would steal a whole litter of 15 adorable Dalmatian puppies, for the sake of making them into a fur coat? Ask yourself instead: what sort of people would have been hoarding that litter of puppies in the first place? Maybe the original novel and film versions of “The Hundred and One Dalmatians” involved a bit of projection on the part of the ostensibly good humans, with their fixation on surrounding themselves with high-strung, genetically defective purebred dogs (given 101 Dalmatian puppies, odds are that 70 of them will be deaf). The dog-pelt coat is a guilty metaphor; if anyone was exploiting dogs, it was the so-called heroes. Cruella de Vil was only stealing them away to the country, so they would at least have room to roam, and so their former masters might see the wisdom of rescuing an unwanted mutt or two.

Citizen Chauvelin — The sinister French agent who stalked the Scarlet Pimpernel doesn’t look half so sinister once you realize his foe was a foppish reactionary. As an agent of Revolutionary France, Chauvelin surely drew inspiration from the example of the American Revolution — that world-changing eruption of liberty that the backward British monarchy tried and failed to suppress. Now he saw the Pimpernel flitting back and forth across the Channel, rescuing imprisoned French nobles, hoping to overturn another uprising of the people. History and justice called for Chauvelin to fight back, by whatever means he could, to defeat the international conspiracy of aristocrats.

Ernst Stavro Blofeld — An ascetic, an internationalist, and a lover of cats, Blofeld pitted himself over and over against the amoral, nationalistic, sexually predatory government assassin James Bond. He scoured the world for the most able and trustworthy assistants — call them “henchmen,” if you must — only to have them wiped out, time and again, and his headquarters and technological innovations destroyed, all by a gun-toting bureaucrat dedicated only to the notion that nothing must ever be allowed to become bigger or better than the Anglo-American axis.

Big Brother — The looming, omnipresent face of government in “1984” has become such a cultural cliche, people seem to have forgotten that a “big brother” is a family member. He projects himself into every house because he is part of every household, taking on its worries and responsibilities, hoping for affection in return. The big brother is the first to graduate from school, the first to get a job, the first to volunteer for military service. He cannot escape his sense of duty to his younger siblings, with their messy, half-formed lives. He has an entire Ministry of Love to show how very much he cares. Bad guy? Big Brother isn’t the bad guy. There are no more bad guys to be found.

Tom Scocca writes the blog “Scocca” for Slate.com. His first book, “Beijing Welcomes You,” will be published by Riverhead in August.
