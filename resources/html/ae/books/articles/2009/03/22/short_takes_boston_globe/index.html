Short takes

By Amanda Heller
March 22, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

In this distaff version of "The Man Who Came to Dinner," Australian novelist Helen Garner provides wry support for the cliché that no good deed goes unpunished, though she does it without stooping to cliché herself.

As the novel opens, a 60-ish woman - named, like her creator, Helen - is lovingly preparing her guest room for the arrival of an old friend, Nicola. Vibrant and unconventional, Nicola has, unthinkably, been stricken with terminal cancer. Dismissive of her doctors in Sydney, she has arranged to take alternative treatment in Melbourne while staying with Helen, who envisions herself managing the crisis like Florence Nightingale with a touch of Martha Stewart style. Within days, of course, Helen is utterly drained by the demands of serving as nurse, housekeeper, confidante, and co-conspirator to a desperately ill woman, a charming, manipulative egotist who has rejected sound medical advice and invested her faith - and her money - in quackery.

Is there room for honesty in such an arrangement, let alone the peaceful attainment of closure and expiation of survivor's guilt Helen has in mind? Garner presents a bracingly sympathetic portrait of compassion fatigue as Helen orbits for the last time the dazzling sun of Nicola's friendship.

Being a sheriff has its peculiar challenges, explains Sheriff Lucian Wing, lawman philosopher of the north country. "The people want you to do your job . . . but not on them." Zen and the art of law enforcement.

Introduced in Castle Freeman Jr.'s "Go With Me," Sheriff Wing is back for another serving of laconic Vermont wit and wisdom, with a modicum of crime solving on the side. Wing has no use for "evildoers," certainly. But unlike the gung-ho deputy who's got his eye on the sheriff's job, Wing isn't so much fixated on locking them up and throwing away the key as he is inclined to lean on them gently till they exit the county and take their troublemaking ways with them.

In "All That I Have" the trouble comes from a predictable quarter - a lawless local youth for whom the sheriff nevertheless has a soft spot - and an unexpected one in the person of a Russian mobster who has built a rural retreat and infested the vicinity with his goons. With help and hindrance from assorted eccentrics, including his own restive wife, Wing calms the waters, though it's not the suspense but the sly, country-cousin charm of Freeman's storytelling that is the main attraction.

In his youth, discovering that he was pretty good at math, Christopher Potter considered becoming a mathematician, until he realized that being pretty good at math is no more likely to lead to a mathematical career than being pretty good at swimming is likely to lead to the Olympics. Instead he detoured into a degree in the history and philosophy of science and a distinguished career in publishing, all the while percolating the idea for this book.

Reading "You Are Here" is like taking a guided tour of the universe with a private car and driver. Potter discourses with ease while all the grand theories and grand thinkers - relativity, evolution, quantum field theory, string theory, Pythagoras, Galileo, Einstein - flash by the window. He meditates on the origins and ascent of life through the eons, on the infinitesimally tiny atom systems within our bodies and the infinitely vast galactic systems beyond our own, cavorting on the edge of the space-time continuum in a way that tends to make people unbearably nervous: to induce existential vertigo, as he puts it more elegantly, giving us in the course of a few hours' time quite an advanced education in certainties and their opposite.

Amanda Heller is a critic and editor who lives in Newton.
