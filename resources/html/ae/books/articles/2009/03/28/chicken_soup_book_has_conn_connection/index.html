'Chicken Soup' book has Conn. connection

By Beth Cooney
Associated Press Writer / March 28, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

STAMFORD, Conn.—Wendy Walker wrote her first novel in the back of her minivan in between car pooling her three boys to school. So when she signed on to edit a collection of inspirational stories for stay-at-home moms, she produced the book the with the same get-it-done-before-the kids-need-me spirit.

She networked by cell phone with celebrity contributors while pushing a shopping cart in the Stamford Target. She got up at 5 a.m. to read through the thousands of essay submissions before it was time to stuff backpacks. "I juggled, juggled, juggled," Walker said. "Like all stay-at-home moms, I did it while doing a million other things at the same time."

For the unsung women whose daily work keeps them close to the kids, Walker has collected "Chicken Soup for the Soul: Power Moms," which includes essays by 14 Fairfield County mothers such as novelists Jane Green of Westport and Elise Chidley of Darien and Mary Himes (wife of U.S. Rep. Jim Himes, D-4th District). Celebrity contributors include maternity fashion designer Liz Lange; best-selling novelist Jodi Picoult; Lynne Spears, mother of recording artist Britney Spears; and Melora Hardin who stars in NBC's "The Office."

"You would think if you got 100 moms together to tell their stories of staying at home with their kids, there would be this commonality, that it might even get redundant," said Walker, a Stamford resident who contributed two of her own essays. "What struck me was the quality and diversity of the stories we received. They range from the stories of mothers who home-school their kids to mothers coping with children with autism. They are also reflecting on the full experiences of motherhood, from birth to making the decision to stay at home to letting your children go and becoming an empty nester."

With almost 200 titles, the "Chicken Soup for the Soul" book series has included stories meant to inspire everyone from middle schoolers and empty nesters to recovering addicts and golfers. So how is it that stay-at-home moms got ignored by the publishing phenomenon for so long?

"We thought it was more than overdue," said Amy Newmark of Greenwich, a former work-from-home mom who is now publisher of the ever-expanding "Chicken Soup for the Soul," titles. Last year, her husband and his business partner purchased the "Chicken Soup" series from originators Jack Canfield and Mark Victor Hansen (who still serve as consultants and get author credits on the books). The company moved from California to Cos Cob.

For Newmark, who years ago left her hedge fund job to work from home and "raise my kids and do the room mother thing," the fact that the series had yet to develop a collection for home-based moms was a glaring omission and a first on her "to-do" list as new publisher. With her youngest child off to college, Newmark made the "Power Moms" book among her first "Chicken Soup" projects.

While the term "Power Moms," may connote images of moms toting briefcases and heading off to the train station in suits, Newmark says it is a reference to "the kind of do-it-all lives women who stay at home lead."

"Mothers who have reorganized their lives to be with their children need a little validation," she said. "It's validation they usually don't get at the cocktail party when someone asks, 'What do you do?'"

Newmark asked Walker to edit the book after reading an article in The Advocate and Greenwich Time last year about Walker writing her first novel, "Four Wives," in the back of her minivan. "I said, 'That's exactly the kind of mom I want to do this.' She's someone who has done the juggling and I knew would develop a mix of stories from traditional stay-at-home moms and moms who have found a way to do their other work from home."

Walker, a former litigator, notes in a recent interview she was "blown away" by the diverse lives many stay-at-home moms who contributed to the collection were leading. "Many, many of these women are working jobs from home. Computers have changed our lives as stay-at-home mothers quite radically. Yet we are still involved in our children's lives in a different way than we might be if we were taking the train into the city."

Lange writes about building her clothing empire while raising her children. Hardin's anecdote is about helping her daughter overcome a fear of heights. Himes writes about how her husband's congressional campaign -- and its demands on their young family -- compelled her to give up a magazine job she had once loved. Spears writes about how she reluctantly gave in to Britney's requests to enter a beauty pageant ignoring her "sixth sense" as a mother, and later had to soothe her shattered daughter after a poor showing.

Walker said while some people might view Spears, who raised two celebrity daughters who have been part of some sensational tabloid stories, as an interesting choice for the book, "I thought her essay was very insightful. She wrote about the importance of listening to your instincts and I think that's something we can all relate to."

There are times when these home-based choices can be isolating and disconcerting, as Walker notes in her own essay.

She tells a story of going to Washington, D.C., on a "girls' weekend," with some fellow stay-at-home moms and visiting the gallery of the U.S. Supreme Court. By coincidence the court was hearing arguments on a case she had worked on during a year she worked for the American Civil Liberties Union. "I sat in the gallery anticipating the justices questions and what my answers would be. This was my case, except I wasn't arguing it."

Did it make her regret her choices?

"No, but I came close to it in that moment," she said. "And my whole point of writing it was to reflect on the idea that we can go there sometimes. There are moments when we contemplate missed opportunities and there is sometimes regret. Ultimately, I've always felt like I did the right thing by staying home."

Contributor Elise Chidley, author of the novel "Your Roots are Showing," wrote her first novel in an attic office of her Darien home, mostly while her three children were still babies.

"I wrote in snatches, whenever I could because what I wanted, even before I had the kids, was to be at home with them. I kind of always knew that would be my choice," she said.

"The most challenging part of this life is that people don't really believe I work," Chidley said. "Having my novel published kind of validated all my protesting that I was busy -- I really was busy -- but when you are not going off to an office people tend to think you have time for things you don't. Now I have deadlines and people respect it more, but it's kind of ironic that I had to get published before anyone took it seriously because I was home with the kids."

Chidley said she was inspired to be included with so many other stay-at-home, work-from-home moms. "We really need some chicken soup," she said. "But sometimes we don't get any."
