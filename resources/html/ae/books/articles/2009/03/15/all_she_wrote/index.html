All she wrote

A comprehensive survey of American women writers - their lives and works - from 1650 to 2000

By Mary Loeffelholz
March 15, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

More than 30 years ago, Elaine Showalter's "A Literature of Their Own: British Women Novelists From Brontë to Lessing" helped launch American feminist criticism. Trading on Virginia Woolf's famous prescription for women writers - their need for "a room of one's own" - "A Literature of Their Own" argued that women writers as a group had passed through three distinct phases: a "feminine" phase of imitating and accommodating male standards, a "feminist" phase of protest against the injuries suffered by women, and a "female" phase of drawing on women's own experience for the source of an autonomous art. In "A Jury of Her Peers: American Women Writers From Anne Bradstreet to Annie Proulx," Showalter tells much the same story, although in another national key and with an even more affirmative ending.

American women writers at the beginning of the 20th century, Showalter declares, found that they "had traversed the three stages of feminine, feminist, and female writing, and had moved into the fourth stage: free." No longer bound to feminist protest as their attitude or to women's experience as their subject matter, American women writers can now write on any subject, taking their chances in the literary marketplace alongside men.

Observing that hers is the first comprehensive literary history of American women writers, Showalter proposes that present-day women's studies has elevated "the encyclopedic anthology of women's writing" over the writing of literary history, "which inevitably makes selections, distinctions, and judgments." Although anyone who has ever faced the task of editing a literary anthology may be astonished to learn that it involves no labors of selection and judgment, Showalter is nevertheless right in distinguishing narrative literary history from the different forms of tact required by the anthology, and she is brave in attempting to "tell a story of American women's writing with a beginning, middle and end," one that passes negative as well as positive judgments along the way.

In more ways than one, the middle is the most rewarding part of Showalter's account of American women's writing. At the heart of "A Jury of Her Peers" lies a robustly middlebrow pantheon of the realistic novel as written by American women, from Harriet Beecher Stowe's "Uncle Tom's Cabin" in the middle of the 19th century, to Harper Lee's "To Kill a Mockingbird," Grace Metalious's "Peyton Place," and Marilynne Robinson's "Housekeeping" amid the 20th. Showalter flavors this centrist canon of women's realism with occasional glances toward other fictional traditions - the spate of late 19th-century utopian fiction by women, for example, or feminist science fiction and women's hard-boiled detective fiction in the later 20th century.

But Showalter does her best storytelling on behalf of American women writers in her long view of the development of women's realistic fiction and in her forceful praise for women's accomplishments in the realm of middlebrow culture. Showalter declares Stowe "a daring and forceful architect of narrative" and "Uncle Tom's Cabin" "an American masterpiece," relishing its "enormous and shameful popularity" that made so many later readers cringe. "Peyton Place," another shamefully popular book in critics' eyes, "defied the unwritten laws against women's sexual frankness in fiction." "To Kill a Mockingbird" followed "Uncle Tom's Cabin" in taking on what Showalter calls "the grand triumvirate of American history - race, sexuality, and the South," and, like "Uncle Tom's Cabin," became a bestseller.

Outside this canon of realistic social fiction, Showalter is less engaged by what she finds. Her treatment of poetry is significantly less coherent and ambitious than her reading of fiction: "A Jury of Her Peers" complains that 19th-century women poets, the familiar exception of Emily Dickinson aside, "devoted their energies to new content rather than new forms," but, closer to home, Showalter herself evaluates the feminist poets of the 1970s, especially Adrienne Rich and Anne Sexton, in terms of their racy new content rather than their explorations in poetic form. Formal innovation, it turns out, is a dispensable virtue that rarely draws Showalter's praise unless allied to clear-cut social content and, preferably, some degree of popular recognition.

Showalter reserves her most negative judgments for self-consciously avant-garde authors, especially authors divorced from the hardworking ethos of respect for the literary marketplace. Elizabeth Stoddard's elliptical, protomodernist novel "The Morgesons" (1862) has been praised by recent critics, but Showalter declares Stoddard's formal innovations "more self-indulgent than illuminating" and blasts her "indifference to the great public issues of her time." Showalter has even less patience with Gertrude Stein, pegging her as "a wealthy salonnière" who "did not need to earn a living" from her writing. Stein and her partner, Alice B. Toklas, were "the pet Jews of a Nazi collaborator" in Vichy France; if that were not enough, Stein is "unreadable, incomprehensible, self-indulgent, and excruciatingly boring."

Not every woman writer, Showalter declares, is a candidate for canonization. Judgments must be ventured, evaluative rankings made, even if it strains feminist sisterhood. Some of Showalter's readers will take her call for judgment in stride, believing not only that evaluation should happen but that it always happens, that all purposeful human activity is selective. More troublesome for many of her readers will be Showalter's assumption that women writers can enter American literary history with finality only by entering as a bounded group. "A Jury of Her Peers" asserts both that "women's writing as a separate literary tradition . . . reached the end of its usefulness" at the end of the 20th century and that feminist scholars need to keep on writing the story of its separate tradition. Feminist scholarship has already branched out beyond this task, though, to write a wider range of histories, including those of women's literary networks with men. The separate tradition-building that Showalter helped found is now one pillar of a larger intellectual project, in which it may be less important to declare women writers "free" than to explore all the ties that have bound them.

Mary Loeffelholz is professor of English and vice provost for academic affairs at Northeastern University and the editor of "The Norton Anthology of American Literature, Seventh Edition: Volume D: 1914-1945."
