For children

Simplicity plus ingenuity

By Liz Rosenberg
March 15, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

After what felt like a long, weary dry spell, picture books have leapt back into their own. Inventive, bold, subtle, this crop of picture books offers variety and charm for the very young.

We think we know what a picture book is: read one page, turn to the next, keep moving forward from start to finish. It takes a special mind to consider radically inventive possibilities. "One Boy," by Laura Vaccaro Seeger, sets off in the general direction of her Caldecott Honor concept book, "First the Egg," whose engaging charm was both softer and sweeter. As in her earlier book, the flip of a page in "One Boy" not only reveals but transforms what we see. It does so with a more antic, let us say a more boyish, personality. ONE boy, sitting, drifts (next page) all aLONE in a sea of empty chairs; three APEs make a daring escAPE, and so on.

"One Boy" is a lively counting book, an ingenious die-cut concept book, a hide-and-seek game of words hidden within words. Colors flash by in jumpy, vivid backgrounds of pumpkin, royal blue, lime, and fire-engine red. Toddlers and preschoolers will gravitate toward its ultra-bright palette. "One Boy" is a book of constantly shifting perspective as well as visual and verbal surprises - a sort of two-dimensional jack-in-the-box.

Say what you will about the Nobel Prize, the Swedes know how to write for children. From Selma Lagerlöf (the first woman writer ever to win the Nobel Prize in Literature) to Astrid Lindgren of "Pippi Longstocking" fame, Swedish writers have a gift for writing straight for, rather than at, children. Their child heroes and heroines tend to be sturdily independent, and experience a range of complex emotions that we Americans consider the sole prerogative of adults.

"Oink, Oink Benny," by beloved contemporary Swedish author Barbro Lindgren, is deceptively simple. Young Benny the pig wants to poke around in the nearby mudhole; so does his little brother. Both adore the older Klara, their favorite friend. When a bully pushes Benny's little brother into the mudhole, Klara jumps in to rescue him, then lets him deliciously "sit with his snout against her cheek." All well and good, but Benny too "wants to sit on Klara's knee with his snout against her cheek." Like a true romantic hero, he splashes into the mudhole. Now both brothers are filthy and wet. How will they sneak home, mischief undetected?

Like any good comedy team, Lindgren and illustrator Olof Landström work in rhythm together, traveling hand in glove across a landscape composed in equal parts of beauty and wit. While many of the pictures are cartoon-like in effect, others, in the deep woods, or amid a drenching slantward rain, have the exquisite simplicity of an old Japanese woodcut. The book's beauty is as elegantly transparent as an Orrefors crystal vase.

Last but by no means least, "The Cow That Laid an Egg," by Andy Cutbill, is an exceptionally weird and hilarious picture book. Marjorie the cow looks depressed. "I can't ride bicycles and do handstands like the other cows," she says. "I just feel so ordinary." Her friends the chickens conspire to do something about it. "I've laid an egg!" Marjorie hollers the next morning. The news galvanizes the barnyard, and the world. Marjorie's egg is spotted black and white, like Marjorie. But her fellow cows are jealous - and deeply suspicious. When the egg hatches, who will be vindicated - the "crafty chickens" or the doubting cows?

Artist Russell Ayto adds a dimension of comic lunacy to this charming, familiar tale. Cows and people have terrifyingly large smiles. The chickens look mechanical. Every figure is larger than life and twice as expressive. No artist has gotten a greater gamut of emotion from a bird since Mo Willems's "Don't Let the Pigeon Drive the Bus!" made its bold debut. "The Cow That Laid an Egg" shares some of that same manic energy.

Liz Rosenberg reviews children's books monthly for the Globe.

Roaring Brook, 48 pp., ages 2-6, $14.95

OINK, OINK BENNY Written by Barbro Lindgren

Illustrated by Olof Landström

R & S, 28 pp., ages 4-8, $16

THE COW THAT LAID AN EGG Written by Andy Cutbill

Illustrated by Russell Ayto

HarperCollins, 32 pp., ages 4-8, $16.99
