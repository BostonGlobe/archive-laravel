The Interview With Carol Windley

The story as 'a mirage in the distance'

CAROL WINDLEY (ROBERT WINDLEY)
By Anna Mundow
March 8, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

The eight stories in Carol Windley's new collection, "Home Schooling'' (Atlantic Monthly, $22), may be confined to Vancouver Island, Canada, but the depth and breadth of human experience that they contain are universal and timeless. A daughter attends her mother's wedding; a child discovers a neighbor's secret; a man, outwardly happy yet bereft, wanders into the forest. With consummate care and precision, Windley evokes entire lives and the place that has formed them (see "Short Takes," Page C5, for a review). She spoke from her home on Vancouver Island.

Q. Was the first story, "What Saffi Knows," disturbing for you to write?

A. I set out deliberately to disturb myself, I think. Because I find it difficult to subject my characters to anything really bad, and that's a failing in a way. I had read a newspaper account of a young boy who was kidnapped and held captive for some time on a street where the neighbors didn't notice anything amiss. I think that when such horrific events occur, someone does, in fact, notice, but cannot communicate his fears. It seemed to me that this would be especially true of a child.

Q. Then you had to write from a child's point of view?

A. Yes. It always raises the question of whether you can write effectively with a child's understanding and vocabulary or whether you take liberties with that, give the child more awareness and self-knowledge. It is a problem. In "What Saffi Knows," I tried to deal with it by including the child as an adult looking back. But some writers do it so well that the child speaks convincingly as an adult because the story is so well constructed.

Q. What do short stories demand of you that novels don't?

A. I prefer writing short stories, I think, although they are not easy. One of the difficulties is the enormous range of possibilities in structure and viewpoint and style. I like the compression of the story, the intensity of language the form allows. It wasn't until I started to read short stories - by people like Alice Munro, Mavis Gallant, John Updike . . . Eudora Welty - that I became excited about the possibilities of writing.

Q. Did you have a shape in mind for this collection as a whole?

A. I conceived of it as dealing with the idea of schooling in the family; how parents pass along viewpoints and ideas, often unconsciously. That was the link that I saw.

Q. Did that confine the stories to the domestic realm?

A. That's an interesting question. I have a preference for writing that deals with domestic issues; even in visual art I like work that focuses on very small aspects of human life. I like movies that have a very narrow focus. I can see how it might be viewed as limiting, but I don't experience it that way.

Q. What span of time do these stories represent?

A. [Laughs.] Too much time. Probably about five years. Probably too much rewriting. I can get really obsessive. I like writing many drafts, and I try not to because it is very time-consuming, especially when you're working on a novel.

But I do like to take a story and reorder it, put things in different places. This allows me to see things in a new and sometimes surprising way. Each story presents a mystery that has to be solved in the process of writing. When I'm at work on a story, I'm completely immersed in that world and in the lives of those characters; they're utterly real to me. Then when I've completed the story it all just falls away. The whole compulsion to understand is over.

Q. Can you think of an example from one of these stories?

A. In the last story, "The Reading Elvis," I played around with many rewrites. I started off without the wife, Sarah. Graham was involved with Kathleen. Then I moved Kathleen to a more distant time in his life. It was nerve-racking. Sometimes it felt like being on a high wire.

Q. In what way?

A. Well, I'm not the first person to describe the short story this way, but it does have the feel of a bubble that you're holding in your hand - this perfect, round, iridescent shape - and you want to write something that has that shape. You're working towards the finished story that you can see like a mirage in the distance.

Q. Is Vancouver Island important to you as a writer?

A. It is, especially in these stories because as I was writing them a lot of change was occurring here - there has been a lot of development over recent years - and I think that these stories were my attempt to deal with my memories of Vancouver Island before it was so disturbed. I was born here and have spent most of my life here, so the setting is definitely a character for me in these stories.

Q. Do you also need to distance yourself from that place to write about it?

A. I've often heard that it's best to write about a place you know well from a distance, to form a perspective you perhaps aren't aware of, and I did work on some of the stories while I was in Saskatchewan. I think that clarified things in my imagination. But I really haven't lived many other places, so I had to make do.

Anna Mundow, a freelance journalist living in Central Massachusetts, is a correspondent for the Irish Times. She can be reached by e-mail at ama1668@hotmail.com.
