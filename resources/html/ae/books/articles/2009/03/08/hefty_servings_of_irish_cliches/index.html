A Reading Life

Hefty servings of Irish cliches

By Katherine A. Powers
March 8, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

Two things spring immediately to mind when people think of Ireland: potatoes and drink. It doesn't seem to matter that the country of my forebears (the few who weren't German) is a globalized, high-tech state that manufactures Dell computers and Viagra and has salmonella outbreaks and a collapsing economy just like our own; Ireland's image remains tied to potatoes and drink. And what's this I see before me to be published on St. Patrick's Day (though available now)? Two proofs of my point: a book about Irish pubs, and another about the potato.

The American writer Bill Barich moved to Ranelagh, on Dublin's south side, some time ago and set out to find the perfect Irish pub. "A Pint of Plain: Tradition, Change, and the Fate of the Irish Pub" (Walker, $25) is an engaging account of his quest and investigations. What he had in mind, and assumed would present no problem in finding, was something like Pat Cohan's, the establishment he admired in the movie "The Quiet Man." In such a place, "a genial but hardnosed publican kept the intrusions and distractions of the civilized world at bay to allow the art of conversation to flourish."

If you've been to Ireland's fair isle in the last decade or so, you're beginning to smirk. In no time at all, Barich comes face to face with the horror that makes up a great deal of pub life in the New Ireland: loud piped pop music; amplified traditional-music sessions - or "seisiuns," as people like to spell them these days; gigantic, bar-dominating TVs; inept bar staff. He visits the Baggot Inn, with its individual tables equipped with beer taps and credit card scanners for pulling your own pint - a development that I call positively antinomian. He visits the trendy Temple Bar district of Dublin, where, as he puts it, "the scene after midnight, with its shoving matches, broken bottles, and pools of vomit, isn't for everyone."

On the run from the Celtic Tiger and its little ways, Barich visits Dublin's more famous ancient pubs and literary drinking haunts, finding most of them not up to snuff. Whether empty or filled with tourists, they're more trophies or shrines to the past than bearers of their own life. On he goes, following various leads looking for his ideal and for "the craic," as it's called. Here I must stop and say how truly grateful I am to this man for explaining whence came that terrifically phony spelling for something now marketed like a commodity. In my day - and I was once myself a Dublin barmaid, to use the nomenclature of the time - the word was "crack," and it meant, essentially, "good fun" of the sort involving sparkling conversation and wit fueled by a certain amount of liquid refreshment. "Craic," explains Barich, "looks as if it's an old Gaelic word, but it derives from the Anglo-Saxon. Pronounced 'crack,' it was spelled that way until the media recast it, faux style, to avoid any confusion with crack cocaine."

Though Barich does manage to find a few pubs that suit him (the Gravediggers' Arms near Glasnevin Cemetery; Grogan's, a.k.a. the Castle Bar, off Grafton Street; the Cobblestone on the edge of the Smithfield Market), they're not Pat Cohan's. So he continues his search in the Irish midlands and on to the West, where the pub is in another sort of decline: The establishments themselves are going out of business, often being leveled to make room for real estate schemes. Also contributing to their disappearance are Ireland's severe drink-and-driving laws and the population's changed idea of a good time, a once-gregarious people preferring to stay at home before the dreary gamut of TV, video games, and the Internet. According to Barich - who seems to have been writing in 2007, right before the Celtic Tiger went belly up - pubs were disappearing at the rate of one a day, the same rate, in fact, that Irish-themed pubs were popping up across the globe.

John Reader's "Potato: A History of the Propitious Esculent" (Yale University, $28) is the second popular history of this greatest of all foods to appear in a little over a decade. It joins "The Potato: How the Humble Spud Rescued the Western World," Larry Zuckerman's wonderful and thorough account of the potato's life and times (North Point, paperback, $15). Frankly, another book on the subject is not really necessary; still, there is a book about potatoes in most of us. Reader brings the plant down to the present from the Andes, where it was first cultivated some 8,000 years ago - though the tubers had to be laboriously processed to remove the poisonous glycoalkaloids. Indeed, therein lies one of history's unsolved mysteries: How did anyone figure out how to eat the ur-potato without dying?

In any case, once the potato arrived in Europe - by this time selective cultivation having developed nontoxic varieties - it was not greeted with the respect it deserved. Though it was considered an aphrodisiac by some, it was also thought to cause leprosy, scrofula, rickets, consumption, and gout. Religious fundamentalists further denigrated it, claiming that it was not fit for human consumption because it was not mentioned in the Bible. The Thirty Years' War (1618-48), however, significantly advanced the potato's fortunes. The tubers could remain hidden below the earth's surface while armies swept across the continent, plundering grain stores and farm animals. Still, as Reader points out, it took 250 years after its introduction into Europe for the plant to take hold. Stopping at each step in its history, he explores the transforming role it played in the social, economic, and demographic makeup of the lands it entered. The case of Ireland is, of course, the most notorious, and he deals with it in great detail.

Reader himself spent 18 months in the west of Ireland in the first half of the 1960s. Existence was hard and austere for most people then, but he found the potato regnant again, as well as a good life of simple pleasures. Invited for Sunday dinner by neighbors, he was served the very best: mutton boiled "for most of the morning . . . set beside the potatoes on our plates, and the water in which it had boiled poured over, the mutton-fat congealing as the liquid spread to the edges of the cold plates." It was a "memorable meal indeed," he writes, followed by a glass of poteen. And there I leave you, with potatoes and drink.

Katherine A. Powers lives in Cambridge. Her column appears on alternate Sundays. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@verizon.net.
