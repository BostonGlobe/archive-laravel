BOOK REVIEW

An uneven account of life as a teenager in India

By Amy Kroin
Globe Correspondent / January 3, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

As a child, Thrity Umrigar had a crush on the von Trapp family. The singing Austrians, with their fresh-scrubbed faces and their perfect pitch, their melodic defiance of the Nazis, represented a completely different kind of family to Umrigar. Where the von Trapps engaged in merry pranks, her family sulked and fretted; where the von Trapps played, her family quarreled. In love with "The Sound of Music" after just one viewing, the 6-year-old Umrigar lured her family to a second showing. And for just one evening in a Bombay movie theater she could pretend that her household was as harmonious - musically and otherwise - as the one celebrated up on screen.

The "Sound of Music" outing is one of only a handful of happy moments chronicled in Thrity Umrigar's memoir, "First Darling of the Morning." It explores the author's tumultuous upbringing in India, where she grew up with a doting father and a mother so given to rage that Umrigar once contemplated puncturing her own eardrum with a compass, just to block out her mother's round-the-clock volley of insults and incriminations.

Umrigar's book is chock-full of vivid characters, from her beloved uncle - the loving man who coined her nickname "first darling of the morning" - to her freethinking next-door neighbor. Unfortunately, she never achieves a sense of balance when introducing these characters; her mother, such a force in the book's early pages, soon after drops almost entirely from view. Indeed, the book has no unifying thread to give it a sense of narrative momentum or drive. Instead, it shifts from one anecdote or experience to another, with Umrigar's unhappiness as the only connective tissue.

Every so often the author touches on what could have been a fertile subject for exploration: her sense of cultural dislocation. As a "Parsi teenager attending a Catholic school in the middle of a city that's predominantly Hindu," Umrigar felt like she was leading a "schizophrenic life." This is rich territory that begs more of Umrigar's attention.

When she does explore the inherent contradictions of her day-to-day life, "First Darling" comes to life in a way it does not elsewhere. In one memorable scene, Umrigar reveals how she confronted writer's block when her elementary-school teacher challenged her to write a different kind of story. "For once in your life," Miss D'Silva said, "do not make your characters blond and blue-eyed. And for heaven's sake give them real names, that is, Indian names, not names like Mr Jones and Mr Henderson." Fed a steady diet of Enid Blyton, Umrigar was an expert on small-town England but a neophyte when it came to crafting fictions set in her own country. Until Miss D'Silva issued her directive, Umrigar's stories featured characters who savored scones; she had no sense of how to render a world where the menu featured bhel puri.

The book's early chapters, with its scenes of family life, are its most absorbing. Once Umrigar turns to her adolescence, "First Darling" loses its footing and - save for a poignant section plumbing the death of her uncle - never regains it. Umrigar was a rebellious teenager who ditched schoolwork, fell hard for rock music, and hung out with the wrong crowd. This is the story of teenagers the world over, and Umrigar isn't able to make her own adolescence any more riveting than anyone else's. The lapses into heavy-handed prose that are present throughout the book become ever harder to overlook in scenes populated by angsty adolescents.

Amy Kroin's reviews have appeared in The New York Times and The Washington Post, among other publications.

Harper Perennial, 320 pp., paperback, $14.95
