A reading life

Stationary pursuits

By Katherine A. Powers
January 25, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

All around me, people, some of them actual friends of mine, are making plans to fly off to sunny climes for winter vacations. I can't imagine anything more horrible - air travel, unstructured days, soul-destroying hours waiting for companions to get ready to set off on pointless excursions. Add to that thoughts about pipes freezing back home and the neighbors forgetting to feed the chickens, and you are looking at a nightmare. I say stay put; but people won't. So I offer the palliative of books, easily portable paperbacks, to transport them from the unpleasantness they've brought upon themselves.

My number-one recommendation is the railway detective novels of Andrew Martin. I don't believe that you have to be a person for whom steam locomotives exert a visceral pull to fall as completely under the spell of these books as I have, but if you are indeed a "steam man," these books, set in Edwardian England, are sooty, cindery ambrosia. I wrote about the first three ("The Necropolis Railway," "The Blackpool Highflyer," and "The Lost Luggage Porter," Harvest, $14 each) a year ago, and now a fourth has just been published in this country: "Murder at Deviation Junction" (Mariner, $13.95). Like its predecessors, it follows the adventures of Jim Stringer, a railway detective whose plans to ascend to the most exalted station on earth, that of an engine driver, were foiled a few years ago. Forever? We shall just have to see.

In the meantime, Jim continues to come across dead bodies, as is his wont, this time in the midst of a blizzard, in a minor, single-line station (with passing loop). The plot, like those of the other three novels, is fairly miscellaneous, so I won't bother with it except to say that it creates nicely regulated tension and involves an implacable villain who wears thick yellow socks pulled over his tweed trousers - a Scotsman, in a word. It also propels Jim through vividly rendered tableaus of early-20th-century industrial Britain, the first granting us the vulcanian spectacle of "the roaring Ironopolis of Middlesborough," a steel-smelting works.

The book, set in December 1909, is a portal to Edwardian England and, like the others, possesses the stilly aura of a diorama: ingenious, precise, and a little eerie. Jim spends much time aboard trains, and the quotidian sights he observes from their windows always seem, as in fact they do in life, to have a melancholy, runic quality. ("I caught a glimpse of the beach as we rocked through the Redcar station. It was snow-covered. A torn white flag planted in the sand flew the word 'TEAS.' ") For its part, the action, though sometimes triggering white knuckles and heart-poundings in the reader, at other times provides the peculiar thrill I associate with model-train layouts, the exhilarating feeling of order and reason. I am referring to the sense of high drama and elegance attendant upon witnessing, as Jim does, one train passing between the legs of a towering viaduct just as another moves over the span. I mean all of this as high praise: These books, with their material detail, stirring mechanics, quiet humor, and melancholy kindliness, are simply the goods.

Quite a different world opens up in Jason Goodwin's series, currently two volumes strong with another to appear in March, starring Yashim Togalu, a former palace eunuch and sleuth in 19th-century Istanbul. Both novels, "The Janissary Tree" and "The Snake Stone," are now available in paperback (Picador, $14 each). Goodwin is a true master of his setting, for he is also the author of a fast-paced, brilliantly entertaining history of the Ottoman Empire, "Lords of the Horizons" (Picador, paperback, $17), itself written with novelistic dash. If, for some curious reason, you consider yourself above mysteries but still possess a wholesome taste for bloodshed and nefarious plot, you might consider packing this in your bag. I picked it up out of idle curiosity and lost half a day to its pages, which conclude - in what I now understand to be Goodwin's idiosyncratic way - with a brief history of the dogs of Istanbul: "your true curs, sly, lazy, lively, flea-bitten and battle-scarred. . . . Like soldiers on furlough they lived rakishly, snoozing in the sun by day, and howling by night.")

Yashim's first outing saw him getting to the heart of a deadly conspiracy to reinstate the elite and awful Janissaries, whose morbid power had been crushed 10 years earlier by the reformer and modernizer Sultan Mahmud II. "The Snake Stone" opens in the summer of 1838: the Sultan is dying; the city is plagued by debased coinage; plots are hatching; and the spatchcocked body of a French archeologist is discovered one night being eaten by the city's famous dogs. One of the last people to see the victim alive was our hero Yashim, an unfortunate fact that drives him to uncover the real culprit. The plot is Byzantine, as well it might be, and it brings us through layers of history, jostling cultures, religions, and nationalities, among them troublesome Greeks with a penchant for secret societies.

At the twisted heart of the story are snake heads taken from an edifice known as the Serpent Column, constructed two millennia ago and erected in Delphi to celebrate a Greek victory and brought to the city by Constantine some 500 years later to enhance his capital. The heads ("fanged, blank-eyed, seeking to terrorize and devour their prey") are now in the possession of Yashim's friend Stanislaw Palewski, the Polish ambassador (a vestigial position, as Poland has been gobbled up by her neighbors). Other characters at large include the mother of the Sultan, a doctor who attended Lord Byron at his death, and a beautiful, willful Frenchwoman.

The most flamboyant character and most vividly evoked, however, is the city itself as the Ottoman Empire enters its twilight: "Looking back across the Golden Horn to the familiar skyline of the city, marked by the great domes of Sinan's mosques; or passing the jumble of buildings - mosques, houses, caravanserai, churches, covered markets, shops - . . . it seemed incredible to Yashim that the city should function from one day to the next and not simply explode, or tear itself apart, or at the very least subside into a confusion of bleating sheep, rotting vegetables, and men gesticulating and thundering in twenty languages." This turmoil is magnificently rendered, as is, far below, a realm of cisterns and water conduits of tremendous antiquity and, as it happens, menace.

Katherine A. Powers lives in Cambridge. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@verizon.net.
