A Reading Life

Unleashing spirits, mystifying and murky

By Katherine A. Power
December 27, 2009

Three novels I’ve read this year have been haunted by ghosts. I don’t know if it’s a trend or whether I’ve just been lucky, or unlucky as the case might be. The first two ghosts were not, in my view, roaring successes, being more irksome than uncanny. One appeared this summer in Sophie Kinsella’s “Twenties Girl” as the youthful shade of a very old woman who has finally died after years of being out of it in a nursing home. The diaphanous young thing cannot rest until her valuable necklace has been restored, wherein lies a tale and a good deal of dressing up as a flapper. The other showed up this autumn in Audrey Niffennegger’s “Her Fearful Symmetry” as the protoplasmic remains of a much loved wife and estranged twin of a surviving sister. This ghost escapes from the ether only to find herself at the mercy of a muddled plot for more than 400 pages.

The third ghost - or whatever she is - is quite a different matter. She makes her first appearance on page four of Paul Torday’s “The Girl on the Landing” (Phoenix, paperback, $13.22), a book I had to put down a number of times to walk about the place to calm myself. Torday is the author of the mordant satire “Salmon Fishing in the Yemen” and, more recently, of the truly brilliant novel, “Bordeaux,” about which I wrote last February. That novel proceeds from the point of view of an alcoholic, self-deluded fabulist who inadvertently reveals quite a different story from the one he has convinced himself he’s telling. The question of what exactly the truth is becomes increasingly urgent for the reader; and when, at book’s end, the answer becomes clear, it is most satisfyingly terrible. A similar tension builds throughout the present novel: Unnatural apparitions do, after all, suggest that something baffling is afoot, and also - oh horror! - their way of going about things is unhindered by conventional morality or inhibitions.

Elizabeth and Michael have been married for 10 bland, emotionally muted years. She has a job she doesn’t really need at an upscale magazine under the thrall of a vixen boss. He has inherited money and “Beinn Caorrun,” an estate in Scotland where, years ago, his mother drowned and, some time later, his father was lost in a blizzard. Elizabeth and Michael live in London, making forays to the decaying Scottish establishment, a place she loathes and he loves. In town, Michael holds a little job as membership secretary at his gentleman’s club, Grouchers, a stuffy, xenophobic outfit, the very antithesis of the famously hip Groucho club. (“One never saw brown shoes being worn with a blue suit, or a loud tie.”) Torday’s portraits of the club’s members and of Elizabeth’s boss afford welcome, indeed necessary, comic respite from the novel’s increasingly awful suspense.

Because Michael is changing. Once a methodical guy who carefully folded his clothes before bed, whose conversation never strayed from the humdrum, who moved through life as through a fog, now, this same person has begun to take on an edge: physically, mentally, conversationally, and, most surprising and pleasant to Elizabeth, in bed - wrinkled clothes be damned. What does it mean? I am forced to reveal part of the answer, though believe me it is only a part: Michael has gone off his meds. Elizabeth gradually learns that his past includes some extreme unpleasantness and that he spent time in a mental institution. He has, until recently, been in a pharmacological straitjacket; but has, for various reasons, decided to return to his real self, a self that grants the couple a fortnight of previously unknown and heady joys. But Michael’s release from medication seems also to have unloosed a mysterious young woman into the world. Is she a hallucination, a ghost, a mortal being? This is for you to investigate.

More to the point: Who, really, is Michael? As Elizabeth observes - and one of Torday’s weirdly deadpan chapter titles confirms: “You Think You Know Someone, but You Never Really Do.” Exactly. Who - and what - is anyone? To what extent is disposition and character hardwired by genetics? What forces prompt us? And are they within or without? Torday, whose prose is a model of poise and grace, takes madness, genetics, evolution, and a smattering of social satire, then adds to all that an ingredient of the eldritch to create a strange and engrossing thriller. If this novel is not the fully realized tour de force that “Bordeaux” was, it is an exquisitely terrifying bijou.

Brian Moore’s “Temptation of Eileen Hughes” (published in 1981, now reprinted: Farrar, Straus, Giroux, paperback, $15) is free of ghosts, still, its substantial power depends on the idea of the diabolical and its snares. Put another way, this chilling little novel tells a modern story that resonates with the traditional Christian understanding that the devil walks the earth in various guises, often as a solicitous benefactor promising all manner of good things to those who will accept his terms. I should say right now, in case you find novelistic parables or allegories as wearisome as I do, that there is not a shred of either here. The main characters - Bernard McAuley and his wife, Mona; and young Eileen of the title, a shop assistant in one of the McAuley’s businesses - are all Irish Catholics and inhabit a Catholic universe the lineaments of which shape the story. Bernard, who was turned from a religious vocation by his father, is the richest Catholic in Lismore, near Belfast, and his beautiful wife married him pretty much for that reason. They have taken young Eileen up as a special project, bringing her on a junket to Dublin and now to London, a place which offers versions of heaven and hell to the young woman. The first is the imposing, park-surrounded beauty of Kenwood House, the other is the endless rows of cheap, dirty hotels for transients.

But why have the McAuleys taken such an interest in Eileen? She is modest with an innocent beauty, but she is not educated or witty or particularly good company. Well, as Torday’s Elizabeth would say and Eileen would agree, “You think you know someone, but . . . .” I can’t reveal more without destroying the plot, a bald statement of which would, in any case, obscure its subtlety and reverberation with the larger theme of the devil’s wiles. The novel is shocking in a quiet way, filled with ruthless intelligence and, I am happy to say, possessed of at least a modicum of mercy.

Katherine A. Powers lives in Cambridge. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@verizon.net.
