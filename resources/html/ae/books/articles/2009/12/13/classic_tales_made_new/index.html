Pop Lit

Classic tales made new

By Diane White
Globe Correspondent / December 13, 2009

Writers are practiced recyclers because some stories deserve to be told again and again. We have here, among other things, an updated version of Dickens’s “A Christmas Carol’’ set in Ireland and another novel that puts a chick-lit spin on the old Pygmalion story.

Irish novelist Cecelia Ahern’s “The Gift’’ is a Christmas parable that unfolds in Dublin sometime during the Irish economic boom of the 1990s and the first few years of this century. Lou Suffern is a workaholic executive who makes a point of multitasking even as he cheats on his wife, ignores his young children, and schemes to advance his career. Early on a cold Tuesday morning in December Lou pauses to give a cup of coffee to a panhandler outside his office building. It’s an uncharacteristic gesture for self-obsessed Lou, but he feels strangely drawn to the man, who introduces himself as Gabriel, (“Everyone who knows me calls me Gabe’’). Gabe has unusually acute powers of observation. He tells Lou several things he’s noticed about Lou’s colleagues’ shoes, details that help Lou deduce which of his fellow executives may be plotting to sabotage his own plan for self-advancement. Thinking he can use Gabe as a spy, he finds him a job in the company mailroom. Suddenly the spiffed up formerly homeless man is a model employee who, Lou fears, may turn out to be a threat. Gabe appears to have the ability to be in two places at the same time. He can also read Lou’s mind. Gabe has been sent to show Lou the importance of family and friendship before it’s too late. Ahern, who has five previous novels to her credit, is capable of providing a well-worn idea with some surprising twists.

Each Christmas Donna VanLiere writes a heart-tugging story meant to inspire warm holiday feelings. This year’s offering, her fifth in as many years, is “The Christmas Secret,’’ the story of Christine Eisley, the young single mother of 7-year-old Zach and 5-year-old Haley. Life is a struggle for Christine. Her ex-husband provides almost nothing in the way of child support and torments her by threatening to sue for custody. Christine works hard as a waitress, but she can’t pay her bills. Her job is in jeopardy because she’s so often late for work due to problems with sitters. One morning Christine saves the life of a woman who works in Wilson’s department store. Her selfless act makes her late for work, and she is fired. She quickly finds another job waiting tables at Betty’s Bakery. Meanwhile the owner of the department store, elderly Marshall Wilson, has been trying to locate the woman who saved the life of one of his key employees. He assigns his grandson Jason to find her. You don’t need to be a psychic to see where this story is heading, but VanLiere takes her time drawing out the plot with misunderstandings, mistaken identity, broken dates, and other complications. It’s a sweet story that celebrates love and generosity of spirit.

The creative art of quilting has enjoyed a renaissance in recent years. As its popularity has a grown, a sub-genre of women’s fiction has emerged, novels about women who come together to share their love of quilts and quilting, and their own stories. “A Quilter’s Holiday’’ is the 15th in Jennifer Chiaverini’s Elm Creek Quilts novels, a popular series that has a near-cult following. Elm Creek Manor is central to the story, a rural Pennsylvania mansion that owner and master quilter Sylvia Bergstrom Compson Cooper has transformed into a quilt camp. As the story opens it’s the day after Thanksgiving. Quilt camp has closed, but a small group of women, familiar characters to series readers, has gathered for the traditional Quilter’s Holiday, a marathon quilting session and potluck that marks the beginning of the winter quilting season and the countdown to Christmas. Some of the best books in the series are those in which the author delves into the fascinating history of quilting. However, with “A Quilter’s Holiday’’ Chiaverini seems almost to be marking time, filling in the histories of some characters and setting up several story lines for future novels. “A Quilter’s Holiday’’ is probably not the best introduction to the series, but it will satisfy her fans until her next book is released.

In “The Overnight Socialite,’’ Bridie Clark updates the Pygmalion story, setting it in contemporary New York. Lucy Jo Ellis, a trailer-park girl from Nowhere, Minn., came to the city with dreams of breaking into fashion designing. Fired from her low-level seamstress job, she encounters Wyatt Hayes while walking home in the rain to her dismal flat. He’s a handsome blueblood who once aspired to be a world-class anthropologist but since finishing his PhD at Harvard has done little more than study the social scene in Manhattan and Palm Beach. Wyatt has just broken up with his horrid girl- friend, Cornelia, a social lioness with her own press agent, and has bet his best friend that he could transform any girl into what passes for a lady in New York society. Enter Lucy, shivering under an awning. Clark is a sharp, amusing writer with a keen eye for detail. The ending is predictable, but Clark pulls some surprises.

Diane White writes every month about new light and popular fiction.
