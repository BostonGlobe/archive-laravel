Hoop dreams

Recounting a great rivalry and the NBA renaissance it fostered

Larry Bird battles for a rebound against “Magic’’ Johnson during a 1984 NBA Finals game in Los Angeles. (Andrew D. Bernstein/ NBAE/ Getty Images)
By Brion O’Connor
Globe Correspondent / December 13, 2009

To be a basketball fan in Boston or Los Angeles in the 1980s was pretty much hoops heaven. Out of the moribund, drug-induced haze that the National Basketball Association had become by the late 1970s came a dazzling, team-oriented game personified by the Celtics and the Lakers and their respective superstars, Larry Bird and Earvin “Magic’’ Johnson. The 13 years that these two clashed following their monumental NCAA championship game in 1979 remain for most hoop aficionados a kind of golden age.

“When the Game Was Ours,’’ by Bird, Johnson, and former Boston Globe writer Jackie MacMullan, is a time-capsule of this era. The two, as MacMullan illustrates, will always be seen as the architects of this basketball renaissance. They were Midwestern boys who made their names in college - Johnson at Michigan State and Bird at Indiana State. As professionals, they were separated by a continent, but their careers are forever joined in the minds of fans.

Most of the Bird-Magic story has already been written, but in MacMullan’s capable hands, the tale is re-energized - from their little-known introduction as college all-star teammates in the World Invitational Tournament in 1978, through their unparalleled rivalry over more than a decade on NBA courts, to their shared captaincy on the 1992 Olympic “Dream Team.’’

The dilemma for MacMullan - a superb journalist with impeccable credentials - is that she gets double-teamed. Bird and Johnson often only tell their sides of the story, casting themselves in the best light. MacMullan knows it, as any self-respecting sportswriter would.

For instance, Bird’s late-night carousing was no secret, but you won’t find anything about that here, with the exception of his comical post-championship hitchhiking adventures in Boston with teammate Quinn Buckner. There’s also the gut-wrenching tale of Bird’s eldest daughter, conceived during a failed reconciliation with his first wife. Corrie Bird was a toddler by the time that Bird was proven to be the father (his attorney asked for a paternity test). “Larry was dating Dinah by then and refused to be a part of his daughter’s life, a decision that haunts him to this day,’’ writes MacMullan. Really? Then why is Corrie mentioned exactly once over the course this book?

Meanwhile, much is made of Magic contracting HIV, which led to his premature retirement in late 1991. But there’s precious little detail about the lifestyle that led to his predicament. Johnson’s sexual escapades threatened to make the proclivities of another NBA legend and former Laker, Wilt Chamberlain, look like amateur hour (and the Big Dipper claimed to have bedded thousands of women). But Johnson is much more concerned about erasing any lingering doubts that he may have had homosexual relationships, and he repeatedly chastises Detroit Piston Bad Boy Isiah Thomas for sharing those doubts. Magic’s peccadilloes, while engaged to his wife, Cookie, are summed up in a mere few sentences, and actually paint him as a victim (How could he help himself, with all those women throwing themselves at him?). In retrospect, he was guilty of the same selfishness that he and Bird would often criticize in teammates - especially those younger players who didn’t share their uncompromising work ethic.

In essence, MacMullan has delivered a gift-wrapped ode to the two superstars. The bow is just a bit too perfect; the creases starched and aligned a tad too flawlessly. Even the title - “When the Game Was Ours’’ - seems self-serving for two players who always insisted on the importance of team chemistry. That said, this present isn’t one you’ll necessarily want to throw away.

Despite its one-sided limits, the collaboration represents a wonderful waltz down memory lane. The examples are abundant: Bird throwing down the gauntlet at the first all-star 3-point contest, his famous “sissies’’ tirade after his team’s Game 3 loss in the 1984 NBA Finals against the Lakers, and his refusal to let former Celtics boss Dave Gavitt trick him into accepting another two years of Celtics money, at $5 million per year, after his decision to retire in 1992. “I know what you’re doing,’’ Bird said. “I don’t want the money. I didn’t earn it, and I won’t take it.’’

There is the well-documented meeting between the two stars at Bird’s home in Indiana to film a Converse sneaker commercial, which ultimately nurtured a newfound respect and genuine friendship. Regarding Magic, MacMullan flexes her journalistic muscle, uncovering terrific details on the Lakers point guard. Consider this great quote from Celtics patriarch Arnold “Red’’ Auerbach: “I was sure we’d be seeing the Lakers in the Finals that spring [of 1983]. I don’t mind telling you that Magic worried the hell out of me. He was so big and strong.’’

To their credit, the three authors do acknowledge the legions who helped Bird and Magic change the game, from teammates like Kareem Abdul Jabbar, James Worthy, Michael Cooper, Byron Scott, Cedric Maxwell, Robert Parish, Kevin McHale, Dennis Johnson, opponents such as Julius Irving, Michael Jordan, Patrick Ewing and Charles Barkley, coaches like K.C. Jones, Bill Fitch, and Pat Riley, owners like Jerry Buss, and even NBA Commissioner David Stern.

Taken as a whole, “When the Game Was Ours’’ is a compelling and enjoyable read, every bit as entertaining as watching Magic and Bird battling on the parquet.

Brion O’Connor, a freelance writer based on Massachusetts’ North Shore, can be reached at brion@verizon.net.
