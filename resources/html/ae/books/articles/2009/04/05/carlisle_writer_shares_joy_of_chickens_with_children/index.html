Globe West People

Carlisle writer shares joy of chickens with children

Arlington lawyer Judson Pierce and Trial Run will perform at a benefit dance Saturday in Lexington.
By Cindy Cantrell
April 5, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

CHICKEN SCRATCHINGS: Terry Golson of Carlisle got her first chicken - a neighbor's leftover 4-H Club project - in 1996. Wanting company for the chicken, she got two more. The brood has now grown to 13, many of which are featured in Golson's first children's book, "Tillie Lays an Egg."

A food writer and cookbook author, Golson worked with photographer Ben Fink of New York City so the chickens that inspired her book could star in it as well. She used corn to train them to pose for the camera and spent a year collecting chicken-themed props that also appear in the book.

Golson will read from her book today at 3 p.m. at Book Ends, 559 Main St. in Winchester, where audience members will be invited to meet the real-life Tillie, a small, white hen.

" 'Tillie Lays an Egg' is a work of fiction, but the true nature of chickens comes through," said Golson, who noted that they are "busy, curious, and funny animals" with distinct personalities. "I hope that people will make the connection between the eggs that they have in their refrigerator and these delightful creatures."

Golson will also conduct book readings at the Groton Public Library at 10:30 a.m. on April 23 and the Sherborn Library at 3 p.m. on July 1. For more information on her schedule, go to tillielaysanegg.com. To watch Golson's chickens, go to hencam.com.

IMPROV AUDITIONS: Steve Marotta of Brookline admits being the "quiet smart-aleck" type in school. Looking back, he said his sarcastic one-liners "may not have been the height of comedy, but it's what I did."

While attending Drew University in Madison, N.J., Marotta met students with similar comedic ambitions and founded an improvisational comedy troupe. There, he honed the keys to his craft: listening; committing to scene suggestions from fellow troupe members; making strong scene offers in return; helping his partners in comedy look good in all circumstances.

After taking a few years off from performing comedy, Marotta joined an improv workshop program in Cambridge to refresh his skills. In January 2007, he successfully auditioned for Improv Jones Boston, a troupe that performs Friday nights at the Arsenal Center for the Arts in Watertown.

"It's a lot of fun," Marotta said. "This is a great group of people who are so sharp and witty that I have to be on my toes the entire time. I feel honored to share the stage with them."

Improv Jones Boston will hold auditions for new members Wednesday at 6:30 p.m. at the Arsenal Center, 321 Arsenal St. Participants should bring a headshot and résumé. For more information, e-mail auditions@improvjonesboston.com.

TRIAL RUN FOR DANCING: Judson Pierce of Arlington is typically on the opposing side when he meets fellow lawyer Leonard Nason of Lexington in the courtroom. Next weekend, however, they will be joining forces to perform music during a fund-raiser to benefit groups working on environmental causes.

Pierce has been playing the saxophone since elementary school, but took an extended leave from the instrument after law school. Nason, a guitarist and vocalist, invited him to join his band, Trial Run, three years ago.

"I love the performing arts, and it's nice to have opportunities like this to help out," said Pierce, who also balances fatherhood with acting in local theater productions. "All communities are dealing with the concept of investing in the future through education, infrastructure, and not abusing the earth more than we already have."

Trial Run will perform at the second annual "Go Green Dance Party" Saturday, from 7 to 11 p.m. at the Knights of Columbus Heritage Hall, 177 Bedford St. in Lexington. Tickets are $15, $25 for two, $10 for ages 18 and under, and can be obtained in advance by e-mailing trialrunrocks@gmail.com and will also be sold at the door.

Proceeds will benefit the Lexington Global Warming Action Coalition, activist Bill McKibben's 350.org campaign, and the Global Warming Education Network.

ADOPT A GREYHOUND: Lisa Fast of Newton long admired a friend's greyhound before adopting her own in 1991. Despite the former racing animal's initially fearful personality, Fast remembers her late pet as the "most wonderful, easiest dog I've ever had to teach." Realizing that support was needed to promote and raise funds for the dogs' adoption and welfare, she helped establish the Greyhound Project Inc. in Framingham the following year.

Fast, who is a board member, said the organization helps hundreds of greyhound adoption groups raise money through an annual calendar featuring adopted dogs. Approximately 12,000 calendars are sold each year by the greyhound charities nationwide, according to Fast, generating up to $80,000 in funds.

The organization also produces a quarterly magazine, Celebrating Greyhounds, which features tips and stories about adopting, training, and caring for the breed.

In recognition of April as National Adopt-a-Greyhound Month, Fast is urging people who plan to adopt a pet to consider a greyhound, particularly as they become more available with racetracks closing across the country.

"I can go on and on and on about these great dogs," said Fast, who has two greyhounds with her husband, Bob. "Each dog has its own personality, but one thing they have in common is they make excellent pets."

For more information, go to www.adopt-a-greyhound.org.

READING TOGETHER: Boston Celtics players and a co-owner brought the team's Read to Achieve program to the Perkins School for the Blind in Watertown last week.

Visiting the school were center Kendrick Perkins, guard Eddie House, forward Bill Walker, and co-owner Wyc Grousbeck, as well as the Weston resident's wife, Corinne Grousbeck, who is chairwoman of the Perkins Trust and a member of the Celtics Women's Group.

The visitors used special books that feature traditional printed pages and Braille pages to read with students.

"It is a lot of fun not just reading to the children, but having them read to us as well," Perkins said in an announcement on the team's visit. "It is inspirational that these students are able to overcome their disability and have a love of reading."

Three Perkins students joined in to read aloud from "Arthur Meets the President." Slater Schuler, a Braintree 10-year-old who is deaf and blind, started it off using an adaptive device that translates the Braille text she reads into audio output. Michael Cote, a 12-year-old from Agawam, was next reading to the crowd assembled in the school gym, followed by Omar Jomaa, 13, of Watertown.

The players also discussed some of their favorite books and the importance of reading in their lives.

"Of course it's exciting for the students to meet the Celtics players," said Rob Hair, a Perkins School administrator. "For these students, though, it means so much to know that everybody - even NBA champions - cares about reading and learning.

"To be honest, I think they kind of like showing off, too!"
