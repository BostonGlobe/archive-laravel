BOOK REVIEW

A daughter's coming to terms

Allegra Huston has written a memoir about her unhappy life growing up as the daughter of film director John Huston. (Courtesy of Allegra Huston)
By Chuck Leddy
Globe Correspondent / May 9, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Reprints|
  • |
Text size – +

Allegra Huston's mother, the fourth wife of legendary film director John Huston, was killed in a car crash when the author was 4. In "Love Child," Huston's coming-of-age memoir, the author describes the famous director as a strong personality: "Dad was a difficult person to be married to. . . . He was unfaithful, egocentric, impatient, judgmental, cruelly cutting, and a gambler."

Being John Huston's wife, the author writes, "gave [my mother] prestige in the world, but it was barren." Although the author refers to Huston throughout as "Dad," we later learn that her mother had extramarital affairs, one of which resulted in the author's birth. Her biological father, she discovers, is an English lord.

Huston's memoir is largely a series of unhappy events, as she longs for her lost mother and can't find a comfortable place in the world. "I sensed that plans were made up for me on the fly. I was an inconvenience - not unloved, but a problem that required constant solving." The author is accompanied by a loyal Irish nanny.

Once she had to stay for a time in Mexico, where her director dad was working, and she grew to hate the place. "I got sick, I got sunburned," she writes. She's horrified on her ninth birthday when she's compelled to hit a piñata with a stick: "I swung again and again, since it was expected of me. All I wanted was for this unfestive, barbaric, inexplicable birthday party to be over."

When the author moves from John Huston's huge Irish estate to her grandfather's house on Long Island, she feels she's coming down in the world. "I didn't like the house much . . . it was flimsy after the thick stone of St. Cleran's." Seeing her new bedroom, she notices "[t]he white candlewick bedspreads were thin . . . they pulled my spirits down." She likes neither her grandfather nor her grandmother. "Grampa saw himself as an unusually wise and spiritually evolved being . . . [someone] above the demands of common kindness." At least John Huston had decent bedspreads.

Later, the author moves to Los Angeles, where she hangs out with her older, famous sister, Anjelica. The author writes about actress Anjelica's famous boyfriends, including Jack Nicholson and Ryan O'Neal. Attending school in Los Angeles, the author tells her classmates about her famous family and their celebrity friends: "My movie star connections made me interesting," she writes.

The author is at her egocentric best after her loyal Irish nanny gets axed by the Huston family. "She was being thrown out into a hostile world," writes Huston. "With me, she'd been protected under the shelter of Daddy's influence. I would be fine without her, but what would she do without me?" After meeting her biological father, an English aristocrat, she discovers that she prefers England to Hollywood. "Southern California," she explains, "had become a glittering, sunlit hellhole of blandness and hypocrisy and worship of fame." She packs her bags.

More bad experiences and horrible places follow. John Huston dies, and the memoir mercifully ends with the heretofore-troubled author accepting her family, her fathers, her mother, and herself: "I was lucky in my two fathers, but I was luckiest of all to be her daughter." Well, at least readers get a tacked-on happy ending for all their trouble.

Chuck Leddy is a freelance writer who lives in Dorchester.

and Found

By Allegra Huston

Simon & Schuster, 289 pp., illustrated, $26.95
