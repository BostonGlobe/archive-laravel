A swing and a miss, and a home run

Dodgers pitcher Sandy Koufax has the bases loaded, but he is no longer nervous as he readies his pitch. (From The Book)
By Liz Rosenberg
May 17, 2009
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Reprints|
  • |
Text size – +

Julianna Baggott is a heartbreaking writer, in part because she so nearly writes great books. Under the pen name N.E. Bode, she has published "The Anybodies," "The Somebodies," and "The Nobodies," popular books that similarly seem to miss perfection, not by a mile, but by a matter of feet.

The opening to "The Prince of Fenway Park" positively shines with genius. Certainly, baseball-loving Bostonians in particular, will be enchanted by the premise. Oscar, a boy of mixed-race, is an outcast wherever he goes. He doesn't belong in school, where he is often taunted, doesn't quite belong even with his loving mother, who is hunting a new husband and a better life for herself and her son. He especially doesn't belong with his hangdog father, a mysterious character who never seems to want Oscar around. But there's something magical about Oscar's dad, as it turns out, and about Oscar himself - if only he can decode his own mystery in time to break the famous Red Sox curse.

The book begins with the same kind of straightforward bravado one found in the opening of the first Harry Potter: "The boy who would break the Curse didn't know that he was the boy who would break the Curse. He was just himself, Oscar, who, at this particular moment on this particular day, was watching his mother, who was standing beside her El Camino, caught in the dark exhaust fog at the end of the line of buses."

Camino, of course, is Spanish for road, or way, so Baggott places us in a Dante-esque scenario, with a boy who has lost his way, and finds himself lost in a contemporary equivalent of the dark woods. Like his famous literary magical counterpart, Oscar is about to turn 12 the next day - that portal not only to magic but to adulthood. He, too, is abandoned, in a manner of speaking, and a certain (baseball) fate hangs in the balance, depending on his skill, bravery, and heart.

The opening chapters contain a dozen felicitous, light-handed moments. Driving with his mother, Oscar sees a painted-over highway "REVERSE CURVE" sign that now reads "REVERSE CURSE." The hope of bleak Red Sox fans that summer is "fragile, delicate - a glass-blown version of hope." Baggott's plot is inventive, her characters compelling, the prose beautiful and flexible.

The book makes several wrong turns, however, until even the author herself seems tangled in its plot lines. By midway through, the energy visibly sags, and the inventiveness begins to feel frantic, as the author pulls out one trick after another in a desperate effort to keep the momentum flowing. Real literary magic stems simply from being true to the story - nothing less, nothing more. Yes, the Red Sox labored under what seemed a curse for more than 80 years. Yes, our young hero also describes himself as "cursed." But the parallels are too neat, and they pile up, in the end, like bricks.

Baggott had a lovely story to tell - a young man discovering his origins, finding the strength to break the curse of all outcasts. The legendary racism of the Boston Red Sox rightly plays an important part in the novel, but Baggott leans even on this material until it buckles. Had she simply waited until the novel was ready, I think it would have been an organic and perfect work of art - and more's the pity, because really, it came so close to a home run.

By contrast, "You Never Heard of Sandy Koufax?!" by Jonah Winter and Andre Carrilho, strikes true baseball gold. Written in vibrant, slangy Brooklynese, Winter grabs our attention right from the opening salvo: "You gotta be kidding! You never heard of Sandy Koufax?! He was only the greatest lefty who ever pitched in the game of baseball." Koufax grew up "Jewish in Brooklyn," where "no one woulda guessed what he was about to become. He was supposed to be a doctor or a lawyer." Instead he was offered a contract with the Brooklyn Dodgers, soon to become the Los Angeles Dodgers.

In 1958, Koufax was hardly a pitching star. In fact he nearly achieved the dubious distinction of walking "the most batters in the National League." He quit the game briefly before returning more determined than ever to become "a strikeout machine."

This is a book written by ear. " 'Hittin' a Koufax fastball,' Willie [Stargell] said, 'was like tryin' to drink coffee with a fork.' " The artwork is exaggerated, caricaturish, in bold red, white, and navy blue with brushes of glimmering gold, adding a strong, dynamic visual aspect to the book's strong voice. Even the cover's image seems to move, with a clever hologram flashing the three stages of a Koufax pitch.

Winter tells the famous story of Koufax's refusal to play on the Jewish High Holy Days. And there's good advice mixed in with all of this cultural history. "We could tell he was throwin' too hard. And when you overthrow, you lose control. And when you lose control, you walk guys." There's even a touch of poetry mingled with the baseball: "Who was Sandy Koufax? Sandy Koufax was a guy who finally relaxed enough to let his body do the one thing it was put on this earth to do. And what a thing of beauty that was."

Liz Rosenberg is the author of the new novel "Home Repair." She has written more than two dozen books for young readers and reviews books monthly here.
