Book Review

A doctor grapples with children's issues on two fronts

By Karen Campbell
September 13, 2008
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

The Mercy Rule
By Perri Klass
Houghton Mifflin, 276 pp., $24

Boston-trained pediatrician Perri Klass has forged a literary career writing about what she knows best - the humanitarian aspects of medicine. Most tellingly, she writes about the relationship between medicine and parenting, and her latest novel, "The Mercy Rule," is right on target, built around a protagonist who struggles daily with the balance of motherhood and her career as a doctor.

But "The Mercy Rule" takes on a bigger issue as well, the important but complicated and deeply flawed quagmire of the foster-care system. Dr. Lucy Weiss spends her days dealing with children at risk and all manner of neglect. She regularly counsels Athena Harris, a Southern transplant who has just given birth to her 10th child, even though social services routinely takes them away. She sympathizes with Delia Devereaux, the beautiful but clueless unmarried young mother of three who is chronically unemployed and favors irresponsible men. Amid all the cases of poverty and ignorance emerges the strange case of Tanya Danvers, the parent of one of her daughter's classmates, whose au pair has reported her for child abuse.

Throughout, Lucy's judgment is colored by the filter of her own unique childhood, spent in the foster-care system after her widower father, whose job kept him on the road, couldn't manage the task of taking proper care of her. Her own situation had a happy ending, when a beloved teacher ultimately adopted her. But as an adult, Lucy grapples every day with the children whose potential to be special no one recognizes. And it is her painful duty as a pediatrician, a "mandated reporter," to make the judgment that could separate children in dire risk from their parents, no matter how loving and well-intentioned the parents might be. "I am left wondering whether I am identifying with Delia or with her children, when really I represent some completely different authority. I am power, I am separation, I am the world and its standards - but I don't think I can save anyone right now."

Then there are Lucy's challenges parenting her own two children with a husband who, though loving, is not always fully engaged. Extremely bright but peculiar 6-year-old Freddy presents daily challenges, with benign but compulsive behavior that seems to border on autism, a condition Lucy never fully addresses. Eleven-year-old Isabel is at the "take-care-of-me-leave-me alone" stage, desperate for attention but most often shunning her mother's attempts to connect with a stubborn independence and diffidence.

"The Mercy Rule" is carried by individual moments of breathtaking clarity and insight. Klass gives Lucy a wonderful diatribe against a mother who permits her son to have a birthday party and invite all the boys in Freddy's class but him. The unadulterated vitriol of Lucy's rant, real and imagined, is exhilarating for any parent who feels for a misunderstood or disenfranchised child. The description of an overbearing father at a Little League game, whence the novel's title is derived, is spot on. And the character of Isabel is vividly revealing. Klass imbues her with the precocious voice of knowing self-absorption that rings achingly true. Isabel's camp experience offers tiny gems of watershed moments, as does her furtive experience reading private musings on her mother's computer.

But structurally, "The Mercy Rule" is all over the place. There is no plot to speak of, no real dramatic arc or momentum, and it can feel disappointingly slow and disjointed. Just as heartbreaking tales of troubled souls like Delia and Athena - even Lucy's own children - begin to develop, they fizzle out.

In addition, the here and now is frequently disrupted by the echoes of yesterday, especially Lucy's fractured past. Instead of cogently informing the present, these tangential asides tend to break any kind of dramatic flow. It makes for long frustrating sections, waiting for action, resolution, wanting something transformative to happen. But perhaps that feeds into Klass's general view - life is messy; clean it up as best you can, and keep moving forward.

Karen Campbell is a freelance writer based in Brookline.
