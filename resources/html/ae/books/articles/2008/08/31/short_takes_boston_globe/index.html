Short takes

By Barbara Fisher
August 31, 2008
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

Dry Storeroom No. 1: The Secret Life
of the Natural History Museum
By Richard Fortey
Knopf, 335 pp., illustrated, $27.50

I have always been entertained by tales of British eccentricity, and Fortey's account of the men behind the scenes at London's Natural History Museum is chock-full of oddballs, charmers, snobs, jokers, and downright lunatics. It is also full of brave, inspired, and unknown heroes.

Fortey, the trilobite man at the museum, has served his entire professional life toiling behind the public galleries in the back rooms of the museum along with other men who have devoted themselves to collecting, naming, cataloging, and classifying one tiny piece of the natural world - beetles, mushrooms, whales, and countless other wonders. Not all were specialists; Edward Heron-Allen, a late-19th-century collector and polymath, was a skilled violinmaker and Persian linguist who wrote novels, poems, and a natural history of barnacles. Another museum man was a botanist who cataloged his sexual conquests on index cards. Odd behavior does not interfere with seriousness of purpose, and Fortey reminds us that "the least microbe or the greatest mammal deserves our attention. The geological history of our planet is as intimately entwined with the history of the life on its surface as the symbiosis between tree and truffle."

This Must Be the Place
By Anna Winger
Riverhead, 303 pp., $24.95

The past isn't dead in Berlin. Although the devastation from World War II bombing has been largely repaired and the wall has come down, the city's scars are still palpable. Walter, born to a German father and an American mother in Bavaria, is a has-been soap star who currently earns a good living as the German voice of Tom Cruise. Hope, a Midwestern American, has come to Berlin from New York recovering from a miscarriage and recoiling from 9/11. Walter and Hope are lonely and lost. After meeting by chance in the elevator of the once-elegant building where they live, they tentatively develop a friendship.

Like the city, the building they live in has a past. Peeled wallpaper and paint expose Puss in Boots and other storybook characters in a succession of what must have been children's nurseries. Writing scratched on a wall reveals a hidden Hebrew school conducted in the basement. People as well as places require and reward digging. The novel moves gracefully and builds surprisingly, carrying more meaning by the end than was promised by the beginning.

Tethered
By Amy MacKinnon
Shaye Areheart, 263 pp., $24

At the center of this creepy novel are orphaned, abused, and murdered children. Although portrayed sympathetically, they are still being exploited here.

Clara, the narrator of this grim tale, bears the scars, internal and external, of these horrors. Orphaned, then raised by an unforgiving grandmother and used by a group of callous teenage boys, Clara escapes to a job in a funeral home that provides her with a private space. When little girls in her Boston suburb go missing, are found dead, or seen almost dead in sex videos, Clara must take notice, care, and help. These things do not come easily to her, since she has avoided most of life's emotional entanglements. Forced to help others, she, of course, helps herself. The plot is fairly obvious, as is its life-affirming conclusion. There is some suspense and prurient excitement in discovering the details and the perpetrators of the horrors.

But it is Clara's perfectly rendered cold, cautious, frightened voice that lifts the novel above mere entertainment. Only a danger that closely resembles Clara's own excites her deadened compassion. Her refusal to talk, act, or participate is convincingly motivated, and the consequences to her and others are neatly plotted out. Clara is an elegant illustration of a nature that, letting nothing in, gives nothing out.

Barbara Fisher is a freelance critic who lives in New York.
