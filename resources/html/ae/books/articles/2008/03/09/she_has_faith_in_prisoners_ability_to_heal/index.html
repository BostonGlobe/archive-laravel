She has faith in prisoners' ability to heal

Author's inmate program teaches forgiveness as a path to recovery

Robin Casarjian says she comes by her work honestly. "I came from a family that was always for the underdog." (Matthew J. Lee/Globe Staff)
Email|Print| Text size – + By Susan Chaityn Lebovits
March 9, 2008

When Robin Casarjian walks into a p rison, she looks beyond the bars and sees potential for growth and healing. For more than two decades, Casarjian has worked to inspire inmates, giving them tools to build productive lives.

In 1992, she founded the National Emotional Literacy Project for Prisoners so they would have access to rehabilitative resources such as relaxation techniques, spiritual awakening, and self-forgiveness, to restore dignity.

The results have been impressive.

Since her book, "Houses of Healing: A Prisoner's Guide to Inner Power and Freedom," was first published in 1995, profound letters from inmates across the nation have poured into her Lionheart Foundation headquarters, now based in Needham.

"If a prisoner wants to heal, they must be completely honest with themselves about what they've done, the impact of the harm, and repair the damage when possible," Casarjian said. "That takes a lot of courage, as does facing other aspects of a painful past."

Richard N. Wolman, a clinical psychologist, researcher, and Harvard Medical School faculty member, did an evaluation of the effectiveness of the Houses of Healing Program in 1999 that showed that the program helped prisoners decrease hostility, depression, self-deprecation, and alexithymia, an inability to understand and manage emotions.

Since 1995, more than 50,000 copies of Casarjian's book have been distributed to most state and federal prisons in the United States, "initiating a wave of change in prisoners and prison counselors' approaches to rehabilitation," Casarjian said.

Dot Walsh, program director at The Peace Abbey in Sherborn, said she has been using Casparjian's books for her own teachings, which she said looks at the roots of violence and shows how to come up with positive solutions. "I often watched Robin teach and admired the way she combined sensitivity while encouraging the men and women to do their best," Walsh said. "Even though she was kind and compassionate, she never wavered about what you needed to do to change your life."

For many people, entering a high-security prison would rattle some nerves, but not Casarjian.

"I've never felt threatened in the prisons," she said. "I think that when people feel respected, they're not going to lose control and hurt you."

Her work in the prison system began in 1988. After Casarjian gave a public lecture on forgiveness, a woman who had attended asked her if she would speak to the men in her addiction recovery class at the state prison in Concord. Casarjian agreed and was then asked to teach another class at MCI-Gardner.

"I arrived at 9 a.m. expecting two or three people and there were 120 men waiting to come in," she said. "Afterward, the [prison] psychologist asked me to teach a full course. By the second week, I felt that this was a calling for me."

Reaching out to help others was nothing new for Casarjian, who grew up watching her father, a shoe-stitcher in a factory, jeopardize his job in order to be a union organizer. Her mother worked a number of jobs to help put food on the table, including cutting hair and doing clerical work.

"I came from a family that was always for the underdog - a kind of working-class, leftist family; my heart was always open for people in need."

Casarjian attended Eastern Michigan University, where she majored in special education for the mentally retarded, then went on to graduate school at Columbia University, where she studied education for the emotionally disturbed and worked with children who had behavioral problems.

One of her first teaching jobs was in 1973 at the Walker Home in Needham and the WessRoss Mini school - a joint venture between the state departments of Mental Health and Education, which was trying to keep people in the community instead of sending them off to residential treatment centers.

Casarjian said she will always remember one boy in particular who was quite small in stature but large in attitude.

"He was very defiant with a tough-guy veneer," she said. What moved her, she said, was a letter that she received at the end of the year that stated: "I know that you love me and the kids. And me and the kids love you."

As Casarjian continued teaching, she took a five-day course in guided imagery, which uses mental visualization to improve well-being. She was so impressed with the class that she enrolled in a two-year certification program and, with her new skills, expanded her practice to include people with life-threatening illnesses. She gave workshops in guided imagery and healing, and stress management. Some of her clients included organizations like the Harvard Community Health Plan and DuPont.

One of the topics that Casarjian said was extremely important was learning how to work with anger and guilt in an effective way.

"To deal with this fully, forgiveness had to be part of the process." She began researching the topic of forgiveness and, after exhausting all resources, began creating ways for her clients to articulate forgiveness. The results became part of her first book, "Forgiveness: A Bold Choice for a Peaceful Heart," and also of "Houses of Healing."

Casarjian said that she hopes her book will serve to dispel some of the prejudice and stereotypes about incarcerated men and women.

"Some of the most thoughtful, mature, compassionate people I've ever met are people in prison doing life and long-term sentences," she said. "They committed their crimes many years ago and have used their time to grapple with their actions, the impact of their actions, their feelings, and their profound and appropriate guilt and remorse." Out of a difficult past, she said, they have re-created themselves as humans of great depth and compassion.

Casarjian said she, too, has had to learn to forgive; one situation was a relationship that ended because of infidelity.

But the most difficult was being raped when she was a college student.

"I lived in a lot of fear in the beginning," she said.

"But I think it's very important for people to know that if I'm [teaching] about forgiveness, it's something that I'm capable of doing."

For more on the Lionheart Foundation, visit Lionheart.org.
