Pop Lit

Forced through celebrity's many circles of hell

Email|Print| Text size – + By Diane White
March 9, 2008

Three Girls and Their Brother
By Theresa Rebeck
Shaye Areheart, 352 pp., $23.95

Remember Me?
By Sophie Kinsella
Dial, 400 pp., $25

Seen It All and
Done the Rest
By Pearl Cleage
Ballantine, 320 pp., $25

These three novels are light and fun to read, underrated qualities in publishing. The first is an effective satire, the second an antic comedy, the third a story of homecoming and healing told in a witty, sophisticated voice.

In her first novel, playwright Theresa Rebeck satirizes the publicity-driven world of instant celebrity. "Three Girls and Their Brother" is pointed and funny, an entertaining, cautionary story about the powerful and the preyed upon. The trouble begins when The New Yorker runs a glamorous photograph of three beautiful red-haired teenagers, granddaughters of the renowned late literary critic Leo Heller. Daria, 18, an aspiring model, is thrilled. Polly, 17, is caught up in the excitement. Amelia, 14, resists. She just wants to do her homework and practice the piano. But she's pushed into public life by the girls' ambitious, feckless mother, a former Miss Tennessee, and by Colette, a voracious agent who takes over their lives. The novel is narrated in segments, from the separate points of view of each sister and their overlooked brother, Philip, 15. Rebeck creates convincing, different voices for all the siblings.

The three "It" girls from Brooklyn are flung into the whirlwind of New York's fashion world. After Amelia bites a 47-year-old movie star who gropes her in a restaurant, the ensuing publicity gets out of hand, with paparazzi laying siege to the girls' school and gossip columnists haunting their home. Their modeling careers take off; their misbehavior escalates. Amelia is asked to appear in an off-Broadway play, which gives Rebeck a chance to poke wicked fun at playwrights, actors, and the theater world in general, a milieu she obviously knows well. "Three Girls and Their Brother" may be satire, but many of the details are, unfortunately, credible.

I'm not a fan of Sophie Kinsella's bestselling "Shopaholic" novels, which undoubtedly says more about me and my neuroses (fear of shopping is one) than it does about Kinsella's writing. But she can be very funny. I enjoyed her stand-alone novel "The Undomestic Goddess," and I like her new one, "Remember Me?" Lexi Smart wakes up in a hospital bed thinking that it's 2004, that she's missing her father's funeral because she was hit by a taxicab the night before. In fact it's 2007, she's just had an auto accident, and she has no memory at all of the previous three years. During that time she has somehow changed from a frumpy, frizzy-haired, snaggle-toothed junior sales associate into a sleek, toned, manicured executive, the head of her old department at Deller Carpets. She has perfect teeth, a fabulous wardrobe, a Mercedes. She has a husband she can't recall, Eric, a handsome if somewhat stiff fellow, who informs her that they live in a loft-style apartment. "He proclaims the words as though they're capitalized," Lexi notes. And she has a lover, Jon, an attractive stranger who tells her she was about to leave Eric for him when she had the car accident.

At work Lexi is surprised to learn that she's a dynamo, a whiz kid. "She's a natural leader," the head of the company says, extolling her talents. "She's inspirational. She puts in 24 hours a day." But when her old work buddies are cool to her, Lexi learns that she also has a reputation as a "snotty cow," as one of her former friends puts it. A total boss from hell, adds another. Lexi starts to think that her new life isn't as perfect as it seems. "Remember Me?" is light reading, with some very funny passages. Amnesia is a much-abused storytelling device, but Kinsella gives it an amusing twist.

"I've been thrown out of places for being too black, too queer, too loud, too drunk, too hip, and too too, but I have never, ever been tossed out . . . for being too American!" rants Howard Denmond on the first page of Pearl Cleage's "Seen It All and Done the Rest." Howard and his best friend, Josephine Evans, the central character and narrator, are consoling themselves with champagne in an Amsterdam café after having been asked to leave the funeral of an Iraqi friend, where mourning relatives from Baghdad had been outraged at their presence. Then Josephine, 58-year-old expatriate actress, African-American star of a local repertory theater, learns that her job is another peripheral casualty of the war: The theater's directors don't want an American actress to open the season.

Josephine decides it's time to return to Atlanta to check on a house she owns and to spend time with her granddaughter Zora, who has dropped out of college in the wake of her tangential role in a highly publicized murder case. Josephine moves in with Zora, who is house-sitting a mansion in Atlanta's West End, a neighborhood Cleage has written about in previous novels. Zora is drinking too much, behaving self-destructively. Josephine finds that her house, her mother's legacy, is a neglected ruin, vandalized by drug dealers, inhabited by a squatter, a recovering addict who has been doing what he can to protect the place. She turns down an insultingly low offer for the house from an unscrupulous developer and determines to rehabilitate the property with the help of new and old friends. The uplifting end of the story is never in doubt, but Cleage provides enough plot turns and setbacks to make things interesting. Best of all, Josephine's voice - independent, smart, wise, and funny - never flags. She's a winning character.

Diane White writes every month about new light and popular fiction.
