An erratic path through the storm

Diplomat's life marked by ideals, pragmatism

Before Iraq, Vieira de Mello served in Cambodia, Bosnia, East Timor, and Kosovo. (Walter chin)
Email|Print| Text size – + By Stéphanie Giry
March 9, 2008

Chasing the Flame: Sergio Vieira de Mello and the Fight to Save the World
By Samantha Power
Penguin, 622 pp., illustrated, $32.95

In her Pulitzer Prize--winning "A Problem From Hell," Samantha Power took on the US government for its failure to stop genocide. Just as ambitiously, she hopes "Chasing the Flame," her account of the life of Sergio Vieira de Mello, the Brazilian super diplomat who was killed in a terrorist attack on the UN compound in Baghdad in 2003, will also serve as "the biography of a dangerous world whose ills are too big to ignore but too complex to manage quickly or cheaply."

Vieira de Mello spent three decades trying to solve humanitarian crises around the world before becoming proconsul in Kosovo and East Timor. Power depicts him as both a "worldly realist" and a "UN acolyte," charismatic but utterly serious. She says he exercised "a principled, flexible pragmatism," meaning that he knew how to bend rules to the breaking point without seeming insubordinate. He was liked by his teams, respected by locals, trusted by the UN leadership, and even tolerated by the Bush White House during the occupation of Iraq.

"Chasing the Flame" is a story of our times, as they say, well reported and written in a clear, crisp style. But at 622 pages, it's twice as long as it should be. A quarter of the book rehashes the Bush administration's amply documented early mistakes in Iraq - marginalizing Baath Party loyalists, disbanding the army - and tortured relations with the UN mission. The hefty chapter describing Vieira de Mello's agony while trapped in the rubble of the UN building in Baghdad feels melodramatic, almost unseemly.

This is also a lot of text for a psychological portrait that doesn't always convince. Power, an admirer but no sycophant, criticizes Vieira de Mello's "seeming eagerness to side with strength" and queasily relays how he hobnobbed with war criminals like the Khmer Rouge leader Ieng Sary and Yugoslav strongman Slobodan Milosevic. Vieira de Mello, she writes, "seemed more interested in being liked and maintaining access than in standing up for those who were suffering." But there's a tension here: While lauding Vieira de Mello for being effective, she decries the traits that made him so. Maintaining access and being liked were his means of influencing the people who had more power than he and so of helping those who had less. It was by cozying up to the Khmer Rouge, for example, that he orchestrated the return of 360,000 refugees to Cambodia. Vieira de Mello saved men not by siding with angels but by engaging with devils.

Power tries in vain to redeem him. She claims "he grew on the job," pointing for proof to a most-wanted poster that later hung in his office and his smuggling of evidence of war crimes out of Kosovo in 1999. In fact Vieira de Mello's approach had always been to give his interlocutors a "test of sincerity," and by the late 1990s, post-Srebrenica, the Serbs had clearly failed it. Meanwhile, he seems never to have regretted trusting the Khmer Rouge. Vieira de Mello's rhetoric on human rights may have changed somewhat over the years, but for a man supremely loyal to whatever job he held, it naturally tracked his career, from refugee advocate to high commissioner for human rights. Power bends the narrative's arc, revealing more about her moral preferences than any transformation on Vieira de Mello's part.

The move also makes her miss some interesting policy issues. One of the hard questions raised by Vieira de Mello's willingness to get his hands dirty is: How do you decide which bad guys to trust? How can you tell, before endangering civilians, who will act like the Khmer Rouge in the 1990s or the Serbs at Srebrenica? Power should have addressed this, as well as done more than skim the related issue of how to balance political accommodation and legal accountability when rebuilding societies.

Throughout the book, Power mentions the shortcomings of this UN mission or that, such as the policing gap in Kosovo or inadequate power-sharing in East Timor. But she doesn't try to distill broad conclusions until the very end, when after the drawn-out Iraq section she asks, "Where do we go from here?" Her answer, bullet-point list and all, seems a bit facile: Legitimacy stems from competence; security must be a priority; humility pays off. Power points out repeatedly (and rightly) that the UN gets a bum rap: Its member states give it sweeping mandates but few resources and then trash it for falling short. But she doesn't identify precisely what responsibilities the bureaucracy does bear. Power describes Vieira de Mello in East Timor decrying the "UN rules that forbade him from spending money directly on the country" yet doesn't make clear who set what constraints. It would have been valuable for her to describe where, in this instance and in others, UN officials, even with their hands tied by donors, can find the slack to get the job done.

"Chasing the Flame" suffers the limitations of its ambition. Rather than set out to write the dual biography of a man and the world, Power should have focused on that man and the UN, more systematically connecting Vieira de Mello's story to that of learning at the organization. For while she zooms in and out between foreground and background, the space in the middle, where lies much of the most interesting material, stays out of focus.

Stéphanie Giry is a senior editor at Foreign Affairs and a freelance writer.
