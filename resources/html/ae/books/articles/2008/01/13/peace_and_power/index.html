Peace and power

Blending history and memoir, Strobe Talbott assesses the prospects for global cooperation

George H. W. Bush (left) with Mikhail Gorbachev in 1999, at 10-year-anniversary celebrations of the fall of the Berlin Wall. (Andreas Altwein/REUTERS)
Email|Print| Text size – + By Alan Wolfe
January 13, 2008

The Great Experiment: The Story of Ancient Empires, Modern States, and the Quest for a Global Nation
By Strobe Talbott
Simon & Schuster, 478 pp., illustrated, $30

In "The Great Experiment," Strobe Talbott, former Time correspondent and current president of the Brookings Institution, has written three books in one. Two of them offer something of value. The third is even more interesting.

Talbott's first book is a history of the idea of a universal state, the dream of replacing age-old hostility between nation-states with peaceful cooperation. Ranging back to biblical times, pondering the works of Dante and Kant, and offering a history of Woodrow Wilson and his struggle to win support for the League of Nations, this book reminds us that although world government is a utopian idea, it has never lost its power to attract visionary minds. There are alternatives to Cheneyism, and Talbott is right to insist upon them.

The second book contained in "The Great Experiment" is a personal memoir. This too is worth having, for Talbott has led a most interesting life. A longtime student of Soviet and Russian affairs, he describes his first trips to the Soviet Union in the late 1960s, and his many meetings with leader Mikhail Gorbachev in the 1980s. "I sensed the weakness of a state that the entire world then considered fearsome," he writes of these visits. Never much of an ideologue, Talbott merely noticed what was going on around him and concluded that totalitarianism, as Gorbachev himself came to realize, "simply didn't work."

Talbott's wife, Brooke, worked for Hillary Clinton during the 1992 presidential campaign, and when Hillary's husband won, Talbott was picked to be, as his formal title proclaimed him, "Ambassador-at-Large and Special Adviser to the Secretary of State for the New Independent States of the Former Soviet Union." Talbott's account of his work during the Clinton administration covers a series of issues and places - African warlords, Haiti, the former Yugoslavia - that have been all but forgotten since 9/11 and the Bush administration's responses to it. They are big ifs, but if the United States ever decides that it faces more problems than Islamic terrorism and requires the help of other countries to deal with the world, we will be coming back to the memoirs of all the key players who worked for Bill Clinton for guidelines about what to do.

And so we come to the third of the books Talbott has written. This one, more implicit than explicit, deals with a subject that, in my view, is the most fascinating of all: What ever happened to this country's white Anglo-Saxon Protestant political elite? Let there be no doubt that there once was such an elite. And it is equally clear that Talbott was a member. Talbott attended prestigious schools such as Hotchkiss and Yale. His parents were what were once called liberal Republicans, related politically, if not personally, to such prominent WASPs as Bill Scranton, onetime governor of Pennsylvania, and John Lindsay, former mayor of New York City.

The Bushes are also an elite family. As it happens, the Talbotts are related to them, in family, not political, terms: George H. W. Bush's uncle, John Walker, was married to Talbott's father's cousin. Talbott attended Yale with George W. Bush, although, he writes of an encounter they had in 1990, "we had barely known each other at Yale and had seen nothing of each other since." Bush, though, clearly remembered Talbott. He once complained to Walter Isaacson, Time's then managing editor, that Talbott represented everything he considered himself in rebellion against. In large part because of decisions made by George W. Bush, there no longer was a viable liberal wing of the Republican Party, one reason a child of privilege like Talbott had become a Democrat.

It is impossible to exaggerate how much the United States has lost because of the decline of the WASP elite. As Talbott notes, families such as his had always been strong on the need to protect their environment. They believed in an idea of the common good that moderated the effects of extreme partisanship. Their advice had once been sought by presidents; had there been something like "wise men" remaining in American public life capable of piercing the Bush bubble, the Iraq war might well have been avoided.

Still, there was a reason the WASP elite went into decline, and "The Great Experiment," inadvertently this time, reveals it. About a year after George W. Bush attacked Iraq, Talbott was invited to address a group of generals. "Not wanting to seem partisan or excessively critical of an enterprise in which they and their troops were risking their lives," he writes, "I downplayed my opposition to Bush unilateralism and my concern about the extent to which the invasion had antagonized world opinion." It turns out that Talbott had misread his audience; these generals were furious at the president and wanted to hear far more critical remarks. I cite the story for another reason. The WASP elite was always a cautious elite, ever careful not to break gentlemanly codes of respect and courtesy. This did not always serve the country well; had Talbott gone public with his observation that the Soviet Union was actually a weak state, he might have put a severe dent in the neoconservative cause. But Talbott was too respectful of the Washington consensus to do that in any dramatic way, and we have all paid the price.

"The Great Experiment" ends, as it must, with the Bush years, ones that are about as far from Talbott's dream of global cooperation as it is possible to imagine. But that is why this book is so important. There will be a new president not so long from now, and even if he or she tries to ignore the world, the world will not be ignored. One way or another, we are going to have to find our way to back international cooperation, and when we do, the life, times, and ideas of a person as thoughtful and patriotic as Strobe Talbott will once again come into fashion.

Alan Wolfe teaches political science at Boston College and is writing a book on the meaning of modern liberalism.
