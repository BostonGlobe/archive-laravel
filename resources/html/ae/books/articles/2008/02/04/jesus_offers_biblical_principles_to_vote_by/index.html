Book Review

'Jesus' offers biblical principles to vote by

Email|Print| Text size – + By Rich Barlow
February 4, 2008

How Would Jesus Vote? A Christian
Perspective on the Issues
By D. James Kennedy and Jerry Newcombe
Waterbrook Press, 259 pp., $16.99

By the measures of this world, the Rev. D. James Kennedy amassed impressive successes. The Fort Lauderdale televangelist, who died in September, left a net worth exceeding $2 million and a legacy of broadcasting his conservative views over hundreds of TV and radio stations nationwide. In evangelical Christianity, Kennedy mattered.

That record and the fact that his posthumously published election-year primer, "How Would Jesus Vote?" comes out under the evangelical-books imprint of big-league Random House, might lead you to think his literary swan song is special. At the risk of speaking ill of the dead, it isn't.

Written with the senior producer of Kennedy's Coral Ridge Ministries, the book stumbles into a misguided reductionism afflicting both right and left in the current religion debates. It's perfectly legitimate and necessary for religious believers to consult their scriptures and church traditions when pondering contemporary political debates. The mistake comes in thinking that those sources alone clinch the matter, as if theology is the only discipline with anything to say about war, peace, poverty, or the environment. (Or as if we all agree on the political implications of religious doctrine to begin with.)

And so we have Kennedy writing that "if Christ is not the person you are trusting in for your hope of eternal life, then you have no hope at all." This is the boilerplate, exclusivist theology of fundamentalist Christianity, a certitude that informs too much of Kennedy's politics as well. Very occasionally, he concedes that good Christians disagree on some issues. More often, he says things like - arguing against government welfare for the poor - "the total equalization of wealth . . . would plunge the world back into the Dark Ages and result in massive starvation." Well, the closest we've come recently to equalizing wealth was during the New Deal, and while FDR's program didn't cure the Depression, neither did it summon back the Dark Ages and starvation.

This is intellectual laziness, heralded in a minor aside early in the book, when Kennedy describes the "final scene" of Mary Shelley's "Frankenstein": The monster has "left scores of people dead, and now he's coming after his maker. He's destroyed his home, and the whole thing is now ablaze." (This passage describes neither Shelley's book nor the Boris Karloff movie.) In fairness, liberal theologians can fall into the same church-knows-everything attitude Kennedy does. The progressive Christian scholar Marcus Borg recently denounced Christians who supported preemptive war against Iraq as an "extraordinary failure of Christian education," since Christian teaching bans starting a war.

Even granting that invading Iraq was a mistake, Borg's conclusion trips on a logical speed bump. There were smart, and liberal, Christians in the political and journalistic classes who supported the invasion. They surely were aware of Christian pacifist and just-war theory. Perhaps they found those doctrines to be the start of a discussion, not the conclusion. Maybe they also consulted non-Christian theologies? Histories of war, international relations, or Saddam's regime?

Kennedy doubtless was sincere in his search for biblical principles to vote by. But his final book is Exhibit A for the case that, if you want to be a thoughtful voter, texts on history, economics and current events should sit side-by-side with scripture on your shelf.
