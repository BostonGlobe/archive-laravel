Book Review

Technology can help explain spendthrift ways

Email|Print| Text size – + By Lisa Von Ahn
Reuters / February 24, 2008

Going Broke Why Americans Can't Hold On to Their Money, by Stuart Vyse, Oxford, $24.95

The key to financial solvency is simple - income must exceed expenses - but it's not so easy to explain why so many Americans can't live within their means.

While many factors are at play, a new book by Connecticut College psychology professor Stuart Vyse focuses on how dramatic changes in lending and retailing have helped decimate the national savings rate simply by making shopping so convenient.

"Going Broke" reminds older readers and informs younger ones of how different things were just 30 years ago.

Back then, people did most of their shopping in stores during regular business hours, and they were much more likely to pay with a check or with cash obtained from a bank teller.

Today ATMs abound, and just about anyone can get a credit card that makes it possible to buy anything from mouthwash to a car online, regardless of whether the purchaser actually has the money on hand.

Meanwhile, technology has created an increasing number of things to want, while a constant stream of ads reminds people of their availability.

"All of the barriers to spending have been removed," Vyse said in an interview. "Now we must always think: 'Should I do this or not? Should I make this purchase or not?'

"Between the two environments, fewer people got into trouble in the old environment."

The ubiquity of the marketplace plays on the all-too-human tendency to seek fulfillment in the present even though that could jeopardize long-range financial goals.

But consumers are not helpless, Vyse writes. For example, they can make online shopping less convenient by using dial-up Internet service instead of always-on broadband, they can reduce their exposure to advertising by canceling magazine and cable TV subscriptions, and they can have retirement contributions automatically deducted from their paychecks.

Vyse also calls for policy changes, such as programs to educate consumers and promote savings.

While he would like to see regulations to limit commercialism and lending, he has some doubts that these will happen. "I think that those kinds of changes would be attacked on the basis of being antibusiness," he said.

Vyse, who himself has carried heavy debts at various points in his life, does not ignore the impact of such social issues as costly healthcare and diminishing job security.

But he said his background as an expert in irrational behavior - which he defines as self-defeating - was particularly relevant in studying Americans' spending habits.

"Given a choice of being comfortable in our dotage, everyone would choose that if we could," he said.

"But what we don't realize is that to get there, you have to avoid a lot of little decisions now that don't seem important at the moment and are quite gratifying at the moment, but they do add up and could mean that a larger dream is deferred."
