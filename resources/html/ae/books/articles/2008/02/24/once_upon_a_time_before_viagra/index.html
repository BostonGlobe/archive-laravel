A Reading Life

Once upon a time, before Viagra . . .

Email|Print| Text size – + By Katherine A. Powers
Globe Correspondent / February 27, 2008

Some time ago a friend of mine who lives overseas and is a vitamin loony ordered some of his special nostrums to be sent to my address for him to pick up later. Thus I gained a spot on some macabre mailing lists: Circulars touting the miraculous powers of countless quack remedies began showing up at the door. At first I was appalled and even thought I should explain the situation to the mail carrier after he delivered a pamphlet displaying an Augean garland of lower intestines, lurid proof, if proof were necessary, that "the Average Person Can Have Between 4 and 25 Pounds of Toxic Matter in Their Colon." After a while, however, I surrendered to my own sense of festivity about crackpots, and now I welcome each day's bounty of extravagant, capitalized claims. ("This Top Secret 'Bio-Defense Weapon' Has Finally Been Released to a Limited Number of Civilians! The U.S. Government Had It 'Locked Up' For 'Decades.' Experts Are Calling It 'Nature's Revenge'! The Drug Companies and the Fat Cats Can't Touch It - Yet It Could Make Some of Their Drugs Obsolete!' ")

I believe that Americans are more gullible about panaceas than other people; after all, the nation was founded in good part as a remedy for ancient ills. Certainly our country has a long and entertaining history of quackery - entertaining, that is, if one manages to suppress pity, which, heartless creature that I am, I do. This brings me to Pope Brock's buoyant contribution to the annals of medical chicanery: "Charlatan: America's Most Dangerous Huckster, the Man Who Pursued Him, and the Age of Flimflam" (Crown, $24.95).

The huckster in question was Dr. John R. Brinkley (MD, PhD, MC, LLD, DPH, ScD), one of the most financially successful practitioners in the "virility racket," which raged in the years between the two world wars. A number of tonics, including "Orchis Extract" (purportedly "the essence of rams' testicles"), and procedures, including the implantation of chimpanzee testes, flourished. By 1924, according to Brock, 750 outfits and individuals were hawking "gland-rejuvenation treatments" in the United States. Brinkley's own specialty was implanting billy-goat glands (testicles to you) in men who sought local and general rejuvenation. His methods were various: Sometimes he inserted a goat testicle into a man's (which "he likened to 'embedding a marble in an apple' "), other times he introduced the potent gland into his patients slivered "like a clove of garlic." Sometimes, Brock notes, "the operation was no more complex than tossing a Christmas present into a bag."

At the time there were, in fact, scientists who believed that the procedure actually worked - and indeed, their investigations led to the isolation of testosterone. But what of Brinkley? "The answer," Brock suggests, "lies perhaps in small things, like his way of calling patients 'old fools' when he was drunk." Dozens of Brinkley's patients died, many more were permanently maimed, and he became the crusading obsession of Dr. Morris Fishbein, the eventual editor of the Journal of the American Medical Association. Fishbein's campaign against Brinkley makes up one strand of this extraordinary tale and provides it with a bravura courtroom finale.

It is clear that Brock (like this reader) cannot get enough of this guy, even though he was, as Brock also notes, nothing less than a serial killer. Still, the book is written with glee, in a style that is pure gusto, a bubbling fountain of metaphor and arresting image ("the notion of priming one's privates came naturally to the Jazz Age, as men and women chased bliss with a butterfly net after the horrors of the war").

Brinkley's methods of exploiting the yearning for eternal youth and potency, and for surefire remedies for disease, decay, and discontent itself, were truly innovative and significant shapers of American culture. He had a feeling for mass psychology and adopted the tactics of public relations peddled by Edward Bernays ("a short braggart with a tremendous mustache"). Brinkley expanded his enterprise beyond virility and tapped into the female market, coming up with the Brinkley Pharmaceutical Association, a franchise that sold a wide range of pills and powders. He promoted the line through circulars and, most effectively, over his radio station, from which pulpit he prescribed with diagnostic abandon.

Brinkley's original station was in Kansas and was the first to demonstrate that the airwaves could be a mighty commercial force. Thanks to the efforts of Fishbein, however, Brinkley's medical and broadcasting licenses were eventually revoked and he departed Kansas - though not before running for governor twice and showing the value of the airplane for political barnstorming. His second radio station, the million-watt "Sunshine Station Between the Nations," was situated on the Mexican side of the Rio Grande, and could be heard across the United States. Here, in addition to "wallet-emptying medico-religious palaver and pastoral bloviations," Brinkley broadcast performances by the stars of country music. He had begun this in Kansas, but now the sound reached across the United States, adding itself to the fabric of American culture.

Brock points out that "a perverse seam in the American mind" caused Americans to embrace quacks. This was their penchant for rating the sense of the common man over that of the educated one. "So high," Brinkley writes, "was the common man exalted that state governments, all but three, actually repealed licensing requirements for doctors."

Richard Hofstadter traced that "perverse seam" from the Puritans to the early 1960s in his brilliant "Anti-Intellectualism in American Life." Now, Susan Jacoby has attempted to bring the story up to the present in "The Age of American Unreason" (Pantheon, $26), arguing that anti-intellectualism, anti-rationalism, and sheer ignorance are on the rise in this country. The reasons lie in the dreadful state of education, the swelling ranks and influence of religious fundamentalism, and the all-pervasiveness of "infotainment" spewed from television and computer screens. I certainly do not disagree with Jacoby's depiction of this mess nor with her demonstration that responsibility for it is shared by both left and right. Nonetheless, I found this book a real torment to read. It is pervaded by an animus against the Internet, a prejudice she continually and proudly displays as if it were the last word in independent thinking. It is also a much-belabored argument for the author's righteous belief in rationalism. As a result the book, shamefully padded out as it is, is like reading a 300-plus-page op-ed column.

Katherine A. Powers lives in Cambridge. Her column appears on alternate Sundays. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@verizon.net.
