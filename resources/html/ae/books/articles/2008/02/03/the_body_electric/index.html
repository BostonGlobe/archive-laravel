The body electric

Transcendent essays about our flesh-and-blood existence

Email|Print| Text size – + By Thomas Lynch
February 3, 2008

The Thing About Life Is That One Day You’ll Be Dead
By David Shields
Knopf, 225 pp., $23.95

‘‘In every man,’’ wrote Montaigne, ‘‘is the whole of Man’s estate.’’ To wit: If you want to understand the species, examine a specimen. To figure out humanity, find a human and have a look. Prod, poke, test, try, measure and inspect, analyze and assay. Like a good internist, the essayist never tires of tests — sounding the language for what rings true, bringing the wisdom of ages to bear on the moment at hand, trying to divine the permanent rule in the particular experience, the planet in the parish.

Whenever Montaigne, the 16th-century master, would find himself at a loss for words, he would pull a book from the shelf — he loved the Latin poets — quote a line or two and make off in the direction it seemed to take him: ‘‘Double the lights of lamps, flowering with flame,./.Double the face of man, his body twain,’’ Lucretius wrote.

Twain bodies are at the heart of ‘‘The Thing About Life Is That One Day You’ll Be Dead,’’ David Shields’s luminous collection of essays: his father’s, approaching 100, and his own, just gone 50. And twin realities, set forth in the title and on every page — that good news always holds a hint of the bad; the good laugh gives way, as it must, to the good cry; vitality and mortality keep the same mean time.

‘‘This is my research; this is what I now know: the brute facts of existence, the fragility and ephemerality of life in its naked corporeality, human beings as bare, forked animals, the beauty and pathos in my body and his body and everybody else’s body as well. Accept death, I always seem to be saying. Accept life, is his entirely understandable reply.’’

It is the palpable tension between these ‘‘gravities’’ that Shields examines in this pre-mortem on humanity. There are paragraphs so finely wrought, so precisely tuned to the narrow-band channels between reader and writer, that the caught breath of inspiration and the sighs of expiration leave us grinning and breathless.

‘‘Testosterone initiates the growth spurt; increases larynx size, deepening the voice; increases red blood cell mass, muscle mass, libido; stimulates development of the penis, scrotum, and prostate; stimulates growth of pubic, facial, leg and armpit hair; stimulates sebaceous gland secretions of oil. Throughout high school, my acne was so severe as to constitute a second skin. Oil leaked from my pores. I kissed no one until I was 17.’’

Such is the method in his good-humored madness: Mix equal parts of anatomy and autobiography, science and self-disclosure, physiology and family history; shake, stir, add dashes of miscellany, pinches of borrowed wisdom, simmer over a low-grade fever of mortality, and a terrible beauty of a book is born. That we die is this life’s punctuation. That we cease to be makes being meaningful. We love and grieve in equal measures. This is not news — Job and Shakespeare have told it in their times — but Shields’s telling of it does renew, refresh, and reaffirm life’s mysteries and death’s. That he manages to retell these cautionary tales without smarminess or Hallmark sentiment is among the book’s triumphs.

Against the predictable linearity of the book’s sections — ‘‘Infancy and Childhood,’’ ‘‘Adolescence,’’ ‘‘Adulthood and Middle Age,’’ and ‘‘Old Age and Death’’ — the chapters, unpredictably repeating riffs, achieve an echoing and interlacing circularity that mimics life: ‘‘Decline and Fall,’’ ‘‘Boys vs. Girls,’’ ‘‘Hoop Dream,’’ ‘‘Sex and Death,’’ not to mention a brush with celebrity in the family’s history, recounted in pieces called ‘‘Bloodline to Star Power’’: ‘‘My father’s birth certificate reads ‘Milton Shildcrout.’ His military record says ‘Milton P. Schildcrout’ (he had no middle name; he made it up). When he changed his name in 1946 to ‘Shields,’ the petition listed both ‘Shildkrout’ and ‘Shildkraut.’ His brother Abe used ‘Shildkrout’; his sister Fay’s maiden name was ‘Schildkraut.’ Who cares? I do. I want to know whether I’m related to Joseph Schildkraut, who played Otto Frank in ‘The Diary of Anne Frank’ and won an Academy Award in 1938 for his portrayal of Alfred Dreyfus in ‘The Life of Emile Zola.’.’’

Then there’s the wincing little truth telling in ‘‘Everything I Know I’ve Learned From My Bad Back’’: ‘‘I go to sleep with a night guard jammed between my teeth and a Breathe Right strip stretched across my nose (to mitigate snoring) and a pillow between my legs. I walk around with an ice pack stuck in one coat pocket and a baggie of ibuprofen in the other. I’m not exactly the king of the jungle. I like the humility and gravity and nakedness of this need, for — and this is apparently a lesson I can’t relearn too many times — we’re just animals walking the earth for a brief time, a bare body housed in a mortal cage.’’

Still, the bottom line for David Shields transcends the cage itself and traffics in the nearly spiritual: ‘‘But now, rather than endlessly rehearsing how my life might have been different, I tell myself how grateful I am for my life.’’

‘‘How is it that I’ve remained a boy all this time?’’ asks Philip Lopate in his essay ‘‘Portrait of My Body.’’ If having a father who threatens to outlive you is one way to keep one striving and alive, fixed on the future and fit for the long haul, then David Shields is well provisioned. They made a great model when they made his father, and a reliable witness when they made the son. This diamond of a book — brilliant with homage and anecdote — might outlive them both.

Thomas Lynch is a poet, essayist, and funeral director.
