A Reading Life

Idylls of the Toad

By Katherine A. Powers
November 2, 2008
  • Email|
  • Print|
  • Single Page|
  • |
Text size – +

This year marks the 100th anniversary of the publication of "The Wind in the Willows," by Kenneth Grahame. I have no idea why a great fuss hasn't been made of this; indeed, I only stumbled on the fact in the course of my daily routine of looking things up for no reason. I imagine that most people opening the Books section have either read this great work or have had it read to them; nonetheless, this is surely the time to revisit its comfort and joy. The appetites and temper it appeals to remain in most of us beyond childhood, or they should. The book is a celebration of the countryside, of the English one in this case, and of nature, both of which are invested with a current of animism. It is also a hymn to domesticity and, above all, to friendship. I'm quite sure that it is the first novel in which an "intervention" is staged. This occurs, if I may remind you, when Rat, Mole, and Badger confront Toad with his destructive behavior and lock him in his bedroom for his own good. (" 'You knew it must come to this, sooner or later, Toad,' the Badger explained severely. . . . 'Independence is all very well, but we animals never allow our friends to make fools of themselves beyond a certain limit; and that limit you've reached.' ")

The scale of the animals has been adjusted for companionship and their size for moving in human society. This is not an unusual conceit at all, but I find it endlessly amusing. There is nothing these creatures like so much as sitting before a cozy fire taking tea and buttered toast after some drenching adventure. They eat chicken sandwiches, lobster salad, bacon and broad beans, and they drink Old Burton ale. They take the train; they go to the shops; the swaggering Toad tips doormen. I completely identify with these creatures - Mole in particular, if you must know. I feel precisely as he does about "dulce domum," home, that is, though I must say I am also at one with Rat's penchant for organization, especially as he equips the friends for recapturing Toad Hall. It's very much like getting ready for a family outing around our place. ("Rat . . . was running round the room busily, with his arms full of weapons of every kind, distributing them in . . . heaps on the floor, and saying excitedly under his breath, as he ran, 'Here's-a-sword-for-the-Rat, here's-a-sword-for-the-Mole, here's-a-sword-for-the-Toad, here's-a-sword-for-the-Badger! Here's-a-pistol-for-the-Rat, here's-a-pistol-for-the-Mole, here's-a-pistol-for-the-Toad, here's-a-pistol-for-the-Badger!' ")

I will not insist that Justin Cartwright's "The Song Before It Is Sung" (Bloomsbury, paperback, $15.99) is heir to "The Wind in the Willows" - it may be that I only see it this way having just read the one and reread the other around the same time and with similar visceral pleasure. But it is not a completely preposterous idea. They both, in quite different ways to be sure, treat of friendship; both display a yearning for some essential Englishness; and both deal with crude intrusions on a preexistent notion of harmony. And I think we'll just leave it at that.

Cartwright's novel combines two impossibly different stories: one, sad and improbably funny, of the marital tribulations of a present-day London journalist; and the other, problematic and tragic, of the downfall of one of the aristocratic German conspirators in the July 20, 1944, attempt to assassinate Hitler. There would seem to be plenty of room for missteps here, of fatuousness, or bathos, or of structural ricketiness at the very least. But no, the book is a miracle of discrimination and form, dexterously blending the main and subsidiary storylines through the consciousness of the central character. This is Conrad Senior, mid-30s, dreamy and ineffectual and, in his fecklessness, a persistent affront to his career-minded wife. She is Francine, a doctor, who correctly detects how little he values the world she esteems.

The reader's sympathies are with Conrad, and yet we do see what a trial he would be as a helpmeet, especially to one such as Francine, for whom life and "career path" are one. Conrad, however, is simply not engaged with workaday reality; his thoughts are the medium in which he lives his life, and his observations are those of a fascinated, faintly solipsistic bystander: He thinks of Francine with disinterested benignity, of "how she would eat breakfast standing up, unaware that he saw her absolute belief that she was going to be late as endearingly irrational," or of how in bed at night she "sometimes appears to be awake, with her eyes wide open and her teeth grinding lightly."

One does see why. Conrad has quit his job on a newspaper to devote himself to investigating the life and execution of Axel von Gottberg, a conspirator (loosely based on Adam von Trott) in the July 20 plot. He has taken this up because his mentor from his university days at Oxford, Elya Mendel, a Jewish, Riga-born Oxford don, has died and left him his papers, asking him to make sense of his friendship with von Gottberg. This involves determining the man's motives in the assassination attempt and his state of mind at execution. The German had been Mendel's friend at Oxford, a relationship severed later when Axel wrote a letter to The Manchester Guardian in 1933 claiming that Jews were not being persecuted in Germany. It was a lie, of course, or, more accurately, a mendacious, fiercely held delusion that Axel, steeped in German Romanticism and Hegel, chose to maintain. He could not accept that Nazi anti-Semitism and thuggery were anything but aberrations from the essential spirit of the German people.

Abandoned by his wife for another man, and living off his credit card, Conrad tracks down the survivors of and witnesses to the Mendel-von Gottberg friendship. His incessantly self-referential mind gives the narrative a unity that, in the end, speaks not only to his own increasingly complex marital predicament, but to the state of the world - of Germany in the 20th century, of England in the 21st. This is a brilliant feat. But there is more. The story also deftly incorporates the philosophical underpinnings of beliefs and ways of believing. You could say that it also demonstrates the virtues of empiricism over idealism, of Locke over Hegel, but that makes it sound far more theoretical than it is. It is simply a great, many-layered novel that does exactly what art should do: bring coherence to life.

Katherine A. Powers lives in Cambridge. Her column appears on alternate Sundays. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@verizon.net.
