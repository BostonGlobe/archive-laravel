Behind the Scenes

Exploring Plymouth’s Jewish legacy

By Robert Knox
Globe Correspondent / August 23, 2012

Karin Goldstein’s upcoming book “Jewish Plymouth” examines a distinctive local community that traces its roots to the great wave of immigrants who fled oppression, prejudice, and poverty in countries such as Russia and Lithuania between 1880 and 1920 to look for opportunity in a country where social status was not assigned by birth.

Some of these “latter-day Pilgrims” made their way to Plymouth, where they made their homes, built businesses, founded the oldest synagogue on the South Shore still in operation, embraced the town’s Pilgrim heritage, and made lasting contributions to their new community.

The book is an important contribution to what Donna Curtin, director of the Plymouth Antiquarian Society, calls “hidden history” — the family and communal strands in the fabric of local history that can easily be lost to living memory.

“I am delighted that Karin is doing this research and coming out with this book,” Curtin said last week. “The story is so rich and interesting.”

Goldstein, curator of original collections at Plimoth Plantation, will offer some of those riches Friday evening in a PowerPoint presentation of photos and stories titled “People of the Book: The Jewish Community of Plymouth, 1890-1930” at the Hedge House, one of the Antiquarian Society’s three house museums. The “Immigrant Tales & Tastes” program is sponsored by the society, dedicated to preserving the history of post-Pilgrim Plymouth.

Without a written record, local history can vanish, Curtin said. This is especially true of the early decades of Plymouth’s Jewish community, since many families lived near the Congregation Beth Jacob synagogue in the Summer-High Street area, lost to urban renewal in the 1960s.  

Goldstein researched the losses and gains of Plymouth’s urban renewal, including the tearing down of many historic but blighted, houses, for “From Pilgrims to Poverty: Biography of an Urban Renewal Neighborhood in Plymouth, Massachusetts,” her doctoral dissertation. “That was her gateway into this topic,” Curtin said.

To research “Jewish Plymouth,” Goldstein traced the European roots of a community of 20 to 25 families in the decades before World War II, looking into why they came to America, “and how did they end up here of all places?” Curtin said.

The answer is often found in the “push-me, pull-you” of circumstances, chance occurrences, and help from friends, she said. “They were all driven by economics.”

In the immigrants’ search to find jobs, Plymouth’s cordage factory and woolen and tack mills played a role in attracting newcomers, Curtain said, but “some stories are more nuanced and have to do with a network of people who help one another.”

Some of Goldstein’s research saw daylight in an essay (”Plymouth’s Jewish Community”) she wrote for a local history anthology, “Beyond Plymouth Rock,” telling how some of the town’s first Jewish families put down roots here, among them the Resnick, Toabe, Penn, and Brody families.

 “A Resnick appears in the 1893 street directory as a ‘junk dealer,’ ” after arriving from Lithuania around 1889, Goldstein wrote in “Plymouth’s Jewish Community.” Family members collected scrap from the Plymouth Cordage Co., and opened a grocery store on South Street, she recounted. Later arrivals traded in horses and cows.

The Toabe family began its American history when Russian immigrant Mottel Toib was rebranded by a Boston immigration officer as Max Toabe. Toabe’s route as a peddler took him to Plymouth, where, delighted to find a couple of Jewish families on the scene already, he set up house on Howland Street and sent for his family.

After the family moved to North Ply­mouth, Toabe was invited by Luigi Cortelli, an immigrant who founded the L. Knife beer distributor company, to sell wares from one of his empty storefront properties. The Toabe family learned to repair and sell damaged goods, birthing what became an extensive hardware business, with stores from Duxbury to Cape Cod.

The accidental nature of immigrant destinies is illustrated by the story of Abraham Penn and his wife, Fannie Brody Penn. Advised by a Boston doctor to look for a home in the country for his ailing wife’s health, Penn boarded a train heading south and got off at the end of the line: Plymouth.

The presence of a few Jewish families here clinched the deal. After her husband’s death, Fannie became a peddler. Her son would become a partner in a landmark Plymouth business, Puritan Clothing. 

Goldstein’s presentation will also include a telling episode in local history rediscovered when Congregation Beth Jacob member Carl Finer found a photo of a rabbi standing over Plymouth Rock wearing his prayer shawl. In 1938, she learned, the local council of churches chose Rabbi Samuel Friedman to preach the Thanksgiving Day sermon in Plymouth’s annual interfaith service. It was the town’s answer to anti-Semitic outrages in Germany.

Friday’s program at the Hedge House will also include some “authentic noshes” prepared from traditional recipes by a Plymouth baker, Kirstin Atchison.
