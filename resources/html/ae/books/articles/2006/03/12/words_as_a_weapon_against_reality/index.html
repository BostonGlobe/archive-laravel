A READING LIFE

Words as a weapon against reality

By Katherine A. Powers  |  March 12, 2006

If there has been one quest that has dominated my life ever since I could read, it is to discover the funniest book ever written -- or at least the funniest by my lights, written in or translated into English. Around 10 novels move in and out of the top five slots, but I think -- as I have mentioned more than once in this space -- that absolutely the funniest book I have ever read, a book that makes me laugh helplessly every time I take it up, is Kingsley Amis's ''Lucky Jim." On the other hand, it is not the greatest funniest book I have read. That distinction belongs to Charles Portis's ''Masters of Atlantis."

Amis's book is a satire and ''merely" viscerally funny. It is about truly awful people, a hapless hero, and ghastly situations portrayed with diabolical precision. It is what you might call a situation comedy, though of a very high order. ''Masters of Atlantis," on the other hand, makes me laugh in places, but not to the extent of spewing tea out my nose. Instead, it ascends to another dimension of language, where clichés and manners of speaking are surreally comic. It is brilliantly deadpan and, even in its most absurd passages, quietly droll. It elevates me to a plane of ineffable joy.

I was brought to contemplate all this by having turned up a couple of novels that approach the comic greatness I associate with ''Masters of Atlantis." They are ''What's for Dinner?" (New York Review Books, paperback, $14) and ''Alfred and Guinevere" (New York Review Books, paperback, $12.95), both by James Schuyler. Even most people who have heard of him think of him as a dead poet. Like Portis's works, the novels put language center stage. ''What's for Dinner?" (a perfect title that makes me laugh just looking at it) was published in 1978 but seems to have been set earlier, in the mid 1960s, perhaps, though the expression to get ''in dutch" with someone smacks of an earlier age. In any case, most of the characters are founts of complacent remark and pleasantry.

There are exceptions, to be sure, chief among them being Mary Charlotte Taylor, an alcoholic housewife whose sensibilities, we feel, are the author's. But, for most of the people in these pages, even occasional forays into the perilous terrain of gossip are restrained. Their conversations, which make up the novel's fabric, click along over the surface of things, over realities not recognized or tolerable, creating, miraculously, a feeling of tautness. It is Schuyler's genius, that of a poet, I guess, which transforms everyday talk into a gravid version of silence. Spoken words resonate with unsounded counterbeats, and behind all that is the palpable condition of aloneness.

I see that this is starting to sound not very funny. But while the aloneness here may be melancholy, it is also compassionate, never grim and despairing. It is not at all the sort of aloneness against which language is impotent. Instead, Schuyler has invested quotidian speech with unmistakable doughtiness. Words don't represent reality so much as bravely resist it, exactly as they do in Portis's world, as it happens. This is mightily pleasing and truly funny. Here is Schuyler's character Biddy, an old woman who lives with her son's family and who prides herself on her industry, one branch of which is crocheting: '' 'Roses,' Biddy said, 'the queen of flowers.' She shook out the crocheted maroon throw, so all could see it. 'Isn't this just the color of an American Beauty?' It wasn't, but if anyone knew it, no one said it."

Most of the novel is made up of conversation, but now and then Schuyler steps forward with marvelous descriptions of the characters. As we enter the novel, we find Mary Charlotte's husband reading the paper, ''deep in the mendacities of one of the columnists who shaped his thoughts," though still able to respond to his wife's remarks, being ''never so absorbed as not to leave a trickle of attention running." Guests arrive, one of whose ''robust frame appeared freshly back from the upholsterer." Every word is right in this book, every one is perfectly placed.

That is also true of ''Alfred and Guinevere," a short novel told chiefly from the point of view of Guinevere, a pre-adolescent girl. Its comic dimension is even more difficult to reduce to its properties than that of ''What's for Dinner?" I am driven to invoke Joseph Brodsky, who wrote that ''any attempt to approach analytically a phenomenon whose nature is synthetic" -- that is, art -- ''is doomed by definition." Some things are just magnificently funny, and this novel and ''What's for Dinner?" are two of them. In ''Alfred and Guinevere," characters fortify themselves against life's uncertainty with language, though here they do so more forthrightly because Guinevere is a great reader and writer. Indeed, her journal constitutes much of the novel: ''Am almost finished reading Madame Bovary by Flaubert," she writes. ''It is very good at showing people up for what they are really like but not for story." Later she reports: ''Finished Madame Bovary by Flaubert last night. The part where she buys the poison and dies is very true to life."

The novel is a miracle of spareness and is, in fact, only 123 pages long. But all sorts of events occur, some very unpleasant. They are recounted from a child's point of view, either Guinevere's or her younger brother Alfred's, and we detect a good deal more than we are being told. At the same time we are never sure just what it is. What is really going on? The fact that we are not given a superior knowledge, only a different sort of uncertainty from the children's, is further evidence of Schuyler's partisanship with his characters. For him, it is clear, stories are what stand between us and chaos; and language, even at its most routine, is a powerful defense. We see this throughout the book, culminating with the two children in bed signing off for the night after squabbling about who finished his or her prayers first: '' 'May I say one thing,' " asks Alfred.

'' 'What is it?'

'' 'Good night, whale.'

'' 'Good night, shrimp.'

'' 'Good night, pig.'

'' 'You disgust me. Good night.'

'' 'I'm rubber and you're glue so anything you say to me will bounce off me and stick to you. Good night.' "

Katherine A. Powers, a writer and critic, lives in Cambridge. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@earthlink.net.
