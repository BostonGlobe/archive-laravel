Tales from the dark side

Jodi Picoult has thrived with novels that delve into the anguish found in society.In her latest work, she descends like never before.

By David Mehegan, Globe Staff  |  March 4, 2006

HANOVER, N.H. -- For years, Jodi Picoult has been the most successful New England novelist you're least likely to have heard of, but that's changing fast.

Her 2005 novel of a woman seeking the truth of her past, ''Vanishing Acts," has spent more than a month on The New York Times paperback bestseller list. Her just-published novel about teenage date rape, ''The Tenth Circle," has a first printing of 350,000 copies. And after 14 years and 12 previous novels, there are more than 3 million of Picoult's books in print in 25 countries.

Her tales of family and community agony, usually set in small New England towns and often focused on a social issue, appear at a rate of about one a year. She says she has the next four already worked out. ''I'm sure I can do 20, at least," she said.

The new work has one innovative feature: A comic-book story is interspersed throughout the novel. The main character of ''The Tenth Circle" -- Daniel Stone, father of the date-raped girl -- is a comic-book artist, and in the wake of the rape he draws a nightmarish version of his family's crisis, with a Dantean trip to hell. When she began writing the book, Picoult hired a young San Francisco-based comic-book artist, Dustin Weaver, to draw this parallel story, called ''The Immortal Wildclaw in the Tenth Circle."

''She sent me scripts [for the graphic narrative]," Weaver said by telephone, ''and chapters of the book as she wrote them. I started by doing designs of a character, then sending them to her. She did a great job breaking it down like a professional comic-book writer. It was clear what I needed to do."

Despite her immense publishing output, Picoult, 39, has a normal busy life -- with three kids and an antique-dealer husband who often works at home -- far from the cliche of the tormented writer at war with everyday life.

Interviewed at her home in the woods near Hanover Center, she and her husband, Tim van Leer, had just returned from a weekend hockey tournament in Dover with Jake, their 12-year-old son. There was a friendly dog underfoot, jackets and skates lying about, and kids' messy bedrooms visible through open doors. She proved to be energetic, cheerful, and voluble, seeming to prove that a productive creative writer need not be a crabby recluse. The door of her attic writing room is always open, she said, and her kids (ages 10, 12, and 14) interrupt her freely.

Picoult grew up on Long Island and comes, she said, from ''a long line of teachers." But she wanted to be a writer and went to Princeton University ''because it was one of the few colleges at the time with an undergraduate program in creative writing." She met van Leer there, they were married in 1989, and they lived for years in the Boston area, where she got a master's degree in education from Harvard. In 1989 she began what became her first novel, ''Songs of the Humpback Whale," while holding a succession of jobs, as public schoolteacher, advertising copywriter, and business textbook editor with publisher Allyn & Bacon. They moved to Hanover in 1994.

From the start, she learned to carve out writing time. At Allyn, she said, ''I used to do all my work before 10 in the morning, close my door, and write the whole day." But after she sold ''Songs" in 1991, her son Kyle was born, and things got more complicated.

''It took several years to figure out how being a mother and being a novelist could coexist," she wrote in a later e-mail, ''and even more remarkably, complement each other. Being able to write for a few hours every day makes the time I spend with my kids that much more precious, and being a mother makes me gravitate toward topics that I worry about as a parent, and wrestle with in fiction in the hopes that I won't have to wrestle with them in reality."

Part of it was learning how to make use of all her available time. ''I don't believe in writer's block," she said. ''It tends to affect people with a surfeit of time. I had three little kids and would write any time they were occupied. If they were napping or 'Barney' was on, they were playing blocks and not hitting each other over the head, I would have 10 minutes and would sit down and write. Tim would come home, and I would throw the kids at him and go write for a couple of hours."

Picoult's novels often center on real problems in American society. ''The Pact" (1998) is about teenage suicide. ''Perfect Match" (2002) concerns child sex abuse by a Catholic priest in Maine. The 13-year-old protagonist of ''My Sister's Keeper" (her first big seller, in 2004), whose parents have used her throughout her life as a source of blood and marrow transfusions for her older sister with leukemia, sues her parents for control of her own body. Next year's book, which is already finished, is about a school shooting in a New Hampshire town.

''She's so good at taking these huge national topics and throwing them in your backyard," said Emily Bestler, executive editorial director of Picoult's publisher, Atria Books, a division of Simon & Schuster. ''We might sit around and think, 'What if there were a school shooting in my town?' I couldn't imagine it, but when I read her novel, I really could imagine it. Everything that is dark and scary and convoluted, she puts on the page. All the good stuff is her own life."

Indeed, Picoult's said, ''I live this ridiculously charmed life. I don't think I could write those things if I had lived them. I need to come down at the end of the night when I am done writing and know that I have three healthy kids and a great husband and a terrific family."

With her one-book-per-year production rate, one might expect Picoult's novels to be shallow fare. But they're highly structured, extensively researched, and intricately plotted, with many characters and scenes and multiple points of view. For ''My Sister's Keeper," she immersed herself in learning about APL, acute promyelocytic leukemia. ''It does have to ring true," she said. ''It's a huge compliment that the book has been reviewed in medical journals."

Though she says her mission is storytelling, not writing useful treatises on social controversies, it's clear that the issues played out in her books are of deep interest to her, apart from the art she makes of them. The first several minutes of the interview were all about the reality of date rape and impersonal sex among teenagers.

Many novelists loathe the obligatory author tour, but Picoult enjoys it. Starting Tuesday night in Natick (7 p.m. at the Keefe Performing Arts Center in Wilson Middle School), she's off on a 50-city national tour, to be followed by another in the United Kingdom. ''The readers are just a number until you see them sitting in front of you," she said, ''laughing or crying." Often, they tell her they've been through what she has written about.

Picoult is proud of her life's work but still seems a bit astounded to consider it as a whole. She said, ''There are times when I look at the sales figures and think, 'OK, my mother's friends can't be the only ones reading this book anymore. She doesn't have that many friends.' There are times I think I'm going to wake up and find out it's been totally a dream, but wow, it's been a great dream."
