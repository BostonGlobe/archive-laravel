'Da Vinci Code ' author takes the stand

March 14, 2006

LONDON -- In a rare public appearance, ''The Da Vinci Code" novelist Dan Brown dismissed as ''completely fanciful" claims by two authors that he stole their ideas. He said that he did not read their book until the structure of his theological thriller was in place.

Yesterday, three years to the week after ''The Da Vinci Code" was first published, the multimillionaire writer found himself on the witness stand in London's High Court, denying accusations of copyright infringement made by authors Michael Baigent and Richard Leigh.

In a witness statement made public by lawyers as he took the stand, Brown said he was ''shocked at their reaction" to his book. But under questioning by the plaintiffs' lawyer, Brown acknowledged that he could not always recall exact dates in the creation of his novel. Both books explore theories that Mary Magdalene was not a prostitute and was Jesus' wife, that the couple had a child, and that the bloodline survives.

''I cannot possibly tell you the precise date I learned that Mary Magdalene was not a prostitute," Brown told attorney Jonathan Rayner James in front of a packed courtroom.

The author, a New Hampshire resident who has given few interviews as his novel has become an international sensation, appeared composed during testimony. Only occasionally did he show traces of impatience with Rayner James's forensic questioning about documents and dates.

''It's as if you've asked me to go back five years or 10 years and asked me not only what I got for Christmas, but what order I opened the presents," he said at one point.

Baigent and Leigh are suing ''The Da Vinci Code" publisher Random House for copyright infringement, claiming Brown ''appropriated the architecture" of their 1982 nonfiction book ''Holy Blood, Holy Grail."

If Baigent and Leigh secure an injunction to bar the use of their material, they could hold up the scheduled May 19 release of the film ''The Da Vinci Code," starring Tom Hanks and Audrey Tautou.

Random House lawyers argue that the ideas in dispute are so general they are not protected by copyright. They also contend that many of the ideas in ''Holy Blood, Holy Grail" do not figure in Brown's novel, which follows fictional professor Robert Langdon as he investigates the murder of a member of an ancient society that guards dark secrets about the story of Jesus and the quest for the Holy Grail.

In his 69-page witness statement, Brown acknowledged reading Baigent and Leigh's book while he was writing ''The Da Vinci Code" -- along with 38 other books and more than 300 documents submitted as evidence to the court. He said Baigent and Leigh's work ''was not a crucial or important text in the creation of the framework of 'The Da Vinci Code.' "

Brown said he had fully acknowledged his debt to the two authors by having a character in ''The Da Vinci Code" refer to the earlier book and its theories. He even named a character Sir Leigh Teabing, an anagram of Baigent and Leigh.

The author's statement and testimony provided tantalizing glimpses of his pre-''Da Vinci" life -- from pupil at Phillips Exeter Academy to semi-successful Los Angeles songwriter.

Brown has also written the thrillers ''Deception Point" and ''Angels & Demons," both million sellers thanks to the ongoing fascination with his most recent novel. He has been working on a new book, again featuring protagonist Robert Langdon. Brown is expected to continue his testimony today.

The third author of ''Holy Blood, Holy Grail," Henry Lincoln, is not involved in the case. A lawyer for the plaintiffs, Paul Sutton, did not to say why he was not participating.
