ON AUDIO

Beware the ringing cell

By Rochelle O'Gorman  |  March 26, 2006

Cell
By Stephen King
Simon & Schuster Audio, unabridged fiction, 12 CDs, 13 hours, $49.95, read by Campbell Scott. Also available on eight cassettes, $49.95, and as a download from www.audible.com, $34.97.

Full Moon Rising
By Keri Arthur
Random House Audio, abridged fiction, four CDs, five hours, $27.95, read by Tamara Lovatt-Smith. Also available as download from www.audible.com, $19.57, or unabridged, read by Justine Eyre, 10 hours and 11 minutes, $34.97.

Micah
By Laurell K. Hamilton
Brilliance Audio, unabridged fiction, four CDs, four hours, $24.95, or one MP3-CD, $24.95, read by Rey Colette.

It seemed like a good idea at the time. After all, who doesn't like a ripping end-of-the-world novel? And audiobooks about werewolves and vampires and creatures that are a little of both are regularly delivered to this reviewer. After looking at them for a couple of years or so, I finally gave in to temptation. Next time I may reach for a silver bullet instead of the CD player.

The most entertaining of these scarefests is Stephen King's ''Cell." It's more like the old King, the one we knew before retirement and his nasty accident. It's also more like the King of ''The Stand" days than the dreadful ''Dreamcatcher" days. It isn't going to blow your headsets off, but it will keep you listening.

Unfortunately, we've heard a lot of this before. King covered a lot of the same ground in ''The Stand." This time dystopia is created because cellphones have made everyone crazy, though we never really find out why. The cellphone zombies have a telepathic connection, which King used for the good guys in ''The Stand," and the ending is too vague to be satisfying.

Despite all this, ''Cell" moves quickly, the characters are believable, and King is such a masterful yarn spinner that he has no trouble reeling us in. It may not be his best book, but it works well enough to keep us plenty entertained.

The production, however, is nothing to phone home about. Campbell Scott is a great reader, and for the most part he captures Boston accents and working-class sensibilities, as well as the fear and confusion felt by the characters. However, he disappears occasionally and another voice, with completely different ambient noise, fills in a sentence or phrase. It is strange and annoying, and it jolts us right out of the story. Also, Scott mispronounces ''Canobie Lake." People in Wisconsin won't notice, but a lot of East Coasters will. Shame on the producer for not tying up the details and for giving King's book such short shrift.

The main problem with the two werewolf-vampire audiobooks reviewed here is that there is too much sex. Before you accuse me of being a prude, just remember that while sex is always a pleasant diversion in any audiobook, so is plot.

In ''Full Moon Rising," protagonist Riley Jenson is a rare hybrid of werewolf and vampire, though her tendencies are decidedly wolf-like. Her twin brother, Rhoan, has ended up more vampire than werecreature.

Because, as the title tells us, this Australian story happens as the full moon is rising, Riley spends about a week in ''moon heat." You don't need to be a brain surgeon to figure that one out, but after a while you just want to scream, ''Stand up. Put some clothes on. Do something scary."

There is a plot about evil scientists doing bad things with these magical creatures and their DNA, but it is never all that clear what is going on or why -- probably because Keri Arthur is leaving those answers for the sequel. While it is somewhat interesting to hear about the supernatural races incorporated into everyday life, Riley reminds one too much of Laurell K. Hamilton's werewolf-vampire creation, Anita Blake.

No worries when it comes to narrator Tamara Lovatt-Smith. She sounds properly Australian, has a voice that is easy on the ears, and is believable in both the sex scenes and those fraught with danger.

Besides ''Micah," Hamilton has written 12 other novels about the vampire slayer Anita, with the 14th, ''Danse Macabre," due out this June. ''Micah" is shorter than most of her audiobooks and more of a one-off, since there is little plot and a lot of characterization.

Anita has been brought in to raise the dead by the feds, who need a soon-to-be zombie's testimony for a criminal trial. But most of the story, as it were, is about sex. Anita has some supernatural (and difficult to quite fathom) creature inside of her that she must ''feed" with sex. And she does so often.

To be fair, this may not have been the best place to jump into a series, but we were not impressed. Hamilton no doubt appeals to romance and erotica lovers, but it does not take long for the clichés and the constant droning about sex to become tiresome. You may want to roll up the car windows at tollbooths and stoplights, for this is extremely explicit material. That this is heard and not read means that the naughty bits seem more real, more jarring, because you can't skim when you've had enough. Just don't bother if you blush easily.

Narrator Rey Colette has a clear, feminine voice and is expressive without overdoing it. That is important in this sex-overloaded novel, because it would have been easy for her to cross the line and make the delivery campy. Colette lowers her voice when reading male parts and has a no-nonsense approach when Anita is wearing her federal marshal's badge.

Rochelle O'Gorman is publisher and editor in chief of audiobookcafe.com, an online magazine featuring daily reviews, interviews, and articles relating to the audiobook industry.
