Shelf Life

By Jan Gardner  |  March 19, 2006

A poet's sanctum
Through his collection of 10,000 books in 26 languages, Henry Wadsworth Longfellow journeyed around the world and back in time. Now a fascinating new tour at his home in Cambridge focuses on his library.

A poet and professor of modern languages at Harvard, Longfellow spoke eight languages and read 12. In the late 1800s, he produced a 31-volume anthology of poetry from around the world that is out of print. (James Shea, the manager of the Longfellow National Historic Site, said he would be happy to receive an offer from Google to digitize the anthology and put it online.)

Longfellow welcomed to his home such literary stars as Charles Dickens, Oscar Wilde, and Nathaniel Hawthorne, as well as young writers like Edith Wharton, whose work he helped get published.

The next tour is at 6 p.m., March 29. Call the site at 617-876-4491 to reserve a spot.

Green Mt. winner
Northshire Bookstore in Manchester Center, Vt., has been named bookseller of the year by Publishers Weekly. The bookstore, owned by Ed and Barbara Morrow, is marking its 30th year in business. John Irving and Chris Bohjalian are among its fans.

The Morrows started small and kept growing. Two years after opening, they added a floor for children's books. In 1985, they took over an old inn. In 1996, they started the Next Chapter to sell used, rare, and out-of-print books. Three years ago, they doubled the size of the store to 10,000 feet and added a café.

For 14 years, Publishers Weekly has selected one bookstore in the country as bookseller of the year. Past New England winners are Harvard Book Store in Cambridge, Brookline Booksmith, Bunch of Grapes Bookstore on Martha's Vineyard, and R. J. Julia Booksellers in Madison, Conn.

You couldn't look it up
Booksellers in Milwaukee, Berkeley, Calif., and Hamilton, Mont., made up three of the words in ''Word Fugitives: In Pursuit of Wanted Words," by Barbara Wallraff (Collins). The new book offers new words to describe what -- until now -- had been indescribable. ''Anticippointment" means ''looking forward to something you know won't live up to expectations." ''Malindropity" is a ''bad coincidence." And ''licorice books" are ''dark, twisted, and not to everyone's liking -- but those who like them can't get enough." (Carl Lennertz of HarperCollins culled these words. His blog, at publishinginsider.net, ranges far beyond his employer's books.)

Wallraff, a senior editor for The Atlantic Monthly who lives in Brookline, will be at Brookline Booksmith at 7 p.m., Thursday, and at Porter Square Books in Cambridge at 7 p.m., Friday.

Coming out
* ''Our Town: A Heartland Lynching, a Haunted Town, and the Hidden History of White America," by Cynthia Carr (Crown).

* ''Prior Bad Acts," by Tami Hoag (Bantam). Police procedural/thriller.

* ''American Theocracy: The Peril and Politics of Radical Religion, Oil, and Borrowed Money in the 21st Century," by Kevin Phillips (Viking).

* ''A Dirty Job," by Christopher Moore (Morrow). Wacky yarn by ''The Stupidest Angel" author.

* ''Home With God: In a Life That Never Ends," by Neale Donald Walsch (Atria). Final installment of his best-selling ''Conversations with God."

Pick of the week
Cathy Anderson, owner of Briar Patch children's bookstore in Bangor, Maine, recommends ''Hanne's Quest" (Penguin, 2006), by Olivier Dunrea: ''A folk tale in which the smallest hen on a farm undertakes a quest to find magical grains, face the Sea God, and lay golden eggs in order to save her beloved farm. . . . The book . . . bridges that gap between early readers and chapter books."
