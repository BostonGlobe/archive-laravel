Ardent heart

A portrait of Malamud, ruled by the passion to write

By Edith Pearlman  |  March 19, 2006

My Father Is a Book
By Janna Malamud Smith
Houghton Mifflin, 292 pp., $24

''He put writing before all else," says Janna Malamud Smith in ''My Father Is a Book," an affecting, astute, and understated memoir of Bernard Malamud. Smith delays this summation of her parent until three-quarters of the way into the story; by that time the reader understands its unassailable truth.

Malamud, recipient of two National Book awards (for ''The Magic Barrel" in 1959 and ''The Fixer" in 1967) and a Pulitzer Prize (''The Fixer"), flourished in the post-World War II decades as novelist and story writer. He was too outwardly unassuming to become legendary; Smith's accomplishment is not the common one of puncturing an inflated personality but the more difficult one of examining a constrained one. She tells us of a man mastered by and mastering his need to write; she tells us of the effect on personal and family life of that ruling passion.

Malamud was born to immigrant Jewish parents in Brooklyn in 1914. His father owned a sorry grocery store. His mother suffered from depression and paranoia, attempted suicide, was hospitalized, and died when he was 15. His younger brother, always difficult, later became psychotic. Malamud discovered literature at the extraordinary Erasmus Hall High School, modeled after Oxford and staffed by PhDs. He discovered also the pleasure of conversation and of telling jokes.

After his pinched and aching childhood and his enriching high school, he acquired a bachelor's degree at the City College of New York in 1936 and a master's at Columbia University in 1942. He married an Italian-American whose musical and linguistic talents were to be subordinated to housewifery and literary handmaidenship. He fathered two children. Malamud practiced an academic career that began blandly in Oregon and flourished in Bennington, Vt., and was allowed to dwindle as the novels and short stories brought fame and honors and cash. He conducted one or perhaps two extramarital affairs. He suffered a sad decline: A stroke during heart surgery left him diminished. Words eluded him. His talent was lodged with him useless -- but he would not easily have retrieved the Milton sonnet that suggests that phrase. He died in 1986.

Out of and transcending the life came the work: the nine novels, the four collections of stories, the one posthumous book. Smith connects some events of her father's life and of their shared experience to some of his fiction; but the fiction is not her main subject. Her subject is the man: beloved and infuriating parent to her, errant but enduring husband to her mother; teacher, learner, inquirer, observer. Scholars will find the book valuable. Smith is admiring, grateful, gravely ambivalent. Her tone is modest; she does not engage in patho-biography; she neither repudiates nor worships.

She is a lively sociologist. She takes us to the places the family lived in and introduces us to the people they lived among. Corvallis, Ore., with its fresh neat streets and its predictable patterns of life. Rome (a fellowship took the Malamuds there for a year), where Smith's mother, freed from housework and fluent in Italian, noticeably bloomed. Bennington in the 1960s, with its lusty faculty and visiting artists -- teacher-student liaisons seemed to be part of the curriculum. In all these places Malamud could manage only a partial engagement: Writing came first, writing every day, writing not to be interrupted.

''He wrote in blue or black ink . . . on yellow or unlined paper. . . . He spaced the lines [for] correction and would begin each morning by rereading and editing what he'd written the day before. . . . Half a page a day was a good yield. . . . My mother or a college secretary he hired . . . would type up the finished stories or chapters. Then he would rewrite. And rewrite. Usually two or three times, occasionally into the double digits of drafts. His sentences and paragraphs were hard won, the result of considered thought and constant revision. He understood that effort and discipline made up his strong suit . . . that his success had come from 10 percent talent and 90 percent hard work."

Reading, always a joy, was also part of the discipline. He read several hours every day, sometimes simply for pleasure, sometimes to support the book he was working on. He read about artists for ''Pictures of Fidelman" and about czarist Russia for ''The Fixer"; he read about Jews (his Jewish education was sketchy) for the stories; he read biographies of baseball players for ''The Natural." But his admirers know that despite the accuracy this dedicated reading provided, Malamud was not only, and not primarily, a realist. He was a mythmaker, as in ''The Natural," a fabulist, as in ''The Magic Barrel," a symbolist, as in ''The Assistant," a moralist, as in ''The Fixer."

The startling work kept coming, because of or despite the rigid ordering of his hours, his apparently narrow emotional range (the important affair, after a heated beginning, became mostly an affectionate correspondence), and life experiences less exotic than those of his contemporaries -- no combat like Mailer's; no exile from an idyllic homeland like Nabokov's; no drugged wanderings like Kerouac's. A plain man in plain clothing, sad-faced, dyspeptic, Malamud shut himself first into his office and then into the sanctuary of his imagination. From that very rich place he relayed his remarkable tales.

''My Father Is a Book" does what the best reminiscences of artists do: It leads us back to the work.

Edith Pearlman is the author of three collections of stories: ''Vaquita," ''Love Among the Greats," and ''How to Fall."
