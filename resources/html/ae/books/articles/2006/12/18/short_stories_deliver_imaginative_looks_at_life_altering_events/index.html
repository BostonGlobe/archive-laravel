BOOK REVIEW

Short stories deliver imaginative looks at life-altering events

Joanna Scott writes with crystalline prose and a flair for descriptive detail. (KATHRYN LONGENBACH)

By Karen Campbell, Globe Correspondent  |  December 18, 2006

Everybody Loves Somebody
By Joanna Scott
Back Bay Books, 272 pp., $13.99

Joanna Scott , whose 1996 novel "The Manikin " was a finalist for the Pulitzer Prize, is one of the most highly lauded writers of contemporary fiction, with nine books to her credit and a slew of high - profile awards to boot.

Her new short story collection, "Everybody Loves Somebody," shows why her work is so compelling in carefully distilled tales that draw the reader in and hold on tight, yet do so with little flash or fanfare. Written with the clear-eyed, unsentimental voice of the omniscient observer, "Everybody Loves Somebody" mostly traces the stories of seemingly ordinary people in everyday situations that spark small epiphanies and major transformations. Her stories are imaginative, yet ring with insight and a quiet truth.

Scott writes with crystalline prose and a flair for descriptive detail that vividly conjures times, places, and characters. The collection as a whole gathers a startling power and momentum as the stories begin early in the 20th century and graduate to present day. The first stories are more straightforward and linear. In "Heaven and Hell," an estranged father making a surprise visit to his daughter's wedding finds himself accidentally locked in a bathroom before the ceremony. In "Worry," an unexamined marriage is shaken when a couple's two young children play hooky to lose themselves in a Manhattan adventure.

But by the final story, "The Lucite Cane," Scott lets the crooked finger of fate create a "six degrees of separation" type web that ensnares a diverse group of characters into connections that are both improbable yet totally convincing, showing how easily life-altering coincidences can snag even the most resolved.

In the title story, Bob, an advertising executive returning to New York City from a successful contract negotiation upstate, convinces himself he leads a happy, meaningful life. He sees the world in black and white, reassured by the concrete security of numbers. "In his experience over the last decade, desired results have been eighty percent obtainable. Considering the stakes, one hundred percent effort equaling eighty percent return is a reliable formula for growth."

As Bob tools along the highway with the top down on his fancy convertible, he says to himself, "I am happy." But the way Scott coolly lets the story unfold, with little interior reflection or subjective assessment, leads us to understand Bob may be overselling his own contentment, not admitting vulnerability to life's mercurial fragility. When his car gets a flat in the middle of nowhere, necessitating some surreal encounters with several offbeat locals, it derails not only Bob's journey home but his perspective on what's waiting for him there.

The most substantial story, "Or Else," is a convoluted, multi-layered game of "what if" following young Nora Owens through various versions of her life depending on choice and circumstance. It's a little schizophrenic as Scott alters not only consequences but tone and point of view, and it ultimately feels unsatisfying emotionally -- just as we feel invested in one scenario, it's subverted by another. But the provocative format and suspenseful plotlines have a page-turning immediacy, and the section in which Nora wrestles with the idea of having sex with her boyfriend but instead allows herself to be seduced by a professor shows masterful insight into the young female mind. Scott has a perceptive appreciation for human foibles, especially our propensity for self-deception.

And this is perhaps the way in which "Everybody Loves Somebody" makes its strongest impact. Scott is both sharply intuitive and resolutely humanist, and in story after story, she quietly exposes and celebrates our common humanity, showing that through examination and illumination, the layers of the ordinary can be peeled back to reveal the seeds of the remarkable in us all.
