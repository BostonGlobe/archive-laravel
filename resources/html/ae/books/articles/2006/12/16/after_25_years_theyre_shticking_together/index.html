After 25 years, they're shticking together

Friends' Jewish humor book keeps up with the times

By Linda Matchan, Globe Staff  |  December 16, 2006

NEWTON — Twenty-five years ago, William Novak and Moshe Waldoks published ‘‘The Big Book of Jewish Humor,’’ a collection of shticks, curses, jokes, and cartoons by Jewish writers and comedians.

The material ranged from wry social commentary to corny self-deprecating wit. As in: How many Jewish mothers does it take to change a light bulb? None: ‘‘It’s all right. I’ll sit in the dark.’’

The book has stayed popular and has never gone out of print: A generation of 13-year-olds has come of age with it, having received one (or two) as a bar or bat mitzvah present. It’s a convenient gift for recuperating hospital patients, and reference guide for anyone desperate for a joke when they have to make a speech.

But after editing 300 pages of humor credited to the likes of the Marx Brothers, Mad magazine, Lenny Bruce, Mel Brooks, and Woody Allen, Novak and Waldoks figured it was enough already with the Jewish jokes, and they went on to other careers. Novak, who lives in Newton, makes his living helping famous people write their memoirs, including Lee Iacocca, Magic Johnson, Nancy Reagan, and Tim Russert. Waldoks became a rabbi, and is the spiritual leader of Temple Beth Zion in Brookline.

Never did they think they’d be doing it again.

Now, here they are — a little older, a little grayer, still kibitzing — with the 25th anniversary edition of ‘‘The Big Book of Jewish Humor’’ (HarperCollins). It includes a new introduction and nearly 100 new jokes reflecting the new face, and faces, of Jewish humor — among them Calvin Trillin, Jon Stewart, Billy Crystal, and Jonathan Katz. (From Katz: ‘‘I had dinner with my father last night, and I made a classic Freudian slip. I meant to say, ‘Please pass the salt,’ but it came out, ‘You putz, you ruined my childhood.’.’’)

It includes material from ‘‘Seinfeld,’’ ‘‘The Daily Show,’’ and Larry David’s ‘‘Curb Your Enthusiasm’’ on HBO — as when Larry appears in court before a judge named Katz and tries (unsuccessfully) to curry favor with the judge by saying, on what is probably a Tuesday morning, ‘‘Hello, your Honor, and may I say ‘‘Shabbat Shalom.’’ It features jokes from cartoon shows ‘‘South Park’’ and ‘‘The Simpsons,’’ including a story about Krusty the Klown’s adult bar mitzvah. At the Simpson dinner table, Krusty is asked to say grace, and recites the motzi in Hebrew.

HOMER: Hee hee hee hee hee! He’s talking funny-talk!

LISA: No Dad, that’s Hebrew! Krusty must be Jewish.

HOMER: A Jewish entertainer? Get out of here.

LISA: Dad, there are many prominent Jewish entertainers, including Lauren Bacall, Dinah Shore, William Shatner, and Mel Brooks.

HOMER: Mel Brooks is Jewish?

‘‘Who would have guessed the world of Jewish humor would have expanded so much, in the 21st century alone?’’ says Novak.

Who would have guessed, says Waldoks, referring to the Borat movie, that in the year 2006, ‘‘someone would make an anti-anti-Semitic film’’? Or that Jewish jokes — once the mainstay of the stage and borscht belt circuit — would be delivered less by stand-up comedians (or ‘‘comics,’’ to use the new, preferred term) than via the Internet and cable TV?

Says Waldoks: ‘‘The Internet has made joke telling obsolete.’’

‘‘I don’t agree,’’ says Novak.

They do seem to agree, though, that Jewish humor is not only alive and well, but more mainstream, says Waldoks. ‘‘It’s not uncommon now to find overtly Jewish elements in American culture that perhaps would have been more hidden before. You can say that America has gotten more Jewish and Jews have gotten less so.’’

‘‘Jews have gotten more so, ’’ Novak says.

Novak — who is the proud father of B.J. Novak, star of ‘‘The Office’’ — says he can’t remember a time when he didn’t know Waldoks. In addition to their day jobs, they lecture on the Jewish humor circuit. After 25 years, they write in the book’s introduction, they’re ‘‘a little worse for wear. But after all this time we’re still friends, still laughing, and still married — but not to each other.’’

They say that despite the changes in the Jewish joke ‘‘delivery systems,’’ certain categories of Jewish humor have stayed constant over the last 25 years. There are still jokes dealing with social situations, ‘‘such as families, friends, matchmakers,’’ Waldoks says. Also authority figures, rich people, and schnorrers (Yiddish for beggars, or more precisely, moochers).

However, one category of humor that is less popular now, says Waldoks, is jokes about Soviet Jews, who 25 years ago were still newcomers to this country, and outsiders.

‘‘I’m happy to say those jokes are no longer applicable,’’ Novak says.
