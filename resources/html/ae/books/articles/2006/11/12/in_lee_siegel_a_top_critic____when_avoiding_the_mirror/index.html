In Lee Siegel, a top critic -- when avoiding the mirror

By William H. Pritchard  |  November 12, 2006

Falling Upwards: Essays in Defense of the Imagination
By Lee Siegel
Basic Books, 337 pp., $25

In his introduction to this collection of essays, Lee Siegel recounts a moment at a party one winter night when someone came up to him, introduced himself and declared "someday someone is going to sue you for the stuff you write, pal" -- then walked away. A prophetic moment?

At roughly the time his book was published, Siegel was unmasked as someone who under the pseudonym "Sprezzatura" mocked, on his blog, hostile critics while praising himself ("wittier than [ Jon ] Stewart," etc). For this he was temporarily suspended from his position as senior editor at the New Republic, which no longer publishes his blog. Interviewed in The New York Times Magazine he was teased, in relation to " Falling Upwards, " as having taken a fall in another direction. Unrattled, Siegel said he had merely fallen a couple of feet, from his chair onto the floor. The implication was he had already more than picked himself up.

At any rate, the promotion of Lee Siegel has been recent and rapid. His publisher terms him "one of America's pre-eminent cultural critics" (this is his first book) and on the basis of his introduction Siegel would wholeheartedly agree. Not only are the essays subtitled "In Defense of the Imagination," as if he were William Blake or Coleridge come back to tell us all, he has grand hopes for what criticism can do by way of affecting a reader's "intellectual choices" as well as "artists' creative prospects" -- no small ambition. He sees his essays as "witty, and coarse, and antic, and subtle," the products of not just a perceptive fellow but "a mime, an ironist, a buffoon, a provocateur." A very windy introduction, all in all, with the ideal critic -- Siegel that is -- serving the ambitions of the true artist who is struggling to break through "all the travesties, inversions, and deformations of his calling to a rich, unfettered imaginative place." We may be forgiven for asking where, for mercy's sakes, is that? When Siegel closes his introduction with the following invitation -- "Dear reader, accompany me, if you will. I was lonely and asthmatic as a boy" -- I was tempted to re-utter Orwell's words about a poem by A.E. Housman: "Hard cheese, old chap," while letting the book drop soundlessly from my hands.

But this would have been a mistake, for once Siegel ceases to trumpet his news about "the businessification of the arts " and the "entrepreneurizing of the artist," he settles down and becomes, in the 20-odd pieces collected here, a first-class example of the critic, performing his task of comparison and evaluation on a surprising variety of subjects, including painting, film, critical theory, and fiction (the only absence I noted was poetry). He has his admirations and his detestations and is frank in the prosecution of both. Although an air of knowingness pervades a New Yorker who keeps in touch with what's going on (he knows that the directors of " Sex and the City " were both gay men), he can be as "aesthetic" as needed in coming to terms with classical fictionists like Anton Chekhov and D.H. Lawrence, or more recent American ones like Bernard Malamud and Richard Yates. He takes special pleasure, it seems, in making a case for an artwork that in his opinion has been unduly belittled, even trashed by the critics. The outstanding example of such a phenomenon is Stanley Kubrick's last film, " Eyes Wide Shut ," which Siegel calls, " one of the most moving, playful , and complex movies I have ever seen." In an attempt to make good this claim against the detractions of critics, his comments and interpretations are focused and lively. But to say as he does that " Eyes Wide Shut " was dismissed because "Art-phobia is now the dominant sensibility of the official culture" seems tendentious and overblown -- a gesture like the vatic ones from the introduction. Is David Denby, who called the big scene in the movie "the most pompous orgy in the history of the movies," an instance of art-phobia in the official culture? I should think not.

Discussing " Sex and the City " and " The Sopranos, " he sees the former as "an assault on heterosexual romantic hope ," but finds the presence of sex on the show "as unreal as its absence was on network television forty years ago." In the course of his account he singles out the Samantha character played "with atrocious acting" by Kim Cattrall, "who could not stand still and convince you that she is a person standing still," and contrasts her with the Miranda character played by Cynthia Nixon, whom he rightly admires. As for " The Sopranos, " he is better than anyone I've read on it with the exception of Clive James, a critic of wide sweep and excellent sense of humor to whom Siegel may be compared. His literary criticism is best displayed in the appreciative accounts of D.H. Lawrence's little-read novel " The Lost Girl " (though not as Siegel asserts his third but his sixth novel) and of John Updike's collected Bech stories.

By contrast, the prime example of Siegel in the attack mode is the longest essay in the book, "The Gay Science," a thorough dismantling of the claims of "queer theory" as it has recently been practiced in academic circles. Here his prime target is Eve Kosofsky Sedgwick, acknowledged queen of the discipline, whose "project" (favorite term of theorists) in Siegel's judgment always comes down to "outing authors through their writing -- to liberating them from their repressive historical moment." Siegel describes some of the "tortured textual readings" perpetrated by Sedgwick and her followers as a "twisting labyrinth of mad interpretations." "Why am I being so hard, so mean," he asks in mock wonder and answers that "criticism" by contending that "The Gay Science" deadens us to beauty as well as to human suffering. Alluding to Allen Ginsberg's famous poem "America," he concludes by declaring "Queer America, I am putting my straight shoulder to the wheel, but I don't like what this is all about. For this cannot be literary criticism, and this cannot be life, and this cannot be sex." There's, of course, no arguing with such a blanket assertion, but in the eyes of at least this reader, he's absolutely right on.

William H. Pritchard is a professor of English at Amherst College. His most recent book is "Shelf Life."
