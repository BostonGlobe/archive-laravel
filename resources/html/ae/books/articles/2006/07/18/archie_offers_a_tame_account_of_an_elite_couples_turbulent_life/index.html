BOOK REVIEW

'Archie' offers a tame account of an elite couple's turbulent life

By Carol Iaciofano, Globe Correspondent  |  July 18, 2006

Archie and Amélie: Love and Madness in the Gilded Age, By Donna M. Lucey, Harmony, 339 pp., illustrated, $25.95

"Archie and Amélie," a real-life tale of ``love and madness" in 19th-century high society, has the potential to be as compelling as a smoldering historical novel.

The two lovers met at a Newport, R.I., society ball in the summer of 1887. Archie was the soulful 24-year-old son of the spectacularly wealthy Astor family who yearned to make his own mark in the world. Amélie Rives was a 23-year-old beauty from a storied Virginia family (Robert E. Lee was her godfather) whose reputation had preceded her to Newport. She had created a ``social and literary tempest" with her Atlantic Monthly story that highlighted female desires at a time when upstanding females were not supposed to have any.

Passion, marriage, and scandal quickly ensued, all against a glittery backdrop of New York mansions, Boston townhouses, and Parisian hotels.

Alas, Donna Lucey's flat academic style is no match for these charismatic lives. The narrative often slows to the plodding pace of a term paper, one in which the distinction between major and minor details is often overwhelmed by stacks of facts. This is truly unfortunate, since Lucey has unearthed a great story and mined an impressive range of original letters, articles, and manuscripts.

It is easy to see why she would have been drawn to this little-known couple. Amélie had been a bright, inquisitive child who read widely from her family's bountiful library. She had a sharp eye for social interaction and wrote elaborate stories that were studded with such pre teen observations as ``Married love is like champagne with the sparkles out!"

Archie was a sensitive young man who was determined to appear the sophisticate to his family, even while battling loneliness at boarding schools and even when devastated, at 13, by the loss of his beloved mother.

The Astors were not happy about opening their very proper homes to a notorious author, so Archie and Amélie did what many of today's pop idols do: They sidestepped press and family with a secret wedding (in Virginia, near Amelie's home). During their seven-year marriage, they moved in the most elite social circles: They entertained Oscar Wilde and other celebrities, and Stanford White designed buildings for Archie's business ventures.

Amélie's provocative best-selling novel, ``The Quick or the Dead?" (still in print today), caused a publishing sensation in 1888. Librarians banned the story of a young widow caught between passion and propriety, newspaper editorials denounced it, and Amélie's feverish writing style inspired numerous satires. The dust jacket photo of the young beauty, in her alluringly form-fitting gown, became a favorite pinup of college underclassmen.

Archie was both proud and envious of his wife's success. He was also plagued by a towering temper and inflamed by (unfounded) anxieties about the devotions of his wife's male fans.

Amélie had her own demons. The woman who could command the spotlight at dinner parties and was a master at being interviewed -- she ``specialized in . . . sexually charged language while posing as an innocent" -- seemed unable to achieve any kind of intimacy with her husband.

The marriage begun in white-hot ardor quickly cooled to frustration, and the couple who had often graced the front page of The New York Times devolved into a Gilded Age version of Page Six luminaries. Amélie increasingly turned to opium and morphine to induce creativity. Archie's business dealings couldn't keep him from dabbling, and then plunging into, bizarre paranormal experiments. These personal pursuits so unnerved his brothers that they had him kidnapped and committed to an insane asylum for a few years.

Maddeningly, just as you're drawn into the latest dramatic twist, Lucey pulls you from the narrative with an extended side trip about an inconsequential relative or tacks on a superfluous sentence of her own to a long quote from a letter or article.

For all the intrigue and heartbreak contained in this tale of an arresting romance, you may close ``Archie and Amélie" thinking that with a little more focus, it could have been an exceptional read.
