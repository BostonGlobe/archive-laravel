ON SPORTS

The enigma of Ruth; the subtext of soccer

By Bill Littlefield  |  July 2, 2006

The answer to the question ``Why do we need another biography of Babe Ruth?" is, ``Because Leigh Montville hadn't written one yet.'`

Ruth as icon is also Ruth as cartoon, and the achievement of this book is the presentation of Babe Ruth as more fascinating and, weirdly, less accessible than he has previously appeared to be. Early in ``The Big Bam," Montville quotes from a letter that Waite Hoyt, a teammate of Ruth's, sent to previous Ruth biographer Robert Creamer. ``I am convinced you will never learn the truth on Ruth," Hoyt wrote, and Montville posits this is because Ruth himself had such a heavy stake in keeping his early life mysterious. The Babe floated various versions of it, in part because the truth was painful enough that he had no interest in recalling it. Ruth was a full-time, long-term resident of an institution meant to be a part-time, short-term solution for families with acute problems. When he finally left St. Mary's School in his late teens, Montville supposes, Ruth felt entitled to pursue the childhood he'd never known. He spent most of the rest of his life glutting the appetites that had gone unappeased, and for the most part he was loved for his excesses. (Not, it should be noted, by everybody. The knife-wielding woman who chased the Babe through the Yankees' railroad car was apparently less than thrilled by his presence.)

Ban Johnson, who was the president of the American League during part of Ruth's career, once said of the most dominant player in what was then the most dominant game that ``he has the mind of a 15-year-old." Montville argues that ``this is probably the best appraisal of the man in all of the words spoken or written." But he also points out that Ruth was heroic in the sense that he bore the dreams of millions, and sad because once he could no longer play, he became ``an inhabitant of that strange twilight that exists for men who have accomplished all they can early in life and will accomplish no more."

Though the 2006 World Cup has nearly run its course, ``The Thinking Fan's Guide to the World Cup" remains a worthy read. Editors Matt Weiland and Sean Wilsey invited 32 writers to approach in any way they pleased the 32 countries represented in the competition when it began on June 9, and the results are spectacularly varied.

Some of the essays make only passing reference to soccer. Eric Schlosser's musings on Sweden begin with Fyodor Dostoyevsky's observation that ``the degree of civilization in a society can be judged by entering its prisons." Jorge G. Castaneda opines of Mexico that a ``penchant for belief in magic, or more forthrightly, wishful thinking," has a good deal to do with the belief of the Mexican team and its fans ``that somehow, some way, things will come out all right, but that little can be done in order to achieve that desirable outcome." The extent to which that brand of magical thinking explains Mexico's ability to emerge from round one, then fall to Argentina, won't necessarily be debated by the federation officials determining who will next coach the Mexican side, but it makes for intriguing reading.

The essays in this book have in common uncommon intelligence, and ``The Thinking Fan's Guide " is blessedly free of sports jargon. It will be a shame if nobody reads it after the World Cup, and another shame if the book's sales aren't sufficient to encourage Weiland, Wilsey, and their publisher to undertake the project again for 2010. (See Page K7 for an interview with Wilsey.)

At one point in ``Full Swing," a memoir, longtime New York Times sports columnist Ira Berkow seems concerned that he earns his daily bread by chronicling the triumphs and stumbles of eternal adolescents. He compares himself with his cousin, a lawyer ``dealing with the most monumental and noble of issues, a man's freedom." Berkow eventually finds his way to a rationalization for his own career path: by writing about such concerns as Tonya Harding's right to be assumed innocent (and allowed to skate in the Olympics) until proven guilty, he, too, has established himself as a player in a game larger than sports. While Berkow is certainly entitled to that argument, it's too bad he feels compelled to make it. Writing honestly and well about anything shouldn't require excuses, especially if the writer is also able to hand his readers the occasional laugh, which Berkow has certainly done.

Tom Coyne does it, too. His vehicle is ``Paper Tiger," the account of his 18-month attempt to see just how much he can improve his golf game. He moves to Florida, seeks the advice of swing coaches and psychologists, and hits balls off the rubber mat of the driving range until (to the horror of his girlfriend) his fingers bleed.

As you have not seen Coyne's name in even the ``did not qualify"-sized type presenting the results of the Yoyo Open, you probably know he didn't make the PGA Tour . . . or any tour. On the other hand, he has written an entertaining book and, as he recognized at the end of his quest, achieved the satisfaction of knowing he has given his dream a try.

``I took my shot," he writes. ``I took millions of them."

Bill Littlefield hosts NPR's ``Only a Game" each Saturday from WBUR in Boston.
