POP LIT

A go-between, an odd couple, and a feline mystique

By Diane White  |  July 9, 2006

Between, Georgia
By Joshilyn Jackson
Warner, 294 pp., $22.99

Ten Second Staircase
By Christopher Fowler
Bantam, 368 pp., $24

Dark Side of the Moon
By Sherrilyn Kenyon
St. Martin’s, 322 pp., $19.95

None of these three funny, entertaining novels is predictable. The first, a Southern family story, is remarkably free of regional clichés. The second is a wildly original and very urbane mystery. The third involves soul-sucking vampires and a human-undead love story.

A good novel about family relationships should leave the reader thinking that perhaps his or her family is no crazier than anybody else's, maybe even slightly saner than some. On that count, and a number of others, Joshilyn Jackson's tart, warm-hearted second novel, ``Between, Georgia," is a winner.

Nonny Frett is at the center of a feud involving two Between, Ga. (pop. 91), families, the upstanding Fretts and the feckless Crabtrees. Thirty years ago desperate 15-year-old Hazel Crabtree took refuge in the Frett home long enough to deliver a baby girl -- Nonny -- on the den carpet. Hazel gladly turned Nonny over to Stacia, one of the three Frett sisters, who adopted her. A few years later Hazel fled town with a circus roustabout, leaving a note for her volatile, heavy-drinking mother, Ona, telling her that the grandchild she never knew she had was stolen by the Fretts. The feud was launched, with Nonny caught in the middle, the hapless victim in a bitter tug of war.

Nonny lives in Athens, the university town, about an hour's drive from Between. She's preoccupied by her imminent divorce from her philandering husband when she gets a call informing her that Ona 's vicious Doberman has attacked her adoptive mother, Stacia, now deaf and blind, and Stacia's twin sister, Gena. Nonny races back to Between and once again finds herself caught between the warring families. The crisis forces Nonny to face up to some of her own shortcomings and limitations, as well as those of her two families.

It's difficult to imagine more engaging crime fighters than Arthur Bryant and John May of the Peculiar Crimes Unit, a division of London's Metropolitan Police Force distinguished by its unorthodox approach to criminal detection. In ``Ten Second Staircase," the fourth in this offbeat series by Christopher Fowler, the two elderly detectives investigate the murder of controversial guerrilla artist Saralla White, drowned in her art-gallery installation, a pro-choice statement involving faux fetuses floating in a 12-foot-high tank of formaldehyde. The only witness, a 14-year-old schoolboy, swears the perpetrator was a large man wearing a cape, a mask, a tricorner hat, and riding a horse.

The artist's drowning is the first in a series of murders of D-list London celebrities. A dissipated TV host, a washed-up actor, and an inept politician turned newspaper columnist are serially knocked off in various ingenious and unpleasant ways. As Bryant and May apply themselves to solving the crimes, their superiors are plotting to do away with the PCU and its superannuated investigators, whose methodology is regarded as ``altogether too vague, intellectual, socialist, and downright arty."

Bryant is a cranky, brilliant intellectual . May, the more rational and organized of the two, is haunted by the unsolved case of the Leicester Square Vampire . The plot is laced with fascinating facts and legends about London crimes and criminals, ancient and modern. ``Ten Second Staircase" is an intelligent crime novel, intricately plotted, with a cast of finely imagined characters. It's written with wit, style, and a keen awareness of social problems. Altogether, it's a treat.

Sherrilyn Kenyon's ``Dark-Hunter" series is the hottest thing in the paranormal romance genre, a subcategory of romantic fiction I confess I'd never encountered before picking up ``Dark Side of the Moon," her latest novel and the first in the series to appear in hardback. Kenyon's writing is brisk, ironic, sexy, and relentlessly imaginative. These are not your mother's vampire novels.

Reporter Susan Michaels, fired by The Washington Post, is reduced to working for a Seattle tabloid that specializes in stories about Elvis sightings. Her editor assigns her to check out a young woman who claims to be a kind of valet to a vampire hunter. Susan's investigation leads her to an animal shelter where she is urgently prevailed upon to adopt a cat. When the creature in question morphs into a handsome man in her living room, Susan, ever the journalist, coolly composes suitable tabloid headlines: ``Cat Turns Into Stunning Naked Man in Single Woman's Home . . . Women Stampeding Local Animal Shelters Everywhere."

Ravyn, the shape-shifting hunk, is a Dark-Hunter who, due to circumstances too complicated to relate here, is an immortal vampire slayer, sworn to protect the human race from bloodthirsty Daimons who prey on them. There's plenty of sex and violence and a large -- and confusing, to uninitiated readers like me -- cast of characters that includes Dark-Hunters, Apollites , and Were-Hunters, as well as ordinary humans. The interspecies love scenes between Ravyn and Susan are easier to follow. Between sneezes (she's allergic to cats), she feels ``the soft tickle of his fangs against her neck" and wonders how to introduce him to her friends: ``This is my . . . what? Significant other? Mate? Pet?" ``Dark Side of the Moon" isn't for everyone. But there is something weirdly appealing about a hero who is half-man, half-cat, and a vampire slayer to boot.

Diane White writes every month about new light and popular fiction.
