BOOK REVIEW

Meddling with the government's sacred cows

By Cecil Johnson, Mcclatchy Newspapers  |  July 16, 2006

Unwarranted Intrusions: The Case Against Government Intervention in the Marketplace
by Martin Fridson
John Wiley & Sons
309 pages, $24.95

A herd of sacred cattle gets scorched in this scathing look at how politicians kowtow to special interests.

In ``Unwarranted Intrusions," professional investor, business writer, and commentator Martin Fridson brands many of the policies pushed by politicians to supposedly benefit middle- and low-income Americans as a ``Robin Hood in reverse" hijacking of American taxpayers.

Fridson makes the reverse Robin Hood allusion while excoriating the public financing of sports facilities.

``Stripped to its essentials, public financing of stadiums amounts to taxing ordinary citizens to increase the wealth of team owners ," Fridson wrote.

He examines many of the stadiums that have been funded in large measure with public money and focuses at length on the financing of the Texas Rangers' stadium, a deal that required a hefty increase in Arlington's sales tax to cover the $135 million in construction costs the city had to guarantee.

He notes that the Rangers put up no cash. The team paid its share through a ticket surcharge and ceding some land back to Arlington that it received years earlier from the city as an inducement to relocate there.

When billionaire Richard Rainwater and his partners sold the team in 1998, Fridson points out, they pocketed $250 million -- three times what the franchise was worth when they bought it in 1989. He adds that the $606,000 George W. Bush borrowed to invest in the deal yielded him $15 million.

Public subsidies for sports facilities, however, are not the most sacrosanct bovines lassoed by Fridson in this roundup of regulation and taxation hypocrisy. Right off the bat, he goes after the deductibility of home-mortgage interest.

``If mortgage deductibility didn't already exist, but was being proposed honestly to voters, low-to-average income voters would hardly be likely to endorse a plan that runs directly counter to the progressive principle of the federal income tax," Fridson wrote.

After thoroughly examining the arguments for and against the incentive, Fridson concludes that its main beneficiaries are the affluent, the real-estate industry, Fannie Mae, and Freddie Mac.

Moreover, Fridson says, there is not conclusive evidence that the deductibility boosts homeownership.

Not content with preaching about politicized ballparks and housing incentives, Fridson has the audacity to meddle with the most sacred of cows -- agricultural subsidies. Those incentives, Fridson wrote, are supposedly in place to save small family farms, but they really fatten the larders of the agribusiness corporate giants.

In that context, Fridson even dares to criticize mandating the mixture of corn-derived ethanol in gasoline to supposedly reduce US dependence on foreign oil. He said that it takes significantly more energy to produce a gallon of ethanol than that gallon of ethanol will yield.

With this well-researched and impeccably reasoned book, Fridson challenges Americans to rethink many government programs and policies. ``Unwarranted Intrusions" merits inclusion in the short list of the best business books of the year.

Make that the decade.
