CRITIC'S VIEW

Beyond measure

'Best'? 'Favorite'? Or 'Beloved'? The books we cherish defy a single standard.

By Gail Caldwell  |  July 16, 2006

Whenever I do any public speaking, I am inevitably struck by the gregarious nature of the book-loving masses. We are supposed to be a shy lot, according to legend and stereotype: shuffling, myopic people who prefer text to other people, or dream-catchers who mumble thoughtfully about arcane theories and highbrow literary trends. 'Twas never so, from my poll of the lively audiences. Critics, too, have a narrow reputation; last year, a lovely independent bookseller said to me, by way of compliment, ``You're so friendly, you don't seem like a reviewer at all!" Realizing her own friendly gaffe, she qualified it: ``I mean, you're not -- well, you seem more like a book seller."

Well, quite so, I suppose I am a book seller, inasmuch as I tout my wares without attention to anything but merit. Critics aren't just hired to sling mud. And inevitably, too, questions from the audience arise about what I like and why I like it -- queries that can range from the sublime to the ridiculous, or prankish. (My sister, baiting me once from an audience in New Mexico, chortled, ``Tell them why you like trash TV!") Usually, though, the question of taste is a doorway to a larger conversation -- about aesthetic considerations, mass culture, the novel as window into the soul of the reader.

Unsurprisingly, I've heard a lot of questions recently about The New York Times Book Review poll, published in late May, attempting to assess the best American fiction from the past 25 years. For readers who've missed this little tempest, the Book Review asked 200 writers, critics, and editors to pick one work from that time range; 124 people answered the letter, and the announced winner was Toni Morrison's ``Beloved " -- which received 15 votes, more than any other choice. The short list included Don DeLillo (``Underworld," 11 votes), Cormac McCarthy and John Updike (respectively, ``Blood Meridian" and the Rabbit Angstrom tetralogy, eight votes each), and Philip Roth (``American Pastoral," seven votes). The most illuminating part of the survey (which, for my money, seemed scattershot and ill conceived) was not the vote, but the essay by A. O. Scott that accompanied it -- a smart, comprehensive performance about American fiction's paths and pitfalls over the past few decades.

People have been railing and caterwauling about the poll ever since the Times began publicizing it a couple of weeks before publication. At a recent BookExpo America panel in Washington, D.C., Cynthia Ozick dared to suggest that ``Beloved" was more historical piece than literature; she had nominated the largely unreadable ``Carpenter's Gothic," by William Gaddis. The London Telegraph reported, somewhat hysterically, that the literary right (who that is remains a mystery) was up in arms over the selection. Roth defenders argue that he was the real winner, with his 21 votes split among seven novels; Roth haters point out that no one novel made the grade. John Irving, we are deeply amused to report, announced that he voted for his own `` The Cider House Rules." (Why? ``A Prayer for Owen Meany" is a better book.) The National Book Critics Circle website has kept a running tally of the feints and jabs, along with personal-essay justifications (why Rick Moody voted for Grace Paley, why Laura Miller didn't vote). And the Times, of course, has glory-danced from all the conversation, even though Book Review editor Sam Tanenhaus has continued to downplay the poll's reach for higher meaning.

The subject came up again last month at a forum in Newton, a town that holds a lot of literary brain power, when an agent provocateur in the back of the room asked me what I thought about the poll. I said I thought the results were specious -- that only 62 percent of the people queried had bothered to answer the letter, that they had only been allowed one vote each, which skewed the odds, and that the winner had received less than 10 percent of the overall vote. Unsatisfied with my reply, the fellow pressed on. ``Let me get this straight," he said with feigned incredulity. ``You're only questioning its methodology?"

Point being, I assume, that I should have hated the whole notion of such a poll. I don't, at all: That would be tantamount to sneering at every end-of-the-year best list that self-appointed ministers of culture (myself included) have to summon each December, or dismissing the scores of qualitative judgments that call attention to books, from Oprah's Book Club to the Swedish Academy's Nobel wreath. No, I tolerate and even celebrate such artificial divisions, even though I would like to believe, as E. L. Doctorow once opined, that literature is not a horse race. The fact is that it may have become one. Thanks to huge publishing advances , the field nowadays is glutted with couldabeens, also-rans, wannabes, and a lot of miniature ponies that should have been left at home.

And with 150,000 books published each year, somebody has to thin the herd. That's where reviewers -- and booksellers, book clubs, prizes, polls, and your own Aunt Margie -- come in. I know one snobbish writer who, whenever a stranger would ask her for a reading recommendation, would smile thinly and reply, ``Try `Middlemarch.' " I hope my own response is broader and kinder: Read what you like, what your friends like, what a trustworthy critic recommended this season. Read ``Charlotte's Web," or ``Humboldt's Gift," or ``Parade's End," or Michael Chabon and Ian McEwan and Zadie Smith and Rachel Cusk and Claire Messud and Colm Toibin and . . . oh good Lord, even Irving, I suppose.

But then quis custodiet ipsos custodes (who will guard the guardians) ? Parlor games like the Times's keep us hopping and keep us honest. They make us invent our own alternative lists: My hilarious and wise colleague, Katherine Powers, making the essential distinction between ``best" and ``favorite ," polled friends and relatives, and thus got in a plug in her column for James, Flaubert, Twain , and Proust. (We think we know who chose Proust, and he should know.) I would further like to suggest a carriage house to the mansion here: All the less showy writers who are never going to be named number one. These keepers of the flame chose a smaller piece of the world to cherish and convey, and thus will always come in second or third or seventh to ``The Portrait of a Lady" or ``Underworld." And yet they're part of the essential scaffolding of this thing called literature -- ``The Good Soldier," say, or ``All Quiet on the Western Front," or ``Brideshead Revisited ," or ``The Moviegoer." Marilynne Robinson's ``Housekeeping," Raymond Carver's ``Cathedral," Pat Barker's ``Regeneration." Each of these books changed the world a little bit every time someone picked it up and wept or wrote an essay for English class or felt that somewhere, in some other place, a writer had understood.

Which is part of a larger point that both my heckler and I were trying to make in Newton: No one vote or ranking can ever encompass the power and glory of what we love about the books we love. In that spirit of far-flung camaraderie, it helps to remember, whatever one thinks about ``Beloved," that Morrison has written and spoken over the years about the deep and pervasive influence of three writers on her work: William Faulkner, Virginia Woolf, and Gabriel García Márquez. She read García Márquez in translation from the Spanish, and anyone who has read the earlier Morrison novels, particularly ``Song of Solomon," can trace the rhapsodic influences of the magical realists in her work. So maybe the last hurrah should go to Gregory Rabassa and every other world-class translator, without whom the vast estate of world literature would be a far less verdant plain -- its citizenry still caught in the provincial village of solitary consciousness.

Gail Caldwell is chief book critic of the Globe. She can be reached at caldwell@globe.com.
