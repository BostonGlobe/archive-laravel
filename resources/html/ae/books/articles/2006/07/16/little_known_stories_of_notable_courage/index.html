FOR CHILDREN

Little-known stories of notable courage

By Liz Rosenberg  |  July 16, 2006

Seven Brave Women
Written by Betsy Hearne
Illustrated by Bethanne Andersen
Greenwillow, 24 pp., ages 5-10, paperback, $6.99

Good Fortune: My Journey to Gold Mountain
By Li Keng Wong
Peachtree, 136 pp., ages 8-14, $14.95

Counting Coup: Becoming a Crow Chief on the Reservation and Beyond
By Joseph Medicine Crow
National Geographic, 128 pp., ages 10 and up, $15.95

This month's column could be called ``Stories You Might Never Have Heard," for each book offers an important American story gone too long untold. In ``Seven Brave Women," author Betsy Hearne remarks, ``In the old days, history books marked time by the wars that men fought. . . . My mother does not believe that wars should be fought at all. She says history should be her story, too, and she tells stories about all the women in our family who made history by not fighting in wars."

``Seven Brave Women" vibrantly delineates the rich lives of seven American women, from great-great-great grandmother Elizabeth, who ``lived during the Revolutionary War, but . . . did not fight in it," to the young female narrator who notes, ``There are a million ways to be brave."

Elizabeth's daughter, great-great-grandmother Eliza, ``made candles and soap and bread and butter and jam and everything else that now you can buy at the store." Once, when a knife slipped and cut her finger open, ``she used the other hand to sew it up with a needle and thread."

The other family women ``did great things, too." One rode all day to take art lessons in town; another journeyed to India as a missionary; still another wrote books about buildings and builders. Grandmother Margaret lived in the same house on Queens Boulevard all her life, but ``took in dogs and cats that had been left to starve in the street."

Bethanne Andersen's oil paintings add color, movement, and whimsy to Hearne's elegant prose. The only flaw is the book's artificial-seeming child narrator, who has the awkward job of talking about her mother (obviously the author) and herself. Still, ``Seven Brave Women" is a glorious celebration of real-life heroines. Families everywhere have great women, and all libraries deserve this award-winning book.

``Good Fortune: My Journey to Gold Mountain," by Li Keng Wong, offers more real-life heroism, revealed through one family's journey during the 1930s, a time of strict anti-Chinese immigration laws. Wong's father was first to come to ``Gold Mountain" -- the Chinese honorific for the United States -- in hopes of forging a better life. But to get here, families often had to lie about their relations -- Wong's mother pretended to be the children's aunt.

Some stories here are familiar -- the long boat ride over -- while others will be fresh and new. I'd never heard of Angel Island, ``Ellis Island of the West," where Wong's family is confined in prison-like barracks as family members are questioned, one by one.

Wong's memoir touchingly reminds us how much we take for granted. The third-class ship compartment is a ``wonderful sight . . . very clean, with a round porthole, two bunk beds, a wall washbasin, electric lights, and a toilet." In San Francisco, the children eat take out food for the first time and marvel at their new home: ``There were three single beds in the girls' room. They had matching blankets, pillows, and mattresses. More luxury! Our beds in China had been flat wooden boards on top of sawhorses."

I admire Wong's unadorned prose, her capacity for creating tension and excitement in this family story, and sympathy for those with whom we might not always feel sympathetic -- after all, the family lies to immigration officials, runs an illegal gambling house, and falls into debt. Yet their determination to arrive at an honest and worthy life shows us that the roots of our most beautiful accomplishments are often humble. Wong doesn't dissemble about any of it, and that is part of the beauty of this honest and moving book.

``Counting Coup: Becoming a Crow Chief on the Reservation and Beyond," by Joseph Medicine Crow, depicts another childhood rich in imagination, experience, and stories. Medicine Crow grew up in what he calls ``hard times" for the Crow Reservation Indians of Montana. ``In those days Crow families didn't have furniture. There were no beds. Everyone slept on the floor on blankets. . . . When friends came to visit, they would sit on the rolled-up beds." Softballs were constructed ``out of buckskin and stuffed with fur from a deer's tail." In Crow society things have value because they have meaning, not the other way around.

Medicine Crow's spare, clear prose underscores the drama, pathos, and occasional hilarity of his stories. Grandfather Yellowtail raised him to be ``tough and strong like the old-time warriors." So the boy had to run barefoot circles outside the house in winter, roll in snow, bathe in the icy Little Horn River. Other experiences were tougher, like suffering the Baptist school nearby, and then, when his grandfather One Star abruptly removed him -- ``Hoh! Come! . . . I'm going to take you to the white man's school in town. You're just wasting your time here" -- the public school in Lodge Grass, where a white girl behind him poked him with her safety pin ``40, 50 times" and where Crow children isolated themselves further: ``If we saw one of our Crow classmates trying to speak English to a white kid, we would say, ` Look at him, trying to be a white man.' We would tease him. Sometimes we would beat him up. We were afraid to use the English language, so we punished ourselves." At age 10, Medicine Crow ``still couldn't read, couldn't write, couldn't even do my ABC's or my numbers." That he grew up to become a hero of World War II, and the first member of his tribe to earn his master's degree, is nothing short of breathtaking. Indeed, the story of his war experiences forms the climax and high point of this fast-paced book.

One becomes a Crow chief by completing ``the four coups. . . . There were no shortcuts. Each coup involved risking one's life." They are: touching an enemy fallen in battle; taking away an enemy's weapon; leading a successful war party; and, most glorious of all, ``to sneak into an enemy camp at night and capture a prized horse." The story of how Medicine Crow managed to ride off with a Nazi horse is worth, as they say, the price of admission, but there are other wonderful stories, about ghosts in hospitals, the Battle of Little Bighorn, and Wise Man, who floated from a high cliff to escape his enemies. Thank heavens for these little-known stories and the authors who have rescued them for us.

Liz Rosenberg reviews children's books monthly for the Globe.
