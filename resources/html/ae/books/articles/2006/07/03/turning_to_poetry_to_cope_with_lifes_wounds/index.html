BOOK REVIEW

Turning to poetry to cope with life's wounds

By Karen Campbell, Globe Correspondent  |  July 3, 2006

Blue Peninsula: Essential Words for a Life of Loss and Change
By Madge McKeithen
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 240 pp.,
$22

By its very nature, poetry has a distilled power and immediacy, and for the ardent reader, it is more than just a rarefied literary form. Carefully considered and impeccably arranged , words can lend solace, insight, revelation, and inspiration. For Madge McKeithen, poetry has become a kind of lifeline during a parental crisis that has transformed her life. In her darkest moments, she has turned to poetry for companionship and strength amid the chaos and ambiguity of her life. For her, poetry has offered both flights of fancy and moments of profound truth.

With ``Blue Peninsula," which McKeithen subtitles ``Essential Words for a Life of Loss and Change," the author chronicles her profound relationship with poetry and makes a brave attempt to connect to poetry with the larger world, providing a way for others to be part of a dialogue. ``I wanted to create something that could be put into the hands of someone facing the unknown, the way I was."

The great unknown for McKeithen was a mysterious degenerative illness that her son contracted eight years ago, at 14. Doctors were unable to make any kind of definitive diagnosis, nor were they able to offer any signs of hope or comfort. As McKeithen traces the sad journey, she shares and illumines the poetry that offers her solace during her grief for her son's slow demise. ``I needed just to know that another someone had experienced something with a shape or sound or smell or texture or hue like my sorrow, the deep-to-the-soul uncertainty, the hunger I suspected might never by satisfied."

McKeithen's is a sobering yet compelling story. At the book's completion, her son is still alive, and she bounces back and forth in time, as if trying to gain perspective, trying to make some sense of it all. It is difficult to wade through at times -- resignation and hope make for uneasy bedfellows. But the writing is vivid, sometimes eloquently metaphorical, other times blazingly direct.

As she incorporates poems into the story, poems that have resonated strongly with where she has been, where she is headed, McKeithen often deconstructs line by line, examining each carefully chosen phrase. But more often, a poem provides an immediate, almost visceral portal to an overwhelming feeling or to coping with a specific experience. She has eclectic tastes, from John Donne and Scottish poet George MacDonald to Marianne Moore and e. e. cummings, and with some poems, her interpretations offer illumination or provocation.

In response to David Whyte's ``The Well of Grief," McKeithen offers a searing, insightful diatribe on well-wishers, whose best intentions often make the receiver feel painfully alone in grasping the severity of loss. Emily Dickinson's ``Without the Loneliness," from which McKeithen derives the book's title, urges the author to question her most basic responses to her circumstances. ``What might I be conveniently, timidly avoiding by staying encased in painful, familiar, safe victimhood?" she writes.

On rare occasion, as with Billy Collins's ``To Bleed," McKeithen almost seems to miss the point, not quite appreciating the big picture in the throes of intense personalization. Yet as each reader comes to a poem from his or her own place, how can interpretations not be different, not be intensely personal? Sometimes the power of poetry lies in its ambiguity -- we each take from it what we need at the moment.
