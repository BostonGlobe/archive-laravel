BOOK REVIEW

Magnificent biography illuminates how Rousseau shaped our world

By Alan Helms, Globe Correspondent  |  April 12, 2006

Jean-Jacques Rousseau: Restless Genius, By Leo Damrosch, Houghton Mifflin, 566 pp., illustrated, $30

Leo Damrosch's splendid ''Jean-Jacques Rousseau" represents an act of courage for the biographer; Rousseau is a daunting subject. As Damrosch says, ''Many people have found his personality exasperating. He was unreliable and temperamental, highly demanding of those around him, with a noble self-image that clashed with his everyday behavior. He was a difficult friend, a disappointing lover, and an impossible employee."

He was also thin-skinned and narcissistic, and suffered most of his life from a persecution mania so severe he was sometimes taken for insane. And he was odd man out among the philosophes, those bright ''lights" of 18th-century Paris, for in a world of sociable people he verged on the misanthropic; in a culture that prized wit, he was slow and ponderous in conversation; in a crowd that thrived on the cultural stimulations of Paris, he despised the city.

But there was another Rousseau, summed up by a friend who said that besides his abundant faults, he was ''gay, humane, compassionate, sensitive, frank, friendly and confiding." The contradictions are those of many shy, diffident people, and it's to Rousseau's undying credit that he never denied them. In an era when people worked overtime to present a coherent character, Rousseau recognized that a seamless self could be achieved only at the cost of the truth, and he was determined to pursue the truth as he saw it wherever it led him. It usually led him into trouble and even exile, but he hung on like a dog to a bone. He's an exemplar of Blake's proverb ''If the fool would persist in his folly he would become wise."

Rousseau became wise in so many ways it's impossible to imagine our world without him. Whenever we repair to nature to restore ourselves or analyze a friend's behavior or try to live more fully in the present, Rousseau is looking over our shoulder. Except for him, we might not have psychoanalysis or children's playgrounds, memoir or autobiography, kindergarten or anthropology. As Damrosch says, he was ''the most original genius of his age."

He was born in Geneva in 1712. His mother died in childbirth, and his father, an irascible watchmaker, left the family when Rousseau was 10. This double abandonment made intimate relationships difficult for Rousseau, and the fact that we find this easy to believe results from Rousseau's discovery that important childhood experiences profoundly affect the rest of our lives. Apprenticed at 12 to an engraver, he fled four years later and began a peripatetic life that in the next 15 years saw him as a music teacher, domestic servant, seminarian, land office clerk, tutor, translator, and secretary to the French ambassador to Venice. Aside from an affair with a rich older woman who gave him the books and leisure he needed to educate himself, those were largely uneventful years. But Rousseau was a hedgehog, not a fox, and inwardly his thoughts began to take shape in response to a question that would haunt him for the rest of his life: Why is everyone unhappy?

Settling in Paris at the age of 32, he formed close friendships with Denis Diderot and Jean le Rond D'Alembert, contributed over 400 articles on music to their revolutionary encyclopedia, and composed an opera that so delighted Louis XV that the king asked to meet him, a request virtually guaranteeing a pension. Rousseau stood him up. In this same period Rousseau also formed a lifelong relationship with Thérèse Levasseur, a laundress who bore the five children Rousseau gave to a foundling hospital, a fact later revealed by his enemies to his eternal embarrassment and shame. But the great event of his mid-30s was discovering an answer to his question about human unhappiness, which was his conviction that all human ills are created by our civilization. We are by nature good, but society corrupts us by teaching us that the only way to thrive is through deceit.

This radical perception released Rousseau's genius and led to the works for which he is famous: ''Julie" (the best-selling novel of the 18th century), the educational novel ''Émile," ''The Social Contract," and his posthumously published ''Confessions," the first memoir. Cantankerous to the end, Rousseau soon learned to hate the fame his writings brought him, but given that his life's work involved exposing the impediments to human happiness, it's good to know that he himself enjoyed a relatively contented old age.

Damrosch's biography is a magnificent accomplishment. It's supremely intelligent and well written, exhaustively researched, well shaped, and judiciously evenhanded. Damrosch allows Rousseau to speak in his own words as much as possible, and his extended comparison between Rousseau and his American antithesis Benjamin Franklin is illuminating. Damrosch occasionally nods (Louis XIV was succeeded not by his 5-year-old son but his 5-year-old great-grandson), and here and there I wished for some explanation, as when we're told that Rousseau was one of the great French stylists without learning why. But these are minor flaws, and unlike Rousseau, I will not bite the hand that feeds me. This is one of the best biographies I've ever read, and I hope it brings Damrosch the abundant happiness he deserves.

Alan Helms is professor of English emeritus at the University of Massachusetts at Boston, and the critic at large for In Newsweekly.
