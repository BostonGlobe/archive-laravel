BOOK REVIEW

Comfortable 'Shoes' extends gentle series

By Clea Simon, Globe Correspondent  |  April 10, 2006

Blue Shoes and Happiness
Alexander McCall Smith
Pantheon Books, 240 pp., $21.95

With some serials, by the time the seventh book appears, the inevitable question is ''Have these titles lost their edge?"

With Alexander McCall Smith's ''No. 1 Ladies' Detective Agency" books, edge is not an issue. The books, which feature the ''traditionally built" Mma Precious Ramotswe, never had the sharpness -- whether from anger, anxiety, or sense of danger -- that propels other mysteries.

What Mma Ramotswe offers up instead, and why these books continue to be so readable, is warmth, a sort of folksy hominess that combines the relative exoticism of life in Botswana (where Mma Ramotswe's detective agency is located) with common-sense humanity. Escapist in both senses, these books let us into a world where even the worst problems are usually resolvable and most are as minor as, say, a fight with a fiance or the question of whether to lose weight.

That said, ''Blue Shoes and Happiness," the seventh of this series, does start off slow. In part, that's because Mma Ramotswe has gathered quite a crowd around her. After several books of dithering, Mma Ramotswe has wed the mechanic Mr. J.L.B. Matekoni, proprietor of Tlokweng Road Speedy Motors, and they are fostering two children. The bespectacled Mma Makutsi is now an assistant detective and has a fiance, Phuti Radiphuti, to worry about, as well as her growing addiction to shoes. And a new garage employee, Mr. Polopetsi, promises to be more involved, and more intelligent, than Mr. J.L.B. Matekoni's two longtime apprentices. Throw in the revered memory of Mma Ramotswe's father, Obed Ramotswe, and it's a full detective agency/garage. With all these characters to plot for, ''Blue Shoes" is a little unwieldy.

After a brief adventure, however, Mma Ramotswe does get down to some real cases, the first involving a cook who is stealing food for her husband. The case becomes uncharacteristically complicated when the thief reveals that she is being blackmailed, but the real issue is relations between the sexes. That cook and her husband simply echo the closer-to-home drama of Mma Makutsi, who is working things out with her intended. And it is here that McCall Smith finally does get a little edgy, and a little out of his territory.

Mma Makutsi and Mma Ramotswe are self-actualized, self-supporting women. They both believe that ''women made the really important decisions for men, subtly and without letting the men know that they were doing it, but doing it nonetheless." But when an affirmation of feminism by Mma Makutsi appears to threaten her relationship, she finds herself declaring, ''I have always thought that men are particularly important," even as her conscience (as voiced by her shoes) reprimands her. It's a silly little subplot. Can any reader of this series doubt that all will work out? But that resolution nudges the usually comfortable series toward an uncomfortably retrogressive corner.

That one misstep aside, ''Blue Shoes" still seduces. McCall Smith's simple, sweet descriptions of everyday pleasures -- a cup of tea, walking around one's garden -- are enticing. And the sharp wit that laced earlier books remains, often at the portly Mma Ramotswe's expense. ''If Mma Ramotswe, stern and articulate defender of the rights of the fuller-figured as she was, could contemplate going on a diet," one observer wonders, ''then what would happen to the ranks of the traditionally built? They would be thinned." Such sly digs are really all the social commentary this series needs.
