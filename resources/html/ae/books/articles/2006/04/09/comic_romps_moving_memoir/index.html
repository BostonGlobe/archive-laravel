FOR CHILDREN

Comic romps, moving memoir

By Liz Rosenberg  |  April 9, 2006

This Little Chick
Written and illustrated by John Lawrence
Candlewick, 24 pp., board book, ages 1-4, $6.99

Goodnight Lulu
Written and illustrated by Paulette Bogan
Bloomsbury, 32 pp., ages 2-6, paperback, $6.95

Fancy Nancy
Written by Jane O'Connor and illustrated by Robin Preiss Glasser
HarperCollins, 32 pp., ages 4-7, $15.99

Little Green: Growing Up During the Chinese Cultural Revolution
By Chun Yu
Simon & Schuster, 112 pp., ages 13 and up, $15.95

Here's the first breath of spring, and with it the first rush of beautiful new picture books. Most of last year's best books were for older readers; it's heartening to see these new books for younger children showing such promise.

Board books, like certain types of aunts and uncles (though not you or I, dear reader), are notoriously sturdy and dull. A truly witty one is as rare as a mild April day, but here to charm us comes ''This Little Chick," written and illustrated by John Lawrence. It's deceptively simple, from the chanting, familiar rhyme scheme to the old-style vinyl engravings, which look like the colored woodblock prints that used to hang in country kitchens. ''This little chick / from over the way / went to play / with the pigs one day. / And what do you think / they heard him say?" Nothing could sound more traditional or sedate. But this chick has a mind -- and a vocabulary -- of his own. He madly oinks with the pigs, quacks with the ducks, moos with the cows, baas with the sheep, and bewilders his poor mother hen with a cacophony of barnyard calls at the end of day. These animals cavort, skip, dance over hills, and comport themselves in a most delightfully undignified manner.

The plot of ''Goodnight Lulu," by Paulette Bogan, newly out in a large, bright, flat paperback, may seem formulaic -- Lulu is afraid of various ferocious creatures who could come while she is sleeping. But here, as with nearly all children's books, execution is the thing, and ''Goodnight Lulu" is a manically wonderful comic romp of a book. Lulu's mother (a mother hen, appropriately) describes the way she'd chase off each imaginary danger, and the double-spread, richly colored illustrations show her a worthy adversary indeed, dragging a tiger off by its tail, flapping her wings at a terrified alligator.

Momma keeps her patience sweetly in place, even under pressure: '' 'Oh my little pookie bear, there are no alligators here,' answered Momma." But Bogan's real genius lies in her illustrative art: the brilliant colors that make the hen house seem so alive; the expressive faces on every animal; yellow stars soothing the night scene; and the beautiful, overall sense of movement and affection that lifts ''Goodnight Lulu" out of the ordinary and into the realm of the exceptional.

''Fancy Nancy," written by Jane O'Connor and illustrated by Robin Preiss Glasser, sparkles from the get-go; even the jacket is heavy on feathers and glitter. Our heroine, Nancy, loves ''being fancy." But she's on her own here. ''Nobody in my family is fancy at all. They never even ask for sprinkles."

Every child who's hankered for the high-flown will sympathize with Nancy's efforts to reform her family and be understood: ''Lace-trimmed socks do help me to play soccer better." Nancy decides to offer ''Easy! Fun! Free!" lessons at home, and her family is touchingly accommodating. When they all go out to dinner to celebrate -- dressed in feathers, fancy hats, and sunglasses -- Fancy Nancy learns a valuable lesson in other qualities worth treasuring.

Author O'Connor strikes just the right note, playing a form of straight man to Glasser's slyly hilarious art. There are great comic touches throughout, and the foreshadowing of the plot's central slapstick moment is perfectly rendered.

It would not be April without some showers amid all this sun. ''Little Green," by Chun Yu, is an exquisitely moving memoir in free verse, about a childhood during the Cultural Revolution. Growing up in a little country town, her father sent off to be re-educated and her mother always under suspicion as a teacher, Chun narrates stories with pathos, lyricism, exactitude, sweetness, even a touch of humor, as when in the middle of the night her mother is recruited to a ''parade" celebrating Chairman Mao: ''They had marched through the rice fields in the darkness / to the nearby town, / where there was a single street -- / the only street around that they could march on, / and so it would be called a parade. / Their slogans echoed on the empty dark street, / where everyone was asleep and no lights were on. / They went back and forth on the same street for a whole hour. / Nobody came out to watch the 'parade.' / 'People,' each time Mama told the story / she would sigh and laugh, 'all must be crazy then.' "

Chun's verse is consciously prosaic and conversational. She captures the sense of wonder, delight, occasional terror, and sadness that marked her growing-up years. The natural world is ever-present: ''Rice fields around our house turned / from green to golden yellow, / rose bushes along the river bloomed and withered, / months of spring and summer passed. / At these times, for us children, / the revolution seemed to be far away." Black dragonflies hover by a graveyard ''with wings like half-transparent black veils." ''Little Green" is bursting with life, and no library in America should be without it.

Liz Rosenberg reviews children's books monthly for the Globe.
