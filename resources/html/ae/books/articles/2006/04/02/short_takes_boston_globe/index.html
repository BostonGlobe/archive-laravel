Short Takes

April 2, 2006

Are You Happy?: A Childhood Remembered
By Emily Fox Gordon
Riverhead, 244 pp., $23.95

Disorganized, sloppy, clumsy, pudgy, a failure at school, a disappointment at home, Emily Fox Gordon was nonetheless a happy child. Rambling alone or tagging along with other outcasts, Gordon made her own adventures.

Williamstown was an idyllic spot. Children were free to wander alone in the pretty college town and its surrounding woods, pursuing their own fun and fantasies. Emily's mother, before she retreated into isolation and alcohol, was a ''partisan of childhood, with a special appreciation for its freedoms and delights." She allowed Emily and her younger brother, Andy, her early and constant partner in exploration, to pursue the great treasure hunt of the world. The Pine Cobble School, where she was despised by the other children and ignored by the teachers, should have been her undoing. But she was largely untroubled. Remarkably resilient, she retreated to the empty bench in the schoolyard, withdrew into her own book in class.

All the while, she was absorbing the world, concentrating her powers of contemplation. By the time she left Williamstown at age 12, she recognized her own difference from other children: ''I lagged a fraction of an instant behind the action, waiting for the blurred surface of the present moment to settle and resolve into the clarity of the past." Here she presents her view of the past, and it is dazzling in its cool, clear-eyed, unsentimental vision of herself.

The Ice Museum: In Search of the Lost Land of Thule
By Joanna Kavenna
Viking, 294 pp., $24.95

Joanna Kavenna, whose wanderlust had taken her to the major cities of the Western world, wanted to withdraw from the city and simplify her life in the empty, silent stretches of the north. This yearning led her to Thule. She discovered that Ultima Thule, mentioned by the ancients and the poets, is a mythic as well as a geographic ambiguity.

Traveling from the United Kingdom's Shetland Islands to Iceland, Norway, Estonia, and Greenland, Kavenna searched for the actual place and its possible meanings. Explorers such as Richard Francis Burton, Fridtjof Nansen, and Roald Amundsen voyaged to test the limits of the known world as well as the limits of human endurance. Nature-crazed Victorians, following a well-worn tourist route, hiked decorously to view brute nature as a wild entertainment, to experience in the frozen north the weird and uncanny.

To the Nazis, Thule represented the origins of the German people, and the Thule Society was code for the Aryan cult of purity. During the Cold War, the United States built Thule Air Base, in Greenland, equidistant between New York and Moscow, as a staging post for nuclear bombers.

Not surprisingly, Kavenna discovers that the exact location and meaning of Thule are impossible to isolate. It could be an island, inhabited, empty, frozen, ice-capped, rocky, glacier smooth, lit by a midnight sun, devoid of light. As a myth it could be invented and reinvented as pure, silent, beautiful, the home of the gods, the birthplace of a race.

The Book Thief
By Markus Zusak
Knopf, 552 pp., $16.95

Aimed at young readers (12 and up), this novel of World War II resembles other, more sophisticated tales of that dramatic and dark time. Although the tale is narrated by Death, the central point of view is Liesel's, and she ages from 11 to 14 from 1939 to '42.

Living with foster parents in Molching, a small town on the outskirts of Munich, Liesel slowly becomes aware of the war, the Nazis, and herself. Her family, good Germans who try (unsuccessfully) to avoid the Nazis, betray themselves by hiding a Jew in their basement. Liesel gives this Jew company and comfort. In exchange, he gives her his primitive illustrated book ''The Stand-Over Man." Liesel is devoted to books, having first stolen one found at her brother's grave, ''The Grave Digger's Handbook." Stealing, reading, and writing them occupy her as books are burned, bombs are dropped, and Jews are marched through Molching on the way to nearby Dachau.

Liesel's naïve and narrow point of view is useful for younger readers, but it is Death who has the larger, colder perspective. And the best lines: ''It's probably fair to say that in all the years of Hitler's reign, no person was able to serve the Fuhrer as loyally as me. A human doesn't have a heart like mine. . . . Still, they have one thing I envy. Humans, if nothing else, have the good sense to die."

Barbara Fisher is a freelance critic who lives in New York.
