PEOPLE

Still starstruck by science

By Susan Chaityn Lebovits  |  April 27, 2006

Alan Hirshfeld delights in converting people into fans of science.

''All my books are written for the nonscientifically trained," said the 53-year-old Newton resident, who is a physics professor at the University of Massachusetts at Dartmouth. ''Although we may not think about it too much, we're all immersed in a world of science: sunsets, snowstorms, cellphones, microwave ovens."

Hirshfeld's latest book is ''The Electric Life of Michael Faraday," about the 19th-century English scientist who developed the electric generator and motor. Hirshfeld said that Faraday was such a vivid character, the fact he was a scientist is almost secondary to his story.

Hirshfeld said his interest was sparked by a passage in Timothy Ferris's ''Coming of Age in the Milky Way" that mentioned that Faraday was mathematically illiterate.

''Mathematics is the language of science," said Hirshfeld. ''It struck me as being so odd and intriguing that this man that every freshman science major learns about accomplished what he did without having any mathematical wherewithal."

In some ways, Faraday's is a Cinderella story. He was born into a poor family at a time when someone from such a background had no prospect of entering the elite field of science. Yet, as Hirshfeld recounts, Faraday went from being a bookbinder to assisting chemist Sir Humphry Davy, one of the most respected scientists of his day.

Hirshfeld combed through more than 3,000 letters, including letters to Faraday and his responses; the inventor's seven-volume laboratory diary; and 19th-century scientific papers.

Soon after, Hirshfeld entered his findings on Faraday in a contest sponsored by The John Templeton Foundation titled: ''Power of Purpose." He walked away with the grand prize of $50,000. The winnings went from the Templeton Foundation directly to the Princeton University bursar's office, where his son was a freshman.

Hirshfeld traces his interest in science back to when he was 10 and his aunt presented him with ''The Golden Guide to the Stars," a paperback book that illustrated the constellations and described the planets.

''I got totally into it," said Hirshfeld, who saved his money and bought his first real telescope at the age of 14 with $74 in coins. ''I'd park myself in my family's driveway in Paramus, New Jersey -- 8 miles outside of the glare of New York City -- and try to find celestial objects."

On weekends, he took astronomy classes at the Newark Museum.

''I wanted to become another Galileo or Edwin Hubble," said Hirshfeld.

He recalls taking apart his mother's vacuum cleaner for a high school science project titled: ''The effects of reduced air pressure on ivy and cactus."

''I sucked the air out of a mayonnaise jar to create a Martian-like atmosphere to subject plants to those kinds of conditions," he said, acknowledging that the results were less than earth (or Mars) shaking: The plants died.

After high school Hirshfeld headed south on the Garden State Parkway to attend Princeton University for astrophysics.

''It was a celebrity sort of place and I was shocked when I got in; there they were -- Martin Schwarzschild, Lyman Spitzer, Jerry Ostriker -- walking through the halls and I'm talking with them!"

Hirshfeld likened the experience of talking to the astronomer and astrophysicists to that of an aspiring actor going to Hollywood and meeting Robert DeNiro.

Realizing that his passion was more in communicating the excitement of science than in conducting the research itself, he focused on teaching and writing. After earning his master's and doctorate in astronomy from Yale, he was hired at UMass-Dartmouth.

Hirshfeld has been married for 31 years to Alexandra Helper, a psychiatrist. They have two sons, Gabe, 16 and Josh, 20.

If Hirshfeld goes ahead with the next biography he has in mind, he had better steer clear of electricity. The possible subject is Archimedes -- the ancient Greek famed for his eureka moment in the bathtub.

Hirshfeld's winning essay on Faraday is at www.templeton.org/powerofpurpose/winners/essay_hirshfeld.html.

He will give a reading and talk 8 p.m. May 2 at the Harvard Center for Astrophysics, 60 Garden St., Cambridge. He'll be at the Newton Free Library 7:30 p.m. May 15.

A GARDEN FOR MARY -- In the summer of 1980, Terry Eagan got down on one knee among peanut shells and popcorn at a Ground Round restaurant and asked Mary and her two children to marry him. He was 26.

Eagan became a father to the two children, then went on to have two more with Mary, who was six years his senior. Eleven years later, at the age of 43, Mary, was diagnosed with acute myelogenous leukemia. She died 15 months later.

Eagan says his wife had a wish before she died, that donations in her name go toward a garden patio -- ''a place where patients can go outside and smell the flowers, hear the birds, and feel the sunshine."

The Mary Eagan Garden was built in 2001 at what is now Children's Hospital Boston at Waltham.

On Saturday night, the 10th annual Healing Garden Music Fest takes place at the National Heritage Museum in Lexington. Half the proceeds will go toward maintaining the garden and the other half toward building a garden at the Ottawa Regional Cancer Centre in Canada, a community with which Eagan has become involved through music and friendships.

Eagan, who lives in Waltham, is director of software development and customer support at Framingham-based Computer Corp. of America.

He recalled that in the immediate aftermath of his wife's death he had his hands full with his young family. ''It was always a goal to have that patio made, but the timing wasn't right."

Five years later while the family was on holiday in Camden, Maine, his son Kevin noticed a poster for a benefit concert in a record store. On the way home, he said to his dad, ''You should do something with music to build mom's patio."

During the 4-hour car ride from Camden to Waltham, Eagan started planning the first concert.

''I figured you put a concert together and everybody comes," said Eagan, who admits that he discovered the truth the hard way. ''I had a 750- seat auditorium and 75 people were in it." He's since learned a lot about how to run things.

Although Mary Eagan had been treated at Newton-Wellesley Hospital, it was unable to allocate any space there for a garden. Money that had been donated in Mary's name went into their general nursing fund.

In 1998, Jeannette Clough, president of the Beth Israel Deaconess-Waltham, heard about Eagan's wish and embraced the idea. The hospital cosponsored the second concert.

The next year, Dana Ramish, Waltham's new hospital president, announced that a cancer treatment center would be built. ''The timing was serendipitous," said Eagan. The garden and cancer center opened the same week.

For details on Saturday's concert, visit www.patiorecords.com/musicfest2006.HTML.
