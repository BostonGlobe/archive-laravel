The melody lingers on

Post-Katrina, the music of two New Orleans icons continues to inspire

By Douglas Brinkley  |  August 27, 2006

Louis Armstrong's New Orleans
By Thomas Brothers
Norton, 369 pp., illustrated, $26.95

Blue Monday: Fats Domino and the Lost Dawn of Rock 'n' Roll
By Rick Coleman
Da Capo, 364 pp., illustrated, $26.95

As Hurricane Katrina marshaled strength in the Gulf of Mexico one year ago, taking aim for New Orleans, the most famous Lower Ninth Ward resident was 77-year-old Antoine Domino (a.k.a. Fats Domino). With a husky voice, Creole accent, and pounding piano style accompanied by a heavy backbeat, Domino became a pioneer rock 'n' roll sensation in the years following the Second World War. He had grown wealthy with such hit songs as ``Blueberry Hill," but after gigs around the world he returned to his modest house on Marais Street , located just 10 blocks from where he had grown up along the Industrial Canal .

At the time of Katrina, Domino had become essentially a relic of the 1950s, condemned to performing golden oldies to all-white crowds at casinos and fairs. Plagued with health problems, he didn't like traveling far from his Lower Ninth compound. As Katrina approached, in fact, Domino simply refused to evacuate. With him were his wife and five children, plus their families. When the Industrial Canal breached, Domino's house was flooded ; in just a few minutes a headlong rush of dark water churned his famous Steinway piano into debris. Luckily the Dominos were rescued by Harbor Police, sheltered at the Superdome, and bused to Baton Rouge. Quite suddenly the displaced Domino clan was major news. ``Though he had rarely sought publicity, the Katrina story was the most national attention that Fats had received in years, shocking even old fans who didn't even know that he was still alive," Rick Coleman writes in his fine biography of Fats Domino, ``Blue Monday."

Fortunately for us, Coleman, a first-rate rock 'n' roll critic and Louisiana native, hasn't forgotten Domino. In encyclopedic fashion, ``Blue Monday" dissects Domino's stellar career, from his hit single ``Walkin' to New Orleans" to being awarded the National Medal of the Arts by President Clinton in 1998 , from performing on ``American Bandstand" to his Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction in 1986 . The result is a well-written, deeply researched biography that deserves the overused label ``definitive" tattooed on its cover.

Given his mild-mannered demeanor, it's easy to forget that a Fats Domino show used to incite riots; he was an incendiary black artist whom Coleman deems an ``unlikely revolutionary." When his ``Ain't That a Shame" burst on the airwaves in 1955 , it was as if a meteor had exploded in the American musical galaxy. ``Decades before today's gangsta rappers were born," Coleman writes, ``Domino shouted out ghetto-accented staccato accusations about sad separations ringing like cannonades across a country divided by segregation -- `You made . . . BOOM! BOOM! . . . me cry . . . BOOM! BOOM! . . . when you said . . . BOOM! BOOM! . . . goodbye!' " The song's cultural impact was both subversive and liberating.

This wasn't the first time New Orleans had changed the face of popular music. As Thomas Brothers, a professor of music at Duke University, makes clear in his brilliant ``Louis Armstrong's New Orleans," Storyville jazz had shattered the prevailing Tin Pan Alley orthodoxy in the early decades of the 20th century. With analytical savvy and smart prose, Brothers dissects the first 21 years of Armstrong's extraordinary life. Born in New Orleans Aug. 4, 1901 (he lied that his birthday was July 4, 1900), Armstrong was a veritable sponge when it came to soaking up the musical traditions of his home place. But Brothers's primary accomplishment in ``Louis Armstrong's New Orleans" is dispelling the notion that Satchmo was merely an improvisational genius from birth, that music was in the sultry air and he just absorbed it like pure oxygen. The Armstrong encountered in these pages is a workhorse, tirelessly trying to perfect his art while taking on common jobs to stay alive. Long before Domino was even born, Armstrong had to confront Jim Crow racism head-on. He discovered that playing his horn in parades allowed him to march through all-white neighborhoods followed by cheers: an unthinkable event in any other vocation. Forever thereafter Armstrong believed that music was capable of, as Brothers puts it, ``dissolving racially charged violence."

Reading these books raises a basic question: Why were both jazz and rock 'n' roll born in New Orleans? Naturally, both authors touch on the African diaspora and the legacy of slavery's whip. But Charleston and Memphis and Atlanta and a dozen other Deep South locales can also claim a rich black cultural heritage. Clearly the answer lies in New Orleans's mixed ancestry, French and African, along with the great migration of 40,000 black-Creole plantation immigrants who arrived in the city between 1880 and 1910. What these blacks encountered in New Orleans was a port city with a host of strange traditions including Haitian voodoo and Catholic syncretism. In a city where sticks and stomping were considered musical instruments, pounding your piano like Domino or second-lining like Armstrong was just part of the daily fireworks. ``In African-American vernacular culture in New Orleans around this time, all performances -- verbal, musical, and kinetic -- were expected to include a degree of improvisation," Brothers explains. ``That expectation came from Africa, and it followed the mandate that social interaction was part of what music was all about."

Given New Orleans's long musical traditions, it's safe to say that Katrina didn't kill the muse. Yet something died on Aug. 29, 2005, besides the Lower Ninth, New Orleans East , Gentilly , and Lakeview . Which brings us back to Fats Domino's grand piano. Anyone staring at the Katrina dust on those desecrated piano keys would realize something had perished. The Armstrong and Domino jamboree was over. Something new, however, would take its place. For one thing is clear: New Orleans is a spirit, not just a place, and its varied sounds will continue to inspire. As these books attest, jazz and rock are two of America's great gifts to the world, due, in large part, to two prodigious revolutionaries named Louis and Fats who always seemed to have smiles on their face even when racism tried to keep them down.

Douglas Brinkley is professor of history at Tulane University and author of ``The Great Deluge: Hurricane Katrina, New Orleans , and the Mississippi Gulf Coast."
