Short Takes

By Barbara Fisher  |  August 20, 2006

The Disappearance: A Novella and Stories
By Ilan Stavans
Northwestern University, 144 pp., $22.95

These two stories and one novella, written in clear and simple language, relate mysteries. Ilan Stavans beautifully demonstrates that the best way to suggest the extraordinary is through the ordinary.

In the first story, Maarten Soetendrop, a famous Belgian-Jewish actor, choreographs his own abduction by an invented neo-Nazi group. After keeping himself missing for a while, he resurfaces and confesses the hoax. His reputation is ruined, but his motivation remains obscure. In ``Xerox Man," an Orthodox Jewish man on Manhattan's Upper West Side obsessively but inaccurately photocopies pages of stolen sacred books. Incomplete replicas represent not a desecration but a sophisticated theory of chaos.

The novella, set in Mexico City during a crucial political election, presents the death of Moishe Tartakovsky, a wealthy Jewish businessman. While he was not a believer in Jewish tradition, his family follows the Jewish laws for easing his soul from one life to the next. Family and friends gather to talk, joke, disagree, and reveal stories and opinions about themselves, one another, and the departed Moishe. The ritual, meant to ease Moishe, eases and disturbs those remaining in this life.

A Madman Dreams of Turing Machines
By Janna Levin
Knopf, 230 pp., $23.95

Kurt Gödel and Alan Turing are unlikely subjects for a novel, and Janna Levin, professor of mathematics and physics at Barnard, is an unlikely novelist.

Gödel, a Viennese logician and mathematician , exercised his intellect in a cafe in the company of a group of abstract thinkers. Turing, a British loner, studied mathematics at Cambridge, was recruited as a cryptologist by the British during World War II to solve the Enigma Code, and finally participated in the building of the massive Manchester computer.

Guileless, innocent , and with a ``near total misunderstanding of ordinary human interactions," Turing was tormented at school, isolated and lonely thereafter. Despite homosexual tendencies, he became engaged to a woman. When he acted on his homosexual impulses, he allowed himself to be arrested and tried for the crime of gross indecency. Eventually he committed suicide.

Gödel was forced to leave Vienna by the Nazis, establishing himself at Princeton, where he became increasingly paranoid and obsessed by Turing's machines and the philosophical error in his work. He too committed suicide. These lives, while fascinating, are not dramatically realized. Eccentric geniuses, Turing and Gödel never become fully human. Perhaps this is Levin's point.

The Ode Less Travelled: Unlocking the Poet Within
By Stephen Fry
Gotham, 384 pp., $25

Stephen Fry's guide to writing poetry resembles an informal class taught by an indulgent teacher. In clearly organized chapters, Fry explains meter, rhyme, form, and diction. At times, he treats readers too much like children in class, requesting that they agree to accept the conditions of the book : ``1) Take your time, 2) Don't be afraid, 3) Always have a notebook with you." There are exercises, as in a high school workbook. What is amazing is that after reading his chapter on meter, you will understand these instructions. Most irritating are encouragements like ``You can do it, believe me you can. And you will be so proud of yourself!" But most readers will be grateful to Fry for describing different rhyming categories and for differentiating forms of poetry. Barbara Fisher is a freelance critic who lives in New York.
