HISTORICAL NOVELS

Dreams shrivel in the wicked heat of Florida

By Anna Mundow  |  August 20, 2006

The Transformation
By Catherine Chidgey
Picador, 306 pp., paperback, $14

The Birth House
By Ami McKay
Morrow, 385 pp., $24.95

The Alibi Club
By Francine Mathews
Bantam, 320 pp., $24

If you detect a nip in the air these mornings and find yourself longing -- just a little -- for the recent heat and unbearable humidity, all you have to do is open Catherine Chidgey's ``The Transformation," a novel that is steamy in every sense of the word and as nauseating at times as heat stroke. Set in Tampa in 1898, Chidgey's unsettling, claustrophobic narrative shuttles between an often grotesquely lush frontier and the increasingly fetid mind of Lucien Goulet, a French wig maker (pardon me, ``perruquier") who has made himself indispensable to Tampa's newly minted bourgeoisie.

``Mine is a profession on the wane," Goulet laments, recalling the 18th- century heyday of ``wigs topped with frigates in full sail, garlanded wigs with water bottles concealed in their folds so that the flowers might remain fresh." Instead he constructs intricate chignons and fringes for Tampa's society ladies as well as hair jewelry fashioned from locks of the recently deceased.

Goulet himself is a horrible creation, exactly how horrible Chidgey takes her time revealing. She has Florida to imagine first, `` this topsy-turvy place, where the hurricanes had left great licks in the grass like the whorls in an infant's hair, and where roots lay above the ground, and where the mangroves, their limbs stained with the salty high tide, stalked the shore." Here a few dreams prosper, but most perish. Railroad magnate Henry Plant builds the splendid Tampa Bay Hotel, where Goulet lives, while Marion Unger loses both her young husband and her orange grove to the first bad winter. Young Cubans like Rafael Mendez work in Ybor City's cigar factory, hearing the works of Zola, Tolstoy, or Dickens read aloud by the lector and dreaming of Cuban independence only to see that dream shattered by the Spanish-American War.

This is the stuff of epic romance, but ``The Transformation" is, refreshingly, nothing of the sort. Chidgey is an unclassifiable Australian writer who excels at creating an intimate, menacing atmosphere by slowly revealing the unsavory habits of her typically elusive characters. Goulet, for example, draws a ``hair map" of the city based on the ethnicity of its neighbo rhoods and orders his new assistant, Rafael, to excavate not only Tampa's garbage but later its graves for the desired hair combings. The wig maker's kinky sexual practices are merely recreation. Hair is his true obsession, one that draws Marion into his orbit just as she learns the sordid truth about her dead husband. The novel's denouement fizzles in the swampy murk, but who could complain when Chidgey has given us horrors such as ``a complete tea set made entirely of human hair"?

Ami McKay's Novia Scotia of 1915 is, paradoxically, a far sunnier place than Chidgey's Florida. In ``The Birth House," McKay's first novel, life in Scots Bay is certainly hard: too many brutal men drink, and too many abused women have too many sickly babies. But diseased minds, à la Goulet, have not made it this far north. Even Miss Babineau, the Acadian midwife with the exotic accent, is exactly who she appears to be: mistress of herbal medicine, expert ``catcher" of babies , and a savior to women in need. We see her and other residents through the clear eyes of Dora Rare, the young heroine who becomes Miss Babineau's apprentice just as science comes to town in the person of Dr. Gilbert Thomas. At a ladies tea to promote the newly opened Canning Maternity Home (and sell insurance), Thomas presents a tray ``cluttered with shining silver knives, scissors, and other medical instruments. . . . He took two medicine bottles and nestled them around the flower arrangement in the centre of the table. `Pituitrin and chloroform, a mother's two best friends.' "

Babineau is not persuaded. ``The danger's in forgettin' who's really in charge," she tells Thomas . ``Science don't know kindness. It don't know kindness from cabbage." To which Thomas replies, ``Science is neither kind nor unkind, Miss Babineau. Science is exact." He is predictably proved wrong and exposed as corrupt, but not before young midwife Dora -- now unhappily married -- is accused of murder; forms a women's group disguised as a wartime knitting circle; and visits Boston, where she encounters suffragettes and much more. By then, unfortunately, the tone has become downright inspirational.

Science and obsession are also at the heart of Francine Mathews 's satisfying World War II novel, ``The Alibi Club," which opens in the spring of 1940 in Paris with the German Army closing in and a young American lawyer found dead in a grotesque homosexual tableau. Very fishy. Philip Stilwell, who was mannequin Sally King's fiance, had recently stumbled upon documents that reveal a shady connection between a fellow lawyer at the firm of Sullivan & Cromwell and I. G. Farbenindustrie, the German chemical company. Meanwhile, physicist Frederic Joliot-Curie, husband of the famous scientist Irene Curie, is working on the weapon that both the Nazis and the Allies covet. Add German, American, and English spies, the infamous Dulles brothers, a flamboyant Josephine Baker character, and a rekindled love affair -- all woven together in a plot that never becomes too outlandish or unwieldy -- and who cares if winter is on the way?

Anna Mundow, a freelance journalist living in Central Massachusetts, is a correspondent for the Irish Times. She can be reached via e-mail at ama1668@hotmail.com.
