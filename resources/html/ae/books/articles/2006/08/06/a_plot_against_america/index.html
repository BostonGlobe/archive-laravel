A plot against America

German-planned attack in 1916 is early example of terrorism on US soil

By Joseph Rosenbloom  |  August 6, 2006

The Detonators: The Secret Plot to Destroy America and an Epic Hunt for Justice
By Chad Millman
Little , Brown, 330 pp., illustrated, $24.99

America has had its share of home grown terrorism. The names Ku Klux Klan and Timothy McVeigh come to mind. But a review of the nation's history suggests that its buffer of wide oceans and remote borders has largely insulated it from foreign-sponsored terrorism.

Until 9/11, that is. The question of just how vulnerable the nation is to further attacks by Al Qaeda or some other terrorist group is now front and center. And it pervades a raft of recently published books.

In his absorbing ``The Detonators ," Chad Millman approaches the subject from a historical perspective. The book takes a fresh look at Germany's campaign on the cusp of World War I to sabotage American munitions facilities, a rare instance in which a foreign power unleashed a terrorist assault on US soil.

Millman's focus is on the July 30, 1916, firebombing of the Black Tom Island munitions depot on the New Jersey side of New York Harbor. Black Tom was the largest munitions depot in the United States, which was still neutral in the war that had engulfed Europe. The British and French were purchasing millions of tons of American-made munitions, three-fourths of which were being offloaded to ships at the Black Tom railhead.

The story of how US-based German officials and their agents plotted and carried out the destruction of Black Tom is a ready-made suspense thriller. Millman, a veteran sports journalist, is only one of several authors to spot the story's potential. The 1989 ``Sabotage at Black Tom " by political columnist Jules Witcover , for example, ably covered much the same ground.

Millman recounts the story deftly, suggesting that it is relevant to the terrorist threat that confronts the United States today. ``Black Tom may be gone, but history has stirred its ghosts," he writes.

If the ghosts have lessons to offer, however, Millman's book does not delve into them. He is content merely to spin the yarn.

The shattering series of explosions at Black Tom, which burst windows as far away as Fifth Avenue in Manhattan and devastated the depot, was only one of countless such attacks orchestrated by the German government at this country's munitions plants and depots while the United States was still neutral in the war, which it did not enter until April 1917. But Black Tom was the most serious by far.

``The Detonators" describes the machinations of US-based German diplomats and military attaches who masterminded the attacks. Franz von Rintelen, a captain in the German Navy who was dispatched to New York City as a spy, typified their attitude when he, famously, said: ``I'll buy up what I can and blow up what I can't."

Though investigations by US authorities forced von Rintelen and other ring leaders to quit the country by the end of 1915, the German authorities had recruited a band of US citizens as terrorist agents by then, and the Black Tom operation rolled forward. The Americans' motives for joining the conspiracy varied. Among German-American recruits a deep-seated loyalty to their ancestral homeland was one factor. Bribery was another.

The motivation of 20-year-old Brooklyn native Fred Herrmann, who smuggled incendiary devices from Germany to his co conspirators, was even more dismaying. Herrmann accepted the Germans' offer to join the plot, Millman notes, ``just for kicks." (Al Qaeda watchers take note.)

Millman chronicles the 15-year quest by investigators, notably John McCloy, to vindicate the claims of American companies seeking compensation from Germany for losses at Black Tom.

McCloy entered the case in 1930, when it seemingly had ground to a halt before the German-American Mixed Claims Commission, which the two countries had created in 1922 to hear disputes arising from the war. Millman's lengthy portrait of McCloy, who rose from the modest circumstances of his youth to become a successful Wall Street lawyer, is a compelling narrative , a sort of book within the book.

Relentless and meticulous (his nickname was ``the laboring oar") , McCloy risked his career by pursuing the long-shot claim on behalf of the Lehigh Valley Railroad because of his conviction, as Millman writes, that the Germans were responding with ``only hubris and denial" and had ``learned nothing" from the war and that America should hold them to account for their ``naked aggression" so it would not happen again.

In 1939 the claims commission reversed an earlier finding against the Black Tom claimants and awarded them $21 million for damages, plus $29 million in interest payments. McCloy's coup in coaxing testimony from an Irish labor activist, who had been privy to scheming among German agents, was instrumental in cracking the case.

Despite Millman's spotlight on McCloy, however, the key to victory seemed less the result of any one person's effort than of the cumulative weight of evidence obtained by a team of American lawyers and officials to prove that so many of the Germans' denials were lies.

McCloy would go on to become a top Pentagon official during World War II. Mindful of the role that German-American agents had played in the sabotage of Black Tom and other munitions facilities, McCloy presided over the relocation of 120,000 Japanese-Americans into internment camps during the later war.

In advocating that policy, McCloy blundered by drawing too broad a lesson from Black Tom. Or so many people believe. Millman leaves the issue -- and other implications of the Black Tom episode for today's counter terrorism strategy -- unexplored. His book would have been more powerfully relevant to today's debate over how best to deal with terrorism had he tackled such questions head-on.

Joseph Rosenbloom is a senior correspondent for The American Prospect.
