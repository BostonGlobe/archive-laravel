The Founders phenomenon

The awesome men of the Revolution

By Andrew W. Robertson  |  August 13, 2006

Revolutionary Characters: What Made the Founders Different
By Gordon S. Wood
Penguin, 321 pp., $25.95

Americans are fascinated with the Founders -- ``a forest of giant oaks," as Lincoln described them -- and like an ancient forest, they inspire awe because we will never see their like again. Studies of the Founders are a growth industry at the moment, fueled in part by a revived academic interest in political history. The last decade has also seen an explosion of narrative character studies written for a popular audience, including best-selling biographies of George Washington, John Adams, Thomas Jefferson, Benjamin Franklin, and Alexander Hamilton.

What accounts for this phenomenon that Newsweek magazine has called ``Founders' Chic"? Part of it may be the public's attention to theatrics. The men and women who had major roles in the Revolutionary era were high-minded, articulate, well-mannered. As Gordon Wood observes in this collection of sketches, the aristocratic milieu they inhabited made them intensely aware that politics in the 18th century was a stage and their roles required attention to every gesture. Thanks to the Internet, cable television news, and the blogosphere, we -- like the Founders -- live in a world where the ``personal is political," where high politics and gutter politics intertwine, where every relationship is open to public scrutiny, every scandal to public exposure, and every foible to public ridicule. Yet it seems to nearly all of us that the Founders simply conducted themselves better, with more energy and wisdom and attention to the public weal than our current cast of power-mongers.

But the Founders' place in American history cannot be attributed entirely to their polished conduct. Wood points out that no period was richer in political ideas than the late 18th century. More than any other historian, he has helped us understand the intellectual transition from republicanism to democracy. He has been awarded the Bancroft Prize and the Pulitzer Prize, and his work has been invoked by everyone from Newt Gingrich to Matt Damon in `` Good Will Hunting. " Wood conveys in his engaging style the ultimate irony of the Founders' successful revolution: by making the world safe for democratization, they rendered themselves politically (but not intellectually) obsolete. This book goes beyond the narrative character studies so prevalent of late to give us an understanding of the male Founders in their intellectual, political, and social context. Regrettably, Wood omits the contribution of women entirely.

Wood's description of George Washington is the most compelling of all, which is surprising given that Washington is the least accessible of the major Founders. Washington nevertheless played a unique and critical role in the Revolution and in the constitutional reordering after 1787. In Wood's estimation, he was ``the greatest president we ever had." What propelled Washington to center stage in American history was his ambition to be a man of virtue. George III supposedly observed in 1783 that if Washington retired from public life and returned to his farm, ``he will be the greatest man in the world." Wood shows how Washington employed (and expended) his enormous prestige with his fellow Americans after 1783 to promote the Constitution and make the presidency into a durable institution.

Benjamin Franklin, the subject of Wood's recent biography, is presented here in all of his contradictions: the most and least American of the Founders. Here is the eminent scientist and the rustic in Paris, the spectacularly successful entrepreneur who retired at 42 to devote himself to public service. In Wood's estimation, Franklin was a man of parts whose finest achievements have been obscured by his own autobiography and the ``bumptious capitalism" it has come to represent.

Wood sees Thomas Jefferson as central to the success of the American experiment. Wood observes that Jefferson's reputation has tended to rise and fall with the nation's self-image; thus he sees recent critical reappraisals of Jefferson as reflecting a larger disenchantment among historians. Paraphrasing an early Jefferson biographer, Wood says of the prevailing contemporary view, ``If something is wrong with America, then something must be wrong with Jefferson." To his credit, Wood refrains from Jefferson-bashing. Wood says that Jefferson was well read, hungry for knowledge, and remarkably receptive to all that was ``new and progressive" -- qualities that put him ``at the head of the American Enlightenment." Jefferson was less successful as president, and in retirement was sometimes given to grave doubts about the success of the American experiment. But he believed passionately in both the people and the future. That is why he has been, in times of optimism, so fondly remembered.

Wood's description of Alexander Hamilton is the least successful of his sketches, perhaps because he sees the New Yorker as the most modern of the Founders. He calls Hamilton ``the man who made modern America," and we sense Wood's discomfort with Hamilton's creation of the ``fiscal-military state." The sketch adds little to the insights of recent Hamilton biographers, and this chapter seems half-hearted and derivative.

Wood saves some of his best analysis of the Founders' legacy for the last chapter. Recent academic historians, including this reviewer, have criticized Wood's generation of narrative historians for focusing on political history from the top down, paying too little attention to the mechanics of politics that led to a transformation of ordinary Americans from an audience of onlookers into participants in their own democratic revolution. His final essay, ``The Founders and the Creation of Modern Public Opinion," sparkles with insights about the adaptability of rhetoric in new political contexts and the power of language to transform civil society.

Ultimately, what makes this study more engaging and important than other collective biographies of the Founders is summed up in the subtitle: ``What Made the Founders Different." Wood demonstrates their difference from us, not just in costume and character, but in the very world they inhabited. In subverting that high-minded aristocratic world, they insured we would not see their like again.

Andrew W. Robertson teaches history at Lehman College and the Graduate Center of the City University of New York.
