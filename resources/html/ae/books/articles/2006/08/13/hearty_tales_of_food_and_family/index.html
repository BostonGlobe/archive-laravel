ON AUDIO

Hearty tales of food and family

By Rochelle O'Gorman  |  August 13, 2006

Heat: An Amateur’s Adventures as Kitchen Slave, Line Cook, Pasta-Maker, and Apprentice to a Dante-Quoting Butcher in Tuscany
By Bill Buford
Random House Audio, abridged nonfiction, four CDs, six hours, $29.95, read by the author.
Also available as a download from www.audible.com, $20.97.

Sweet and Low: A Family Story
By Rich Cohen
Audio Renaissance, abridged nonfiction, six CDs, seven hours, $29.95, read by the author.
Also available as a download from www.audible.com for $20.97.

Insatiable: Tales From a Life of Delicious Excess
By Gael Greene
Time Warner AudioBooks, abridged nonfiction, four CDs, five hours, $24.98, read by Nancy Travis and featuring the author.
Also available as a download from www.audible.com, $17.49.

The Omnivore’s Dilemma: A Natural History of Four Meals
By Michael Pollan
Penguin Audio, unabridged nonfiction, 13 CDs, 16 hours, $39.95, read by Scott Brick.
Also available as a download from www.audible.com, $27.97.

Americans love to eat. We also love to hear about food, as is evident by the recent slew of audiobooks about eating, cooking, and living the life of a foodie.

A middle-aged guy who liked to cook but had no formal training, Bill Buford walked away from a desk job to become the ``kitchen slave" of Mario Batali, the star of the Food Channel's ``Molto Mario" and co-owner of the famed Greenwich Village eatery Babbo Ristorante e Enoteca. That Buford could even pull this off is astounding enough, as cooking is a tough job when you are 20, let alone 40. That he pulled it off in such an entertaining fashion should make ``Heat" your number-one pick for those last days on the beach.

A writer and editor, Buford is delightfully descriptive and slyly humorous as he details a year in which he works as a line chef, goes off to Italy to study where Batali studied, learns to make gnocchi, returns to the line, and then heads back to Italy to learn from an opera-singing butcher in a Tuscan village populated by eccentrics.

This delectable treat is slightly marred by the author's narration. In many ways he is the perfect person to read the book -- it is his story and he knows how to punch up the funny bits. But his voice is a little too high-pitched, and he sometimes reads too quickly. Ultimately it doesn't matter, because this is such fun, and so cleverly written, that we can ignore a few missteps. One does wonder, however, why Random House put this fine book on a diet and released only half of it on CD, as we could have ingested the whole thing. (An unabridged version is available from audible.com; 12 hours and 17 minutes, $27.96, read by Michael Kramer.)

``Sweet and Low" is only partly about food. It is mostly about deception, familial failings, disappointment, greed, and, ultimately, facing all of this with a sense of humor.

Rich Cohen is the grandson of the man who invented the artificial sweetener. He is also a member of the only faction of the family to be disinherited from an enormous fortune -- his mother and her three children were left out of the will when his grandmother died.

This is not just about family squabbling. It is about a Jewish cafeteria worker, Ben Eisenstadt, who invented the sugar packet and then invented artificial sugar. It is about obesity and fitness and family dynamics and big business, and even bigger corruption. It is also, finally, about the ruination of a family.

The author's narration is better than average, but he is a little unpolished and can be monotone in spots. However, he also brings out the humor, and this material is often hilarious. Even if we keep in mind that Cohen may have an ax to grind, this is a fascinating deconstruction of a family.

``I was born hungry," Gael Greene declares in her new audiobook, ``Insatiable." As she feeds that hunger for both food and sex, this spicy ragout becomes a tasty romp through her years as a restaurant critic for New York magazine and the sexual revolution of the '70s and '80s.

Greene attacked life and her writing with the same verve, so this uninhibited exploration of a life well lived is not the kind of memoir to pop in the car for family drives, but is exactly what one needs to enliven a ho-hum day. She takes us along for all manner of adventures as she details the cultural shifts in food, fashion, and sexual attitudes over several decades.

Greene is a clever writer with a mischievous sense of humor that is not lost on narrator Nancy Travis. We can hear both glee and passion in her pretty voice. Her pacing is superb; she never misses a chance to underscore humor or enhance a particularly sensuous tidbit.

Michael Pollan, author of the popular ``The Botany of Desire," brings our food to the table, literally, with ``The Omnivore's Dilemma."

Most people have only a vague idea of where their meals originate. We know that cows come from a farm, grapes from vines, juice from a carton. Pollan breaks down the food chains, industrial, organic, and self-foraged, so that we have a clear, if sometimes painful, picture of how our McNuggets came to be. Though more challenging and less frothy than most audiobooks about food or chefs, this is a must-hear for those concerned about what they put into their body. It is read with an easy surety by audiobook veteran Scott Brick, who guides us in and out of conversations and through some of the more complex avenues of food production and distribution without losing our interest.

Rochelle O'Gorman is publisher and editor-in-chief of audiobookcafe.com.
