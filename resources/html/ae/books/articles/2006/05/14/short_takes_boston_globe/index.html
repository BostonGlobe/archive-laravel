Short Takes

By Barbara Fisher  |  May 14, 2006

My Mother's Wedding Dress: The Life and Afterlife of Clothes
By Justine Picardie
Bloomsbury, 239 pp., $22.95

The light romantic title of this collection of essays belies its darker, more serious intent. Justine Picardie, a former editor at British Vogue, cares about clothes, but she also knows about fashion, about the many deeper meanings of what we put on our bodies, what we seek to reveal and expose, how we hope clothes will transform who we are, connect us to others, compel recognition, repel misfortune.

The title essay, which describes the perfect short black dress Picardie's mother wed in, provides a portrait of her mother and her mother's South African family. In ''Ghost Dresses" she not only gives the history of the label Ghost and its gossamer creations, but also tells of the early death of her dear sister. Mother and sister appear again and again in their relation to items of dress: a Gap jacket, Charlotte Brontë's ring, ornate gold embroidery on black velvet. Interviews with Donatella Versace and Claude Montana provide an unsavory and unsettling look at the world of high fashion. ''The Return of the Little Black Dress" vividly repeats many of the well-known stories of Coco Chanel's determined climb from poor orphan to ruling potentate. Picardie touches on many of the stories and images that move young girls -- Pippi Longstocking, Jane Eyre, the Brontë sisters, Sylvia Plath, Emily Dickinson, Holly Golightly, Zelda Fitzgerald. And while the stories of these women feel almost universally familiar, Picardie gives them a personal significance and makes them her own.

Living on Air
By Anna Shapiro
Soho, 240 pp., $22

Maude's schoolgirl crush is primarily on her school, Bay Farm. Artsy, progressive, and back-to-the-land, it's an expensive private school for the children of the rich. Maude attends by scholarship, since her father is a genuine artist and thus too poor to pay the tuition.

At Bay Farm, Maude meets Weesie, short for Louise, and her crush has a focus. Weesie's family lives in a grand house, vacations in a grander one in Maine. Weesie's parents are everything Maude's are not: formal, elegant, distant, WASP. Maude, under the spell of everything that is new and different, suffers the pangs of every adolescent, trying to sort out what is solid in her family, what is valuable in the larger world. She feels annoyed at her family for not having taught her to sail, drink tea, or shop at L. L. Bean, but seems unaware that her easy references to Giacometti, weltschmerz, and ''Swann's Way" were caught in her own family's wide cultural net.

After she loses her scholarship at Bay Farm, she suffers through the usual reverses: her parents' divorce, the revelation of family secrets, boy trouble. From all of this, Maude should have learned to put Bay Farm into a larger perspective, but it seems she hasn't. Her story ends with a sentimental reunion at the school's graduation, where all of Maude's adolescent dreams, instead of being reevaluated and revised, are unbelievably realized. Her former friends gather round her in delight, Weesie will be her roommate at Sarah Lawrence the next year, her boyfriend hugs her to his manly chest. What seemed to be a coming-of-age story was in fact a return to childhood.

Ticknor
By Sheila Heti
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 119 pp., $18

This monologue of disappointment and envy compels a reader's curiosity even as it repels sympathy. George Ticknor, the long-suffering narrator of this ramble, compares his sorry life with that of his illustrious and productive friend Prescott.

William Hickling Prescott, a 19th-century historian and author of ''History of the Conquest of Mexico," was injured and almost blinded by a childhood accident. Nonetheless, he maintained his sweet temper and developed his agile mind. Witness to the childhood injury, Ticknor remained Prescott's lifelong friend and eventually his biographer. Ticknor describes Prescott as a man ''to whom everything comes so naturally, and before whom the whole world opens itself up, bestowing upon him all of God's gifts." Ticknor himself has the opposite temperament, taking everything too hard, refusing to recognize or take pleasure in the gifts he has. Unmarried and living alone, he believes that others find his company tedious, that the invitations he receives are only out of obligation, that his few published articles are worthless.

Prescott's life is a triumph of spirit and will. Ticknor's is a self-fulfilling prophecy of despair. But neither the range of Prescott's accomplishments nor the shape of Ticknor's mind is interesting enough to merit this exploration.

Barbara Fisher is a freelance critic who lives in New York.
