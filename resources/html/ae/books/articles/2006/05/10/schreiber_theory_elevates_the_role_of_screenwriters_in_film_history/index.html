BOOK REVIEW

'Schreiber Theory' elevates the role of screenwriters in film history

By Saul Austerlitz, Globe Correspondent  |  May 10, 2006

The Schreiber Theory: A Radical Rewrite of American Film History
By David Kipen
Melville, 150 pp, $12

It has now been, remarkably, nearly 40 years since film critic Andrew Sarris dropped his bombshell on the world of movie love, ''The American Cinema." Significantly subtitled ''Directors and Directions," Sarris's book sought to translate the politique des auteurs of French filmmaker-theorists like Jean-Luc Godard and François Truffaut, and render it palatable to American audiences. Honoring Orson Welles, Alfred Hitchcock et al. by elevating them into his personal pantheon, and slotting directors into an idiosyncratic set of categories, Sarris made the study of film a director-centric endeavor. If the debate over auteurism, as it came to be known stateside, never ebbed entirely, its language has nonetheless become the official shorthand of film criticism.

As with any theory, auteurism had its strong points and its weaknesses. Excellent at finding the threads of continuity in seemingly disjointed careers, auteurism was at a competitive disadvantage in coping with films lacking strong directorial presences, tending to dismiss them as anomalies. Most crucially, in killing one king (the studio system), auteurism had crowned another. While the director could more reasonably claim to be the bottom-line author of a film, enshrining directors as such required turning a blind eye to the most fundamentally obvious aspect of the filmmaking process: its collaborative quality. Furthermore, the very terminology of auteurism raised a question: If the director was the auteur (or author) of the film, what of the poor ink-stained wretch who had actually written the script?

This last question dogs the mind of David Kipen, who weighs in with an alternative-world counterpart to ''The American Cinema" with ''The Schreiber Theory." Named after the Yiddish word for ''writer," Kipen's manifesto attacks auteurism head-on, describing it as the cinematic equivalent of a shelf of novels arranged by editor's name. Labeling his book ''an attempt to put the disarranged library of American film back in order," Kipen proposes a radical reordering of American film theory's mental furniture.

Ironically, for a thinker so dedicated to writing Sarris's wrongs, Kipen entirely adopts the enemy's strategy, from his book's format to the willful overstating of its main point. ''The Schreiber Theory" is also guilty of the auteurists' crime of glibness, whereby an entire career is boiled down into a two-sentence explication of common themes that ignores the textures of individual films. The problem with Kipen's thesis is that it breaks apart on impact; it is no accident that most every film by Hitchcock or Howard Hawks is worth seeing, and many are legitimate classics, while the work of even the greatest screenwriter is bound to be more uneven, for the very basic reason of having provided only a blueprint toward an eventual film.

''The Schreiber Theory" is not content with making the long-overdue point that writers have been shafted when it comes to apportioning credit in the world of American filmmaking. Kipen attempts to assign all the credit to them. For decades, auteurists have been futzing with half-hearted apologias for their theory's essentialism, like claiming that the director's name is merely a stand-in for the collaborative process at whose center they reside. It would be silly to knock the directors from their pedestal, only to replace them just as blindly with writers. Kipen knows this. His polemic is only half-serious (or three-quarters serious, to assign a more exact percentage), leavened by such witticisms as describing one writer as having won ''enough Tonys for a Mafia funeral."

Even when we take these shortcomings into consideration, Kipen does cineastes a major service, recentering the frame on film history in the hopes of giving writers their rightful place. The book's entries rediscover overlooked writers like Paul Dehn (''Goldfinger," ''The Spy Who Came in From the Cold"), Peter Stone (''Charade," ''The Taking of Pelham One Two Three"), and Dudley Nichols (''Bringing Up Baby," ''Stagecoach," ''The Big Sky"), as well as the entirely forgotten clock-punchers of the studio era, who banged out picture after picture without recognition or acknowledgment.

As Kipen would be the first to acknowledge, ''The Schreiber Theory" is merely an outline, sketching in skimpy details for a proposed mountain of scholarship on the American screenwriter. For every writer included in its index, ''The Schreiber Theory" leaves out a giant. Kipen has begun the work, but he calls on film critics, historians, and graduate students to fill in the blanks. A complete ''American Cinema" for screenwriters awaits other times, and other hands; in the meantime, we have this flawed but fundamentally right-on book with which to realign film history.

Saul Austerlitz is at work on a book about music videos.
