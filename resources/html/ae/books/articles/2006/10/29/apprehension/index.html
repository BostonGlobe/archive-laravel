Apprehension

In a suspenseful re-creation of a 1910 manhunt, an inventor's breakthrough helps ensnare a fleeing murderer


(Anastasia Vasilakis)

By David Liss  |  October 29, 2006

Thunderstruck
By Erik Larson
Crown, 463 pp., illustrated, $25.95

At the beginning of his phenomenally successful "The Devil in the White City," Erik Larson presents a prologue in which one of his principal subjects, Daniel Burnham , sails aboard the ocean liner Olympic while his long-time associate, Frank Millet , sails in the opposite direction aboard its doomed sister ship the Titanic. There is a kind of symmetry here, a sense of proportion and doubling that Larson both looks for and imposes on his historical subjects. Things happen in pairs in his narratives. In "Devil in the White City," Burnham's design of the World's Columbian Exposition is paired with the serial killer H. H. Holmes 's use of the fair as a stalking ground for his murderous impulses. The blueprint is both simple and engaging: two minds together at a single important historical moment, one a source of invention and creativity, the other a collection of unsavory impulses whose crimes will be, in some fundamental way, affected by the first.

Clearly it's a formula that worked well for him, though I was never quite convinced it worked for the story. Historical narrative is a tricky business, because history doesn't always unfold with the pace of a good story. In the end, it seemed to me that Larson had a fascinating tale to tell, but his formula overshadowed, rather than enlivened, the material.

The formula, nevertheless, is lovingly reprised in the author's most recent work of narrative nonfiction, "Thunderstruck," but not always to its benefit. The creator in this case is the Italian Guglielmo Marconi , often -- if somewhat controversially -- credited as the inventor of radio. The criminal, Hawley Harvey Crippen , a meek American physician , will, despite his unremarkable appearance, emerge at the center of one of the most sensationalized murders of the early 20th century. These two men, Larson makes clear at the beginning of "Thunderstruck," are on a collision course, and the bulk of the book describes that course, with only the final portion detailing the collision. It's too bad Larson spends disproportionate energies on windup instead of pitch because the ultimate intertwining of the two narratives makes "Thunderstruck" riveting and, quite frankly, unforgettable.

In the early sections of the book, Larson handily sets up the world in which his characters are destined to play a very public role -- sometimes with a bit of a kitchen-sink approach, featuring digressions on subjects like spiritualism and magic performances that, while interesting, are never sufficiently synthesized into the main narrative. Nevertheless, he paints the turn of the 20th century as, among many other things, a time of scientific inquiry and discovery. While many scientists experiment with the use of wave technology to send communications, it is Marconi, an autodidact with no formal scientific training, who files for a patent, setting off a storm of anger and resentment among Britain's scientific community as the various factions quibble over who first discovered what, and when that discovery took place.

It's all great stuff, but once Marconi forms his own company and sets about pursuing his dream of trans-Atlantic wireless communication, his story becomes a bit less compelling. The details of the inventor's many attempts, failures, and reapplied efforts to perfect his system are probably only slightly less tedious to read about than they were for him to pursue. It's too bad, because the story of Dr. Crippen, told in parallel chapters, is both engaging and effectively rendered. Consequently, by the middle of the book the Marconi sections begin to feel more like the price of admission than a complementary pleasure.

Crippen's tale enjoys the slow but effective build of narrative tensions as Larson details how this small, unattractive man marries Belle Elmore , a beautiful, voluptuous, and voluble woman with an endless capacity for self-aggrandizement. The doctor dotes on his wife through her embarrassing attempts to establish a stage career, her demands for ever more furs and jewels, and, finally, the taking of a lover under his nose. When Crippen makes his secretary his mistress, even while Belle torments and threatens him, the story assumes the pacing of an accomplished thriller.

There is no way seriously to discuss the book without revealing the end, so readers who want to be surprised by how things turn out should proceed at their own risk. Events come to a head, however, in the last quarter of the book when, in 1910, Belle disappears and Crippen and his lover decide it would be a good time to flee the country, even while Belle's friends suspect Crippen of something sinister. The investigation reads like an Edwardian police procedural, and once Crippen is abroad, and the police discover the fussily deboned remains of a woman they believe to be Belle in his London home, history's first truly modern manhunt, complete with regular updates and the rapt attention of millions, begins.

At the beginning of the book, Larson describes "the great hush" -- the silence that envelops ships at sea. With no access to the undersea cables that sent Morse code, once ships embarked upon a trans-Atlantic voyage, they were isolated until they docked once more. Over the course of the book, Marconi's wireless machines change all that. Ships begin to install wireless transmitters on board, enabling them to send and receive messages. When a diligent ship's captain suspects that a couple in disguise are Crippen and his mistress, Larson evokes an almost Hitchcockian tension as wireless transmissions fly back and forth. Newspaper readers all over the world, already captivated by the story, can read about what Crippen has eaten at meals, what jokes he told and listened to, all while Crippen himself has no idea that his disguise has been penetrated.

As frustrating as I found the book at times, these pages stand out as some of the most crisp and effective popular history I've ever read. Larson is at the top of his game and in full control of his material as he details this scandalous instance of how wireless not only changes how people live their lives, but provides the very means of documenting the changes as they happen. It is micro and macro history lovingly packaged with sex and murder. That's what I call a good read.

David Liss is the author of four novels, most recently "The Ethical Assassin."
