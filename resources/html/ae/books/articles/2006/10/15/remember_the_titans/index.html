BOOKS

Remember the titans

Like latter-day colossi, Carnegie and Mellon bestrode the worlds of industry, politics, and philanthropy

By David M. Shribman  |  October 15, 2006

Andrew Carnegie
By David Nasaw
Penguin, 878 pp., illustrated, $35

Mellon: An American Life
By David Cannadine
Knopf, 779 pp., illustrated, $35

For years my older daughter attended orchestra rehearsals at Carnegie Mellon University, played concerts in Carnegie Music Hall , and took walks in Mellon Park. My younger daughter checks out books and music recordings from the local branch of the Carnegie Library and accompanies her father to sporting events at Mellon Arena. Carnegie and Mellon are part of the architecture of our lives and culture, especially in Pittsburgh.

So it was especially heartening that two important publishing houses should release two important biographies of the two men who, along with H. J. Heinz and Henry Clay Frick and some other coal and timber billionaires, helped build the economy not only of Pittsburgh but also of the nation. These are giants of our history, and, at 878 and 779 pages, they are the subjects of giants of the biographical genre.

David Nasaw's portrait of Andrew Carnegie and David Cannadine's portrait of Andrew Mellon represent the biographical arts at their best: distinguished biographers taking on some of the largest figures of our business, political, and cultural history. These books are long, to be sure, but so are the shadows cast by these men. Carnegie was the signature industrialist of his time, and no history of American business, labor conflict, literacy, philanthropy , or diplomacy can ignore him. Mellon was the signature banker, investor , and treasury secretary of his time, and no history of business, tax policy, art collecting, or education can ignore him.

In different times and with different styles, both were members of the sprawling Pittsburgh elite, so big that in 1888 The New York Times listed 100 millionaires in the city. This elite is a species that exists to this day, but it reached its apotheosis several generations ago, and Carnegie and Mellon were the personification of it. One was an up-from-nothing tale of an immigrant with pluck, luck , and determination, and the other was a classic example of inherited wealth taking a sound and conservative path. But they both sketched their lives on a grimy Pittsburgh canvas where members of the ruling establishment , in Cannadine's words, `` were devoted to making money . . . accumulated wealth and advantage over the generations . . . despised leisure and, even more, idleness, and . . . had little appreciation of culture or the arts."

These two men were, of course, brilliant variations on this theme. Carnegie ``retired" from his business interests more often than did Mario Lemieux, the Pittsburgh hockey star who had so many comebacks, and no account of the bitter labor confrontation at Homestead omits the unavoidable fact that Carnegie was away on holiday as the blood flowed. Mellon's appreciation for the arts grew with the years until he accumulated one of the finest collections of paintings ever assembled, then donated them (as Thomas Jefferson had with the books that formed the spine of the Library of Congress) as the nucleus of the National Gallery of Art in Washington, D.C.

At the heart of the genius of both men was that most precious of mettles, judg ment. Listen to Nasaw on Carnegie: ``He was not an engineer and had no technical training, but he enjoyed an insider's understanding of the mechanical arts and the iron trades and was a shrewd judge of talent." Now compare that to Cannadine on Mellon: ``He was thought to possess exceptional powers of judgment, both of the character and capacity of the men who sought help and of the feasibility and potential of the schemes which they presented -- and he was especially open to the commercial possibilities of emerging science and technology."

Mellon's father, the founding father of the family's wealth, knew Carnegie but was skeptical of him, mostly because he found giant manufacturing enterprises odious and considered philanthropy frivolous. But records of correspondence between the two exist. ``I think often of you," Carnegie once wrote. ``There is a freemasonry between characters that commonplace people cannot enter. You are always in that circle in my thoughts."

But Carnegie, the greatest rags-to-riches success story of our history, had many concentric circles, as befitting a life that touched on the railroad, coal, iron, and steel industries, was involved in one of the most passionate labor conflicts of our history, seeded the countryside with libraries , wrote serious philosophical tracts about the burdens and opportunity of wealth (and about the prerogatives of labor, words that would ring sad and ironic after Homestead), and dedicated his final years to the quixotic pursuit of peace in a world that always seemed to hurtle toward war.

He had the immigrant's instinct for American values and, to appropriate the American argot expressed so well by George Washington Plunkitt, whose life span roughly coincided with Carnegie's, he seen his opportunities and he took 'em. ``He accepted the codes of capitalist behavior without a moment's pause," writes Nasaw, who this year was invited by the Kennedy family to undertake a biography of Joseph P. Kennedy.

Carnegie accumulated wealth for the pure pleasure of the pursuit of it, and he was ruthless in his treatment of his workers, in part because he could be . But he never fully believed in the mindless accumulation of millions, nor in the wanton treatment of workers, and for decades scholars and commentators have argued about the conflict between Carnegie's account book and his moral accountings.

He was opportunistic, and for him opportunity was the highest value and virtue. So he had no problem declaring the reprise line of his gospel of wealth that ``the man who dies . . . rich dies disgraced." There are internal contradictions to so much that Carnegie said and thought, but Nasaw tells us how Carnegie was able to see his philosophy whole: ``The gospel of wealth provided an ideological antidote to socialist, anarchist, Communist, agrarian, single -taxer , and labor protests against the unequal distribution of wealth by arguing that the common good was best served by allowing men like himself to accumulate and retain huge fortunes," he writes. ``The more wealth that landed in wise hands, the more that could be given away -- wisely -- by the retired capitalist."

Mellon was an acquisitor of an entirely different stripe. Shy, awkward, deeply private, an abject failure in love, Mellon threw himself into work, mostly so that he could avoid most human involvement. ``Mellon's drive to acquire," Cannadine writes, ``was an essential, perhaps preponderant, element of his nature, and had already become largely a substitute for, and an escape from, personal intimacies and commitments."

The last of the Pittsburgh financial giants, Mellon left his fingerprints on every aspect of the economy of his time, not only as a banker but also as a major figure in Alcoa, Koppers Industries , and the old Gulf Oil Corp. ``Many of these businesses," Cannadine writes , ``were fully integrated concerns, extending all the way from the extraction of raw materials, to processing and refining, to production and sales."

As treasury secretary, Mellon preached his own gospel, of low taxes and financial prudence, even as the gales of the Great Depression blew. He served under the three Republican presidents of the 1920s, all the while seeking to run the nation's economy the way he ran his family's businesses, which is to say with a great skepticism about extravagance and borrowing. He cut both taxes and spending, then as now a favorite policy of Republicans and conservatives. He was laissez-faire personified. ``He did little and stayed quiet," Cannadine writes, ``still governed by his faith in the self-regulation of markets." And he was no alarmist. ``I see nothing . . . in the present situation that is either menacing or warrants pessimism," he declared in 1929. His was a view, and a policy approach, that were repudiated in the Franklin Roosevelt election of 1932.

Mellon's darkest hours and greatest legacy occurred during the FDR years, when he endured lengthy legal proceedings growing out of charges of tax evasion and when he proposed to the new president the establishment of the National Gallery. ``Over a period of many years," he wrote to Roosevelt, ``I have been acquiring important and rare paintings and sculpture with the idea that ultimately they would become the property of the people of the United States." Roosevelt accepted Mellon's offer immediately.

So what is left of these men? Their names are on buildings, in history books , and, this season, on the cover of magisterial biographies that, like their own lives, reward the work they entail. Carnegie and Mellon built fortunes and shaped the times in which they lived. But their legacy rests more on what they gave away than on what they accumulated. There's a lesson in that for us all, and it's more philosophy than it is history.

David M. Shribman, for a decade the Globe's Washington bureau chief, is executive editor of the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette.
