In the final analysis, a fateful summer

A detailed look at Nixon's collapse and the consequences

By David M. Shribman  |  October 1, 2006

31 Days: The Crisis That Gave Us the Government We Have Today
By Barry Werth
Doubleday, 398 pp., $26

There have been fateful summers in American history . In 1862 Lincoln prepared his Emancipation Proclamation. In 1968 protesters disrupted the Democratic National Convention . In 1998 Bill Clinton testified to a grand jury about his sex life. But there never has been a summer quite like the summer of 1974.

In that summer the House prepared for an impeachment vote on President Nixon, negotiations led to the resignation of the president, Gerald R. Ford moved into the White House, the nation faced uncertainty at home and abroad, Washington was convulsed with conspiracy theories and negotiations over the Nixon presidential papers, prosecutors readied a battery of court cases against Nixon, new crises in Cyprus raised war fears, and there were wild stories circulating around the capital raising questions about just who was in charge of the nation's war-making apparatus, including its lethal nuclear weapons.

And that was before Ford pardoned Nixon and things really got hot.

``31 Days" is Barry Werth's engaging and rewarding chronicle of those anxious and agonizing summer days, the days in which the presidency was resigned for the first time, a man who was never elected by the entire nation became chief executive, and some of the principals of our time, including Donald Rumsfeld and Dick Cheney, took their first star turns.

It is hard to believe that those days are a third-of-a-century old. Indeed, Watergate is still in our minds and vocabulary -- this year's Ohio state political scandal, involving rare coins, was immediately dubbed ``Coingate " -- and the personalities who steered the nation out of its midsummer misery are, once again, in charge. Thus Werth's beguiling subtitle .

Even so, at the center of this story, which is divided into daily chapters, one for each day beginning Aug. 9, 1974, is Richard Nixon, who had been part of our national life since the end of the 1940s, whether as anti-Communist congressman, vice president, presidential candidate, unsuccessful California gubernatorial candidate, president , and, always, the trickster.

In the White House he spoke of peace and prosecuted the Vietnam War, spoke of law and order and presided over Watergate, celebrated free markets and imposed economic controls, stood against Communism and created openings with the Soviet Union and China, spoke about the American dream and, in a rambling televised valedictory , spoke of ``the nightmare end of a long dream." In short, a pretty complex character.

Ford asked the nation ``to confirm me as your president with your prayers," and for a time the new president, a former congressional leader, had the nation's attention, sympathy , and support. He was, after all, so different from his predecessor, and the most poignant reminder of that are the words George H. W. Bush scrawled in his diary the day Nixon left Washington. Bush, who had almost found himself selected as Ford's vice president, wrote of ``the shame and wonder" of Nixon, a man of ``no morality" and ``gratuitous abuse."

From the first there were warring factions in the Ford White House . This volume is rich in detail on the maneu vers between the Nixon and Ford camps , and on Ford's efforts to draw lines between his administration and the byzantine one that preceded him. Many of those details are interesting today only in how they underline how urgent it was for Ford to step away from Nixon's shadow.

What retains its fascination is what has emerged only in retrospectives and memoirs, principally the struggle to determine Ford's vice president. The choice came down to Nelson A. Rockefeller of New York and Bush, with Rumsfeld as standby. All three were on the left of the Republican Party , and the result was an alienation of the right that propelled Ronald Reagan into a presidential campaign in 1976 and into the White House in 1980.

Now a word about the most famous pardon in American history. Today Ford is saluted for that pardon , but at the time the decision caused a firestorm. These pages carry all of the thrusts and parries of the pardon debate, both inside and outside the White House (where there were worries that Nixon might die or kill himself , presumably of depression or a broken heart ) and outside the White House (where the air was full of suspicions about secret, sordid deals).

Werth leaves us with an irresistible analysis: ``Ford hadn't struggled for the presidency, hadn't won it, but it was hard for anyone, especially those who had survived Nixon's collapse, not to admire his faith and determination that he would not fail at it, even as a good number of them distrusted his abilities, goals and politics."

By the time it is finished, Werth's book puts Ford in the center of the drama. That, ultimately, was what the 31 days were about, and we have to concede that the man who succeeded Nixon was, himself, a success.

David M. Shribman, for a decade the Globe's Washington bureau chief, is executive editor of the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette.
