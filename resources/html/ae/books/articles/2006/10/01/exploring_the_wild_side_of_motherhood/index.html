SELF-HELP

Exploring the wild side of motherhood

By Naomi Rand  |  October 1, 2006

Mommies Who Drink: Sex, Drugs, and Other Distant Memories of an Ordinary Mom
By Brett Paesel
Warner, 336 pp., $23.99

The Homework Myth
By Alfie Kohn
Da Capo, 256 pp., $24

Roadtrip Nation: A Guide to Discovering Your Path in Life
By Mike Marriner and Nathan Gebhard, with Joanne Gordon
Ballantine, 304 pp., paperback, $13.95

As far as I can tell, there are three discrete parenting stages. The first begins at birth and ends when your child enters kindergarten. The second covers the 12 years in between. And the third involves the transition from college to independent life.

Stage one is the subject of ``Mommies Who Drink," by Brett Paesel. And don't be put off by the title, this is not a tell-all about alcoholism. It's actually a memoir-parenting guide. This is a fun book that is honest, affectionate, and smartly written. It is also definitely not a book for the puritanical mom. Paesel is an actress who tries to figure out what having an acting career really means when you're raising small kids on a shoestring budget. Luckily she's got a group of friends who help her make it through. Their Friday meeting during happy hour at a local bar is the setting for discussions of what motherhood is really all about; as in sex, drugs, husbands, partners, and ambition. In other words, being a mom doesn't mean you have to give up living your life.

Paesel's book is a welcome antidote to every serious pregnancy and child-rearing guide out there. Here, Paesel offers a preview of the first two years of motherhood.

``You should be able to: build a tower of two blocks by yourself without weeping. Babble. You will probably be able to: Show your baby how to build a tower of two blocks without saying, `How simple is this? It's just two blocks on top of each other. See? One. Two.' Sleep through the night without having a nightmare about your child losing limbs or shrinking to the size of a grain of sand. "

If you're like Paesel, you will also be able to hang out with your friends, for at least a few hours of sanity a week, and discover that the person you once were actually still exists inside the mom you feel you have to be 24-seven. Make that 24-six, with a time out just for you, during which you'll get to be raunchy, real, and laugh-out-loud funny. You'll also realize that phase one doesn't last forever; yes, there is life after babyhood.

See phase two -- school daze. What a blessed relief, they're off and, if you're staying at home, you have some down time. If you're at work, there's less stress too, right? Wrong. Because when your child returns home he or she will be weighted down by a backpack that will cause permanent curvature of the spine. Yes folks, it's homework time. These assignments are cunningly designed to bring every member of the family to tears. This is why Alfie Kohn's ``The Homework Myth" should be required reading for every teacher, principal, and school district head in the country. Most especially my eighth-grader's geometry teacher, who seems to believe that three hours of homework a night is not just acceptable but somehow desirable.

Kohn's book isn't exactly fun reading -- the style is dense and academic. But his point is on target. What are the benefits of all this homework? There is no proof that it helps children learn. Kohn cites plenty of research to back up his thesis. None of it shows the slightest connection between homework and independent thinking. Kohn argues that homework is a burden to children, and, not surprisingly, their parents. I wholeheartedly agree.

And, ``Who benefits when people are taught not to question the value of what they have been told to do but simply to toil away at it -- and regard this as virtuous? Follow this query where it leads and what initially looked like uncontroversial claims on behalf of homework turn out to reveal a very specific, very debatable set of cultural values." Kohn believes these values depend on unquestioning obedience and loyalty, and create students who are anything but imaginative. It's hard not to see his point. Or wish that we could find other ways to measure intelligence and nurture the curious minds of children.

There is, of course, a place where children are encouraged to become independent thinkers. We call this college. Unfortunately, thinking for one's self doesn't necessarily lead to a job. Or a career. Thus ``Roadtrip Nation" was born.

I'm behind the curve with this book and the PBS show it spawned. For the uninitiated, ``Roadtrip Nation," by Mike Marriner, Nathan Gebhard, and Joanne Gordon , compiles interviews conducted by three college graduates who decided to cross the nation in an RV and interview people who were successful and intriguing to see how they managed to get from college to their career goal s. They meet plenty of powerful men and a few women. (My only quibble with this book is that there are many more men than women, and few minorities represented.) The stories are always intriguing. What one takes away from these interviews is the value of persistence, dedication, and love of what you do for a living.

You'll hear from captains of industry, architects, photographers, TV directors, and lobstermen. These interviews prove that there is simply no right way of getting from here to there. It's a truth that should make worried parents breathe a little easier. Or as Charlie Trotter, chef and restaurateur says, ``It's nineteen years later and I don't even feel like I've had a job. I feel a little guilty at times because I do something that I cannot believe I can make a living at." Reading these stories shows that there are a million roads to take, but if you really want to enjoy your working life to the fullest, you have to start with the one given and follow your heart.

Naomi Rand's latest Emma Price mystery is ``It's Raining Men."
