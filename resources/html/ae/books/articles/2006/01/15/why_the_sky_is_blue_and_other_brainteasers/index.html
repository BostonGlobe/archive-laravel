Why the sky is blue, and other brainteasers

January 15, 2006

Sky in a Bottle
By Peter Pesic
The MIT Press, 256 pp., $24.95

The Equations: Icons of Knowledge
By Sander Bais
Harvard University Press, 96 pp., $18.95

Sixteen years ago, my brother Chris walked into a conference room for his oral qualifying doctoral examination at MIT. He had already earned degrees from MIT in aeronautical engineering and electrical engineering. He had taken two full days of written exams; he had been rereading his class notes for months.

The first question the professors put to him was about multivariable control theory. No problem: He had recently taken that course.

Then a professor asked him: Why is the sky blue?

It's a fastball of a question, isn't it? Every parent has to field that one. A doctoral candidate at MIT ought to hit it out of the park.

And yet it leads swiftly into a briar patch of uncertainty. Why isn't the sky green? Or fuchsia?

Aristotle couldn't figure it out. He determined that water droplets in clouds acted as tiny mirrors, but that's about as close as he could get. The great ninth century Muslim philosopher al-Kindi believed the sky's color had to do with ''earthly particles" suspended in the air. These particles, he believed, heated by sunlight and starlight, ''intermingle into a color in the middle of shadow and light and this is the azure-blue color."

Hmmm. . . . DaVinci claimed ''minute, imperceptible particles," glowing in the atmosphere, explained the color. Newton argued that the blue was due to suspended droplets. John Tyndall conjectured that light was scattered by microscopic ''sky matter" or ''floating organic germs." But a thousand years had passed, and was Tyndall all that much closer to solving the riddle than al-Kindi?

Why wasn't the sky violet, for example? Violet light has a shorter wavelength than blue light and would certainly scatter off particles as easily. And what were those suspended particles? Water? Dust? Salt? Could light be scattered by air molecules themselves?

In 1869, Tyndall wrote that ''the blue color of the sky, and the polarization of skylight . . . constitute, in the opinion of our most eminent authorities, the two great standing enigmas of meteorology."

If only Tyndall could have put his hands on an advance copy of science historian Peter Pesic's luminous new book, ''Sky in a Bottle."

In 10 cleanly written, well-paced chapters, Pesic traces the progression of our understanding of atmospheric hue through dozens of scientists, philosophers, and artists. His smartest move was to structure ''Sky in a Bottle" like a mystery story, coaxing us puzzle by puzzle through the (very) uneven advances of knowledge over the centuries. You find yourself forgetting what you think you already know. What is air? What is reflection? Is light a particle or a wave, anyway? Why is the sky red at sunset? What role, if any, does interference play?

If science is a systematic search to understand the universe, understanding why the sky is blue is perhaps the most accessible question the universe can offer. It's also a beautiful illustration of how the very largest things in our world are affected by the very smallest. Without atoms, our sky isn't blue. Indeed, except perhaps for our bodies themselves, the color of the sky might be the quintessential example of how excruciatingly small things -- molecules, and the atoms that make them up -- manifest themselves in our physical reality.

So do you want to know why the sky looks blue? First, remember that the wavelength of blue light is shorter than that of red light. Second, remember that the atmosphere is molecular; it (primarily) consists of molecules of nitrogen and oxygen. Third, remember that I'm a novelist by profession and barely know what I'm talking about.

Regardless, here's your one-sentence answer: Since the wavelength of blue light is short enough to interact with molecules of oxygen, it gets scattered all over the sky and reaches our eyes from all sorts of different angles.

There's more to Pesic's explanation, but that's a foundation. The intensity of scattered light varies (inversely, and by the fourth power, if you're interested) by the wavelength. This relation is known as Rayleigh's Law, and there is a formal equation to calculate Rayleigh's scattering coefficient.

I'm not about to paste a copy of that equation here. Nobody wants to see an equation on Sunday morning.

Well, maybe some people do. Harvard University Press has an intriguing little book out right now called ''The Equations: Icons of Knowledge" by Sander Bais. The book is founded on a quarrel with what the author calls the ''fashionable dogma" of not including equations in science books intended for general readers. ''The veto is like asking somebody to explain art without showing pictures," Bais argues. And he's right. His book is worth a look for anyone feeling brave enough to journey into the language of physical science on its own ground: the terra firma of mathematics.

My brother couldn't explain the blueness of the sky, by the way. The same professor then asked a different question, about how a quarter-wave plate works, and Chris got it right away. He correctly answered the questions of the third professor and was sent away. Afterward, they told him he needed an understanding of electromagnetic scattering theory to answer the sky question correctly, and since he had not taken a course on that, he was allowed to pass. My brother promptly went to Arizona and learned to fly jets through the wild blue.

Anthony Doerr is the author of ''The Shell Collector" and ''About Grace."
