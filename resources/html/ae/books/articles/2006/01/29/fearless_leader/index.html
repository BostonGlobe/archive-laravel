Fearless leader

Churchill's relationship with America shaped the war -- and the peace that followed

By David M. Shribman  |  January 29, 2006

Churchill and America
By Martin Gilbert
Free Press, 501 pp., illustrated, $30

In the year that Franklin Roosevelt and Adolf Hitler both took office, a sometime politician named Winston Churchill sent a copy of a book he had written to the president of the United States. ''With earnest best wishes for the success of the greatest crusade of modern times," he wrote. That note began a friendship and a correspondence that would change both men -- and the world.

Of course the first crusade (rescuing the Western capitalist economy) would soon be succeeded by a second one, equal in challenge and urgency (saving Western democracy), and in that second crusade the relationship of Churchill and Roosevelt would be nothing short of indispensable. They coached, comforted, cajoled each other. Together they nurtured the one value (the endurance of freedom) that was most under siege. They cobbled together an alliance that would keep the English-speaking peoples together, an alliance that in fundamental ways endures to this day.

This alliance is the backdrop of ''Churchill and America," the latest volume out of the Churchill factory presided over by Martin Gilbert, himself one of the great treasures of the English-speaking people. Gilbert is the official biographer of Churchill but he is more than that; he is a writer of skill and grace and a historian without peer. This newest contribution plows over familiar territory, to be sure, but like the principals of the piece, the reader does not grow weary. That itself is a remarkable achievement.

The relationship between Roosevelt and Churchill was the centerpiece of ''No Ordinary Time," Doris Kearns Goodwin's remarkable portrait of the Roosevelt household during the war. But the Goodwin book is only one leg of the stool of understanding this relationship. The six volumes of Churchill's World War II history (the matched set that sits on your parents' bookshelf but that you, missing one of the great reading experiences of our time, sadly have never examined) constitute the second leg. Gilbert's new work is the third.

For in this volume is more than the 1,700 messages FDR and Churchill exchanged, more than the nine wartime meetings they held. This is a portrait of Churchill's broader relationship with the United States, not simply his wartime relationship. It begins with his mother, who was American, and continues through his wilderness years, pauses at his ''Iron Curtain" speech at Fulton, Mo., touches on his often-forgotten peacetime administration in the 1950s, and brings the reader all the way to his honorary American citizenship at the end of his life. Churchill had many relationships -- familial, marital, political -- but his relationship with the United States should certainly be counted among the most important.

Because Churchill loved America: its spaces, its cities, its vigor, its idealism, even its folly. There were moments of despair, desperation, and hopelessness, to be sure, in this relationship; Roosevelt did not, or could not, respond swiftly enough, or generously enough, to Churchill's entreaties in the period when Great Britain was at war and the United States was at peace. Toward the end of the war there were frustrations, too, inevitable ones as the United States began to assume the role in the world that Britain (reluctantly) relinquished.

Here is a good glimpse into some of that frustration. In 1938, just after the Munich Agreement, Churchill delivered a radio broadcast to the United States, saying there was ''no doubt where American conviction and sympathies lie" but asking the reluctant giant across the Atlantic this chilling question: ''Will you wait until British freedom and independence have succumbed, and then take up the cause, when it is three-quarters ruined, yourselves alone?"

Even so, Churchill never lost the belief that Gilbert uses to open his book -- the notion that, as he said in the United States the year before Roosevelt became president, ''whatever the pathway of the future may bring, we can face it more safely, more comfortably, and more happily if we travel it together, like good companions."

Churchill and Roosevelt (and, more broadly, Churchill and America) were, above all, good companions. It was a companionship that won the war and fashioned the peace and, moreover, gave shape to the world we knew and now know. That is in part because Churchill had more than just knowledge of, and affection for, America. He experienced America.

He wrote for American magazines, including the Nation, the Saturday Evening Post, and Colliers. He traveled widely in the United States, taking a special interest in Civil War battlefields. He met many of the signature Americans of his time, including Woodrow Wilson, Bernard Baruch (who would become a lifelong friend), Al Smith, Charlie Chaplin, Howard Heinz, John D. Rockefeller, and Walter Lippmann. That roster is as good a portrait of America -- as good a course in America -- as any available, then or now.

There is a well-known and well-loved story that involves one of Churchill's many prolonged visits to the White House. The prime minister stepped out of the bath and wrapped himself in a towel, all the while dictating to his valet. The towel dropped. At precisely that moment FDR entered the bedroom. Churchill said: ''You see, Mr. President, I have nothing to conceal from you."

In truth, Churchill could never conceal his convictions or his sentiments. The former were preoccupied with freedom, the latter with passion. His passion for freedom was what fueled his passion for America. He was better for it, for course. But, and this is the measure of a great relationship, we were better for it, too. We still are.

David M. Shribman, former Washington bureau chief of the Globe, is executive editor of the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette.
