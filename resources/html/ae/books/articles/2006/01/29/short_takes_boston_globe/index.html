Short takes

By Amanda Heller  |  January 29, 2006

She Got Up Off the Couch
By Haven Kimmel
Free Press, 304 pp., $24

In this second volume of memoirs, the gifted Haven Kimmel regales us with tales of her girlhood years as the Pippi Longstocking of Mooreland, Ind. Wild-haired and barefoot, she was gypsy queen of the tomboys. She ate pebbles and stuffed beans up her nose. At Christian summer camp she remained a stubborn apostate. Once she broke her arm so badly that a wizard surgeon had to be summoned; even he was impressed by the damage.

The ''she" of the title refers to the author's pious, downtrodden mother. Heeding a sign from a feminist God, she stopped waiting for her ne'er-do-well husband to drag the family out of poverty, earned a college degree in record time, and got a job as a teacher, a domestic revolution that intrigued yet puzzled her youngest child.

Although the genre has been under attack lately for false advertising, we need not inspect Kimmel's memoir too closely for the seams between fact and fancy. All children occupy a parallel universe, which intersects occasionally with the real world they must eventually inhabit. Kimmel brings an adult's insight and humor -- often hilarious -- to the vivid, innocent immediacy of childhood experience. The result by any name is art.

Covering: The Hidden Assault on Our Civil Rights
By Kenji Yoshino
Random House, 304 pp., $24.95

Yale law professor and sometime poet Kenji Yoshino introduces a new term into the American social lexicon. In our ''Don't ask, don't tell" culture, ''covering" is the new ''passing," the new ''closet." ''To cover," says Yoshino, ''is to tone down a disfavored identity" under duress, whether overt or covert. If a man mentions his girlfriend, it's unremarkable; if he mentions his boyfriend, it's considered offensive, and there will be a price to pay. By distinguishing between being and seeming, civil rights law only enforces covering. You can't be fired for being black or Latino, but you can for wearing cornrows or speaking Spanish, cultural markers that offend against nothing but an arbitrary demand for conformity.

This is no conventional work of legal scholarship. The precedents Yoshino cites are drawn, with a searing emotional honesty, from his own troubled experience as the American-born son of Japanese parents, forever perceived as a foreigner in his native land. It took him years to come to terms with his homosexuality; he couldn't bear to pile one burdensome identity onto another.

Is authenticity for all a constitutional right, and if so, is law the way to achieve it? Provocative and affecting, ''Covering" challenges us to be as open with one another as Yoshino is willing to be with us.

The Ruins of California
By Martha Sherrill
Penguin, 318 pp., $24.95

Martha Sherrill examines the predicament of growing up absurd in 1970s California. Inez Ruin (a family name more forced than convincing), a child of serial divorce, lives in Los Angeles with her Latina mother and makes occasional forays to San Francisco to visit her father, Paul, a high-tech entrepreneur, a scion of old money, and a magnetic narcissist with a limitless supply of new girlfriends for Inez to meet.

As she grows into her teens, Inez finds herself dangerously drawn to the hip glamour of Paul's hedonistic existence, and to the person who best understands her unrequited love for her father -- her older half brother.

Surfing, sports cars, caftans, drugs, microchips, est: the novel is a veritable California time capsule. It is nevertheless a little too Californian in its ''whatever" attitude toward narrative. Incident is the essence of fiction; without enough of it, artfully shaped, catatonia sets in. Sherrill's evocation of timely details is appealing, but the basic structure on which they're strung lacks direction and urgency.

Amanda Heller is a critic and editor who lives in Newton. See ''Bookings," Page E8, for information on a local appearance by Kenji Yoshino.
