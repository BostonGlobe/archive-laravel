The apprentice

Through her new novel's heroine, and her own journals, Gail Godwin reveals the maturation of a young, hungry writer

By Madeleine Blais  |  January 8, 2006

Queen of the Underworld
By Gail Godwin
Random House, 336 pp., $24.95

The Making of a Writer: Journals, 1961-1963
By Gail Godwin
Edited by Rob Neufeld
Random House, 333 pp., $25.95

The late great Washington columnist Mary McGrory once said that she always felt a little sorry for people who had never worked at a newspaper, and certainly readers of Gail Godwin's first new novel in six years, ''Queen of the Underworld," will likely feel the same way after observing Emma Gant find her fate as a feature writer at the fictional Miami Star.

The wizardry of this novel is its pitch-perfect rendition of what it was like to be a true solo artist, a female of ambition coming out of the 1950s America, where social expectations for women did not routinely include real careers in big cities at major media outlets. The narrative provides a wonderful balancing act between Emma's undisputed talent and intelligence and her age-appropriate silliness and narcissism. Not only does she steal another woman's husband, but she also has the gall to be jealous of her beautiful pampered feet. In Emma we have a character in love with words and with publishing who remains resolutely upbeat: She is Sylvia Plath and Anne Sexton with some of the insecurities but without the killer angst. As such, the novel is something of a remediation of the once commonly held, but not necessarily accurate, assumption that literary talent and outsize ambition in a young woman are a deadly combination.

The wide-eyed, world-hungry, note-taking heroine is a newly minted University of North Carolina graduate who heads south in the summer of 1959 at the start of what was widely assumed would be Fidel Castro's short-lived Cuban revolution. Emma wants nothing less than to become a great writer, to learn how to tell the story behind the story and to avoid the quiet desperation of small towns where people are ''steeped like old tea bags in their humdrum lives." Her plan is to become a ''crack journalist in the daytime," and in the evenings and on weekends, she will ''slip across the border into fiction. . . . My journalism would support me until I became a famous novelist. Perhaps I would become a famous journalist on the side, if I could manage both."

Godwin also wisely casts Miami as a main character. It is filled as usual with a boisterous mix of humanity -- kept women, gangsters (one of whom is clearly a Meyer Lansky clone), Holocaust survivors, poolside musicians, cranky retirees, political refugees, and hard-boiled editors -- who all rate Emma's fierce evaluating gaze. She is the proverbial ingenue on the make, the kind of Jamesian character on whom nothing is lost. Blasphemous though it may be in some circles to acknowledge this, Emma takes the time-honored if hardly noble path of going through a powerful male benefactor to help her realize her dream job, landing in Miami thanks to the shepherding graces of her lover. ''My first married man as well as my first Jewish man," she exclaims breathlessly. Paul Nightingale is a hotelier twice her age whom she met as an employee at his resort in her home state. He is fond of pointing out the difference in their years with comments like ''You were just starting high school" when he recalls the time he founded his first club on Miami Beach, or ''You'd just started grade school" when invoking the war years in Miami Beach when it was used as a training range for soldiers.

Such is Emma's peculiar and potent combination of neediness and wiles that even Paul's wife, Bev, has fallen, or at least has pretended to fall, under Emma's spell. Emma's brittleness, or at least her armor, appears to be the direct result of an unfortunate relationship with a creepy stepfather who liked to visit her room in the middle of the night and feel her up under the bedcovers. ''Nocturnal raids" is her euphemism dismissing the violation. She also lost her father as an infant. Having survived both events, with humor and brains intact, she appears to be able to survive anything. Among her ''weapons," she lists ''guile, subterfuge, teeth-gritting staying power, and the ability, when necessary, to shut down my heart."

The title of the book refers to a former madam in a famous local house of prostitution who is the subject of a series by that name at the Star, but obviously the title works for Emma as well as she aims to become the queen of her own private underworld. Emma begins in the salt mines of routine obituaries, angling all the while to get assignments that will draw attention to her wit and her enterprise. She relishes the intrigue of the city room, the jockeying for prestigious beats, the high that ensues while meeting deadlines, even finding ''something erotic about a press run, with its increasing tempo of excitement, its acceleration toward full speed and no return." Her big break comes when Stella Rossignol, a noted perfumer and Paul's beloved aunt, dies, and Emma gets to write a substantial profile of this spunky woman who planned her career while imprisoned in the Nazi death camps as a way to distract herself from the odors.

Emma pitches her tent in the tropics in the Julia Tuttle Hotel, named after the ''Mother of Miami" who convinced Henry Flagler to bring his railroad south to her city. It is there in the commotion of the lobby that Emma gets her first lesson in what longtime residents of Miami have always known: It is a town where you wonder not only what people do, but what they really do. Thus the socially polished Latin dentist is not importing the drills he says he is, but trafficking in explosives as part of the plan to wrest Cuba from the ''bearded ones" who have taken over.

The Julia Tuttle is bristling with the kind of drama that enthralls Emma; its only drawback is the slow-moving elevator to her room on the fifth floor of the 12-story hotel. The symbolism of such a sluggish conveyance is not lost on a young woman who is in such perpetual motion that it takes her less than a week to wear down the heels on her one pair of good shoes. At night she writes in her ''Go, Tar Heels!" notebook, making lists of the day's new vocabulary words and bons mots (''commensurate" and ''sic transit notitia" and ''curiosity = a reporter's chronic ailment, to be exacerbated at every opportunity"). Emma is thrilled to embrace her new polyglot world, to learn the difference between a yarmulke and a guayabera, and to learn such life lessons as sweet drinks mixed with alcohol are by definition a bad idea.

After little more than a week on the job, her success has netted her a position in the women's department of the Broward bureau. This prospect strikes Emma as hell and Siberia put together, squared, and then distilled into a hideous workaday nectar. To sweeten the news, her boss dangles the plum that now she will have the chance to learn more about municipal government. The very notion fills Emma with weariness in advance, and at novel's end she is plotting her escape by finally writing the story behind the story.

''Queen of the Underworld" is a delight in and of itself, but what makes it even more mesmerizing is its companion piece, the simultaneous publication of the journals the author kept during the time when she was herself a reporter in Miami, at the Herald. There she was famously fired in a memo from an editor that she dutifully records. I worked at the Herald from 1979 to 1987, and these words were often mysteriously found slipped under the carriage of the typewriters of female reporters, perhaps as a cautionary tale or maybe just to provide a laugh about the antediluvian days when it was apparently wrong for women to have great expectations:

''I have spent more time working and worrying over your future than I have spent on the entire rest of the staff combined. I must confess I've been a failure. I apologize for my mistakes. But the fact remains that I cannot see any further benefit from my efforts or yours and I am convinced it would be to your benefit to find someplace to 'start over.' This has been harsher than I intended it to be. I really feel badly that I have failed to make a good reporter out of obviously promising material. I hope you can use this experience somewhere but I'm afraid you won't do it successfully until you look facts in the face and at the same time quit expecting to get to the moon in one day."

The journals document Godwin's first few years after her dismissal from the Miami Herald, when the restless writer explored Europe and New York City and even went home from time to time. These youthful writings provide the author with a wonderful ally as she captures the contradictory nature of Emma, who is conniving and naive, shy and aggressive, faltering and self-assured, all at once.

The journals are not the least bit necessary for enjoying the novel, but they would likely appeal to readers interested in the artistic process who want to compare the raw footage with the finished product, to check out how the seam was made and to examine the sturdiness of the workmanship. As such, they are a gold mine.

As in all self-respecting journals, the writing is a bit scattershot, with some lengthy, easily accessible set pieces and the usual truncated renditions of events whose deepest emotional content can only be hinted at, since their long-range significance requires the passage of time.

What is boggling is to see a stray comment by a real person recorded in the early '60s (''Each year I learn to say less and less") make its way into the mouth of an invented character in 2006 or to see recycled the headline or the punchy lead paragraph from a real story from decades earlier. The generosity of the author in sharing these volumes, which by definition have the quality of rumpled bed linens, speaks to a risk-taking streak that she appears to have in common with her main character.

Like other shining females in literature, Emma Gant is, above all and most endearingly, a woman of appetite at life's feast who refuses to nibble when she can devour.
