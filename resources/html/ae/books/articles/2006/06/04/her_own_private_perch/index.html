Her own private perch

Dogged research bolsters biography, but secret world of Harper Lee remains out of reach

By Dan Cryer  |  June 4, 2006

Mockingbird: A Portrait of Harper Lee
By Charles J. Shields
Holt, 337 pp., illustrated, $25

What engrossing drama in the story of Harper Lee -- the rise to fame of the author of one of the best-selling American novel s in the 20th century, followed by a flight from the limelight and the humiliation of writer's block, a deep-seated friendship ultimately sabotaged, and the apparent absence of a love life. Unfortunately, given Lee's desire for privacy, any biographer is likely to be frustrated in the effort to bring this narrative to vivid life.

Charles J. Shields, author of biographies for young readers, has gamely persisted anyway. In the face of his subject's refusal to be interviewed, Shields proves a dogged researcher. He has sought out residents of her Alabama home town; tracked down countless classmates and acquaintances; discovered out-of-the-way early interviews; and sifted through letters by her literary agents and erstwhile pal Truman Capote.

``Mockingbird" is as amiable and unassuming as its subject but devoid of her sharp wit. While useful for framing the arc of Lee's life, it is driven by necessity to expand where the evidence is more extensive (and less relevant) and to shrink where the questions are more to the point. Thus we learn more about the making of ``To Kill a Mockingbird" into a movie than about why its author has been a one-book wonder.

Shields dismisses the hoary canard that Lee couldn't write a follow-up because she didn't write it in the first place. There's simply no evidence that Capote served as behind-the-scenes author. Had the rumors been true, given his enormous ego and envy over its Pulitzer Prize, he surely would have hinted at co authorship.

On the other hand, Shields demonstrates that Lee was indispensable to researching Capote's ``In Cold Blood." While his arrogance and fey style alienated small-town Kansans, her warmth and genuineness charmed them into opening doors. Had they remained closed, his true-crime book would have lacked its animating, mesmerizing details.

The two had grown up as playmates in Monroeville, near the Gulf Coast, under very different circumstances. He was a neglected child dumped with relatives, she the adored daughter of one its most respected citizens. A. C. Lee, the model for her novel's Atticus Finch, was a lawyer, state legislator, newspaper publisher , and Methodist churchman. Because Frances Lee suffered from manic depression, she was incapable of close relationships with their four children. Nelle Harper (the first name dropped from her byline because it was often mispronounced as Nellie) was the youngest, born in 1926.

Shields nimbly escorts us through Nelle's progression from scrappy tomboy (``a fearsome stomach-puncher, foot-stomper , and hair-puller") to wise-cracking, cigarette-smoking, fashion-resistant co ed at the University of Alabama.

As children, she and Capote would pound out stories on a typewriter her father had given them. Once in college, Lee wrote caustic columns for the student humor magazine. Nonetheless, she was poised for a career in law, like A. C. and her oldest sister, Alice. Eventually, though, Nelle came to loathe her studies and dropped out of law school a semester shy of graduation. In 1949, at the age of 23, she followed Capote to New York in pursuit of that chimera, a writing career.

Eleven difficult years passed before ``Mockingbird" changed Lee's life forever. In the meantime, she worked as an airline ticket agent and wrote in off -hours. Then, on Christmas Day 1956, her friends Michael Martin Brown and Joy Brown, a composer-lyricist and a former ballet dancer, presented her with a singular gift, enough money to allow her to quit her job and concentrate on writing.

Shields speculates that Lee's creative breakthrough was due to ``the power of community . . . a handful of people in the arts who believed in her." Once that community disintegrated, her powers were so diminished that she was unable to produce another book.

Aside from the Browns -- and entr ee provided by Capote -- Lee's sustaining forces were her agents, Maurice Crain and Annie Laurie Williams, and her editor at Lippincott, Tay Hohoff. The aspiring novelist had written fragments of a story based on a real-life rape case in Monroeville, but struggled to develop a credible point of view. Crain and Williams added faith in her talent and a cottage in Connecticut for work or escape. Hohoff counseled and cajoled, knowing that Lee needed ``professional help in organizing her material and developing a sound plot structure."

Once published in 1960, the book became a phenomenon. But after a moment in the spotlight, its author retreated to a quiet life divided between Monroeville and Manhattan's Upper East Side. She hoped to be a latter-day Jane Austen, paying homage to the small-town South.

Alas, Lee remains the poster girl for F. Scott Fitzgerald's adage that there are ``no second acts in American life." She kept writing, but no second book emerged. Perhaps, as Shields believes, it was because her support group faded away. Capote destroyed their friendship. Crain died, then Hohoff. According to a hard-to-credit account by her sister, a burglar in the 1970s made off with the manuscript of another novel. In the next decade, Lee began research on an ``In Cold Blood"-like nonfiction book based on a serial murderer in Alabama, but the effort came to nothing.

The truth may be more straightforward. Perhaps Lee recognized that when the first act is so revered, there is simply no need for another. As she reportedly told a cousin, ``Richard, when you're at the top there's only one way to go."

Dan Cryer is a contributor to `` The salon.com Reader's Guide to Contemporary Authors. "
