Clean living? Not quite, but Ruth was a force of nature

By Glenn C. Altschuler  |  June 4, 2006

The Big Bam: The Life and Times of Babe Ruth
By Leigh Montville
Doubleday, 390 pp., illustrated, $26.95

As a bulked-up Barry Bonds bears down on the all-time home-run record, with Baseball Commissioner Bud Selig and former US senator George Mitchell in hot pursuit, Babe Ruth looms large in American popular culture. He was, Leigh Montville notes, ``a big and natural man who did big things," sans steroids, with nary an appearance before a grand jury or a subcommittee of Congress. Ruth has been the subject of more than two dozen biographies, but Montville, who has written for Sports Illustrated and The Boston Globe, thinks it's time to explain to ``the SportsCenter generation" why Babe was so much better than his contemporaries and why millions of Americans went ``gaga over him."

``The Big Bam" tells a familiar story. Montville takes Ruth from St. Mary's Industrial School for Boys to the Baltimore Orioles as Jack Dunn's ``Babe"; to stardom as a pitcher for the Boston Red Sox; to the decision of Red Sox owner Harry Frazee to sell him to the New York Yankees; to the Bronx, where the ``Sultan of Swat" built the most lucrative franchise in the history of sports; and to boredom in retirement, cut short by death from cancer in 1948. The Babe was hungry for love and excitement: He chased gin, skirts, silk shirts, horses, and hot dogs. Friendly to everyone, he was intimate with no one.

``The Big Bam" 's style is breezy and carried away by occasional gusts. Many stories about Ruth are apocryphal, and reliable information about his personal life is in short supply. ``The fog will be part of the magic," Montville maintains, by making Ruth at once ``the patron saint of American possibility" and ``forever accessible, universal."

Drawing on interviews and newly discovered material from Ruth's scrapbooks, Montville tries to puncture some of the myths that surround him. Ruth was not, he argues, ``an overweight fat man." After a disastrous 1925 season, the 31 -year -old Babe signed up for ``physical reconstruction" at the gym of Artie McGovern, a former flyweight boxer. He rose early, did leg lifts, strengthened abs and hips, sat in a sweat box, rode a stationary bike, and cut out red meats and sweets. In six weeks, he had lost 44 pounds and almost 9 inches off his waistline. His blood pressure rose from 107 to 128; his pulse rate dropped from 92 to 78. Ruth remained a big man with an oddly shaped body, but appearances now deceived. With the musculature of ``a Thoroughbred racehorse," Ruth had ``rediscovered the athlete at his core."

Or had he? Six weeks probably didn't alter the habits of a lifetime. He `` would not exactly become a leader of temperance and fitness movements," Montville acknowledges. The legend had it about right: Ruth was not (Mc)Govern-able.

Montville also adds new details to the portrait of Ruth as an irresponsible and indifferent husband and father. In 1914 he married Helen Woodford, a waitress he met at Landers Coffee Shop in Boston. He was 19 and she was 16. From the outset, ``there was an accommodation for infidelities." Woodford, Montville suggests, was not the mother of Dorothy Ruth. By 1929, when Helen died in a house fire, Ruth had lost track of her. He married Claire Hodgson three months after Helen's death. Claire put Babe on an allowance, enforced a curfew, limited beer consumption, and banned the daily racing form. She took ``firm control of his daily life," Montville claims . Her influence diminished over time. Seven years into the marriage, recalls Dorothy, who loathed her stepmother, not only were Claire's ``mental capacities for anything but baseball statistics and money slipping, but her lush beauty was deteriorating." Babe was on the road a lot anyway, without Claire, but he spent less and less time with his wife when the Yankees were at home.

His real homes were Yankee Stadium and home plate, which he touched every time he belted the ball out of the park. Almost single-handedly, Babe made baseball the national pastime. In 1920, the Yankees helped six American League franchises break attendance records. Ruth's contemporaries thought his compensation more than commensurate with his value to the team. One newspaper calculated that he'd make $4.33 a minute if he played all 154 games, at an average of one hour and 45 minutes per contest.

Montville puts his salary in a fresh perspective . Ruth's $52,000 salary in 1922 was worth about $500,000 in 2005 dollars (the average salary in the major leagues these days is $3 million). After a slow and arduous climb, he reached $80,000 in 1931, as much as President Hoover . After that, though he continued to be the game's premier home -run hitter and fan favorite, his salary went south. Asked to take a pay cut in 1932, Ruth said, ``I didn't see much of a depression with the Yankees." Nonetheless, Yankees owner Jacob Ruppert squeezed $5,000 out of him. The next year, $23,000 more was trimmed, and Babe was back to $52,000.

He deserved a lot more, because, as Montville claims, he was one of a kind . ``The Big Bam" doesn't change our perceptions of Ruth all that much . But it doesn't have to. Now and forever, the Babe stands with and for the boys of summer.
