A childhood of many hungers

By Janna Malamud Smith  |  June 11, 2006

Perishable: A Memoir
By Dirk Jamison
Chicago Review, 212 pp., illustrated, $22.95

Every memoir nowadays needs a hook, and for ``Perishable," Dirk Jamison's vivid, well-written story about seven difficult years in his family life, that hook is his father's midlife discovery of dumpster diving, and with it the notion that it would be a good thing to feed the family ``free" off the nation's discards. Anyone of a certain age will recall that dumpster diving first became a lifestyle choice in the 1960s as part of the decade's brief flirtation with anti materialism and anti-waste. It's probably just as well that this mode of recycling never went mainstream: While one can appreciate some of its philosophic sources -- think Monty Python meets Thoreau -- the night the Jamison family's banana dessert crawled with worms suggests that the outcome can be iffy.

But the memoir's power lies less in the actual grossness of what could appear on the dinner table and more in the putrification of the family soul -- for which the garbage-as-food provides a correlative. Jamison's story is a page-turning, well-told tale of his family's meltdown, and particularly of his parents ' inability to figure out the basics about each other or about caring for their children. One gets the sense that they wanted to parent, and tried, but that their own urgencies -- the mother's for security, the father's for freedom from responsibility -- trumped all else. The parents marry, separate, get together, separate, divorce. The father loses jobs and lugs home garbage treasures; the mother eats and diets and eats, and confides way too much in her son .

The book opens with a marvelous scene of Jamison's mother baking him a birthday cake, ignoring her son while ranting on the phone about the way her husband eats the hearts out of watermelons . Jamison's father dresses in a pirate costume for the birthday party, but his participation is short-lived: ``He says `arg' as I blow out the candles. Then he tilts up his eye patch and opens a mason jar full of banana wine that's been brewing above the fridge for two weeks." He chugs it down and spends the rest of the birthday night vomiting on the lawn.

As the book progresses, these quirky stories are followed by less benign ones: Jamison's mother repeatedly entrusts him to Gary, a Boy Scout master with a reputation for child molestation, even sending him off with his cousin and the man to an isolated cabin in the woods. Meanwhile, Jamison's father takes both sons with him to a drunken party where strippers are flinging off clothes. ``A woman next to us comments on how cute two little boys look standing in the middle of so much debauchery."

Toward the end of the book, Jamison identifies an instant when he feels his mother understands he has needs that ought to be met, even if she cannot meet them. But her sorry insight is a flash in an otherwise cold pan, and one well-drawn anecdote after another leaves you wide -eyed at the absence of safety in the family life. To make matters much worse, Jamison's older sister reacts to the parenting void by unleashing her frustration and sadism on him. She instigates what is perhaps the book's most heartbreaking moment when she arranges the gratuitous gelding of his beloved pony. Jamison is so traumatized in the aftermath that he hears voices.

Parental incompetence is a predicament for a memoir writer who must find a fresh angle on an oft -told tale, and a style of telling that neither scares off readers by overwhelming them with pain, nor alienates them by revealing self -pity in the writer. The honor code of the genre -- well exemplified by Tobias Wolff in ``This Boy's Life" -- dictates that the narrator cannot whine, that he must describe rather than belabor the impact of brutality received, and that he needs to focus as much on his own ``bad" as on other people's. The memoirist also gains points for hilarity, and for a well -developed sense of the absurd. Jamison grasps these rules, and what's more, he manages to convey some real feeling for both his obese Mormon mother and his feckless dad.

My quibble with the book is its paucity of context. Other than a few paragraphs about his father's own awful upbringing, Jamison offers no adequate backstory for either parent before their marriage -- really no context at all for anyone or anything that happens. The time is now, the place is here. There is nothing else. This approach conveys well the child's dumbfounded confusion at finding himself trapped in a crazy household: The reader's lack of knowledge mirrors the narrator's disorientation. On the other hand, this foreshortening also diminishes the story's depth. There is no past, no self -reflection, no perspective. The narrator describes what happened to him, but he gives away little about the larger impact. This reader hungered to know Jamison better . That said, the book is a keeper, and dumpster divers are going to be sorely pressed to find free copies.

Janna Malamud Smith is the author of ``My Father Is a Book."
