POP LIT

A book fiend and a devilish mystery in the city of angels

By Diane White  |  June 11, 2006

Literacy and Longing in L.A.
By Jennifer Kaufman and Karen Mack
Delacorte, 336 pp., $22

Shamus in the Green Room
By Susan Kandel
Morrow, 336 pp., $23.95

Owl Island
By Randy Sue Coburn
Ballantine, 354 pp., $23.95

Once again I'm up to my ears in chick lit, some of it very entertaining, like the first two of these three books. Both are inventive light reading.

You meet someone. You can't stand her. You end up the best of friends. It happens in life, and it happens in literature, too. Dora, named for Eudora Welty, is the depressed, neurotic book snob at the center of ``Literacy and Longing in L.A." She's pretty insufferable at first, but she grows on you. The novel is the work of two writers -- Jennifer Kaufman, a former writer for the Los Angeles Times, and Karen Mack, a film and television producer.

At 35, Dora is still nursing psychic wounds inflicted by a runaway father and an alcoholic mother. She's separated from her second husband, Palmer, the head of a Hollywood studio, and living in a high-rise apartment where she hides away and binges on books. A little wine, a bubble bath, a stack of books, and she's on a reading bender that may last for days. Her particular form of escape recalls her literature-loving mother, who, when Dora and her sister Virginia were small, would pile them in the car for drunken-driving tours of famous authors' homes.

Virginia, now married with a baby, tries to prod her sister into re-engaging reality. Dora's money is dwindling fast. She needs a job. She used to work as a reporter, but she's lost the confidence to apply for another newspaper job. When Dora leaves her apartment to buy books she meets Fred, a disheveled academic type who works at her favorite bookstore. Soon they're involved in a precarious emotional tango that's part lust, part literature. Fred invites Dora to meet his mother, Bea, who is taking care of Harper, the 6-year - old daughter of Fred's drug-addicted sister. Dora is drawn out of herself and into the lives of Harper and Bea, and she begins to sort out her problems.

The appended list of books and authors in ``Literacy and Longing in L.A." might give the impression that it's a very literary novel, perhaps dauntingly so. It's not, although you could say it's chick-literary. It's a slickly written, engaging story about a woman who prefers fiction to reality.

The delightful Cece Caruso is back in ``Shamus in the Green Room," the third in Susan Kandel's series featuring the perpetually 39-year-old ex-beauty queen turned mystery author/ amateur detective. These saucy, well-crafted mysteries are a lot of fun.

Cece is thrilled when superstar actor Rafe Simic options her biography of Dashiell Hammett, ``Dash!" But because Rafe would rather surf than read her book, much less Hammett, his manager, Will Levander, hires Cece to brief him on the subject. There's more to Rafe than his muscles, as Cece soon learns. ``Did Hollywood ruin him?" he asks, after Cece relates the sad story of Hammett's screenwriting career. Cece leads Rafe to San Francisco for a tour of Hammett-related sites, but their trip is cut short by a call from the LA coroner's office. The body of a woman, presumably a suicide, has been found, and the only clue to her identity is a waterlogged photo stuffed in the pocket of her jeans, a snapshot of a very young Rafe with his arm around a long-haired girl. Rafe identifies her as an ex-girlfriend, Maren Levander, sister of his manager. Cece senses something is amiss after she digs out another old photograph of Rafe and a young girl identified as Maren who looks nothing like the dead woman.

``Shamus in the Green Room" doesn't lack for subplots. Peter Gambino, Cece's police detective fiance, takes a dim view of all the time she's spending with Rafe. An outlaw surfer tries to teach her to surf. Fans of the series will be pleased that her passion for vintage clothing never gets old, much like Cece herself.

Randy Sue Coburn's ``Owl Island" is a melodramatic story of mothers and daughters, of secrets and illusions. Phoebe Allen, a 40-ish widow and single mother, has built a thriving fishing net business on Owl Island, in the Pacific Northwest. The wind - swept island, beautifully evoked by Coburn, is owned by a Native American tribe. For years it has been home to a small population of fishermen, artists , and artisans. Now a bridge has been built, linking the island to the mainland, attracting the sort of people who can afford architect-designed houses.

Among the new residents is a figure from Phoebe's past, Whitney Traynor, an acclaimed independent filmmaker. More than 20 years ago, when she was 18, Phoebe fell in love with the 31-year -old Traynor, then a disc jockey with a seductive manner and lots of ambition. She helped him write his first successful film, for which he gave her no credit. After a messy breakup, Phoebe quickly married someone else and soon found herself pregnant. She has never been sure if Whit or her late husband is the father of her daughter, Laurienne.

The narrative shifts among points of view, so we see the story not only through Phoebe's eyes, but also from the perspectives of Laurienne, Whit, and Whit's psychic young wife , Jasmine. Any suspense is undermined by what we learn of Whit. He reveals himself early on to be a self-centered jerk, so readers have a pretty good idea where it will end. It would have been interesting if Coburn had chosen a less predictable ending, but it's not that sort of novel.

Diane White writes every month about new light and popular fiction.
