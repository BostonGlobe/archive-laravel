BOOK REVIEW

Collection of literary profiles makes a surreal study

By Richard Eder, Globe Correspondent  |  June 21, 2006

Written Lives, By Javier Marías, Translated, from the Spanish, by Margaret Jull Costa, QUIM LLENAS, New Directions, 192 pp., $22.95

It was more than clocks that Salvador Dalí bent in what is no doubt the best-known of his paintings. It was the solemn nature of time itself; and physicists are still gingerly poking at the notion that it runs both forward and back.

Whether or not God (or Random Nondesign) is a Surrealist, it was no accident that one of the movement's founders was a Spaniard. The disposition toward a style of stand-on-your-head paradox has a Spanish root to it, with antic absurdity tapping into a deeper vein of reality than reality itself can manage, though without much chance of channeling it beyond the burble of the tapping.

History is cited, sometimes -- as facile epigram, perhaps, more than scholarly reasoning. The West's first great transoceanic empire collapsed, absurdly, with the Armada's huge galleons upended by the banty-cock Brits. Spain's land was stripped barren for spars and masts to sail back treasure that became fool's gold as the world came to find more plunder in trade than in plunder.

The future had left Spain behind while other nations set their course s toward progress. Yet as Haitians, never much in the progress line, say that beyond the mountains there are mountains, the Spanish surreal tradition has it that beyond the future there is the future. Of the mountain-engendering sort, and not one to pin serious plans on.

In this blithely absurdist tradition are such late Buñuel films as ``The Discreet Charm of the Bourgeoisie." There were the ``greguerias" invented in the 1920s by Ramón Gómez de la Serna : perverse one-liners, tiny shards of distorting mirrors easier to cite than describe: ``A crocodile is a handbag traveling on its own." ``The brain is a collection of wrinkled ideas."

And, long before, Don Quixote's moonlit disruptions across the received ideas of his time.

``Written Lives," a collection of miniature literary biographies, flows within such a vein. Javier Marí as, whose work has won increasing international attention, wrote them a half dozen years ago.

Marí as dresses the facts as something very close to fiction, cutting the weights that hold them to the ground and releasing them to bob in an odd and oddly buoyant aerial display.

In a foreword he explains that, some time ago, he had written brief biographical sketches for a collection of short stories by unknown writers. So fragmentary and bizarre were his facts that he was accused of making them up.

He noticed, though, that when read together ``these briefest of brief biographies constituted another story, doubtless as unique and spectral as the stories themselves." It occurred to him to use the same approach with major writers, ``to treat these well-known literary figures as if they were fictional characters, which may well be how all writers, whether famous or obscure, would secretly like to be treated."

Like the ``greguerias," Marí as's bits are easier to cite than explain:

William Faulkner. Shabby yet elegant dress. Avid horseman who keeps falling off. Buries his 5-day-old daughter himself, disdaining a funeral because ``she had no time to become a person."

Isak Dinesen. Unable to have sex after contracting syphilis from her husband, maintains Devil recompensed her with the power to deliver stories. A solitary and eccentric life. She has the cook's little boy dressed up each night to dine with her. To writers: ``Do the things you can see; they will show you what you can't see."

Mme. du Deffand. Sharp and desolate wit. A cardinal relates that the beheaded St. Dionysus miraculously walked 5 miles with his head under his arm. ``It is only the first step that is difficult," she observes.

The brevity, in other words the speed, is part of the effect. A seemingly arbitrary selectivity makes a juxtaposition that transforms. An account that is all hollows creates a powerful reverberation.

Does it echo what these writers have written, or the transformations they set off in our mind?

Deeper hollows and an even more haunting echo come at the end, in a speeded-up version of the sketches. We get a series of photographs with a few lines on each:

Dickens with pen, deliberately nonchalant. ``He is not pretending to be writing, which would be both vulgar and difficult to do, instead he is pretending to be thinking." Mallarme, anxiously courting the camera ``with a naive sense of wonderment at progress, as he might wonder at a sonnet ending in -yx." Nietszche, near madness, staring: ``His face is that of someone rushing forward at high speed: it is as if his composure were held together with pins."

And T .S. Eliot's tight, contained, orderly formality gets an utterly unexpected switch: ``His whole face exudes a strange, almost vehement sense of hope, and that is why he could also be an inventor."

Marí as has pointed out what we might never think of seeing; and immediately recognize. His bent clocks tell a different time. They shift our appointments.
