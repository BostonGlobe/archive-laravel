Black holes

A passionate critique of African-American leadership has some flaws of its own

By Glenn C. Loury  |  September 10, 2006

Enough: The Phony Leaders, Dead-End Movements, and Culture of Failure That Are Undermining Black America -- and What We Can Do About It
By Juan WilliamsCrown, 243 pp., $25

Juan Williams, a political correspondent for National Public Radio and frequent commentator on public affairs for the Fox News Channel, has thrown down the gauntlet in his provocative and brave new book, ``Enough: The Phony Leaders, Dead-End Movements, and Culture of Failure That Are Undermining Black America -- and What We Can Do About It." The book is, on the one hand, a candid indictment of contemporary black youth culture, adherence to which, Williams believes, lowers the odds of success in mainstream society. It is also a spirited critique of black leadership , which, Williams alleges, has been guilty of failing to renounce these dysfunctional cultural tendencies while continuing to portray black people before the larger society as preeminent victims of American racism.

This very readable book is written with verve, in a crisp, direct journalistic style that graphically makes the author's case, while barely managing to conceal his rage. It is way past time, Williams intimates, for someone to tell the truth about what, by and large , has been the pathetic performance of African-Americans in this post-civil rights era. It is time someone told black youngsters to stop whining, to pull their pants up to the waist, to hit their books , and keep their noses clean. Why, Williams wonders, with the state of lower-class black American life being so dire, have the most outspoken black public figures (e.g., Jesse Jackson and Al Sharpton -- yes, Williams is naming names here) been so unwilling to do this?

Williams is, of course, right to be concerned about the social condition of poor blacks -- it is bad, and on some fronts is getting much worse. For instance, the number of incarcerated black men has more than quadrupled since 1980. (Experts agree that the now 20-year -old war on drugs has played a major role in this historically unprecedented run-up in black incarceration.)

Moreover, according to studies undertaken by the US Department of Education, on average black 17-year-olds perform at about the same level as 13-year-old whites on tests of reading and math skills. (The federal No Child Left Behind law does not seem to be changing this.)

What with the success of the civil rights movement at fighting discrimination, and given the dramatic change for the better in racial attitudes among whites over the past half-century, these continuing racial deficits suggest to Williams, and to many others, that further progress can be made only if cultural reforms are instituted within the black community. And such reforms can happen, Williams argues, only if black leaders change their tune : They should stop complaining about racism and start exhorting their co-racialists to live better lives.

One has to admire Williams's courage in publicly siding with Bill Cosby's widely reported criticisms of the black poor (the black ``lower economic types " are ``not living up to their end of the deal, " Cosby has famously said). But courage and forthrightness are no substitutes for a cogent social analysis.

Any serious consideration of why progress for the black poor since 1965 has been so slow and uneven would have to look beyond the ``culture" of black ghetto dwellers, and beyond the public performances of black ``leaders, " so as to reckon with larger social, political , and economic developments in American society. Specifically, a serious discussion of reform must consider why the social policy - making process at all levels of government has failed to come to grips with the increasingly dire condition of our nation's most disadvantaged persons.

Denouncing Sharpton's opportunism or the sleaziness of lyrics in some rap music, while laudable in itself, is just too easy. Lowering the teen pregnancy rate, raising test scores, and reducing criminal offenses among millions of impoverished, socially segregated, ghetto-dwelling youngsters is a much taller order.

I am aware of no instance in human history where behavioral change on the scale that would be needed to reverse the dire straits of America's black poor has come about through the exhortation of communal leaders. Social pathologies were not unknown in the European immigrant communities of the early 20th-century America. Arguably, they were overcome not by hortatory campaigns of behavioral reform, but rather by massive social policy innovations like the New Deal and the GI Bill.

In this regard, Williams's indictment of the ``culture of failure " among poor blacks is especially to be questioned. In this modern age of mass communication and transcontinental social interactions, it is virtually meaningless to ascribe intrinsic cultural characteristics to racially defined populations. Where, one wonders while reading this book, does Williams suppose that black culture ends , and American culture begins ? Hip-hop, for example, is a commercial enterprise whose content is as much influenced, one could argue, by the urge to adolescent rebellion in the broad American middle class (to whom most rap music is sold) as it is by some cultural tic peculiar to the residents of urban black ghettos. And, if hundreds of thousands of black drug sellers are being imprisoned, this is in large part a result of the fact that many millions of black, white, Asian , and Hispanic buyers patronize the casual drug markets to be found in our urban centers.

My point here is that what Williams calls ``black culture " is intimately connected with the larger American commercial, social , and cultural ethos. Better black leadership will do nothing to change the latter, though better American leadership might help. Black Americans are not a ``people " in the sense that Williams seems to intend when he calls us to higher ground. That is, blacks are not a coherent social and political collective in control of the institutions, media, and social policies that most affect the lives of their children. What blacks can do on our own about these problems is not nothing, but it is far less than Williams seems to think. In a national conversation about how to help the ghetto poor, the only relevant ``people " are the American people.

It's a shame that, somewhere between renouncing the reparations movement and exposing the shenanigans of the Rev. Al Sharpton , Juan Williams didn't say so.

Glenn C. Loury is Merton P. Stoltz Professor of the Social Sciences and Professor of Economics at Brown University.
