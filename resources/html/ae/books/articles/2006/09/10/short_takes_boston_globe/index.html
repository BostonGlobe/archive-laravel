Short Takes

By Amanda Heller  |  September 10, 2006

Rise and Shine
By Anna Quindlen
Random House, 269 pp., $24.95

For years, Bridget Fitzmaurice has been leading the high life vicariously through her older sister, Meghan. Bridget, a social worker at a Bronx housing project, lives in a one-bedroom walk - up with her cat, while Meghan is only the most famous woman in America, host of the TV morning show ``Rise and Shine." Suddenly, in an unguarded moment, Meghan loses it all -- stardom, adulation, the celebrity social whirl -- declaring it worthless. So what does that make Bridget? She has the rest of the book to figure it out.

Like her heroine, Anna Quindlen takes her time deciding what the novel is all about. Once Meghan exits, trailing clouds of paparazzi, our attention turns by default to Bridget, who is, as fictional heroines go, just another unfulfilled single woman whose biological clock is striking midnight. At length, when the plot boils over in a crisis touching everyone, Meghan returns, and the two sisters are forced to see themselves and each other as they really are.

Though attenuated in structure and sometimes superficial, even stereotypical, in characterization, the novel has its strong suits, not least Quindlen's dissection of the vicious Manhattan meritocracy. The contrast between the sisters may be contrived, but the family feeling is genuine, like a daisy in a hothouse.

The Driftless Area
By Tom Drury
Atlantic Monthly, 224 pp., $22

Not since ``Twin Peaks" has the rural surreal had such an artful airing. Tom Drury sets ``The Driftless Area" in the midwestern hinterland of the same name, the place the Ice Age forgot, as did apparently nearly every other event of moment since then.

In what turns out to be a prologue, we meet Pierre, the novel's gormless romantic hero, as a teenager whose girlfriend is about to break up with him by proxy. Owing to this and other shocks to the system, several years later we find Pierre still in the little town where, so to speak, he grew up, now a bartender with a drinking problem and more than enough drift in his makeup to compensate for the lack of it in the local landscape. One winter day he falls through the ice on a pond and is rescued by a beautiful young woman who may not be what she seems, and who is in the confidence of an older gentleman who is most certainly, spookily, not what he appears.

Pierre eventually runs afoul of a band of desperado s, who gain the upper hand, when suddenly the author throws open the door to a fourth dimension, and the story folds up and slips through to an unsettling, ironic, and strangely lovely coda. Drury is nothing less than a wizard.

The Revenge of Gaia: Earth's Climate Crisis and the Fate of Humanity
By James Lovelock
Basic, 176 pp., $25

Decades ago the British climate scientist James Lovelock coined the ``Gaia theory," which holds that Earth is a living organism -- and that the developed world is slowly killing her. Now this aged Jeremiah speaks again, telling of doom more imminent and dreadful than even he had forecast. The fire we lit to keep us warm is consuming the planet. The 30,000 weather-related deaths in Europe's torrid summer of 2003 were but a distant early warning of the fate that ``global heating" (Lovelock's ungentle term) has in store for us. The die, he says, is cast. Although disaster for ourselves or our descendants cannot be averted, Lovelock recommends some radical stalling tactics, which range from controversial (nuclear power) to bizarre (synthesizing food and returning farmland to nature).

Readers without scientific degrees may lack the credentials to weigh Lovelock's reasoning, but his fury, his fear, and his sorrow are unmistakable. Give Stephen King a rest. If you want to read a terrifying book, this is the one.

Amanda Heller is a critic and editor who lives in Newton.
