Once more into the breach

An enthusiastic look at scholarship and spats inspired by Shakespeare

By William E. Cain  |  September 24, 2006

The Shakespeare Wars: Clashing Scholars, Public Fiascoes, Palace Coups
By Ron Rosenbaum
Random House, 601 pp., $35

In ``The Shakespeare Wars," the journalist and critic Ron Rosenbaum focuses on ``Shakespeare the writer," whose dramatic language possesses ``bottomless" depths of meaning. Exploring the richness of the texts both as literary works and as scripts for the theater and for film adaptation, Rosenbaum pursues the mystery of ``the Shakespearean" -- the uniquely captivating and perhaps even frightening nature of this writer's verbal artistry and intimate understanding of character and consciousness.

Rosenbaum begins by describing his response in 1970 to Peter Brook's acclaimed production of ``A Midsummer Night's Dream." He had studied Shakespeare on the graduate level at Yale, but it was this later experience that made Shakespeare electrifying and propelled Rosenbaum to immerse himself in the poems and plays, which he read and re read, examined and brooded over, in many different editions.

In a sense, then, ``The Shakespeare Wars" is Rosenbaum's intellectual autobiography: It recounts key periods in his investigations of Shakespeare. But it is also a work of literary reportage and analysis that draws extensively upon Rosenbaum's informative, often entertaining, interviews with scholars, editors, actors, and directors.

We hear about their interests, discoveries, and arguments, and, as he did with the intellectuals, philosophers, and others he interviewed for his previous book, ``Explaining Hitler" (1998), Rosenbaum puts his cast of characters in ``The Shakespeare Wars" into a lively conversation with one another. All the while, he is in the middle of things himself, reflecting on his experiences and playing a prominent role in the back-and-forth of discussion and debate.

Rosenbaum is not writing for an academic audience, but is, instead, seeking to enlighten general readers about major changes in Shakespeare scholarship and in the world of the theater that, in recent years, have been proposed and heatedly debated. As Rosenbaum's subtitle implies, these changes have begun to reorient our conception of Shakespeare's work in revolutionary ways.

Rosenbaum does an especially fine job in the chapters that describe bold new scholarly approaches to the editing of Shakespeare's plays, ``Hamlet" and ``King Lear" in particular, and many readers outside the academy will be surprised, possibly even shocked, by the innovative (and controversial) work in the field of textual scholarship and criticism he surveys.

In the past, an editor typically operated under the assumption that it was his or her task to scrutinize as carefully as possible the available Quarto and Folio versions in order to supply us with the text that, in the editor's view, Shakespeare intended. By bringing these versions together for comparison and contrast, and by relying on well-informed speculation and sheer surmise, an editor thus organized and constructed a single ``conflated" text.

Now, however, leading experts in the field emphasize that we cannot hope to recover what Shakespeare intended. What we must accept, they say, is the reality of affiliated but discrete versions of the same play. Thus, there is not really a single ``Hamlet ," for example, but at least two (the second Quarto and the Folio versions), and maybe three (the much shorter, much different first Quarto), and perhaps four -- if we feel we must supply a conflated text for those who insist on it.

Among the most memorable passages in the play is Hamlet's Act IV soliloquy, ``How all occasions do inform against me." It is present in the second Quarto (1604-5), but not in the Folio (1623). A conflated text would surely include it -- it is too good, and too famous, to leave out. However, a consensus is emerging in favor of a separate second Quarto and a separate Folio ``Hamlet ," and this means that one has the soliloquy while the other does not.

When many such variations are multiplied across the span of ``Hamlet," the consequence is that we are led to different perceptions of the play's extraordinary protagonist, the characters surrounding him, and the central themes and meanings that radiate from the work (or, perhaps, works) as a whole.

Rosenbaum also includes a provocative account of modern editions of the plays that return to and use the ``original spelling" of Shakespeare's language ( for example, not ``tomorrow," but ``to morrow") and that thereby alter the sound and sense of many words and phrases.

In addition, Rosenbaum gives a stimulating report on the work of the critic Stephen Booth (editor of a landmark edition of Shakespeare's sonnets, published in 1977 ) and cogent interviews with Peter Hall (founder of the modern Royal Shakespeare Company), the actor Steven Berkoff, and Brook. There is, too, a thought-provoking chapter on Shakespeare films by Olivier, Welles, and Brook, which Rosenbaum maintains are more rewarding -- indeed, more Shakespearean -- than any stage production we are likely to see.

Rosenbaum is brimming with enthusiasm about his subject, but, at times, his prose grows too effusive and extravagant, most notably about the impact on himself of Brook's 1970 production. He is also unduly severe on the Shakespeare biographer Stephen Greenblatt and the critic Harold Bloom. His grumbles about Bloom seem especially misplaced, since his own elation about Shakespeare's genius is akin to Bloom's. Rosenbaum respects scholars and critics who devote themselves to Shakespeare, but he is irritatingly prone to echo the standard complaints about scholarly jargon, literary theory, and careerism.

Despite these shortcomings, Rosenbaum's rewarding book bears witness to Shakespeare's unrival ed appeal and fascination, and general readers will find that it leads them even more deeply into the amazing complexities of his art.

Nearly everyone agrees that Shakespeare is the greatest and most intensely studied of all writers, but, as ``The Shakespeare Wars" shows, he remains the most elusive and, in Booth's arresting phrase, ``most underrated" writer as well.
