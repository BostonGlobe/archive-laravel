FOR CHILDREN

The origins and bravery of young souls

By Liz Rosenberg  |  September 24, 2006

Before You Were Born
Retold by Howard Schwartz
Illustrated by Kristina Swarner
Roaring Brook, 32 pp., newborn and up, $16.95

I'll Protect You From the Jungle Beasts
Written and illustrated by Martha Alexander
Charlesbridge, 32 pp., ages 3-7, $9.95

To Everything There Is a Season
Illustrated by Jude Daly
Eerdmans, 24 pp., all ages, $16

I am interested to see that some of the most charming and daring new picture books are coming from independent presses. This could signal that the world of children's books, which began as a modest literary enclave, may at last be returning to its roots. It may be David against Goliath, but for this month, at least, the slingshot is in the hands of the small.

Religion, indeed anything hinting at the spiritual, has long been banished from children's books. ``Before You Were Born" tells the story of what happens to the soul before it makes its journey here, according to Jewish mysticism and folklore. It is a gem of a book for newborn babies and their families.

And while it tells the story of a soul's bright beginning, ``in the highest heaven, in the Treasury of Souls," where the angel Lailah watches over the yet-to-be-born, it places itself as much in the realm of the fairy tale, or the grandmother story, as in religious teaching. Howard Schwartz, a highly decorated author, folklorist, and poet, writes with a light hand. There is never an instant's forcing or misstep. And illustrator Kristina Swarner's art is breathtaking, prepared with linoleum printing, watercolor gouache, and colored pencil. Her effects are soft, tender, dreamy , and gentle, her colors lush and luminous. ``As you slept, Lailah taught you all the secrets of the world. / She taught you seventy languages, the language of all the animals ." A peaceable kingdom of animals gathers for this full-spread illustration, with fish and butterflies swimming through the air, geese, foxes, elephants, and peacock gathering on the green grass.

Schwartz provides a folklorist's back note, citing sources, including his mother's story telling and a midrash, or rabbinical teaching, published in Constantinople in l522. He relates this story of the guardian angel Lailah to other stories that feature guardian angels, from fairy godmothers to other angels and spirits.

``Before You Were Born" also ``explains" a physical phenomenon. Just before the baby is born the angel puts her finger to its lips, making the baby forget all that it has learned -- and if you ran your finger above your lip, ``sure enough, there was an indentation there. But don't worry, you have the rest of your life to learn all those wondrous secrets again."

Whether one regards it as truth or myth, ``Before You Were Born" is a beautiful story for families of the newly born, a visual and verbal delight.

Martha Alexander's ``I'll Protect You From the Jungle Beasts" takes one child's fears of the dark and ``the jungle beasts" to wonderful comic heights, then lets them subside again. The genius of this book depends on Alexander's clever use of proportion and scale. When the boy begins his journey he says bravely to the stuffed bear in his arms, ``Oh, yes, Teddy, there are lions and tigers and elephants in this forest -- big ones. But don't worry. I'll protect you from the jungle beasts."

With each step he takes, his fear grows -- as does his teddy bear, little by little, almost imperceptibly, till the boy and the bear are walking side by side, and at last the bear is large enough to carry the boy home. Alexander, who died in January, is beloved for her Blackboard Bear series. Many of her finest books are wordless, and ``I'll Protect You From the Jungle Beasts" uses the minimum of words -- but just enough of them. Similarly, her art, which lies close to the sketch and the cartoon in its absence of adornment, indulges in no superficial effects, none of the visual hoopla we've come to expect from picture books -- and is all the more endearing for its sweet simplicity.

Forest and bear are brown, the background an open beige, and the boy with his yellow hair and blue pajamas seems a spark of color walking through his nighttime fears to a bed that sits perched in the middle of nothing else -- no other furniture, no door, no furnishings. Alexander leaves room for the child's imagination to breathe, and gives without preaching the comforting assurance that when morning comes, one's fears -- and one's teddy bear -- may well appear small and manageable.

Ecclesiastes says, ``What has been will be again, what has been done will be done again; there is nothing new under the sun," words that surely apply to this year's ``To Everything There Is a Season," hauntingly and newly illustrated by Jude Daly. In it, some of the most famous verses of Ecclesiastes appear broken down into small phrases and beautifully rendered by Daly, a South African artist who treats the work with sober respect and minute attention. Daly's art is arch-simple, connected always to the elemental -- a full white moon, a sprinkling of stars, grass, and water. Her figures, too, have a starkly simple and timeless quality, like stick figures elevated to the level of religious icons. Some of the pictures, like the one illustrating ``a time to love," are easy for young children to access. Others are more suggestive and mysterious, as in the landscape that accompanies ``A time to keep silence," or the fishing boat whose net is just barely visible in ``A time to kill."

``To Everything There Is a Season" is a fully integrated and poetic work, simple enough to be read aloud to a toddler, rich enough to engage an adult. The text of Ecclesiastes 3:1-8 appears in full at the end of the book. ``To Everything There Is A Season" gives us reason to celebrate the renewal of the ancient.

Liz Rosenberg teaches in the English Department at the State University of New York at Binghamton. Her 18th picture book, ``Nobody's Home," will be out next year. She reviews books for young readers each month.
