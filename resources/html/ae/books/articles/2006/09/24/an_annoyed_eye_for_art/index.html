An annoyed eye for art

Critic reflects on the many things he dislikes

By Tom Alderson  |  September 24, 2006

Things I Didn't Know: A Memoir
By Robert Hughes
Knopf, 395 pp., $27.95

In 1984, amid the driving rain of broken crockery perpetrated by Julian Schnabel, Robert Hughes published the poem ``The SoHoiad," a satire of the contemporary art scene in New York modeled on Pope's ``Dunciad." Between the lines lacerating the likes of ``Jean-Michel Basketcase" and ``Keith Boring ," the reader could detect the familiar sensibility of the annoyed provincial, a sensibility that resonates at a peculiarly American frequency, though the possessor in this case was of Australian origins.

Those origins are the subject of ``Things I Didn't Know." The memoir was triggered, Hughes says, by the experience of a horrific car crash on the eve of his 61st birthday, on a remote road along the western edge of his native land. He saw death and passed through a coma state and a vision of Goya and five years of painful recovery. Then there was the existential trauma of a lawsuit, an experience as absurd and debilitating in the courts of Australia as we know it to be in the United States. It sent him back, as many a mid life crisis has to many another, to the beginning. How did I come to this? The child is father to the man.

The Hugheses were an Irish family, transplanted to Sydney in 1839 as free emigrants. By the time Robert appeared, in the late '30s, the family was comfortable, not rich but ``semi-rich ." Hughes's father was a lawyer, a Royal Flying Corps veteran of World War I, and a Catholic so loyal to the faith that Pope Pius XI bestowed on him the title of Confidential Chamberlain of the Sword and Cape . Hughes himself endured a Jesuit education. He lost his faith but retained some of the habit of mind inspired by Loyola's ``Spiritual Exercises": `` If you didn't feel something, you couldn't know it." As this book makes clear, that became something of his credo as an art critic.

Whipsawed as he was between his good-burgher parents, the strap-wielding Jesuits, and his burgeoning interest in all things aesthetic, Robert started down the road traveled by all provincial aesthetes: the road out of town. After studying architecture at Sydney University, he passed into Sydney's bohemian underground. Benzedrine, all-night talks, residence at the Royal George pub, contributing cartoons to the Observer -- that was life until one day the art critic of that paper reviewed a museum show without seeing it. When the editor fired the critic, Hughes happened to be sitting in the staff room. ``You're the cartoonist," the editor said to him. ``You ought to know something about art. Good. Well, now you're the . . . art critic." And Hughes's career was underway.

Australia, as he tells us again and again, was an artistic backwater. The conventional and the middle-brow reigned. But Hughes's upbringing in a culture that projected a provincial resentment and inferiority complex toward the European artistic tradition instilled in him the divided sensibility that has him denouncing both the self-conscious avant-garde and the mass-entertainment industry. He is both a grumpy ``elitist" -- proud to claim the title -- and a winningly redneck strafer of the commercial art world, the world of dealers and celebrity artists and inflated auction spectacles. Hostile to the ``spiritual aridity and intellectual mediocrity" of his native realm, he has even less regard for American academic art writing, ``an abstract, colonial parody of French post-structuralist jargon, thickened with gobbets of decayed Marxism." Like Orwell, he can't abide ``Stalinists, Trotskyites, vegetarians, anarchists, and other loquacious weirdos"; like Mencken, his tolerance for ``American Kmart mystics" is severely limited. For refugees from the American South and Midwest, looking at New York City with a mixture of fear, awe, and scorn, it's a familiar trajectory.

So Hughes left town. He passed through Los Angeles, Las Vegas, and the 1964 World's Fair, where he saw the ``Pietà" from a moving conveyor belt ``not unlike the belts used to carry animals into slaughterhouses." That experience fired the hostility he still feels to the invasion of museums by carnival hucksterism. His contemporary complaint about it in The Nation brought him denunciations as ``an ingrate, an elitist, and a pessimist." Guilty, guilty, guilty, and proud of it. Next he went to Italy, where he began ``at last, to derive from art, from architecture, and even from the beauty of organized landscape a sense of transcendence that organized religion had offered me -- but that I had never received."

By the late '60s he was settled in London, writing for the BBC and turning out art criticism in the face of that decade's heady fatuities. The London underground scene was no more impressive to Hughes than the home-grown Sydney version had been.

The only part of the story that disappoints is the end. In 1970, Hughes is hired as the art reviewer for Time. This is presented as a kind of apotheosis, and this is how the memoir wraps up, with our narrator on the plane to New York. The last 60 pages peter out in a kind of centrifugal dispersal of energy, quite in contrast to the blistering opening around the car crash. One can infer that ``Bush's brutally sanctimonious imperium" and the Luce flagship magazine are not all that far from the sensibility he was fleeing when he left Australia, and that he traveled a long way to get back to a home he still can't applaud.

Tom Alderson is the author of ``Michelson in the Desert," a collection of short stories.
