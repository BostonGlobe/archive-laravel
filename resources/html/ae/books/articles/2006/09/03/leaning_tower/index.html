Leaning tower

In a new book about Frank Lloyd Wright, the master learns from his apprentices

By Clay Risen  |  September 3, 2006

The Fellowship: The Untold Story of Frank Lloyd Wright and the Taliesin Fellowship
By Roger Friedland and Harold Zellman
Regan, 689 pp., illustrated, $34.95

How to explain Wright-mania? Alone among American architects, Frank Lloyd Wright is a cultural icon. His designs grace calendars and posters, coasters and doormats. And almost every year sees a major work on the master builder: In 2003 it was Franklin Toker's ``Fallingwater Rising," the history of that famous home Wright built for a Pittsburgh department-store baron in the Pennsylvania woods; in 2004 it was Ada Louise Huxtable's compact yet magisterial biography. This year's entry is Roger Friedland and Harold Zellman's ``The Fellowship."

Part of our Wright obsession stems from his work itself. Wright remains the apotheosis of a distinctly American aesthetic. His work speaks to us in ways that, say, Philip Johnson's Glass House cannot. In Wright's oeuvre, we see America. But it's not just about the buildings. Alive, he was constant fodder for tabloids; today, he is the stuff of myth. He wooed women effortlessly, sired children irresponsibly, and surrounded himself with cultish admirers. Imperious, impetuous, imprudent, Wright acted as if immune to all laws save those of nature (though he was eager to defy even them). Which is why, aside from the hundreds of books on his work, there are dozens of biographies, memoirs, and histories of Wright and his circle.

Not all of them are good; some are mere hagiographies, others scurrilous scandal sheets. But ``The Fellowship," which focuses on the quasi-institutional apprentice program Wright began in the 1930s, is better than most. Friedland, a cultural sociologist, and Zellman, an architect, posit that, far from being colorful adornment on Wright's career, the scores of Lost Boys for whom he played a wizened Peter Pan were integral to his later, greatest work: ``Without the Fellowship, the landmarks for which Frank Lloyd Wright is best known today -- Fallingwater, Johnson Wax, the Guggenheim Museum -- might never have been created."

For those steeped in the myth of Wright as a sui generis American mind, that's a tall claim. But the authors do an admirable job of explaining how his later, more organic work was heavily influenced by his environment (he began spending winters in Arizona); by his third and final wife, Olgivanna, a Montenegro-born follower of the mystic Georgi Gurdjieff; and by his numerous apprentices, who, like elves around a bumbling Santa, saved more than a few of his masterpieces by quietly improving on his outlandishly ambitious designs. In doing so, Friedland and Zellman make a great contribution not only to Wright scholarship, but also to our understanding of how even great artists depend on, and are in part defined by, the nameless standing just behind them.

Wright founded the Taliesin Fellowship in 1932. Named for his estate in rural Wisconsin, where it was based, it was ostensibly to train the next generation of ``organic" architects. But it was also something of a scam, a way to raise desperately needed funds for the spendthrift Wright. A few dozen apprentices paid $1,100 a year for the honor of doing menial labor around the estate, including farming and maintenance, with the prospect of assisting the master on commissions (though at the time, during the trough of the Depression, even Wright had a hard time landing work).

Largely composed of other-directed scions of the American elite, the overwhelmingly male fellowship was intensely creative and artistic; many were homosexuals who found a safe haven in Taliesin's unorthodox communitarianism. Beginning in the late ' 30s, every winter the Fellowship would caravan from Taliesin to Arizona, where, outside Phoenix, they built Taliesin West. Between the two, Wright crafted an alternative universe, a Midwestern Brigadoon. When World War II came, he commanded his acolytes to resist the draft; almost all did, and two even went to jail. And as they bonded to their newfound father figure, the apprentices also developed strong ties to each other, which were tested by the rampant sexuality of the place: Olgivanna, the group's dictatorial queen, inserted herself into their lives, importing women and encouraging promiscuity (gay and straight). Taliesin, said one associate, ``made Peyton Place look like Utah."

The Fellowship's intense loyalty proved indispensable to Wright's late, great expressionist works. Diverging from the solid geometries of his Prairie Style, his new buildings seemingly defied gravity -- think of Fallingwater's perilous cantilever, Johnson Wax's reed-thin structural columns. But while he had an amazing intuition for the impossibly possible, he relied on his apprentices (some of whom, at his death, had been ``apprentices" for over 20 years) to translate his intuition into acceptable blueprints. It was no small achievement -- an MIT researcher, reviewing the Fallingwater documents in the late 1980s, found that apprentice Wesley Peters had developed solutions thought to have only recently been derived, with the help of computers.

``The Fellowship" has created a scandal in Wright circles in part because it puts great emphasis on Olgivanna's corrupting influences. After Wright's death she took over the group; she commanded adherence to her bizarre mysticism, even as she crassly profited off her late husband's work. By her death, in 1985, the Fellowship had dwindled in size, output, and quality; today it teeters on the brink of dissolution.

Friedland and Zellman conclude that ``the Fellowship did a better job making architecture than making architects." Indeed, the only famous practitioner to come out of Taliesin was the Arkansan Fay Jones, and even he spent a mere three months there. The story of the Fellowship, then, is one of the power of creative genius to gather lesser talents around itself and consume them. Without his anonymous apprentices, Wright would be the subject of far fewer books; without Wright, some of them might be the subjects of a few books themselves.

Clay Risen is the managing editor of Democracy: A Journal of Ideas.
