Slavery's development and demise

By James Smethurst  |  September 3, 2006

Inhuman Bondage: The Rise and Fall of Slaveryin the New World
By David Brion Davis
Pantheon, 440 pp., illustrated, $30

The institution of slavery and its meaning and legacy for the United States continue to be the source of some of our most vexing social questions. Was slavery a sort of anomaly that somehow grew up alongside the fundamental American principles of life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness? Or was the freedom of white Americans in some crucial ways dependent on the existence of black slaves? These are only a couple of the many questions that have a great deal to do with how we understand ourselves, not to mention what might be owed those who are the descendants of slaves by a nation that benefited mightily from their labor.

Not surprisingly, slavery has long been the subject of often contentious study by historians. The last few decades have seen a blossoming of such scholarship, often drawing on other intellectual fields. Much of this recent work has been both more local and more global , in many instances digging deeply into a particular locale or industry and in others using an international framework. The new historiography of slavery has generally featured an increased attention to culture and has often made use of technological advances to create databases allowing fresh analyses of archival materials.

David Brion Davis has been among the most eminent of the historians of slavery over the last 40 years. In many respects, his new book, ``Inhuman Bondage," is his most ambitious. In it he attempts to summarize, synthesize , and present this new scholarship to a general audience.

As the title suggests, Davis examines New World slavery and the slave trade in a deeply comparative fashion, devoting considerable space to how slavery has been practiced in different societies over the centuries. He also looks at, among many other things, the slave trade and its impact on Africa, the rise of racism in the Americas, the character of slavery in the Caribbean and Brazil, the Haitian Revolution and its international repercussions, the end of the legal trans-Atlantic slave trade, the rise of the British anti slavery movement, and the abolition of slavery in the British colonies. However, the primary focus of Davis's study remains the development and demise of slavery in the United States.

Davis is resolutely liberal , taking issue, mostly in an indirect manner, with nationalists of various stripes, conservatives , and Marxists . Drawing heavily on the work of Orlando Patterson, Davis argues that while slavery is not economically essential to every society in which it has been practiced, it nearly always involves some sort of dehumanization , providing a socio-psychic benefit to the non-slave . He also maintains that New World slavery was a modern and economically successful system, doing much to enable the growth of industrial capitalism, especially within the United States. He claims that slaves in the Americas were able to significantly shape their identities , drawing on African traditions and sensibilities as well as the conditions and cultures they encountered in the New World. He also considers how societies might make a moral choice that seems to run counter to their material interests -- as he argues the British did when they abolished slavery in their colonies . However, he also reflects on the difficulties of making such a choice when he takes up the evolution of slavery in the United States, as well as the problems with post-Enlightenment notions of progress that are challenged by the failure of Reconstruction and the rise of Jim Crow segregation in the United States. Both the somewhat optimistic notion that, as the anti-globalization activists say, ``another world is possible " and Davis's more pessimistic reflections on the crisis of liberal notions of progress resonate deeply with his sense of the early 21st century and the ``war on terror."

While the book basically moves chronologically, its reach from slavery in the ancient world to the memory of slavery in the Reconstruction and post-Reconstruction United States makes any tightly integrated story impossible. Instead, ``Inhuman Bondage" consists of a series of relatively discrete, if interrelated, chapters focusing on particular issues and historical moments. This might make the book sound less engaging than it is. The individual chapters are clear, lively, and thoughtful treatments of moments and questions that help provoke in the reader a larger sense of the trajectory of slavery in the Atlantic Rim even if they do not cohere seamlessly. Still, Davis might have paid more attention to the work of black abolitionists and anti slavery organizations in the United States and Britain -- not to mention the work of black scholars other than Patterson who have been so important to the rethinking of slavery. .

``Inhuman Bondage" is a skillful, if a bit restricted, synthesis of the current state of scholarship on various aspects of New World slavery and the slave trade . For anyone seeking a better sense of what contemporary historians can tell us about slavery and its meaning, this is an essential starting point.

James Smethurst teaches in the Department of Afro-American Studies at the University of Massachusetts at Amherst. He is the author, most recently, of ``The Black Arts Movement."
