BOOK REVIEW

In sharp 'Rose,' a teen's growing pains take a dark turn

By Karen Campbell, Globe Correspondent  |  February 20, 2006

Rose of No Man’s Land
By Michelle Tea
MacAdam/Cage, 306 pp., $22

Michelle Tea's new ''Rose of No Man's Land" is both a riotously funny coming-of-age story and a poignant cautionary tale that smacks of ''there but for the grace of God" heartbreak. Its central figure is 14-year-old Trisha Driscoll, a lost, self-described loner living in the stultifying morass of North Shore mall culture with her hypochondriac mother and her mother's boyfriend, Donny, a sluggish cave man type whose chief attribute is that he doesn't ''bother" Trisha and her sister, Kristy.

''Like we, me and Kristy, should drop to our knees and kiss the peeling linoleum and prostrate ourselves to the patron saint of creepy dudes for sending us such a winner. I think the biggest problem between me and my family . . . is we have different standards."

Different standards, indeed. Trisha is smart as a whip and fighting to develop some kind of moral compass in a veritable vacuum. She faces the prospect of a long, empty summer with dread. ''It seems like on the outside of high school there's nothing but time. Big, dark, eerie time, endless amounts of it. . . . After high school comes life. Don't ask me what I'm supposed to do with it."

While Kristy spends every waking hour practicing to be a candidate on ''The Real World," Trisha gets a job at a trendy clothing store at Square One Mall. But Trisha is so out of step with the popular local girls who frequent the shop that she finds herself picking fights with the customers. She is abruptly fired, but not before she meets Rose, a kindred misfit from the mall's hamburger joint. The two bond over cigarettes in the parking lot, and Trisha is sucked into a whirlwind adventure of bisexual romance, deceit, drugs, theft, tattoos, and nothing-to-lose ingenuity. It's a wild, funny, scary ride into a dark, seedy suburban subculture that will curl the hair of mothers with teenage girls.

However, ultimately ''Rose of No Man's Land" is about survival, about coping and adapting and figuring out how to move forward. And in that sense, it's about every child who struggles to find a place in the world.

Tea has a cult following in lesbian feminist circles. Though Tea has written four memoirs (by the ripe age of 34) and is the founder of the notorious all-girl performance poetry tour Sister Spit, ''Rose" is her first novel.

Written in first person, it could almost be yet another offbeat autobiographical tale in the way it flows with an easy, unpretentious rhythm that crackles with off-the-cuff digressions and ruminations. But Trisha's cynical, wisecracking descriptions are almost too brilliantly evocative, too clever as she illuminates the story's small cast of characters with vivid, telling details. Her reflections are certainly more insightful than anyone could expect from a 14-year-old. That's OK. Suspension of disbelief is part of the game, and somehow Tea manages to balance Trisha's snarky edge with moments of a sweetly sad, naive vulnerability that beautifully capture those mercurial midteen years. What gives ''Rose of No Man's Land" its power and resonance is that it is fueled by both anger and yearning.
