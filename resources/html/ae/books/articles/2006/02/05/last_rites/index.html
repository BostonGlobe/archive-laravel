Last Rites

Exploring the American way of death, as well as its roots and reflections

By Glenn C. Altschuler  |  February 5, 2006

Death's Door: Modern Dying and the Ways We Grieve
By Sandra M. Gilbert
Norton, 580 pp., illustrated, $29.95

On Feb. 11, 1991, Elliot Gilbert had surgery. Eight hours after his malignant tumor was successfully removed, he bled to death, alone in a recovery room. Death ''suddenly, gigantically," enveloped Sandra Gilbert, Elliot's wife, a poet and professor of English at the University of California, Davis. She sued for wrongful death. And, although she knew that ''accounts of the unspeakable . . . lie and leave a hole in the heart," Gilbert felt compelled ''to write the wrong."

Drawing on history, literature, and contemporary culture, ''Death's Door" is far more than a memoir. It is a sprawling, sophisticated, and somber meditation on mortality and mourning in America. For centuries, Gilbert observes, the bereaved believed, ardently, wishfully, and sometimes wistfully, that their loved ones had ''expired," with the end a beginning, ''clothed in the 'musical brocade' of traditional religious belief." The pastoral elegy was a paradigmatic expression of faith, as were portraits of family members gathered around a deathbed, praying with and not for a father, mother, or child, confident that after one short sleep ''we wake eternally."

Then came World War I, the Holocaust, materialism and modernity. ''Reeking with mud," staring with ''fixed, empty eyes and shiny, yellow faces," buried in mass graves, the war dead lost their individuality. And belief in redemptive afterlife became a casualty of the conviction, as the historian Carl Becker put it, that men and women were ''chance [deposits] on the surface of the world, carelessly thrown up between two ice ages by the same forces that rust iron and ripen corn." For many Americans, expiration became termination, and consolation turned to dread.

Modern technology added new terrors to death. Eighty percent of Americans now die in hospital cubicles, undressed in hospital gowns, tethered to tubes, commanding only a TV remote. Family and friends are visitors, subject to the ''hospitality" of neurologists, oncologists, dietitians, and registered nurses. As doctors manage pain, with health-care proxies and morphine drips, social workers tolerate a sob or a shriek until the casket is lowered into the ground, then advise survivors to move on: ''The sooner you let go, the better." Mourning has become so privatized that even C. S. Lewis, the prolific and profoundly religious writer, published ''A Grief Observed," with its ''morbid jottings," under the pseudonym N. W. Clerk.

With these developments, Gilbert argues, the pastoral become a ''godforsaken curio," replaced by ''monstrous elegies," which gave poetic testimony to death as a ''bleak termination of life." Emily Dickinson and Walt Whitman were godparents to the new genre. In ''I heard a Fly buzz when I died," '' 'Tis so appalling -- it exhilarates," and ''Because I could not stop for Death," Dickinson stared into the blankness sardonically, skeptically, and with ''virtuoso anxiety." Whitman thought death ''different from what anyone supposed, and luckier." But even before he witnessed what might be called ''the gangrening of America" in the Civil War, he became less sanguine. In ''As I Ebb'd With the Ocean of Life," he wrote a passage so graphic and bleak (''See, from my dead lips the ooze exuding at last") that James Russell Lowell excised it from the Atlantic Monthly.

In the 20th century, William Carlos Williams, Wallace Stevens, and Sylvia Plath became masters of ''the monstrous." A doctor-poet, Williams brought clinically precise descriptions of death to much of his work. In ''The Emperor of Ice-Cream," Gilbert writes, Stevens, by day a life-insurance executive, emphasized that ''there is no ruling principle of existence beyond the chill finality of death (ice) and the sensuous delight of life (cream)." And Plath, before taking her own life at the age of 30, wrote macabre, terrifying, hallucinatory, and ironic poems, embodying her view that ''black is death, with no light, no waking."

By mid-century, Gilbert asserts, pervasive discomfort with traditional attitudes toward death and an afterlife elicited ''popular confusion and distress" as well as high cultural gloom. She acknowledges that, in a recent poll, more than 80 percent of Americans affirmed a belief in a heaven where people live forever with God. But these people do not appear in ''Death's Door," a book for -- and about -- secular humanists, blue staters, and devotees of Stravinsky, ''Gravity's Rainbow," Volvos, and vegetables.

Gilbert does demonstrate that Americans are experimenting with new approaches to death and dying. They grieve at the Vietnam Veterans Memorial, designed by Maya Lin, flouting, defiantly, the privatization of mourning. The shrines assembled spontaneously in the aftermath of 9/11, with photographic collages, toys, trinkets, and testimonials, are personal, ''whether awfully or awesomely so." At their best, they allow grievers to bear witness to and come to terms with loss. As does the Virtual Memorial Garden, with its e-mail memorials to loved ones. Millennial mourning, according to Gilbert, has several features in common with the ''monstrous" elegies: ''exacting, often excruciating attention" to the death scene; preoccupation with the dead body; a detailed re-creation of the lives of the dead -- and their relationship with the survivor; and an explicit statement of resignation to the loss.

Does millennial mourning provide consolation? Gilbert does not believe, as Whitman did, that writer and subject are reincarnated in texts, and will live as long as words about them are read. But she takes some solace from the reimagining of a life in the ''arduously visionary" act of elegizing. Even when that act is as ''monstrous" as Allen Ginsberg's scatalogical description of his mother in the bathroom in ''Kaddish." Elegiac evocations of the dead, she claims, convert ''the particularities of a completed life" into elements, common to all of us, and yet ''radiant with new meaning."

To many of us, Gilbert knows, this is small consolation. When we provide ideas and images to fill a sea ''that streams into infinite distances," she asks, is it ''a triumph of emptiness or a triumph of those who stand on its threshold?" Even to stare into a void we cannot stare down, she concludes, perhaps to convince herself as well as her readers, even to look at death's door, which is ''perpetually open, is in itself a victory."

Glenn C. Altschuler is the Thomas and Dorothy Litwin Professor of American Studies at Cornell University.
