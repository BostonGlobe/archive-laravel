Painters in opposing corners

An artistic rivalry that sparked a revolution

By Daniel Swift  |  February 5, 2006

The Judgment of Paris: The Revolutionary Decade That Gave the World Impressionism
By Ross King
Walker, 448 pp., illustrated, $28

''The Judgment of Paris" opens and closes with Ernest Meissonier, who was during the 1860s and '70s the most famous and successful painter in France; he is not, however, our only hero, for the epigraph introduces our second character, Édouard Manet. With ''The Judgment of Paris" Ross King -- the author of the best-selling ''Brunelleschi's Dome" -- returns to the power struggles that underpin the history of art, structured on the varying fortunes of these contemporary painters.

Meissonier, who was described by a friend as looking like a gymnastics teacher, was celebrated for his staggeringly lifelike paintings of great battles: ''He did not feel at home or at ease in the nineteenth century," writes King, and describes his paintings as ''recherché figments of an antiquarian imagination." He lived in a folly of a house outside Paris, used himself as a model for his portraits of Napoleon, and was so obsessed with verisimilitude that he built a railway track in his garden so he could ride alongside charging horses, sketching their movements. That we have not heard of him today only exacerbates the quiet pathos of King's portrait. He built wax models of martial scenes; for one picture, of a winter campaign, he covered the model with sugar, which was promptly eaten by mice.

Manet was 17 years younger than Meissonier but lived in another world. He wore ''intentionally gaudy trousers," was a friend of the bad-boy poet Charles Baudelaire, and painted the people he saw in the world around him: a rag-and-bone man he met in the Louvre, for example, was the model for the ''rough-looking drunkard" he portrayed in ''The Absinthe Drinker." In what is probably his most famous painting, ''Le Déjeuner sur l'herbe," he simply painted ''a nude scene of modern-day Parisians."

The rival fortunes of these two painters give King his structure, but this book is at heart an institutional, rather than artistic, history, and the story is punctuated by the successive openings of the annual Parisian art show known as the Salon: of 1863, 1864, 1865, 1866, 1867 . . . It is never clear whether the Salon is really an interesting or important subject. In terms of the history of art, its judges inevitably chose wrong, preferring scenes of classical mythology and soft-porn Venuses to the exclusion of everything else. One year, 1867, the judges managed to turn down entries by Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Camille Pissarro, and Paul Cezanne while Manet, discouraged by previous rejections, didn't submit any paintings. King, however, escorts us briskly through the minor gossip, the infighting, the reviews, and the rivalries that accompanied each year's Salon, reveling in the small print of what seems to be no more than a minor footnote to history.

King may have chosen to tell his story this way because it is very, very difficult to describe artistic style, to account for and to do justice to its ramifications, twists, and turns. It is the ability to write clearly and relevantly about style that distinguishes Robert Hughes as an art critic, Stephen Greenblatt as a literary critic, and Edward Said -- in his late writings -- as a music critic. King does not share this gift. He notes that Manet ''applied his paint thickly and in broad brushstrokes," which gave the pictures ''a slightly slapdash appearance" in contrast to the perfectly finished sheen of Meissonier's canvasses; later, King again points to ''Manet's apparently clumsy brushstrokes and lack of clarity," without ever commenting on why this might be important.

King avoids the question of what makes Manet important in the history of art, which is close to saying what made him radical. He can point to why he was controversial: For ''Olympia," Manet borrowed the pose of a Tintoretto nude, but by shifting the position of the model's hand could suggest masturbation. But the question of Manet's actual innovation is barely approached. King notes in passing that Manet used popular engravings for sources and even, for ''The Execution of Maximilian," newspaper reports: For that painting, it seems likely that Manet altered the face of one of the executioners to make him look more like Napoleon III, the emperor of France. But King skips over this possible radicalism in two short paragraphs.

People did find Manet shocking. At one showing, ''an atmosphere of hysteria and even fear predominated. Some spectators collapsed in 'epidemics of crazed laughter.' " The best of this book is its thoroughly detailed small episodes. Paris in the 1860s, we learn, had 12,000 cafes and 125,000 prostitutes, and during the Siege of 1870 the city's inhabitants were reduced to eating rat pâté and the animals from the zoo. There are fascinating minor characters: the radical journalist Emile Zola, who as a young man was so poor he had to sell all his clothes and wear a bedsheet; Richard Seymour-Conway, the fourth marquess of Hertford, an extravagant art collector who boasted that ''when I die I shall at least have the consolation of knowing that I have never rendered anyone a service"; and Baudelaire, who it was widely believed had not only murdered but also eaten his stepfather.

''The Judgment of Paris" has all the accoutrements of a gripping story -- cliff-hanging chapter endings, vivid anecdotes, war and revolution -- without the actual story. Meissonier wins prizes, but his work declines from public view; Manet moves from youthful failure to middle-aged success and posthumous veneration. In his overhasty ending, which attempts to explain the current state of these artists' fortunes and the logic of the book as a whole, King is left with too much work still to do. ''Manet was indeed greater than many people thought," he claims, and then a couple of pages later, ''The difference between Meissonier and Manet was actually over artistic aims and techniques," and while this is doubtless true, it's striking that King still needs to say so in the closing pages. Nothing that precedes them has really explained why, or how.

Daniel Swift has written for The New York Times, The Nation, and Bookforum.
