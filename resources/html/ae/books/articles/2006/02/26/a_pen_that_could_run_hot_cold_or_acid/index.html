A pen that could run hot, cold, or acid

By William H. Pritchard  |  February 26, 2006

The Letters of Lytton Strachey
Edited by Paul Levy
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 698 pp., $40

Probably the best-known anecdote about Lytton Strachey is his behavior at a tribunal in London, 1916, at which he stated his case as a conscientious objector to the war in progress. Seated on a pale-blue air cushion (he was suffering from piles) with three of his sisters in attendance, he replied to the tribunal's standard question -- ''What would you do if you saw a German soldier trying to rape your sister?" -- with, according to Paul Levy's note in this edition of Strachey's letters, ''I should attempt to come between them." (Levy says there was a ''significant pause" between the words ''come" and ''between.") However it really went, it was delivered in his usual piercing voice. The tribunal was unimpressed, although a few days later it declared Strachey unfit for service.

What Strachey was fit for was the writing of handsome English sentences, even though his gifts as a historian and biographer tend to be overlooked in favor of his advocacy for what Bertrand Russell called the Higher Sodomy. These letters reveal how central to Strachey's being were his homoerotic concerns, from early Cambridge student days up to the time he succumbed at age 51 to a previously undiagnosed stomach cancer.

The early letters, many of them written to Leonard Woolf, who was then a colonial administrator in Ceylon, depict the ups and downs of Strachey's romances and flirtations with a number of young men, most notably the painter Duncan Grant, who became the lover of Maynard Keynes (and others) and would later father a child with Vanessa Bell, Virginia Woolf's sister. Any line between fantasy and reality is expunged when Strachey invents -- in a letter for the excited eyes of Keynes -- a beautiful young man named Horace Townsend (''Horry") who is provided with godlike beauty, ''lying out at full length in the heat in all his unimaginable splendour. . . . the moulded sinuosity, the divine strength and colour and firmness and flow!" Such creation, in 1908, shows Strachey as an early contributor to the world of camp, but it differs scarcely at all from his floridities about a real person, the mountaineer George Mallory, who would eventually lose his life climbing Mount Everest: ''His body -- vast, pink, unbelievable -- is a thing to melt into and die. I have melted, but, so far, I haven't died."

Most of the material in these letters is familiar to us through Michael Holroyd's masterly biography, but Levy is as assiduous an editor as could be desired. He can be intrusive, as when he calls one of Strachey's letters ''over-excited," since overexcitement is the going Strachey mode, full of disgusted reflections on London, the weather, various digestive ailments, and most other people. Strachey's favorite response to such matters is ''Quel horreur," and he is especially eloquent on the awfulness of marriage.

Later, in perhaps his most astonishing piece of overexcitement and with a bad cold, he proposes to Virginia Stephen, as yet unmarried to the Leonard Woolf to whom Strachey is writing. ''I was in terror lest she should kiss me," he confides. Almost immediately both he and Virginia had thought better of the whole idea.

As might be expected from the author of ''Eminent Victorians," ''Queen Victoria," and his other biographical works, Strachey as a portraitist is superb, and not just in a satirical, purely destructive way. Probably the person he admired most was the philosopher G. E. Moore, and a rendering of Moore's contribution during a discussion of someone's paper at a philosophical meeting illustrates how real admiration needn't be devoid of satirical touches. Moore's ''intellectual display," Strachey writes, ''was the very opposite of brilliant -- appallingly sensible, and so easy to understand that you wondered why on earth no one else had thought of it. The simplicity of genius! But the way it came out -- like some half-stifled geyser, throbbing and convulsed, and then bursting into a towering gush." To Virginia Woolf, who brings out his most imaginative and wickedest side, he describes Henry James in Rye looking out the window, ''so conscientious and worried and important . . . like an admirable tradesman trying his best to give satisfaction, infinitely solemn and polite." (Strachey wonders ''that such an appearance could have produced the Sacred Fount.")

His deep relation with Dora Carrington, the woman whose virginity, amazingly, he took, and whose devotion to Strachey was unbounded, comes through movingly in a letter at the time of her marriage:

''You do know very well that I love you as something more than a friend, you angelic creature, whose goodness to me has made me happy for years, and whose presence in my life has been, and always will be, one of the most important things in it."

The other important things -- writing and single-sex romances -- he kept at assiduously until his sudden end. The year before he died he was inspecting the Duveen Room in London's National Gallery, but also looking for a pickup. He follows one possibility, then perceives ''a more attractive tart -- fair-haired this time -- bright yellow and thick hair -- a pink face -- and plenty of vitality. So I transferred my attentions, and began to move in his direction, when on looking more closely I observed that it was the Prince of Wales." Strachey flees the scene but not without imagining that ''perhaps it might have been the beginning of a really entertaining affair."

William H. Pritchard is a professor of English at Amherst College. His most recent book is ''Shelf Life: Literary Essays and Reviews."
