A man not under the influence

Cassavetes chose an alternative to Hollywood

By Dan Wakefield  |  February 26, 2006

Accidental Genius: How John Cassavetes Invented the American Independent Film
By Marshall Fine
Miramax, 482 pp., illustrated, $27.95

Once upon a time, before there were university film schools, and no one made feature films on maxed-out credit cards (I am telling of a time so distant there weren't even credit cards), the only movies at your hometown cinema (that's ''cinema" without the ''-plex") were made by giant Hollywood studios. Yet even in this dark and backward time there was hope for cinema buffs, because ''before there was anything that anyone thought of in the aggregate as 'independent film' there was John Cassavetes."

In his readable and well-researched new biography ''Accidental Genius," the movie buff and critic Marshall Fine, author of books on director Sam Peckinpah and actor Harvey Keitel, has followed in the large footsteps of Boston University's Ray Carney (''Cassavetes on Cassavetes"), as well as other cineasts, in paying homage to the director/actor/writer/producer/distributor who is regarded as the father of the independent film.

Cassavetes raised money to make his own movies by acting in hits like ''Rosemary's Baby" and ''The Dirty Dozen" as well as cinematic lemons and long-forgotten TV series such as ''Johnny Staccato." His actress wife, Gena Rowlands, pitched in by taking TV and Hollywood movie roles to bring in cash, and also played leads in her husband's films.

His movies could truly be described as ''homemade" since he often financed them by taking another mortgage on the family house, and used its rooms to shoot interiors. ''Oh John, not the house," Rowlands moaned on hearing her husband again had taken out a second mortgage.

A college dropout from a well-to-do Greek-American family, Cassavetes developed his own extended family in a makeshift repertory company and crew with Rowlands and actor friends Peter Falk, Ben Gazzara, and Seymour Cassel. Falk not only played the role of the husband in Cassavetes's powerful and disturbing domestic drama ''A Woman Under the Influence," but also put up money of his own to help finance the film, which earned Rowlands an Oscar nomination for best actress.

The emotional reactions stirred by Cassavetes's work were evidenced by the response to ''A Woman Under the Influence," which ranged from critic David Sterritt hailing it as ''a masterpiece" in The Christian Science Monitor to Stanley Kauffman dismissing it as ''utterly without interest or merit" in The New Republic. Even his admirers might agree with the judgment of Paul Zimmerman in Newsweek that ''like Eugene O'Neill and Thomas Wolfe, Cassavetes sacrifices structure and brevity for impact." That's not bad company to keep in the context of criticism.

The word ''accident" crops up often in discussions of Cassavetes, not as a put-down, but rather as apt description of the way his dynamic personality turned defeats and frustrations into art and entertainment. Denied membership in Lee Strasberg's Actors Studio, the Valhalla of '50s theater whose students included the icons Marlon Brando and Marilyn Monroe, Cassavetes started his own acting school. In an effort to teach improvisation more effectively, he filmed his students in an impromptu project that became ''Shadows," his first film, financed initially with money raised from an appeal to listeners of Jean Shepherd's offbeat New York nighttime radio show.

The seemingly haphazard wanderings and romances of young people in New York in the 1950s, including the perils of an interracial love affair and the hopes and dreams of idealistic youth to remain true to their dreams, ''Shadows" is one of those films that seem to get better with age. It captures the speech and sound and social mores of a decade more truly than all the slick studio portrayals of the time and place, from that era's ''Marjorie Morningstar" to the more recent ''Far From Heaven."

Cassavetes got Charlie Mingus to contribute music to the film, feeling he was on the same wavelength of improvisation. First screened in its current form in 1959, ''Shadows" was a hit at the British Film Institute's ''Beat, Square and Cool" festival in 1960.

Cassavetes was part of the counterculture explosion in the arts in New York in the '50s, bringing to film the improvisational style that Pollock pioneered in painting; Ginsberg, Kerouac, and the Beats in literature; and Davis, Mingus, and Coltrane in jazz. Martin Scorsese was one of the young students of the era inspired by the impact of ''Shadows." He told Fine in an interview for this book, ''You can't gauge the amount of hope that such an act created for young people. It was like a starting pistol for a race that still keeps going."

Cassavetes and his collaborators wanted to ''tell it like it is" in middle-class American marriage in their 1968 production ''Faces." Cassel, one of the actors, said ''it became a way of life, a film against the authorities and the powers that prevent people from expressing themselves the way they want to, something that can't be done in America, that can't be done without money."

Since Cassavetes's death, in 1989, the honors and attention to his work continue to increase, which would surely amuse the man who had to fight for his films and their financing every step of the way. Though he showed the harsh realities of life in his films, Cassavetes was not a dour or depressed sort of man, but joyfully thrived on his work and the challenge of making it. He joked to a friend, ''I don't mind dying. At least then, I don't have to have lunch with anybody."
