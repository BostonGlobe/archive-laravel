Stage, craft

Dramatist and secret agent, Marlowe led a life still shrouded in mystery

By Joseph Rosenbloom  |  February 19, 2006

Christopher Marlowe: Poet and Spy
By Park Honan
Oxford University, 421 pp.,
illustrated, $32.50

The cobweb-draped mysteries surrounding the life and murder of Christopher Marlowe continue to intrigue biographers. Marlowe's life story is a tabloid editor's dream. Just enough is known about his murder to permit endless conjecture about why it happened, yet it remains tantalizingly unsolved.

So what if the murder occurred 413 years ago? Why stop hunting for clues?

The latest to take the dare is Park Honan. In ''Christopher Marlowe: Poet and Spy" he plunges into the questions still swirling around the illustrious playwright. Was Marlowe slain in a contract hit or in a brawl over a meal tab, as the official version had it? What about his moonlighting escapades as a spy for the Privy Council? Was he an atheist? Was he gay?

Honan has the credentials to peer deeply into the murky bottom of Marlowe's life. An emeritus professor of English at the University of Leeds, he knows his way around literary archives. The shadowy landscape of the Elizabethan era is terra firma for Honan, who has also written a biography of Shakespeare.

Scholars steeped in the minutiae of Marlowe's life will applaud Honan's diligent labors to glean truths from documents more than four centuries old. The Latin scribbling of the bursar at Cambridge's Corpus Christi College, which Marlowe attended on scholarship, show down to the farthing how much the student spent on beer and lamb chops and just when he was absent from the university. The records appear to support the theory that Marlowe's meager finances caused him to enlist as a spy, taking frequent leaves from his studies to ferret out subversive Catholic loyalists.

That bit of scholarship, however, merely corroborates a well-established fact. Marlowe was a part-time spook. In that capacity he traveled on the government's business to the Netherlands and possibly France (Honan hedges on France) in the treacherous demimonde of ''moles and watchers, turncoat papists and agents provocateurs," as the author puts it.

Actually, many of the broad outlines of Marlowe's life are well known. The son of a shoemaker, he studied the classics at Cambridge, burst onto the London theater scene as a brilliantly innovative and controversial playwright with works such as ''Tamburlaine the Great" and ''Doctor Faustus," and died from a stab wound to the head in 1593.

Honan treads the familiar turf with sure-footed steps. In this portrait of the milieu in which Marlowe lived and worked, the author's erudition is on full -- some would say pedantically digressive -- display. He writes that ''Tamburlaine" is ''the first play to mix blank verse effectively with speech rhythms, to break it up with dactylic feet and to give it lightness, flexibility, and a haunting power."

Ever the scholar, Honan trains his skeptical eye on the mysteries surrounding Marlowe. He proceeds cautiously.

The fragility of the endeavor extends to the identity of the faintly mustachioed, laser-eyed young man that ''Christopher Marlowe" depicts on its jacket. The portrait's discovery came about in 1952, when a Cambridge University student ripped up two oak planks that were supporting a gas fireplace in his room. The circumstances point to Marlowe as the subject.

Some scholars agree. Honan tilts that way, but withholds judgment. If the image that adorns his book's jacket is not Marlowe's, the biographer would be at a loss to describe his subject's physical features. No other likeness of Marlowe is extant.

Clearly on the record is a claim by some of Marlowe's friends in the London of his day that he was an atheist. In 16th-century England, blasphemy could be a capital offense.

Marlowe offered an inviting target for those who would denounce him. Some of his most vivid characters, including Tamburlaine and Faustus, utter blasphemous remarks and spurn the conventions of Christian morality.

Of course, the religious views that Marlowe's characters express may not coincide with his own. Honan makes the obvious point, implying that Marlowe was not a religious polemicist but a storyteller who employed atheistic themes to pose searing questions about the human condition.

That some of Marlowe's closest friends quote him in letters as having expressed atheistic views (he would ''jest at the divine scriptures" and ''gibe at prayers," according to one such account) suggests the contrary, but Honan dismisses those reports as a red herring. He theorizes, not convincingly, that Marlowe's comments to his friends may have been simply an act, a pose to ''sound as outrageous as his plays were said to be."

As for Marlowe's sexuality, conclusive evidence is similarly lacking. Conceding the point, Honan nonetheless insinuates that ''there is no reason to think he was particularly chaste at Cambridge or later on."

What about ''Edward II," Marlowe's play about a homosexual king whose overweening passion for male lovers leads to his death? The work ''legitimizes homoerotic desire by treating it more realistically than any English playwright had done before," Honan observes, pointedly. Was Marlowe thus a homosexual? Honan straddles the fence.

Nor does Honan's inquiry into the circumstances surrounding Marlowe's death come close to solving that mystery. He effectively dispatches the coroner's version that Marlowe was fatally stabbed in a mundane disagreement over settling a dinner bill. But what about the web of suspicious circumstances that surrounded the murder? Marlowe was under Privy Council scrutiny for heresy; his assailant worked as the business agent for the playwright's espionage handler, Thomas Walsingham. Could the risk to Walsingham of the potentially ruinous disclosure that Marlowe was one of his operatives have been the motive in a murder conspiracy?

Honan leaves the question hanging. His teasingly inconclusive biography does have a saving grace. He is candid in acknowledging that much about the Marlowe mysteries eludes him and that ''history holds its doors open" for final answers, should any ever be forthcoming.

Joseph Rosenbloom is a senior correspondent for The American Prospect.
