BOOK REVIEW

Sudden plot twists leave 'Rant' empty

Chuck Palahniuk wrote "Rant" in the form of oral-history interviews.

By Kevin O'Kelly  |  May 16, 2007

Rant: An Oral Biography of Buster Casey, By Chuck Palahniuk, Doubleday, 318 pp., $24.95

Reading the latest Chuck Palahniuk novel is an invariably gripping, always disturbing, and -- more and more often -- ultimately disappointing experience. He's a writer of remarkable talent, willing to look unflinchingly at despairing lives and their often-warped quests for even momentary redemption. He's a painfully deft chronicler of the meaningless job, the poisonous relationship, and of all the myriad damaging and deadening effects of so-called normal life. But time and again he manages to blow it: Whatever emotional depth he achieves is undercut by his seemingly incurable adolescent streak.

"Rant" is written in the form of oral-history interviews with people who knew Buster "Rant" Casey. He may have been a serial killer. He was definitely the cause of a nationwide rabies epidemic, having been an asymptomatic carrier for years. Buster's very life begins as a disaster for others: He was born when his mother was only 13. As a neighbor of the Caseys said, "The first bright future he ever wrecked was his mama's." He grows up in Middleton, a small town of improbable wretchedness. Wild dogs roam the streets. Frequent high winds knock over garbage cans, leaving streets strewn with trash.

The outer bleakness of Middleton is matched by the brutality of Buster's home life. As a child hunting Easter eggs in his parents' garden, Buster is bitten by a poisonous spider. His father refuses to call the doctor until he's finished finding the eggs: " If you don't take too long, maybe you won't lose that hand."

As he grows older, Buster takes refuge from the life around him in an unusual kind of thrill-seeking: He sticks his hand down animal burrows in hopes of getting bitten. The fear, the sudden pain, and then the equally sudden knowledge that it's all over give him a rush. "A rattlesnake's just my vaccination against boredom," he says. He develops an addiction to the venom of various animals and, from one of the nonpoisonous animals that bites him, catches rabies. Unfortunately, the disease can be spread by mere human saliva, and Buster is popular with the girls.

After high school, Buster moves to an unspecified big city, where a new mode of thrill-seeking awaits him. "Party Crashers" are teams of four who drive around the city at night, looking for other Crashers to chase and, ultimately, ram into. It's a very organized sport with detailed rules and multiple winners: all those people who get the high that comes with knowing they've been hit by a car and are still alive.

This section of the novel presents an appallingly believable future along the "high-tech-and-low-life" lines of cyberpunk. Virtual reality has reached a new level of intensity and ubiquity, thanks to "ports" that directly link multimedia with your central nervous system. Radio has reached a new low: The most popular program on the air is "Graphic Traffic," detailed, live reports of automobile accidents, complete with descriptions of driver injuries. And a new form of segregation thrives in urban society, in which much of the population, largely a burgeoning underclass, can leave their homes only at night.

Nevertheless, it's in the final chapters of this section -- the final chapters of the book -- that Palahniuk completely betrays the readers whom he's led so far. Note to all writers of dystopian fiction: If you've written a story set in a plausible dark future, don't completely destroy its believability by throwing in a sophomoric plot twist that attempts to weave together time travel, reincarnation, and Nietzsche's idea of the eternal recurrence . If I didn't know better, I would think the last 90 pages were written by a college freshman poaching ideas from his physics, comparative religion, and introductory philosophy classes.

It's infuriating: Chuck Palahniuk could be so much better than this.

Kevin O'Kelly is a regular reviewer for the Globe. He has a blog at notesand comments1.blogspot.com.
