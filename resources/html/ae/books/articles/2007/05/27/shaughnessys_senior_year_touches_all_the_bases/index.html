Shaughnessy's 'Senior Year' touches all the bases

By Brion O'Connor  |  May 27, 2007

Senior Year: A Father, a Son, and High School Baseball
By Dan Shaughnessy
Houghton Mifflin, 228 pp., $24

Every summer, my wife makes a beeline to the beach with an armful of new books, along with the kids, sunscreen, cooler, and blankets. I'm not that ambitious, especially since my attention span, given all the distractions, isn't conducive to serious book browsing. Magazines, something cerebral like Sports Illustrated, are more my speed. But now I can recommend a real book. Dan Shaughnessy, a sports columnist at The Boston Globe, has penned the perfect antidote for my affliction, "Senior Year." Blithe, breezy, funny in a disjointed, diary kind of way, "Senior Year" is a tasty slice of Americana, Eastern Massachusetts-style, that explores Shaughnessy's relationship with his only son and youngest child, Sam.

Baseball, a passion shared by both, is a convenient and constant backdrop, but "Senior Year" is more a touching snapshot of father and child tiptoeing through today's generational minefield. To wit, Shaughnessy cautions parents who think they know exactly what their children are doing when out of sight. "No way," he writes. "As a parent of a teen, your best bet is to set an example, show them the way, and then pray that nothing goes drastically wrong."

This is not a literary tour de force, which is fine with me. There's poignancy, which you'd expect from any rite-of-passage tale, especially one exploring the bond between father and son. The opening scene, juxtaposing the author fidgeting to watch Sam get one last late-game at-bat before leaving for the wake of a cousin's son, is jarring and powerful. For the most part, though, the fare is light hearted, often bouncing between Shaughnessy's hilarious recollections of growing up in rural Groton and the present-day reality of his son's final year at upper-crust Newton North. There's plenty of old school meets new school.

Indeed, "Senior Year" is much like Will Ferrell's wacky "Old School," but with a reality regulator. Rather than reliving his youth, Shaughnessy employs it as a prism to provide insight into Sam's world, and to illustrate how much times have changed in three short decades. Shaughnessy offers a touching, often humorous tour of his Newton household and neighborhood. Admittedly, I'm the ideal audience for Shaughnessy's musings. A graying, 40-something baby boomer of Irish descent, an avowed Boston sports nut and a father of two active girls, I share many of the author's sentiments, interests, and his uncanny knack for being on the road when bad things happen at home . I also share his distinct aversion to today's touchy-feely approach to parenting, youth sports, and political correctness. Often I found myself nodding in agreement (or smirking) with his withering, dead-on portrayal of New Age Newton do-gooders who substitute Tug of Peace for Tug of War, ban Halloween in elementary schools, and delete the asterisk signifying scholastic excellence from the high school graduation program at Newton North. As social commentary goes, it's sidesplitting, if not always subtle.

Maybe Shaughnessy's wise-cracking, sport-centric view of the world, well known to readers of the Globe sports section, explains his refreshing unwillingness to conform. Sports, at least at the collegiate and professional levels, invariably focus on winning and losing, and winners and losers. It's a clear-cut, black-and-white bottom line. That, apparently, doesn't always square with the social agenda of soccer moms and dads. "We don't want to hurt anyone's feelings and once again we celebrate sameness rather than reward individual achievement. We did it when they were 6 and we made sure they all got trophies even if they didn't win a game. We did it when they played soccer and we didn't keep score so that nobody would feel badly."

In fairness, the author's acid-tipped pen isn't reserved solely for his Newton neighbors. When the Notre Dame baseball coach, who had courted Sam, leaves to take over the Louisiana State University program, Shaughness y writes: "[Coach Paul] Mainieri took the staff with him, including Terry Rooney, the enthusiastic recruiter who'd told all the high school players, 'If you just want to be good, go someplace else -- if you want to be great, come to Notre Dame!' Turned out Rooney and friends . . . ultimately settled for just being good, not great." Shaughnessy even delights, as he does in his Globe columns, in skewering Boston sports icons such as Red Sox ace Curt Schilling .

The counterweight to this satire is Shaughness y's self-deprecating humor, which is both disarming and endearing. He refers to himself as a "doofus dad," admits to "old man" bathroom breaks at 3 a.m., and wallows in his Irish Catholic guilt over parenting missteps. He's rarely mean-spirited. Sarcastic, yes, but not vicious. And through it all, we get to watch father and son share a front seat in the testosterone-fueled roller coaster of high school. It's an enjoyable ride, with all the tension and exhilaration of teenage foibles, but coupled with the overriding sense that all will end up fine.

Sam's career, of course, isn't over. He's playing collegiate ball, and if his postsecondary career follows the same trajectory, we can expect a sequel -- "Senior Year 2" -- sometime in 2010, provided Sam offers his father the same unfettered access Shaughnessy enjoyed while writing the original. My wife might pass, but I'm looking forward to it.
