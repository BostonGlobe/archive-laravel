BOOK REVIEW

Horses, family, and mystery are at its heart

By Caroline Leavitt  |  April 10, 2007

The God of Animals, By Aryn Kyle, Scribner, 320 pp., $25

Fictional debuts, like the young colts in this fine first novel, often are more about the exciting promise of a neophyte than the actual talent shown. More than often autobiographical, first novels can also be a little familiar. Not so with "The God of Animals," a first novel that's so strong, startling, and moving, that it's a thoroughbred from the first page. Based on the short story "Foaling Season," which won a National Magazine Award for Fiction for The Atlantic Monthly, Aryn Kyle's novel is an eloquent and haunted testament to grief, family attachments, and the rocky coastline of the human heart.

Kyle's novel focuses on one particular summer in the life of 12-year-old Alice Winston. Caught in the dangerous precipice between adolescence and adulthood, Alice lives on a ramshackle horse farm with her parents in heat-soaked Desert Valley, Colo. At the very start of the novel, Polly (a casual classmate of Alice's) has been found dead in the canal, an event that immediately haunts Alice and suffuses the book with equal parts mystery and melancholy. Alice's gorgeous older sister, Nona, the mercurial and prize-winning rider in the family, has run off with a cowboy. Alice's mother is so depressed, she's been in bed since Alice was a baby. Even Alice's father, Jody, is not up to the task of being a father. Desperate for money to save his failing ranch, Jody scrapes by boarding the horses of wealthy housewives (derogatively called catfish) and teaching Sheila, a rich and untalented girl, to ride. It's left to Alice to fend for herself, to navigate her world, and come of age as best she can.

In stark, gorgeous prose, Kyle tunnels into the dark heart of the connections between people and place. Alice stubbornly turns away from the desperate companionship that lonely Sheila offers her, and instead feeds her increasing obsession with Polly. When she discovers Polly had a relationship with a male English teacher, Alice begins an unsettlingly creepy friendship with the teacher, too, lying to him that she and Polly were best friends. As Jody begins to get chummier with one of his female clients, Nona and her husband return to the ranch, broke, beaten down, their marriage fraying. Through these webs of relationships, Alice begins to see how much cruelty, secrecy, and lies infuse the adult world -- and her own. As she discovers that children can hurt one another as terribly as adults can, a shocking act of violence propels the novel to its shattering conclusion.

Part of the pleasure of this novel is the way Kyle infuses her story with a rich sense of time, people, and place. The death of Polly runs like a tapestry thread through the whole of the novel, glinting here and there under the larger concerns and themes. Alice's relationship with the English teacher, based on a lie, never veers into the usual melodrama the reader might expect, but stays dangerously undefined and completely sympathetic and original. Kyle's West is a world of show horses, barrel racers, and jumpers, and of the gritty details of mucking out a stall, birthing a foal, and putting a horse to stud. It's a universe where breaking a horse can make you feel like God, but as Alice intuits, "Riding a horse once didn't mean that it was broken," and sometimes things are never the way you think they are.

"The God of Animals" does what the best fiction does -- it creates a whole living, breathing world and unfolds it in front of us, granting us entry into a place that, like this author, is impossible to forget. And as Alice becomes an adult, she realizes that childhood never really ends, that everyone gets hurts, and that sometimes the best thing you can do for the people you love is to keep their secrets.

Caroline Leavitt's most recent novel is "Girls in Trouble." She can be reached at carolineleavitt.com
