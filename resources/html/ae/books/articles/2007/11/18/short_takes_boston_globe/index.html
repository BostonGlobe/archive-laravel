Short Takes

Email|Print| Text size – + By Amanda Heller
November 18, 2007

The Quiet Girl
By Peter Hoeg
Translated, from the Danish, by Nadia Christensen
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 408 pp., $26

From the land of Kierkegaard comes this self-described philosophical thriller by Peter Hoeg, author of the 1993 cult bestseller "Smilla's Sense of Snow." Kasper Krone, the protagonist of this paranoid phantasmagoria - world-class circus clown, violinist, and deadbeat - is, like Smilla, gifted with extraordinary perception: a magical sense of hearing.

Much good it does him. Broke and solitary, on the verge of deportation, Kasper is pressed by an order of nuns, who practice an unusually muscular Christianity, to find a little girl, one of a dozen children with paranormal powers who have fallen into the hands of sinister forces. Or perhaps, more convoluted than that, a dark coalition of church, state, and industry is using the children to profit from the natural disaster that has submerged half of Copenhagen. As Kasper's geologist ex-girlfriend returns and helpful strangers materialize at his side, we know only that no one is telling the truth, least of all to us.

Like James Bond on existential steroids, Kasper dives into a vortex of increasingly dense mystery and grotesque violence. Hoeg spins a gripping tale, though it may be more of a cerebral workout than fans of truth, justice, and linear storytelling are in the market for.

The Vanishing Act of Esme Lennox
By Maggie O'Farrell
Harcourt, 256 pp., $23

This haunting novel begins in the period between the world wars with two young Scottish girls growing up in India, though the moral universe it describes harks back to the Victorian era. Kitty, the elder of the two girls, is on her way to becoming the demure young lady of her parents' expectations. Her sister Esme, impulsive and ungovernable, is another matter. When tragedy strikes, the girls' repressed parents turn all their grief and anger against Esme, making her their scapegoat. With the family's return to Scotland, Esme deflects their emotional abuse with defiance until, after a final crisis, her parents exile her in cold fury to a mental institution, effectively cutting her out of the family forever.

The enormity of Esme's victimization begins to come clear when the novel shifts to the present and a young woman named Iris Lockhart receives a staggering phone call: The institution is closing, and Esme, now an old woman, is to be released into the custody of Iris, the great-niece who never even knew Esme existed. Readers expecting a soft-focus tale of family reconciliation will find themselves shockingly misled, for Maggie O'Farrell is a feminist avenging angel who wields the modern Gothic like a gleaming sword.

The Far Traveler: Voyages of a Viking Woman
By Nancy Marie Brown
Harcourt, 306 pp., $25

In the construction of history, perceptions count. Five hundred years before Columbus "discovered" America, there were Norse settlements in Vinland, the Vikings' name for what is now Newfoundland. We accept this cognitive dissonance because we think of the Vikings as barbarian marauders whose feats couldn't possibly compare to those of Renaissance entrepreneurs. But archeological finds from Scandinavia to the New World support what the Norse sagas relate: that the Norsemen were farmers compelled by the exigencies of a feudal economy to sail ever westward to the end of the sea in search of new pasturelands.

The sagas speak of a chieftain's daughter, Gudrid the Far Traveler, who crossed the North Atlantic eight times and bore a son in the New World. A Christian convert, in advanced age she made a pilgrimage overland to Rome.

Nancy Marie Brown, touring archeological digs in Iceland, Greenland, and Canada, throws herself into imagining the details of life in the medieval Norse diaspora. Although the scantiness of actual evidence beyond the occasional cloak pin or spindle makes Gudrid's story one of "may haves" and "could haves," Brown's enthusiasm is infectious as she re-teaches us our history.

Amanda Heller is a critic and editor who lives in Newton.
