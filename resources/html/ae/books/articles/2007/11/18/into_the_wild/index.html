Into the wild

William Kittredge's essays immerse us in the myth and heartbreak of a vanishing West

(WACSO)
Email|Print| Text size – + By Rick Bass
November 18, 2007

The Next Rodeo: New and Selected Essays
By William Kittredge
Graywolf, 240 pp., paperback, $15

William Kittredge has rightly been called the dean of Western American literature. In this collection of essays written over 30 years, the reader can follow the evolution of Kittredge's discovery and exploration of the notion that the stories we tell ourselves shape us as individuals, and that a larger sense of story also shapes culture - particularly on a tablet in the West that, from the perspective of the written word, is still as relatively blank as the region's largely unpopulated landscapes.

About his writing, the Chicago Tribune has said, "Kittredge's essays intersect mightily with the myth of American expansion and 'improvement' of the 'wilderness.' . . . Kittredge's voice should be a prophetic one."

Kittredge's essays are dense with ideas as well as language. They often read like a short story, where the author rolls up his sleeves and settles in to give the reader not just a vision, but a pervasive, gripping mood, sometimes exultant, or elegiac, or other times brusque; sometimes humorous, other times daunting and somber. These moods are earned and precise, never sentimental or weak or indulgent.

Again and again Kittredge speaks in terms of one's life as a story, and of not just the opportunity, but the responsibility, to tell that story, and to adjust it, when possible, toward a greater moral goodness. One can hear the echoes of the great Wallace Stegner in Kittredge's essay "Owning It All," in which he writes of his childhood in Oregon's Warner Valley, and the dozing and draining of vast wetlands. "We baited the coyotes with 1080, and rodents destroyed our alfalfa; we sprayed weeds and insects with 2-4-D Ethyl and Malathion, and Parathion for clover mite, and we shortened our own lives. . . . We had reinvented our valley according to the most pervasive ideal given us by our culture, and we ended up with a landscape organized like a machine . . . a machine that creaked a little louder each year, a dreamland gone wrong."

Later in the same essay, one can hear the lyrical Kittredge, in a voice and sensibility that are his own, but that are also reminiscent of a contemporary of Kittredge's, the great Texas writer John Graves. "But never again in my lifetime will it be possible for a child to stand out on a bright spring morning in Warner Valley and watch the waterbirds come through in enormous, rafting vee-shaped flocks of thousands - and I grieve."

In Kittredge's view - and any careful reading of history thus far would seem to bear him out - the stories that white European immigrants to the West constructed for this landscape were flawed from the beginning. "A mythology can be understood as a story that contains a set of implicit instructions from a society to its members, telling them what is valuable and how to conduct themselves if they are to preserve the things they cherish."

Those things - the waterbirds in their immense V-shaped flocks, and countless other treasures, previously taken for granted by white Westerners - are going away now, or gone, and Kittredge, as much as any Western writer, has had a ringside seat in their going-away.

The myth of agricultural ownership - like so many other myths - was just flat wrong from the beginning, says Kittredge. "The truth is, we never owned all the land and water. We don't even own very much of them, privately. And we don't own anything absolutely or forever. As our society grows more and more complex and interwoven, our entitlement becomes less and less absolute, more and more likely to be legally diminished. Our rights to property will never take precedence over the needs of society. Nor should they, we all must agree in our grudging hearts. Ownership of property has always been a privilege granted by society, and revokable."

To be certain, men and writers like Kittredge and Graves are pragmatic ideologues. They have seen too much, and know too much about human nature - and still like some, though certainly not all, of what they see - to give up on the whole sorry mess. Kittredge's beloved West is facing dramatic ecological, social, and economic threats, but he still is willing to give counsel on its, and our, behalf.

At the end of the last essay, there is a statement that, it occurs to me, it took Kittredge all of his life to write. It's lengthy, but I want to quote it because I think he would like it to be widely read. It's a gift of the wisdom of his intelligence and experience: "The West where I grew up was inflexible and because of that, irresponsible. . . . Citizens who base ethical judgments on rigid political, economic, or religious doctrines, however commonly accepted or deplored those notions may be, are at least partly dysfunctional in democratic societies because they have essentially abandoned their responsibility to work out problems for themselves. We hope future liberals and conservatives aren't blinded by concepts and precepts and don't turn into just another set of rigid, dogmatic 'true believers.' The quandaries westerners face will have to be flexibly resolved by the raggedy and the rich, up-country and downtown. Love life; maintain patience and strength of will while attempting generosity."

If you're already a fan of Kittredge's, you'll enjoy this anthology of new and selected work, and if you haven't read him, this is a great text that will guide a reader into the rest of his important work.

Rick Bass is the author of 23 books of fiction and nonfiction. His memoir, "Why I Came West," is slated for release in June.
