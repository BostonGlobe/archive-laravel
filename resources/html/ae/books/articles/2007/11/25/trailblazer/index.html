Trailblazer

On the trail and in camp, the 1907 "White Mountain Guide" became a bible for hikers. The book is now in its 28th edition. (AMC Library)
Email|Print| Text size – + By Jan Gardner
November 25, 2007

In 1907, the White Mountains were in rough shape. They had been devastated by fires and clear-cutting, and a political debate raged over their future. That year, the Appalachian Mountain Club published the "White Mountain Guide." It soon became a bible for hikers. Now in its 28th edition, the book has fostered a vocal constituency for the preservation of the Whites.

To celebrate the oldest continuously published trail guide in America, the AMC has released "White Mountain Guide: A Centennial Retrospective." With dozens of vintage photos (such as below), it is a folksy history of the volunteers who climbed and measured every trail and who understand wilderness survival.

AMC hut master Joe Dodge made his Pinkham Notch insect repellent with creosote, pine tar, olive oil, and citronella, noting that it is not recommended for "delicate complexions or sensitive nostrils." For hikers contemplating an overnight trip, the advice in an early edition involved nothing so uncomfortable as sleeping on the ground: "Write in advance to Miss M. H. Buck, West Rindge, N. H., and engage accommodations at Wapack Lodge."

The whole 'Hiawatha'

Michael Maglaras will portray more than 45 characters -- including monsters, magicians, and talking animals - in what is expected to be a five-hour performance of Henry Wadsworth Longfellow's poem "The Song of Hiawatha" on Saturday. To keep up the audience's strength, complimentary refreshments will be served at four intermissions.

The performance - in celebration of the 200th anniversary of Longfellow's birth - starts at noon at St. Lawrence Arts & Community Center, 76 Congress St., Portland, Maine. It is cosponsored by the Maine Historical Society, which owns the house in Portland where Longfellow grew up.

Out and about

The Boston-based Gay Community News was always a shoestring operation with outsize ambitions. Founded in 1973 - before AIDS and gay weddings - GCN was run by activists who were as interested in making news as they were in covering it.

In "An Army of Ex-Lovers: My Life at the Gay Community News," former GCN editor Amy Hoffman, now editor of the Women's Review of Books, recalls with wit the heady debates that roiled the staff. Although GCN never had a circulation of more than 5,000, it was a vital connection for readers coming out of the closet. In addition, the paper was a training ground for lesbian and gay leaders who, Hoffman writes, "went on to run practically every gay organization in the country."

Pick of the week

Lori Kauffman of Brookline Booksmith recommends "The Scent of Desire: Discovering Our Enigmatic Sense of Smell," by Rachel Herz (Morrow): "Since reading [it] I've noticed myself inhaling more deeply, wanting to capture the smells around me (at a restaurant or florist - even on the bus). This clever examination of the physiology and psychology of scent will wake you up to the world around you."
