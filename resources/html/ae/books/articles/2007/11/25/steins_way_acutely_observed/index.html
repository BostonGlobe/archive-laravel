Stein's way, acutely observed

Malcolm dissects writer's successes and silences

Gertrude Stein (left) and her companion, Alice B. Toklas, in Bilignin, France, 1938. (Cecil Beaton/From "Gertrude Stein: In Words and Pictures")
Email|Print| Text size – + By Richard Eder
November 25, 2007

Two Lives: Gertrude and Alice
By Janet Malcolm
Yale University, 229 pp., illustrated, $25

Janet Malcolm is a crusading writer and a consummately elegant one. Her crusades, perhaps uniquely, are inward. She is a kind of confessor-inquisitor or inspector general of the shapings, elisions, and shortfcuts practiced by writers of nonfiction including, at times, herself.

Two of her best-known works question the justice to be found, respectively, in a much-admired journalistic book about a doctor convicted of murder, and in accounts of the tragedy of Sylvia Plath that cast her husband, poet Ted Hughes, as its villain.

More profoundly, Malcolm argues the inherent contradiction between writing's art and its truth. She could be a latter-day Plato, assailing artists as corrupters of truth, and using her own art to say so. In her new book, "Two Lives," she notes the tendency of biography, her specialty, to flatten secondary characters in order to make its protagonist stand out.

The biographer "must cultivate a kind of narcissism on behalf of his subject that blinds him to the full humanity of anyone else," she writes. "As he turns the bracing storylessness of human life into the flaccid narrativity of biography, he cannot worry about the people who never asked to be dragged into his shaky enterprise."

Malcolm's latest sally into the battle of truth and lies makes Gertrude Stein its target. Except that here we find Malcolm riding alongside the adversary almost as often as against her. "Two Lives" (a play on Stein's celebrated collection "Three Lives") is as much excursion as incursion, as much a wandering feast as a focused fight.

It begins by asking how Stein and Alice B. Toklas, Jewish and lesbian, managed to go on living in their lovely country home in France under Vichy and the German occupation. It cites the warnings they received through their lawyer urging flight, and Stein's decision to stay put. She'd been a cosseted child, and in her autobiographical writing had constructed an image of cheerfully god-like invulnerability. "Nobody can do anything but take care of you," she wrote.

Malcolm extends this formidably constructed self-confidence - part real, part mask - to the aura of beauty (Hemingway found her sexy) she sustained despite being exceedingly fat. "She always seemed to like her own fat," Mabel Dodge Luhan wrote.

With a mix of irritation and bemusement, Malcolm considers the extraordinary switch Stein pulled off in "The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas." By pretending that Toklas had written it, she could write about herself without seeming to do so. With this device, and with her extraordinary blend of surreal comedy and lethal bite (the book was enormously appealing and a rare commercial success), she was able to depict herself without apparent brag as the unruffled Sun Queen of a court that included just about every artistic and literary celebrity in her world.

Irritation turns to anger, though, when Malcolm writes about the gaps in Stein's account of their wartime stay in France. She cites Stein's silence about the full role of Bernard Fay, a high Vichy official sentenced to life imprisonment after the war. A viciously anti-Semitic writer and a Gestapo collaborator, he was a friend of Stein's (as in: some of my best friends are Jews) and the translator and promoter of some of her most difficult experimental work.

Not only did Fay visit Stein and Toklas, but also, as one of Marshal Petain's top cultural advisers, he got the local prefecture to make certain that his vulnerable friends were safe.

Much of Malcolm's material about Fay comes from the scholarly research and reportorial verve of Professor Edward Burns, a Stein expert; much of her insight into Stein's writing comes from the literary scholar Ulla Dydo. She has mined their discoveries, while paying a tribute that includes appealing sketches of each. What she has added is a lively narrative and her illuminating, sharp, and occasionally crotchety comments.

To Malcolm, Stein's failure to air Fay's entire role approaches the unforgivable. You might argue, though, that her writing, whether (relatively) plain or impossibly curly, was her art; and artists' art is not necessarily expected to include their living (much less survival) arrangements. Picasso did not require his mistresses to have both eyes on one side of their nose.

And even on the biographical question, Malcolm comes partway around. (In writing, it is the circle, not the straight line, that encloses territory.)

Steinwas boastful and refused to admit darkness "never got in the way of her understanding of human insignificance," Malcolm writes. " 'The Autobiography of Alice B. Toklas' slyly mocks the immortality biography seeks to bestow on its subjects. If you listen to the book's music, you will catch the low hum of melancholy. If you regard it as an exercise in whistling in the dark, you will understand its brilliance."

Richard Eder reviews books for several publications.
