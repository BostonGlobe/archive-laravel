Awash with memories

27 years later, book gives storm survivors' account of tragedy

Email|Print| Text size – + By Kathleen McKenna
Globe Correspondent / November 29, 2007

Fall is always a tough time for offshore fishermen who trap and haul lobster eight months a year. Besides the freezing temperatures and the limited daylight, hurricane-size storms become more frequent as the fishing season draws to a close.

For Peter Brown, a fisherman's son who now captains a lobster boat of his own, fall is tough for another reason. It brings him back to the monstrous storm in late November 1980 that took the life of one crew member and nearly killed Brown and two others. Three of the four men working on a second boat 10 miles away also died.

"It was one of those situations that's just branded into your soul and your mind forever," said Brown, who survived the storm after a daunting rescue effort by a third fishing boat. "When I look back I'm amazed that I continued fishing."

Gary Brown (no relation), 35, of Plymouth fell overboard after a 70-foot wave washed into the 50-foot wooden boat, Sea Fever, knocking the vessel sideways and blowing out the side of the pilot house. The other three men aboard clung to the boat and tried in vain to save their friend.

"I can still see his face when we were trying to rescue him," Peter Brown said in a recent telephone interview from his home in Bourne. "We were so close, and then he was just gone."

A monument at Manomet Bluffs in Plymouth was erected in memory of Gary Brown, whose wife gave birth to their only child soon after his death.

Twenty-seven years later, Michael J. Tougias of Franklin, a writer who has penned a dozen or so books of disaster and survival in New England seas, came across Coast Guard reports of the 1980 storm. He was impressed by its relentless fury, and by the courage and heroism of its survivors.

Tougias contacted the men involved, conducted extensive interviews, then chronicled their stories in a new book called "Fatal Forecast: An Incredible True Tale of Disaster and Survival at Sea." The book is topping South Shore bestseller lists, and is ranked first in the amazon.com shipping/shipwreck category.

"Fatal Forecast" is a compelling account of the Sea Fever and the other vessel, the Fair Wind, which capsized and trapped three men inside. All three died. The fourth Fair Wind crew member, Ernie Hazard, swam to a covered rubber raft that was tethered to the boat, where he rode out the storm for an agonizing 50 hours.

Georges Bank, about 130 miles off the Cape, is known as a fertile yet dangerous fishing ground, due to its location in the Atlantic and its tremendous currents. Until they lost radio power, the crew of both boats kept in close contact. All were stunned by the magnitude of the storm, mainly because the forecast was nothing out of the ordinary. If the men had known beforehand about the hurricane-force waves they'd face, they told Tougias, they never would have left shore.

Peter Brown's father owned the Andrea Gail, the boat of "Perfect Storm" book and movie fame. But the books are vastly different, Tougias said, because there were no survivors of the Andrea Gail; author Sebastian Junger drew his own conclusions from land reports.

Tougias reasons that the surviving fishermen were willing to talk to him because 27 years have elapsed. "I couldn't have written the book right after it happened," he said. "No one could. The guys just couldn't have opened up."

He was particularly struck by the extraordinary survival and rescue of Ernie Hazard, who for two long days was forced to brace himself against the walls of his rubber raft so he wouldn't fall back into the freezing sea. When he called Hazard and asked to interview him, the now-retired fisherman invited him to his home in California for a week. Tougias accepted, and they became friends.

"He's a great guy, a very different guy, and kind of a maverick," Tougias said. He believes that Hazard withstood the storm because he was able to break his ordeal into small parts.

"He didn't think too far into the future," Tougias said. "And whenever he started thinking about the past - about his friends he knew were dead - he forced himself to push it to the back of his mind."

Family members of the four men who died sued the National Weather Service on the grounds that it had neglected to repair two weather buoys. They won the case, but the decision was overturned when a judge deemed that the government couldn't be held liable.

While he was researching the book, some of Tougias's interview subjects took him lobster-fishing so he could get an idea of the challenges they face, even in good weather.

"It was exhausting," he said. "And they gave me the easy jobs."

Peter Brown said he thinks Tougias did an "excellent job" with the book, but acknowledged that "some parts are painful to go through."

"I was a very young man then, and those two boats were just tremendously overpowered," he said. Nowadays, he said, the Sea Fever and the Fair Wind would probably be deemed too small to brave Georges Bank.

Tougias, who recently quit his day job as an underwriter for an insurance company, has created a documentary-style slide show that includes photos of the Coast Guard vessel rescuing Hazard, and waves big enough to overturn a boat. He's scheduled to speak at the Hull Lifesaving Museum on Dec. 6.
