Music gift guide

Sound advice on books

Email|Print| Text size – +
November 23, 2007

"Born in the Bronx: A Visual Record of the Early Days of Hip Hop,"
edited by Johan Kugelberg

As you turn the pages of this lovingly curated look at the birth of hip-hop in the "Boogie Down" Bronx of the 1970s and '80s, you can practically hear the snap of breakbeats, smell the fumes of spray paint, and feel the bodies bumping on neighborhood basketball courts. The book attempts to capture the volatile borough, which was both a vibrant melting pot and racial pressure cooker filled with social activists and destructive gangs, good music, and bad drugs.

Editor Johan Kugelberg lays out an eye-popping assortment of artifacts from this criminally under-documented formative period. Photos of early pioneers such as Grandmaster Caz and the Cold Crush Brothers, many by scenester Joe Conzo, contrast with stark images of burnt-out lots strewn with garbage and dotted by abandoned cars. A clutch of flyers illustrates how the scene morphed from house parties, with handwritten invites on lined 3-by-5 cards, to swanky affairs announced by the elaborate, Art Deco-inspired creations of Buddy Esquire.

A foreword by Afrika Bambaataa and a timeline by hip-hop historian Jeff Chang further tell the tale of how urban blight and youthful enthusiasm combined to create this new art form. Kugelberg also shares the personal soundtrack he used while putting the book together as well as lists of other must-have and hard-to-find artists of the era, many of whom never made the transition from vinyl glory.

While most of the names will be unfamiliar to all but the most hard-core hip-hop heads, this is history worth learning for fans of everyone from Kool Herc to Jay-Z.

>Autumn de Wilde, "Elliott Smith"
Culled from years of friendship and photo shoots with the late, great indie rocker, de Wilde includes interviews with Smith's confidantes and collaborators, handwritten lyrics and ephemera, and a five-song EP of previously unheard recordings.

Robert Matheu and Brian J. Bowe, "Creem: America's Only Rock 'n' Roll Magazine"
A first-rate retrospective of the magazine that put the snark into rock criticism and photography without ever sacrificing respect for the music.

Jonathan Gould, "Can't Buy Me Love: The Beatles, Britain, and America"
Think this tale has already been told? Check out Gould's thoughtful examination of the Fab Four's rise with emphasis on both the music and the social and cultural contexts.

Slash, "Slash" and Nikki Sixx, "The Heroin Diaries: A Year in the Life of a Shattered Rock Star"
Two horns up for these voyeuristic peeks into the debauched lives and songwriting processes of two of LA sleaze metal's biggest heroes.

Vivian Cash, "I Walked the Line: My Life With Johnny"
Before June there was Vivian, Johnny Cash's first wife, who was far more interesting than the vexing harpy depicted in the 2005 biopic "Walk the Line." In her posthumously released autobiography, she recounts the untold side of Cash's career and personal life while revealing she was every bit as feisty as the Man in Black she married at age 20.

Peter Barsocchini, "High School Musical: All Access"
For the tween on your list who can't get enough of the Wildcats, this colorful picture book offers a glimpse inside the lockers, to-do lists, and cookbooks (!) of the East High regulars.

Phil Ramone, "Making Records: The Scenes Behind the Music"
Having worked on Bob Dylan's "Blood on the Tracks" and Paul Simon's "Still Crazy After All These Years," Grammy-winning producer Ramone has written an engaging backstage memoir about his illustrious career in the music biz.

Ben Ratliff, "Coltrane: The Story of a Sound"
In his exhaustive, accessible biography of the jazz titan, New York Times scribe Ratliff examines Coltrane's stellar art as well as his vast legacy.

Various, "Best Music Writing 2007"
The eighth edition of the annual compilation is edited by rock-crit's grand poo-bah, Robert Christgau, and includes think pieces from the usual suspects as well as reflections from Richard Hell (on the closing of CBGB) and Jonathan Lethem (on "Being James Brown").

Ronnie Wood, "Ronnie: The Autobiography"
Clapton can claim the higher-wattage life story and sexier solo, but the Rolling Stones axman spins a livelier yarn by far - and spills more beans on the George/Patti/Eric love triangle, to boot. Wood's artwork is featured throughout.

Oliver Sacks, "Musicophilia: Tales of Music and the Brain"
In this collection of artfully rendered case studies and essays, the physician and author explores the wide-ranging power of music through the experiences of people who've been accidentally anointed, excruciatingly afflicted, and otherwise transformed by tones.
