Short Takes

June 17, 2007

The Big Girls
By Susanna Moore
Knopf, 224 pp., $24

What repels attracts. Novelist Susanna Moore ("In the Cut") has made a career exploring this perverse truth, paying painterly attention to the iridescent sheen that bedews suppurating psychological rot.

The first voice we hear in "The Big Girls" is that of Louise , chief psychiatrist at a women's prison to which a wounded deer named Helen, the second voice we hear, has been sentenced for murdering her young children. Although her crime was monstrous, Helen is no monster but rather a piteous victim. A walking diagnostic manual of psychotic symptoms, she is catnip to Louise, who is haunted by her own maternal and professional inadequacies. A third narrative voice belongs to Ike, a hard-boiled prison official, and a fourth to Angie, an opportunistic Hollywood starlet living with Louise's ex-husband.

Helen is the proverbial accident from which we can't avert our eyes. Through her horror story in particular we are made complicit in the novel's pervasive masochism. A compelling read (and a compelling case for penal reform), the plot whirls in a tightening spiral -- or is it spinning out of the author's control? -- as Angie comes to play an increasingly pivotal if credibility-straining role. Cynical moderns, we can accept the dank underside of human behavior, but Dickensian coincidence is beyond the pale.

Presence: Stories
By Arthur Miller
Viking, 176 pp., $23.95

When he died two years ago, at 89 , revered playwright Arthur Miller was putting the finishing touches on his last work, this collection of short stories.

Themes recur, particularly the autumnal motifs of loss and regret. Several viscerally affecting fictions involve incidents seen through the telescope of time. In the bittersweet "Bulldog," a 13-year-old boy voyages to terra incognita -- the other end of 1920s Brooklyn -- to buy a puppy. He comes away with the dog, and also with his sexual initiation, and doesn't know quite what to make of either. In "The Performer," a tap dancer recounts the bizarre experience of having danced for Hitler. And in "The Turpentine Still," an aging man recalls meeting on a visit to Haiti in the 1950s an American expatriate with an obsession so all-consuming that, looking back, he can only liken it to an artist's compulsion to create a body of work to leave behind as his testament.

Some of the pieces may have been more finished than others at the time of Miller's death. His sensibility, his life's experience, nevertheless speak powerfully throughout, as he takes command of a genre in which he was, after all, only moonlighting.

The Meaning of Life
By Terry Eagleton
Oxford University, 187 pp., illustrated, $19.95

The instructions for programming your latest electronic gizmo probably run longer than this cheeky philosophical meditation. " 'What is the meaning of life?', " writes Terry Eagleton, "is one of those rare questions in which almost every word is problematic."

For Eagleton, the first step toward answering it is to determine what the meaning of "meaning" is, a conundrum that would send most readers straight to the exits if not for the wit with which he approaches it and a comprehensive cultural intelligence that leaps from Shakespeare to Schopenhauer and from "Waiting for Godot" to "Monty Python" and "The Buena Vista Social Club."

When not impersonating a philosopher, Eagleton is of course an influential literary-cultural critic. He is most engaging here when explaining how modernism is paradoxically backward-looking, a movement in mourning for a past supposedly steeped in stable, universal meaning, a nostalgia rejected by Eagleton's own, postmodern generation, which sees the truth as far more complex and slippery than that.

Eagleton is writing for and from within a secular British intellectual culture. His ultimate prescriptions for locating the meaning in life are unlikely to sway those inclined, when mega-questions arise, to point to religion (their own, naturally) and consider the case closed.

Amanda Heller is a critic and editor who lives in Newton.
