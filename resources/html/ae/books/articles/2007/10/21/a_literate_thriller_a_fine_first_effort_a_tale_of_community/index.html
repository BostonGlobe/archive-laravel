Pop Lit

A literate thriller, a fine first effort, a tale of community

By Diane White  |  October 21, 2007

No Time for Goodbye
By Linwood Barclay
Bantam, 338 pp., $22

Isabella Moon
By Laura Benedict
Ballantine, 351 pp., $24.95

The View From Mount Joy
By Lorna Landvik
Ballantine, 349 pp., $24.95

Readers with a high thriller threshold should be pleased to meet Canadian writer Linwood Barclay. First-time author Laura Benedict makes a mighty effort, and nearly succeeds. Lorna Landvik gives her fans another folksy story full of lovable characters, plus one readers will love to hate.

Fourteen-year-old Cynthia Bigge wakes up one morning to a quiet house. Her mother, father, and brother are nowhere to be found. Cynthia had been acting up; she'd broken all her parents' rules. But no matter how angry they might be, they wouldn't just leave her, would they?

Barclay's "No Time for Goodbye" is a literate, well-constructed thriller that plays on the childhood fear of parental abandonment. When the police can't find any trace of Cynthia's family, she goes to live with her mother's sister in a town not far from her former home in Milford, Conn. Years later, Cynthia is back in Milford, married to high school English teacher Terry Archer. They have an 8-year-old daughter, Grace. Terry narrates most of the story in the first person. He has an appealing voice - low-key, funny, observant, a little cynical. To mark the 25th anniversary of her family's disappearance, Cynthia agrees to tell her story on a cold-case TV crime show, hoping that someone will come forward with information. Shortly after the program airs, strange things start happening to the Archers. A car keeps driving slowly past the house; there are disturbing telephone calls. One day, Cynthia and Terry return home and find an old fedora on the kitchen table. Cynthia is certain it belongs to her father. She hires a private detective who can't find her father's name in any public record. Who was he? Barclay takes his time solving the puzzle, deftly building suspense.

On the run from an abusive husband, Kate Russell settles in charming Carystown, Ky., and soon finds herself a suspect in the disappearance of a little girl. Laura Benedict's first novel, "Isabella Moon," mixes the paranormal, crime, and romance in a plot that exposes the sordid side of a small town. Nine-year-old Isabella Moon has been missing for two years when her ghost appears to Kate and leads her to her secret grave. Sheriff Bill Delaney is suspicious when Kate tells him about the apparition. Maybe she had something to do with the girl's disappearance. Or maybe she's just crazy. Before he can investigate, Kate tells her elderly friend, Lillian, about Isabella's ghost and shows her the child's grave. Not long after that, Lillian is murdered. When the sheriff at last gets around to digging in the spot Kate indicated, he finds Isabella's body.

Readers may guess pretty quickly which of Carystown's respectable citizens is a murderer, although they may not figure out the killer's motivation until later. Benedict takes her time spinning out the story. She's a good writer, almost good enough to get away with a plot that relies heavily on supernatural revelations. The story is told from the points of view of several characters, interspersed with flashback-like passages that reveal some nasty details of Kate's former life. Elements are predictable. Some of the characters are stereotypes - the hippie drug dealer, the spoiled playboy, the maniacal ex-husband. The plot sometimes seems labored. But the ending holds more than one surprise.

The word "heartwarming" turns up a lot in descriptions of Lorna Landvik's novels, and it fits. She has an easy, engaging, narrative style laced with humor. Her characters are, for the most part, ordinary people, maybe a little eccentric, but likable. It's a formula that has won Landvik a loyal following. She has written a half-dozen popular novels, among them "Angry Housewives Eating Bon Bons" and "Patty Jane's House of Curl." Her latest, "The View From Mount Joy," is set, like most of the others, in Minnesota, but for the first time her central character is a man. Landvik portrays Joe Andreson's life from high school athlete in the Vietnam era to the present, when he's evolved into a happy, middle-aged husband and father, and successful businessman.

Joe isn't all that fascinating, so it's fortunate that Landvik's story is as much about the women in his life as it is about him - his widowed mother, his lesbian aunt, his intellectual friend Darva, wife Jenny, daughter Flora. The most interesting, if not the most likable, woman in Joe's life is Kristi Casey, who introduced him to sex in the high school audiovisual room and has been popping into his life for years. Bitchy and bewitching by turns, Kristi was the head cheerleader, the girl all the boys wanted and all the girls hated. After college, Joe drifted into a job as a supermarket manager and eventually prospered. Kristi wandered off, surfacing occasionally to tell Joe about her boyfriend, the abusive Danish pornographer; her career as a drug-runner; and how she found God beside a BMX track in Barstow, Calif. Kristi has become a successful televangelist, host of "On TV With God," and married a right-wing politician. As the book closes, she's running for vice president. Kristi seems believable at first, but she evolves into a cartoon, as if Landvik were trying to stuff all the sins and excesses of the last 30 years into one character to serve as a counterpoint to Joe's decency. She may not be heartwarming, but the novel comes alive whenever she appears.

Diane White writes every month about new light and popular fiction.
