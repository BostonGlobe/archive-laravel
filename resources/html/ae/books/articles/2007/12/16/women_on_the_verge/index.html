On Audio

Women on the verge

Email|Print| Text size – + By Rochelle O'Gorman
December 16, 2007

A Thousand Splendid Suns
By Khaled Hosseini
Simon & Schuster Audio, unabridged fiction, 11 CDs, 12 hours, $39.95, read by Atossa Leoni; also available abridged on five CDs, six hours, $29.95, or as a download from www.audible.com for $27.97 unabridged and $20.97 abridged

Escape
By Carolyn Jessop with Laura Palmer
Random House Audio, abridged nonfiction, four CDs, six hours, $27.95, read by Alison Fraser; also available as an unabridged download, 15 hours and 52 minutes, from www.audible.com for $31.50

One Thousand White Women: The Journals of May Dodd
By Jim Fergus
Audio Partners, unabridged fiction, 12 CDs, 15 hours, $44.95, read by Laura Hicks; also available on 10 cassettes, $39.95, or as a download from www.audible.com for $31.46

Taking Flight
By Adriana Sevan
L.A. Theatre Works, original material, two CDs, one hour and 55 minutes, $25.95, performed by the author

My mother told me I should write about women's issues, and because I always (ahem) do as my mother wishes, we'll start with the audiobook that caused her to pick up the phone in the first place: "A Thousand Splendid Suns," by Khaled Hosseini.

Hosseini is an Afghan-born physician whose first novel, "The Kite Runner," was released to critical acclaim. As in that novel, political turmoil is at the heart of the story, but this time Hosseini depicts daily life in Afghanistan through the eyes of two women, Mariam and Laila. They hail from different backgrounds and are a generation apart, but their lives intersect when both unwillingly marry the same man (polygamy is common in their world).

Though they find joy where they can, their days are ruled by religion and an unforgiving regime that is especially punitive to women, though the plot eventually travels from horrific to hopeful. Narrator Atossa Leoni has a lovely voice, perfect diction, and an uncanny ear for accents. However, we do not always hear the (fleeting) pleasure or profound suffering of these powerless women. While overblown drama is never welcome, a little more emotion could have further enhanced this already haunting audiobook.

While on the subject of polygamy, Carolyn Jessop, the author of "Escape," was a member of the Fundamentalist Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints, a radical offshoot of the Mormon Church found along the Arizona-Utah border. One of an ever-increasing clutch of wives and the mother of eight children, she escaped in 2003 with her kids and $20 in her pocket.

Jessop's flight - she is believed to be the first woman in the country to flee from the FLDS and get her children out too - was accomplished despite nearly impossible odds; women in the FLDS were at the mercy of their fathers and husbands.

"Escape" is well worth hearing, even though the text is repetitive. Narrator Alison Fraser carries us from Jessop's naiveté through her angry awakening. Though she is sometimes a bit flat, her pacing and energy are on target. More of Jessop's plight is told in an author interview at the end of the production.

And another entry dealing with polygamy: "One Thousand White Women: The Journals of May Dodd," by Jim Fergus, is a fictional account of an 1854 treaty between Ulysses S. Grant and Little Wolf, chief of the Cheyenne Nation, to barter 1,000 horses for 1,000 white women with the notion of assimilating Native Americans into white society. And so May Dodd volunteers her way out of a mental institution to become the third, and most outspoken, wife of Lone Wolf.

For much of the production narrator Laura Hicks does a fine job. Her performance is energetic, she manages regional accents with finesse, and to the untrained ear she effectively speaks Cheyenne. But her foreign accents sound cartoonish, especially her overbearing Irish brogue and a Swiss accent that sounds strangely Nordic.

Marriage and children are so often the basis for books about and by women, but let us not forget the fluidity of friendship.

In "Taking Flight," an L.A. Theatre Works production of author Adriana Sevan's one-woman play, a solid and long-standing friendship between two women is severely tested by tragedy. Starting with a real event, Sevan uses bawdy humor and potent pathos to depict the long-term effects of 9/11 on a woman who was so severely injured by the attacks she nearly died. Sevan, interestingly, is the friend whose life is twisted out of shape when her every focus becomes her wounded best friend. This unusual viewpoint is surprisingly insightful and extremely entertaining, while Sevan's performance is polished and vibrant. A lengthy interview with her offers even more understanding of how her life changed after this experience. One thing we can be grateful for is that it made her into a playwright.

Rochelle O'Gorman is publisher and editor in chief of audiobookcafe.com.
