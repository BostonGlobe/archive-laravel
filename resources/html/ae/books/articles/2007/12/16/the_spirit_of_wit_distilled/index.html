A reading life

The spirit of wit, distilled

Email|Print| Text size – + By Katherine A. Powers
December 16, 2007

I am constantly startled at how much I have in common with Winston Churchill. Like him, I was born in November of an American mother; I often work in bed; and I am fond of pigs and books of quotations. It's uncanny. As it happens, Churchill himself has 25 entries (where, to be honest, I have none) in one of the most entertaining books on my shelf: the "Oxford Dictionary of Humorous Quotations," edited by the late Ned Sherrin. Happily, the third edition has recently been issued in paperback (Oxford University, $19.95) just in time to ease the rigors of the gift-giving season.

The book contains over 5,000 humorous aperçus, maxims, aphorisms, epigrams, snippets of repartee, blunders, and oddities arranged by topic with an index of contributors and key words. The contents include the words of lesser people as well as the great comic geniuses, among them my heroes G. K. Chesterton, Mark Twain, H. L. Mencken, W. S. Gilbert, S. J. Perelman, P. G. Wodehouse, Saki, Mae West, Evelyn Waugh, Gore Vidal, Tom Stoppard, Alan Bennett, and the two heaviest-hitting contributors of all, Oscar Wilde and that old wag, Anonymous. Not all the entries were hammered out on a hot funny bone; some are inadvertent gems. Politicians are particularly gifted in this area: Donald Rumsfeld's parsing of knowns and unknowns is here, as is onetime Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney's "I am not denying anything I did not say." Margaret Thatcher is, as in so many other areas, a mistress of the form ("We have become a grandmother"), although she has nothing on our president, who has had whole books devoted to his utterances. Strangely, however, he has only three entries in these pages, another instance of his being, in his own parlance, misunderestimated.

Indeed, if the book has a fault - aside from some things striking me as not funny at all - it is the preponderance of British entries over American ones, especially in the selections that have been drawn from the press, radio, and TV. This is the result of the editor's having been British. Though I, personally, don't regret the absence of, say, Maureen Dowd or the American convert Christopher Hitchens, and could have done with far less P. J. O'Rourke, it is not right that only one entry apiece comes from Casey Stengel, Dave Barry, and Molly Ivins, and not a single one from Ian Frazier, David Owen, James Wolcott, Stephen Colbert, Erma Bombeck ("If a man watches three football games in a row, he should be declared legally dead") or the late, great George Frazier, columnist for the paper you hold in your hands ("Wouldn't it be simpler if New York just legalized crime?"). But even though this is not the exact volume I would have put together, it provides an exceedingly pleasant way to spend time ("People say that life is the thing, but I prefer reading" - Logan Pearsall Smith).

Every person should have at least one book of aphorisms on the shelf. The two I have are, alas, either out of print or "out of stock." But both ("Viking Book of Aphorisms," also published as "The Faber Book of Aphorisms," compiled by W. H. Auden and Louis Kronenberger, and "The Oxford Book of Aphorisms," compiled by John Gross) are widely available secondhand, so get on it. Both books make a stab at defining what an aphorism actually is, and neither definition is short - or even a definition. For its part, "The American Heritage Dictionary" tells us that an aphorism is "a tersely phrased statement of truth or opinion; an adage," but that is not quite enough to get us into the game. It's easier - for me, at least - to say what an aphorism isn't. It isn't just a gobbet of wisdom, nor is it merely a stylistic coup. Some formulations that I find in books of aphorisms strike me as being too baggy to meet my own indefinable criteria; others, though excellent fun and often penetrating, are more acrobatic and mischievous than truly aphoristic.

What you really want, or I do, is pithiness and heft with clean lines and a flick in the tail. An aphorism, to my taste - and it does come down to taste in the end - should have the zing of an epigram and the profundity of a philosophical statement. For instance, "War has no fury like a noncombatant" (C. E. Montague), though an excellent epigram, lacks, to my mind, quite enough heft to make it an aphorism. On the other hand, "A man who says no patriot should attack the war until it's over . . . is saying no good son should warn his mother of a cliff until she has fallen" (G. K. Chesterton) is substantial enough, but has the manners of a proverb, being didactic and without inner torque. Then again, "Wars begin when you will, but they do not end when you please" (Machiavelli) is the genuine article - though it is also an epigram.

James Geary has thought about the whole subject far more systematically than I have or ever will. In "Geary's Guide to the World's Great Aphorists" (Bloomsbury, $19.95), he has given himself over to the passion for categorization that so often arises when a subject eludes simple definition. In his earlier book "The World in a Phrase," he came up with "Five Laws of the Aphorism," and now he has identified eight types of aphorisms (the Chiasmus, the Definition, the Joke, the Metaphor, the Moral, the Observation, the Paradox, and the Pensée). He has also divided around 150 of the world's great aphorists - including Churchill ("When you have to kill a man it costs nothing to be polite") - into nine sections, consisting of eight different "species" and one grab bag of "Strange Beasts." Each author has been given a brief biography followed by a sampling of his or her wares.

Although this organization would not do at all for a general compilation, which is best served by arranging aphorisms by topic, it is very useful for the reader who knows her favorite aphorists. I flipped quickly to Karl Kraus and so present you with this: "An aphorism does not need to be true, but it should outwing the truth. It must get beyond it with a spin." And as if to prove that, here is doughty old Churchill spinning away: "In time of war, when truth is so precious, it must be attended by a bodyguard of lies." Suffice it to say - as La Rochefoucauld once did - "We try to make virtues out of the faults we have no wish to correct."

Katherine A. Powers lives in Cambridge. Her column appears on alternate Sundays. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@verizon.net.
