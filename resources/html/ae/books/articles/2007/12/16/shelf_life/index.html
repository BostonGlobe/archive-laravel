Shelf Life

From the new collection of Richard Avedon photographs, Caroline Kennedy and 6-week-old John Jr., January 1961. ("THE KENNEDYS")
Email|Print| Text size – + By Jan Gardner
December 16, 2007

'Domestic' drama

Sometimes a novel imitates life too closely for a publisher's taste. That apparently is the case with Simon & Schuster and "Domestic Partners," the as-yet-unpublished work by Anne Bernays.

Simon & Schuster has declined to comment, but Bernays said company editors and lawyers told her the novel could not be published because it bore too close a resemblance to the drama-filled final years of her father's life and they feared a lawsuit. "It was devastating," said Bernays, author of nine novels and a Cambridge resident, of the news.

Bernays's father, Edward Bernays, was a defining figure in public relations. He died in 1995, at the age of 103, after a legal struggle with Bernays and her sister over his finances and mental health. A key figure in that drama was a woman he hired to help him out at home who said her role evolved into an intimate relationship.

While "Domestic Partners" does mirror her father's later years, Bernays said, the live-in companion in the novel differed in many respects from the woman in her father's life.

"Domestic Partners" was due to be published next month. Bernays will read from it at an event sponsored by PEN New England on Jan. 2 at the Hotel Marlowe in Cambridge.

Sweet Caroline

Richard Avedon's photographs of President-elect John F. Kennedy's family launched a new era of image-making. Now more than 75 unpublished images from that 1961 session have been collected in "The Kennedys: Portrait of a Family" (Collins Design), by Shannon Thomas Perich, a curator at the Smithsonian.

Historian Robert Dallek writes in the book's foreword that the images "humanized Kennedy and his family and encouraged the view that here was a man and a woman who were America at its best and were born to govern." According to Avedon, Jackie Kennedy was a "great tour de force" while her husband, during the photo session, was "ill at ease" in "a very charming way."

Yet it is 3-year-old Caroline who outshines the rest of the family with her energy and her emotion, especially when she holds her baby brother and kisses him on the forehead.

Belief systems

Nonfiction titles questioning the existence of God are among this year's notable books. Now fiction writer Ron Currie Jr. of Waterville, Maine, enters that territory with "God Is Dead" (Viking), a dark and comic collection of interrelated stories, in which God inhabiting the body of a Dinka woman in the Sudan is killed, and parents turn to worshiping their children while dogs walk on water.

Coming out

"Jack Kerouac's American Journey: The Real Life Odyssey of 'On the Road,' " by Paul Maher Jr. (Thunder's Mouth)

"Hunger: A Modern History," by James Vernon (Harvard University/Belknap)

"Condoleezza Rice: An American Life," by Elisabeth Bumiller (Random House)

Pick of the week

Jill Cadogan of Willow Books & Cafe, in Acton, recommends "The Tenderness of Wolves," by Stef Penney (Simon & Schuster): "My favorite novel of the year. This epic story of suspense and adventure is set in the wintry landscape of northern Canada in 1867. . . . The atmosphere and time period are so well defined that it was impossible to put down."
