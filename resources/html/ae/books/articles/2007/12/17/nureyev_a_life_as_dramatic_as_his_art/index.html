Book Review

'Nureyev': A life as dramatic as his art

Rudolf Nureyev (above with Lucette Aldous in "Don Quixote") reinvented the role of the male ballet dancer.
Email|Print| Text size – + By Karen Campbell
December 17, 2007

Nureyev: The Life
By Julie Kavanagh
Pantheon, 786 pp., $37.50

No other ballet dancer of the past century has captured the public's adoration and imagination as feverishly as the great Rudolf Nureyev. An undisputed cultural icon, he reinvented the role of the male ballet dancer and helped transform ballet from a strictly elitist art form into one that could be celebrated by the masses on "The Ed Sullivan Show."

Julie Kavanagh's "Nureyev: The Life" offers a critically authoritative biography of the legendary dancer that should appeal to scholars and casual fans alike, combining exhaustive research with delightfully juicy, gossip-filled anecdotes to paint a remarkably full-blooded portrait. Kavanagh's research draws not only on interviews with Nureyev's inner circle, but on previously undisclosed letters, diaries, and home-movie footage (not to mention her own dance background - a former ballerina, she is also the author of the prize-winning "Secret Muses: The Life of Frederick Ashton").

However, "Nureyev" is not propelled by exhaustive scholarship as much as terrific storytelling. Kavanagh seeds the book with vivid details and, one suspects, a good deal of interpolation. The book's opening scenes of Nureyev's birth, "shaken out of the womb," read like compelling historical fiction. She sets the scene on a Russian military train in 1938, as Nureyev's pregnant mother, Farida, and his three sisters travel to join Nureyev's father, Hamet, stationed with the Red Army's Far Eastern Division. "His birth, he would later say, was the most romantic event of his life, symbolic of his future statelessness and nomadic existence. It was to be a life lived mostly en route to places, navigated by what he called 'his vagabond soul.' "

Throughout the book, Kavanagh offers psychological insights into both the man and the artist, with special attention to Nureyev's lesser-known early years. Nureyev's peasant parents, both raised Muslim, traded their religious beliefs for faith in the Communist regime and the once-undreamed of possibility of education. Hamet threw himself into the movement, becoming one of the senior "thought police" relied on to instill the new ideology, thereby sparing his family the multitude of horrors enacted during Stalin's purges. But in 1941, when Hitler invaded Russia, Nureyev's family was forced to leave Moscow and endure years of dislocation, deprivation, and a constant hunger - Nureyev called it "the potato period . . . six people and a dog, all in one room." However, Hamet was rarely home, leaving Nureyev the small man of the house in the company of his doting mother and sisters.

Despite their difficult lot, Farida always aspired to greater things for her children, and when Nureyev was only 7, she splurged for one ballet ticket, hoping to smuggle in the whole family. "Rudolf was mesmerized; the wonder of the theater's crystal chandeliers, stuccoed interior, classical murals, and velvet curtains patterned with colored dancing lights transported him at once from the gray world he knew." He felt possessed. "I knew. That's it, that's my life, that will be my function. I wanted to be everything onstage."

Much to his father's dismay, ballet soon became the center of Nureyev's existence. He pursued it with unwavering commitment and passion. He was often picked on at school, where classmates found him "somehow different . . . like a white crow." But in the ballet studio, he found a home for the wild, spontaneous spirit that was to make him one of the most captivating, if sometimes unconventional, performers in ballet history. Finding no role models in the male Russian dancers of the day, he slowly developed his own distinctive style - an unconfined quality, with an elongated line, soft expressive arms and a high releve, which he assimilated from his female partners.

Kavanagh traces Nureyev's life from his humble beginnings at the famed Vaganova School through his meteoric rise to superstardom and dramatic, rather spontaneous defection to the West in 1961, the first Russian artist to defect. (He subsequently lived with near-constant guilt and the fear of Soviet reprisal, with rumors he was being considered for "elimination" or maiming.) The book chronicles Nureyev's myriad artistic collaborations, most famously his brilliant partnership with Margot Fonteyn. However, Nureyev also worked with nearly every major ballet company and seminal choreographer of the 20th century. For the casual reader, the exhaustive detail can be wearying, but cognoscenti are treated to stories of Nureyev's relationships with some of the most important figures in the world of dance

Anecdotes from friends, critics, colleagues, and lovers paint a portrait of a vain, selfish, arrogant artist driven to perfection. But Nureyev could also be charming, witty, and surprisingly generous. Complicated and mercurial in temperament, he was a loner and nonconformist, yet often vulnerable and craving attention. Kavanagh doesn't sugarcoat Nureyev's years of failing technique and health as AIDS began to ravage his body. (He finally succumbed in 1993.) An unashamed hedonist, he was attracted to and had tremendous appeal for both men and women. But after decades of mostly homosexual relationships, he was diagnosed with HIV in 1984.

Nureyev struggled to dance, despite diminished physical capabilities, until 1991, when no one would hire him anymore. He then turned his remaining energy to choreographing, directing, even conducting, drawing on his lifelong love of music. But it was always dancing that fueled his inexhaustible spirit, finding in each performance a kind of resurrection. "When the lights are extinguished, I die," he admitted. "But tomorrow I will dance; tomorrow I will be reborn."

Karen Campbell is a freelance writer based in Brookline.
