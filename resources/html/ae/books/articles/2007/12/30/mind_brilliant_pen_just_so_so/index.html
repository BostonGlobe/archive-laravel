Mind: brilliant. Pen: just so-so.

Tale of cracking genetic code often disappoints

J. Craig Venter calls his memoir "a tale of . . . quests and grand objectives." ("A LIFE DECODED")
Email|Print| Text size – + By Mary Ambrose
December 30, 2007

A Life Decoded: My Genome - My Life
By J. Craig Venter
Viking, 390 pp., illustrated, $25.95

J. Craig Venter is world famous as one of the few scientists to crack the code of the human genome. He's the first scientist to map his own genome, and he's made millions doing it. He's also a world-class sailor. How he arrived at these heights is a classic American story, but few tell their own story well, and Venter is among them.

Lousy at school and more interested in surfing the California waves than hitting the books, Venter changed when he was a medic in Vietnam. After seeing so much death he returned committed to life, and planned to become a doctor. Then he found his true love, research. He put himself through university, became a top scientist, and changed the world.

His story has everything. Politics: He and his wife chatted with the Clintons so cozily he knows that Hillary thinks the bed should breathe and Bill thinks it should be made immediately. Drama: Venter commits his company to cracking the genome map on a timetable that will allow it to be announced jointly beside Tony Blair and Bill Clinton. But the computers keep crashing. Will he make it in time? (Yes.) High-end gossip: James Watson, who won a Nobel with his partner, Francis Crick, for discovering DNA, is portrayed as a petty, jealous pedant. Emotional turmoil: How does Venter respond to the Faustian deal of possibly making millions from the questionable business of patenting genes? (Goes back and forth.) Cutting-edge ecology: His latest project is trolling the gene pools of the ocean. This both monitors the ocean's health as well as hugely expands our knowledge of life on our planet. And of course, science for the lay reader: This book chronicles discovering the genome, surely the most exciting science news of the last decades.

But where's a writer when you need one? Are a handful of beautifully written memoirs to blame for people thinking anyone can write one? For a man who prides himself on his professionalism it seems audacious, to say the least, to assume that he can write well enough to make his life's work a good read. Barring the insightful first chapters, Venter has reduced his personal life to taking potshots at people he doesn't like, and his explanations of the science are consistently less than illuminating for a lay reader. Compelling information is often buried under extraneous facts about a massive cast of characters, or bureaucratic operations. His surname often seems apt. I sensed an ignored editor lurking in the background.

As for the race to discover the human genome, Venter's tale feels like a person detailing his divorce, too personal and only true in ways. His version is suspect because the teller admits he's an externally oriented man, committed to facts and action. Reflection takes a back seat, as when Venter asserts that his staff always adores him; his wives don't understand his dedication to science; and most of his bosses are idiots. Please.

Then there's the writing. Venter's scientific abilities must be more elegant than his prose. He describes a newspaper piece by Nicholas Wade like this: "A red flag to the public genome bull, it still managed - with the benefit of years of hindsight - to hit the right note."

Clunky though that is, you can at least glean most of his meaning. When he's writing about science, it's truly heavy sledding. Having read "A Life Decoded," I still have only the foggiest notion of what mapping a genome actually entails. Venter finds it difficult to find an analogy or explanation that makes the science clear without reducing the idea completely.

He often describes the process of sequencing genes, for example, as putting together a complex jigsaw puzzle, which suggests it could be mapped by a few sharp, patient, and very careful seniors during quiet afternoons at the community center, and certainly doesn't require billions of research dollars.

Recounting even something as universal as computer glitches becomes incomprehensible. Outlining the troubles his staff faced in sequencing genes with ineffective computers, he admits they "could anticipate some errors, such as when a 384-well plate was read the wrong way, and then use software to spot the telltale error pattern and correct for it," whatever that means. He unhelpfully explained the problems to his unscientific boss, citing "the incredible failure rates, quantified as the mean time between failures and the repair times." Perhaps it's no surprise that he didn't get along with that boss very well.

Although his book is often tedious in its obfuscation, it is not without merit. In the text Venter includes breakout boxes where he details what he's learned about himself from his genes. The cool factor here outweighs the sometimes difficult science. For example, he suspects that his formidable endurance for ocean swimming is because of his genes rather than his muscles. He lacks a very common mutation in the AMPD1 gene, which "causes a deficiency in the enzyme and, as a consequence, can lead to aches, cramps, and early fatigue."

And he looks at the link between his genes and his asthma, and touchingly even his own bout with melanoma and basal-cell carcinoma. Although the tumors were caught and treated early, Venter regrets not having "enough tissue to analyze the genetic changes that caused [them], and it would have been fascinating to see how my genome had spun out of control within those cells." He's such a consummate scientist he's a tough guy to dislike, but he's also a tough guy to read.

Mary Ambrose is managing editor of New America Media in San Francisco.
