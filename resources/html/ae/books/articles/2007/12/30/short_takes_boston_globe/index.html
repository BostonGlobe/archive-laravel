Short takes

Email|Print| Text size – +
December 30, 2007

Never Enough
By Joe McGinniss
Simon & Schuster, 358 pp., illustrated, $25

The merest whiff of the saga of Rob and Nancy Kissel must have set an old newshound like Joe McGinniss to salivating.

Rob Kissel was a hard-nosed investment banker with a talent for making obscene amounts of money. After five years in Hong Kong, a plum assignment for Rob, his wife, Nancy, a material girl of few inner resources, was growing dangerously discontented inside the gilded cage of expatriate life. The couple bickered constantly. Nancy loved the money Rob was bringing home; she just didn't love Rob. When a detective confirmed that while on vacation at their ski McMansion in Vermont, Nancy was playing Lady Chatterley with the home-theater-installation guy, Rob moved to toss her off the gravy train. Nancy saw only one solution to her dilemma, and it wasn't spelled d-i-v-o-r-c-e.

McGinniss's sardonic reconstruction of the lurid details of Rob's demise and Nancy's trial for murder leaves little doubt about the essential facts of the case. He does, however, open the window just wide enough to admit a glimmer of pity for a woman, an entire family, with more arrogance than sense and dollar signs where their hearts should be.

The Catastrophist
By Ronan Bennett
Bloomsbury, 336 pp., paperback, $14.95

The present of this compelling political novel is 1960, the scene the teeming streets and serene colonial enclaves of Leopoldville in the Belgian Congo, a country tense to the breaking point on the eve of independence. Arriving as other Europeans are making their exit is James Gillespie, an Irishman sick of political conflict, come to seek a reconciliation with the woman he loves, a crusading Italian journalist named Inès. More a committed partisan than a reporter, Inès has attached herself to the faction of Patrice Lumumba, the tragic idealist. She has little patience for James's privatism, his emotional needs and erotic demands.

Inès and James, it becomes clear, speak different languages and see through different eyes. For James, the turmoil in the Congo - the power struggles, the irreconcilable agendas - serve as a backdrop to and a commentary on their embattled relationship, while for Inès, the romance of revolution is the highest love, the only thing worthy of her sacrifice and submission.

Reissued after first appearing in 1999, "The Catastrophist" remains timely, for just as there must always be lovers doomed to disappointment, there will always be battered lands where freedom is the dream and civil war the brutal reality.

A Force of Nature: The Frontier Genius of Ernest Rutherford
By Richard Reeves
Norton, 207 pp., illustrated, $23.95

While not a household name like his contemporary Albert Einstein, Ernest Rutherford was the father of nuclear physics, a science that within decades of his discovering the particle structure of the atom would produce both life-saving and life-destroying innovations.

Unlike the theorist Einstein, Rutherford was a "tabletop" researcher who brought into the laboratory the mechanical skills he had acquired growing up on a hardscrabble farm in New Zealand. A pioneer in a more primitive yet more romantic, even heroic, scientific era, Rutherford, says Richard Reeves, would casually "toss bits of radioactive material in his pocket" and carry them around. Miraculously he survived this suicidal behavior - and considerable English snobbery - to become head of the prestigious Cavendish Laboratory at Cambridge University, to win the 1908 Nobel Prize for chemistry (physics would not be recognized for another 30 years), and to be created Baron Rutherford in 1931.

Reeves, best known for writing about politics, is an engineer by training, and he begins with flair by reenacting on replica apparatus Rutherford's seminal "scattering" experiment. The book, part of the series "Great Discoveries," has some of the hand-me-down feel of the typical series entry, though Reeves makes the science accessible, and his portrait of Rutherford the eccentric country cousin is rather charming.

Amanda Heller is a critic and editor who lives in Newton.
