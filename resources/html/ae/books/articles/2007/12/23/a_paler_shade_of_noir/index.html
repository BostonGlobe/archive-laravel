Historical Novels

A paler shade of noir

Email|Print| Text size – + By Anna Mundow
December 23, 2007

A Pale Horse
By Charles Todd
Morrow, 360 pp., $23.95

Signed, Mata Hari By Yannick Murphy
Little, Brown, 288 pp., $23.99

Zugzwang
By Ronan Bennett
Bloomsbury, 288 pp., $24.95

We have entered the monochrome time of year, particularly here in the woods, where all color seems to have retreated and the world now presents itself in tasteful black and white. After fall's foliage pyrotechnics it's time to give the senses a rest and to soothe yourself with some monochrome novels. A quieter cousin of noir fiction, the monochrome is more at home in dun-colored fields than on city streets and populated with characters who are more sensitive than hard-boiled. "A Pale Horse," by Charles Todd, is a good example.

The 10th novel in the satisfying Inspector Ian Rutledge mystery series takes its name from the ancient figure etched into the chalk downs of Berkshire, and even in the spring of 1920, that ghostly creation seems to be the brightest thing in an otherwise gloomy England. Rutledge, shell-shocked from the First World War and haunted by the voice of Hamish, a Scottish soldier executed under his command, reluctantly takes on another homicide case. A corpse wearing a gas mask has been found in a ruined abbey, and Rutledge, dispatched from London to investigate, is soon drawn into the circumscribed lives and the murky pasts of the victim's acquaintances. He is also drawn back to a particular horror of the war: poison gas, which plays a lethal part in the cunning plot. One of the best things about Todd's writing is its laconic compassion. When a young man complains that the army wouldn't take him, Rutledge mildly replies, "You were lucky. It was not a war you'd have liked." Yet we never feel simply sorry for Rutledge, or for any other character. The atmosphere of these novels is, to Todd's credit, too cloudy for such clear judgment.

Yannick Murphy, by contrast, seems to want us wholeheartedly to pity and to love Mata Hari, the Dutch exotic dancer who was accused of spying for Germany, was executed in 1917, and is now worshipfully resurrected in Murphy's novel "Signed, Mata Hari." Shuttling between the French prison where the dancer is incarcerated, the Holland of her youth, and the Indonesia of her married life, the novel reveals the central tragedy of Margaretha Zelle's life to have been the loss of her two children, removed from her in appallingly different ways by human vindictiveness.

In a series of intense episodes that have the immediacy of a snapshot, Murphy eloquently describes her heroine's watery childhood, perilous youth, desperate marriage, and brief celebrity in wartime Europe. The earlier journey from Holland to Indonesia is one from darkness into light - and into color, spices, silk, and sex. Murphy's steamier passages fairly throb on the page (more dully, however, with each throb), and the contrast with the sepulchral darkness of the Saint Lazare prison is suitably bleak and disorienting. "The rats in the hallway on the way back to her cell were surprised. . . . They squeaked and crisscrossed in front of her, not sure which way to run with so little time to think about the danger," one chapter ends, while the next one begins: "By the light of the full moon I went to watch the turtles crawling up from the sea."

Sadly the character of Mata/Margaretha lacks such delicate shading. Most martyrs do. She is, above all, a suffering mother and a victim of war who bravely advises at the end, "If you are about to be executed, don't accept the blindfold they give you. . . . Shake your head no to the ropes . . . that they would use to tie your body to the stake made from the young tree limb thrust into the ground."

The hero of Ronan Bennett's latest novel, "Zugzwang," strikes no such poses. In March 1914, in St. Petersburg, Dr. Otto Spethmann is a renowned psychoanalyst whose own past remains an open wound. Spethmann's wife has recently died, and his 18-year-old daughter blames him for this and for every other misfortune. His patients are complicated, psychologically and politically. One of them is the exiled Lenin's deputy in Russia, another is a Polish chess master on the verge of a breakdown, and his newest is a socialite whose father, an industrialist, has ties to the secret police.

"I lived in a city built on a marsh stiffened with the bones of a hundred thousand serfs who died of starvation, disease and cruelty in its construction," Spethmann explains. "In every part of the empire we lived with the Cossack, the spy and the secret policeman for our neighbours." Those categories become terrifying realities in the doctor's life when he is visited by thuggish police officers who threaten not only him but also his daughter. A liberal newspaper editor has been murdered. A second killing has occurred, and the police are convinced that Spethmann is involved.

The already somber mood darkens incrementally, as Bennett at once heightens the suspense and restricts our vision until we feel as helpless as the narrator. "The snow-swollen water slurred like a thick tongue," he writes of the Neva. "On the bare branches above us spectral white arms stretched up into the night." And that is a love scene. The deftly unveiled conspiracy involves Bolsheviks, chess, assassination, and revolution, and is, judging by Bennett's acknowledgments, historically possible if not entirely plausible.

Anna Mundow, a freelance journalist living in Central Massachusetts, is a correspondent for the Irish Times. She can be reached via e-mail at ama1668@hotmail.com.
