BOOK REVIEW

'City of God' is a raw depiction of hell on earth

Paulo Lins writes about a Brazilian slum where family ties can be cut like string. (Marko Lipus)

By Lylah M. Alphonse, Globe Staff  |  January 4, 2007

City of God, By Paulo Lins, Translated from Portuguese, by Alison Entrekin, Grove/Atlantic/Black Cat, 448 pp., paperback, $15

Raw, brutal, and graphically violent, "City of God," by Paulo Lins, is a multifaceted story about hellish life and early death in a Brazilian slum, where family ties can be severed as easily as a kite string.

Based in part on Lins's childhood in the Rio de Janeiro favela known as Cidade de Deus, or City of God, the book was originally published in Brazil in 1997 and made into a film in 2002. While the movie focused on the relationship between two of the characters, the novel is a fast-moving and intense series of snapshots about a complex network of young thugs during the 1960s, '70s, and '80s. Most of them are known only by their nicknames -- Hellraiser, Green Eyes, Sparrow -- and all of them struggle with issues of class, race, and power.

While there's rarely enough money for food or medicine, the gangsters always have enough for cocaine or weed. The thugs rely on the two drugs -- they snort coke to get wired and then smoke pot to relax enough to eat or rest, and they sell both to finance their lifestyle. Guns are "for mugging, killing and being respected" and are used at any opportunity.

Nothing is more dangerous than a person with nothing to lose, and most of the boys who grow up in the City of God are that way to begin with. "Look man, I smoke, I snort coke, I bin beggin' since I was a baby, I've washed car windows, shined shoes, killed, stolen . . . I'm not a kid. I'm a man!" insists 8-year-old Steak-and-Fries. Desperate for a measure of fame and respect, these kids display a heartbreaking mixture of ignorance and innocence. "When Tiny found out his name was in all the newspapers, he was so excited that he got Russian Mouse -- the only member of the gang who could read and write -- to read him the newspapers every morning. Russian Mouse said they only had to read the police pages, but Tiny insisted that he read every section of every newspaper in the city, including the classifieds, hoping to find his name." Because the gangsters' lives are so disposable, their victims' are, too. Anyone can be ruthlessly killed for the sake of wounded pride, passion, or revenge: an elderly man, a cheating wife, a 9-year-old go-between for a rival gang, even a baby whose light skin makes a man doubt the child is his. "He'd waited for the right moment to kill his ex-girlfriend," we're told as Butucatu and Potbelly rape and torture a pregnant woman who had left Butucatu for another man. "He could have pointed a gun at her from afar, but he'd preferred to wait for the chance to kill her slowly, because traitors have to die like that." Police protection is laughable -- the cops are really just another gang, with another cold-blooded leader, the only difference between them and the thugs they chase is their badges.

A gorgeous young man, nicknamed Knockout because of his stunning good looks, is reborn as the Avenger after being forced to witness the violent rape of his fiancee by Tiny, the sadistic head of a local gang. Humiliated, Knockout seeks revenge, and while he clings to his God-fearing, play-by-the-rules way at first, he quickly manages to justify his new gangster life. His vendetta against Tiny begins a war that last s for two years and renders the slum a bona fide combat zone.

Salvation comes too late for those who live in the City of God.
