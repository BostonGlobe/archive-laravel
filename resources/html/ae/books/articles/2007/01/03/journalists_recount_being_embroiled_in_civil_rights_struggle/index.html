BOOK REVIEW

Journalists recount being embroiled in civil rights struggle

By Sarah Schweitzer, Globe Staff  |  January 3, 2007

In the fall of 1962, journalists streamed into Oxford, Miss., to witness James Meredith's attempt to become the first black student to enroll at the University of Mississippi. Segregationists had vowed to stop him, and as expected, a dramatic confrontation and violence ensued. But instead of observing from the sidelines, journalists found themselves at the center of the melee.

Mobs descended on reporters, beating them and killing one. As Gene Roberts and Hank Klibanoff recount, when a group of reporters retreated to a classroom, they listened as rioters "carrying sticks and black rubber hoses . . . began breaking into the building and its laboratories, looking for soda bottles that could be fashioned into Molotov cocktails and chemicals that could be turned into weapons. The newsmen's nest was no longer safe."

In the growing body of civil rights scholarship, Roberts and Klibanoff offer a new twist in "The Race Beat: The Press, the Civil Rights Struggle, and the Awakening of a Nation," telling the story through the lens of the reporters and editors who documented the movement.

Narratives about journalists can be tricky. Journalists, by definition, are chroniclers, not protagonists; they follow the story that others create. Roberts and Klibanoff argue that in the case of the civil rights movement, the journalists were unique. Not only were they at the center of the story as frequent targets of vitriol and violence, they also were the movement's catalysts, since their reporting awakened the rest of the nation to the effects of segregation in the South.

For the first half of the 20th century, the authors write, as Jim Crow laws maintained racial division in the South, major media outlets ignored the story of segregation. No major publication anchored a news bureau in the South. Black newspapers and a handful of Southern papers with liberal editors were the sole outlets to recognize racism as a story early on. When a correspondent was installed by The New York Times in 1947 in Chattanooga, Tenn., he was a Southerner who insisted on writing about the region's progressives and failed to accurately portray segregation's defenders.

The civil rights movement, note Roberts and Klibanoff, took hold only after the national press in the 1950s and '60s "[force-fed] the rest of the nation a diet so loaded with stories about the cruelty of racism" that segregation could no longer be ignored by those within and outside the South.

Veteran journalists themselves (Roberts has served as executive editor of The Philadelphia Inquirer and managing editor of The New York Times; Klibanoff is managing editor for news at The Atlanta Journal-Constitution and a former Boston Globe reporter) , the authors provide gripping accounts of journalists' efforts to get the story in Arkansas and Mississippi and Alabama. Most come across as stoic and single-minded in the pursuit of truth, particularly progressive Southern editors.

But there is also critical analysis. The authors rap those who breached the journalism ethic of arm's-length detachment, such as the Times's Benjamin Fine, who was seen comforting one of the nine black students seeking entry to Little Rock 's Central High School in 1957 and telling her, "Don't let them see you cry."

The book is marbled with biographical sketches of editors and reporters, which, at times, create a confusing tapestry of names and facts. The interludes are particularly distracting in chapters with a natural dramatic arc, such as one about the 1955 trial of two white men for the murder of Emmett Till , a black teenager . The narrative would have been better served had the authors focused on a few representative journalists.

Meanwhile, the authors' role in the coverage is described opaquely. Roberts, who covered the movement for the Times, was present for some of the action described. But he is referred to in the third person, and the extent of his involvement is not detailed, a gap that makes for a detached, academic tone.

Yet, at a time when newspapers are in paroxysms of uncertainty about their role in the new journalism landscape of bloggers and declining circulation, "The Race Beat" is a compelling reminder of the need for a vibrant and free press, with the resources and resourcefulness to shine a light on the nation's wrongs.

The Race Beat: The Press, the Civil Rights Struggle, and the Awakening of a

Nation By Gene Roberts and Hank Klibanoff, Knopf, 518 pp., illustrated, $30
