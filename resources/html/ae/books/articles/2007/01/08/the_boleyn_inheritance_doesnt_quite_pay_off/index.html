BOOK REVIEW

'The Boleyn Inheritance' doesn't quite pay off

Philippa Gregory has written five Tudor court-based novels. (Sven Arnstein)

By Clea Simon, Globe Correspondent  |  January 8, 2007

The Boleyn Inheritance
By Philippa Gregory
Touchstone/Simon & Schuster 528 pp., $25.95

Philippa Gregory is running out of royalty. The historical - fiction star, who made Anne Boleyn's luckier sister famous in "The Other Boleyn Girl," has spun the Tudor court into romantic adventure in five meaty novels. In addition to Mary Boleyn (the king's mistress, who was discarded rather than beheaded), she's tackled the young Elizabeth ("The Virgin's Lover") and the rather dour Katherine of Aragon ("The Constant Princess"), along with various courtiers and hangers - on. This time out, she combines two of Henry VIII's later queens, Anne of Cleves and Katharine Howard , and a surviving Boleyn (Lady Jane Roch ford ) to mine a previously unexploited smidgen of history.

The conceit almost works: Covering 1539 to 1547, during which the king would take his three last wives, Gregory captures a court in free fall. Henry, the beautiful prince of her earlier books, has become a sick old man, literally stinking from an open leg wound, constipated, impotent, and prone to murderous rages. But while he's terrorizing his own country, wielding his now-absolute power indiscriminately, he's also the only game in town in terms of riches, advancement, and freedom.

That's the impetus for Anne of Cleves, an unhappy German duchess who longs to escape her cruel brother's minor court. Henry also piques the greed of Katherine, a vain teenager who wants to be a star. For Lady Jane Roch ford, whose testimony condemned her husband , George Boleyn , and sister-in-law, the first Queen Anne, he's the path back to respectability and wealth. While each of these women has some awareness of Henry's irrational brutality, all think they can play the odds.

Gregory usually has a deft way with characters. With other minor historical figures (notably Mary Boleyn), she showed how circumstances shape people, and how they grow and change. This skill has elevated the English writer to the top of the genre. But in "The Boleyn Inheritance" she stumbles. Dividing the book among the three main female characters, she fails to make each first-person point of view equally strong. While the narrative flows smoothly, from Anne first sitting for her Holbein portrait through Katherine's summons to be one of the new queen's waiting women to the inevitable tragedy, the personalities lag and falter.

Anne comes through as the most sympathetic, as well as the most mature. Gregory plays the young noblewoman -- who was underestimated by the court, perhaps because of her language difficulties -- as scared, but smart. Anne alone sees the king as he is, disastrously at first when he approaches her in costume and she reacts in disgust. But this clear sight, which may have condemned her marriage, saves her life. Although her spoken English trails her comprehension, she understands the panicked fickleness of court: "Good God, it is a poisonous place. " She realizes at last that she is lucky to escape disgraced, rejected, divorced -- but alive.

Neither Katherine nor Jane is given such range. Katherine, in particular, is written as a dolt who counts her possessions like a 16th-century Bridget Jones ("I have three gowns . . . a piece of French lace. . ."). Jane, meanwhile, is blinded by massive denial. She loved her husband. Everything was her sister-in-law Anne's fault. Neither of these royal nitwits seems capable of learning, and their fate takes both unaware.

Gregory mentions Henry's final wife , Katherine Parr, only as an aside in Anne's final entry. She leaves us with Anne, a largely unknown figure, poised to begin an independent life. It seems like the opening of a better book.
