A genius for both style and giving

At her wedding party, Brooke Astor in a Balmain gown. (cecil beaton studio archive)

By Renee Bergland  |  July 29, 2007

The Last Mrs. Astor: A New York Story
By Frances Kiernan
Norton, 307 pp., illustrated, $24.95

In 1910 China, there were still a few upper-class girls whose feet were bound in order to make them perfect wives for rich men. In a sense, one of them was Brooke Russell, the American daughter of a US diplomat who lived in the Legation quarter in Peking. She was forced to wear high, tightly laced boots to keep her ankles slim, because she was being raised to be the wife of a very rich man.

Today, Brooke Russell Astor's life seems as distant and foreign as the dowager empress's China. She was born in 1902, but even by the standards of a century ago, her childhood was old-fashioned. She grew up as a perpetual poor relation -- "the little sister of the rich." Her mother and grandmother and a covey of aunts worked together to groom her so that she could marry money. And marry it she did -- three millionaires in a row, the first one a few weeks after her 17th birthday.

Brooke's first husband, John Dryden Kuser, was a bad bargain -- an abusive alcoholic who depended on his parents for an allowance. The couple had one child. In 1929, they divorced. Soon afterward, Brooke married her second wealthy man. Her 20-year marriage to Charles "Buddie" Marshall was very happy, but although he was the heir to a shipping fortune, he and Brooke were financially strapped because of his punitive divorce settlement. When Buddie Marshall died suddenly, the bulk of his estate went to his first wife, and Brooke was left in the awkward position of being a 50-year-old widow with a meager bank balance and a remarkable talent for being a rich man's wife.

Vincent Astor was the grouchy, sexagenarian solution to Brooke's predicament. Although he was already married, his wife, Minnie, was actively searching for her own replacement. Her first recruit, Janet Rhinelander Stewart, refused to consider the offer -- when Vincent assured her that he would live only three more years, she tartly asked, "What if the doctors are wrong?" Brooke's refusal was gentler, and so he pursued her zealously. In 1953 she took the reins from Minnie to became the next Mrs. Astor.

Brooke Russell Astor followed a long procession of Mrs. Astors. The most prominent, Vincent's grandmother Caroline, built her ballroom to hold exactly 400 people, so she would have the power of choosing "the 400" at the top of New York society. Vincent's father, John Jacob Astor IV, achieved his own modicum of greatness when he politely escorted women and children from the decks of the Titanic and stayed behind to drown with the gentlemen. Vincent was a thin-skinned, unpopular man with little to recommend him until his death. But the doctors had not been far wrong. The curmudgeonly old millionaire died, as promised, after a mere six years.

Brooke Astor's life started when Vincent's ended. At 57, she inherited a personal fortune of $67 million and the management of a charitable foundation of equal worth. Frances Kiernan's biography, "The Last Mrs. Astor," brings out the fascinating paradoxes of Brooke Astor's courageous, fiercely inventive career as a wife and widow. The book takes off just when its subject's improbable career took off: in her late 50s, when she was finally rich, and finally free to use her wealth however she liked. At times, the narrative dissolves into a blur of socialites' names. But Brooke Astor's social exploits were usually linked to her philanthropic ones, and she used her vast social network for "the alleviation of human misery," as the mission statement of the Vincent Astor Foundation put it. She was a savvy philanthropist who wielded her great social power to benefit New York, the city she loved.

Although Brooke published five books and wrote three poems that appeared in The New Yorker, her greatest achievement was the Astor Foundation. From 1959 to 1997 she used her sharp wits, her fat Rolodex, and her formidable charm to lead New York's charitable world. She gave away a great deal of money, and she convinced her friends to give even more. She supported public institutions from the New York Review of Books to the playgrounds of Bedford-Stuyvesant, in Brooklyn; she campaigned against racism. It is hard to imagine New York today without the revitalized Central Park or New York Public Library, the expanded Bronx Zoo or the grand, light-filled galleries of the Metropolitan Museum. Brooke Astor had a golden touch.

In the past few years, the headlines about her have moved off the society pages and become much sadder. Her son, Tony Marshall, managed her estate with draconian frugality, firing her favorite servants and leaving her wholly isolated. He took her favorite painting, Childe Hassam's "Flags," from the wall of her living room and sold it, telling her (untruthfully) that she could no longer afford to keep it. Eventually, control of her assets was wrested from her son, and after a long legal struggle, her friend Annette de la Renta became her guardian. At 105 years old, she lingers at her suburban house, Holly Hill, visited by a few close friends.

This winter, "Flags" hung in the Impressionists exhibit at the Met, a sad testament to Brooke's son's depredations. But elsewhere in the museum the Chinese scholar's garden she commissioned still radiates peace and cosmopolitan intelligence. Thanks to her, there will always be beautiful corners of New York that take us back to the long ago, when a few girls were raised to marry wealth, and one did so with enough ingenuity to remake New York in her own elegant image.

Renee Bergland, a professor at Simmons College, is writing a biography of astronomer Maria Mitchell.
