COLIN THUBRON | THE INTERVIEW

Surprises, change on Islam's borderlands

By Anna Mundow  |  July 1, 2007

Colin Thubron, a sublime travel writer in the tradition of Freya Stark and Patrick Leigh Fermor , has in 40 years of wandering been repeatedly drawn to the remote regions of Asia. For his latest book, "Shadow of the Silk Road" (HarperCollins, $25.95), he spent eight months traversing 7,000 miles along the legendary trade route between China and Turkey, passing through China's Muslim territories, crossing Afghanistan and Iran, capturing the most evocative details in the landscape and in the lives of ordinary people with lyricism, compassion, and wit. Thubron, who is also an acclaimed novelist, spoke from his home in London.

Q Why did you choose the Silk Road?

A Out of instinct. Asia has fascinated me all my life, and this book draws together China, the ex-Soviet Union, Islam. The thing that connects them is the Silk Road. I became fascinated in particular by the human traces that the past had left behind there. The falsity of borders, the strange ethnic mixes.

Q Is the individual, for you, where the truth of a place resides?

A I've always concentrated less on politics than on the impact that politics has on ordinary people; what they believe in, what they value. That sounds rather highfalutin, but I feel that is the reality that is most accessible to the traveler and most inaccessible to, say, a politician, a diplomat, or even a journalist. It's the ordinary person you meet along the way who often upsets the stereotypes delivered by conventional journalism.

Q Stereotypes about Islam, for example?

A Yes. I met so many people who occupy the borderlands of Islam. It's just as varied and complex as Christianity is, perhaps more so.

Q What was the biggest social change you perceived in modern China?

A I think the biggest trauma for ordinary people is the movement away from the public to the private, in business and in personal life. The move, socially, from country to the town, which often means from the family collective to the nuclear family in a high-rise apartment.

Q Is the great division now between the old and the young?

A In the Confucian system prestige lies with age and therefore with the past. But China is looking to the future and erasing the past. That's very new, to find truth in the future. In the last 30 years, China has had such phenomenal social change that old people now find themselves stranded, redundant. It's very sad. You don't hear about them, the people in the villages who get left behind.

Q Is there an enduring impression from this journey?

A I think it's the falseness of national boundaries and the appropriateness of others that aren't on the map. That's what strikes me in retrospect. How fluid nations are. Almost all the borders I crossed have no ethnic meaning. People were the same on either side. Yet, throughout my journey I was struck by how people changed across frontiers not marked on the map. A thousand miles inside China, for example, the world becomes non-Chinese, it belongs to the Muslim Uighur people.

Q You write that "the modern traveler fears emptiness." How?

A There's a lot of romanticization of travel writers, but basically we're like journalists. We're looking for copy. You want the sensual experience of the country, you want to live it. The worst thing that can happen to you is that nothing happens. It's almost as if there are two of you going, the one who's traveling and the one who's going to write about it, sitting on the other's shoulder. Just as the traveler is getting mugged or shot at, the one sitting on his shoulder is saying "What good copy."

Q You take notes but not photographs?

A I take copious notes because otherwise the details get lost. Little things like the texture of the rock or how somebody's eyebrows met in the middle. I don't take a camera partly because when police or others go through your belongings they think no camera, no spy.

Q So you have many notebooks by journey's end?

A Four or five, perhaps. My writing's very tiny, a sort of shorthand.

Q You've never lost them?

A No, that's the terror. Only once, after a journey in the old Soviet Union, were my notes almost confiscated. The KGB had been following me, and I was stopped in a place called Chop, not a very encouraging name. They couldn't decipher my horrid little handwriting, so they made me read aloud. The officer pointed to the heading "Odessa," so I read out how the sun was sparkling on the waves, how lovely the Soviet Union was. After half an hour, the KGB officer said "This is very poetic. You could publish this" and handed it back. I could have kissed his boots. Even as I read it to him, I realized all the details -- the things that give a book life -- that I had forgotten and might lose.

Anna Mundow, a freelance journalist living in Central Massachusetts, is a correspondent for the Irish Times. She can be reached via e-mail at ama1668@hotmail.com.
