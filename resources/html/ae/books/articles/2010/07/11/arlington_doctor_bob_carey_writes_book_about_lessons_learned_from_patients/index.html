Doctor learns humility from his patients


Dr. Bob Carey’s book focuses on what his patients have taught him during his 56-year medical career. (Dina Rudick/Globe Staff)
By Brock Parker
Globe Correspondent / July 11, 2010

As his five children were growing up in Arlington, Dr. Bob Carey said he never told them about the patients he was seeing at work.

He never dared to share stories with the children about people who were seriously ill or nearing death, and he wouldn’t even discuss patients with his high school sweetheart and longtime wife, Mary Carey.

“Basically I never told her anything,’’ he said.

But a few years ago, after he’d retired from a 56-year career in medicine, Carey began to think it would be nice to share some of what he’d learned as a doctor with his now-adult children, and eventually his grandchildren.

So Carey, who turned 81 June 27, began writing about experiences he had during his career. The vignettes begin with him as a Boston University medical student in the 1950s at what is now the Boston Medical Center, then take him to Okinawa, Japan, back home, and into private practice in Arlington, and later to South America, where as a visiting doctor he treated the poor for free.

Instead of a clinical review of his career, what has emerged from Carey’s writings is a 191-page book, “Patients Teach a Doctor about Life and Death,’’ focusing on the lessons he learned from the people he cared for.

It’s been an eye-opening read for Carey’s daughter Kathy, now 50, who said she was shocked by some of the cases her father had been exposed to early in his career.

Carey wrote about gunshot victims, a horrific case of sexual abuse he saw in an emergency room, and a woman who at 98 years old asked to be tested for syphilis because she wanted to know if she’d contracted the disease when she was raped many years earlier.

Kathy Carey said the opportunity to read about her father’s interactions with patients, and what he thought about them at the time, added a new dimension to how she views her dad.

“You don’t usually talk to your parents about all of their experiences,’’ Carey’s daughter said. “As a child they don’t tell you everything that they’ve been through. Basically they help you go through life and they use that experience to help you. But you don’t really know what [their experiences are].’’

Carey said one of his first lessons as a doctor came when he was a medical student and was assisting with the treatment of a 60-year-old woman who was having repeated heart attacks. The woman, whom Carey called Matilda in his book to hide her true identity, became the first pacemaker patient in the United States. But at that time a pacemaker was administered externally and was very painful for the patient, Carey said.

For two weeks as Maltilda’s heart failure grew worse, the pacemaker was used with increased frequency to revive her. Carey got to know her during that time, and would talk to her about her children and about his engagement to Mary. Eventually Matilda told him that she could not take the pain of the treatments anymore. She said she knew there was nothing more the doctors could do, and she wanted to die.

When Matilda told him that, Carey said, he became upset and told her they couldn’t let her die. But when he returned to the hospital after the weekend, Matilda was nowhere to be found.

Carey thought she had simply walked out of the hospital, but then he heard a scream. Someone had found Maltida, who had hidden in a closet at the hospital over the weekend and died.

Carey said he learned a lot from Matilda’s bravery in undergoing the pacemaker treatments and from her death.

“We can keep people alive miserably, and that was the first time I ever saw it,’’ he said.

Other patients also had humbling lessons for Carey.

When he was still in medical school in the 1950s, Carey began working with a patient who was diagnosed with Hodgkin’s lymphoma. The patient, whom he refers to as Gladys, began treatment for her enlarged lymph nodes, but Carey said that at the time, few people survived the disease. Soon she decided to stop treatment long before its completion.

Gladys told the doctors she wasn’t afraid to die and she wanted to go home, forget her disease, and take care of her children, Carey said. But before she left, she told Carey he was going to be a fine doctor and thanked him for all he had done for her.

In the 1960s when Carey was working at Symmes Hospital in Arlington, a patient was referred to him, and when he walked into the room, Gladys recognized him immediately. She had put on weight over the years, but she told Carey that after she stopped her treatment for Hodgkin’s disease, she had slowly begun to feel better. She lived another 20 years before dying of heart disease and kidney failure.

Carey said it was a humbling case, because despite the doctors’ dire prognosis for Gladys, she had proved them wrong.

“You never know,’’ he said.

But it was Carey’s ability to assume that he didn’t know everything that enabled him to learn so much from his patients, said the Rev. Robert Thomas, a missionary with the Society of St. James the Apostle in Boston who later worked with Carey to help impoverished people in South America.

“I think one of his most important characteristics is he doesn’t feel he has all of the answers,’’ Thomas said. “His manner is that of listening, not just to the patients, but to the other doctors.’’

For the last 30 years, Thomas said, Carey has been working to help people in countries such as Bolivia and Ecuador, and unlike many other Western doctors, Carey always showed respect to the doctors of South America rather than simply giving commands while treating a patient.

“He’s an outstanding example of reaching out and serving other people, and he keeps doing it,’’ Thomas said.

Even now as he’s trying to sell his book, which was published last year, Carey said the proceeds are being donated to a foundation he established to provide scholarships for medical students to work with doctors in poor countries.

But Carey said he just wanted to write a book about his experiences and the lessons he learned so he could pass them along to his children and grandchildren.

“I love my grandchildren a lot, and I thought it would be nice to leave that behind,’’ he said.
