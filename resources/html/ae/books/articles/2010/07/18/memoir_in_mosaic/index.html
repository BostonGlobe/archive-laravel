Memoir in mosaic

Gordimer’s collected nonfiction, while a mess, traces a major writer’s thinking over decades

Nadine Gordimer treats “Telling Times’’ like a bin, into which she throws many of her writings. (Radu Sigheti/Reuters)
By James Zug
July 18, 2010

“The truth isn’t always beauty, but the hunger for it is.’’

The grand old dame of African literature, Nadine Gordimer, now a feisty 86, is a fantastic writer. Deep within her 14 novels and numerous collections of short stories are many vivid characters and lines that hauntingly linger.

This aphorism, however, appears not in a novel but in one of the 91 pieces that make up what is essentially the collected nonfiction of the Nobel Prize laureate.

For such a celebrated writer (15 honorary degrees), it is an astonishing mess. A congeries of essays, book reviews, obituaries, letters (public and semiprivate), journal articles studded with footnotes, conference lectures, and travel pieces, “Telling Times’’ is inherently fragmentary. Unlike her earlier nonfiction collection, “The Essential Gesture’’ (1988), it is organized chronologically rather than by theme, so subject matter and style drastically hop, skip, and jump, first person, second person, third-person singular, and even a bit of first-person plural, all in the space of a couple of pages. Toward the end, fed up with dim-witted journalists making the obligatory pilgrimage to her Johannesburg home, she even interviews herself.

“Essential Gesture’’ was edited and introduced by a skilled writer and critic, Stephen Clingman. “Telling Times’’ needs that sort of helping hand. Some pieces just don’t belong — a toothless bit of fluff about serving on the Cannes Film Festival jury, for example, and others repeat and repeat. Three separate times she quotes the same poem by the South African poet Mongane Wally Serote. Only a handful of pieces are annotated with the place of publication (others get just the year) so the context and intended audience is usually unclear.

Even though the book functions as a bin into which she throws every last scrap of wordsmithing, she omitted a few pieces. She could have included her 1983 Paris Review interview (maybe she was too embarrassed — it notoriously began with her interviewer asking: “Do you have seasons in South Africa or is it hot all year round?’’) or her stunningly masterful 2001 New Yorker vignette about visiting George Sacks, an elderly South African exile in London, while George’s wife, Betty, was apparently out having an affair. And one wishes the book didn’t end in 2006 (the subtitle, “1954-2008’’ is wrong), partly because it would’ve been fascinating if she had written about her reaction to the robbery she suffered toward the end of that year: Four African men burst into her house in Parktown, an exclusive Johannesburg neighborhood, and after taking jewelry, cash, and car keys, locked the nation’s most famous writer and her longtime, aged domestic servant in a storeroom. Apparently they didn’t recognize Gordimer, or they didn’t care. It was an incident straight out of a Gordimer novel.

With her acute mind, though, Gordimer clearly paid some attention to the book. Her Nobel Prize acceptance speech is included, though oddly edited and labeled from 1992 even though she delivered it in 1991. Stranger still is the fact that she reprints, unedited, her 1954 New Yorker memoir, “A South African Childhood’’ with its magical tale about a cousin from Natal named Roy who rode a bicycle over one of the hills of coal outside her Transvaal mining town of Springs. (This was an act of childhood rebellion, since the hill could collapse in a landslide and bury a trespasser inside, where the coal still burned.) One of the many thunderclaps of news that exploded when Ronald Suresh Roberts published his brilliant and controversial biography of Gordimer in 2005 was that Roy was fictitious. Gordimer had made him up and slipped him past the New Yorker’s fact checkers, along with a four-page imaginary outing to Kruger National Park. Since the story has now been publicly outed, why reprint it? (Interestingly, it didn’t appear in “Essential Gesture.’’)

After the experience she had with Roberts, when she granted him total access to herself and her papers for five years and then turned against him when his biography had critical sections, she said she would never write a full-length memoir. “I am much too jealous of my privacy,’’ she explained. Instead, this book serves as a subtle autobiography. It contains the delightful, if perhaps unintended thread that has twisted around Gordimer’s whole literary career: the paradox of being a privileged white liberal writer in a racist, overwhelmingly poor South Africa.

Here one glimpses her dewy innocence in her 1961 journey up the Congo River; her grappling with the complex, contentious, contradictory South Africa political scene in the 1970s; and her arrival in the 1980s as a activist and a writer: “In South Africa the ivory tower is bulldozed anew with every black man’s home destroyed to make way for a white man’s,’’ she wrote in 1985. “Yet there are positions between the bulldozed ivory tower and the maximum security prison,’’ and that is where Gordimer staked out a place for herself, committed, relevant, involved but displaying her activism in her writing. She tried to shrink the metaphorical distance between herself and her suffering countrymen — the 8 miles between her lovely house in Parktown and the shanty towns of Soweto — without eliminating it. That was her “essential gesture.’’

It is fascinating to follow that journey. However flawed this book is, it still stands as a monument to a woman who found a way to help liberate her country through her words.

James Zug is the author of “The Guardian: The History of South Africa’s Anti-Apartheid Newspaper.’’ He can be reached at jzug@earthlink.net.
