Their earthly concerns

Bill McKibben and Anna Lappé talk about their new books

Bill McKibben (Nancie Battaglia)
By Irene Muniz
Globe Correspondent / April 27, 2010

Bill McKibben and Anna Lappé are bringing the battle to save the environment to your house, with books making the case for adjusting the ways we think, live, and eat right now if we are to avert a global crisis.

To call McKibben the king of green may be overstating his status, but not by much. He has long championed action on climate change, sustainability, and environmentalism.

In 1989, McKibben wrote “The End of Nature,’’ one of the first books to detail the impact of global warming. Those warnings have gone largely unheeded, drowned out by the shrill dissent of skeptics.

McKibben said his new book, “Eaarth: Making a Life on a Tough New Planet’’ (Times Books), advises readers on how to preserve what’s left of our out-of-balance planet.

“Earth is different enough nowadays, which is why I added another ‘a’ to the title,’’ said McKibben, who emphasized that global warming is no longer a problem for the future, but is with us here and now.

Here he lays out the evidence for how warming has already changed the planet. He doesn’t focus on big-picture solutions but instead offers suggestions for local, personal approaches to help mitigate damage. He lavishes praise on those who are developing local food systems, insulating homes, and working toward a future where we will become a nation of solar panels and small windmills.

Lappé also focuses on lifestyle, specifically food. After traveling the world interviewing farmers, scientists, and food advocates, she wrote “Diet for a Hot Planet: The Climate Crisis at the End of Your Fork and What You Can Do About It’’ (Bloomsbury), for which McKibben wrote the foreword.

In it she addresses the connections between food production and climate change and offers various solutions for our planet’s essential healing.

“The food and agriculture sector is responsible for a third of all global emissions,’’ said Lappé.

Lappé says her latest effort represents a continuation of a conversation Frances Moore Lappé, her mother and the author of “Diet for a Small Planet,’’ started about 40 years ago. In that 1971 book, Frances Moore Lappé examined the social and environmental costs of industrial food production, especially factory-farm meat and dairy.

In “Diet for a Hot Planet,’’ Anna Lappé suggests farming methods that reduce emissions and promote sustainability. She promotes diet principles such as seeking out organic food and cutting back on meat, perhaps the least climate-friendly food because producing it requires large amounts of energy, generates pollutants, and consumes inordinate amounts of grain.

If all this has whetted your appetite to learn more, you can join the Lappés today, and McKibben on Friday, at the Brattle Theatre at 6 p.m. for discussions of their books. Tickets are $5 and are available at Harvard Book Store, www.harvard.com, or by calling 617-661-1515.

ANTIQUARIAN FAIR
Dealers from around the country will gather this week to sell and display antique and out-of-print books, manuscripts, photographs, and paper ephemera at the 2010 Boston Antiquarian Book Fair. The two-day event begins Friday from 5 to 9 p.m. and continues Saturday from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m. at Boston University’s Track and Tennis Center, 100 Ashford St. Admission for adults is $6, for students $3, and children under age 12 are free. For more information, visit www.bostonantiquarianbookfair.com.
