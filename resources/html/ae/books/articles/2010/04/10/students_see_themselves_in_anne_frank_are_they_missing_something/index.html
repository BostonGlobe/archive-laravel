Anne Frank, typical teen?

Students often see themselves reflected in the pages of her famous diary. But they may be missing the big picture.

By Don Aucoin
Globe Staff / April 10, 2010

Few historical figures haunt the imagination the way Anne Frank does. Even now, 65 years after she died in a concentration camp at age 15, we wonder who she might have become.

But to a generation of young people who are discovering her story for the first time — in the pages of the landmark diary she left behind, in a brand-new biography aimed at 9-to-12-year-olds, or in TV adaptations like PBS’s “The Diary of Anne Frank,’’ which airs tomorrow night at 9 on WGBH-TV (Channel 2) — it is who Anne was that matters most.

They know the horrible fate that befell Anne, but what they find striking is how much like themselves she was. Anne kept her diary from ages 13 to 15, and for all of her precocity, it is the unmistakable voice of a teenager that comes through. She was extraordinary, but she was also in some ways typical.

Her friction with her mother, her close bond with her father, her rivalry with her older sister, her uncertain steps toward a first romance, her dreams, her belief that no one fully understands her: All this adds up to a picture of adolescence, and of family life, that resonates with the Facebook generation.

Yet some scholars see a danger in a too-easy identification with Anne as a kind of Everyteen. To view her book, “The Diary of a Young Girl,’’ as a coming-of-age story is to obscure the most important fact about her: that she was a victim of the Holocaust, and that she was, as the critic Harold Bloom has noted, “emblematic of hundreds of thousands of murdered children.’’

Perhaps it was inevitable that Anne would also become a symbol of adolescence, given that her diary reflects the preoccupations and passions of teenagers at all times and in all places. “She wanted to be an actress, she wanted to be a writer, she wanted people to remember her name. She was a lot like me,’’ says Rebecca Gray, 18, who played Anne this year in a production at Swampscott High School. “I feel really connected to her.’’

Rachel Hiromura, 14, of Boxford, feels that same sense of connection, to Anne and to the rest of the Franks. “Personally, I could really relate to her, the way she thinks about friends and family,’’ she says. “She was always kind of into boys. She was very outgoing and social. They struck me as a normal family.’’

A similar realization dawned on British actress Ellie Kendrick, who had not read “The Diary of a Young Girl’’ but was aware of her iconic status before being cast as Anne for the new “Masterpiece Classic’’ version on PBS. Kendrick was 17 during filming (she’s 19 now), and she says she found herself re-reading her own “angsty’’ diary each night.

“I had to think of her as an ordinary teenage girl, which she was,’’ Kendrick said in a phone interview. “The appeal of Anne is that everyone can identify with her. She had faults. She could have tantrums, and be feisty, and say horrible things to her parents. We’ve all been through those things ourselves.’’

But Amy Boesky, who taught “Diary’’ last fall in her class on adolescent literature at Boston College, says that for all the seeming universality of the emotions captured in Anne’s diary, it is essential that readers and viewers remember that their own lives are very far removed from the experiences of a young Jewish girl in hiding from the Nazis in Amsterdam during World War II. “I worry about losing the specificity of the context, which is unique to the historical tragedy of these families,’’ says Boesky, who recently took her teenage daughters to the Anne Frank House in Amsterdam. “We imagine that we know this family and this young woman, but we don’t. This is such a tiny piece of who she was and who they were. These were complicated people living complicated lives under terrible circumstances.’’

One way or another, we continue to try to understand Anne Frank and to see beyond the mystique that has grown up around her book, which has been described as the second best-selling nonfiction book in history, after the Bible.

Francine Prose, in her influential 2009 book, “Anne Frank: The Book, the Life, the Afterlife,’’ gave extensive attention to Anne’s remarkable literary craftsmanship, but she also noted: “What makes it even more impressive is that this deceptively unassuming book focuses on a particular moment and on specific people, and at the same time speaks, in ways that seem timeless and universal, about adolescence and family life.’’

“Anne Frank: Her Life in Words and Pictures,’’ a just-published biography by Menno Metselaar and Ruud van der Rol, tries to bring that adolescent into full view with Frank family photos and excerpts from Anne’s diary in her original handwriting. In PBS’s “The Diary of Anne Frank,’’ there are many moments when Anne seems to speak to, and for, all teenagers. “Do your parents ever annoy you?’’ she says to Peter van Pels (also known as Peter van Daan). “Mine do, especially mother.’’ At one point, she confides to her diary: “Nobody really understands me, but then, I don’t really understand myself. I feel so lonely sometimes.’’ And at another: “Every day I feel myself maturing.’’ She tells her father that when her generation is in charge, “We’re going to make a better job of it than you grownups did.’’

That is the voice — alternately uncertain and assertive — that continues to echo today. “It was written as the diary of a young adolescent, so of course it’s going to reflect all the turmoil, the life issues, the tensions that kids that age are going to deal with,’’ notes Dr. Gerry Koocher, a senior associate in psychology at Children’s Hospital in Boston. “What adds tension to it is the fact that here they are, cooped up in these scary circumstances. So when readers grab it they’re saying to themselves, ‘Oh my God, this is a real person, like someone in my family or like I was at one time, and what they had to live through, and what awful things happened to them.’’

Marnie Butler has certainly found that to be the case in the six years she has been teaching “The Diary of a Young Girl’’ at Masconomet Regional Middle School in Topsfield. As if they were discussing a friend, her students engage in lively debates over Anne’s budding romance with Peter, asking one another whether she would have been interested in him if they had not been cooped up together. “They usually decide no,’’ said Butler.

“My students are now trying to have some emancipation from their parents,’’ she added. “So they are also cognizant of what she had to go through by living in such close proximity with her parents, 24-7, for 25 months.’’

To 14-year-old Jonathan Arone of Boxford, the personalities and the family dynamics in “The Diary of a Young Girl’’ have a remarkably contemporary flavor. “Before the Nazis came in to where they lived, honestly, I’d say it was pretty similar to what most of us are going through,’’ he said. “Our families are close, but they all have their problems with each other. If I was in the same circumstance, hiding in a small space for so long, our relationships would probably deteriorate in the same way.’’

All in all, young readers are discovering what readers of all ages have learned since “Diary’’ was first published in 1947 (and in an English translation in 1952): To meet Anne Frank in print is to be profoundly, and sometimes permanently, affected by her. Rebecca Gray, whose performance as Anne won an award last month at the Massachusetts High School Drama Festival, plans to attend NYU in the fall. She has set herself the ambitious goal of concentrating in theater, journalism, foreign languages, creative writing, and sociology.

“It sounds a lot like what Anne would have studied,’’ she says, proudly.
