A transformative year

New insights on the tensions between gender and race and two generations of feminists in the 2008 election

The misogyny Hillary Clinton faced on the campaign trail was an eye-opener for younger feminists. (Stan Honda/ AFP/ Getty Images)
By Kate Tuttle
Globe Correspondent / October 3, 2010

The 2008 presidential election was a season of firsts, in which both Hillary Clinton and Barack Obama (and, to a lesser extent, Sarah Palin) broke through boundaries and energized an electorate accustomed to a parade of white male candidates. But too much groundbreaking at once can feel like an earthquake, and as Rebecca Traister points out in her new book, the competing claims of race and gender made 2008 “a harrowing race for the American presidency,” in which history was made both triumphantly and, at times, painfully.

Traister, a political reporter for the online magazine Salon, reminds us that in the run-up to 2008 it was Clinton whose candidacy was poised for triumph; it seemed inevitable we would have a woman president before a black one. The primary season that unfolded, then, after Obama emerged as a strong candidate running a brilliant, innovative campaign, increasingly tested loyalties.

There was the tension around whose turn it was — the black man or the white woman — that renewed political grudges, Traister points out, going all the way back to a little more than a decade before the Civil War and the pioneering Seneca Falls Convention on women’s rights. It didn’t help that in the century and a half after Seneca there had been ample instances of racism in the modern feminist movement and sexism among male civil rights leaders or that both women and African-Americans had been forced to compromise policy goals for the greater good of a Democratic Party that sometimes seemed to take their support for granted.

Besides the race-versus-gender conflict, there was tension between feminists — the second-wavers who marched and protested throughout the ’60s and ’70s and their daughters. “Deciding who to vote for in the primary might have been easier,” Traister writes, “if it hadn’t been for my mother.” Traister, who describes herself as “a feminist caught between old and new, senior and junior, retro and nouveau,” is well-positioned to sketch the mutual misunderstandings, resentments, and grudges that mark the generational divide between women who considered themselves feminists. Feminism, as she points out, is at best a loose coalition, gathering women (and sympathetic men) who care about economic and educational opportunities, reproductive rights, and social and legal equality. But modern feminism, by embracing choice as its primary rhetorical concept, has morphed into less a movement than a style — if Palin and her antiabortion sisters can call themselves “Feminists for Life,” does the word mean anything at all?

For a generation of white women who had been raised with confidence in their own power, many of whom weren’t even planning to vote for her, the misogyny Clinton faced on the campaign trail was shocking — and older feminists were quick to point out that sexism wasn’t even remotely dead. From young men bearing posters reading “Iron My Shirt” to cable hosts who said she reminded them of “everyone’s first wife,” Clinton couldn’t catch a break, and the hostility that surrounded her candidacy proved eye-opening to a generation of younger feminists.

After losing in Iowa as Obama’s campaign caught fire, Clinton’s near-tear episode in New Hampshire sparked an intensely gendered reaction among the media. “Hillary was being portrayed as a hand-in-the-air, know-it-all girl, grating and unpopular,” Traister writes, while “Obama . . . had been cast as the swaggeringly unprepared cool guy, a characterization that may have been intellectually unjust and more than a little bit racially inflected.” After Gloria Steinem published an editorial that seemed to compare the burdens of racism and sexism, the cycle of pain and infighting was complete; as Traister points out, “what should have been the most exhilarating election in modern memory” had turned into a grudge match in which “nobody was having any fun and everybody was pissed off.”

Much of the book expands on Traister’s Salon columns as she reported the race from its earliest primaries to election night and beyond; as such, it will feel largely familiar to many readers who followed the event through online journalism. While the book at times lacks historical heft — one wishes for more than the shorthand glossing of major civil rights movements — it’s a genuinely fun and provocative read. One of the best features of online journalism is its chatty eagerness to make connections, name names, even give credit, and Traister generously showcases some of the strongest women’s voices to cover 2008, from her Salon colleague Joan Walsh to Jessica Valenti of Feministing to MSNBC commentators Rachel Maddow and Melissa Harris-Lacewell.

In a book chock-full of voices, Steinem’s emerges as the most fascinating and sympathetic (despite her op-ed misstep). When a younger colleague weeps bitterly at Clinton’s concession speech, Steinem comforts her, pointing out that she will likely see a woman president in her lifetime. “But you won’t,” her colleague points out — a devastating observation that the septuagenarian activist greets with clear, dry eyes. If Steinem is the book’s unlikely heroine, Clinton adviser Mark Penn is first among an unappealing crew of villains, which also includes Chris Matthews and other male pundits whose anti-Clinton quips now seem to confirm what Steinem told Traister after the election: Clinton could never have won this race. “It’s always been OK for women to sing the blues,” she says. “Just not so good for us to win. We all know deep in our hearts if we want to be loved we have to lose.”
