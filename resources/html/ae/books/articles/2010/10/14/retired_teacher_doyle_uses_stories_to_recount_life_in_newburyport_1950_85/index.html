‘Life in Newburyport,’ one story at a time

By Joel Brown
Globe Correspondent / October 14, 2010

The issues? The future of the Crow Lane landfill and beach erosion at Plum Island. Affordable housing and parking meters and Merrimack River dredging. And always, always, the question of how to develop the waterfront.

The more things change in Newburyport, the more they stay the same. That, at least, is the impression you might get reading “Life In Newburyport, 1950-1985,’’ the second volume by Jean Foley Doyle, 80, a native of the city’s South End.

She retired from teaching history at Newburyport High School in 1999 and began working on the first book “because there was nothing about the 20th century,’’ she said. “All they ever do is write about the 1600s, 1700s, 1800s. I kept telling people, this is history too.’’

Like its popular predecessor, “Life In Newburyport, 1900-1950,’’ the new book isn’t a straightforward historical narrative or scholarly tome. Rather it’s an episodic chronicle that lives up to its title, giving a picture of what residents were talking about month after month, whether it’s City Hall shenanigans and major development initiatives, or news of a bad fire or a new business.

“Let me make plain at the beginning, writing books is not my forte,’’ Doyle said. “I was not an English major, I was a history major. This is the last thing I wanted to do. I thought, I’ll do the research, along with some other people, and we’ll find someone who’ll write it. Well, that never happened.’’

If there’s an overall arc to the book, it traces Newburyport’s redevelopment from a run-down port and factory town to the tourist destination and bedroom community it is today. After countless hours poring over old newspapers and interviewing the principals, Doyle is able to chart the sometimes arduous process that resulted in the brick-and- flowerpot cityscape we know today, and makes a point to give credit to those who might have been overlooked.

“The biggest disappointment of course is the waterfront,’’ said Doyle, who wishes the powers that be had been able to finish development sometime in the last three decades. “Everybody had very good intentions. They forgot one thing: You’ve got to sit down and compromise.’’

That’s not the only touchy topic. Consider Andrew J. “Bossy’’ Gillis, Newburyport’s on-again, off-again mayor for decades, a divisive figure, both populist and bully. Doyle is clearly not one of those won over by his folksy manner.

“Remember, I knew him, and that was the difference,’’ she said with a chuckle. “It’s too bad because he held up progress. People fall for that thing, ‘Oh, he’s a man of the people,’ but people didn’t gain from it. They’d have him in for two terms and throw him out and have him in again. It was ridiculous. It was more about getting back at the High Street people, by putting him in, than anything else.’’

Newburyport native Bill Plante Jr. was close to many of the town’s biggest moments as a reporter, editor and manager at the Newburyport Daily News beginning in 1951; he rates his own section in the book. He says Doyle’s achievement is to offer a perspective on the redevelopment not found in a more formal history or an anthropological study like the “Yankee City’’ project that analyzed Newburyport in several books in the 1940s.

“She did an excellent job in quoting the people and dealing with the people who actually started it. That is a flesh and blood history in a very meaningful way,’’ said Plante, who retired in the 1980s but continues to write a weekly column. “I think what she did was to rebirth some of the texture of the community.’’

For scholars who come along later, Doyle’s work “will add flavor to whatever histories of this period will follow,’’ Plante said.

Doyle has the credentials to write what she does, as her life tracks many of the changes of the decades it spans. Born in December 1929, she grew up next door to the Emma Andrews Branch Library in the South End.

“I was very fortunate. My father had a low-paying job but he had a job, he was a salesman for Swift & Co., selling meat to the grocery stores,’’ she said. She went to Immaculate Conception School. “I was expected to walk to school in the morning, come home for lunch, go back after lunch, and then home again.’’

She graduated from Newburyport High School in 1947, and worked briefly at the Towle Manufacturing Co. silver factory before marrying and starting a family. She had seven children — “We started the baby boom,’’ Doyle says — then went back to school, attending Merrimack College full time. In her senior year, her son Jimmy was a freshman, and they got mother-son scholarships.

Graduating from college in 1969 at age 40 — she would get a master’s degree from the University of New Hampshire three years later — Doyle became a history teacher at the high school, at a time when students, school, and Newburyport were all changing rapidly.

When she was in school, “the goal was not to build your self-esteem, the goal was to make sure you didn’t get a big head. It was very different when I was teaching,’’ she said with a laugh. “All of a sudden I’m supposed to spend most of my time building self-esteem. It wasn’t too easy for me sometimes.’’

The times were reflected in discussions around the dinner table; Jimmy was very opposed to the Vietnam War, while his brother David left high school early to join the Marines and fight in Vietnam.

Now Doyle is twice widowed, the grandmother of 11, and with a smile complains that she’s not yet a great-grandmother. She’s coping with a variety of health problems, including the after-effects of breaking her neck in a fall while on vacation in Greece earlier this year.

Still, she’s intent on sharing her book with as many people as possible — “a resource, and a gift to the city.’’ She sits patiently for an interview, though in truth she would rather talk about her beloved garden at her Rolfe’s Lane home.

Thanks to the Internet, she’s had response from people all over the country. One thread went through them all, she reports: “These people said, ’My happiest years were in Newburyport; I wish I could live there.’ ’’
