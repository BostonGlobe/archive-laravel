The Interview

Out of Africa, a debut about a young man’s odyssey

By Anna Mundow
Globe Correspondent / May 2, 2010

“Her lips were slightly parted, as if a fruit were about to be brought to her mouth, as if there were thoughts in her, and words waiting to take flight.” Adam Schwartzman’s first novel, “Eddie Signwriter,” opens with the drowning death of an influential woman in rural Ghana. As local suspicion grows, young Kwasi, the lover of the woman’s niece, is banished, flees to Senegal, and then illegally to France. Along the way, he learns his trade and becomes Eddie Signwriter, a memorable ingénue in a chaotic world that Schwartzman evokes with consummate skill and rare compassion.

Schwartzman, who was born in Johannesburg, attended Oxford University, worked in international finance, and is the author of three books of poetry. He lives in Istanbul and spoke while visiting Boston.

Q. Where did Eddie come from?

A. I went out looking for him. Not him at first, but the story. I knew what I wanted to write about, but not how. I knew that Ghana — a place I had been many times before — would be central in the novel. When I arrived in Accra I saw these magnificent painted signs everywhere, and I eventually bumped into the sign writer who became Eddie’s mentor in the novel. One event led to another. I’m a great believer in chance, in life, and in writing. My travels across the continent provided different parts of Eddie. Having grown up in South Africa at the end of apartheid I was throwing off the identity that apartheid had given me and entering into the experience of the continent. I was determined not to write an autobiographical first novel, but I found myself going through an internal journey similar to Eddie’s.

Q. As a white South African, were you hesitant to write about the black African experience?

A. Yes, more than I should have been. A lot of people ask me if I am an African writer, and my answer is that I am for the only reason that counts: I come from there. In my last book of poems I wrote a line that goes, “I am not a boy, I own this” and that’s pretty much how I feel. But there was a long period of anxiety and working through that issue.

Q. You cunningly weave a crime story through the novel. Did you have a model for that?

A. In one way the model was Henry James’s “The Turn of the Screw.” What happens cannot be spoken. There aren’t words for it. Though for Eddie there are images and sensations. There are signs for the reader. The most important things can’t be said. They have to be shown.

Q. Are there places in the novel where your prose and poetry meet?

A. They’re not for me two separate worlds although I often wonder if being a poet helped me to write the novel. I don’t ultimately know. The texture of the prose is really important for me. The smaller the unit of meaning — the word rather than the paragraph or chapter — the more you can encode the language. But narrative was new for me, and it is a powerful force to wield. Stories are entry points into people’s souls; with a good one you can change a life.

Q. In portraying African migrants, how much research was involved?

A. Quite a lot. But at the same time I was very much writing about people around me — friends, acquaintances, and in some senses about myself. It is, of course, difficult to write about people who are different from you because you have to enter into their lives, and it’s a thorny philosophical question whether that’s really possible. I think it is. I believe in the power of compassion and empathy. You find people aren’t so different from you. A novel doesn’t have to be true. It just has to be plausible.

Q. Are you involved with any South African literary movement?

A. Not hugely. I’m not really part of any literary world. But it’s very important to me that the novel comes out in Africa. My major engagement with South African literature has been poetry. The guiding principle for the anthology that I edited a while back was to choose poems that embrace their own origins. I wanted to cut across the sterile debate about whether literature should be an instrument of the revolution or a purely aesthetic pursuit.

Anna Mundow, a freelance journalist living in Central Massachusetts, is a contributor to the Irish Times. She can be reached by e-mail at ama1668@hotmail.com.
