Picture imperfect

In Gunter Grass’s autobiographical novel, family photos transform events, invoking a meditation on art and life

“The Box’’ is a fictionalized memoir told from the perspective of Gunter Grass’s eight children. (Susana Vera /Reuters/File 2007)
By Matthew Peters
Globe Correspondent / November 21, 2010

In Gunter Grass’s 2007 memoir “Peeling the Onion,’’ the writer recounts the favorable impact his early poems and stories made on the members of a Berlin writing group in the 1950s. Soon after he received these accolades, however, Grass became dissatisfied. He felt that his art would remain insignificant unless it addressed a subject that was central to German post-war life: “the massive weight of the German past.’’

Grass’s novel of the World War II era and its aftermath, “The Tin Drum’’ (1959), with its extravagant word play, its extreme and controversial subject matter, and its disturbing and mesmerizing narrator, reflected the monstrous realities of the immediate German past. For his refusal to turn away from these realities, and also for his constant regard for their legacy in contemporary German life, Grass became one of the most public and outspoken of celebrated writers.

But the scope of his new book, “The Box,’’ is far more modest, directing its attention instead to Grass’s private life and its influence on his art. It is a fictionalized memoir, told from the perspective of Grass’s eight children. At the heart of their narratives is Marie, a friend of their father’s, who, as they grew up, is said to have recorded their lives and that of Grass himself, by constantly taking photographs.

Their voices quickly make the kind of dreamlike, fantastical swerves that are common to Grass’s writing. The photographs do not simply record. They recapture scenes and events that happened years before they were taken; they wildly transform moments from the children’s lives.

Marie’s camera is in one sense a metaphor for the artistic imagination. It supplies Grass with snapshots, so that they may be reimagined and modified: “Whenever Marie snapped a picture of something with the Agfa box, it came out of her darkroom looking totally different.’’

More particularly, the metaphor of the camera gives Grass a new perspective from which to reconsider one of the great preoccupations of his critical writing: the relations between his writing and the past.

In 1982, Grass declared that he had always “written against the passage of time. The past made me throw it in the path of the present to make the present stumble.’’ In “The Box,’’ Grass’s children are heard to complain that their father cares for nothing but “what can be recounted’’ and that “he thinks purely in terms of the past’’; Marie’s pictures provide him with “a clearer image of the way things were in the past’’; his work is said to be “a plunge back in time.’’

But this immersion in the past is not a wholly unlimited one. The “father’’ — the Grass-figure in the narrative — admits that he “had left the really awful things, things that could hurt, in [Marie’s] darkroom or had cut up the negatives.’’ The camera’s gaze is not constant: It “turns away and is ashamed, it has that ability.’’

In the immediate context of “The Box,’’ these “awful things’’ are the troubled moments in Grass’s first marriage from which his family suffered. But this turning away of the camera also makes us think of Grass’s revelation in “Peeling the Onion’’ — that he had been a member of the SS in the final stages of the war.

The admission attracted criticism, not because Grass was directly implicated in wartime atrocities, but because he had kept it a secret. This was seen to be particularly damaging to a writer who had always sought to draw attention to those parts of German society that he did not feel had fully acknowledged the implications of their actions or their inaction during the Nazi years.

But “The Box’’ raises a broader question about the nature of Grass’s art. The children accuse their father of “lying.’’ This is not an accusation concerning exact statements of private fact, but a more deep-rooted criticism: His compulsion to tell tales is a form of lying.

The father maintains that without the camera he “would know less about his children . . . there would be no darkroom tales.’’ In the act of modifying the past, selecting details and excising others, and through the creation of fantastic scenes and stories, the writer comes to a form of knowledge that historical fact is incapable of finding.

“The Box’’ is not a defense of Grass’s art or an apology for it. The book offers rather an acknowledgement of the difficulties and paradoxes involved in the revelation of truth through fiction — an acknowledgment that acquires an extra weight and interest when made by an author much of whose work intricately meshes fact and fiction in an attempt to see the past and the present more clearly.

Since much of the narrative of “The Box’’ reflects on Grass’s previous work, it is not perhaps the best starting place for a reader new to his writing. And while its autobiographical themes are related with brilliant care and subtlety, readers of Grass should not expect a repeat of the panoramic reminiscences of “Peeling the Onion.’’ “The Box’’ offers instead the spectacle of a superb writer examining with playful seriousness and intelligent candor the relations between his work and the past.

Matthew Peters is a freelance critic who lives in Cambridge, England. He can be reached at peters.matthew91@yahoo.com.
