Harvard grad’s flawed memoir captures life as prison librarian

By Steve Almond
Globe Correspondent / November 28, 2010

Early in “Running the Books,’’ Avi Steinberg, a wayward Harvard grad and failed novelist, describes his job as a prison librarian: “I am living my (quixotic) dream: a book-slinger with a badge and a streetwise attitude, part bookworm, part badass. This identity has helped me tremendously at cocktail parties.’’

It’s the sort of glib introduction that made me hesitant to keep reading. Was this going to be another adventure in self-congratulatory shtick lit?

Thankfully, Steinberg’s debut memoir offers more than voyeuristic banter. In its best moments, the book is a freewheeling meditation on the nature of incarceration and a moving chronicle of a population that remains, by design, hidden from view.

That being said, Steinberg can be an exasperating narrator. He’s so eager to present the juicy material that he often races past the basic facts. We hear about his mugging at the hands of a former inmate, for instance, long before we learn what prison he actually works at (Bay State Correctional Center). And we never get a sense of where Bay State fits into the vast prison industrial complex. Steinberg also spends far too much time detailing his various petty feuds with correctional officers. The book is disorganized and at least 100 pages too long.

Still, Steinberg — a former yeshiva boy who spent high school obsessively studying the Torah — has a knack for incisive commentary.

Relating the fascinating history of Bay State, he observes, “As it ages and the condition of a prison worsens, as the place falls into severe disrepair, overcrowding, and neglect — as prisons always do — the more truly prison-like it becomes. As the structural facades of reform and hospital efficiency peel, leak, rust, and rot away, a truer prison emerges.’’

He also captures the poignant rituals by which inmates seek relief from the grinding loneliness and monotony of their lot. They swap urgent notes (known as “kites’’) using the pages of library books as their means of transport. Male and female inmates “skywrite’’ each other. “I’d spied male inmates standing at their barred windows, furiously signaling skyward,’’ Steinberg writes. “The signals involved large sweeping hand motions — formal, almost nautical — that I later learned were letters scripted backward. . . . Through the silent darkness of prison night, there were at least five conversations soaring back and forth.’’

Many of the sharpest insights come from the inmates themselves. One explains, with considerable awe, how Hasidic Jews are regarded as the “epitome of gangsta’’ because of their willingness to protect their turf. “Those dudes guarded their neighborhood by any means necessary.’ ’’

At one point, Steinberg falls under the spell of a loquacious pimp with the piquant handle “C.C. Too Sweet.’’ He even helps C.C. edit his memoir, which he quotes at length. (Reading these passages, I got a queasy sense that Steinberg was exploiting his charge, though it’s hard to imagine C.C. was ever going to land a publishing deal.)

In the great debate over whether incarceration is intended primarily to punish or rehabilitate, Steinberg clearly favors the latter approach. “The beauty of this job, if there was a beauty,’’ he writes, “was in giving people like C.C. a shot to do something right and do it well. To remind them that they’re more than criminals, if they choose to be.’’

This sounds great in theory — until Steinberg discovers the horrifying nature of C.C.’s crimes. “Should I write a letter to Miss Manners,’’ he wonders. “What’s the etiquette for breaking up with a pimp friend?’’

In fact, the struggle to navigate his emotional attachments to prisoners forms the emotional core of the book. He begins teaching creative writing classes in the hopes he can reach inmates through literature.

But he’s painfully aware that most criminals will never carry out the grandiose plans they craft in prison. Whatever good he can do for them as a literary emissary, he’s virtually helpless to change the course of their lives. One of his former students commits suicide. Another is fatally shot shortly after his release from prison. Both incidents devastate him.

In a sense, Steinberg has placed himself in danger, by violating one of the basic tenets of prison life. He’s gotten himself involved.

Prison is an institution in which even the smallest act of generosity — giving one of your loyal staffers a cupcake for his birthday, for example — is regarded as suspect.

“I took out the cupcake and handed it to him quickly. I was officially giving contraband to an inmate — contraband that could be sold in prison at three times the amount I’d paid for it. Immediately I felt nervous. Then I felt guilty for feeling nervous. Then I felt stupid for making such a dramatic show of anxiety over a pastry.’’

Steinberg does best in these small, human moments, when he resists the urge to play to balconies or crack wise. He tenders no final judgment on the American impulse toward incarceration.

Watching the quietest of his prison staffers shelving books, he offers a more humble lesson. “The gentle way he dusted and kept notes and piles, the care with which he arranged the shelves, his silence, made me appreciate how order is created,’’ Steinberg concludes. “Not through grand schemes — to which I was often drawn — but by small graceful actions, repeated often and refined with time.’’
