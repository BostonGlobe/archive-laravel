BOOK REVIEW

Chief Joseph, FDR, John McGraw had it

Leadership and its unteachable intangibles

By David M. Shribman
November 3, 2010

Barack Obama was a spectacular student, John Kennedy a middling one, Ulysses Grant a horrible one, and Abraham Lincoln wasn’t a student at all. Ronald Reagan was outgoing, Woodrow Wilson was introspective, John Adams was probably a misanthrope. Franklin Roosevelt came from a famous family, Richard Nixon from a modest family, Bill Clinton from a family led by a single mother.

It takes all kinds — not only for the presidency, but for leadership as well. Today many colleges have created courses in leadership, but in truth it cannot be taught and perhaps cannot be learned. Leaders are born and made, but not in class — and not because of class.

That lesson screams from the pages of “Profiles in Leadership,’’ an anthology of essays assembled by Walter Isaacson. It is a brisk, engaging examination of a dozen leaders in this country from George Washington, who almost certainly is on your list of great leaders, to John J. McGraw, a baseball player and manager you wouldn’t have thought to put on your list, to Pauli Murray, a lawyer and civil rights activist you may never have heard of but who certainly belongs in this pantheon.

Leadership has no specific set of ingredients but is composed of certain raw materials, most of them either natural or growing out of experience. And though we cannot learn leadership, we can learn from leaders, which is why this volume is so engaging and valuable.

Here we revisit Chief Joseph, a Nez Perce who was “a model of leadership — cagey and dogged, courageous, brilliant in a fight yet humane toward innocents in harm’s way, a great warrior who balanced determination with integrity,’’ and get reacquainted with evangelist Charles Grandison Finney, who made revivalism a tool for improvement. For him, prayer was not a part of politics, but he believed Christians had to be “useful in the highest degree possible in advancing God’s kingdom.’’

There is a superb essay by Annette Gordon-Reed, of Harvard Law School, on W.E.B. DuBois, who expressed leadership through the written word as well as through activism. “Because he believed that the doctrine of white supremacy was at the root of the problems blacks faced,’’ she writes, “he understood early on that racism was a global problem that had to be confronted at all levels.’’

FDR has long stood as the epitome of leadership, and so it is no surprise that he makes more than a cameo appearance in this volume, chiefly in an essay by Alan Brinkley, of Columbia University, who used the 32d president as a framework for viewing leadership. “The world,’’ Brinkley writes, “is a complicated and ever-changing place, and a great leader must be capable of adapting to change and understanding the diversity of the ideas and principles that shape history.’’

Agreed so far. But let’s add this codicil from Stanford professor David M. Kennedy’s opening of his essay on Dwight Eisenhower: “Few leaders are men for all seasons. The qualities that define an effective leader in one circumstance may be useless or even mischievous in another.’’

Stuck in the middle of these biographical sketches is a formidable essay by Robert Dallek, the presidential scholar who has taught, among other places, at Boston University and Dartmouth College. He writes about what happens when presidents become weak, and his argument is that many bring trouble on themselves. Their downfalls: “poor policy decisions or stumbling leadership, especially, but not exclusively, in response to foreign crises.’’ Proof, maybe not that history repeats itself, but surely evidence that leadership cannot be learned.
