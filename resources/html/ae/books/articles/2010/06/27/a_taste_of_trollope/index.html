A taste of Trollope

A sparkling modern comedy of manners for steamy season


Susan Wilson
Stephen McCauley offers a witty glimpse at the life of Richard Rossi, a 50-something man trying to achieve balance four years after a lover’s death. (Susan Wilson)
By Roberta Silman
June 27, 2010

This novel, Stephen McCauley’s sixth, is a delicious book, a perfect read for a summer afternoon. Once again McCauley gives us a witty take on gay life in the Boston environs in prose that positively twinkles and with a voice so engaging that you keep reading even as you suspect what is going to happen.

Actually, that’s part of the fun: The narrator, Richard Rossi, is sometimes a bit thick, and the reader often knows more than he does. This is not easy to do, and McCauley executes it superbly, thus giving this work a wonderful sharpness and intensity.

Even its organization is interesting, no real chapters, but short takes — with titles like “Is That All There Is?,’’ “Fitness Works?,’’ “Blameless and Dashing,’’ “How Come?,’’ “Tenderness,’’ “Routine,’’ “Slapped in the Face’’ — that sometimes move the plot but also go off on little tangents crucial to the story.

It is 2006, and Rossi, an attractive man in his 50s, is trying to achieve some balance four years after the death of his lover, Sam. Although his heart is really in English literature, especially Trollope (he is reading “He Knew He Was Right’’), he is stuck in a well-paying job in the human resources department of Connectrix, a computer company whose offices are in one of those ubiquitous glass boxes of the new century:

“In an attempt to create a semblance of privacy, almost everyone had odd arrangements of books and clothes and soshi screens and plants scattered around their fishbowl offices . . . [and] all the makeshift barriers made the interior look like a tenement in Brasilia.’’

In these offices are an amusing and sometimes pathetic mix — the snooping secretary who adores God, the self-serving boss, the spoiled employee, the paranoiac in a discrimination lawsuit, and several others whom McCauley presents with such precision that you have no trouble remembering who is who.

To escape his stultifying job and his present significant other, Conrad, Richard has become an exercise freak — with memberships at two gyms and appointments with a very funny personal trainer. More important, he owns a tiny studio apartment called The Club where he and his insignificant other, Benjamin, meet.

There is no way I am going to reveal any of McCauley’s plot. Suffice it to say that the dilemmas, concerns, and agonies that Richard endures with Conrad and Benjamin, his colleagues at the office, his sister, Beth, his memories of his parents, his friend, Jerry, his trainer, Walmi, and Conrad’s business partner, Doreen, could be ours. Indeed, they are ours, and, wonder of wonders, I identified almost completely with Richard. Except for one little quirk. It is received wisdom that most men think more about sex than most women and that gay men think more about sex than most men, but there were times when I felt that “the love that dares not speak its name” was wearing its delight with too much bragging, like wearing a purple jacket and a yellow ascot. No heterosexual narrator would need to say: “I said goodbye and shook Ben’s hand, which, considering what we’d just done upstairs, seemed perverse.”

But that is a small point. The people in this novel are as vivid as both our contemporaries and their Trollopian ancestors, and one of Richard’s most charming traits is how he rationalizes almost everything that frustrates him with an “At Least List,’’ e.g. “I might be going gray, but at least I’ve got hair.’’ “My face might look like a topographical map of the Andes, but at least I don’t have skin cancer.’’ “At least I don’t need glasses to see how I look.’’ “At least I don’t have to pretend to be interested in her plants and her feet anymore.’’ And when the denouement begins in an indoor golf course, of all places: “At least we’re not in love. . . . I might be in love with him, but at least I’ve never told him how I feel.’’

Then, a page later: “I told him how I felt about him, but at least we officially ended things. We officially ended things, but at least I told him how I felt about him.’’

You get the idea. But the joy is getting there, and one of the more specific joys of this book is watching someone take control of his life. When his friend Jerry says, “The funny part . . . is that the whole experience [of illness] has made me realize I’ve been worried about exactly the wrong things all along,” Richard is pulled back to memories of his beloved Sam, who said he was merely leaving the play before the third act. “And everyone knows the third act is often the weakest part of the show.” But if you’re lucky enough to stick around for it, Richard thinks, “The trick would be to find the beauty in the otherwise unattractive aspects of aging.” Hoorah, I wanted to shout. What a pleasure it is to know that Richard has not only pondered life’s mysteries, but also grown and changed and can cope with whatever awaits him.

Roberta Silman is the author of a short-story collection and three novels and has recently completed a new novel, “Secrets and Shadows.’’ She can be reached at rsilman@verizon.net.
