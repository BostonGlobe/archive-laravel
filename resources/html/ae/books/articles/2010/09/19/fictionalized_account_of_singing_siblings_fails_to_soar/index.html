Sounding a false note

Fictionalized account of singing siblings fails to soar

By Alec Solomita
Globe Correspondent / September 19, 2010

Rick Bass’s novel “Nashville Chrome” is a fictionalized biography of the Browns, real-life singing siblings who, after a number of country-pop crossover hits in the 1950s, fell into obscurity. Unlike Gore Vidal’s “Lincoln” or the biopic “Walk the Line,” “Nashville Chrome” is not historical fiction. Nor is the novel, like Virginia Woolf’s “Orlando,” a crafty mélange of the historical and the unabashedly whimsical. Bass rearranges and fabricates events, chronology, and even characters. The result is a truly strange literary hybrid that fails to thrive.

The novel’s structure is straightforward enough. The Brown children’s rise and fall, beginning during the Depression in rural Arkansas, where their alcoholic father runs a sawmill, is related by an omniscient narrator; these chapters are interspersed with others from the point of view of an aging Maxine Brown, reminiscing sometimes sweetly, more often bitterly, about her life as the trio’s oldest sibling.

But within this structure, fact and fiction puddle into each other in a way that seriously undermines the narrative. Bass says he doesn’t want the facts to get in the way of the truth in his effort to “portray the emotional truths of their journey” and to meditate on the nature of fame. In theory, this is clever and comprehensible. In practice, his contrivances jolt the reader out of the suspension of disbelief requisite to enjoying most fiction. For instance, Bass elevates a rather ordinary group of singers to demigods: “The Browns’ tempered harmony was soothing, and it healed, for a little while, the wounds of whoever listened to it.” “[E]veryone who heard them loved them, would love them forever.” “The sound, once unleashed into the world, flowing downhill, spreading and pooling. Powerful, beautiful, treacherous, unmanageable.” Strong talk for performers whose songs include “Big Daddy” and “Pop a Top.”

Bass also exaggerates the group’s influence. The Browns repeatedly outsell their friend Elvis Presley; they have more No. 1 hits than anyone else; the Beatles travel to Nashville to learn harmony from them. If The Browns were completely fictional, this manipulation would, perhaps, make sense by expediting a postmodern reverie on fame in America; but characterizing this bland, successful little family as the font of all great country music, the inspiration of the Beatles, the nourishment of Elvis, turns out to be more mystifying than mythical.

Bass’s factitious story is not helped by the prevalence of a slovenly verb tense and the wildly inconsistent voice of the third-person narrator, folksy one moment, Nutrasweet spiritual the next, and distantly analytical the next. Much of the book is told in the nonspecific conditional, a tense appropriate for setting a scene or mood. “Once daily the sawmill operators would shut down for lunch in the rising heat of the day. The engines would fall silent and songbirds back at the shadowy edge of the forest would resume their calls.” This lyric evocation is welcome in small doses but does nothing to move a story or illuminate character. And then there are other voices, including one that went to college and studied sociology: “The promoters of Graceland hold a major festival each August . . . They celebrate everything about him, from the sweet country boy he was to the bloated extrapolation of insatiable American appetite and surface showmanship that he became.” A true enough, if commonplace, observation, but who, the reader cries out, has just come on the scene?

The Browns are themselves an oddity. Their earliest songs, “Looking Back to See,” I’ll Take the Chance,” and a few others, are charming and pleasant. Very soon, however, pleasant became bland and bland became cloying, culminating in 1959 with their biggest hit “The Three Bells,” a song so sappy that some sufferers couldn’t get it out of their heads until “MacArthur Park” came out in 1968.

Bass writes, “The Browns’ voices were shiny and elegant, and utterly controlled; the spirit of their voices had the Appalachian hillbilly music as its rootstock, but without the nasal whine and twang.” This is, if immoderate, accurate — the good stuff, “the nasal whine and twang,” is absent, and the smoothness of their sound is the musical equivalent of Woody Allen’s mother running “a chicken through the de-flavorizor.”

Fatally, Bass’s grail — “emotional truth” — is compromised out of deference to the still-living subjects of his novel. As he ingenuously admits in his acknowledgements at the end of the book: The Browns “are not perfect — no more or no less than anyone else — but it might well serve a reader in the delineations between novelistic excess and ‘regular’ life to view anything distasteful or negative in these pages as only an exercise of the novelist’s imagination . . . rather than true-life revelation of flaws or failings in the Browns or their family.” This sentence holds a world of disappointment for a lover of literature. To say a person is “not perfect — no more or no less than anyone else” describes the antithesis of a novelist’s job. The “more or less than anyone else” is what he is supposed to be occupied with discovering and communicating. And to tiptoe around the “flaws or failings” of your subjects is appropriate to a eulogy not a novel. Bass has sacrificed the “nasal whine and twang” of real life, a disservice to his characters as well as his readers.

Alec Solomita, a fiction writer and critic living in Somerville, can be reached at alecsolomita@ymail.com.
