A Reading Life

I’ll hate myself in the morning

By Katherine A. Powers
Globe Correspondent / September 5, 2010

Most readers have an appetite for some genre of which they are not exactly proud. I asked a couple of dozen friends and acquaintances what theirs were, and here is what I came up with: Thrillers figured large, among them Stieg Larsson’s sanctimonious revenge fantasies and “chick mysteries.’’ Online reading showed up many times (“I come away with wormholes in my head’’), but I’ll stick to bound volumes. One person confessed to nutrition and weight-loss books and another to the memoirs of rock-band groupies (“can’t put them down’’). Young-adult fiction, super-hero comic books, accounts of disasters and animal attacks, guides to music and movies (“that tell me what I should like, thus saving me the trouble of thinking for myself’’), all there, all excellently discreditable. Obviously I can’t say how much economy of truth was exercised, but a number of self-secure people claimed to approve of their own taste in all their reading (“If I like it, it must be fantastic’’).

The reason for snooping into the debased proclivities of others is that I have just finished wallowing in the sort of reading that I can’t stand myself for liking as much as I do: books by and about the Mitford sisters. For the most part, these works appeal to my pathetic Anglophilia and are completely out of accord with my sense of myself as a fair-minded, democratic American. In fact, with the exception of Jessica (and not always her), the Mitfords and all their ways pretty much make me sick — their birth and bearing, their cloying private argot, their boorish snobbery treasured as a pearl without price.

Just in case you have not gone a-Mitfording much — or at all — let me set them before you. The sisters and their one brother sprang from the reactionary loins of Lord and Lady Redesdale of Northumberland. From youngest to eldest they are Deborah, who married the duke of Devonshire; Jessica, a member of the Communist Party, civil rights activist, muckraker, and memoirist; Unity, an ardent supporter of Hitler who shot herself in the head when Britain declared war on Germany; Diana, another bad ’un who married a Guinness but left him for Sir Oswald Mosley, head of the British Union of Fascists; Thomas, a fascist sympathizer who died in the war; Pamela, a country-dwelling chicken fancier; and Nancy.

Novelist, biographer, and social commentator, Nancy is best known in the last respect for her glossary of “U and non-U’’ usage in her 1954 essay, “The English Aristocracy.’’ This comprised a list of words and expressions that distinguish the upper class (notably, the Mitfords) from the middle class. The latter, for instance, bluntly say “house,’’ “die,’’ and “graveyard,’’ while the former, in their genteelness, say “home,’’ “pass on,’’ and “cemetery.’’ It is an unsavory exhibition of condescension; nonetheless, it expresses in a bald way what Nancy Mitford’s novels portray with a wicked wit that I find irresistible. For, at the heart of U and non-U usage is the comic poignancy of middle-class people aspiring to rise, but believing that the more prim the manners, the more euphemistic the locution, the higher the status. Mitford’s novels have the radioactive glow of this and other species of social misunderstanding; and, unkind though they undoubtedly are, they are very, very funny.

Five of Nancy Mitford’s novels have just been republished as Vintage paperbacks ($14.95 each). They include her two most popular ones, “The Pursuit of Love’’ and “Love in a Cold Climate,’’ as well as my favorite of all her novels, “The Blessing.’’ First published in 1951, it begins toward the start of World War II when Grace Allingham, engaged to an Englishman, suddenly marries Charles-Edouard de Valhubert, an aristocratic Frenchman. He bears her off to France, where they have a child, Sigi (“the Blessing’’). Despite not-so-veiled warnings from a number of people, Grace doesn’t quite grasp that she can expect her husband to have mistresses: He is a Frenchman after all. Though the couple’s divergent understanding in this matter fuels the plot, the novel is filled with far funnier though subtler instances of cultural incomprehension.

Eddies of solecism ripple through the story, as when the French hold forth on England, their knowledge of that country being “very much like the knowledge some astronomer might have of the moon after regarding it for many a long night through a telescope.’’ For its part, the English view of the French is marvelously expressed by Nanny, Sigi’s caretaker, a vigorous and awesome incarnation of Britannia, disgusted with everything Froggish, especially the food (“nobody there can cook’’) and the lack of “decent railings’’ at the Tuileries Gardens. The most grotesque misapprehensions of both France and England, however, belongs to an American, Hector Dexter, a self-satisfied, oblivious bore of the first water, a triumph of jargonistic logorrhea.

I shall leave the fiendish maneuverings that make up the plot of “The Blessing’’ to your own joyful discovery and turn now to Jessica Mitford. She is the sister I find easiest to take as a person. Her excellent collection “Poison Penmanship: The Gentle Art of Muckraking’’ has just been republished (New York Review Books, paperback, $15.95). I can’t help thinking that it was a distaste for upper-class euphemism and niceness that drew this Mitford to her most famous subjects: the funeral, prison, and obstetrics industries. Indeed, in her valuable introduction to the book, Mitford extols the virtues of trade magazines, written as they are with no regard for public sentimentality or squeamishness.

A number of essays here concern the funeral business and how her exposé affected her life, making her name synonymous with death and cheap funerals. (“A total stranger came up to me in a dress shop and with a knowing wink asked, ‘Are you shopping for a shroud?’ ’’) Other selections include witty and trenchant pieces on the Famous Writers School, a shameful scam; the censoring of the subject of syphilis by NBC in 1964; race relations in the American South in the mid-1960s; a fat farm, “Egyptomania,’’ and touring the U.S.A.: “When it comes to dining along the American highway, the connoisseur of descriptive writing is likely to fare better than the gourmet.’’

Katherine A. Powers lives in Cambridge. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@verizon.net.

Correction: Because of an editing error, a listing of expressions that Nancy Mitford links to the upper class in her essay “The English Aristocracy’’ was incorrectly attributed to the middle class and vice versa. According to Mitford, members of the upper class say “house,’’ “die,’’ and “graveyard,’’ while the middle class, trying to affect a more genteel tone, use “home,’’ “pass on,’’ and “cemetery.’’
