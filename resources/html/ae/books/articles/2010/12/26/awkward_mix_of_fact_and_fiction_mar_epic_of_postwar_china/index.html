Awkward mix of fact and fiction mar epic of postwar China

By Anna Mundow
Globe Correspondent / December 26, 2010

“Under Fishbone Clouds,” the first novel by Sam Meekings, a Scot living in China, opens with a fairy tale, wonderfully told. “In a small border town huddled at the furthest reaches of the northern province, there was an old teahouse. It was winter there, thousands of years ago.” An impoverished old man who appears at the teahouse door is given shelter. He repays the owner by sketching on his wall five stately cranes standing in water. The following night, when a visiting musician plays, the cranes come to life. The drama that follows, like many myths, is a parable of kindness and greed. It is also a lyrical prelude to a frequently brutal epic that spans almost seven decades of modern Chinese history.

The transition from mythical to quotidian is swift. Having charmed us with the dancing cranes, Meekings abruptly drops us into a city in modern China. An old woman is returning home from the hospital where her husband lies dying. As she walks, a childhood story arrives unbidden in her exhausted mind. “The cranes represented karma,” Yuying recalls of the childhood fairy tale. “Everyone gets what they deserve in the end. Yet after all she had been through, Yuying was not sure that life was ever that simple.”

The city’s labyrinthine streets seem to echo this thought as Yuying trudges reluctantly “to her daughter’s third-floor flat near the massage alley instead of wandering on towards the courtyards and houses guarded by stone lions, deeper into the past.”

The past, of course, is where we are going, but with frequent detours to the mythical realm. Our guide is the Kitchen God, a plainspoken spirit who has made a bet with the omnipotent Jade Emperor that he, the Kitchen God, can reveal “the workings of the human heart.” To do so, he follows the lives of Yuying and her husband, Jinyi, from their first meeting to their final separation.

Meekings has selected such a dense period of Chinese history that his choice of a god as narrator seems sensible. Who better, after all, to survey the tumult of decades?

It is the smaller details, however, not the novel’s epic sweep or mythical digressions that quicken the narrative. On the day of her arranged marriage in 1946, for example, 17-year-old Yuying observes, “As they raised the clear liquid in the ornamental thimbles several of the older guests already appeared red-faced and sweaty. These were her father’s associates . . . those few who had been in collusion with the Japanese, now denying everything.”

Such asides vividly illuminate the murky atmosphere of postwar China while the novel’s historical facts, by contrast, are often crudely grafted onto the narrative. “It had taken a rebel general’s desperate action back in 1936 to provide a united front against the Japanese as they began to spread out from the puppet-state of Manchuria,” Meekings informs us. “For this patriotic action, Zhang Xueliang, was arrested and imprisoned by the Kuomintang for the next forty years.” These facts are part of a conversation that young Jinyi and a workmate are having about China’s civil war, but they nonetheless dilute the immediate sense of place and time that Meekings so effectively creates. The balance between fact and imagination is rarely achieved by Meekings.

Repeatedly in “Under Fishbone Clouds,’’ the reader, like the Kitchen God, is granted a lofty perspective on the interminable struggles of Yuying, Jinyi, and their families, but the characters shrink in the viewing. At other times, history itself is subjected to the god-like gaze. “[T]his is the way the house of history is built,” the Kitchen God observes of 1930s China, “and you are already locked inside. The door has no key, and what you thought windows are simply finely drawn pictures, blurred from the touch of too many fingers.”

The trials of the main characters begin when Jinyi insists that his young bride, Yuying, and their baby son return with him to his family’s rural home. Their trek across a famine-stricken land and the realities of rural life are portrayed without lyricism or sentiment. “There are two countrysides,” Meekings writes as Yuying and Jinyi stumble away from their tiny son’s grave. “One people carry with them, carved from the words of books read in the safety of well-lit houses; the other exists beyond people, and its words are indecipherable howls, its language that of barks and bracken and vine. It is the second people face, if they face the world at all.”

This calamitous episode sets the tone for the novel’s subsequent upheavals, the most memorable revealing the human drama at the heart of the Long March, for example. “They were walking backwards into the future,” Meekings writes of the latter, “a few ragged mules, single-file on mountain tracks, and men with typewriters and bond-bending munitions balanced on their backs.”

Through it all, the Kitchen God observes the human heart and draws some conclusions about love, endurance, and collective insanity. “If a whole country believed that it could rip apart four thousand years of history in an instant and begin again, then who could stop it?’’ the deity observes about the Chinese Revolution. “[M]illions of teenagers in identical shirts and caps line up in Tiananmen Square, wetting themselves for a glimpse of an old man waving his short, dumpy hands from a shaky podium.”
