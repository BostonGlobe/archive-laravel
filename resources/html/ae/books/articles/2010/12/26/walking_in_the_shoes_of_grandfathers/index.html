A Reading Life

Walking in the shoes of grandfathers

By Katherine A. Powers
Globe Correspondent / December 26, 2010

Joseph O’Neill is the author best known for “Netherland,’’ a novel set in a New York traumatized by the attacks of Sept. 11, 2001. A bestseller, it went on to win last year’s PEN/Faulkner Award for Fiction and was short-listed for this year’s International IMPAC Dublin Literary Award. It is that book’s success that, no doubt, brings the republication of “Blood-Dark Track: A Family History’’ (Vintage, paperback, $15.95). Originally published in 2001, this strange and enthralling exercise in memoir, empathy, and historical sleuthing is an account of the author’s inquiry into the lives of his two grandfathers, one Turkish, one Irish, both imprisoned by the British during World War II.

“In general,’’ writes O’Neill, “I’m as content as the next man to proceed on the footing that any information of importance — anything that has a bearing on my essential interests — will be brought to my attention by those entrusted with such things: families, schools, news agencies, subversives.’’ As these pages show, much of the past — a family’s, a community’s, a nation’s, the world’s — simply disappears from human cognizance: Certain incidents seem irrelevant at the time, others are lost to a silence connived at. O’Neill was 10 or 11 when he learned that both his grandfathers had been incarcerated during the war, but he was 30 before he gave any real thought to “the curious parallelism’’ of their lives. The book details his ferreting out of the facts of both cases and his own “slow, idiotic awakening’’ to the bearing of historical circumstance on these men and their outlook.

At the most basic level, O’Neill sets about figuring out what actually happened, the sequence of events and involvements that led to his grandfathers’ imprisonment. The author’s Irish father, Kevin O’Neill, met his future wife while working in Mersin, a small Turkish port on the Mediterranean. This was Lina, daughter of Joseph Dakad, a Christian of Syrian stock, an enterprising hotelier, small businessman, and trader — characteristics that taken together marked him as a “Levantine,’’ a distrusted outsider in Turkey, a fact that probably contributed to his future travails.

In 1942, attempting to return to Turkey from a buying trip to Palestine, he was arrested by the British under suspicion of spying for the Germans, and was eventually imprisoned in an internment camp for three years. He was never charged and his incarceration constituted a grueling limbo of uncertainty, deprivation, and physical hardship. He emerged broken in health and darkened in mind. He died in 1964, the day after the birth of the author, who was named after him.

O’Neill’s Irish grandfather, Jim O’Neill, was from West Cork — a region where the past is ever present — from a family riven by bad blood over the ownership of land and scarred by the murder of two members during the early Troubles. A member of the IRA for years, Jim O’Neill was a hard man whose hard life, his grandson says, had developed in him “a character and outlook calculated, first and foremost, to withstand and reduce the world.’’ Interned in Ireland in 1940 and not released for four years, he would never speak about what he experienced there, though his grandson gathers the grim and demoralizing details elsewhere. The interning of Irishmen by Irishmen was only one of the distasteful, hushed-up episodes that marked the Emergency, as the war years were called in neutral Ireland.

At another level, O’Neill tries to understand, even to experience, the two men’s sense of reality by going to the places they were born, lived, worked, and incarcerated. In the case of his Irish grandfather, this included visiting the stretch of river from which he poached salmon, conjuring up “the danger and awful thrill of it,’’ and the lawless spirit that the landed Protestant upper class provoked in many Catholic Irish people. He also visits the relatives of Vice Admiral Henry Boyle Townshend Somerville, a victim of an IRA murder in 1936, a crime in which his grandfather either did or did not participate — a matter settled, stunningly, by book’s end.

Finally, O’Neill’s inquiry is a moral one and concerns itself with the makeup of conscience and character. He wishes to understand whether the men were culpable, whether Joseph Dakad was indeed a spy for the Germans and why he would be if he were. Later, as his historical understanding deepens, he investigates the nature of Dakad’s silence on the persecution, expulsion, and slaughter of Armenians, which he clearly witnessed. On the Irish side, the author wants to know whether Jim O’Neill was a cold-blooded killer. He ruminates on whether one generation has an obligation to previous ones for their sacrifices and on the nature of victimhood.

These cogitations are the least successful aspects of this otherwise extraordinarily acute and rich book. O’Neill’s real triumph is in conveying that, as he puts it, “historical dark matter surrounded and manoeuvred my grandfathers more than they ever knew or, perhaps, could have known.’’

“Exiles,’’ Michael J. Arlen’s memoir of his parents, originally published in 1970, has just reappeared (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, paperback, $15). The author’s father, a celebrated novelist, was the son of an Armenian merchant. His mother, the daughter of a Greek count, spent her formative years in island exile. In marrying, the two seemed to be embarked on a union in which everything was possible: “My father, so successful, full of energy, in command of things. My mother, so seemingly established — a title even, money somewhere in the background, big houses, gardens, furniture, wax for the furniture, polish for the silver, manners, style, and more than style or manners, a seeming feel for independence, for being a “new woman.’’

It didn’t work out. The book is an impressionistic portrait of an emotional landscape, deftly drawn, striking in its imagery, and subtle in its suggestion of the disappointment and betrayal that ultimately blighted these lives. Arlen summons brilliant scenes that distill the unhappy way in which the past and the promise of the new malfunctioned. One will suffice: Here is his mother’s grandmother’s salon, a room filled with ancient artifacts, “marble and porcelain and velvet,’’ and “there off to one side, built into the wall, one of the first automatic record-changers, a marvelous untamed contraption consisting of a gramophone turntable and a sort of obscene metallic hand, a hand, that whirred and clanked and extended slowly out of the wall, and grabbed the record, and then usually dashed it to pieces against the floor.’’
