BOOK REVIEW

Dame West's `Mexico' is still rich

By Richard Dyer, Globe Staff, 12/3/2003

"Survivors in Mexico"; By Rebecca West; Edited and introduced by Bernard Schweizer; Yale University Press, 264 pp.; $26.95.)

ADVERTISEMENT

Dame Rebecca West (1892-1983) lived nearly as long as the century she chronicled with such vigor and passion in novels, nonfiction, and journalism. She lived a flamboyant life yet found the time and discipline to become one of the best writers of her time -- and one of the most prolific. Since West's death, scholars have continued to pore through her archives and publish additional works. New books have continued to appear as regularly as they did during her lifetime, including at least five novels, a collection of letters, and a volume of early writings.

Now they are joined by "Survivors in Mexico," all that remains of a great book that she wanted to write but lacked the stamina and circumstances to complete.

In 1966, the editors of The New Yorker magazine invited West to go to Mexico to interview the grandson of Leon Trotsky, one of the leaders of the Russian revolution. West did, in fact, meet Seva Trotsky, but she never wrote the article. Instead she began an impressive meditation on Mexican history and the national character, probably thinking along the same spacious lines of her 1,200-page masterpiece about Yugoslavia, "Black Lamb and Grey Falcon," published in 1941. Like that great book, "Survivors in Mexico" is part memoir, part travelogue, part history, part journalism, part analysis as her mind ranged over topics her presence in Mexico and her voracious reading suggested to her.

By 1966, however, West was 74, and her husband Henry Andrews was in failing health; caring for him became a major preoccupation. After Andews died in 1968, West traveled again to Mexico, and at various times fussed over what she had already written -- some passages exist in many alternate drafts. Ultimately she gave up on the project because there were other things that she wanted to do more, such as complete the trilogy of novels that had begun with her most radiant book, "The Fountain Overflows."

"Survivors in Mexico" is therefore only a fragment, or series of fragments, pieces in a larger design we can only begin to discern, but we can be grateful that Bernard Schweizer has edited the various versions of the text and has published the result, which runs to 205 pages, supplemented by an appendix of three reviews by West of books on Mexican subjects, an introduction, notes, and an index.

What remains is characteristically shrewd, tough, amusing, compassionate, and committed, and much of it is sumptuously written. Among the subjects she tackles are the painters Frida Kahlo and Diego Rivera; the assassination of Trotsky; Mexican history and politics, ancient and modern and their interaction with European history and politics; Mexican art, culture, and clothing; anarchism; and, of course, survival. "The history of mankind is a history of more or less controlled schizophrenia," West observes.

Her range of reference is staggering. In describing ancient statues she writes, "There is the Lord of the Flowers, patron of love and dancing and games, sitting cross-legged and beautiful as Cassius Clay, and as bitchy about this beauty, but also an angel. Intimations of reality as remote as float on Rilke's lines are here made concrete." She describes Puvis de Chavannes, a French ancestor of the Mexican muralists, "as the reconstructor of an antique world populated by wan nudes who had evidently been boiled for soup."

History is past but also permanently present. "We can grasp Montezuma's position only if we imagine the British and American governments of today faced with phenomena which might be either a hostile expeditionary force of spacemen or the second coming of Christ."

Every paragraph holds a plum, but after a while the style does not draw attention to itself but to insight and morality; human frailty sometimes amuses her, but her indignation is mighty.

Of the misdeeds of King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella she writes: "They were entering their forties, a time when men and women should have committed enough sins to be afraid of further guilt and developed the intelligence to dodge it."

It's a good thing that new writings of Rebecca West continue to appear; we still need her, permanently.
