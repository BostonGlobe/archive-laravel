The value of hypocrisy, pretense, and fraud. Honestly.

By Carlo Wolff, 12/21/2003

Faking It
By William Ian Miller
Cambridge University, 290 pp., $29

ADVERTISEMENT
 

William Ian Miller, a professor of law who is Jewish and who suggests that his Scottish middle name gives him scholastic probity, scratches the itch of authenticity and relieves the ache of morality with delicious determination in ''Faking It."

Miller's inquiry into the selves we cart around, hide behind, arrange, and deploy is as toothsome as a Sacher torte. ''Faking It" is a heavy serving, however. I'm not as familiar with the Bible and Renaissance literature as I should be to grasp the full Miller. He seems to have read everything, tying together La Rochefoucauld, Chaucer, Hume, and Erving Goffman, thinkers from different periods and sensibilities, with an ease attesting to a fine teaching style. (A professor of law at the University of Michigan Law School, Miller also has written books about courage and disgust.)

''The terrain of faking it is vast," he writes. ''Virtually all social interaction and much psychic life lie within its bounds."

Miller uses aphorism, maxim, and anecdote, personalizing his ruminations to steer clear of academic slog. ''Giving grand significance to things that cannot bear the weight of the pretense is one of the most common forms of fakery academics engage in," Miller preaches. He doesn't practice it, however.

Miller doesn't have a ''point"; this book, ''best seen as a quasi-novel," is nonlinear. It ranges wide, the unruliness of Miller's observation tempered by the rigor of his interpretation. He also mitigates the psychological discomfort he might engender with self-deprecating, revelatory humor. It's unusual to find a book about ''moral psychology" funny. It's also a relief.

By the end, when Miller comes to qualified terms with authenticity, you might feel reassured, but such reassurance comes only after chapter upon chapter dealing with praise, flattery, hypocrisy (and its grounding in religious observance), prayer, pretense, the multiple meanings of ''craft," the troubling ambiguities of love, and the value of artificiality.

If there's reassurance, there's also roil. Take this interior colloquy about breast implants, one of many topics that author Miller uses to give his discourse a contemporary feel.

''Would you not feel a little bit betrayed by a guy taking Viagra or a woman with breast implants? Wouldn't a guy, in the latter case, feel embarrassed if he got aroused by them knowing that they were fake? He, however, would have a response to that: 'Fake? What's fake and what's real? I have been reading the fathers of the church and various monastic writers, and they have always suggested that even so-called real breasts are fake, a false appearance, for all they are is skin covering disgusting goo, mostly fat. Would a bowl of fat turn you on? No, it is only the location and the skin over the fat that transforms the fat into a breast. So what is wrong with skin over silicon or salt water? Rather better in fact.' "

Part of what makes this book endearing is Miller's personality. Permit me to follow his example and shed objectivity for a moment. When a friend told me he was engaged to a woman with implants, underlining the information with testimony about the couple's sexual pleasure, all I could say was I was pleased for him. I didn't tell him that what he said made me squeamish; our friendship caged my bluntness. Besides, I pride myself on my tolerance (maybe that's my rationalization for what I consider cowardice in this situation). Miller's book -- a tonic for worrywarts -- gives me an opportunity to come clean.

To me, there's something untoward about breast implants, Botox, Rogaine, and the California governorship of Arnold Schwarzenegger, an Austrian bodybuilder-actor who seems to be leaving one area deeply involving role-playing for another. All strike me as commodifying, objectifying, dehumanizing.

To me, authenticity denotes quality and durability. You get what you pay for. You trust what you touch. Miller is a Midwesterner who respects the mundane. I suspect he harbors similar feelings, even as he brings the very definition of feeling into question. His discussion of politeness, behavior that can hamper truth, is maddeningly on its ever-shifting target:

''How do we get our attempts at politeness understood as polite rather than as an offense? Should I use a lot of cuss words talking to this working-class guy? Does it make any difference if I am pretty naturally foul-mouthed anyway? If he is cussing, is that a sign of his refusal to make any concessions to my class and education, or is he too dim to know the rules, or is it his way of outmachoing me? Or is it that he is engaging in that heavy male refusal to engage in the niceties of feminized politeness, a refusal that is itself a form of manly politeness? No, no, try not to talk about basketball with the black guy, but then what if he brings it up? Is he doing that because he is graciously conceding that that is all we might have in common? Or is he parodying some role he expects I will expect him to play, thus making a fool out of me? How is one to avoid self-consciousness in this setting, when not faking it can itself be so easily construed as fake?"

Such questions could freeze us in our own frames. Miller's talent, peculiar and bracing, is to analyze them so thoroughly that one vaults over skepticism to clarity.

Carlo Wolff is a freelance writer from Cleveland.
