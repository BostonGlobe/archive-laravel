A long, strange trip

A young man's drug-enhanced odyssey is filtered through his own fiction

By Sandra Shea, 12/21/2003

Call Me the Breeze
By Patrick McCabe
337 pp., $24.95

ADVERTISEMENT
 

Novelist Patrick McCabe has little hope for lasting peace in Ireland, mainly because he is preoccupied with the most difficult kind to achieve: the personal peace that eternally eludes a damaged soul.

McCabe has been aptly called by one reviewer ''the high priest of rural Irish dementia," and given his lyric powers, the impulse to invoke James Joyce is hard to avoid, but in his latest novel, ''Call Me the Breeze," we hear Joyce channeled through a postmodern, poetic outlaw sensibility of, say, Eminem.

McCabe is best known for his Booker Prize-nominated ''The Butcher Boy," in which he became master of creating broken, brutal, but charismatic characters; in ''Butcher Boy," it was Francie Brady, a charming juvenile homicidal maniac. In his next Booker nod, ''Breakfast on Pluto," it came in the form of Patrick ''Pussy" Braden, a transvestite prostitute who finds herself at the center of the IRA terrorist bombings of London in the '70s, but has a harder time finding her own center.

In ''Breeze," McCabe's newest antihero is Joey Tallon, who latches on to blow-up dolls, drugs, and highly wrought delusions of love and fame in his search for peace.

His story begins as a fragmented memoir that shifts back and forth over two decades, and includes journal entries, novel excerpts, and fantasies. The result, at least in the first third of the book, is a kind of stream of unconsciousness, a somewhat chaotic narrative that keeps the reader slightly off balance -- not only because Joey is enchanted with LSD (and its required syllabus, Hesse and Castaneda), but because it's immediately clear that much of his life is a figment of his imagination.

Joey is obsessed with Jacy, a pretty blonde who arrives in his small North Ireland town from America. As he worships ''The Jace" from afar, Joey concocts an ongoing fiction about their mystical union, complete with a soundtrack of Dylan, Joni Mitchell, and J. J. Cale, the blues-rocker responsible for the book's eponymous song. Just as these unrequited fantasies threaten to become tiresome, McCabe begins dropping hints of the darkness to come, as Joey starts to talk about his ''preparations" for the ''Karma Cave" and steps up his screenings of ''Taxi Driver," Travis Bickle poses, and references to Charles Manson.

''I had a little garden now beside the caravan and I tended it every day. . . . I thought of it as being a shrine of sorts, in honour of what Charles Manson might have been. The problem with Charlie was that he had made the wrong choices."

Joey, too, makes a few wrong choices. But since this is a novel in which a peace rally gets bombed, we shouldn't be surprised that it's in prison where the hero ultimately finds freedom. Thanks to a warden who encourages his writing, Joey quickly comes into his own by establishing a library and literary club with his daft sidekick, Bonehead. The Mountjoy Literary Society begins promisingly, despite a small problem: The prisoners, Bonehead informs Joey, are '' 'cutting pictures out them special books to take back to the cell to [expletive] with. It's not good enough, Joey!' Then he shows me this book of Rembrandt plates and shakes his head, disgusted."

Throughout ''Breeze," Joey's interior monologue is at once amusing, exasperating, and heartbreaking in its self-deception. But this level of self-deception in a character has only one of two places to go: eventual enlightenment or an even higher level of delusion. It will be no surprise to followers of McCabe that he opts for the latter, and this is where the novel grows giddy and hilarious, as his delusions suddenly transform Joey into a filmmaker, erstwhile politician, and, finally, a wildly successful novelist. McCabe comes bitingly close to suggesting that these occupations require more brilliance at self-delusion than at anything else.

''Breeze" has reverberations of what Kingsley Amis might have created if he'd chosen to send up the faux-mysticism of '70s music and drug culture. But McCabe is not interested in farce. While Joey's delusions of success are so extreme they include, on the eve of the election for a council seat, a dream of his own JFK-like assassination, in the end, he's nastily returned to terra firma by an act of brutality, the kind of local, pitiless savagery Joey has shaped his existence around trying to escape.

The structure of the novel is not always easy to follow, especially in the beginning, when Joey's combined diet of meat pies and lysergic acid diethylamide is driving the narrative bus; but later, Joey's account becomes more sharply focused as McCabe reveals just how grim and violent the reality of his life is. Most of this reality is handled lightly by McCabe, although occasionally, he describes it in stunningly beautiful language: ''Even flipping through it is enough to make the gloominess start insinuating itself again. You almost go into a foetal crouch as you turn each weighted and weary page, with talk of nothing -- only strikes and abortion referenda, rain and misery in a country that seemed ruined."

Joey's optimism, his overbright view of the world, makes him likable, but since he rarely allows himself to come close to the howling edge of extreme despair of McCabe's earlier characters, this makes him less compelling and, oddly, less human. We expect little redemption for any of McCabe's characters; Joey's blindness to reality may be why we're slightly less interested, by the end of ''Breeze," in his failure to find it.

Sandra Shea is an editorial writer for the Philadelphia Daily News and author of ''The Realm of Secondhand Souls."
