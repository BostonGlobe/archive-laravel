A READING LIFE

From topgallants to thrills, an authentic voyage

By Katherine Powers, 12/21/2003

I went to see the movie "Master and Commander: The Far Side of the World" filled with the novel reader's dread that it would be a travesty of the books upon which it is based and that, once again in life, I would find myself marching out of a movie house, mid-presentation, bursting with indignation and grand affront. But, through the work of some miracle, the film is brilliant and, except for Paul Bettany miscast as Stephen Maturin, perfectly obedient to Patrick O'Brian's sensibilities and vision.

There are, however, some people, victims of literary snobbishness and a creeping piety of soul, who do not know how to have a good time. A dozen years ago I knew of any number of this sort, who, often without having read them, dismissed O'Brian's novels as mere generic naval action dramas. Somehow, and certainly silently, many of them came around when it became intellectually fashionable to admire O'Brian's work -- about the time the author appeared at the 92d Street Y in New York in 1994. Now, today, the same sort of people cannot countenance the movie.

Stanley Kauffmann, film critic for The New Republic, has, for a wonder, never departed from the principled position of dismissing O'Brian's novels without reading them ("feeling that this particular niche in my literary experience had been well filled by C. S. Forester"). The same jaded disdain informs his view of the movie, which he calls an "old-time sea-faring epic," and wonders how we can "be concerned with such a blatantly romantic view of war." Unless Kauffmann saw a version of the movie that left out the death, mutilation, and blood that transformed a friend of mine into a protesting heap in her seat beside me, he is being intentionally misleading and ill willed.

Ill will and condescension appear to be at the heart of Jason Epstein's carping consideration of the movie in The New York Times. Here is a fellow who has (he claims) read the novels, and, indeed, Kauffman calls him an "expert in O'Brian" who has "defended the author against the film," caring "so much about O'Brian that he must speak."

That's a little more like it: The punctilious bookman takes on the desecration of literature -- and does so, first, by waxing epistemological. According to Epstein the film's craftsmanship lends it "if not the feeling of truth -- that this is how it must have been for these people on that ship on those waters -- at least the feeling of truth's earnest, impoverished second cousin, verisimilitude: that this is how it may have looked." Put baldly, he objects to artifice, the creation of illusion, and exciting nautical action. He writes sneeringly of the film's historically accurate costumes and sets, of its sophisticated technology and camera work, of its substitution of a French enemy for the American one in the novel "The Far Side of the World" (Norton, paperback, $13.95), and of moving the action from 1812 to 1805. He doesn't like Maturin -- we're with him there -- but he also doesn't like it that Maturin is not identified as a spy; he doesn't like blood and gore; he doesn't like "the wild absurdities of the plot." The movie may please yobbos who like action films, but, according to Epstein, it "provides none of the pleasures to be had from the novels."

It seems impossible to me that a person with such a pinched view of entertainment could have enjoyed O'Brian's novels at all. 1812? 1805? Big deal. O'Brian himself was self-confessedly cavalier about chronology. French? American? Most of the novels, including "Master and Commander" (Norton, paperback, $13.95), take the French as the enemy. Moreover, it's not only the French who are absent from the movie, but the land scenes and women who exist in the novels as well. This is a movie, after all, a work shaped and tenanted to convey visually and through sound the general sensibility and understanding -- and beauty, as it happens -- of O'Brian's creation. With the exception of Maturin -- who is meant to be dark and crabbed, an unhandsome man with a reptilian gaze -- the casting is inspired, starting with Russell Crowe as a splendid Jack Aubrey. In "a near run thing," wrote O'Brian, "it is a captain's duty to be on deck, urging his ship through the water by the combined effort of his will and belly muscles." It is exactly that, will and belly muscles, which this actor gets across, just as he does Aubrey's bumptious humor, Tory disposition, and aura of authority.

Though it departs from the plots of the two novels upon which it is based, the movie is, in fact, the rare thing, a movie that one can appreciate even more if one has read the work. Its greatness is not dependent on it, but deepened. In the novel "The Far Side of the World," the troubles of Hollom -- the superannuated midshipman whom the crew come to see as cursed -- are greatly compounded by his carrying on an affair with the wife of a crew member. But the essential fact of his character is that he is inadequate to his position on the ship, bereft of that power of natural command and intuitive decisiveness upon which Nelson's navy depended. This and Aubrey's ingrained conservative understanding of the degrees and difference in men are brought out beautifully in the movie. We see with our eyes of what stuff a "Jonah" is made and are reminded of O'Brian's genius for portraying an unhappy ship as we gaze upon this murmuring crew.

The explanation of why this movie is as good as it is may be found in "The Making of 'Master and Commander: The Far Side of the World,' " by Tom McGregor (Norton, paperback, $19.95). Here are explained the decisions that went into the movie's narrative content, including why events begin in the middle of things and not as they do in "Master and Commander," with the fateful meeting of Aubrey and Maturin (to circumvent the clichs of a buddy movie). We discover here too that the members of the nonspeaking cast, a rough-looking lot, were drawn in large part from the former Soviet bloc and, as such, possessed faces free of the homogeneity and blandness that is one product of a modern, marketed lifestyle.

McGregor is guilty of a couple of mistakes about O'Brian's novels, which will scandalize some readers as much as it did me. Still, this is an excellent book of its kind and will please the O'Brian fans on your holiday gift list -- should you be so improvident as to have one unfinished.

Katherine A. Powers, a writer and critic, lives in Cambridge. Her column appears on alternate Sundays. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@earthlink.net.
