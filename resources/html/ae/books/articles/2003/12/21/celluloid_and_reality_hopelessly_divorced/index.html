Celluloid and reality, hopelessly divorced

By Elsbeth Lindner, 12/21/2003

Garbo Laughs
By Elizabeth Hay
Counterpoint, 294 pp., $25

ADVERTISEMENT
 

Film trivia freckles the pages of ''Garbo Laughs" as liberally as stars dot the Hollywood firmament. In a running refrain, Harriet Browning and her teenage son, Kenny, debate, compare, and bicker over movies the length of this diverting novel. Who is the better performer, Frank Sinatra or Gene Kelly? What about Chaplin vs. Buster Keaton? Does Sky Masterson make Sarah Brown happy in ''Guys and Dolls"? In compensation for her deprived childhood, Harriet has steeped herself in movies like a pickle. She has transmitted her obsession both to Kenny and her daughter, Jane, as well. Only her patient husband, Lew Gold, remains immune.

Harriet, who describes herself as a Miss Havisham whose clock stopped when Cary Grant died, is a writer and unsuccessful teacher of comic prose. One of her short stories has drawn rather too freely from her life and offended her great-aunt Leah. In her head, Harriet maintains a far more satisfactory and direct correspondence with Pauline Kael, the film critic whose energetically incisive opinions are Harriet's true gold standard. When Dinah Bloom moves in down the street, Harriet discovers another passionate movie aficionado on her doorstep, as well as a comic sidekick, with repartee as whip-crack smart as Rosalind Russell's. Dinah institutes the Friday night movie club in the Gold household, and the family falls for her instantly, again with the exception of Lew. His love for Dinah arrives larger and later.

Much of the first half of ''Garbo Laughs" is devoted to the setup. Events play second fiddle to the rich repertoire of characters Elizabeth Hay takes her time establishing. Harriet's neighborhood in Ottawa is brimming with quirky figures, like Jim Creak, the song detective whose radio show is a weekly excavation of lost melodies and memories. Soon to arrive are Leah herself, descending on Harriet for a visit as memorable as that of ''The Man Who Came to Dinner," and Jack Frame, Leah's stepson and a Rod Steiger lookalike, whose burly form and occasionally cruel manner quicken Harriet's pulse.

When the great ice storm of 1997 arrives, the curtain truly rises. Leah's poisonous martyrdom is driving Harriet insane. Lew's feelings for Dinah and Dinah's silent reciprocation have become apparent. A kind of frozen limbo descends, as the city shuts down and the power cuts out. Meanwhile, Harriet slowly wakes up to the dangerous gap between her celluloid dreams and her life. Mild-mannered Lew is neither Cary Grant nor Sean Connery, although he suffers from Rhett Butler syndrome: He wants his wife to fall in love with him. It is a measure of this novel's charm and maturity that it eschews the easy predictability of an affair between Lew and Dinah for a more interesting consideration of the emotional seesaw that is marriage.

Harriet is not the only character in the novel with choices to make. Lew and Dinah must order their priorities too, but it is Harriet who carries the brunt of the dilemma, Harriet whose name parallels the one Greta Garbo would use when trying to pass incognito -- Harriet Brown. Garbo, solitary and swathed in mystery, represents the pinnacle of Hollywood unattainability. When she finally laughed in ''Ninotchka," she was dubbed. The novel's real focus is on how much and what kind of romance -- or reality -- it is possible to hold on to in adult life. Perhaps Hay could have juxtaposed the movie stars with a dirtier kind of realism than Harriet's, which is itself wrapped in a layer of pink gauze. This would have pointed up the options more starkly, but would also have denied the reader the continuous pleasures supplied by this offbeat novel and its flavorsome troupe. As it is, most of the ''ordinary" characters seem only a size or two smaller than the celebrities. Harriet, for example, is both a wag and a woman genuinely confounded about how to love her husband better. Dinah, a hard-bitten journalist, is also enough of a best friend to choose not to damage Harriet's marriage.

With Leah eventually dispatched as gloriously as Lady Catherine de Bourgh in ''Pride and Prejudice," and Jack deflected, Harriet and Lew can be reunited. Their affections have weathered a long freeze and emerged intact. But the book, perhaps unwisely, is not yet over. Hay continues to dance the fine line dividing Hollywood scenarios from what passes for normality in fiction, by delivering an ending that slips in a few more movie tropes, including a conclusion pregnant with second chances. This is a bridge too far.

Hay's previous novel, ''A Student of Weather," while not wholly conventional, was a definably literary novel with a historical setting. Her cherishable new book is both a step sideways and up. How partners bridge the gulf between fantasy expectation and humdrum disappointment, how love sustains itself over time, are topics worthy of scrunity, and this bittersweet, mercurial book gives them due consideration. If it leaves itself open to accusations of excessive and undifferentiated romanticism, that may be because a spoonful of sugar helps the medicine go down.

Elsbeth Lindner is a writer and publisher who lives near New York City.
