SHORT TAKES

Short Takes

By Barbara Fisher, 12/28/2003

Getting Personal: Selected Writings
By Phillip Lopate
Basic, 399 pp., $25.95

ADVERTISEMENT
 

Arranged chronologically, these essays delicately describe the arc of a life -- the invention of a personality and the discovery of a career. Phillip Lopate grew up in a tenement in Brooklyn in the 1940s and '50s. His struggling, squabbling parents never learned to listen to each other, while Phillip developed a fine ear and a keen eye, an ambition to write, and a gift for teaching.

Candidly, he exposes his inadequacies (mostly sexual) and fears (mostly of women). His childhood delight in movies grows into an obsession and a possible profession at Columbia College in the '60s. Teaching takes him to Texas and then back to New York City, where, as a visiting poet in the public schools, he madly decides to mount a production of "Uncle Vanya" with fifth- and sixth-graders. "Chekhov for Children" is the appropriately modest title of the brilliant essay that describes the hilarious and heart-wrenching process of introducing restless, innocent 11-year-olds to the self-deluded and remorseful adults of Chekhov. With great exactness he describes his own brooding, bristly character, his deep suspicion of living in the moment, and his righteous rejection of joie de vivre. Most moving is his identification with his father, his recognition that, despite all his activity and success, his father's "vagueness, shlumpiness, and mania for withdrawal inhabit me like a flu." In spite or perhaps because of his willingness to expose the tender parts of himself, he becomes not simply engaging but endearing.

Siegfried
By Harry Mulisch
Translated, from the German, by Paul Vincent
Viking, 180 pp., $22.95

Rudolf Herter, a celebrated Dutch author, becomes the source of a long-suppressed story about Adolf Hitler. Herter remarks in a television interview in Vienna that only through fiction can the full truth about Hitler be comprehended. He is then approached by an elderly couple, the Falks, who tell him an improbable story of Hitler's romantic, parental, and personal life.

Hitler, they assert, fathered a child with his mistress Eva Braun. For political reasons, he insisted that the child, Siegfried, be raised as the son of the Falks, his personal servants. Unacknowledged by him or Eva, the child is brought up according to plan. While he is doted on by Eva and dear to the Fuehrer, no one outside of Hitler's inner circle suspects the truth of his parentage. When, because of jealousy over succession, Hitler's closest allies accuse the boy of racial impurity, Hitler orders his execution without hesitation or regret. Hitler marries Eva as compensation for the loss of the child before taking his own and her life. Siegfried's life and death remain a secret.

The story as told by the Falks with its petty domestic details gives solidity to the inhuman, hollow Hitler. His shallow personal feelings are easily sacrificed to his political ambitions. But when Herter reviews the story, finding parallels and portents in the philosophy of Kant, Schopenhauer, and Nietzsche, its force becomes lost in larger issues of good and evil.

The Good Wife Strikes Back
By Elizabeth Buchan
Viking, 304 pp., $24.95

The decorous plot of this old-fashioned English novel hardly lives up to its title. Fanny, long-suffering wife to a self-absorbed and ambitious member of Parliament, strikes back with surpassing mildness. For years she is "an adjunct. A perfect, smiling willing adjunct who wears tights."

Without grumbling or complaining, she endures a minor infidelity, an unpleasant home, a permanent live-in sister-in-law, and numerous small slights and inconveniences. When she finally makes her break for freedom and autonomy, she takes a brief unannounced trip to the Continent. Fanny's devoted father says to her, "You must be kind to yourself. Women generally aren't." Fanny seems to believe she is taking this advice, but by current American standards she hardly cuts herself a break. Fatigue, boredom, and powerlessness are her lot in life, and after her small, elegant gesture of defiance, she returns to them. Perhaps in England this ending rings true, but here it has a decidedly false note.

Barbara Fisher is a freelance critic who lives in New York.
