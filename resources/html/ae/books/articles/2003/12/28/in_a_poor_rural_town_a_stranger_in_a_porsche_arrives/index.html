In a poor, rural town, a stranger in a Porsche arrives

By Jodi Daynard, 12/28/2003

The Book of Hard Things
By Sue Halpern
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 228 pp., $22

ADVERTISEMENT
 

Had film director David Lynch decided to write a novel instead of producing the 1990s hit TV series ''Twin Peaks," it might have looked a little bit like Sue Halpern's first novel, ''The Book of Hard Things." Like Twin Peaks, Halpern's fictional town of Poverty struts a sleazy world of rural roughnecks and quirky, inbred characters limited less by poverty than their own narrow culture.

As the story begins, Thomas ''Cuzzy" Gage, 19, wakes up on a beach with his girlfriend Amber-Rose. His father has been committed, the mother of his child has left him, and his employer has kicked him out. Cuzzy seems willfully destined for nowhere, his greatest ambition to stay off the highway work crews.

Enter Michael ''Tracy" Edwards, urbane New Yorker driving a silver Porsche. Tracy has come north to sort through the scholarly papers of his deceased friend Algernon Blackwood. But, on the urging of a local do-gooder, the Rev Jason Trimble, Tracy also agrees to ''redeem" Cuzzy by putting him to work on Algernon's family estate.

The Larches, a sprawling estate reminiscent of the country homes of the Gilded Age robber barons, provides a suitably gothic setting for Cuzzy Gage's metamorphosis from rustic boy to worldly man. In this context, Algernon's posthumous ethnomusicology scholarship represents the apex of human culture, bringing into dark relief that barely literate, debased world from which Cuzzy has sprung.

While at first the whole arrangement seems a bizarre mismatch of clashing temperaments and cultures, Cuzzy quickly grows attached to Tracy's rationality and gentleness; Tracy grows to love Cuzzy's tabula-rasa-like potential. Soon, Cuzzy loses touch with criminal pals like cousin Joey, who did time for sodomizing a dog and raping his own sister.

In the absence of a larger plot, one hangs onto the incremental growth of these two men, watching with fragile optimism as Cuzzy grows beyond his own limited world. We watch as Tracy, too, attempts to overcome the indelible memory of having rebuffed the homosexual Algernon in a moment of panic and inexperience.

In watching Tracy's growth, Halpern has us think of Jesus's story of the Good Samaritan: ''There, he had done it," Tracy thinks after succeeding in engaging Cuzzy. ''Like it or not, he was working his way toward eternal life." Ignorance to knowledge, sin to redemption -- these are two of the subtle story lines that exist in the novel. But there is yet a third and even more complicated movement in these two characters' growth: that from sexual naivete to sexual sophistication.

D. H. Lawrence once wrote words to the effect that friendship between a man and woman is impossible because there must be sexual intercourse; and that friendship between man and man is impossible because there must not be sexual intercourse. Nearly 100 years later, Lawrence's dark take on human relationships still plays itself out in Halpern's novel, as if the human tolerance for the gray area of sexuality has hardly grown in the intervening years.

''The Book of Hard Things" seems to be about this very hardness: the hardness of the human heart, and the rigidity of human beliefs, especially when confronted by hard facts beyond the comfort of the paradigm. In the end, however, it feels even darker than that: It feels like a gothic ode to death -- the ultimate hard thing -- an exploration of the way infinity casts everything inauthentic in a lurid light, makes every petty action grotesque, every human being deformed as if from within.

In the carefully crafted prose, the well-drawn characters, and its conceptual depth, ''The Book of Hard Things" offers a great deal to admire. And yet it's this very intellectual strength that turns out to be the novel's chief handicap. For one, the prose passages that head off each chapter are irritatingly esoteric: ''Ivory: 'The hard creamy-white modified dentine that composes the tusks of a tusked mammal' . . . Ivory as composition, composition as the prelude to music. We are so concerned with first principles. How about this: solace before all!" This passage and a dozen or so others expose an author working out her inner material, work that one not only doesn't need to know, but shouldn't know -- at least, not from anything beyond the fiction itself.

Of course, taken on their own, the contrived nature of these passages might seem like a small blip in an otherwise sharply realized work. But the intellectual note that they sound never fully abates. Rather, it crescendos in the novel's climactic scene, one that feels utterly manufactured, not born of the characters themselves.

Violence in fiction is prevalent, to be sure, and darkly alluring. To many genres, it's even necessary. But, beyond certain formulaic necessities -- such as the dead body that must begin a mystery -- it is also the hardest thing to come by honestly, the hardest thing to earn. And, without giving anything away, what happens to Cuzzy and Tracy at the end of ''The Book of Hard Things" has not been sufficiently earned, not to the point where one is willing to suffer for it. So, despite a gifted lexicon, an original cast of characters, and a crystalline setting, you can add this reviewer's heart to this book of hard things.

Jodi Daynard is a frequent contributor to The Boston Globe.
