BOOK REVIEW

Dissecting the fall of 'World's Leading Company'

By Joseph Rosenbloom, Globe Correspondent, 12/28/2003

No one ever accused Enron Corp. of lacking self esteem. Years ago, Enron termed itself the World's Leading Energy Company. When its reported revenue topped $100 billion in 2000, it adopted a shorter, if not exactly more modest, slogan: the World's Leading Company.

On Dec. 2, 2001, staggering under $38 billion in debt, it filed the largest bankruptcy claim in US history.

What happened?

Much of the answer is already on the record in newspapers, magazines, and books about Enron, which stands as the archetype of corporate rascality during the 1990s. But for a story of Enron's appeal and complexity, there's still room for the definitive word.

"The Smartest Guys in the Room: The Amazing Rise and Scandalous Fall of Enron," by Bethany McLean and Peter Elkind, attempts to deliver that definitive word. Although the authors, who are senior writers at Fortune, had access to inside sources and a trove of relevant e-mails and other documents, the book falls short. Its conclusions are hedged and murky, and the narrative bogs down in opaque detail about Enron's complex financial transactions.

As did the rest of the business press, Fortune missed the real story of Enron's rise during the 1990s from a small-time operator of natural gas pipelines to a hyper-growth conglomerate. McLean was one of the first to sniff something foul. Her story in Fortune on Feb. 19, 2001, "Is Enron Overpriced?", struck a skeptical note.

"The Smartest Guys" reconstructs the whole appalling yarn. What the book does best is draw vivid portraits of the top Enron guys (there are a few gals, too), notably Ken Lay and Jeff Skilling.

On paper they appeared smart. Lay, Enron's seemingly high-minded chairman and possessor of a PhD in economics, sensed the business potential of energy deregulation early on. Skilling, the CEO whose credentials included a Harvard MBA and a lightning-fast rise at blue-chip consulting firm McKinsey & Co., crafted Enron's widely acclaimed, cutting-edge strategy: pursuing high-margin businesses, such as energy trading, to leverage the company's "intellectual capital."

The company's all-consuming goal was satisfying Wall Street's expectations for quickly rising earnings. What was good for Wall Street was great for Lay and Skilling. Higher earnings translated into a higher stock price, which determined the value of their stock options and bonuses. Skilling propped up the stock by portraying Enron as a fast-growth "logistics" company, thus "fundamentally misrepresenting the business," write McLean and Elkind. Enron was really a volatile trading company with voracious capital requirements and perpetual cash-flow problems, the authors argue, convincingly.

Andy Fastow, Enron's chief financial officer, resorted to gimmicks worthy of Rube Goldberg to camouflage the company's true debt. Fastow, who benefited handsomely from his financial stake in an elaborate array of off-the-books partnerships that he devised for Enron, is an obvious candidate for bete noire of the Enron story. Yet it was Skilling, say McLean and Elkind, who turned Enron "into a place where deception became almost inevitable."

No deception of Enron's scale could have been possible without a massive collapse of safeguards: the company's own board of directors; its outside auditor, Arthur Andersen; and Wall Street's securities analysts, investment bankers, and credit-rating agencies. As "The Smartest Guys" documents extensively, the watchdogs were either asleep or pretended to be because doing so served their selfish interests.

Unfortunately for a book that purports to have the last word, "The Smartest Guys" is appearing before most of the Enron-related litigation has run its course. Investigations by federal prosecutors and the Securities and Exchange Commission are still going strong. The trial of Fastow, who faces about 100 criminal charges, is pending. Many of McLean and Elkind's observations are unavoidably tentative.

Beyond the issue of criminality, was Enron merely an extreme byproduct of the nation's speculative binge during the 1990s or did it signal a deeper corruption in American business? McLean and Elkind duck the question, saying an answer must await the next bull market.
