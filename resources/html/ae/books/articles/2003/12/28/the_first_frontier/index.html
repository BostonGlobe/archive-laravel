The first frontier

By Joan Druett, 12/28/2003

Sea of Glory: America's Voyage of Discovery -- The U.S. Exploring Expedition, 1838-1842
By Nathaniel Philbrick
Viking, 452 pp., illustrated, $27.95

ADVERTISEMENT
 

On Sunday, Aug. 18, 1838, six ships set sail from Hampton Roads, Va., headed for the far side of the world. The flagship Vincennes was in the lead, her massive hull varnished black save for a white streak regularly interrupted with square black gun ports, her lofty rigging manned by dense rows of cheering sailors. Following in order of rank and size were the sloop of war Peacock, the store ship Relief, the gun brig Porpoise, and the ex-pilot schooners Flying Fish and Sea Gull. This was the first, great United States South Seas Exploring Expedition, commanded by Lieutenant Charles Wilkes, crewed by 346 officers and men, and with seven scientists and two artists on board.

Almost four years later, on June 10, 1842, the Vincennes arrived in New York, followed a couple of days later by the remnants of the expedition, comprising the Porpoise and the brig Oregon, which had been purchased on the northwest coast of America to replace the wrecked Peacock. Much had been accomplished -- huge tracts of the ocean had been charted, plus 800 miles of scarcely known Oregon shore and 1,500 miles of entirely unknown Antarctic coast. The Stars and Stripes had fluttered off the lagoons of well over 200 tropical islands, and more than 4,000 artifacts and 2,000 scientific specimens had been collected, an enormously rich fund that became the foundation of the collection of the new Smithsonian Institution. Yet instead of returning to the United States in triumphant command of his quarterdeck, Lieutenant Wilkes chose to slink on shore by hitching a ride on the pilot boat.

The welcome would have been lukewarm, anyway, because over the intervening years the American public had lost interest in the expedition. The fashionable topic was the American West, lustily publicized by traveler John C. Fremont, whose book, ghost-written by his energetic wife, Jessie Benton Fremont, was just coming out. The expedition did get some media attention when Wilkes was put on trial for overzealous discipline, personality clashes, and the slaughter of 87 Fijian natives in revenge for the killing of two careless officers; and there was another flicker of interest when his five-volume ''Narrative" came out in 1845, to the accompaniment of intriguingly hostile reviews. As the 23 worthy but tedious volumes of the scientific account of the voyage followed, however, public interest died, and despite many subsequent studies of the expedition -- one of the most recent being the wonderful Smithsonian publication ''Magnificent Voyagers," edited by Herman Viola and Carolyn Margolis (1985) -- it has never revived. This is the situation that National Book Award-winner Nathaniel Philbrick aims to remedy by revisiting Wilkes's ''Narrative," with frequent reference to a recently unearthed collection of Wilkes's letters to his admirable wife, Jane Renwick Wilkes (whom he called the ''moderation" of his existence), and to the letters and private journal of Midshipman William Reynolds.

There are great hurdles, quotation being one of them. In contrast to the bluffly poetic, eminently quotable Captain James Cook, Wilkes's writing style is self-serving, tedious, and embarrassingly rancorous. Midshipman Reynolds is not much better, being an overemotional young man irritatingly prone to underscoring and hyperbole. Too, he made no secret of the fact that he utterly loathed Wilkes, mostly because he had hero-worshiped him at the start, and then his idol had let him down. Without doubt, his boss was a prickly, dictatorial character, but Reynolds's impressions seem very unbalanced, particularly since Wilkes's qualities as a surveyor and navigator were worthy of more respect. Additionally, there is a constant ambiguity -- that while the Exploring Expedition was a huge success, in that it established the United States as a scientific and academic nation, it was also a failure, because of poor public relations both on board and on shore. Wilkes was responsible for both, and because of the second, he lost out on the recognition he deserved for the first.

Whether ''Sea of Glory" can restore this forgotten saga to its rightful place in the historiography of America and the Pacific remains to be seen. When Philbrick is at his best he is very good indeed. The details of contact and conflict with Oceanic peoples tend to be disturbingly ethnocentric, but descriptions of battles with storms and Antarctic ice are outstandingly vivid reading. His research is both comprehensive and painstaking. That Wilkes is such an unattractive subject is a stumbling block; as ''Sea of Glory" amply demonstrates, despite his great accomplishments, the leader of the expedition is impossible to picture as a hero.

It is tempting to wonder what could have saved him -- and, incidentally, what could have made Philbrick's job a lot easier. If the mutiny Wilkes constantly feared had taken place, and he, like William Bligh (another lieutenant with poor communication skills), had been put in a small boat to save himself with his considerable navigational skills, he would have gained his place in history. Or he could have followed the example of a French predecessor, Louis de Freycinet, and smuggled his wife on board. Not only would Jane have relieved his awful loneliness and moderated his tyrannical ways, but she could have helped him write a better book, too.

Joan Druett is the author of ''In the Wake of Madness: The Murderous Voyage of the Whaleship Sharon."
