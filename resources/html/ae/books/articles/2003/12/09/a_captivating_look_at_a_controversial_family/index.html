BOOK REVIEW

A captivating look at a controversial family

By Michael Kenney, Globe Correspondent, 12/9/2003

("Family Circle: The Boudins and the Aristocracy of the Left"; By Susan Braudy; Knopf, 460 pp. illustrated; $27.95.)

ADVERTISEMENT

In a Google search for "Family Circle," the first hit promotes the December issue of that comfortable middle-American women's magazine. Before the search is modified to add "Boudin," the point has been made: the fatal disconnect between mainstream America and its privileged dissenters.

Time and again that sense of disconnect intrudes in a reading of Susan Braudy's account of the life and times of the "family" that circled around Leonard Boudin -- not least the willful daughter Kathy, whose romantic craving for a violent revolution exposed the contradictions between ideals and actions within the circle.

And like the events that it chronicles, "Family Circle" should carry a warning label. In the month since it was rushed into publication to piggyback on Kathy's release from prison, a number of people, including several of Braudy's sources, have cited errors in the book -- a school photo misidentified, quotations distorted, an affair denied.

With that important caveat in mind, "Family Circle" is a riveting book. However repellent the sexual antics of Leonard Boudin, a prominent lawyer, may have been, or Kathy's mindless violence, the reader is compelled to read on.

Before her craving spiraled out of control into bomb-making and armed robberies -- and the murders that sent her to prison -- Kathy was among America's most privileged young people.

From her parents -- called "the most gorgeous couple of the left" on their wedding day -- Kathy had instant entree into leftist artistic and intellectual circles. Among the guests at a brunch to celebrate Leonard's Supreme Court victory in a right-to-travel case were Joan Baez and Paul Robeson, artist Rockwell Kent, and journalists Dwight Macdonald and I. F. Stone, a Boudin in-law. The philanthropist Corliss Lamont "reminded Kathy, now almost 15, that his tennis pro was still begging to train her without fee."

But all was not well in the family circle. Leonard flaunted his affairs in front of his wife and children, seducing at least one of Kathy's friends. And there was tension between Kathy and her older brother, Michael, now a federal appeals court judge in Boston. She was "fearful," Braudy writes, "of losing her place as Leonard's most cherished offspring."

Braudy was the "wallflower at the orgy," in Nora Ephron's memorable phrase, living across the hall from Boudin at Bryn Mawr College in the early 1960s (and down the hall from equally privileged future militant Diana Oughton), where she was both awed by Kathy's assured contact with the famous and her "confident charisma" and intimidated by her "moralistic scoldings."

Braudy's story follows divergent tracks -- tracing Leonard's high-profile cases during the Vietnam War, including the defense of Dr. Benjamin Spock and others against antidraft conspiracy charges, and Kathy's descent into self-defeating revolution.

Kathy escaped -- naked from a sauna -- from the March 1970 explosion at the Weatherman "bomb factory" in a Greenwich Village townhouse in which Oughton's body was shredded. After 11 more years in the radical underground, she pleaded guilty to felony murder and armed robbery charges for her role as a getaway decoy in the October 1981 armored car robbery and shootout at Nyack, N.Y.

After the explosion, Braudy writes, "Kathy's decision to hide from the law . . . forced Leonard to pursue her. She infuriated him by keeping him waiting for hours at designated pay phones. He was also dismayed because she was endangering his reputation for reverence for the law."

Kathy's actions, Braudy writes, citing an interview with journalist Jon Snow, "immersed Leonard in feelings of failure, a state that he loathed. When he was not thinking up ways to get charges against her dismissed, Leonard was trying to make sense of his own life, constructing arguments that distanced himself from Kathy and the robbery." He mobilized members of his legal circle -- though not son Michael -- to work on Kathy's behalf. Leonard died after suffering a heart attack on Thanksgiving Day, 1989, some five and a half years after negotiating his daughter's plea bargain.

"Family Circle" will be troubling for readers who participated in the antiwar and social justice movements or who reported on Leonard's legal actions, as this reader did. Were those protest movements and those legal battles so doomed, so self-indulgent, so far from mainstream America? One incident suggests an answer.

In June 1969, thousands of members of Students for a Democratic Society gathered in Chicago for a national convention, a year after their demonstrations at the Democratic National Convention. Kathy and the newly formed Weatherman group broke up the convention and led hundreds of "high-spirited young people through streets of Chicago's ghetto." To them, "it actually felt like revolution," Braudy writes. Yet "something big collapsed that night in Chicago: the hundreds of thousands of young people in the protest movement throughout the country hated the idea of violence as the `what's next.' "
