A READING LIFE

A little pulp in your eggnog?

By Caroline Leavitt, 12/14/2003

When it comes to holidays, I like the unexpected. A tinsel-topped ficus tree instead of a traditional evergreen. Gifts wrapped in highway maps instead of store-bought paper. And of course, it's de rigueur that holiday books should be anything but ho-ho-hum. So this year, bring on the quirky.

Nostalgia is often home for the holidays, and what better remembrance of things past than three pulp-fiction reissues from an inspired new series from the Feminist Press called "Femmes Fatales: Women Write Pulp"? Pulps were novels meant for the masses, stories teeming with dastardly dames, wisecracking working gals, and hard-boiled gumshoes. Each of these rediscovered gems boasts its original, gleefully provocative cover art, with Day-Glo type, snappy-looking "broads," and hilarious taglines. Plus they've got spiffy new commentaries putting each title into modern perspective.

"Skyscraper," by Faith Baldwin ($14.95), is a 1930s romantic serial about ambitious young Lynn Harding, as in love with a dashing co-worker as she is with the zip and hum of her big-city career. But times are tough, and if she marries him, she loses her job. Could a powerful older man somehow be the solution? What's subversive in this pulp is that Baldwin doesn't present 1930s business as usual: Lynn happily controls her own life, prizes female friendship over paramours, and not only gets to have her cake, but makes sure it's frosted as well.

Lesbian pulps of the '50s were meant to be racy, but Valerie Taylor's "The Girls in 3-B" ($13.95) pushes the boundaries by showing how the perils of home, hearth, and husbands can be much more taboo. Roommate Annice dabbles in the brave new beatnik world until its tawdry misogyny sends her back to the safety of the status quo; Pat yearns after her married boss but, fearful of becoming used, abused, and abandoned, persuades herself to love a regular Johnny instead. Only Barby finds real love, with an older lesbian -- and though I wish her coming out wasn't due to a traumatic childhood rape, it's still one of the first undeniably sensitive portraits.

Ah, but the revelation of the series is the literate psychological noir "In a Lonely Place," by Dorothy B. Hughes ($14.95). Adrift in postwar Los Angeles, serial killer Dix Steele quietly stalks and kills his female prey, until he falls for femme fatale Laurel Grey and something snaps. Written from Dix's viewpoint, in prose as hard-edged and rare as diamonds, the novel builds to a crescendo of impending doom. This isn't pulp, it's got permanence -- and I plan to give myself every Hughes novel I can find.

Got a cook on your list? Instead of the usual celebrity-chef cookbook, my recipe for the perfect gift would be David Rosengarten's delicious "It's All American Food: The Best Recipes for More Than 400 New American Classics" (Little, Brown, $29.95). American food is as vast a melting pot as our culture, but instead of denigrating our culinary hybrids, Rosengarten celebrates every comforting mouthful.

Children's books are an irresistible treat for any age group, but please run from Madonna's fake morality tales (actually, forget any book written by a celebrity). Go instead to the real thing, the Grimm brothers' fairy tales, in "Grimm's Grimmest" (Chronicle, $22.95). Giants thunder, witches scheme, and damsels like Rapunzel are shockingly distressed in these 19 gloriously illustrated tales based on the original 1822 edition by Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm. Read one story and you'll realize why they are classics.

Inspired silliness is always a perfect gift, so I'd wrap up "How to Keep Dinosaurs," by Robert Mash (Weidenfeld & Nicolson, $14.99), a tongue-in-cheek manual about adopting dinosaurs as pets. Divided into breathtakingly witty sections on breeds, health care, and what belongs in your basic dino tool kit (a sturdy shovel is key), the book is filled with sublimely realistic-looking photographs of prehistoric reptiles lounging around or cavorting with their proud owners. Plus there are nifty icons for each breed's traits, like "kleptomania" and "indiscriminant courtship," and details on weight, size, and origins. Fractured reality that makes you yearn for a lively little saurus all your own.

Are alternative realities on anyone you know's list this year? "What Ifs? of American History: Eminent Historians Imagine What Might Have Been," by Anthony Beevor (Putnam, $26.95), engages in wishful thinking that's grounded in fact. Eighteen esteemed contributors including David McCullough and Caleb Carr speculate on all our "roads not taken" and how our world might have changed if they had been. What if the Mayflower never sailed? What if there had been no American Revolution or JFK had not been assassinated? And, of course, what if there were no thought-provoking book gifts like this one?

Surprise. Amaze. Delight. If the holidays don't do that for you or a loved one, I'm betting these books will, and like the best kind of gift, they can be opened more than once.

Caroline Leavitt's newest novel, "Girls in Trouble," will be out in January. She can be reached at www.carolineleavitt.com.
