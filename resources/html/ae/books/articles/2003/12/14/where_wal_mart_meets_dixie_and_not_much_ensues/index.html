Where Wal-Mart meets Dixie, and not much ensues

By Katherine A. Powers, 12/14/2003

Heavenly Days

By James Wilcox

ADVERTISEMENT
 

Viking, 199 pp., $23.95

James Wilcox has been chronicling the doings of the good, and not so good, people of Tula Springs, La., for 20 years now, beginning with his first novel, "Modern Baptists," and continuing with real comic brio through "North Gladiola" and "Miss Undine's Living Room." His subsequent novels retained connections with Tula Springs, but had pretty much left town. Then came a silence, which is now finally broken after five years and the appearance of "Heavenly Days," a novel that has once again returned to the lives, loves, decorating decisions, and civic demands of Tula Springs.

Though it is still a place where mediocrity and pettiness rule, the town has seen a few changes since we last took its measure. Among the signs of the times are the replacement of Sonny Boy Bargain Store by a chain store, the presence of a Wal-Mart, and now the cropping up of a rumor -- though just a rumor, as it turns out -- that a Ruby Tuesday will be opening up. And, distressing to those who followed her hard-fought campaign in an earlier novel, Mrs. Olive Mackie has been unseated in a recent election from her position as superintendent of Streets, Parking, and Garbage (by a single chad). The most dismaying development of all, however, is that it seems possible that Tula Springs has had its day as the subject of Wilcox's best writing.

To be sure, many of the ingredients that made the author's earlier novels so wonderful are present in this one. Wilcox still uses bathos to excellent comic effect and retains his virtuoso ability to take the rote pronouncements of public speech and make of them mots justes. Also in evidence are the nimble mustering of quotidian detail, the eye for petty exploitation and minor bullying, and the ability to convey how much of life is a series of inextricable involvements and never-ending annoyances. Indeed, a sense of comic futility and poignant resignation is at the heart of all Wilcox's fiction. Our passage through life, as he portrays it, is far less the product of will than of some unspecific tropism. But here, unfortunately, the movement of the novel itself seems inadvertent and aimless. In place of a plot, there is simply a massing up and piling on of fantastic detail.

Lou Jones, the book's heroine, is in her early 50s and is an unremunerated columnist for the North American Bassoon Society. Despite her degree in music theory and past position as college teacher, she is now employed as a receptionist at WaistWatch, a Christian-oriented fitness concern. She has an uneasy relation to her work companion, Maigrite, whose husband, Mr. Pickens (the new superintendent of Streets, Parking, and Garbage), appears to be conducting an affair with Burma Van Buren, whose own husband had not only "stopped paying WaistWatch for the TotalPackageMakeover Mrs. Van Buren had gotten in lieu of a honeymoon," but "started rolling over her bare toes -- on purpose. With the golf cart he used everywhere, even inside the house."

Lou is married to Don Jones, who has PhDs in plant pathology and computer engineering but, nonetheless, is unemployed and, for the last six weeks, has been living across town in his parents' house (they moved, "for health reasons," to a houseboat in a retirement community on a man-made lake in Arizona). He is here to defend the house against former deadbeat tenants and has a temporary guest in the person of Mrs. Ompala, a very white, British colonial snob ("Oh, yes, Harvard -- in what they call Cambridge, I believe") who is the putative mother of the Joneses' maid Alpha, a black woman from Mombasa taking night classes at St. Jude Community College, where she is politically active and deeply involved with hiring decisions. The novel abounds with additional characters and complications, but to what end is the question.

It is, to be sure, part of Wilcox's Southern way of telling a story that every person comes with a baroque pedigree, every event with a capsule tale of how it came to be. Even finding a parking space acquires a historical dimension -- as when Lou parks in a handicapped space: "It's either that or drive across the railroad tracks and squeeze in front of the Feed and Seed, then trudge back through those sunflowers where the waitperson at the Bulgar Barbeque Service Two got bit by a deer tick and emigrated back to her native Mississippi." This device is often very funny, but in "Heavenly Days," it is relentless, reducing the narrative to a harried recitation of events that has, instead of shape, only contiguous detail upon contiguous detail. One does not like to say it or even think it, but it appears to be time for us all to bid adieu to Tula Springs, La.

Katherine A. Powers is a writer and critic who lives in Cambridge.
