From more liberal beginnings, the GOP gradually gave way to conservatism

By David Gergen, 12/14/2003

Grand Old Party

By Lewis L. Gould

ADVERTISEMENT
 

Random House, 597 pp., illustrated, $35

Republicans were spoiling for a fight as they gathered in Chicago for their national convention in 1912. Mr. Dooley, the beloved Irish bartender, predicted that it would be ''a combynation iv th' Chicago fire, Saint Batholomew's massacree, the battle iv th' Boyne, th' life iv Jesse James, an' th' night iv th' big wind." He was right -- and it was one of the most important brawls in American political history.

In one corner were the party's conservatives, demanding a second term in the White House for President William Howard Taft. In the other corner were Progressive Republicans, pushing for the nomination of former president Theodore Roosevelt, who had soured on Taft and wanted another turn. Roosevelt argued furiously that the conservatives had stolen 60 to 80 delegates from him: ''With unflinching hearts and undimmed eyes, we stand at Armageddon, and we battle for the Lord."

Roosevelt, as usual, had the upper rhetorical hand, but this time he didn't have the votes. Taft and his fellow conservatives cruised to the nomination and Roosevelt stomped out, along with his Progressive friends. As Lewis L. Gould argues in this engaging and balanced account, the 1912 convention became ''a seminal event in the history of the Republicans." From then on, the GOP was on the path toward becoming the nation's conservative party.

Since the party's founding in 1854 as a spearhead against slavery, the two presidents who most defined Republicanism -- Abraham Lincoln and Theodore Roosevelt -- were champions of an active national government. Lincoln not only wielded the power of government to win the Civil War and free the slaves but also, true to his roots as a Whig, supported a steady stream of internal improvements by government. Roosevelt, as Gould writes, ''advocated policies that very much looked forward to the modern welfare state," including graduated income taxes, inheritance taxes, limits on corporate power, regulation of big business, and laws to curb child labor, provide minimum wages, and workers' unemployment compensation. As Republicans abandoned Teddy, the Democrats embraced his ideas and turned most of them into laws -- over Republican opposition.

Conservatives had won a key battle in 1912, but they had not yet won the war. It took some seven decades of long, continuing struggle before they gained full dominance over the Republican Party. Liberals of today could learn a lot from their tenacity and skill. When Roosevelt bolted from the convention, he quickly launched a third-party bid that guaranteed the election that November of Woodrow Wilson. Democrats then held on to the White House for eight years. The Republicans -- led by conservatives -- enjoyed a brief renaissance of power in the 1920s, but then the Depression swept them from office and gave way to the longest Democratic reign in history: five straight presidential elections. Franklin D. Roosevelt's successes not only transformed the Democrats but provided a temporary boost to the moderate wing of the GOP. For much of mid-century, the moderate, Eastern establishment of the GOP dictated the party's presidential nominees -- from Wendell Willkie to Thomas E. Dewey to Dwight Eisenhower.

But conservatives were not to be denied. Republicans, they snorted, should offer the country ''a choice, not an echo," and in 1964, they stormed the Cow Palace in San Francisco to nominate Mr. Conservative: Barry Goldwater. Even though he went down to disastrous defeat, a new generation of conservatives had now founded a movement and discovered its voice: Ronald Reagan. For the next 15 years, they would plug steadily away, building up grassroots organizations, recruiting cadres of young followers, and going on the intellectual offensive. The academy and the media may be stocked with Democrats, but the conservatives are the ones who seem to understand and relish a combat of ideas. By 1980, the GOP was moving more sharply to the right and was bringing the country with it. For six presidential elections in a row -- from Reagan through George W. Bush -- conservatives have now headed the Republican ticket. And the moderate wing of the party looks more like a feather.

Not everything has changed in the Republican philosophy. Gould points out that from its beginning, the party ''has been willing to use national power in noneconomic areas to produce a more moral and upright society." The party that employed moral arguments to oppose slavery now uses them to oppose abortion. Since its inception, the GOP has also been much more willing to put constraints on labor than on business. Strikingly, Gould says, Republicans have also been rather consistent in accusing Democrats of lacking full-blooded patriotism -- from the Civil War through the McCarthy period and -- sotto voce -- in the war on terrorism.

How does all this Republican history add up? Gould gives the party high marks for its political acumen; as it celebrates its 150th birthday next year, the party can look back on 22 victories in presidential elections compared with 15 for Democrats. Gould argues that its proudest accomplishments came early: saving the Union, abolishing slavery, and winning passage of the 13th, 14th, and 15th amendments. In more recent times, he says, ''Republicans evidenced a better appreciation of . . . the Soviet threat than did the Democrats" and have been ''a constructive participant in the national dialogue" on moral questions. But too often, he says, zealots have led the party over the line (McCarthy and Watergate), and their embrace of Southern whites strikes many as giving cover to segregationists, betraying the spirit of Lincoln.

For conservatives, the unanswered question is whether they can eventually gain the same dominance in the country as they have in their party. So far, as Gould argues, they have been unable to convince voters to accept their full agenda. But this history suggests that they will keep trying and may one day succeed. If liberals don't like it, they should stop groaning and get back in the arena, too.

Former adviser to four presidents, David Gergen is Public Service Professor of Public Leadership and Director of the Center for Public Leadership at the Kennedy School of Government, Harvard University. He is the author of ''Eyewitness to Power: The Essence of Leadership, Nixon to Clinton" (Simon & Schuster, 2000).
