I read the news today, oh boy

By Margot Livesey, 12/14/2003

Double Vision

By Pat Barker, Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 258 pp., $23

ADVERTISEMENT
 

The British novelist Pat Barker has always been fearless in her choice of subject matter. One early novel was about prostitution; others dealt with working-class life in the north of England. During the 1990s Barker achieved wider recognition with "The Regeneration Trilogy," which was set during the First World War and revolved around the relationship between the poet Siegfried Sassoon and a psychologist named William River. The novels vividly portray the violence wrought by war on both the body and the mind, the soldier and the civilian, and are remarkable for their spectacular prose and their psychological acuity. Since then Barker, who is 60 this year, has continued her unflinching exploration of difficult and topical territory.

Her new novel, "Double Vision," is set in and around a small village in the northeast of England shortly after Sept. 11. At the heart of the novel lies the vexed and pertinent question of how to look at the unbearable, and what to do with that looking. The epigraph comes from the Spanish painter Francisco Goya, famous for his frank paintings of brutal subjects: "No se puede mirar. One cannot look at this. Yo lo vi. I saw it. Esto es lo verdadero. This is the truth." Is it possible to look on scenes of dreadful violence and depict them appropriately rather than voyeuristically? What can viewers or readers do in the face of such depictions? And how can we continue to live our lives after such events? Barker is a bold enough writer to ask these questions directly: One of her central characters is a sculptor working on a 15-foot Christ for the local cathedral; another is a journalist who used to report from war zones. But she is also canny enough to ask them covertly through her complex and suspenseful plot, which draws together two unlikely couples.

Driving home alone one cold January night, after a party, Kate Frobisher, the sculptor, skids off the icy road. While she lies in her wrecked car, slipping in and out of consciousness, another vehicle stops and Kate is aware of someone approaching: "He didn't move, didn't open the door, didn't check to see how she was, didn't ring or go for help. Just stood there, breathing." Fortunately another driver stops, an ambulance is called, and Kate slowly and painfully begins to recover. In this regard she is luckier than her husband, Ben, a war photographer who has recently died in Afghanistan. "She would never, never, never be able to accept his death, and she didn't try. This wasn't an illness she would recover from; it was an amputation she had to learn to live with. There was a great and surprising peace in acknowledging this."

When Kate recovers consciousness, her biggest concern is how soon she will have the strength and mobility to work on her sculpture. The local vicar recommends a young man who has been tending the churchyard since the local sheep were sacrificed to foot-and-mouth disease. Kate remembers noticing him scything the grass and thinking how attractive he was. When she interviews him, he seems like the ideal assistant: strong, competent, intelligent (he studied English at university), unobtrusive, with a nice sense of humor and an engaging smile. The charming Peter Wingrave enters her life and so the wheels of the plot begin to turn.

Paralleling Kate in artistic endeavors and in involvement with a much younger person of the opposite sex is Stephen Sharkey, the journalist. Stephen often used to work with Kate's husband; they were in Bosnia together, and in New York on Sept. 11. At the end of that harrowing day Stephen, heedless of the time difference, phones his wife in London only to discover that she is having an affair. This revelation, his own domestic tragedy in the face of so much other tragedy, precipitates him into giving up his job and trying to write a book about the way wars are represented. His brother, a successful doctor who happens to live not far from Kate, offers him the use of a cottage. There, as he works on his book, Stephen gradually becomes involved with his brother's au pair, Justine, the vicar's daughter, at first carnally and soon in more complicated ways. All's well in rural England.

But of course it isn't, and that too is part of Barker's cunning intention. As Stephen sits writing about Afghanistan and Bosnia, as Kate chisels away at her Christ, both underestimate their young friends. When Justine tells Stephen that because of glandular fever she's had to postpone going to university until she's 20, he tells her not to worry: " 'I expect they have a lot of mature students.' 'I suppose so,' she said, not detecting irony." And throughout their early encounters he has trouble crediting Justine with fully adult reactions and emotions. As for Kate, she aptly enough gets her first hint of Peter's hidden nature when she discovers that the spectacles he wears are clear glass.

A dark current of violence runs through "Double Vision," always threatening to erupt, seldom doing so. When it does, happily it does not reach the heights that Stephen experienced one night in Sarajevo. He and Ben were walking back to their hotel when they came across "a girl huddled on a mattress. She didn't speak or cry out or try to get away. Ben swung the beam along the wall until it found her face. Eyes wide open, skirt bunched up around her waist, her splayed thighs enclosing a blackness of blood and pain." Stephen pulls down her skirt and wishes he had the courage to close her eyes. Over and over, in nightmares and daydreams, the girl returns to him. And then months later, going through Ben's photographs, he finds her again. His friend had returned the following morning and restored her skirt to its original position, for the camera.

There are crimes for which there may be no forgiveness, events for which there may be no forgetfulness, and Barker does not seek to ameliorate these hard facts. What she does do in this absorbing novel is emphasize the importance of continuing to ask certain questions and of seeking human connections wherever we can find them.

Margot Livesey is the author of several novels, most recently "Eva Moves the Furniture." She is a writer in residence at Emerson College.
