BOOK REVIEW

Author's artistry is revealed in superior crime novel

By Andy Solomon, Globe Correspondent, 12/10/2003

(Tribeca Blues: By Jim Fusilli; Putnam, 273 pp., $24.95)

ADVERTISEMENT

When we left writer-turned-private-investigator Terry Orr last year at the end of "A Well-Known Secret," he'd just solved a murder case that led through a group of Bronx high school friends to some rotten New York City cops.

However, the killer he most wanted to find still eluded him: the madman who purportedly threw Orr's wife and infant son onto the subway tracks to be crushed by a train, leaving Orr to raise his 14-year-old daughter, Gabriella, alone. This proved to be "A Well-Known Secret" 's primary flaw; its backstory was far more engaging than its main plot.

In this far superior third installment in Jim Fusilli's Tribeca series, that backstory moves to the forefront. Fusilli brings readers up to date immediately with two pages of case notes from Orr's psychiatrist, Elizabeth Harteveld. She finds Orr "playful and charming" but also "contentious" and "narcissistic," clinging to the image of his wife and son's deaths and his survivor's guilt. Readers, though, may feel more sympathetic to Orr's pain than to Harteveld's diagnosis. After all, she lists among his symptoms "the idealization of his spouse." Who, except perhaps a second wife, would blame the widower for that?

As the novel opens, Orr's friend Leo Mallard has died. To mutual buddy and rock-music critic Dennis Diddio, Mallard leaves his bar. To Orr he leaves a request: Find Leo's ex, Loretta, and "make her pay" for what she did to him.

With Diddio, Gabriella, her friend Daniel Wu, and New York district attorney Julie Giada, whom Orr's been dating warily for a year, Orr heads to New Orleans for Leo's funeral. There he receives news of a death that shakes him even more. Elizabeth Montgomery Weisz, one-time scheming social climber married to a doting philanthropist, has died. Weisz had aspired to join New York's cultural elite so obsessively that she badgered her son Raymond into becoming a piano prodigy who gave public recitals at 10 and became psychotic by 14, when he ran away and was found living near-naked with wolves in the Bronx Zoo.

There's no one Orr wants to find more than Raymond Weisz, the man eyewitnesses said shoved his son's stroller onto the subway tracks. He rushes back to New York for Elizabeth's funeral in the hope that her son will appear.

Raymond doesn't attend, but Jean-Pierre Coceau, a witness to the subway deaths, does. Coceau relates an account far different than Orr had heard: Raymond didn't shove the stroller but tried to grab it to save the toddler. Worse, there may have been a painful reason Orr's wife was delayed in reaching their son.

This is where Fusilli displays an artistry far beyond that shown in his earlier books. In those, we saw a heartbreaking story: the bereft widower who has lost the love of his life and now, alone, must raise their child. A second-rate writer wrings from this theme all its sentimental juice and lets his reader float along on its wistfulness. The courageous and original writer works against the grain of expectations, looking to make our experience not easy but illuminative and true.

To Orr, Raymond becomes an object of compassion, the victim of a well-meaning but dangerously driven mother. The idealization of Orr's spouse, too, may be in jeopardy. In an old PBS program, Orr's painter wife Marina had said, "What it is we see isn't necessarily what it is. It is what we believe it is. What you bring to your vision is your emotions. This changes everything." Orr begins to understand what she meant.

He also starts to untangle a cat's cradle of connections from Raymond to Coceau to Loretta and back to Raymond, from murder to art fraud to schizophrenia, and from New York to New Orleans and back and forth again.

Unlike the prequels, here plot tension remains taut, and earlier flaws ripen into virtues.

One serious flaw remains. Readers will feel cheated by the limited role given to Gabriella, who is by far the most engaging of Fusilli's characters. As can happen in single-parent/only-child situations, Orr has turned Gabriella into his ideal companion, one capable of conversation worthy of a bright adult yet every inch a teenager.

This endearingly precocious 15-year-old with a fancy for fedoras and bowling shirts displays a spectacular range of color. She has completed a publishable manuscript of a novel, says of Giada that she "resembled the leisurely courtesan in Manet's `Olympia,' " calls her father from New Orleans to say she "saw a bus named Desire," names a pet basset hound Beagle, and has friends named Glo-Bug, Grumpy Eleanor, and Benny the Girl.

At the pace Fusilli's craftsmanship appears to be growing, though, who knows what twists Terry and Gabriella Orr's lives will take next time.
