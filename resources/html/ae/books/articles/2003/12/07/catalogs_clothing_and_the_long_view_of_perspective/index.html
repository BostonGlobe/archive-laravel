Catalogs, clothing, and the long view of perspective

By Christine Temin, 12/7/2003

The great German artist Anselm Kiefer is known for the weight of his paintings, both in messages and means: One of his favorite materials is lead. So the sheer slimness of "Anselm Kiefer: The Heavenly Palaces -- Merkabah" (Yale University, $35) comes as a surprise, as does the wispiness of the images within. This new volume reproduces an important artist's book Kiefer made in 1990, and with three essays added, it also reinforces the messages of these photographs of haunted spaces. The original book is in Harvard University's Busch-Reisinger Museum; Busch curator Peter Nisbet edited this fine adaptation.

Some of this year's best art books are catalogs of exhibitions that resurrected reputations. "Medardo Rosso; Second Impressions," by Harry Cooper and Sharon Hecker (Yale University, $50), and "Jean-Antoine Houdon: Sculptor of the Enlightenment," by Anne L. Poulet (University of Chicago, $85), fall into that category, both giving well-deserved attention to great sculptors of different eras.

It's been a big year for books on clothing -- not trends, but treatises. "Veil: Veiling, Representation and Contemporary Art" (MIT, $25) is one of the best. Edited by David A. Bailey and Gilane Tawadros, it explores the many meanings of covering the head in various cultures and eras, from Greek myth to the codes of modesty that have affected filmmaking in post-revolutionary Iran.

Also on the contemporary front, Robert Gordon's "Deborah Butterfield" (Abrams, $50) celebrates the sculptor who has spent the last quarter-century turning out ghostly, skeletal, life-size horses made from scrap metal and found wood.

And another entry in the contemporary field, the best represented in this year's heap of art books: "Art: 21 -- Art in the Twentieth Century 2," by Susan and Marybeth Sollins (Abrams, $45), is the written version of the smart PBS series, featuring interviews with 16 A-list artists.

Michael Rush's "Video Art" (Thames & Hudson, $45) documents a medium not yet a half-century old, but one long past the gimmick stage. Rush's book is, at the moment, the definitive look at the field.

For the 15th century, perspective was what video is today: a thrilling new world. From the 1400s onward, painters made significant progress in creating the illusion of three-dimensionality. "The Treatise on Perspective," edited by Lyle Massey (Yale University, $60), is an intricate analysis of their struggle to achieve depth in space.

David Bomford's "Art in the Making: Underdrawings in Renaissance Paintings" (Yale University/National Gallery, London, $30) is another fascinating examination of the process of painting. Looking at what's underneath the surface of a painting is a relatively new phenomenon, made possible by the development of infrared photography. It's like looking at the artist's underwear.

"Paintings That Changed the World," by Klaus Reichold and Bernhard Graf (Prestel, $19.95), is an ambitious title. "Paintings That Changed the Way We See" might have been better. However, for a survey that goes from the cave paintings of Lascaux right through Andy Warhol, this compact book does the trick, and in entertaining fashion. If you need to bone up on art to impress a date, this book is for you.

In "Signs of the Artist: Signatures and Self-Expression in American Paintings" (Yale University, $40), John Wilmerding traces American artists whose signature was more than a final flourish in the lower right corner; it was part of the pictorial composition itself, turning up in a wide and inventive array of locations.

And, finally, if you've been naughty rather than nice this year, Santa may reward you with "Art: A Sex Book," by John Waters and Bruce Hainley (Thames & Hudson, $29.95). Waters is working on a movie about sex addicts; Hainley writes for Artforum. They took time out to examine sex through the often filthy filter of contemporary art. There's lots that's flagrant here, along with some stuff you could actually hang on the wall of a kindergarten classroom.

Christine Temin is the Globe's art critic.
