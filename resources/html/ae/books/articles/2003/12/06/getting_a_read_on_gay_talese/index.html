Getting a read on Gay Talese

By Don Aucoin, Globe Staff, 12/6/2003

As Gay Talese made his way through the glittering array gathered to celebrate Time magazine's 75th anniversary in March 1998, he saw a familiar figure detach himself from the crowd and head his way: Joe DiMaggio.

It had been more than three decades since Talese's classic Esquire magazine profile of DiMaggio, "The Silent Season of a Hero," demystified the baseball legend by opening a window on his irascible nature and somewhat desolate life. On this night, almost one year to the day before his death, DiMaggio had only one question for the author. "Are you still writing for that rag?" he demanded.

From beneath his fedora, Talese smiles slightly at the memory. His lanky form is jammed into a Boston taxi. His tailoring is as trim and compact as his prose. He wears a dark-green, double-breasted Italian suit, and a yellow tie with tiny red polka dots. A red handkerchief peeks from his left breast pocket. For Talese, this is relatively casual dress, meant to get him through a day of interviews and train travel.

He repeats DiMaggio's question as though he is still struggling to decode that enigmatic figure. " `Are you still writing for that rag?' This is the greeting I got from Joe DiMaggio," he says.

For a dazzling period of a few years in the mid-1960s, before he moved on to writing "The Kingdom and the Power" and "Honor Thy Father," Talese's profiles and essays for "that rag" helped transfigure American journalism and nonfiction writing generally. With persistent reporting, unshowy craft, and no agenda beyond what he calls "taking your time, getting it right," Talese penetrated to the essence of such solitary, complicated figures as DiMaggio, Frank Sinatra, Floyd Patterson, Peter O'Toole, and Joe Louis. Those revealing profiles are among the 14 pieces, including some written in the past decade, included in a new anthology, "The Gay Talese Reader: Portraits & Encounters" (Walker & Company).

But how wide a public does Talese's work await nowadays? In the December Atlantic Monthly, literary editor Benjamin Schwartz writes that "The Gay Talese Reader" contains "some of the best American prose of the second half of the twentieth century." However, Schwartz also laments that many readers under age 40 "don't know Talese's work at all."

It is an assertion that the 71-year-old Talese finds entirely plausible. "I have a little position in writing classes; maybe some professor trots me out," he says with a shrug. "I do the best I can do." There is no emotion in his face or in his voice. He is clearly understating his renown, but it's equally clear that the even-keeled lucidity he brings to his portraits of the famous extends also to his own self-appraisal. David Halberstam may have called him "the most important nonfiction writer of his generation, the person whose work most influenced at least two generations of other reporters," and "The Gay Talese Reader" may be No. 3 on Entertainment Weekly's "Must List," but Talese seems to have little interest in his own mystique. Nor does he subscribe to the widespread notion that he, along with Tom Wolfe and Hunter S. Thompson, was one of the godfathers of New Journalism. "What the [expletive] did I do that was new?" he asks.

"It isn't rigid, old-fashioned, formulaic journalism, but it's not New Journalism," he adds. "It is the use of the writer's tools, meaning storytelling, the great tradition of storytelling, through the visual sense, bringing the reader into the room with you, or to the realm with you. It is using dialogue; it is exploring the notion of being someone else and wondering what it is like to be this person."

Initially, as an aspiring writer at the University of Alabama, Talese drew inspiration from fiction writers such as John O'Hara and Irwin Shaw. Only later, working as a copyboy and then a reporter at The New York Times, did he discover such masters of nonfiction as A.J. Liebling and Joseph Mitchell, and conclude, he says, that "Nonfiction is just as creative." He carved out a distinctive niche at the Times by writing about everything from the city's 400,000 stray cats to the captains of the Staten Island ferries. Still, he chafed at the space limitations of writing for a newspaper. In 1965, Talese left the Times and signed a one-year contract with Esquire for $15,000.

The magazine got its money's worth: In that first year Talese wrote "Frank Sinatra Has a Cold," "The Silent Season of a Hero," and "Mr. Bad News," a memorable profile of Times obituary writer Alden Whitman. With the likes of Talese, Wolfe, and Norman Mailer filling up its pages, it was a golden age for Esquire. "The great editor, Harold Hayes, was the reason you had that period, because he respected writing and he'd never tell you what you were supposed to do," says Talese.

"There were a lot of times when we gathered and didn't write," he adds. "There was a lot of work going into it before you write, because there's the desire to know deeply, to know in great detail what you're about to write, because in the detail you get the very special quality of the writing, because you know so much -- or you've tried to know a lot."

Next February, Talese will publish the second volume of his memoirs (the first was 1992's "Unto the Sons.") It will focus on his journalism career and "how I've wandered around looking for stories," he says. He was a dogged wanderer. When Talese first showed up in San Francisco in 1966 in hopes of interviewing DiMaggio, the latter accused him of invading his privacy and implied he was ready to take legal action. "He thought I'd dig up dirt on Marilyn Monroe or himself," Talese says, adding that he had no intention of doing either. But Talese found an ingenious way to ingratiate himself: He got a golf buddy of DiMaggio's to invite him to their golf game, and then used his boyhood caddy skills to find several balls the legend shanked into the woods. "He was a cheap guy," Talese recalls. "He didn't want to lose a golf ball. . . . I think it endeared me to him."

That same year, Talese wrote his now-legendary profile of Sinatra even though his subject refused to talk to him. Besides battling a cold, Sinatra was upset about a CBS documentary he feared would explore his alleged connections to the mob. "I just happened to get him on a bad week or a bad month," says Talese. So the author constructed the profile from the outside-in, describing the effects the singer had on those around him as he moved through Las Vegas, a recording studio, a movie set, a boxing arena, and the taping of a TV special. The story remains an invaluable snapshot of Sinatra at his charismatic best and bullying worst.

Some of the pieces in "The Gay Talese Reader" appeared in 1970's "Fame and Obscurity"; they are gathered with newer pieces like his 1996 account for Esquire of Muhammad Ali's visit with Fidel Castro, which he sees as thematically linked to the Sinatra piece. " `Ali in Havana' is the same kind of piece in that I don't talk to the main guy I'm writing about," he says. "But you follow their light."
