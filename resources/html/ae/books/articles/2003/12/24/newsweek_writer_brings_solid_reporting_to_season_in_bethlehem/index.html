BOOK REVIEW

Newsweek writer brings solid reporting to `Season in Bethlehem'

By Charles M. Sennott, Globe Staff, 12/24/2003

A Season in Bethlehem: Unholy War in a Sacred Place
By Johsua Hammer
Free Press, 288. pp., $24

ADVERTISEMENT
 

LONDON -- In April 2002, Israeli forces invaded Bethlehem, the steel treads of two-ton Merkava tanks rumbling down the narrow streets of the West Bank town and rattling across the cobblestones of Manger Square.

Palestinian fighters ran across the square toward the Church of the Nativity, where they shot the lock off the doors of the Franciscan monastery and entered the fortress-like basilica. There, the gunmen remained holed up along with dozens of Palestinian police officers and civilians who sought sanctuary, and along with priests and nuns who reside there. Israeli forces moved in and surrounded the church.

Thus began a 39-day siege at one of the holiest sites of Christendom, where tradition holds Jesus was born in a stone grotto that lies beneath the altar of a church dating back to the fourth century.

In Joshua Hammer's new book, "A Season in Bethlehem," this siege provides the climax of a gripping narrative. As Newsweek's Jerusalem bureau chief, Hammer covered the siege and all of the disheartening violence of the Palestinian intifada, or "uprising," against Israeli occupation as it unfolded from the fall of 2000 to the present.

As Hammer writes in the prologue, he set out in his book to chronicle not the full sweep of the intifada but rather to "hold one piece of the mosaic under the light" and to undertake "a biography of a place."

Hammer succeeds masterfully. Like the ancient, ornate tapestries that hang from the basilica's walls, Hammer's craftsmanship takes the strands of Palestinian and Israeli lives and weaves them together in Bethlehem.

At the center of the book is the Abayat tribe, a Bedouin clan whose sons became leaders of the Al Aksa Martyrs' Brigade, which to Israel was a terrorist cell behind a surge of attacks in the weeks before the siege and which to Palestinian Christian residents was to be feared as a band of thugs who set out to put their historic town in the center of the conflict.

Other characters include: the Palestinian governor who saw the danger in the brigade's tactics but could do little to stop them, a Christian businessman and interpreter who watched his town descend into chaos, a female Palestinian honor student turned suicide bomber, and an Israeli soldier whose mother was a founder of Israel's Peace Now and wrestled with his role as a commander of the Bethlehem invasion.

Hammer's reporting is superb, and the sheer weight of his work provides a momentum that pulls the narrative along at an engaging pace. The writing is spare and powerful. The re-creations of dialogue have a ring of truth as resonant as the church's bell tower.

(I knew many of Hammer's central characters, and several were featured in my book "The Body and the Blood: The Middle East's Vanishing Christians and the Possibility for Peace." Even as close as I was to the details of their lives and the roles they played in the siege, Hammer's dogged reporting brought out new facets of their stories and depth to their characters.)

Hammer takes readers inside Bethlehem and its surrounding villages, stripping away the cliche image of Hallmark nativity scenes and bringing to life a place full of despair and trying to cling to hope under both Israeli occupation and the corrupt leadership of Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority. Hammer alerts readers to the problems of the Holy Land's Arab Christian population -- revealing their diminishing presence as well as their sense of being squeezed by the forces of militant Islam and the army of the Jewish state.

Any reservations about this book have to do with an occasional tone deafness to the powerfully emotional chord that Bethlehem, as a place and an idea, can strike for so many Christians. In describing Bethlehem's early biblical history, Hammer writes, "It was here that the Biblical king David grazed his flocks of sheep and goats as a boy." He also writes, "Abraham and Lot camped here as they journeyed to Egypt."

He weaves this history of the Hebrew Bible's patriarchs with authority. But virtually every reference Hammer makes to the significance of the town in the Christian faith is stated with much less certainty. Hammer repeatedly states that Bethlehem was "purportedly" or "supposedly" the place where Jesus was born. The constant qualifiers clang like a missed note for a reader who might like to hear them put more elegantly, as "tradition" or "belief" rather than words more apt for a police blotter.

Nitpicking? Yes. But these phrasings serve as a hint of a more significant discordance in the brief, hasty conclusion.

In the final pages of the last chapter, Hammer accurately points out the storming of the holy site by armed Palestinian gunmen "exacerbated the ill will" felt by Palestinian Christians toward the Palestinian leadership. Then Hammer too quickly absolves the Israeli military of violating the sanctity of the church.

Hammer concludes that the Israeli military successfully achieved its strategic goals "without causing physical damage to the holy site or irreparable harm to their relationship with the Vatican and the Greek and Armenian Orthodox churches."

Beyond the fact that Israeli snipers shot out windows and left ancient walls pocked by gunfire and the upper floor of the Franciscan chapel burned from an Israeli-fired flare, there were seven people killed in and around the church by Israeli forces. Among the dead was a deaf and mildly retarded bell ringer whom Israeli soldiers conceded was mistaken for a terrorist.

Officials in the highest levels of the Vatican whom I interviewed were upset with the damage and blamed both sides for this. But the Vatican and Christian clerics from other denominations, such as Lutherans and Anglicans, were even more troubled by the extrajudicial killing that took place in the church, and for that they laid blame with the Israeli military.

From Rome to Bethlehem, there was a widely held belief among Christians that the damage to the stones could be repaired but the killing in a place that symbolizes birth and life was the more grave and lasting violation of its sanctity.

If Hammer was relying on the considerable weight of his reporting for readers to arrive at a balanced conclusion, then the facts are indeed there. But as soon as he set out to explore how history might judge this siege, his carefully crafted book owed it to readers to bring more shape -- and balance -- to its final assessment.
