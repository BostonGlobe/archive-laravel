'THE LIBERTY POLE' CHAPTER SIX OF A CHILDREN'S STORY IN EIGHT PARTS

A secret mission

By Jackie French Koller, 11/18/2003

Today, The Boston Globe continues its eight-part serialization of "The Liberty Pole," an original work of historical fiction for young readers by Jackie French Koller. The series will run Tuesdays through Dec. 2 and is geared for children in grades 4 through 7, though readers of all ages may enjoy it. Koller, who lives in Western Massachusetts, writes fantasy, fiction, and historical fiction. Her novel for young adults, "If I Had One Wish," was turned into a television movie. Teachers who would like to get The Boston Globe and a special teachers' guide based on this story should contact the Globe's Newspaper in Education office, 617-929-2639.

A few days later, a dozen young men of Homse's Hole left for the mainland to join the fight for independence. Among them were Polly's oldest brother, Sam; Maria's cousin William; and of course, Caleb. To keep their mission a secret from the British, they went by fishing boat rather than ferry, and much as Homse's Hole wished to give them a hero's sendoff, no public fuss was made over their leaving. Polly and Maria and Nell walked out to West Chop, though, and watched from the bluff as the little fishing skiff made its way out of the Hole. Polly tried to be brave and ignore the dreadful fear that churned in her belly. The three girls waved and waved until the sail was just a speck on the sparkling sea, then they linked arms and walked home solemnly.

"What if they don't come home?" Maria finally asked, her voice breaking.Polly fought back tears.

"They'll come home," she said."How do you know?" asked Maria.

"Because God will look after them," said Polly."Why would God look after the Americans and not the British?" asked Nell.

"Because the Americans are good people," said Polly, "and the British aren't.""Abel is a good person," said Nell. "I'm sure there are lots of other British soldiers like him, who really don't want to fight at all."

"Then they shouldn't fight," said Maria. "They should surrender and this whole thing would be done."

Nell sighed. "I just wish I knew for sure that war was the right thing to do," she said quietly.

A few months ago, Polly would have jumped at Nell for such a statement. Now, with their much-loved young men sailing across the sound and into harm's way, Polly wasn't so sure herself.

The British were highly agitated to discover that the young men of Martha's Vineyard were missing, no doubt guessing where they had gone. Abel was soundly reprimanded for not discovering Sam Daggett's intentions and stopping him.

"I'm afraid I'm going to have to keep a closer eye on you folks," he declared apologetically, with a nod in particular to Polly's father and her second oldest brother, Peter. "It'll be my head in a noose if any more of you turn up fighting for them rebels."

On June 14, the Continental Congress voted the American Continental Army into existence and appointed George Washington its commander in chief. Just days later, the bloody battle of Bunker Hill made the war starkly real, with 1,000 British and 500 Americans dead. The British won, despite their heavy losses, but the rebel blockade of Boston continued.

Since most of the cloth in the country had been imported from England, General Washington sent out a desperate plea to the women of the colonies to spin and weave cloth to outfit the army.

Glad of something to do to help the war effort, Polly, Maria, and Nell set to work with the rest of the women of Homse's Hole and spent the fall of 1775 spinning and weaving and sewing homespun coats for the soldiers.

Each afternoon, the three girls ran to join the group of villagers who met the ferry, hungry for news of the war. Word came of skirmishes and far-flung battles, but as to the whereabouts and well-being of the men of Homse's Hole, nothing.

"No news is good news," the women comforted each other.

The weather turned cold and gray and snow began to fly. Polly stared out at the lacy flakes that usually brought her such delight, wondering where Caleb and Sam and William were. Were they quartered in a warm patriot home as she hoped, or dug into a trench somewhere, shivering in their homespun coats? Was it all worth it? As the months dragged on and the numbers of casualties on both sides mounted, she began to have her doubts. So many young lives -- gone.

In February, the people of Homse's Hole began to hear about a little pamphlet called "Common Sense," written by a man named Thomas Paine. It had all of the colonies buzzing. At last, several copies were smuggled in and passed around the village. When Polly and Maria and Nell finally got their hands on one, they sat huddled around the hearth in the Daggetts' keeping room on a wintry March afternoon, reading by candlelight. Abel sat nearby, cleaning his gun.

"The cause of America is in great measure the cause of all mankind," Polly read aloud from the introduction.

"You ought not to read that treason in front of me," Abel interrupted.

Polly smiled. "What are you going to do, Abel, arrest us?" she asked.

Abel snorted. "I get no respect in this house," he grumbled.

Polly went on reading Mr. Paine's plainspoken words about the evils of governments and in particular of the British monarchy. Thomas Paine seemed to believe that the colonies were in a unique position to begin a new kind of government, the kind that all men craved by nature, a government conceived in and dedicated to liberty.

"The birthday of a new world is at hand," Polly read aloud. She looked up at Nell and Maria.

"What a thrilling idea," said Maria."Yes." Nell nodded slowly. "I just wish there were another way besides war to achieve it."

A knock came on the door and Abel rose to answer it. Maria's younger brother Anthony rushed in, ashen-faced.

"Maria, you've got to come home right away," he blurted.

"What is it?" asked Maria, rising to her feet.

"It's Caleb," said Anthony, then he turned and fled before they could ask another question.

Polly clutched her heart. For a moment it seemed that all three girls were frozen in place, then Abel broke the spell. He took Maria's cloak from a hook by the door and held it out to her.

"I'll walk you home, Miss Maria," he said. In a flash all three girls pushed past him into the storm.

"Hold on now," Abel shouted, grabbing two more cloaks and running after them. "You need to put these on!"

But Polly couldn't feel the cold. She knew Maria and Nell couldn't either. All they could feel was the pounding fear that throbbed in their throats.

By the time they arrived at the tavern, it looked like half the women of Homse's Hole were already there. One glance at their faces confirmed Polly's dread. Maria let out an anguished cry and rushed into her mother's arms. Nell covered her eyes and slowly sank to the floor. Polly stood still as a statue.

No. No, her mind kept saying. Don't look. Don't think. Don't let it be.

Then there was a step behind her and she turned and saw red.

"You!" she screamed, lunging at Abel and beating him with her fists. "You bloody Redcoats killed him!"

Abel grabbed her by both wrists and let her scream and twist and fight until her fury was spent. Then, when she began to cry, he pulled her close and held her tight.
