BETWEEN THE LINES

A book, and a life, tempered by fire

By Robin Dougherty, 11/9/2003

Maxine Hong Kingston lost her father, her home, and her manuscript all in the same short period in 1991. On the day of her father's funeral, she drove to the home in the Oakland, Calif., hills she shared with her husband only to find it had been destroyed by a wildfire. Along with her possessions, she discovered the remains of her novel in progress in the ashes. ''Paper had returned to wood grain" is how she describes it. ''I placed my palm on this ghost of my book, and my hand sank through it."

Stunned, she returned to the book but found that she couldn't devote herself strictly to fiction. Instead, she decided on an experiment in format. The result, ''The Fifth Book of Peace" (Review, Page D7), contains a reconstructed section of the original novel, sandwiched between her memoir of the fire and an account of her experience leading writing workshops for veterans, an activity she undertook soon after the fire and continues with.

Kingston, 61, won the National Book Critics Circle Award for her seminal 1976 memoir, ''The Woman Warrior," one of the first accounts of Asian-American assimilation and its stresses. She lives in Oakland, where she teaches creative writing at the University of California, Berkeley.

The losses that you suffered in 1991 were all devastating. Did you find that only other writers understood the import of losing your manuscript?

No. Most people who heard of it would say, ''How terrible." But I knew that Toni Morrison lost her house [on another occasion] and her writings. ... I read an interview with her in which she said she would only talk about that with me, because I'm the only person who knew [what it was like]. I can see how writers would be especially pained. ... A lot of paintings were [also destroyed in same fire], including [those of] a young artist getting ready for his first show. I know all of us writers and creators felt for one another.

The landscape of the fire made you think of war.

Yes, it did. And I immediately felt that I was in an aftermath of a firefight and thought this must be what a battlefield looks like. Then I doubted my perceptions because it's not as bad as war. ... It's just an act of nature. But then I heard so many people who had been ... in Europe in World War II and people who had been at Hiroshima or Nagasaki [who said] it reminds [them] of when the bombs fell.

What were the original three Books of Peace -- a legend or volumes that actually existed?

They are a legend, and they may have existed and then burned [or] gotten lost when there were changes of regime in China. It's been a tradition from the First Dynasty that whenever there was a change in government, the new government would burn the [old regime's] book and kill the historian. And this lasted through the Cultural Revolution.

One of the first things you did after the fire was to pay attention to your dreams. Why did you do this?

The book that burned in the fire was fiction. I could not write fiction again after the fire. In fiction, we care about other people -- even people we invented. I just wanted to shrink back and take care of myself, and I didn't seem to have the expansiveness of heart to have compassion for others. I think that dreams are something deep inside of us ... a creativity that is taking place whether we are trying or not trying. They come to us almost as a gift. When I lost my writing, I paid special attention to my dreams, thinking the inspiration and muse would come to me another way.

It happened that I had agreed to go to this conference in Texas. There were friends of [Carl] Jung, people from the Stanford [University] sleep disorder lab and there were sociologists and psychiatrists and dream experts from all over the world. ... I thought it would be so good to be around dream experts, so I could learn how to think more about where creativity comes from. ... I could get at my imagination again.

I met Ernest Hemingway's psychotherapist. I had all kinds of questions about Hemingway. His wife had lost [his early manuscripts]. She was bringing all his manuscripts in a suitcase and was going to meet him in Paris and ... she left the suitcase on a train. ... How did Hemingway get it back?

Did you find out?

No. You know what [the therapist] said? He said ... he makes a vow to himself never to talk about what they spoke about in their sessions and that he would especially protect Ernest Hemingway.

You learned that your father had also lost books to a fire.

My mother said that he had written six large volumes of poetry and they're all lost. ... I look at all the writers who have lost a book. So many people have this in common -- work being lost. ... It could be true that there are platonic books and they are whole and complete and they make their ways to people who want to write. [If they get lost,] they find their way to someone else.

You couldn't read for six months after the fire, and you couldn't write for nearly two years. You decided to gather a writing community around you. Why did you pick veterans to be that community?

Because I was interested in a book of peace. ... How can peace come about under the most terrible conditions? Is peace then an impossibility? Or is there a way to come out of war and find peace?

Robin Dougherty, a writer and critic, lives in Washington, D.C. She can be reached at inkrd@aol.com.
