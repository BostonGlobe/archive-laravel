Naked 'Lunch': life stripped bare of its trappings

By Sven Birkerts, 11/9/2003

Lunch at the Piccadilly, By Clyde Edgerton Algonquin, 252 pp., $22.95

ADVERTISEMENT

The endlessly quotable Gustave Flaubert once wrote to Louise Colet that he would like to write ''a book about nothing . . . which would be held together by the inner force of its style . . . a book that would have almost no subject or at least in which the subject would be almost invisible." If the master never came close to attaining his dream -- there were always unhappy provincial women and stuffed green parrots getting in the way -- he did at least express a desire familiar to many writers, that of getting rid of the cumbersome machinery of plot and theme. But where Flaubert aspired to what was finally a liberation of pure style, there are other reasons for shedding those traditional expectations. Chief among these would be creating a space for the ordinary uninflected movements of domestic life, giving a sense of the unstaged moment.

This is the approach that Clyde Edgerton has refined to a quiet perfection, and in this, his eighth novel, he reveals the delights, and the limitations, of the approach.

Making good on its title, the novel opens with what many more impetuous writers would deem the most unpromising sort of scene: a quiet bachelor, Carl, taking his elderly aunt, Lil, out for a meal at the Piccadilly. He helps her with her walker, they get their food, and carry on the desultory sort of conversation nephews and their aging aunts might carry on -- indeed, you could walk into thousands of cafeterias at this very moment and overhear these two people talking to each other just like this, the nephew lobbing easy questions, the aunt rattling on about people she lives with at the Rosehaven Convalescence Center, where she is recovering from a recent fall. Have I piqued your interest yet? No? I understand, but if you are like me, you read on because you simply can't allow that this might be all there is.

And it isn't. For the next scene has us privy to a battle of wills between nephew and aunt as Lil asserts her determination to drive back to Rosehaven, and Carl tries to bring her around to accept that her driving days are over.

He's right, of course, and we soon see why. Lil starts the car and to Carl's horror the thing takes off, filling him with the fear of the Lord before it circles back. The tone is perfect, matter-of-factness tinged with antic absurdity: ''Here she comes. He sees the front left headlight as the car turns toward him. Then there is the full front end. The car looks like it's smiling. Just over the steering wheel he sees the top of her head." I think of the old ''Where's the beef?" commercial. Why are little old ladies in big cars funny?

And here you have it, if not a prcis of the novel, then certainly a representative tissue sample. This will be the scale of events, the pace, the essentially unperturbed tone, though it will be the variations, the masterfully executed shifts that will keep the reader moving forward.

Most of ''Lunch at the Piccadilly" takes place at Rosehaven, where we meet Lil's friends Maudie Lowe, Beatrice Satterwhite, and Clara Cochran, whose main activity, as one might expect, is desultory porch conversation, and where we also meet the pretty social worker, Anna, with whom Carl becomes shyly smitten.

Are you still with me? What excitement there is -- and I use the word ''excitement" guardedly -- is generated by one L. Ray Flowers, a self-styled evangelical preacher with a past who is as interested in working up country songs on his guitar as he is in writing down wigged-out associative sermons.

Flowers is a catalyst, an agent of change; he roils the atmosphere. Is he on the run from something? Is he exaggerating his leg injury? Did he expose himself to one of the residents? Or is he a visionary, a man inspired with a mission to combine churches and nursing homes into something he calls ''Nurches of America"? Whatever his story, he exerts charismatic power over many of the elderly ladies at Rosehaven -- they are ready to give him money, go to Florida with him. And even Carl gets involved -- taking up electric bass so that he can back Flowers up when he does his enthusiastic performances, combining songs with the word of God and adding in notions that he's penned on stray scraps of paper, like: ''Listen. Old people are still alive. Alive. Their corpuscles breathe and move like tiny white things in tomato sauce. It's all any of us are given at the outset: life. It's all any of us lose: life."

If the progress of ''Lunch at the Piccadilly" resists summary it's because at no point does it really feel like a progress. Rather, Edgerton gives us a stray-seeming arrangement of days in which -- just as in life -- things do happen, but never with the ramped-up sense of cause begetting effect that we expect from most of the novels we read. Yes, Flowers creates social agitation wherever he goes -- not because he intends to, but because he is one of those people who do (we all know them). Yes, Carl makes awkward self-conscious overtures to Anna. And yes, Lil takes ill and fades and dies (but almost not so you'd notice), thereby bringing the novel to a close. But at no point do these events gather toward climax or resolution.

But I don't view this as a failure on Edgerton's part. He clearly intends the weave of his fabric to be just this loose, and the decision reflects a specific artistic ambition. He wants to shine the beam of his attention into the least likely place -- a convalescence center -- and upon the least likely people -- a group of the believably ordinary elderly -- to offer the ever-important reminder that the human comedy (and drama) plays out everywhere and always. Sven Birkerts is the author of ''My Sky Blue Trades: Growing Up Counter in a Contrary Time."
