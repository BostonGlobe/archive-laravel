BOOK REVIEW

Learning about business, and life, at a pawnshop

By Joan Axelrod-Contrada, Globe Correspondent, 11/9/2003

Everything Jack E. Rossin knows about business he learned in a pawnshop.

ADVERTISEMENT

That's the disclosure with which Rossin begins his highly entertaining book about life in the pawnshop. "I learned things about business and marketing that they don't teach at Harvard Business School," writes Rossin, now a business consultant. "I learned things they'd be afraid to teach at Harvard."

Rossin worked at Nate's Loan Office (not its real name) in Chester, Pa., in the 1960s. The experience gave Rossin, a fatherless boy, male mentors and money for college. He learned how to understand the customer and to successfully market a product.

"The Pawnshop Chronicles" reads like a collection of short stories. Rossin uses his skill as a storyteller to bring to life a world in which people down on their luck pawned anything saleable to get by. At the center of the pawnshop was Nate, a small, dapper man with a cigar in his hand.

The pawnshop made loans on physical collateral -- jewelry, small appliances, and the like. Customers who retrieved their property within four months paid back the loan with interest. Property not retrieved within four months was sold for any price the pawnshop could get.

These parameters gave Nate the flexibility to cash in on his understanding of human nature. Nate knew, for instance, that customers had an emotional attachment to the guns they pawned. Instead of putting the guns up for sale at the end of four months, Nate usually held them in storage. Customers who returned were so grateful to retrieve their guns that they readily agreed to pay the high interest.

The pawnshop also sold used clothing, the specialty of flamboyant salesman Inky Saperstein. Inky could sell used suits to anyone and so figured an aggressive style would work for processing loan applications, too. If a customer wanted $3 on a radio, Inky automatically offered less.

One day, Rossin overheard Nate correcting Inky on his lending style. Nate said that with regular customers Inky should always lend the maximum the item was worth. The business, he said, wasn't about squeezing someone for a dollar less than the asking price. Instead, it was about developing loyal customers.

Nate was a master of the "soft sell." With a hard-to-find item, he'd say, "We have one, but it's very expensive. You're probably not going to want to spend that much. It's top of the line." Even though the item was rarely top grade or expensive, the customer was always ready to buy.

Nate knew, instinctively, when he had leverage. If a woman came in to pawn an engagement ring, she usually wasn't in the mood to shop around for the best offer. She was in a seedy part of town and wanted to get the sale over with quickly. Her fiance might have paid $1,000 for the ring, but, when Nate got done with her, she'd be lucky to walk out with $75.

The pawnshop also sold items on layaway. The king of layaway items was a Ludwig drum set similar to the one Ringo Starr played. The pawnshop displayed it in the window along with a picture of Ringo Starr seated at his drum set. Nate collected 300 layaway tickets and nearly $4,500 on the drum set before finally selling it.

"It is the nature of people to have an appetite larger than their wallet," Rossin writes. "We took advantage of their nature."

Unethical? Perhaps, but Rossin writes about the many instances of pawnshop trickery without rendering moral judgment. Each anecdote ends with the matter-of-fact refrain, "It was just another day at the pawnshop."

In some instances, it's hard to say which is more striking: The deception or the brilliance. Take the "timing table," for example. Customers often bought back used watches, complaining they didn't keep accurate time. The pawnshop offered customers the option of having their broken watches put on the "timing table." Sixty minutes later, a kitchen timer would ring, and they could pick them up.

Although customers might have thought their watches were being scientifically recalibrated, they were simply placed on a dirty old card table in the back room. At the end of the hour, one of the pawnshop workers set the watch ahead or behind, depending on the problem. Customers, though, truly believed their watches had been fixed. Nate created a whole mystique around the timing table.

"The Timing Table was a deception, but the packaging around it was very real," writes Rossin. "It underscores a fact of marketing that some manufacturers struggle with: what's on the package is often more persuasive than what's in the package."

Whether or not such advice encourages shady business practices is the subject for another book. A book on business ethics, though, might be considerably less entertaining than "The Pawnshop Chronicles."
