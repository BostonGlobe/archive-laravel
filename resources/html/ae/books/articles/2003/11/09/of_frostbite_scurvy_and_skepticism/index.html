A READING LIFE

Of frostbite, scurvy, and skepticism

By Caroline Leavitt, 11/9/2003

In the early part of the 18th century, shipwreck stories were as popular as party nuts. Most were pure inventions, and the few factual ones were often embroidered to ratchet up the drama. Daniel Defoe's ''Robinson Crusoe," for example, was based on the exploits of Alexander Selkirk, a sailor who, unlike Crusoe, asked to be stranded for a few years. Readers didn't care if such accounts were true, as long as they sounded authentic and were thrilling. But when two astonishing real strandings, both in the Arctic, did occur, instead of celebrating the survivors, the public maligned them. What was going on -- and why?

David Roberts's riveting ''Four Against the Arctic: Shipwrecked for Six Years at the Top of the World" (Simon & Schuster, $25) begins in 1743, when a Russian whale-hunting ship, bound for the high Arctic, ran into treacherous ice and sank off the coast of Svalbard. Only four of the 24-man crew survived, carrying ashore one musket, a pouch of tobacco, and a bag of flour. For six years, the whalers managed to stay alive in a desolate, icy hell, battling polar bears, scurvy, and blistering cold, fashioning bows and arrows from ''driftwood, flattened nails, polar bear tendons and bird feathers," before they were rescued. Though sure they would be hailed as heroes, the survivors were viewed with suspicion, because surely no man could survive such an ordeal. They were asked to repeat their stories endless times in attempts to trip them up, and finally their narrative sank into legend.

Roberts is determined to track down the true tale, and decides the only course is to try to reenact the men's exploits himself. Of course, his deck is stacked a bit: He has a walkie-talkie, a survival suit, and locator devices. And he and his crew sail on a modern cruise ship, which, Roberts frets, ''trivializes the wilderness" even as they themselves are warned that their presence ''might compromise the experience of the other passengers." It's funny in a ''Gilligan's Island" sort of way, but once Roberts and his crew enter the frigid wilderness, the story comes exhilaratingly alive.

Although Roberts goes off on tangents, and some of his prose is truly wince-worthy, he pieces together the incredible mystery of how survival was indeed conceivable. Roberts and his crew board the doors and windows of their shelters against man-hungry polar bears and battle crippling cabin fever. He perfectly captures the terror -- and the beauty. In one singularly mesmerizing passage, he describes watching a polar bear rolling blissfully in algae-covered snow, slowly turning her white coat peach.

''The truth about the past was always slipping through our fingers," Roberts says, and in the end, he can't be certain this shipwreck story is true, just probable. But even so, what we get -- a gripping account of survival against extraordinary odds -- is so full of wonder and suspense, you can't help but believe.

Compellingly researched and told with novelistic flair, ''Ada Blackjack: A True Story of Survival in the Arctic," by Jennifer Niven (Hyperion, $24.95), is as brilliant and multifaceted as sunlight on new snow. In the 1920s, famed Arctic explorer Vilhjalmur Stefansson made it his secret mission to claim desolate Wrangel Island for the British. With false assurances that he would lead his crew, Stefansson put together an unseasoned group of four young men and a diminutive 23-year-old Inuit named Ada Blackjack, who was so desperate for money for her sick son that she willingly signed on as seamstress. Blackjack might seem an unlikely choice, but she was one of the most important expedition members because Arctic survival depended on warm, watertight clothing.

Almost immediately, the trip was a disaster. The cold was vicious, the brutal landscape unconquerable, and Ada fell prey to a kind of ''Arctic hysteria" and refused to work. Supplies ran out almost as fast as Stefansson's money, forcing three of the men to strike out for Siberia. It was up to Ada to care for the remaining man, Lorne Knight, now dying of scurvy, and to teach herself to hunt seal, trap fox, build a boat, and even fight polar bears. When Knight died, the astonishing Ada carried on alone for six more months until she was rescued. Immediately, she was heralded as a female Robinson Crusoe. But Ada never saw herself as any sort of a hero, and refused to speak, frustrating the press, who, knowing a good story when they saw one, began to portray her as a marriage-hungry harlot. It was only when they blamed her for Knight's death that Ada finally gave her account.

The stories we choose to believe tell a lot about ourselves. The Russian crew were forgotten because the wisdom of the day held that their success was impossible. Roberts found no tangible proof either, but his heartfelt re-creation led to remarkable support for the sailors' account. Blackjack was scorned because it was hard to accept that a woman could do what four men could not, but her diaries, and her own account, are revelatory. Maybe the real truth is told only when we're ready and willing to hear it.
