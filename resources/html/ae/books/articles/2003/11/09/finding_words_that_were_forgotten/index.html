ON POETRY

Finding words that were forgotten

By Peter Davison, 11/9/2003

Inner-Course: A Plea for Real Love, By Toni Blackman, Villard, 160 pp., $16.95

ADVERTISEMENT

X: Poems, By James Galvin, Copper Canyon, 67 pp., $14

Collected Later Poems, By Anthony Hecht Knopf, 256 pp., $25

We've heard plenty of loose talk about ''economic recovery" of late, but also a lot about a ''revival of interest in poetry." I'm a little dubious whether either is genuine. Doesn't the ''revival" mean that we may simply have come to ask something different of American poets? We have made the writing of poetry an official subject on university campuses and have gone so far as to endow poets with legitimate careers at public expense, including salaries, health insurance, tenure, pensions, and faculty meetings. (That, dear friends, is a big change. Picture Keats or Rilke or Whitman at a faculty meeting.)

Those who are looked to as spokesmen for poetry in this new century do not look much like Wallace Stevens or Robert Frost or Marianne Moore, and not only because our figureheads are (with one exception) younger. Take the three poet laureates since 2000: Stanley Kunitz, a venerable and magnificent holdover who once wrote, ''I suffer the twentieth century"; Billy Collins, a sly, witty, and accessible jazz-loving poet with comfortable Frostian outreach; and now, recently appointed, the admirable Louise Glck, a spiritual relative of Emily Bront and Emily Dickinson. They all teach, or have taught, the writing of poetry. We have a poet, Dana Gioia, for the first time, at the helm of the National Endowment for the Arts in Washington; we have a poet, Edward Hirsch, for the first time, guiding the Guggenheim Foundation. We have a poetry editor, Joseph Parisi, at the head of a new $100 million foundation in Chicago.

Has poetry itself changed? Not a lot, I think, except that talking about poetry in public can itself now, for the first time, constitute a paid career. Whatever a poet may do for poetry in public, however, when writing he or she still must confront the facts of life and nature, the tangle of his or her own emotions, the circumstances of a frightening planet, and the balky sounds and resources of language.

Toni Blackman, a celebrated hip-hop artist, in her first book (''Inner-Course: A Plea for Real Love") sounds confident of her crowd of hearers: Her language is that of every day, her sentiments dangerously close to those of public relations. Between the simplicity of her emotions and the conventions imposed by amplified performance, freshness of language and singular feeling tend to get lost in the cracks. Here's a complete poem entitled ''illin' ": ''even though my heart / beats faster before i dial / your number and / my stomach gets knotted / right before i see you / being love sick / never felt so good." There is no harm in calling this kind of sincere utterance ''poetry," but trying too hard to be accessible never got anyone a date with the Muse. Blackman's readers will be limited to the fans who can hear her voice, live or recorded.

''True feeling leaves no memory," Stendhal once suggested. It's worth wondering about that. When reporters ask a survivor ''How did you feel during the earthquake?" the reply is usually some inadequacy like ''Words can't describe it." One task of the lyric poet is to find a way around forgetfulness and speak the words that others have forgotten. James Galvin, a gifted Coloradan, in his sixth book of poems simply entitled ''X: Poems," conjures up one lyric after another, salted with wit and peppered with disillusion, which seem to find words for nearly everything but his book's title. His poems unfold a tale of foundered marriage, which ends with the exhausted poet's being able to do little more than scrawl his mark (X). They speak of a subtly undependable outer world of double rainbows, earthquakes, forest fires, and treacherous cities, evoked through a cunning and witty interplay of images, ideas, sound, and meaning: ''Here's / what the earthquake feels like: something you'd like to step off of, / please, onto something else, but can't." His book tells of the terrible grief that, as Dante says, comes of recalling happy times during misery. In the best of these poems -- the ones that avoid self- pity -- Galvin conveys true feeling.

Compared with young Toni Blackman and middle-aged James Galvin, the stately measures of octogenarian Anthony Hecht's highly formal poems sound majestic, orderly, and intense. He is one of the contemporary masters, perhaps even one of the last masters altogether, of the high traditional English style. The poems in his eighth book, ''Collected Later Poems," insistently remind the reader of lust, mortality, and decay; their conduct is biblical, classical, grave even when witty, given to prophecy. Some of them are illuminated with eerie woodcuts by Leonard Baskin.

This compendium comprises Hecht's three latest books in one volume. Some of the latest poems here, those from ''The Darkness and the Light" (2001), glow like expensive carved furniture stored in a dark dusty room; some indulge in a spate of unearthly words like ''enthymeme" and ''sororal"; others behave like a prophet walking on tiptoe; whereas the poems in ''The Transparent Man" (1990) and ''Flight Among the Tombs" (1996) are like drypoint renderings of nature as though incised through a clear window.

Here is a delicious segment from ''Proust on Skates": ''. . . preoccupied and intent / On a subtle, long-drawn style and pliant script / Incised with twin steel blades and qualified / Perfectly to express, / With arms flung wide or gloved hands firmly gripped / Behind his back, attentively, clear-eyed, / A glancing happiness." Whatever poetry is supposed to do, it seldom gets more precise and articulate than that.

Peter Davison's most recent book of poems is ''Breathing Room." He is poetry editor of The Atlantic.
