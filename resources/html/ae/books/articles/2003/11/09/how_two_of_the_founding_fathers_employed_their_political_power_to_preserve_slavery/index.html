How two of the Founding Fathers employed their political power to preserve slavery

By William E. Cain, 11/9/2003

''Negro President": Jefferson and the Slave Power, By Garry Wills, Houghton Mifflin, 274 pp., $25

ADVERTISEMENT

An Imperfect God: George Washington, His Slaves, and the Creation of America, By Henry Wiencek, Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 404 pp., illustrated, $26

We know Thomas Jefferson and George Washington owned slaves -- we commend ourselves for being able at last to acknowledge this fact in the lives of great Americans we admire and honor. But are we mature enough as a nation to face the possibility that we should not admire or honor them at all?

This is the question strongly implied by Garry Wills's and Henry Wiencek's new books. With devastating force of argument and meticulous detail, they demonstrate that Jefferson and Washington, owners and sellers of slaves, conducted public policy throughout their careers to make certain that slavery was protected and perpetuated.

Wills's book is shorter, more briskly paced, and more dependent (as he acknowledges) on specialized studies by Leonard Richards, Paul Finkelman, and other scholars. He is not concerned here with Jefferson's notorious relationship with the slave Sally Hemings or with the role that slavery played in enabling Jefferson to maintain his elegant lifestyle at Monticello. Nor does Wills say much about Jefferson's many, often contradictory, comments about slavery as an institution and African-Americans as a race. Instead he focuses on Jefferson's persistent attention to slavery in his formation of national and foreign policy, particularly during his two terms as president (1801-1809).

Wills's point of departure is that the US Constitution counted each slave as ''three-fifths" of a person. This meant that slaveholding states gained more seats in the House of Representatives and, as a result, more votes in the Electoral College than their white population alone would have garnered for them.

The ''three-fifths compromise" was the price that the South had demanded in exchange for its support of the Constitution: It allowed the Constitution to be written, approved, and ratified even as it chained the United States to a system of human bondage that violated the principles of freedom and equality affirmed in the Declaration of Independence.

As Wills, a gifted essayist and acclaimed author, shows, Jefferson would not have won the presidency without the additional votes in the Electoral College that the three-fifths rule gave him. His Federalist critics in the North, most notably Timothy Pickering, former secretary of state under Washington and John Adams, assailed Jefferson as the ''Negro President" who had been elected only because of the three-fifths bargain. To Pickering, the United States had fallen under the sway of ''the slave power," a phrase that dramatized, according to Wills, ''the power," permeating all branches of government, ''that slave states wielded over non-slave states."

Wills proves that before, during, and after his presidency, and on one important issue after another, Jefferson protected and strengthened slavery. The Haitian Revolution, the 1800 election, the appointment of judges, the 12th Amendment (which concerns the procedure for selecting the vice president), the Louisiana Purchase, the Embargo of 1808, the Missouri Compromise of 1820 -- in all of these, and many more, Jefferson displayed his commitment to slavery and ongoing effort to ward off any prospect of abolition.

Even the choice of locating the seat of the federal government in the District of Columbia was made to secure slavery. Jefferson, Washington, and James Madison wanted to site the US capital in the South, far away from the threats that slaveholders would encounter if the nation's business were done in New York, Philadelphia, or another city in the North. A swamp by the Potomac, Wills observes, was a strange locale for the capital, but it suited Jefferson and others who were ''fiercely driven" to ''carry the seat of government into plantation territory."

Henry Wiencek, a masterful historian and author of the award-winning book ''The Hairstons: An American Family in Black and White" (1999), does not address Washington's part in the selection of the capital. But this is perhaps the only omission in an otherwise brilliantly written, richly researched book. Wiencek has combed all of the relevant archives, inspected and probed countless documents and records, and explored and sifted through many oral histories. His account of Washington's life-long involvement with slavery is riveting from beginning to end.

Washington's Mount Vernon home, Wiencek says, was ''the manor house of a colonial potentate" -- the imposing product of slave labor. Made all the more privileged from his marriage to the wealthy widow Martha Dandridge Custis, Washington held the lives of hundreds of slaves in his hands. He watched over them with dogged, remorseless attentiveness, and he made sure they worked hard (16 hours per day in the summer, six days a week), once even demonstrating to an injured slave that hoeing could still be done with one's arm in a sling.

The food and clothing Washington provided to his slaves were pathetically inadequate; he ordered the lash wielded to cut the backs of slaves to pieces; and he assigned to women slaves the harshest, most distasteful labor (spreading manure, cleaning stables, removing stumps from swamps).

Washington also engaged in a lottery, held in a tavern, in which slaves were raffled off as prizes, and as Wiencek notes, ''he routinely separated husbands from wives and fathers from their children." When one of Washington's female slaves escaped in 1791 while he and Martha were living in Philadelphia, he did everything he could -- even using illegal means -- in a desperate but futile attempt to recapture her.

Washington did insert in his will an instruction that, after the death of his wife, his 124 slaves should be freed. (About 190 more slaves at Mount Vernon were part of his wife's estate; he did not hold title to them.) Yet it is impossible to view this, as Wiencek wants to do, as the triumphant outcome of Washington's ''moral struggle," especially because, as the author is impelled to concede, Washington failed ''to act upon his convictions during his lifetime." Washington was a slaveholding general and president who regarded black persons as his property to make use of as he pleased: When he wanted false teeth for himself, he ripped them from the mouths of his slaves. His last wishes cannot confer upon him a moral stature whose absence Wiencek's book as a whole so completely exposes.

These harrowing books testify that at last we may be seeing the extent and depth of slavery in American history. It is unnerving to realize the changes in our thinking that will result if we can summon the courage to confront the truth. We feel we still must honor Washington and Jefferson for the deeds they performed, however tainted they were as slaveholders. But Wills and Wiencek have now established that Washington and Jefferson used their public authority and political power to preserve the reign of black slavery. Should we honor such men or look elsewhere for our heroes?
