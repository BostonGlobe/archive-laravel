BOOK REVIEW

Author recalls a frightful past, but not in haunting prose

By Karen Campbell, Globe Correspondent, 11/17/2003

Giving Up the Ghost: A Memoir

ADVERTISEMENT

By Hilary Mantel

Holt, 240 pp., $23

For a memoir to be brilliant, it needs two things. Most obviously, it needs to hinge on an extraordinary life, a remarkable series of events, or a transformation compelling enough to draw in and resonate with the reader. Second, the author must have a distinctive writing voice, pairing clarity with a sophisticated command of language. Whether a work is powerful and poetic, darkly acerbic, or charmingly witty, it needs both persuasive style and technical mastery to move the reader.

Alas, most memoirs fall somewhat short, as does Hilary Mantel's "Giving Up the Ghost." The author of eight acclaimed novels, Mantel grew up in rural postwar England in an extended but poor Irish Catholic family full of secrets and simmering feuds. There were abusive teachers, bullying classmates, dysfunctional aunts and uncles, an absent father, and a stepfather who never actually married her mother. Her childhood comes across as grim and dark, essentially joyless, which makes it a bit tedious to slog through in spots. But what is remarkable is Mantel's ability to recall (and perhaps creatively reconstruct) her childhood in vivid detail. The extent of her visual and visceral memory is astonishing, and at its best, Mantel's writing is captivating.

Unfortunately, the style wanders, as does the narrative itself. Mantel tells her story in capricious, episodic bursts that prance back and forth in time and make it difficult to keep track of the basic framework and cast of her story. Instead of being meaningfully distilled, the memoir is too often indulgently abstruse, reading like an unfiltered stream of loosely connected memories. For long stretches, she writes in the flat, disjointed tone of a depressed child, reporting her history rather than translating it with any insight. She writes of the members of her large family being spread over adjoining flats, summoning one another by knocking on walls. At age 3, she is convinced she will grow up to be a boy. By age 5, she is practicing to be a priest. At 7, she has an imaginary confrontation with the devil, after which she is "always more or less ashamed and afraid."

Mantel recalls striving for "an unspotted soul, a soul edged with light, like a clean but open window." However, her religious aspirations only doomed her to failure. "I found that trying to be good wore me out and frayed my temper. It involved scrupulous vigilance, over your thoughts as well as your conduct, and it went on for all the hours that you were awake and struck in again if you woke in the night." But by the time she hit her teens, the nuns at her convent school had turned her into a Top Girl, her competence tempered by "a nervous politeness, an appearance of feminine timidity."

The defining element of Mantel's life is a mysterious chronic illness that began at age 4, when a series of fevers wracked her body and left her frail for the next 25 years. Though the litany of Mantel's ailments, diagnoses, and treatments is horrific, it's laid out in rather cursory fashion. We are told about the antidepressants, the antipsychotics, the radical surgery that leaves her unable to bear children. It is a heartbreaking tale, but it tends to elicit more pity than true empathy.

The memoir is severely lopsided, leaning toward early years most of us can barely recall, and there are huge gaps in her history. She only skims through ages 11 through 18, and her husband is barely a footnote. Of course, like memories themselves, a memoir can be fractured. It needn't give a crisp, balanced chronology. But "Giving Up the Ghost" lays out too much information that begs for further explanation.

Mantel does have other strengths. Occasionally, in the midst of her recounting, she interrupts short, blunt sentences with blazing insights or poetic discourses that rattle the soul, and that's where the book has its power.

Mantel creates vividly colorful, often florid metaphors that are stunningly imaginative. The most direct go straight to the heart of who we are. "You come to this place, midlife. You don't know how you got here, but suddenly you're staring fifty in the face. When you turn and look back down the years, you glimpse the ghosts of the other lives you might have led. All your houses are haunted by the person you might have been.

"The wraiths and phantoms creep under your carpets and between the warp and weft of your curtains, they lurk in the wardrobes and lie flat under drawer liners. You think of the children you might have had but didn't."

At her best, Mantel doesn't simply hit close to home, she knocks at our closets and opens our doors.
