Mining that feline mystique

11/16/2003

Calabash Cat and His Amazing Journey, Written and illustrated by James Rumford, Houghton Mifflin, 30 pp., ages 4 and up, $16

ADVERTISEMENT

The Grannyman, By Judith Byron Schachner, Puffin, 32 pp., ages 3-8, paperback, $6.99

Animal Scene, Written by Diane Ackerman and illustrated by Peter Sis, Knopf, 48 pp., ages 8 and up, $14.95

I am allergic to cats but not, thank heavens, to reading about them. It's unclear why cats have put in their appearance in so much children's literature -- from Wanda Gag's classic ''Millions of Cats" to the tale of ''Puss in Boots," to the many mysterious cats lingering around the edges of the Harry Potter novels. Perhaps because they seem to us enigmatic and wise and, when we are lucky, affectionate. Perhaps also because they seem less tame somehow than dogs, and therefore more akin to the untame world of childhood.

The calabash cat of ''Calabash Cat and His Amazing Journey" is not a real cat at all. He is clearly a carved-gourd sort of a cat -- this is clear not only from the illustrations but from author/illustrator James Rumford's note at the back. But stranger things have happened in children's fiction than a carved and fancifully engraved calabash cat setting off to find the very end of the world.

''In the middle of Africa there lived a calabash cat," the book begins-- an opening exotic enough to intrigue future adventurers. He sets off ''down the road to see where the world ended," and each creature he meets-- camel, horse, tiger, whale -- takes him a little farther on his journey. Each one sets him down where his or her own experience ends, with these words: ''Here, my friend, is where the world ends." Each is surprised at the ignorance of the animal preceding; no animal questions the complete truth of his or her own understanding.

''Calabash Cat" is told in the simple, straightforward language of good tales everywhere. I think it is a general rule that simple language is an invitation to magic. The color scheme, too, is as plain as can be -- black and white on a golden background, with just a line of color flowing through to indicate where we are: green for the jungle, wavy blue for the ocean, and so on. Rumford's artwork is dynamic and dramatic, done with thick black lines in ''primitive" style, always with a sense of motion. Alongside the English text runs the Arabic version, giving a wonderful sense of worldwide-ness to this story that is, after all, about how full and large the world is -- if we can only open our vision enough to see it all.

Judith Byron Schachner's ''The Grannyman" is not only a wonderful book about cats, but about old age and youth, and the deep, true affections that spring up between the two.

Simon is a very old Siamese cat, ''blind and deaf, and his bones creaked as he climbed up and down the stairs." Still, his family adores him. He lives a peaceful life, finding the very warmest places to sleep -- tops of radiators, under lampshades, sunny windowsills-- sometimes ''staring into space while he shuffled through a lifetime of old memories."

We learn all about Simon, as a kitten, a parent, a classical music aficionado. But too much life is behind him and too little ahead. He's ready to put his legs up and ''breathe his last" when a newcomer shows up, unexpectedly and a tad unwelcome. How Simon is won over and becomes ''the grannyman" is told with wit and affection in words and pictures. It is not often that one finds a book written and drawn with such commensurate skill, and even the best artists are not always their words' best accompanists. But Schachner knows how to tell her story on both sides -- a great deal of elegance in the prose, a fine freedom and humor in her softly color-washed art. Now out in this brand-new paperback edition, ''The Grannyman" is also a bargain.

For slightly older readers, Diane Ackerman's latest book of poems, ''Animal Sense," provides a fascinating view into animal life by way of each of the five senses. Ackerman is well known both as a nonfiction writer and a poet for adult readers. ''Animal Sense" embraces a wider audience than most children's poems, in language that is sophisticated yet takes a child-friendly approach. For the sense of touch, the ''alligator, for example,/has a skin shock-full of dimples/and puckers wrinkled like a blouse." Or, for the sense of hearing: ''Bats, when they are on the wing/make a tiny, searching ping!/as they hurl their voices at the world." Ackerman has written beautifully about the living, breathing world in most of her 20 published books, and this one proves no exception. ''A bird does not sing because it has an answer:/it sings because it has a song."

True, there are one or two unfortunate moments of personification (''Bees hate the movies,/they loathe TV") and animal dialogue that feel out of place in a book so otherwise exact. But Ackerman is mistress of the swiftly moving metaphor -- ''the owl: a pair of binoculars with wings"-- and Peter Sis supplies characteristically haunting artwork in uncharacteristically plain and simple color lines. The colored typeface is occasionally hard to read, but the graphics are otherwise bold and appealing. There's even a poem here about a cat: ''Sprawled alone along a branch,/the leopard lurks, awaiting lunch."

Liz Rosenberg, the author of ''17: A Novel in Prose Poems," reviews children's books monthly for the Globe.
