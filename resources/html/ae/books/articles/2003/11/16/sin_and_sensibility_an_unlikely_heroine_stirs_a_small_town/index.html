Sin and sensibility: An unlikely heroine stirs a small town

By Christina Thompson, 11/16/2003

Our Lady of the Forest, By David Guterson, Knopf, 323 pp., $25.95

ADVERTISEMENT

In his new book, the author of ''Snow Falling on Cedars" takes us back once more to the Pacific Northwest. It is autumn now, not winter, and what is falling is rain. Rain dripping from the boughs of alders, rain rising as damp in the rectory walls, rain floating in the air as a perpetual mist through which, one day, a waif-like mushroom-picker named Ann Holmes sees a ball of light hovering between two trees. Ann drops her bucket of chanterelles and runs for cover, but the light pursues her.

As it becomes more distinct she realizes that it is a human figure, ''a spectral, wavering face and a pair of incandescent hands." Ann falls to her knees, covers her face, and swears to God that if he will just protect her she will never sin again.

''Our Lady of the Forest" is a wry novel about sin and sinners, guilt, denial, and expiation. Guterson, who is fond of a twist and goes in for things that are not what they seem, takes an agreeably ambiguous approach to his material. What at some points feels like a sendup of Marian sightings -- for the figure in the glowing nimbus turns out to be none other than the Virgin herself -- appears elsewhere as a penetrating inquiry into devotion, faith, and the nature of religious feeling. Nor are the characters exactly straightforward. The most loathsome of them is capable of a redemptive turn, while the innocents are not so much chaste as just more sinned against than sinning. This is not a world of moral certainties, but rather one in which right and wrong appear and disappear like fir trees in the fog.

When the story gets out that the Virgin Mary has appeared to a ''diminutive, sparrow-boned" 16-year-old runaway, everything changes in the town of North Fork. A defunct logging town with a drunk and demoralized population, North Fork is suddenly transformed into a new Mecca. Motels and the campground overflow, cafes and restaurants see back-to-back seatings, even the hardware store picks up, doing a brisk business in tarps, flashlights, and batteries. Ann becomes an instant celebrity, a fact that eludes her, but that is not lost on her neighbor, a clever, disaffected mushroom-picker named Carolyn Greer. Carolyn, whose frank disbelief in Our Lady never wavers, becomes Ann's handler, controlling access to the visionary and collecting money on her behalf with a view to financing a winter trip to Mexico.

Eventually the hubbub is such that the church is forced to intervene. A representative from the diocese arrives to help Father Collins, the local priest, determine whether this is a legitimate apparition. Like Carolyn, though for different reasons, neither priest really believes that the Virgin has appeared. The visitor pooh-poohs the possibility from the start, citing Ann's unworthiness as a vehicle, the fact that she is not a Catholic, and her admitted habit of taking drugs. For Father Collins, on the other hand, the whole issue of the apparition's religious significance is swamped by his powerful sexual attraction to Our Lady's messenger. And though he urges giving her the benefit of the doubt, it is clear even to him that he does so for quite the wrong reasons.

Meanwhile, in another part of town, there is another drama unfolding. At its center is Tom Cross, an out-of-work logger with a bad conscience and a not entirely blameless role in the accident that paralyzed his son. Divorced, underemployed, and as bitter and twisted as a man can be, Cross is a curious element in the novel. Of all the characters, he is the most convincing, so vividly portrayed (and consequently so unnerving) that you cannot help but feel the author's deep and personal knowledge of the type. By comparison Ann is a mere cipher and Carolyn and Father Collins interesting caricatures of intellectual pretension and moral dishonesty.

But Cross is curiously inessential to the larger action. For most of the book he lurks in the wings, waiting for his cue. We see him violating the restraining order against his wife, stealing a mattress from the motel room he has been asked to vacate, perfunctorily copulating with a barmaid. But none of this has anything to do with Ann. Or does it? Cross's trajectory finally connects with the rest of the story at the climactic moment in the sort of tidy denouement for which Guterson has already shown a talent.

''Our Lady of the Forest" is atmospheric without being overwritten, clever without being contrived. As a stylist Guterson has come a long way from his early stories. His prose in this novel is wonderfully judged, funny in some places, rapturous in others, effortlessly swinging between the rhetorical excess of the transcendent and the deflating self-referentiality of the depressed. In terms of its message, this book leaves a lot to the reader. Within the damp and damaged world of North Fork, everything invites revision, everyone is flawed, and there are as many ways to construe grace as there are characters in search of it. But that, suggests Guterson, is OK. That is what it means to be human. That is how things are. ''The pilgrims looked up to the sky without hope. It was going to keep raining in all probability." They shifted inside their rain gear and waited, watching for a sign.

Christina Thompson is the editor of Harvard Review.
