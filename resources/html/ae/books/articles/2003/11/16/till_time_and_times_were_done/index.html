Till time and times were done

In his final decades, W.B, Yeats was passionately involved in politics, poetry, and occult experimentation

By William H. Pritchard, 11/16/2003

W. B. Yeats: A Life, II: The Arch-Poet, 1915-1939, By R. F. Foster, Oxford University, 798 pp., illustrated, $45

ADVERTISEMENT

On the final page of ''W.B. Yeats: The Apprentice Mage" (1997), the first volume of R.F. Foster's now completed biography, Foster lists some of the things Yeats would experience in his life after 1915 (the first volume ends with Yeats age 50). These include political revolution, war, supernatural revelations, marriage, fatherhood, and the publication of his greatest poetry to public acclaim. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in 1923; his masterwork, ''The Tower," was published four years later; and when he died in January 1939, he was ready to be canonized, a process that began with W. H. Auden's lines from his 1940 elegy ''In Memory of W. B. Yeats" written a month later: ''Earth, receive an honoured guest:/William Yeats is laid to rest./Let the Irish vessel lie/Empty of its poetry." In the subtitle of Foster's exemplary new volume, Yeats had become, in Oliver St. John Gogarty's words, the ''arch-poet" -- the master-in-charge whose artistic gifts and historical significance were on the way to universal recognition. Even T. S. Eliot, who had demurred earlier, finally came round in his posthumous Abbey Theatre lecture of 1940.

As with the previous volume, Foster -- a historian at Oxford -- practices what he calls ''rigorous reliance on chronology" to tell a story that reconstructs the everyday in order to establish convincingly a life's continuity. With Yeats the task is enough to make strong men (and putative biographers) quail. One of them, the novelist Sean O'Faolian, after assembling notes on his subject, gave it up, finding that Yeats had ''dived down so many caverns of knowledge and as quickly returned" that he was reluctant to make similar dives, figuring he would not return so quickly from them. The astonishing thing about Foster is his intrepid following of wherever Yeats, at any particular phase in his career, decided to go. So theater, politics, nationalist controversy, sexual intrigue, and occult matters generally all receive serious, extended attention. These are the years when Yeats partook (if that's the word) with his wife in automatic writing, producing items that led to publication of ''A Vision" in 1925 and had significant influence on many of the late poems and plays. Foster is never cowed by all of this, and almost manages to speak about arcane matters with a sympathy seasoned with wry skepticism. The reader never feels bullied into accepting as obviously true and important everything to which Yeats put voice or pen.

For example, there is ''A Vision," in which Yeats's ''system" is presented in its panoply of gyres, mask, phases of the moon -- all the paraphernalia readers found and still find formidable, if not off-putting. Foster says it represents ''a little of the best of WBY, and most of the worst." Its ''leaps of imagination, audacious strokes, unforgettably sonorous phrases" are flashes lighting up material that is all too often ''ponderous, self-regarding, wildly didactic, inconsistent, and unconvincing." This is surely the response anyone must feel who has struggled to possess a book that, as soon as it was published, Yeats himself found full of defects. Twelve years later, the year before his death, he brought out a revised edition, no less formidable if (Yeats thought) more sound.

The person who emerges most vividly and impressively from Foster's narrative of Yeats's later years is the woman he married in 1917, Georgie Hyde Lees, immediately renamed ''George" by Ezra Pound, a nickname Yeats adopted. Yeats had come to her precipitously, having proposed for a final time to Maud Gonne as well as, for good measure, Maud's daughter Iseult, with whom he was having a very strange ''fatherly" relationship. George was not beautiful, but owned a sanity and wit neither Gonne nor her daughter possessed. On the Yeatses' honeymoon at the forbidding Ashdown Forest Hotel in Sussex -- its prospectus, thoughtfully provided by Foster, featured ''Good English Cooking," with ''Invalid and Children's Diet a specialty" -- George brought Yeats out of deep gloom and a fear of mental collapse by beginning the experiments in automatic writing that proved crucially life-giving to the distraught bridegroom. As the so-called instructors began to speak through George, Yeats found an emerging and clarified pattern in his life that now centrally included his new wife. Although not everyone was impressed (Pound's comment on the activity was ''very very bughouse"), the wisdom George imparted, however it came about, was, in the biographer's words, ''astonishing" in its ''depth, ingenuity and oracular confidence." These experiments ended two years later, and eventually George would become thoroughly bored with the project and its results. But the immediate result was that the marriage got off to a superb start through a fusion of sex, the supernatural, and artistic creativity -- always for Yeats a potent combination.

In Vol. I, Foster wrote that in the interests of following a many-sided life he would not attempt to give critical accounts of Yeats's works as they appeared. Yet I was heartened to see in Vol. II how tellingly he manages to highlight and make judgments about the major poems as well as take the measure of Yeats's best collections of poetry, ''The Tower" (1927) and ''The Winding Stair" (1931). The commentary on ''Easter, 1916," Yeats's conflicted treatment of the martyred heroes of the Easter week uprising, is especially interesting. Foster quotes a letter from Maud Gonne telling Yeats why she disliked the poem (''It isn't worthy of you & above all it isn't worthy of the subject") and its insistence that ''too long a sacrifice/Can make a stone of the heart." Gonne said that his historical and philosophical studies should have told him that ''a sacrifice has never yet turned a heart to stone though it has immortalised many." Thus for all the poem's beautiful lines, it was not ''a living thing which our race would treasure & repeat." Seldom has there been a more striking example of the difference between a revolutionist's enthusiasm and a poet's double-mindedness.

At the risk of displaying insufficient reverence to the memory of W.B. Yeats, I found a highly satisfying part of Foster's biography to be its provision, through words by some of those who knew him, of a comic perspective on the various noble self-dramatizations Yeats loved to engage in. Ample account has been given of the importance of the tower (Castle Ballylee) he purchased, refurbished, sometimes lived in, and made a fact and symbol in various poems. But I hadn't known of Pound's referring to it as Yeats's ''Phallic symbol on the bogs -- Ballyphallus or whatever he calls it with the river on the first floor." The school-visiting out of which came his great poem ''Among School Children" (''I walk through the long school room questioning./A kind old nun in a white hood replies;") shows as hilarious when written up by George: ''Terrible 0 terrible . . . Willy asking blushing nuns how often the floors were washed . . . 'Do the children come clean? Or do you have to wash them?' More blushes." One of the visited schoolchildren 60 years after the event remarked that ''he [Yeats] was seeing a school-age Maud Gonne, and I was seeing a strange man who briefly interrupted my day."

''You were silly like us," wrote Auden, rather superciliously. Yet as Yeats's life draws to its close and the biographer places before us his remarkable last poems -- ''A Bronze Head," ''The Circus Animals' Desertion," and ''Cuchulain Comforted" -- we find that the comedy has become no laughing matter but something more rich, more like Dante's comedy perhaps. One of Yeats's many adoring lady friends, Dorothy Wellesley, describes him a fortnight before his death reading out loud a just-completed play, then throwing up his hand and exclaiming ''I have not been so excited about things for years. I want to find out the truth about the new ideas. That is why I want to live!" R. F. Foster has done marvelous justice to that life as Yeats lived it.
