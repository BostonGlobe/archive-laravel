HISTORICAL NOVELS

Back in the day, romantic excess and indiscretion

By Anna Mundow, 11/16/2003

The Taking, By J.D. Landis, Ballantine, 384 pp., $24.95

ADVERTISEMENT

The Mistressciass, By Michele Roberts, Holt, 289 pp., $23

One Last Look, By Susanna Moore, Knopf, 288 pp., $23

''The history of love," V. S. Pritchett wrote, ''is the history of absence, of arrival and departure." I am in Ireland, visiting my old hometown, where the fair colleens now flaunt pierced navels and yak relentlessly on cellphones. They are less real to me, these blank-faced lovelies, than are the ghosts crowding the pavements. Uniformed convent girls, tweed-suited ladies on bicycles. Their past -- my past -- seems more substantial than this beep-loud, accelerated present, and this realization, I suppose, makes me one of the relics of that vanished era.

At least the novels I brought along are reassuring, particularly ''The Taking," by J. D. Landis, which presents loss as a transcendental, even erotic state. I hear the rustle of your raised eyebrows and had the same initial reaction myself. ''The Taking" sounds unlikely; a novel that revolves around the state's flooding of five Massachusetts towns in 1939 to create the Quabbin Reservoir, that tells a love story reminiscent of Hawthorne, introduces a heroine who is obsessed by Emily Dickinson, and a young minister, Reverend Treat, who is unhinged by the approaching world war.

Treat has only one true convert, 17-year-old Sarianna Renway, who has recently arrived to teach the Treats' only son and whose devotion to Emily Dickinson has drawn her to a valley facing imminent erasure. ''Few of us would cover our tracks as well as Emily," Sarianna reflects. ''Yet how much better to know her lost. How much better to know that I'd be lost and Jimmy lost and Ethan lost."

Ethan is a Heathcliff type, all moods and traplines, who may be Mrs. Treat's brother, was certainly her first love, possibly the father of her son, and who is eventually wooed by Sarianna. The couple spend Hawthorne-ish nights in the forest, and the whole thing could have dissolved into a ''Blithedale Romance"/''Marble Faun" parody were it not for the sense of place and time that Landis eloquently creates. We see the forests, fields, and villages of the Swift River Valley suspended in the translucent light of a late summer evening and accept the romantic excesses of a story that has convincing roots in this transcendentally beautiful land.

The characters in Michele Roberts's Bront-inspired novel, ''The Mistressclass," flounder in the wake of loss, inexorably drawn back to the past as the narrative shunts between London, France, and the Yorkshire moors. The real location, however, is the mind, specifically the mind of the female artist. A gloomy place, judging by the masochistic letters that Charlotte Bront writes to her ''dear Master," Monsieur Heger, who taught and unwittingly captivated her in France. Charlotte, a true romantic, predictably burns these missives instead of sending them, but her yearning at least has a fascinatingly deranged tilt to it. The novel's modern characters, on the other hand, mope about in their tedious love triangle, discussing ''art." Dull company for a Bront, and Roberts's attempts to link the alternating 19th-century and 21st-century chapters is mechanical at best.

''Dull" is not the word for Lady Eleanor Oliphant, narrator of Susanna Moore's masterful new novel, ''One Last Look." Arriving in Calcutta in 1836 with her brother, the novice governor-general, frosty Eleanor soon realizes that she is being seduced. ''The danger of this place is that I am learning to deny myself nothing," the 34-year-old aristocrat confesses of an exotic world that she -- and eventually the entire British Empire -- must leave.

It is not just the opium. Eleanor is enthralled for the first time by something outside herself. India, where ''twisting clouds moved heavily across the horizon and sheets of silent silver lightning flattened the plain."

Eleanor's imagined journal (based on actual diaries) spans seven years but has the intensity of immediate reality whether she is describing monsoon rain, a servant's face, or the disastrous British foray into Afghanistan. She also reveals the family secret in feverish scenes that suggest the coziness of the nursery -- or the brothel. The invitation to ''give your brother a kiss" has never been so shocking, a reminder that Moore, author of the graphically sexual novel ''In the Cut," is equally capable of restraint.

If Eleanor's sin is a cramped, furtive affair, her larger transgression -- falling in love with India -- is the opposite. ''My life -- once a fastidious nibble -- has turned into an endless disorderly feast," she writes as she travels from Calcutta to Simla. There is nothing silly or noble about this transformation. Moore wisely resists giving Eleanor a conscience or an entirely generous heart. Instead she is granted the kind of sensibility and intelligence that makes her an inspired observer, even a dangerous critic.

''I know what England will be like," she remarks as she leaves Calcutta with her mentally derailed sister and disgraced brother; ''cool climate, good roads, white people, food inedible." Her London bedroom, however, will be painted saffron yellow.

Anna Mundow, a freelance journalist living in Central Massachusetts, is a correspondent for the Irish Times.
