A READING LIFE

The things that make fiction real

By Katherine Powers, 11/16/2003

''Twice in your life you know you are approved of by everyone," wrote Penelope Fitzgerald, ''when you learn to walk and when you learn to read." As a very young person I could not wait for that species of approval and pretended to read years before I actually knew how, sitting around the place in a prominent manner, clucking and hmmm-ing over the dreary, pulpy pages of Commonweal or The Nation. (The New Yorker, with its cartoons, was too frivolous for ''reading" purposes.) My parents, I later learned, far from being happy, were distressed by this sight and felt in it a reproach, because somehow they had been given to understand -- and, more incredibly, almost to believe -- that children should not be taught to read at home, that this was the proper task of schoolteachers; and, so my state of illiteracy lasted far longer than was natural or good. And then, oh, the dull days with Dick, Jane, Sally, and Spot in first grade, the brutal tedium of sitting there while the class, reading aloud, singly or in unison, ground out each word: no reading ahead permitted, no prospect at all, at this cruel slog, of ever getting to the text-rich adventures that lay at book's end.

With this dismal memory in mind, I am shot through with envy reading Fitzgerald on her own entrance to the reading world: ''I began to read just after I was four. The letters on the page suddenly gave in and admitted what they stood for. They obliged me completely and all at once, in whole sentences." This idyllic scene appears in ''Schooldays," one of a number of short autobiographical reminiscences that appear in ''The Afterlife," a collection of short pieces and reviews by Penelope Fitzgerald, all but one written between 1980 and 2000, when she died (edited by Terence Dooley with Christopher Carduff and Mandy Kirkby, with an introduction by Hermione Lee, Counterpoint, $26.95). This is a great and welcome treasure for the Fitzgerald devotee, which I am -- indeed, I will go so far as to say that ''The Bookshop" is one of the 10 best novels I have ever read in my life.

The subjects addressed in this collection are wide-ranging, taking on an improbable assortment of writers and artists, including Jane Austen, D. H. Lawrence, Philip Larkin, Roddy Doyle, the Arts and Crafts Movement, and M. R. James and his ghosts. Fitzgerald brings to these pieces that mixture of reserve and forthrightness, of kindness and mordancy, that is completely hers -- or, at least, that was hers in her later, published work, for we learn here that, as a child, she helped produce a weekly humorous magazine called IF, or Howl Ye Bloodhounds -- no samples of which, alas, appear in the present volume. She was, we see from this collection, an exceptionally generous reader and extremely interested in the lives of fictional characters, approaching them with as much curiosity and good will as she might have done if they had walked the earth. She clearly considered their acts and motives to be just as real and as open to scrutiny and discussion as those of so-called real people. It is this that makes her reviews so engaging, and it is also this, the sense that fiction is real, that informs her novels.

In the little piece called ''Why I Write," she admits that ''I get great satisfaction out of making people believe that this event happened at that time. Unlike history, fiction can proceed with confidence." With confidence and with enormous compassion: For me, Fitzgerald's novels are the most consoling I know. She was preoccupied in her writing, as she summarized it, with ''the courage of those who are born to be defeated, the weaknesses of the strong, and the tragedy of misunderstandings and missed opportunities." All this, she declared, ''I have done my best to treat as comedy, for otherwise how can we manage to bear it?"

For the person for whom reading is life, especially those of the novel-reading persuasion, there is a good deal of confusion about what is reality, for we pretty much inhabit our lives as stories whose character, plot, and moral substance are drawn from our reading. This state of affairs is most thoroughgoing in the teenage years, when one is forging, in both senses of the word, an identity. Michael Dirda's just published memoir, ''An Open Book: Coming of Age in the Heartland" (Norton, $24.95), wonderfully conveys this with self-deprecating humor, as does Tobias Wolff's ingenious first novel, ''Old School" (Knopf, $22).

Wolff's book is a further translation of his life, already the subject of his two memoirs, into fiction -- or back into fiction, as it may be. The young narrator is a pupil at a boys' prep school that is run according to egalitarian principles, but that is, nonetheless, rife with an unspoken understanding of class and ethnicity. The narrator has kept the fact that he is partly Jewish under close wraps, an evasion that subtly undermines his efforts at shaping an identity and achieving authenticity.

The story begins in the autumn of 1960 with a competition among the students to write a poem that will be judged best by Robert Frost. I give only a little of the book's abundant plot away by telling you that the winning verses, an admiring attempt by a boy to emulate Frost's imagery and sensibility, are judged by the great man to be an entertaining sendup of his own work. That misapprehension, galling though it is to the young disciple, is nothing compared to the denouement of the next competition, in which students write stories to be judged by Ayn Rand -- which, in turn, pales before the dark and deepening complexity of the final competition, where identity and truth and literature become absolutely confounded. This is a very fine novel, a deft tour de force that is not only strangely exciting, but that, by its end, achieves real profundity.
