BOOK REVIEW

Examining the brains vs. the brawn behind the battles

By Michael Kenney, Globe Correspondent, 11/25/2003

("Intelligence in War: Knowledge of the Enemy From Napoleon to Al-Qaeda": By John Keegan; Knopf, 416 pp.; illustrated; $30.)

ADVERTISEMENT

On the question of just how valuable intelligence is to the conduct of war, John Keegan, the British military historian and master storyteller, tips his hand early on.

"Popular attitudes to intelligence work," Keegan writes, have been shaped by spy fiction, but, he observes, "very few of even the most celebrated spy stories actually establish a connection between the spy's activities and the purpose for which [the spy] presumably risks his life in the field."

Erskine Childers's young yachtsmen in "The Riddle of the Sands" sleuth out how Germany could mount an amphibious assault on Britain, but Keegan points out that there is no suggestion that they "caused the Admiralty to take appropriate precautions." And for all the "intricate delusions and deceptions" of John le Carre's Cold War spies and counterspies, the Cold War continued. "They were playing a game," writes Keegan, "and the point was to keep the game going, not to win."

Similarly, in Keegan's case studies of intelligence operations from the Napoleonic wars to the war on terrorism, he writes, "[the] theme is that intelligence in war, however good, does not point out unerringly the path to victory," for victory is "bought with blood rather than brains."

Keegan makes a good, if not conclusive, case. The problem is that in illustrating it with case studies -- which allow Keegan to display his gift for storytelling -- he often seems to be disproving his own thesis.

In Nelson's 73-day chase through the eastern Mediterranean in 1798 -- superbly mapped, as are the other major case studies -- intelligence about the whereabouts of Napoleon's fleet was typically days, even weeks, behind the fact, and Nelson eventually deduced, mainly by process of elimination, that the French fleet was headed for Egypt, where he found and destroyed it. That's sort of like proving that it's dark by the absence of light.

A grand account of Stonewall Jackson's 1862 campaign in the Shenandoah Valley notes that Jackson, realizing that terrain was the key to victory, commissioned an out-of-work schoolteacher to produce a set of maps "based on local knowledge and derived from contemporary observation." The result, writes Keegan without caveat, was "the most brilliant exercise in manoeuvre warfare, depending wholly upon superior use of intelligence, in the broadest sense, perhaps ever achieved." That hardly proves his blood not brains thesis.

Although Keegan has built his reputation on a series of books detailing combat on land, including "Fields of Battle," his account of warfare in North America, his major case studies from the two World Wars involve the war at sea -- the British pursuit of German commerce raiders in World War I and World War II, the American victory over the Japanese at Midway, and the Battle of the Atlantic in which German U-boat packs attempted to cripple the convoys carrying war materiel to a beleaguered Britain.

With the Battle of the Atlantic, Keegan gives himself an opportunity to address one of the great intelligence battles of modern time, one in which both sides had access to superior sources of information about the other.

In one of his blood rather than brains conclusions, Keegan writes that "it was the stolid endurance of the merchant seamen, sticking to [the orders of] convoy commodores," and the "dogged determination of the escort crews to fight back, that did the U-boats in."

But he gets there by an account of the rival intelligence sources that seems to torpedo his conclusion.

Both sides were able to intercept and decrypt the other's coded messages: the Germans to read convoy radio traffic and the British to read transmissions to submarine commanders. While bad weather played a role in shielding convoys from attack, Keegan writes, "the deliberate rerouting of convoys away from patrol lines and wolfpacks . . . was the most productive method."

As for the war against terrorism, intelligence operations have been "frustrated," Keegan writes, because of the elusive nature of the Al Qaeda network. Attacking it successfully will require "the oldest of all intelligence networks, direct and personal counter-espionage" employing "brave individuals, fluent in difficult languages and able to pass as native members of other cultures."

And the model? One that only a storyteller himself would choose, Rudyard Kipling's Kim, the street urchin able "to shed his European identity and to pass convincingly as a Muslim message-carrier, Hindu gallant and Buddhist holy man's hanger-on, far superior to any holder of a PhD in higher mathematics."

It would be "ironic but not unlikely," Keegan writes, "if the literature of the imagination supplies firmer suggestions as to how the war against terrorism should be fought than academic training courses in intelligence technique." And his readers will know just where historian-storyteller Keegan stands on that matter.
