BOOK REVIEW

Fascinating account of Iran coup sheds light on Mideast situation

By Charles A. Radin, Globe Staff, 11/24/2003

All the Shah's Men: An American Coup and the Roots of Middle East Terror

ADVERTISEMENT

By Stephen Kinzer

Wiley and Sons, 272 pp, $24.95

Stephen Kinzer's new book on the CIA-sponsored coup that overthrew the government of Iran in 1953 has everything a good novel of espionage and international politics should have -- conspirators in high places, spy masters in the shadows, and a convincing, colorful cast of supporting actors.

But "All the Shah's Men" is not fiction. Rather, it is a careful journalistic reconstruction of perhaps the most profound decision US policymakers have ever made regarding the Middle East, a decision that set the stage not just there but in Asia and Africa as well for a sea change in perceptions of the United States.

Before the CIA arranged the removal of Prime Minister Mohammed Mossadegh, the United States was loved in much of the underdeveloped world as a defender of freedom and self-determination.

After the coup and the succession of similarly rationalized and ultimately disastrous interventions it inspired in Latin America, the Congo, and Vietnam, the United States is widely despised as an arrogant defender of rapacious and racist corporate interests.

Kinzer, a New York Times reporter whose previous book dealt in great breadth and depth with modern Turkey, goes deep again here, but keeps a razor-sharp focus on the plot. It is a good choice, for the result is fast-reading, thought-provoking prose with great contemporary relevance.

President Dwight D. Eisenhower wondered aloud, just before he let himself be convinced that the coup was necessary to protect national security, why it was not possible "to get some of the people of these downtrodden countries to like us instead of hating us." It's a question that has become one of the most frequently posed since Sept. 11.

"All the Shah's Men" supplies some answers.

The similarities in rationalizations regarding Iran in 1953 and Iraq a half-century later are scary. Power-seeking Iranians then, like certain Iraqis before the recent war, convinced American policymakers of what they wanted to be convinced: that it would be "impossible . . . to remove the present government by their own efforts."

Of course, 25 years after the coup, Iranians proved entirely capable of routing the shah and his American supporters, despite the fact that the shah had infinitely more military and police power than Mossadegh ever dreamed of.

Mossadegh and Saddam Hussein are vastly different characters, to be sure, and most Iraqis are still grateful that the United States toppled Hussein. But the fears and complaints that are fast rising among Iraqis about the course of the American occupation have at least some of their roots in the knowledge of what the United States did in Iran.

There should be no doubt that if Americans rather than Iraqis select Hussein's successor, one day the Iraqis will throw off that regime and join the ranks of America's bitter enemies.

What distinguishes "All the Shah's Men" from a raft of unreadable foreign policy tomes and editorials that preach such lessons is that Kinzer does not preach at all. He tells a gripping story, and the morals emerge organically.

Kinzer gets a lot of help in doing this from a terrific cast of characters.

Shaban the Brainless is as comic as he is thuggish in the role of leader of the mobs that roared through the streets of Tehran on CIA orders, creating the appearance of chaos that was needed for the CIA to justify destroying Iran's government to save the country from communism.

The directors of the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company -- an outfit now known as British Petroleum -- are as piggish and pig-headed as any evil "suits" Hollywood could dream up.

The shah is a whimpering coward, ready to live out his life in gilded exile in Rome, until the United States stiffens his spine and crushes his opposition so that he can instead enter history as a murderous autocrat.

Kermit Roosevelt, the puppet master who engineered the whole thing, is an escapee from the pages of "Smiley's People." And Mossadegh, his target, is interesting, tragic, noble, and courageous, at once shrewd and naive.

Had he not been toppled by the United States, this moderate, democratic, secular Muslim might have set a course for the Middle East that's radically different from the one that has evolved over the past 50 years.

Thousands of lives might have been saved. And some of the people of these downtrodden countries just might not hate us so much.

Charles A. Radin is the Globe's Middle East bureau chief.
