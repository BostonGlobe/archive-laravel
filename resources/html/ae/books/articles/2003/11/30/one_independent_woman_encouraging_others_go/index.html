BOOK REVIEW

One independent woman encouraging others: Go!

By Kathy Shorr, Globe Correspondent, 11/30/2003

A guidebook for the independent woman traveler seems like an oxymoron. After all, someone whose idea of a good time is hitchhiking across Japan or being the only guest on a cross-Atlantic freighter with an all-male crew hardly needs advice on how to stay open to new experiences while avoiding trouble. Curiosity, flexibility, and highly developed instincts are second nature for this kind of girl.

That said, plenty of women get nauseated at the thought of a group tour but don't quite have the moxie of a Gertrude Bell, known as the female Lawrence of Arabia, who was captured by Bedouins and stayed in a harem. And those in this wide-ranging category should find Zepatos's book useful, even inspirational reading.

Zepatos herself is an inveterate traveler, having roamed in more than 50 countries, for up to 18 months at a stretch. Nonetheless, she maintains, she felt great trepidation at first about taking off alone, and the book is a compendium of the advice and encouragement she has received and given over the years.

The result is a hodgepodge of practical tips, resource information, thoughts about the nature of travel, and personal stories by Zepatos and other female travelers. In this third edition are a lot of updated information and new material, like a chapter on traveling safely in a post-9/11 world.

It all makes for an interesting read, though it suffers from a sometimes awkward mishmash of styles and voices. And the polyglot approach makes it difficult to go back and retrieve information.

There's a little something for everybody, though Zepatos clearly isn't interested in anything along the lines of a "Great Cathedrals of Europe" trip. There's no list of must-do's or must-see's. The emphasis is instead on the kind of travel she prefers: to places off the beaten path and away from tourist sites, where the traveler can get to know local people and participate in the ordinary daily life of a place.

Many of the practical tips can be used by any traveler of either gender -- things like how to find cheap airfare or arrange a homestay, how to avoid diarrhea, how to manage life back home if you decide on an extended stay abroad.

Other topics are geared specifically to women, such as dealing with sexual harassment. Zepatos clearly has a lot of experience in this area, and she does a great job of mixing strong cautionary advice with humor. But what most sets the book apart are her ruminations on travel: Cultural exchange and understanding are, for her, the whole point of traveling.

She discusses such notions as how to figure out the social rules of a new place (where you may not speak the language), or how to understand the impact your visit may have on the people in a small, poor village. Zepatos often illustrates her point with entertaining stories of her own adventures in cultural understanding, as well as misunderstanding. In one episode, she takes two walks with a young man around his Greek village, without realizing that the villagers now consider them betrothed.

Some of her advice seems obvious, like "let go of expectations and judgment." But much of it any traveler will find engaging. And it's refreshing to see someone look at the drawbacks as well as the benefits of ecotourism -- bringing so many visitors that they end up driving away wildlife, or making the area so popular that the resulting development drives out the locals, for example.

Perhaps the most compelling story to encourage others to venture out comes from a woman named Kay Grasing, whose many travels include a trip to Turkey with a female friend. After her companion has an accident in Istanbul and winds up on crutches with her leg in a cast, Grasing persuades her to stay on for the remaining six weeks of the trip. It's a success: Some Kurds even help them climb a mountain, wrapping the injured woman in rugs, and carrying her by mule.

Ah, for the folly of youth, one might think. But Grasing was 67 at the time, and her friend on crutches was 70. "Here's my advice to women travelers of any age," Grasing writes. "Don't stay home because something might happen." She offers her own tips, guaranteed to nudge the slightly timid, including this: "I've found that hairdressers are pretty much the same the world over. I've had my hair done in Mongolia just as well as at home."

Kathy Shorr is a freelance writer who lives in Wellfleet.
