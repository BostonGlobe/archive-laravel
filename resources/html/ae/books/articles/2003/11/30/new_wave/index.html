New Wave

A flood of swashbuckling movies and books explores the hardships and perils of life afloat.

By Scot Lehigh, Globe Staff, 11/30/2003

First, there was the merest suggestion of something on the distant entertainment horizon. Then the unmistakable sight of a sail -- and suddenly a British frigate hove into view, streaming toward action on the big screen. For a lover of sea stories, the bowsprit-on image of a warship that arrived in late summer in the Sunday New York Times was enough to whet one's interest.

But it was the movie's title that truly quickened the pulse -- Master and Commander: The Far Side of the World. The film, which sailed into theaters this month, has brought the vividly imagined world of nautical novelist Patrick O'Brian to cinematic life. Russell Crowe stars as swashbuckling British officer, erstwhile ladies' man, and amateur violinist Jack Aubrey. Paul Bettany plays Stephen Maturin, ship's surgeon and cellist as well as friend (and early romantic rival) of Aubrey's. With Crowe at the ship's helm, Master and Commander is sure to attract thousands previously unfamiliar with O'Brian's work.

Yet that movie is but one of several ways the seafaring world, which as a movie genre seemed to have gone the way of the Western, has beckoned in recent months. On a lighter note, Disney's summer lark, Pirates of the Caribbean: The Curse of the Black Pearl, starring Johnny Depp, Orlando Bloom, Geoffrey Rush, and Keira Knightley, did so well at the box office that a sequel is planned.

In the port of the printed page, Caroline Alexander, whose book The Endurance helped make Ernest Shackleton a household name, has unraveled fascinating new twists in an old mutiny in her superb piece of historical sleuthing, The Bounty.

And in The Way of a Ship, maritime writer Derek Lundy has re-created the experience of a harrowing voyage around Cape Horn on a late-19th-century square-rigger, a voyage his great-great-uncle actually made (and the attempt at which Captain Bligh was daunted after a monthlong assault as the Bounty set out on its ill-fated breadfruit-gathering expedition).

Last fall, in Blue Latitudes: Boldly Going Where Captain Cook Has Gone Before, Tony Horwitz retraced the voyages of the legendary English explorer whose epic expeditions helped complete the map of the world.

Back in 1840, when Harvard-student-turned-swab Richard Henry Dana wrote Two Years Before the Mast, part of his purpose was to dispel the romantic notions of life at sea and to draw attention to the hardship, danger, and drudgery that were a seaman's plight. (Reading "Anarchy at Sea," William Langewiesche's absorbing piece in the September Atlantic Monthly, one can't but conclude that there are times when the life of poor sailors on derelicts sailing under flags of convenience is just as dreary and dangerous now as it was a century and a half ago.)

"No sooner has the young sailor begun his new life in earnest than all this fine drapery falls off," wrote Dana, "and he learns that it is but work and hardship."

Yet despite Dana's best efforts, today it's both a sense of adventure and an interest in the rigors of a life lost to time that the movies and books mine.

A ship, after all, is a world unto itself, a floating society of men and masters, of danger and discipline, of curses and commands, a world where spars, sails, lines -- and indeed, hell itself -- breaks loose when a gale closes in. It's a world made vivid by a rich textural stew of terms and jargon, the mastery of which is both a rite of initiation and one of the pleasures of reading O'Brian's expert prose.

The world afloat was also a laboratory of human psychology, a microcosm where, as Alexander shows, order could be so fragile that an intemperate accusation of theft of a handful of coconuts made to a proud and brooding master's mate could cause him to forget what side his breadfruit was buttered on and trigger a rebellion that would echo down through the pages of history.

Peril and hardship being most appreciated when least endured, it is those qualities that make sailing tales so memorable. As Lundy writes of deep-water sailors like his relative: "They went aloft a hundred feet or more on icy ratlines and footropes, up masts that could whip to and fro through 90 degrees of an arc in a few seconds, to grapple with homicidal sails, certain death just one small mistake, a slip, away."

It's a life that few of us would have wanted to live. And yet it's a world that proves irresistibly alluring when experienced from the plush comfort of a favorite armchair or theater.
