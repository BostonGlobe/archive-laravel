BOOK REVIEW

Grit and glamour on the front line

Getting the whole story on Martha

By Mary Leonard, 11/30/2003

Gellhorn:

A Twentieth-Century Life

By Caroline Moorehead

ADVERTISEMENT

Holt, 463 pp., $27.50

These times would have made journalist Martha Gellhorn's juices flow: chaos in Baghdad, suicide bombings in Israel, terrorist evil from Indonesia to Afghanistan. Armed with a diary and a flask of whisky, Gellhorn well might have stashed herself in an ox cart or hidden her golden blonde hair under a burka to find the human drama behind the frontline war reporting.

That was her trademark when, as that rare species of female foreign correspondent in the 1930s and '40s, Gellhorn dashed from one dangerous war zone to another, breaking the rules, telling truth as she saw it, and using whatever it took -- bravery, brains, her beauty -- to get the story.

"I had a theory that you can do anything you like if you are willing to pay the full price for it," Gellhorn once observed.

Until now, with the publication of Caroline Moorehead's "Gellhorn: A Twentieth-Century Life," the rich, colorful, and in many respects tragic story of Martha Gellhorn was largely untold. Ironically, the probing reporter and novelist was so fiercely private that she refused to give interviews or allow reprints of her work.

Sometime in the 1960s, Gellhorn donated all her correspondence and personal papers to the Department of Special Collections at Boston University. Moorehead, whose mother was a friend of Gellhorn's, used family connections to obtain exclusive access to 20 cartons of material after Gellhorn's death in 1998, and from it she draws a portrait of Gellhorn that is comprehensive and complex.

Moorehead does not dwell on the most tortured and best-known piece of Gellhorn's past -- her marriage to Ernest Hemingway from 1940 to 1945. The affair that began during a chance encounter in Key West, blossomed as a romantic adventure covering the Spanish Civil War, and turned from a boisterous marriage to a brutal rivalry in Cuba, was so painful that Gellhorn threatened legal action against anyone who thereafter put Hemingway's name with hers.

"I simply never want to hear his name mentioned again; the past is dead and has become ugly; I shall try to blot it out as with amnesia," Gellhorn wrote to her mother. "A man must be a very great genius to make up for being such a loathsome human being."

Gellhorn was a headstrong, liberated woman, born in 1908 to a St. Louis suffragist and social reformer. But, as Moorehead repeatedly observes, Gellhorn favored relationships with men -- whether she was trying to please them, as with her father, a physician and demanding intellect; learn the ways of the world from them, as she did with her lover Bertrand de Jouvenel, the passionate French pacifist; or use them for their inspiration (Hemingway), their attention, or their money. Eventually, she would leave them.

Easily bored, ever inquisitive, and temperamentally unsuited to sinking roots, Gellhorn had all the attributes of a foreign correspondent but the unfortunate birthright of being female. She dropped out of Bryn Mawr College and headed almost immediately to Paris to become a writer. Her gender and credentials didn't impress any employers, and she was soon back in the states, using her mother's friendship with Eleanor Roosevelt to become a roving reporter for the first lady, tasked with traveling to textile towns and the Carolinas and writing about the Depression's human toll.

There, in the personal stories of poverty and pathos, Gellhorn developed a writing voice that combined outrage and compassion. It was distinctive, authoritative, and far from objective. It made Gellhorn one of Collier's magazine's most popular correspondents for more than two decades, and a prolific chronicler of the 20th century through her stories and novels.

"What made it her own was the tone, the barely contained fury and indignation at the injustice of fate and man against the poor, the weak, the dispossessed. Nothing so enraged her as bullying, superiority, the misuse of power; nothing touched her so sharply as people who had become victims, through the stupidity or casual brutality of others, or children who were frightened, in pain, or did not have enough to eat," Moorehead writes. "For the next sixty years, in wars, in slums, in refugee camps, she used this voice again and again, handling it with such a sure ear for nuance that only very rarely did she stray over that fine line into the mawkish or shrill."

" 'All that objectivity [expletive],' was Martha's shorthand phrase for her belief that in war honorable reporters had to take sides," Moorehead writes. Indeed, Gellhorn became more opinionated the longer she worked and the farther she traveled, relentlessly criticizing US policy in Vietnam, staunchly defending Israel, and expressing contempt for the Arab world in general and the Palestinian cause in particular.

If there was compassion in her journalism, it did not carry over to her personal life. Gellhorn was famously vain, made few women friends, and almost always chose married men as her lovers. She was devoted to and dependent on her mother, but left town when Edna Gellhorn was dying and missed her funeral. Gellhorn adopted an Italian orphan but quickly lost interest in child rearing and wrote a will that made his inheritance conditional on losing weight.

Moorehead suggests that Gellhorn was clinically depressed, through she never saw a psychiatrist and would not indulge in introspection. She traveled and wrote until she was in her 80s and nearly blind, and even after that she cultivated young friends and kept the laughter and parties going in her flat in London, where she had lived alone for many years. Suffering from cancer and unable to read, travel, or keep up her appearance, Gellhorn put on a cream silk nightgown, arranged a vase of tulips, took a deadly pill, and committed suicide on a Saturday afternoon in 1998.

This could have been her epitaph: "I do not want to be good. Good is my idea of what very measly people are," Gellhorn wrote in a letter to a friend in 1942. "I wish to be hell on wheels, or dead. . . . Only a fool would prefer to be actively, achingly, dangerously unhappy, rather than bored, and I am that class of fool."

Mary Leonard is a reporter in the Globe's Washington Bureau.
