ON CRIME

Murder among the golf holes and pols

By jim fusilli, 11/30/2003

In Pete Dexter's ''Train," the threat of violence hovers over Los Angeles in 1953, and it's manifest at a country club where white guests scorn their black caddies and black caddies maneuver to establish a higher place among their peers. Observing the tension, and trying his best to avoid it, is Lionel Walk Jr., a circumspect young black man known as Train, who's a better golfer than anyone else on the course. He's also a youngster who seems like easy prey to heinous men twice his size, one of whom has taken up residence with Train's mother, another of whom runs the caddies at the club.

Into Train's life comes Miller Packard, a white Orange County cop with a sense of racial justice and an even more powerful will to self-destruct. When Packard is called to the scene of a double murder and rape on a boat in a Newport Beach marina, he does what his nature compels him to: He falls for the surviving victim, Norah, the scarred, newly minted widow of a wealthy, much older man. Norah doesn't invite trouble, but she and it are familiar companions, and enormous is her disdain for everyone she knows. When Packard, Norah, Train, and his punch-drunk ex-boxer friend Plural converge, it's terrifyingly clear that there will be no limit to the amount of ensuing turmoil.

''Train" isn't an ordinary crime novel, no more than was ''Paris Trout," Dexter's National Book Award-winning work. The bitterly wry writing sparkles, as Dexter's story weaves and insinuates, creating shades of morality in an atmosphere where violence not only foreshadows the eventual cataclysm, it defines daily living. No good will come of everything, the terrifically gifted Dexter says here, except, maybe, for a boy who despite it all may survive and, as impossible as it may seem, have a chance to thrive.

Jim Patton is walking in Elmore Leonard's footsteps in his second novel, ''Dying for Dana," a smart, crisp tale of murder and romantic obsession in the Pacific Northwest. No one does Leonard like Leonard, so Patton puts himself at a disadvantage, but his story's a compelling one, and soon we forget we're not reading the master but one of his gifted admirers.

District Attorney Max Travis falls for Dana Waverleigh, a knockout who craves love and rejection in equal measures. Before the two came together, Dana not only witnessed the prologue to a robbery and violent assault at the home of one of Max's friends, she was the one who tipped ringleader Jack Nizhl to the loaded safe inside. Jack, who was Dana's first love 22 years ago and the man she left her husband for just last year, knows she witnessed the crime, but her link to Travis makes her valuable, at least for now. Meanwhile, Jack's meth-smoking partner Nicky ''Nutcase" Bortolotti is on a one-man crime spree, which includes acting on his obsession for a slow-witted TV newsreader. As for Max, he's head over heels for Dana, which renders the gifted prosecutor vulnerable in more ways than one.

Patton's patience as he tightens the circle around the characters, bringing together the inevitable confrontations not a moment too soon, is what's special about ''Dying for Dana." And in Ms. Waverleigh, he's created a maddening character who somehow manages to win our sympathy despite her myriad bad choices. Derivative though it may be, as a saga of losers brought down by their own misguided impulses ''Dying for Dana" is a winner.

In William G. Tapply's 20th Brady Coyne novel, the nifty ''Shadow of Death," Boston-based lawyer Coyne attempts to unravel a series of untimely deaths somehow linked to Ellen Stoddard, a candidate for the US Senate from Massachusetts, and her missing husband, Albert, a Tufts history professor.

Brought in by political dealmaker Jimmy D'Ambrosio, Coyne hires private investigator Gordon Cahill to track Albert, who, we're told, hasn't been himself lately. When a body identified as Cahill's is found immolated in the PI's charred Corolla, the State Police want details of his final assignment. Despite his pleas, neither D'Ambrosio nor Stoddard is willing to release Coyne from his vow of trust, so the affable attorney must pick up Cahill's investigation. Soon he's in Southwick, N.H., where he seeks the links among the missing Stoddard, some suspicious financial dealings, and what once seemed the accidental death of a young teen during a snowstorm 30 years ago.

Tapply's yarn builds slowly, as Coyne gathers seemingly disparate facts while he tries to understand what's what with Professor Stoddard, and we meet sympathetic characters along the way who unwittingly face the same danger as Cahill did. By the time the attorney pieces together the puzzle, the body count has risen, and it's quite possible he may be next. A tasty and entirely plausible twist wraps up this satisfying yarn by Tapply, a consummate pro.

Jim Fusilli is a novelist and critic.
