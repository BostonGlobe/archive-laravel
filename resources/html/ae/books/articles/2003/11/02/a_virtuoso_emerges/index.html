BOOK REVIEW

A virtuoso emerges

By William H. Pritchard, 11/2/2003

The Early Stories, 1953-1975, by John Updike, Knopf, 838 pp., $35

ADVERTISEMENT

When asked whether he planned to leave anything out of a new collection of his poems, Robert Browning replied sharply: ''Leave out anything? Certainly not. What I have written I have written." In taking on the forbidding and potentially melancholy task of collecting his shorter fiction, John Updike is of Browning's persuasion. In the foreword to the volume at hand, he confesses that anything that makes it into print from what were ''initial hurried scribbles" takes on ''a certain valor," and his instinct is not to scrap it ''but to polish and mount it new." This he has done for 103 such valorous productions, with only a mere four pieces not making the cut. These ''early" stories are the product of the writer from fledgling 21 to veteran 43 -- from the year before his Harvard graduation to the year after he separated from his first wife and lived solo in Boston. We should expect the later stories, when they appear, to cover a roughly equivalent number of years and to be of a size similar to this ample 800 and more pages.

The next question he had to face was the order of stories, and here Updike has been ruthless in breaking up his published collections as they appeared and arranging them in eight sections. For the reader familiar with those collections, the arrangement takes some getting used to. But our way into things is eased by encountering, as a first section, ''Olinger Stories" first published 40 years ago and containing the ones about a young man whose life bears many resemblances to what we presume Updike's to have been. The 11 Olinger stories show the writer in a state that -- in the words of one of the best of them -- is ''The Happiest I've Been," and they provide heartening proof of Updike's belief that ''family life kept me in touch with American life." The hero is variously named John Nordholm or Allen Dow or David Kern, but he lives in a Pennsylvania farmhouse with his parents and grandparents, suffers social and sexual excitements and embarrassments, marries and moves away but comes back regularly to participate again in the family romance. A number of these stories -- ''Pigeon Feathers," ''Flight," ''The Happiest I've Been," and the two essay-fictions that close the section (''Packed Dirt, Churchgoing, a Dying Cat, a Traded Car" is one of the titles) -- are equal to anything produced by an American short story writer from the past two centuries and belong in the league of Nathaniel Hawthorne, Ernest Hemingway, and J. D. Salinger. Interestingly enough, Updike names Hemingway as the writer to whom he owed most, who ''showed us all how much tension and complexity unalloyed dialogue can convey, and how much poetry lurks in the simplest nouns and predicates."

But Updike didn't get his great subject, family life, from Hemingway, nor from his slightly older fellow companions in The New Yorker pages, Salinger and John Cheever, whose more fabulous narratives often transform the everyday. (The Glass family is no ordinary family, and Cheever's husbands take on mythical qualities.) The ''Family Life" section from this volume, in which Richard and Joan Maple first make their appearance, is especially notable for the way its stories manage to register, in neither fabulous nor tragic ways, the discontents of a young married couple with small children. In his foreword to the 1979 gathering of Maple stories, ''Too Far to Go," Updike said about the husband and wife, ''They are shy, cheerful, and dissatisfied. They like one another, and are mysteries to one another." These qualities are unfailingly rendered in the earlier Maple stories, from ''Snowing in Greenwich Village" to the humorously tilted ''Giving Blood" and ''Marching Through Boston" (a civil rights march to which the unwilling husband with a cold is dragged by his wife). But they are there also in stories like ''Toward Evening," ''Sunday Teasing," and ''Incest," in which the marrieds are not named Maple but are recognizably the same.

The most immediately puzzling of the eight divisions is ''The Two Iseults," and it has stories only the Updike aficionado is likely to remember, such as ''Leaves," ''The Stare," ''Museums and Women," and the wonderful ''Harv Is Plowing Now." More like poetic meditations than plot-and-character-driven narratives, they explore -- through the mind of the troubled protagonist in ''Leaves" -- ''the long darkness of self-absorption and fear and shame" in which the man has been living, poised or rather quivering painfully between wife and the ''other" woman. They exhibit some of Updike's densest writing, as far away in style as could be from the expert comedy of Maple stories like ''Your Lover Just Called" or ''Sublimating." In fact, readers who come to the collection with the canard that Updike is exclusively the laureate of suburban sexual follies (the author of ''Couples," that is) should become thoroughly disabused. For me his range and variety of fictional creation announced itself this time through three substantial stories I hadn't quite taken the measure of: ''The Gun Shop" (son, father, and grandfather in a Pennsylvania venue), ''The Hermit" (doomed effort of a man who has taken to the woods), and ''Transaction" (some business with a hooker in New York City) -- stories written within an eight-year stretch that included 30 or so others.

Maybe the least essential of the sections is one titled, wryly, ''Far Out," including items featuring various figures from the Jurassic Age and other far-out places. Here Updike the virtuoso is on display, as in ''The Baluchitherium," where our intrepid narrator interviews the largest mammal that ever lived on land. Even as we may grow impatient for a return to the familiar rural territory north of Boston, we marvel at the knowingness that went into the following notation of ''One of his extraordinary feet -- columnar, ungulate, odd-toed" -- that is however covered, like the entire body, with ''a lovely fur, bristly yet lustrous." Seldom at a loss for the right words, the interviewer promptly finds them: ''Such are the soft surprises with which reality pads the skeleton of hard facts" -- a sentence we living mammals would love to have composed.

Updike's sonnet, ''Enemies of a House," lists those enemies, mainly natural processes, then culminates in closing lines that introduce the human component: ''voracious ivy; frost heaves; splintering;/carpenter ants; adultery; drink; death." The final section in ''Early Stories," ''The Single Life," contains ''Separating," in which Richard Maple, about to lead such a life, tells his children of the impending separation between himself and his wife. This consummate story is made piercingly unforgettable through the consciousness of the man who has been an enemy of his own house. At a lobster dinner with the children assembled, preparatory to Richard's making his announcement, he finds himself in tears that ''became . . . a shield for himself against these others -- their faces, the fact of their assembly, a last time as innocents, at a table where he sat the last time as head." A perilous temptation for the writer to overdraw from the well of sentiment, yet as Richard cracks the lobster and sips champagne, ''the raw clench at the back of his throat was delicious. He could not help himself." In these, as in so many other moments from Updike's stories, all blessings are mixed and people incorrigibly themselves. William H. Pritchard is professor of English at Amherst College. His most recent book is ''Shelf Life: Literary Essays and Reviews."
