ON SPORTS

Long-distance running as reading; pistols at 2,500 feet

By Bill Littlefield, Globe Staff, 11/2/2003

Perhaps the most ambitiously literate of the sports-related books piled around me is ''Running in Literature: A Guide for Scholars, Readers, Runners, Joggers, and Dreamers," by Roger Robinson. Robinson, a professor of English at Victoria University in New Zealand, is a runner himself, and he's married to Kathrine Switzer, known hereabouts for running the Boston Marathon as ''K. Switzer." (After which, as they say, all the fish jumped out of the bathtub.) ''Running in Literature" provides Robinson with ample opportunity to celebrate his own favorite poetic accounts of triumphant running, as well as faltering, stumbling, falling, athletes dying young, and things of that nature. The pieces -- or pieces of pieces -- cited range from literature written by Homer, Saint Paul, and Edmund Spenser to a Linda Barnes novel (''Dead Heat") set in the Boston Marathon, during which somebody running around the Chestnut Hill Reservoir gets shot at.

Less literate is ''Dancing the Wave: Audacity, Equilibrium, and Other Mysteries of Surfing." This one begins: ''Balance. It is what brings about success in love as well as in a tightrope act." If you can get past what seems to me the inescapable (and not entirely comfortable) image of love on a tightrope, you next encounter: ''From harmony to the state of rest, from calmness and communion to fullness and serenity, there seem to be many advantages to living in a balanced universe."

Long ago in this very universe, I and a lot of other public-school third graders were led into a dark room with a linoleum floor to see a movie about ''Our Friend, the Sun." I'm pretty sure it began with the solemn recital of the words in the preceding paragraph. ''Dancing the Wave" was written by Jean-Etienne Poirier, so perhaps the problem was in the translation.

The 2003 edition of ''The Best American Sports Writing" has just arrived. I'll acknowledge up front that I was the guest editor of this annual in 1998, that I've worked on various projects with the series editor, Glenn Stout, and that all that pull notwithstanding, my own magazine piece on the passing of Seattle Slew didn't make the 2003 cut.

It will surprise nobody familiar with this series that the 2003 volume is full of terrific stuff. Especially outstanding are a piece by Michael Agovino entitled ''My Dad, the Bookie" and the account S. L. Price of Sports Illustrated wrote of the legend Pancho Gonzalez. Perhaps the most delightfully peculiar entry in this book is Los Angeles Times sportswriter Bill Plaschke's tale of Walter Donaldson, a combat-damaged, hallucinating golfer for whom the game is therapy. ''Golf . . . does not criticize or judge," Plaschke writes. ''Golf is one of the few things that allows Donaldson to be happy with who he is." When they have finished their nine holes on the ramshackle course Donaldson plays continuously, Plaschke acknowledges that Donaldson has beaten him by five strokes. '' 'Oh yeah?' says Walter Donaldson, looking confused for the first time this morning. 'I didn't know we were keeping score.' "

This brings me to the question of whether dueling is a sport. I choose to regard it as such, because otherwise how am I to immoderately praise ''Gentlemen's Blood: A History of Dueling From Swords at Dawn to Pistols at Dusk" in this column? Barbara Holland is the author of this remarkable and very funny history. She is wise and clever enough to disarm those who might object to her choice of subject matter by defending dueling as ''an improvement over the informal ambush, or sending henchmen to break the enemy's skull by night on the highway." She is also a gifted and joyful storyteller. She delights in recounting in a straightforward manner stories you couldn't make up: the encounter, for example, between two men, Monsieur le Pique and Monsieur de Grandpree, who were determined to conduct their duel from hot air balloons: ''The wind stood fair from the north-northwest. The balloonists managed to stay within roughly eighty yards of each other, and when they'd risen to about twenty-five hundred feet, M. le Pique fired and missed. M. de Grandpree fired back, apparently not at his opponent but at the more obvious target, his balloon. It dropped like a stone and smashed the duelist and his second to pieces on the housetops. Triumphant, the victor soared majestically off into the sky, descending unhurt some twenty miles away."

''Honor," one imagines Grandpree howling into the wind with a grin. ''I'll none of it."

For WBUR, Bill Littlefield hosts NPR's ''Only a Game." He co-edited ''Fall Classics: The Best Writing About the World Series' First 100 Years" with Richard Johnson.
