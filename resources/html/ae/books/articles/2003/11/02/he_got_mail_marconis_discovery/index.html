BOOK REVIEW

He got mail: Marconi's discovery

Tapping into telegraphy and its reverberations

By John Steele Gordon, 11/2/2003

Signor Marconi's Magic Box: The Most Remarkable Invention of the 19th Century and the Amateur Inventor Whose Genius Sparked a Revolution, By Gavin Weightman, DaCapo, 312 pp., illustrated, $25

ADVERTISEMENT

There are inventions and then there are inventions. Some are trivial, some very handy. Some change the world, such as the automobile. And some are so fundamental that they bring into being a whole new civilization.

The steam engine was one of the last. For the first time since the windmill, a new power source was available. And, unlike windmills and water wheels, it could make its own energy, not just capture it.

Within a hundred years, the world that James Watt, the inventor of the modern steam engine, had been born into no longer existed. An economy based on agriculture had been replaced by one based on manufacturing; a political system dominated by the landed gentry had been replaced by one dominated by the middle class.

We are still living with the unfolding ramifications of the steam engine, but we are now in the early stages of a new revolution that will be at least as profound, this one caused by the microprocessor. The world of our youth is destined to seem to our great-grandchildren as remote and technologically primitive as Watt's 18th century seems to us.

Where does wireless telegraphy rank in this hierarchy of invention? Gavin Weightman, in his new book, ''Signor Marconi's Magic Box," calls it ''the most fabulous invention of the nineteenth century."

That's some claim, considering that the 19th century saw the introduction of the railroad, the electric light, cheap steel, the telephone, the phonograph, movies, the automobile, and much else.

All of these strike me as at least as fabulous as wireless telegraphy. After all, it came more than 50 years after Samuel F. B. Morse first tapped out ''What hath God wrought!" in the US Capitol and his partner, Alfred Vail, 33 miles away in Baltimore, received the message almost instantly. Wired telegraphy was something very new under the sun indeed. For the first time in human history massive amounts of information could be moved cheaply across thousands of miles at the speed of light. It was also the first time that electricity was put to practical use.

Telegraphy revolutionized communications in a historical eye-blink. By the time Morse died, only 28 years later, it was possible for news from San Francisco to reach India within hours instead of months. To a world accustomed to waiting days for news coming from just a few hundred miles away, Morse's telegraph was fabulous indeed.

Guglielmo Marconi's invention, on the other hand, merely increased the reach of Morse's, allowing ships at sea (notably the Titanic) to communicate with land and for remote islands to be tied in with the world telegraph net. Still, wireless telegraphy had profound consequences, not so much for what it was but for what it led to. It was the direct technological ancestor of radio and then television, revolutionizing both the news and entertainment industries. Marconi's invention was thus a major step on the road to the global village. It is a story well worth the telling. I just wish that Gavin Weightman had told it better.

The book has three problems. First, it has an annoying number of niggling errors that the author should not have made and the copyeditor should have caught. It is county Somerset, not Dorset, that has a coastline on the Bristol Channel. The founder of the Cunard Line was named Samuel, not Thomas. Far more important, the author has largely failed to set his story in the technological context of the 19th century. Wireless telegraphy was just one step on a long road from Benjamin Franklin's early experiments with electricity to the wireless BlackBerry in my hand giving me my e-mail. But Weightman's discussion of that road is sketchy at best and sometimes just plain wrong. William Thomson, later Lord Kelvin, is not even mentioned. But, in a perfect example of what can happen when a great scientist asks the right question, it was Thomson's inquiry into what transpires between the time a wire is first energized and when the flow of electricity settles down to a steady current that led to Marconi's breakthrough and all that followed.

And Weightman tells the reader that it was Vail, not Samuel Morse, who devised the Morse code, the only truly original part of the Morse system of telegraphy. This is a well-known and much-retailed story, but it's not a true one. Even Vail himself said it was Morse who devised the code.

Finally, Weightman simply fails to dramatize his tale. Marconi's career, to be sure, doesn't help, for it was amazingly smooth sailing to fame and fortune. No starving in garrets for Signor Marconi. With his father an Italian landowner and his mother a member of the wealthy and well-connected Jameson family of Irish whiskey fame, Marconi was born to privilege, spoke impeccable, upper-class English, and traveled first class his whole life. He made his fundamental breakthrough before he was 22, and received substantial backing through his mother's connections to extend the range over which messages could be sent and to establish companies to exploit the technology. By the time he was 35, Marconi was world famous, rich in his own right, and had been awarded the Nobel Prize in physics.

Still, Weightman's book comes across as a series of sketches, one individual incident following another with little rhythm or distinction between what was important and what was less so. His characters, even Marconi, simply fail to come alive. ''Signor Marconi's Magic Box," while very useful for reference, is, I'm afraid, not a very enjoyable read.

John Steele Gordon is the author of ''A Thread Across the Ocean: The Heroic Story of the Transatlantic Cable," just out in paperback from HarperCollins.
