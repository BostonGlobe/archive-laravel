BOOK REVIEW

A kingdom by the sea, built of secrets and deception

By Thrity Umrigar, 11/2/2003

Love, By Toni Morrison, Knopf, 202 pp., $23.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Toni Morrison has become predictable.

As predictable, that is, as a Babe Ruth home run or a Lennon-McCartney composition.

Yes, Toni Morrison has become predictable in her brilliance, in her seemingly endless repertoire and ability to dazzle us with her shiny, luminous prose and breathtaking insights.

So, in that sense, her latest novel, ''Love," should fail to astonish. We have come to expect nothing less than perfection from our master storyteller.

And yet, like love at first sight, the novel has the ability to startle you out of complacency, to hurl you into a new and wonderful world.

The world that Morrison has so carefully created in her new novel revolves around Bill Cosey, the dashing, elegant owner of the now-defunct Cosey Hotel and Resort, a place where the black elite once flocked to unwind, discuss politics, socialize, and dance on the beach until daylight.

When the novel opens, Cosey is long dead but his memory is kept alive by the two still-squabbling women in his life -- his child bride, Heed, and his granddaughter, Christine, the child of his dead son, Billy Boy. Now in their 60s, Heed and Christine live in Cosey's large, crumbling home, encased in their individual loneliness, fighting each other as well as the ghosts from the past.

Like some modern-day Gatsby, Cosey also looms large in the minds of the residents of Silk, many of whom he rescued from their dead-end jobs in the local cannery by providing them employment at his resort. One of them, Vida, remembers him as a man whose ''pleasure was in pleasing," a man known for ''his laugh, his embracing arm, his instinctive knowledge of his guests' needs."

It speaks to the peculiar genius of Morrison that even when we learn the shocking truth -- that Cosey married Heed when he was 52 and she was a child of 11 -- some of the glow from his sunlit mythology still remains. And yet, Morrison makes us understand fully the horrors that sprang from that alliance: Cosey may have thought of it as love but we can see it as nothing more than a kind of purchase, where the wealthy hotel owner takes Heed off the hands of her poor and relieved family. The marriage also destroys the close friendship between the 11-year-old Heed and Christine, who is eight months older but now finds herself in the absurd position of having her childhood friend become her step-grandmother.

The arrival of Junior Viviane, the young, street-smart runaway who answers an ad for a personal assistant placed by Heed, threatens to disturb the wary, hostile truce the two elderly antagonists have negotiated, much as it unsettles the dust in the closed-up rooms in Cosey's mansion. Junior's presence hurls both women into actions that fray further a relationship that's made up of mutual distrust, hurt, fear -- and underneath it all, to our great surprise, love.

In some ways, this is the oldest story in the world -- how destroyed love transforms itself into hatred. In Morrison's able hands, however, this revelation does not feel trite or clichd. Rather, it gives the two female characters a heartbreaking majesty, a tragic heroism that eludes them for much of the book. And it makes us understand with new urgency the destruction that Cosey's impulsive decision to marry a child has wrought. Junior makes a good addition to Morrison's pantheon of strong, complex, self-guided female characters -- Pilate from ''Song of Solomon," Sethe from ''Beloved," Sula from the novel ''Sula." With a few deft touches, Morrison explains to us the roots of Junior's amorality, her easy sexuality, her frightening self-absorption.

Despite its universal title, ''Love" in many ways reads like a smaller, more domestic novel compared with some of Morrison's more ambitious works such as ''Beloved." And yet, the smaller canvas only highlights each painterly stroke. In language as elegant as a suite at the Ritz, Morrison creates two different worlds -- the glittering world that Cosey strode through and the shrunken, dusty world that he left behind to the women who had to pay for his mistake. In between those two worlds was the passage of time, and Morrison gives us a peek at that time also -- the civil rights movement, the sit-ins, the urban riots.

''Love" also abounds with that other Morrison trademark: an understanding of human nature that's so sharp, some of her insights land like a spear. Describing the desolate lives led by Junior's young uncles, she characterizes them as ''idle teenagers whose brains had been insulted by the bleakness of their lives, [so that they] alternated between brutality and coma."

When Junior carries a bottled cottonmouth to school for a classmate, the uncles, who have dropped out of school, do not understand the gesture: ''Something belonging to the Settlement was being transferred to the site of a failure so dismal it had not registered on them as failure at all -- but as the triumph of natural light over institutional darkness."

It is this blend of psychological and sociological insight that is the hallmark of Morrison's best work. Indeed, Morrison has become one of our shrewdest observers of class distinctions in America. Many of the major characters in ''Love" make the journey from childhood impoverishment to bourgeois comfort and middle-class security. But most of them remain as hungry as ever because, as the novel's last few devastatingly haunting pages tell us, material comfort doesn't buy them love.

And that is why most of them live in a world desolate as the moon, which Morrison memorably describes as ''a cold stone even incapable of indifference."

Thrity Umrigar is the author of the novel ''Bombay Time" and an upcoming memoir, ''First Darling of the Morning: Selected Memories of an Indian Childhood." She lives in Ohio.
