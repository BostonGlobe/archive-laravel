BOOK REVIEW

In French media mogul's fall, a Universal lesson

By Peter J. Howe, Globe Staff, 11/2/2003

The rise and catastrophic fall of Jean-Marie Messier, who was forced to resign as chief executive of the French media giant Vivendi Universal in July 2002 after an eight-year, $77 billion frenzy of deal-making, still perplexes many Americans.

ADVERTISEMENT

"The things that are going on in France are of France, and I don't think we can figure them out anyway." So said Hollywood television mogul Barry Diller, as he is quoted in this biography of his onetime boss at the USA Networks Entertainment unit.

There are many American parallels in this classic Icarus-crashing-from-the-sky tale spun by two Paris journalists, Jo Johnson of the Financial Times and Martine Orange of Le Monde. Messier, a made member of France's political corporate establishment, starts out as a Jack Welch-style diversifier and empire builder. Then he enters a Steve Case phase of overreaching media deals. He winds up his career in the manner of a Richard Nixon, making only guarded admissions as Vivendi Universal heads toward bankruptcy.

"Mistakes have been made," Messier declared only days before his ouster.

Despite these parallels, Messier's career makes sense only in the unique context of French corporate and popular culture, as Johnson and Orange make clear.

Messier was a product of the Ecole Nationale d'Administration, an institution as powerful in France as Harvard Business School would like to think it is in America. That prestigious academy inducts just 120 of the Gallic best and brightest each year into the tight fraternity that runs France's government and top corporations.

In 1994, after stints in the French finance ministry and Lazard Freres, Messier, at age 37, was chief executive of what is invariably described as "a sleepy 150-year-old water and sewer utility," Compagnie Generale Des Eaux. Messier itched to expand into then-sexy telecom and media. He found a key willing partner in Edgar Bronfman Jr., lead heir to the Seagram liquor fortune, whose family in the late 1990s parlayed what Edgar called a "boring" 24 percent stake in chemical giant Dupont into control of the Universal and Polygram music and movie studios.

Messier's $43 billion purchase of the Bronfmans' Universal empire in late 2000 came months after America Online and Time Warner announced their plans for a $175 billion merger. Seeking to outdo the Americans at global media domination, Messier zestily took on the mantle of "la France qui gagne" -- "the France that wins." Because he was given to signing his name "J2M," satirists soon lampooned him as j6m.com, "Jean-Marie Messier, moi-meme, maitre du monde," which can be translated as "I, Jean-Marie Messier myself, master of the world."

(Among the companies run through the Messier maw was the venerable Boston publisher Houghton Mifflin, which Vivendi bought in August 2001 and unloaded 16 months later -- at a $600 million loss -- to Bain Capital and Thomas H. Lee Partners.)

By December 2001, however, Messier inflicted the fatal wound of declaring "dead" France's vision of "a cultural exception." He took on national sacred cows like guaranteed slots for French-language movies on Vivendi's Canal Plus cable TV unit and threatened to revoke Canal's $150 million annual handouts to French moviemakers to make bad French movies.

Rage against Messier snowballed. Bizarre demonstrations outside his Paris office ridiculed him with obscene effigies. Messier engaged in one desperate maneuver after another to cover Vivendi's debts and prop up its collapsing stock. Finally his club of French bankers, industrialists, and corporate CEOs decided he had to go to save nothing less than French capitalism and national honor.

For good and bad, Johnson and Orange's account has many of the hallmarks of books by newspaper business beat reporters. They have cobbled together their clips and added some narrative, personality, and color, but retained long, indigestible accounts of abortive bank loan deals and threatened bond downgrades. Choppy writing also reveals how English is not a native tongue for at least one coauthor, with awkward gallicisms like "all Paris played at frightening itself" and Messier's "fight against the flow with a double project."

Johnson and Orange acknowledge that story "is the perfect prism through which to examine France's ambivalence toward America and to globalization," but then largely fail to mine that context. Still, as a gossipy, score-settling outlet for insiders to give their spin, "The Man Who Tried to Buy the World" offers a satisfying account of one of the most spectacular business implosions of the past generation.
