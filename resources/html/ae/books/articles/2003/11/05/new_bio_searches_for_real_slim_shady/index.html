BOOK REVIEW

New bio searches for real Slim Shady

By Renee Graham, Globe Staff, 11/5/2003

("Whatever You Say I Am: The Life and Times of Eminem" by Anthony Bozza; Crown, 278 pp., illustrated; $23.)

ADVERTISEMENT

Rappers usually have to die violently to garner the kind of serious, wide-ranging sociological consideration Eminem has received in just a few years.

Since his 1999 major-label debut, ``The Slim Shady LP,'' the man born Marshall Bruce Mathers III has been the subject of intense debate among politicians, feminists, gay-rights activists, and lots of people who know about as much about rap music as they do nuclear fission. It isn't just that Eminem is one of the best rappers around, though he certainly is that. He's been elevated to the status of cultural barometer for 21st-century America. Eminem has parlayed his contradictions - teen dream and public menace, homophobe and Elton John buddy, misogynist and doting father, young white man in the predominantly African-American rap game - into multimillion album sales, a shelf's worth of Grammys, a big-time movie debut, and an Oscar for his song ``Lose Yourself.''

Either by calculation or happenstance, Eminem has developed a rare kind of social and cultural malleability; he's adaptable to everything and everyone.

In ``Whatever You Say I Am,'' which takes its title from an Eminem lyric, author Anthony Bozza sets out to solve the enigma of Eminem. Bozza would seem the right man for the job. A former Rolling Stone editor and writer, Bozza has interviewed Eminem several times, though not specifically for this book. His 1999 story made Rolling Stone the first national publication to feature Eminem on its cover - a fact Bozza brings up at least twice in this book, adding that his magazine profile ``remains the most thorough chronicle of his upbringing until (and if) Eminem decides to tell us all the secrets he's kept to himself.''

Reading this book, one has to wonder if Bozza isn't auditioning for the job of the rapper's Boswell. Bozza hasn't written his book to be a straight biography, but he couldn't have intended this to be the unabashed hagiography it is, complete with a gushy dedication to Eminem himself.

Perhaps Bozza feels too close to his subject. He begins many chapters with his true-life tales of hanging out with Eminem - first in 1999 when the rapper was still living in a double-wide trailer, and three years later when he became one of the biggest stars in the world. Bozza's vignettes are intended to portray a coziness with the rapper, possibly to distinguish his book from all the other quickie biographies that have sprung up in recent years.

Far more compelling are Bozza's insights about Eminem's appeal. In his most persuasive chapter, Bozza considers the rapper's success through the prism of race, and he manages to avoid most of the shallow ideas that come up when considering the success of a white man in a largely African-American genre.

Of course, Eminem was not the first white rapper to achieve success. There's been the good (the Beastie Boys and 3rd Bass), the bad (Marky Mark, who now that he's an actor prefers to be called Mark Wahlberg), and the ugly (Vanilla Ice). But none have ever achieved the degree of commercial and critical domination enjoyed by Eminem, thus sparking fevered debates about hip-hop in literally black and white terms.

While he gives Eminem props for his witty wordplay, inimitable cadence, and never attempting to ``act black'' (the kiss of death for white rappers), Bozza also contends that ``denying that Eminem enjoys greater commercial success and a more diverse fan base because of his color is ridiculous,'' a point that the rapper himself has made in several songs.

``It isn't Eminem the person or the artist whom the artists (for the most part), executives, and pundits of the black hip-hop community oppose; it is Eminem as a symbol of institutional white domination in a genre reserved for blacks that is hard to accept. In this light, Eminem's popularity is a product of the white system.''

Bozza is less commanding when examining the homophobia and misogyny in Eminem's lyrics. He largely gives the rapper a pass, allowing it as just another clever, contradictory pose. He's dismissive of those who denounce Eminem's hateful lyrics, as though those who condemn him are simply too uncool to get the ``joke.'' Nor does he make much of Eminem's diatribe against Wahlberg, spiced with antigay epithets, which may say more about the rapper than a calculated duet on an awards show with a gay pop star could. Bozza also hurts his credibility with silly mistakes, such as misspelling the names of basketball stars Allen (not Alan) Iverson and Larry (not Byrd) Bird.

``Whatever You Say I Am'' is a solid read, but it stretches your patience. Whether readers are willing to endure layers of sycophantic proclamations may depend on how much they care about Eminem. If they do, they'll find a provocative portrait of a young man as reviled as he's revered, as well as telling observations concerning what it says about the rest of us when such a divisive figure becomes a pop-culture phenomenon.
