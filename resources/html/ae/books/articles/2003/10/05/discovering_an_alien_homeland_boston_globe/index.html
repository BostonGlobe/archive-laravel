BETWEEN THE LINES WITH SAIRA SHAH

Discovering an alien homeland

By Robin Dougherty, 10/5/2003

Saira Shah's documentary ''Beneath the Veil,'' which aired on CNN just after Sept. 11, 2001, gave us a glimpse into the horrific lives of Afghan women living under the Taliban. In her new memoir, ''The Storyteller's Daughter,'' the award-winning BBC journalist describes the dangers of entering Afghanistan to make the film.

Hiding a camera under her burqa, she captured unsettling images of the abuse that occurred. Her travels as a foreign correspondent also helped her lift the curtain on complexities of her own heritage.

Shah, who had an Afghan father, a Parsee mother from India, and a Scottish grandmother, was born in England and grew up in suburban Kent, among a family that observed a liberal strain of Islam. She was weaned on the heroic tales of her father's homeland, a place where ''coloured birds sing in the fruit-laden orchards. The pomegranates burst and their insides are rubies.''

At 21, Shah traveled to the Middle East to cover the Soviet-Afghan conflict. Here, first encountering the Taliban, she was shocked to realize that ''they have corrupted all the qualities I grew up believing to be quintessentially Afghan: generosity of spirit, courage, boundless self-confidence and . . . a sense of humor.'' Her memoir documents her attempts to reconcile myth and reality.

It must have been devastating to realize that the Afghanistan you had grown up hearing about and the one you experienced were not the same.

Yes, it had been a gradual process. I grew up hearing a version of it that was myth and legend. I had started to travel [there] when I was 21. I had begun to see the isolation and decline. The mujahideen became more and more corrupt. The Taliban was a reaction to that. When I went to look at life for women, that was the nadir. You couldn't actually have anything as diametrically opposed to what I grew up with. My father told us that ''women are the twin halves of men.''

When did your Eastern and Western cultures first intersect? Did you grow up in an exile community?

I'm from a strange background. . . . Growing up in Britain is different from growing up in America, where everyone is from somewhere else. I didn't have a choice but to look somewhere else. My father was ready with stories, and I wanted desperately to belong to this magical place. [He] thought it was really important for us kids to have a place where we belonged, that sense of identity. . . . I did have amazing experiences in the real Afghanistan. . . . It means a lot to me that it should be better than other places. . . . It's a wonderful place. It's been through hell.

Did your father assume your family would someday go back to Afghanistan?

Yes, when I was a young child, we always had the impression we'd go back any day. We absolutely all through my childhood believed it was a temporary thing. . . . When I was 15, the Soviet Union invaded and there was no more of that. . . . When I was 21 and said I wanted to go, [my father] went absolutely gray.

Do you have a sense of the role Afghanistan played in the Western imagination before the Soviet invasion? Wasn't Sherlock Holmes's Dr. Watson a veteran of an Afghan war?

Yes. There's a group memory in the West, certainly in Britain, about the campaigns between 1839 and 1919. There were three campaigns. In two, the British empire invaded, and, in the third, Afghanistan invaded the British empire. [In 1842], the British Army was wiped out except for one man. They very well remember Afghanistan. In the '70s, it was on the hippie trail. I run into these people who went there then and remember it fondly. I wonder if in my lifetime, people will say that. . . . I'd love to have other people share it.

What was the turning point for you, when you realized the Afghanistan in your father's memory was different than the one on the ground in Kabul?

In the '80s, I came across a story about the mujahideen who had sold their [American-supplied anti-aircraft missiles] to Iran. It was Khomeini's Iran at the time. They had done this for money. The thought was that the mujahideen [were] a noble fighting force and had been since the Soviet invasion. It was really a battle for me to realize this could happen and then to report it. I had to make a decision between my Afghan romanticism and my role as a reporter. I did report it. . . . I got death threats from the mujahideen, but more interesting was the American journalists who were in Peshawar at the time. The expat journalists there saw their role as a political one, they saw the mujahideen as noble. I was the last person they expected to report [the mujahideen's sale of the missiles]. I was in the terrible position where the expats really ostracized me. . . . I think it was when I realized that literal truth did matter to me. But having said that, I love the myths. I think myths can explain things that facts can't.

You had an experience that revealed how myths come into being.

A boy who was making bricks. He'd seen something happen he couldn't really explain. He told it slightly different each time. The director [of a documentary] made him repeat the [story] and he was getting more and more upset. It was about his neighbor being killed. In the end, he couldn't bear it anymore and he said, ''I saw the daughter shot and the mother came and she closed her eyes and she got married and there was a great celebration.'' If the book has a message, it's that we need literal truth, and we need myth and story, and we need them in some kind of balance, and we need to be able to identify each.

Robin Dougherty, a writer and critic, lives in Washington, D.C. She can be reached at inkrd@aol.com.
