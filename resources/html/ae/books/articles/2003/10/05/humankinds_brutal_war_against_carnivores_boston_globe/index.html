Humankind's brutal war against carnivores

By David Baron, 10/5/2003

Thirty thousand years ago, an unknown hand took charcoal to limestone and painted, on the wall of a cave now called Chauvet in what is now southern France, a remarkable mural. The scene, sketched with precision and drama, depicts a collection of animals hungrily eyed by a pride of great cats, artistic testimony that Europe, not so long ago, was home to lions.

Europe's lions are, of course, gone. As our human ancestors grew in number and technological sophistication, they altered the lions' habitat, stole the lions' prey, and slaughtered the lions as competitors. In other words, as David Quammen writes succinctly in ''Monster of God,'' ''Civilization rose, lions fell.''

This, broadly, is the theme of Quammen's exquisite and engrossing book, which takes as its subject not just lions but the entire assemblage of large, meat-eating, and occasionally man-eating, beasts that he labels alpha predators. Tigers in Asia, wolves in the British Isles, grizzlies in North America, and many other species of fierce animals have retreated (and in some cases completely vanished) in the face of human expansion.

Quammen offers a bleak prognosis for these creatures. ''The foreseeable outcome is that in the year 2150, when human population peaks at around eleven billion, alpha predators will have ceased to exist - except behind chain-link fencing, high-strength glass, and steel bars.'' What will be lost, Quammen argues, is a visceral, gut-level (literally) connection between humankind and nature. ''Alpha predators have kept us acutely aware of our membership within the natural world,'' he writes. ''They've done it by reminding us that to them we're just another flavor of meat.''

Books on man-eating predators tend toward sensationalism, but Quammen - for 15 years the writer of Outside magazine's Natural Acts column, and author of the highly praised ''The Song of the Dodo'' - brings to the subject his signature wit and keen perception of ecological and sociological truths. He takes readers on a journey to three continents, with side trips through history, mythology, and literature, to explore the connection between human beings and animals that sit one link higher on the food chain.

In India, Quammen visits the Gir forest, home to the last remaining population of Asiatic lions as well as traditional herdsmen, the Maldharis, who live in compounds protected by thorn fences and who fend off raiding felines with a hoe-like weapon called a kuwadi. In Australia, he meets with the Yolngu, Aboriginal people who revere the saltwater crocodile as a deity, a belief system that presents a wildlife-management dilemma when three 15-foot crocs terrorize a community that dares not kill them. Quammen treks into Romania's Carpathian Mountains, where a surprisingly large population of brown bears (European cousins of the grizzly) survives, and speaks with shepherds about the challenges of grazing sheep within the habitat of Ursus arctos. And he bravely wanders the frozen woods of the Russian Far East to interview the Udege, an indigenous tribe that has coexisted for centuries with the largest living cat the Amur tiger.

Part anthropologist, part talk-show host, Quammen peppers his witnesses with queries about what its like to live with lions/crocodiles/bears/tigers. Are these animals revered? Reviled? Feared? The responses seem, at first, without pattern. ''One man's monster is another man's god,'' writes Quammen. But an important theme emerges. Although alpha predators can be destructive, annoying, and deadly, the people who live with these animals generally suffer greater hardships from the actions of other people or from the neglect of society at large. (One Maldhari man, the victim of two lion maulings, tells Quammen he is more concerned about the lack of a local school for his son than the threat posed by the resident big cats.)

And the theme has a corollary: Those who suffer most from alpha predators are the indigent, the powerless, the disenfranchised. ''Is it inevitable that the costs exacted by alpha predators be borne disproportionately by poor people,'' Quammen asks, ''while the spiritual and aesthetic benefits of those magnificent beasts are enjoyed from afar? If not, then how should society redistribute the costs?'' He provides no answer. This is not a policy manual; it is an extended, personal essay.

I do not entirely accept ''Monster of God'' 's loomy premise, that alpha predators are doomed in the wild. In the United States, large, carnivorous animals - alligators in Florida, wolves in the Upper Midwest, mountain lions out West - are making a remarkable comeback, having benefited greatly from state and federal wildlife-protection laws. Even the creatures that Quammen focuses on - Gir lions, Australian crocodiles, Carpathian brown bears, and Amur tigers - have rebounded in recent decades, the intended result of conservation programs and of treaties banning the trade in endangered species.

Still, the continued existence of these species is by no means assured, and I do not doubt that the prospect of being eaten by a wild animal will seem extraordinarily remote to Earth's urban-dwelling billions a century and a half from now. In this way, ''Monster of God'' could serve as an important record for posterity, like a message in a time capsule, a souvenir of a time when people really did live among majestic, frightening creatures - the literary equivalent of those evocative, ancient paintings on the walls of Chauvet Cave.

Monster of God

By David Quammen

Norton, 515 pp., $26.95

David Baron, former science correspondent for National Public Radio, is author of ''The Beast in the Garden,'' to be published by Norton in November.
