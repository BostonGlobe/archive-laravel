ON AUDIO

Across the USA, via jalopy, fiction

By Rochelle O'Gorman, 10/5/2003

Ghost Riders

By Sharyn McCrumb

HighBridge Audio; unabridged fiction; four CDs; four hours and 45 minutes; $26.95; read by the authors. The same production is available on four cassettes for $26.95.

Since quite a bit of Americana has been arriving on audio of late, why don't we just take an aural tour, starting with the Civil War?

North Carolina writer Sharyn McCrumb has penned another of her multilayered tales set in Appalachia. ''Ghost Riders'' is primarily about the Civil War and the effects it had on families, neighbors, politics, and, eventually, history. Based on real people and events, the book turns a horrific era into an intensely personal tale.

Is it her best? Well, no. This is a decently written historical drama, but we have seen more spirited stories from McCrumb (''If Ever I Return, Pretty Peggy-O''). She once again endows characters with the Sight, covering ground she has been over several times before. And her jumping from the past to the present may come together in the end, but it weakens the more interesting tale set in the Old South, which is richer in detail and action.

However, this is extremely well read by the husband-and-wife team of Dick Hill and Susie Breck. The two conjure up a host of characters, giving each person his or her own accent, tone, and cadence. This is not a radio drama; there are no sound effects or music, other than a few lively measures bookending each cassette. However, these two bring a dramatic sense of urgency to the novel. I'd recommend it only on audio, because it flies by and easily holds one's interest. If you want a good read, look to McCrumb's other novels first.

Fast forward from the Civil War to the beginning of the last century for ''Horatio's Drive,'' the latest effort by filmmaker Ken Burns (and co-writer Dayton Duncan) and the companion volume to the PBS documentary about the first automobile trip across the United States.

Though a surprising paucity of detail leaves one asking questions once too often, this remains an excellent production that is well read by an array of voices and enlivened with varying musical passages that often wittily underscore the action.

Burns may not have the most pleasing of voices, but he is a professional speaker with a great sense of timing and is comfortable with the medium. Though not quite as peppy as Burns, Duncan, with his deeper voice and deliberate pacing, remains an enthusiastic and entertaining narrator.

Other readers bring to life the voices of those involved in this arduous cross-country road trip. Tom Hanks reads with great sympathy the letters Horatio Nelson Jackson wrote home to his much-missed wife as days turned into weeks and then into months.

''Washington Schlepped Here'' is part of a new series of travelogues by well-known authors with a connection to the area they describe. It is reasonably priced and read with confidence and professionalism by author Christopher Buckley. Buckley takes us on a tour of Washington, D.C., that is supposed to be funny, and occasionally is. It is also occasionally touching and rather cranky. It is not a lot of anything, though, for Buckley is not an architect, historian, travel guide, or artist. He does cite plenty of other sources, but he never offers much more than cursory and sometimes inane observations.

The book is not terrible, but neither is it terribly insightful, and it raises the question ''Why bother?'' It's the kind of superficial fare one would give foreigners as a companion piece to their guidebook.

It is oddly humorous that ''Wigfield,'' the best of this bunch of audiobooks, is set in a fictionalized America that exists only in the (obviously demented) minds of its authors. The entire audio is purposely overwrought. The prose is purple and badly written in a funny way - much like the entries in the annual Bulwer-Lytton Fiction Contest staged by San Jose University.

The tale is told from the point of view of a thoroughly self-serving ''journalist'' (read ''reprobate''), desperate for a story to complete a book deal when he hits on the idea of investigating the town of Wigfield. (''Like mold on cheese, a complete idea began to grow.'') And so he delves deep into the psyches, and pockets, of the residents of a small town about to be flooded when the local dam is torn down. But is this a real town? Or is it a scam perpetuated by a bunch of ne'er-do-wells hoping to scam federal relocation funds?

You would think that the joke would wear thin, but it never does. ''Wigfield'' is naughty, outrageous, inventive, and rather addictive (I plan to hear it again). All three authors are veterans of Second City, and each portrays several different people. Their characterizations are edgy, bizarre, over the top, and as humorous as the material. It is one of the best audiobooks released this year.
