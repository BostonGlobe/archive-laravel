A READING LIFE

Trollope mere: audacious, sharp-eyed observer

By Katherine A. Powers, 10/5/2003

''I do not know that she was at all points a lady, but had Fate so willed it she would have been a thorough gentleman.'' Thus, in ''Can You Forgive Her?,'' did Anthony Trollope sum up Glencora, a character whom he seems to have patterned in some degree after his mother. Indeed, the existence of so many audacious, irrepressible, and, often, inconvenient women in his fiction is also part of her legacy. To be sure, some people think that Trollope was churlish toward this remarkable woman in his autobiography, that he showed resentment and was unduly harsh about her literary merit. But it strikes me that such people are missing whatever is necessary for understanding Anthony Trollope at all, and might be better off reading writers who are less interested in human nature's heterogeneous substance. I believe he reveled in her as in a rare bird of mixed plumage. She was something like a force of nature, not only in her strengths - her indomitability, industry, geniality, and capacity for joy - but in her weaknesses too - her snobbish prejudice, her imprudent enthusiasms, and her inspired exaggerations. Given the ingredients of early 19th-century England, you couldn't make her up if you tried.

It's always high time to bring back Fanny Trollope, and certainly she has had her minor revivals. It may be that one is building right now. Tomorrow is the 140th anniversary of her death, and not only has Edmund White just placed her in a novel, but her work itself belongs to the canon of ''anti-Americanism'' - a prejudice rife these days, for one reason or another. As writer rather than mother, Fanny Trollope is best known for ''Domestic Manners of the Americans'' (Penguin, paperback, $14), which appeared in 1832 and was the first of her 41 published works, all of them written after she was 50 years old. ''Her career,'' her son observed, most truly, ''offers great encouragement to those who have not begun early in life, but are still ambitious to do something before they depart hence.'' It also offers hope to women married to impossible men, her husband being a prime specimen. Prey to headaches, temper, and impractical schemes, he spent his final years alone in his room working on an ''Ecclesiastical Encyclopedia,'' an enterprise never finished, and now, as his son wrote, ''buried in the midst of that huge pile of futile literature, the building up of which has broken so many hearts.''

''Domestic Manners'' is a book I return to time and again, for it is one of the funniest travel books ever written, and on the subject of the general uncouthness of the American male during the age of Jackson - of which his ''incessant, remorseless spitting'' played no small part - it is incomparable. Writing of traveling on a Mississippi steamboat from New Orleans to Memphis, she states that ''it is with all sincerity I declare, that I would infinitely prefer sharing the apartment of a party of well conditioned pigs to the being confined to its cabin.'' Of its carpet, so direct a beneficiary of manly expectoration (this is how one begins to talk after reading her work), she can say only that ''I will not, I may not describe its condition; indeed it requires the pen of a Swift to do it justice.''

Crackpot ideas and failed schemes figure large in ''Domestic Manners'' - the most disastrous for the author being the Cincinnati Bazaar, a theater, promenade, and shopping emporium that she caused to be built and that helped plunge the family into two bankruptcies. In fact, the trip to America was, in part, prompted by utopian hopes, even if they were not really hers. Fanny, her two daughters, one of her sons, and a Frenchman called Auguste Hervieu were recruited by the wealthy abolitionist and women's rights advocate Fanny Wright to join her colony at Nashoba, in Tennessee. Here freed slaves were, Wright claimed, working hard and happily to receive the education that would set them up for life and convince the nation that more wealth could be extracted from a free man than a bound one. Fanny Trollope thought she would stay in this rosily painted community for a few months before going on to Cincinnati and the making of her family's fortune. The squalid reality of Nashoba floored her: ''Desolation was the only feeling - the only word that presented itself.''

The situation calls for a novel, and this is what Edmund White has supplied in ''Fanny: A Fiction'' (Ecco, $24.95). The book purports to be the unfinished biography of Fanny Wright written by a decidedly disenchanted Fanny Trollope in her old age. ''Fanny Wright,'' she begins, ''had undeniable virtues [develop this thought by the bye]. But she had, just as undeniably, some faults which I, as her friend and confidante, was particularly privileged to observe.'' White's Fanny Trollope is just as much the woman to spell out a fault as the one she is patterned after. In other words, this is a delightfully catty rendering of Frances Wright, who, like so many other well-heeled social visionaries, was pretty hard to take.

The book goes over much the same territory as ''Domestic Manners'' but adds characters and deeds that history never knew. White's embellishments, some of a salacious nature, are amusing and deftly woven into the record. Moreover, his Fanny Trollope is wonderfully astute in her appreciation of character, not only of Wright's, but of all around her. In one especially well-wrought scene - one that never could have been summoned up in this way by a real early 19th-century writer - White has Fanny Trollope showing the eel-like manner in which Fanny Wright squirms out of replying what it feels like to purchase slaves (which she did to set them free to work at Nashoba). As a study in evasion and self-delusion it is brilliant.

Though her name became anathema to most people in the United States after the publication of ''Domestic Manners,'' Fanny Trollope did have her champions, one of the most fervent being Mark Twain. ''She knew her subject well,'' he wrote, ''and she set it forth fairly and squarely, without any weak ifs and ands and buts. She deserved gratitude - but it is an error to suppose she got it.''
