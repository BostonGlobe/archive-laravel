BOOK REVIEW

Plenty of local flavor in `Fall Classics'

By Jim Kaplan, Globe Correspondent, 10/15/2003

Does any sporting moniker rival "fall classic," the ever-so-appropriate handle for baseball's World Series? I covered every Series from 1973 through 1985, and I can't describe any as a dud. Indeed, leafing back through baseball history, I consider just one a disgrace -- the 1919 Black Sox scandal.

In "Fall Classics: The Best Writing About the World Series' First 100 Years," edited by Bill Littlefield and Richard A. Johnson, Thomas Boswell of The Washington Post spotlights the appeal of baseball's Oktoberfest: "From Mickey Owens' passed ball to Bill Buckner's boot; Mazeroski's homer to Don Denkinger's blown call; the Babe's called shot to Fisk's foul ball polka; the Big Six's three shutouts to the pebble that finally gave the Big Train a Series win, baseball has consistently suspended disbelief in October."

Both editors have Boston roots. Littlefield lives in Massachusetts, hosts the NPR radio show "Only a Game," and writes a sports books column for the Globe. Johnson is curator of the Sports Museum at the FleetCenter and coauthored "Red Sox Century." You expect plenty of writing about the Red Sox, and you get it, but rightfully so: the 1903 World Series, first of the modern era; the unforgettable 1912 tilt with the Giants; the 1967 Impossible Dream; the 1975 Fiskian drama; 1986's Game 6, when our world fell apart.

The 1903 lid-lifter, by former major leaguer Tim Murnane of the Globe, depicts a world far removed from ours, one in which thousands of fans stood behind a rope in the outfield and writers infrequently visited the clubhouse.

The New Yorker magazine's redoubtable Roger Angell reports with his usual wit and clarity on the 1986 horror, in which the Red Sox were 13 times within one pitch of their first Series title since 1918, only to find that brass ring just out of reach. Describing the national hatred of the Mets, he writes, ". . . there is nothing that unites America more swiftly or happily than bad news in Gotham or a losing New York team."

Hands down, the best entry is "The Sixth Game" (1975) by ESPN's Peter Gammons, of Sports Illustrated and formerly of of the Globe. Anyone who was sentient at the time remembers the highlights of that Red Sox-Reds game: Bernie Carbo's homer to tie the game, Dwight Evans's catch-and-throw to save it, Carlton Fisk's "woodcutter's swing" that ended the game and sent church bells pealing all over New England. "Bernie is the only man I know who turned fall into summer with one wave of his magic wand," the eccentric pitcher Bill (Spaceman) Lee tells Gammons.

Editors Littlefield and Johnson ingeniously ferret out some diamonds in the rough: Minneapolis columnist Joe Soucheray's trip to the borough of dreams after the 1981 finale a few miles away at Yankee Stadium ("Brooklyn No Longer Place to Be When Dodgers Win"); Murray Kempton's 1956 story on Sal Maglie, losing pitcher in Don Larsen's perfect game; fiction and poetry as well as prose about the Black Sox; and, most gratifyingly, Frazier (Slow) Robinson's account of the 1942 Negro World Series (How do you pitch Josh Gibson? High and tight if he leans over the plate, low and away if he stands upright).

And yet, "Fall Classics" rates as only a semi-classic. Joe Trimble and Arthur Daley were hacks; why include their writing? I can't believe Bill Mazeroski's walk-off shot to end the Game 7 to beat all Game 7s (1960) merits nothing better than an AP column on Maz missing his late father. The immortal Red Smith is used twice, in 1941 and 1973. If the editors had consulted The New York Times's Ira Berkow, author of a biography and play about the great man, he'd have directed them to a superior 1977 Smith column on Reggie Jackson's three-homer game. And why only one entry from Sports Illustrated?

"Fall Classics" hits plenty of literary homers. Too bad it didn't strike more base hits and sacrifice flies.

Jim Kaplan is a former Sports Illustrated baseball writer and author of "Lefty Grove: American Original" (2000, SABR).
