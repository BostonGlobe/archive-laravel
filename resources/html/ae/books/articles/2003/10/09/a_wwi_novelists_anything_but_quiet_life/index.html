BOOK REVIEW

A WWI novelist's anything-but-quiet life

By Mary H. Meier, Globe Correspondent, 10/9/2003

Erich Maria Remarque: The Last Romantic, By Hilton Tims Carroll & Graf, 288 pp., $26

ADVERTISEMENT

Since its publication in 1929, Erich Maria Remarque's World War I novel "All Quiet on the Western Front" has never been out of print. The title paraphrases periodic German military bulletins -- "Im Westen nichts neues" -- and describes the butchery and nihilism of a shellshocked generation. It remains the most famous antiwar novel of all time.

In his highly readable biography, "Erich Maria Remarque: The Last Romantic," Hilton Tims traces the writer's wartime journey. Drafted at 18, Remarque, along with his co-conscripts, was dispatched to Houthulst Forest in Flanders to confront Allied infantry divisions in a stalemate of trench carnage. Bombardment began, and Remarque sustained five wounds to his neck, left leg, and right arm as he tried to carry another wounded comrade to safety. He was sent to the St. Vincenz Hospital in Duisberg.

Remarque began writing in the hospital, which, in his famous book, became the scene for young soldiers' memories and their enduring sadness and futility.

Home again in Osnabrueck, he finished a first novel called "Die Traumbude" ("The Dream Room") and helped pay for its publication.

After an unrewarding stint as a village schoolteacher, he sold grave memorials, then won a job writing advertising copy in Hanover for the Continental tire company. After test-driving racing cars, in 1925 he moved to Berlin as editor of the illustrated athletic magazine "Sport im Bild."

In Berlin Remarque reshaped his image into a boulevardier and fancified his name from what he thought was the too-plain Erich Paul Remark. He occasionally posed as a former officer, complete with monocle, and even bought a minor aristocratic title. Moving now in flashy society, he married a beautiful model, Jutta Zambona.

Zambona was just the first of his glamorous loves, for he adored notoriety. Other women included Marlene Dietrich, with whom he had a lifelong friendship, and the Hollywood movie star Paulette Goddard, his last wife.

First serialized in the periodical Vossiche Zeitung, and then published with a large press run in 1929, "All Quiet on the Western Front" broke literary ground with a stark and uncomproising writing style.

Nazi university students publicly burned the novel, in the first infamous bonfire, at Berlin's Opernplatz on May 10, 1933. The previous year, Remarque, drinking in a Berlin bar, had been warned by a friend with links to the Nazis that he was close to arrest. That night the writer drove his beloved Lancia sports car across the border into Switzerland.

By the time war broke out, he was in New York and then in Hollywood, which he dubbed the "ante-room to hell." He agreed with a fellow exile that nowhere except Hollywood was the word "happy" heard so often in a place where no one was. But the screen rights to Remarque's novels, including "Arch of Triumph" made the author a rich man.

Remarque was safe in the United States, but his family in Hitler's Germany was not. His sister Elfriede was beheaded by the Nazis for "defeatist" remarks made to her landlady. She was unquestionably the victim of Nazi hatred of her brother.

Tims's biography portrays an elegant, often rootless man who never got over the fear he might have to flee and always kept a suitcase packed. Remarque did establish a home in Porto Ronco, Switzerland, which Goddard never liked but where she spent her widowhood after Remarque's death in 1970. Tims gives us a writer who was a gentle romantic with women, whose focus was chiefly on his novels and unsuccessful plays, and who was an alcoholic.

The biographer began his book by gaining access to World War I records at the Imperial War Museum in London and then to the Hollywood episodes recollected by the German author's Hollywood friend Luise Rainer.

Dietrich, whom Remarque always called "Puma," sent a wreath of white roses to his funeral and remarked, "He was a great writer, but about women -- always so stupid."
