BOOK REVIEW

History of Series makes pitch, throws it away

By Bill Nowlin, Globe Correspondent, 10/21/2003

The title of Joseph Wallace's "World Series: An Opinionated Chronicle -- 100 Years" seems to offer more than the book itself delivers. Any attempt to cover a full century of Series history inevitably glosses over detail, but this book's billing implies a thought-provoking or innovative approach that never develops.

Had Wallace supplied more text, and fewer photographs, the book might have been more engrossing. Some of the most dramatic Series stories were never elaborated on, and though pictures were in abundance, the selection was disappointing. Wallace, for instance, devotes five paragraphs to one moment of the 1980 World Series: Phillies third baseman Mike Schmidt complained tongue-in-cheek that reliever Tug McGraw was getting all the photo coverage, despite Schmidt's own newsworthy accomplishments. He declared to all that he would jump on McGraw after the final out so he could get in the picture, too. Later, McGraw said it happened "just like we had joked about. He dove at me, and the photographs captured it."

Yet the only image offered is a shot of Schmidt posing long afterward, wearing his World Series championship ring. Wallace set the stage nicely for another photograph, but the reader is let down, and perhaps left a bit confused, after turning the page in search of the promised photo.

Wallace's text is uneven as well. Though he ranks the 1912 and 1924 Series among the best five ever played, his account of the 1924 games is much stronger than the thin story of 1912 (a seven-game Red Sox triumph), which devotes only

three paragraphs to the action on the field. There was none of the tension, drama, and sudden twists and turns of this Series.

Wallace's coverage of the 1918 Series -- the last won by the Red Sox -- lacks even a passing reference to Boston's George Whiteman, though historians consider him the key figure in that win. Sox fans may also notice an omission in Wallace's description of the 1975 Series, second among his "Ten Best." He notes that the Red Sox had two exceptional rookies that year -- Fred Lynn and Jim Rice -- but neglects to mention the one fact that may have made all the difference in such a tight seven-game Series: Rice never played. His wrist had been broken by a Vern Ruhle fastball on Sept. 21, three weeks before Game One.

In 1960, the Pittsburgh Pirates unexpectedly unseated the seemingly invincible Yankees' dynastic hold on the Series with Bill Mazeroski's walk-off home run. Wallace doesn't let 1960 crack his top 10, though most aficionados recall this as a much more meaningful Series win than 1962, when the Yankees prevailed over the Giants. It's the 1962 Series that made Wallace's final selection.

There are some nice moments, such as his coverage of Ruth's legendary "called shot" in 1932 and his balanced treatment of Game 7 in 1946, between the Red Sox and St. Louis Cardinals, where he does not accept at face value the notion that Johnny Pesky held the ball as Enos Slaughter completed his "mad dash" and scored.

Nonetheless, some of the best writing in this book is by others. Wallace wisely chose to quote players often and turn to other writers at length, and to include Pete Hamill's 1989 essay "Faith, Hope, and Charity" and Stephen King's 1991 essay "Perfect Games, Shared Memories."

We can also thank Wallace for reminding us of the 1991 World Series between Atlanta and Minnesota, which, though it happened not that long ago, does rank in the list of any "Ten Best." It's a Series many of us might not have accorded such respect, but he ranks it first, and he presents a good case.

Anyone who loves the game of baseball will want to enjoy a volume such as this, but the book, though sometimes pleasant, is ultimately unsatisfying. A better choice is "100 Years of the World Series" by Eric Enders (Barnes & Noble, 2003, $19.95), which, for half the price, offers 50 percent more pages and -- taking advantage of a larger format -- about double the text and photographs, with full color throughout. It also includes numerous interesting features, such as a list of the last play of each World Series and side stories on the World Series for the women's league and even on Series ejections!

An appendix provides box scores of each and every Series game ever played. The photographic selection is also superior, even inspired, and Enders's research yields many original findings. But, perhaps most important, his prose better captures the spirit and passion of championship baseball.
