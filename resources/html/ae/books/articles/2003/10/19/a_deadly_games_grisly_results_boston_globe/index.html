A deadly game's grisly results

By James Alan Fox, 10/19/2003

Judgment Ridge: The True Story Behind the Dartmouth Murders

ADVERTISEMENT

By Dick Lehr and Mitchell Zuckoff

HarperCollins, 418 pp., $25.95

Over the past 20 years, the public's fascination with grisly killings has spawned a cottage industry in quick and dirty treatments of high-profile cases. Dick Lehr and Mitchell Zuckoff's ''Judgment Ridge," a carefully researched examination of the murder of two Dartmouth College professors by a pair of Vermont teenagers, is not your average true-crime book, however. Rather, it should join Truman Capote's classic, ''In Cold Blood," as one of the standards in crime writing.

As in their other books, Lehr and Zuckoff pay great attention to detail and accuracy, a refreshing change from most truecrime stories. Benefiting from their assignments covering the Zantop murder case for the Globe, these former staff reporters offer a richly textured, bird's-eye view of events surrounding one of the worst crimes in New Hampshire history.

The book's portrayal of the victims, Half and Susanne Zantop, is a wonderful tribute to their devotion to each other and the high esteem in which they were held by others. Through recollections of their daughters, students, and colleagues, it is easy to sense the tremendous loss suffered by their respective professions and the entire Dartmouth community. The Zantops were a special couple who died just because they happened to reside in a home targeted at random and were particularly solicitous in responding to an academic ruse concocted by two young strangers.

To help make sense of this seemingly senseless crime, we find in ''Judgment Ridge" an array of factors that encouraged a pair of ordinary teenagers from an ordinary town to plan and execute a crime of extraordinary brutality. We witness the arrogance of 17-year-old Robert Tulloch, the leader, and the humiliation he endured at school that facilitated his act of aggression. We observe the developing symbiotic relationship with his devoted follower, 16-year-old James Parker, which fulfilled needs for both youths.

We also learn about the role of situational factors -- how boredom in small-town life and lack of scholastic challenge fueled the teens' overwhelming urge to escape their daily routines. For their goal of world exploration, they only needed a get-rich-quick scheme. After failed attempts at nonviolent criminal enterprises, the boys decided that robbery (and murder, to silence the victims) was the only way. Despite rumors and speculation in the crime's aftermath about possible connections between the victims and their murderers, there was none, except that the Zantops appeared to have something that Tulloch and Parker wanted -- cash.

It is instead the close bond between the two assailants that holds the key to understanding this tragic episode. Tulloch exulted in his leadership position, feeding off the admiration of his younger partner, while Parker sought the praise and approval of his older mentor. Had Tulloch and Parker been apart, arguably this crime would never have occurred. In tandem, they brought out the worst in each other.

According to his confession, Parker had second thoughts even up to the moment of attack. Tulloch, while appearing to enjoy the role of ruthless killer, may also have been fulfilling the role of fearless leader that he perceived his friend expected of him. In what criminologists have called ''shared misunderstanding," teenagers often participate in horrible acts not so much because they are committed to them, but because they believe their friends are.

The story surrounding the Zantop murders is nothing less than riveting, and it is well told by the book's authors. The events preceding the murders, investigative steps in tracing crime-scene evidence, efforts by the two youths to escape justice, and legal maneuvers that encouraged both to plead guilty are unveiled in chilling detail.

It may simply be my own ''just the facts" preference, but the level of detail in the book -- descriptions of people, places, and events with only tangential connection to the main characters and the crime -- at times seems excessive. Moreover, the authors' ambition to be thorough occasionally borders on invasion of privacy. The book reveals certain sensitive matters and family skeletons about truly innocent people that have no bearing on the crime and perhaps should have been edited out.

Besides these lesser concerns, my only significant disappointment with ''Judgment Ridge" is the authors' over-simplistic explanation of Tulloch's criminality. Without psychiatric data, they conclude that he is a psychopath, borrowing heavily from the seminal work of psychologist Robert Hare on the psychopathic personality type, also known as sociopathy and the antisocial personality disorder.

While it might be tempting to label the young mastermind a psychopath in view of his apparent callousness and remorselessness, this is a difficult diagnosis to make for someone his age. The kind of insensitive veneer he displays is not unusual among adolescents who sometimes fail to appreciate the repercussions of their actions. Although they know right from wrong intellectually, youthful offenders often have trouble comprehending the harm they cause or empathizing with their victims. This is not so much an issue of character disorder as it is of emotional and cognitive immaturity.

Recent neurological research suggests that adolescents have limited capacity for thinking through consequences. As my colleague Jack Levin once remarked, some teenagers are ''temporary sociopaths." Given the similarity between the immature, self-centered reasoning common among adolescents and the thought processes of genuine sociopaths, even the standard classification manual of the psychiatric profession cautions against this diagnosis for youngsters under age 18.

Overall, ''Judgment Ridge" is a superior piece of nonfiction. It presents a textbook case of two apparently good kids who turn bad. The volume is so informative and insightful that I will indeed treat it as a textbook case -- literally, when I assign the book for the class in criminal violence that I will teach next semester.

James Alan Fox is the Lipman Family Professor of Criminal Justice at Northeastern University in Boston.
