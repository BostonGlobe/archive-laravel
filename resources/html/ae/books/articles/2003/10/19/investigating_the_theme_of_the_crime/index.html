BETWEEN THE LINES

Investigating the theme of the crime

By Robin Dougherty, 10/19/2003

Crime writer Michael Connelly can't exactly define a good mystery, but he knows one when he sees it.

ADVERTISEMENT

The creator of the popular Harry Bosch detective series took a break from his own work to edit the 2003 edition of ''The Best American Mystery Stories," (Mariner, $27.50), which features works by Elmore Leonard, Walter Mosley, Joyce Carol Oates, and 16 others. According to Connelly, he read the first batch of 25 submissions sent by series editor Otto Penzler in one weekend.

The result is a crazy-quilt of short stories, peopled by everyone from pickpockets to forensic scientists to Sherlock Holmes impersonators. Connelly's brand of hard-boiled detective stories sit side by side with tales of backstage sleuthing, last confessions, and all manner of desperate situations.

Readers waiting for the next Harry Bosch adventure need to hold on until May 2004 for ''The Narrows." In the meantime, Connelly, a onetime crime reporter for the Los Angeles Times and winner of the prestigious Edgar Award, the Maltese Falcon Award, and France's Grand Prix, holds forth about the tradition of crime writing, the evolution of the genre, and the selection process for this collection.

His own short stories occasionally appear on his web site, www.michaelconnelly.com.

Has mystery writing suddenly become respectable?

I've never felt like a second-class citizen. . . . In the last couple of years, our world has become more fearful, and people are grasping more toward mysteries. There's an aspect of reassurance in them that we've lost in real life. On another level, mysteries are very immediate. The tradition in crime fiction is to get a new book out every year. That has facilitated a genre that is immediately reflective of what's going on . . . There are already dozens of books that reflect the changes since Sept. 11.

Dennis Lehane said that he set his most recent book during the McCarthy era so that he could comment on our time.That he thought about that -- that powerful literary writers are choosing to stay in the genre and who are thinking of social commentary before they even think of the plot -- that makes me feel strongly about what we are doing and the importance of the genre.

Define ''mystery story." In this anthology, it's not necessarily a story with clues and red herrings and a solution, right?I wasn't looking for ''Oh, that's a wonderful plot." I was looking for windows into people's worlds . . . [''The Jukebox King," an entry by Doug Allyn] talked about how hot it got in the Ford plant. It opened the window to what it was like to work in a place like that. A lot of them are internal like ''The Skull" [by Joyce Carol Oates], about the guy who is reconstructing a face. What that does inside his mind. A lot of these stories would expand what has been traditionally held as mystery stories.

What's the difference between a mystery story and crime fiction?

Most writers I know just keep our heads down and write a story. We don't worry about being classified -- including myself . . . I would say everything from cozies to hard-boiled to police procedurals, the bottom line is that there is a crime running through it . . . The critics are always on the lookout for the next great American novel. I wonder if there will ever be a great American novel without a crime in it.

Have books like ''The Lovely Bones" raised the bar for what crime stories can do?We're writing about the real world. There are all these stories about larger-than-life characters becoming serial killers like you never see in the real world, but that's window dressing inside stories about people overcoming the odds. You don't make a lot of money writing mysteries. It's about the craft. I'm the lucky guy. I'm the one on the bestseller list and then I sit down and read these guys, and I see how well they are crafted, and I see how long it took, and I just revere them.

The range of publications these stories first appeared in is quite wide -- from Playboy and Hitchcock's Mystery Magazine to tiny, obscure literary reviews.That's to Otto Penzler's credit. He goes beyond the traditional places to find them.

Many of the stories in the collection reflect or celebrate the origins of the hard-boiled crime novel. They are set in LA or a place that feels like LA.That's a reflection of me . . . I'm a writer who was steeped in the tradition of these books . . . One of the great mystery writers, Ross McDonald, wrote in the '50s and '60s . . . He said in some commentary about his character Archer, ''If he ever turns sideways, he would disappear." He was not interested in character . . . He heavily influenced me, but my books are so much about the character. I'm not alone. This is the tradition now. You move forward . . . In the 1970s, with [Robert] Parker, it shifted to the detective solving something about himself or herself at the same time as putting the pieces together.

Is it hard to sustain a series character for that reason?With a series character, what is amazing and good is what is also difficult about it at the same time. You peel off layers of the onion and you have to keep digging deeper and deeper with each book. That's where the evolution of the crime novel has gone. He can't just be solving another case.

Is that why you had Harry Bosch retire from the police force and become a private eye?

Yes, you end up thinking all the time, ''How do I keep him alive?" You spent 11 months writing a book, and if Harry Bosch is not interesting to me anymore, then it's going to come out in the writing. I switched to first-person [narrative], I switched his job, and in the end of his most recent book, a life-altering change happens to him. Even with all that, there's an end in sight. I look at Parker and wonder how he's done it at the level he has for so long. I don't know if that's me.

Robin Dougherty, a writer and critic, lives in Washington, D.C. She can be reached atinkrd@aol.com.
