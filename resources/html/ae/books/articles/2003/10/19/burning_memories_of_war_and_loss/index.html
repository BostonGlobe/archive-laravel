Burning memories of war and loss

Sifting through ruins of love and chaos in postnuclear Hiroshima

By Gail Caldwell, 10/19/2003

The Great Fire

By Shirley Hazzard

ADVERTISEMENT

Farrar, Straus and Giroux 278 pp., $24

More than two decades have passed since the publication of Shirley Hazzard's ''The Transit of Venus," a novel so frankly magisterial that it dared to open, with no comma and no explanation: ''By nightfall the headlines would be reporting devastation." Yes, and by novel's close the story would be, too, its devastation both global and personal. Envisioning the tragic consequences of two sisters' transits across time (the mid-20th century) and space (Australia, England, the Far East), Hazzard exhibited a narrative bearing that brought to mind the 19th-century greats. Her psychological observations could be as slyly meandering as Henry James's; her omniscient wisdom -- merciful and acute -- ventured toward the golden bossiness of George Eliot.

So not for her the ''little" novel, aiming low and easily meeting its mark. ''The Great Fire" is Hazzard's first fictional work since ''The Transit of Venus" (two nonfiction works have appeared in between), and it returns to many of the same themes and locales: the dislocation and disrepair of war; the chaos and reparations of one's heart in accord with geography and politics. Opening in Japan in 1947, the novel travels between the postnuclear desolation of the East to the wilds of Australia and the sorrows of London; in between, of course, are the tales of the individuals who are among the war's ''victors." But that is an equivocal term in any war, as Hazzard so carefully conveys throughout her fiction. In ''Transit," she summed up the immutable fact of Hiroshima with one nonsentence: ''A catastrophe of which no one would ever say, the Will of God."

This, then, is the great fire her characters are living within memory of, though Hazzard's title is meant also to suggest the conflagrations and sacrifices of other great fires and great wars: Ypres, Dresden, the 17th-century burning of London. In keeping with the gravitas of its subject, ''The Great Fire" is a hugely determined effort; it is also an oddly lacking one, claiming neither the reach nor the structure of its predecessor. The novel can be brilliant but desultory, wandering dazedly across the shadows of characters who surface and disappear, or introducing subplots of whimsical inconsequence. Hazzard is capable of displaying more casual intelligence in a sentence or paragraph than some novels might achieve in a chapter; she describes war, simply and unforgettably, as ''the red mess and shallow grave." But her magnificent voice, which in ''Transit of Venus" assumed a tenor of authority, has taken on an imperious quality here; stylistic quirks -- half-sentences and tangential hypotheses -- now sound weary as often as they do well conceived. The effect can be specifically dazzling but wholly episodic, even with the straightforward love story at the novel's center.

The wounded good soldier at the heart of the book is 32-year-old Aldred Leith, a major in the British Army who has spent the two years after the war traveling through and writing about China. His counterpart is an Australian veteran named Peter Exley, whom he befriended in Cairo in 1942; Exley is in Hong Kong, stationed there to study war crimes committed by the Japanese. Joined by decency and the intimacy of what they've witnessed and survived, Leith and Exley play point-counterpoint to one another, both trying to find shards of meaning in a postwar existence. A young aesthete who came of age in genteel, isolated Australia, Exley dreamed of Europe, ''remote as Paradise and more convincing." His efforts to transcend the limitations of his past (Australia, Hazzard's birthplace, appears continually as a metaphor of banishment and inconsequence) will define his war experience as well as the sacrifices he makes in its wake. Loneliness is a prerequisite to consciousness, in Hazzard's view; its terrible apex, though, is the loneliness brought on by war's devastation. Exley embodies this soul-to-soul combat, and his eventual martyrdom is as fully realized as any event of the novel.

Leith had his own battles to wage: an overbearing father, whose fame as a writer removed him even further from his son; a series of half-meaningful romances and a brief war marriage that have left Leith callously alone. Now he's come to Japan to observe the effects of Hiroshima, though this purported mission seems largely symbolic to the novel -- an unfortunate eclipse of the real horror that underscores Hazzard's tendency toward the oblique. Billeted at a brutish medical director's quarters on an island off mainland Japan, Leith finds himself in the cloistered company of the director's two younger children: Benedict, a 20-year-old young man who is dying of a degenerative nerve disease, and his sister, Helen, a beautiful, waif-like ''changeling" who is 17 but seems younger and older both. The Driscoll children are inseparable; they read to each other constantly -- Conrad, Gibbon -- and they have traveled the world together, fighting against what time Ben has left. They adore Leith almost instantly -- he is a decorated war hero, and kind -- and he soon enough finds himself in love with the girl, particularly after she ends a letter to him: ''We fear to weary you with our high feelings," she writes, ''but they don't change."

Now that is lovely and slightly ludicrous both, and therein lies the danger zone of ''The Great Fire." While the burgeoning affair between Helen and Leith is gratifying in a sort of tweedy, warm-hearth way, it also strains the contemporary imagination to accept it wholeheartedly, without irony or foreboding. But that's how Hazzard has chosen to present her lovers, their farewell to arms being a sort of fair trade for having lived through horror and loss. One is left with a rather gauzy sense of consequence, more contrived and thin than its larger subject requires.

Which is, persistently, the grievous result of war and loss. When Hazzard ventures into the bleak terrain of the past -- of Leith's heroic action under fire, of a Dutch skipper's memories of the costs of war -- then her narrative depth achieves a grandness reminiscent of ''The Transit of Venus." And her grasp of history, of the bill exacted by progress, suggests a shimmering intelligence rare in contemporary fiction. But too often ''The Great Fire" assumes the mantle and legacy of its battle-wracked cast: sorrowful but diaphanous, unable to find its way home.
