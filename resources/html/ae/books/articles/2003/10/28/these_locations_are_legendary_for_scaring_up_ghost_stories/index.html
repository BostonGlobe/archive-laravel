BOOK REVIEW

These locations are legendary for scaring up ghost stories

By Michael Kenney, Globe Correspondent, 10/28/2003

Over the years, upcountry editor Charles J. Jordan has collected tales traditionally told in northern New Hampshire, tales of apparitions and local characters. Such stories are the building blocks of regional folk memory and its history -- and of Halloween storytelling.

And as Judith Richardson suggests in her exploration of hauntings in the Hudson Valley, ghost stories can not only define a region but also shape its literature.

"The shapes and contents of this landscape" of towering cliffs and tangled swamplands, Richardson writes, "have proven fertile ground for hauntedness." Not only has it provided "landmarks to which stories become attached," but also "the landscape itself has resonated with fears and associations rooted deep in both human psychology and cultural history."

But Richardson also writes that "understanding how the Hudson Valley gained its particular reputation for hauntings requires paying special attention" to the tales of Washington Irving.

As Richardson, a lecturer in English at Stanford University, pursues the subject, it begs the question of just how much the region's association with "hauntedness" is purely the literary invention of Irving. While regional folklorists have argued for local sources -- in one such view, "the scenery of the Hudson obviously demands a phantom ship and a headless horseman" -- literary scholars have traced Irving's best-known tales, "Rip Van Winkle" and "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow," to "identifiable German folk tales."

And it was during a long stay in Britain and continental Europe, Richardson writes, "that Irving discovered how the Hudson Valley might be haunted, or rather that he was able to synthesize his Hudson Valley experiences and his literary and philosophical sensibilities." A major influence was Walter Scott, who had woven legends of the supernatural into his novels.

But whatever the influences on Irving, whether local legends or European folk tales, his influence on his region cannot be understated. "Irving had a tremendous influence in the Hudson Valley," Richardson writes, "not only because prereadings of Irving colored the view of generations of outside visitors to the region, but also because Irving's spirits . . . [left] traces in regional writing, landscape and experience."

Richardson teases out numerous "explicit traces of Irving and his tales . . . in regional writings" and notes that visitors were urged to read Irving while touring -- but were warned that "the host at `Rip's Cottage' was not a descendant of Rip Van Winkle."

The discussion of Irving's role in shaping the Hudson Valley's image provokes a question of why Nathaniel Hawthorne has not had a similar role in New England.

While New England has some apparition-provoking landscape -- back to Jordan's collection shortly -- its sense of the past seems to be more rooted in history than in legend.

The two writers draw from those respective sources. While Irving conjured up his phantom ships and headless horsemen, Hawthorne linked his tales to historical events, as in, for instance, "Howe's Masquerade," in which a ghostly procession of Puritan worthies chills the royal governor's Province House ball just as the cannonading of Boston could be heard as it began on Dorchester Heights.

Richardson's detailed discussion of Maxwell Anderson's 1937 play, "High Tor," suggests a more contemporary comparison of the uses of legend and history. While "High Tor" is set on that Catskill peak and peopled with the region's ghosts, Arthur Miller's "The Crucible" is a dramatization of the Salem witch crisis.

Still to be found today, writes Jordan, editor of Northern New Hampshire Magazine and two local papers, are "mysterious tales linked to curses, legends and peculiar circumstances," especially to be found in New Hampshire's northern mountains, "where people still warm up their long winters with an old-fashioned `ghost story.' "

While some of Jordan's tales come from the same kind of antiquarian sources mined by the 19th-century folklorists who followed Irving, others he relates "directly from sources as they happened."

One such tale involves a "mystery girl" sighted in the woods around Stewartstown Hollow in August 1972, always running away when spotted and perhaps the source of muffled cries heard at night.

The original newspaper account, Jordan writes, "really shook the bushes up north." With the coming of winter, sightings tailed off, but the story has rooted itself "into local lore that continues to this very day."

So in the end, whether it is Irving and his Hudson Valley antiquarian imitators, Hawthorne, or Jordan's upcountry tellers of tales, it is perhaps as Richardson puts it neatly: "Haunting is the work of tellers as well as ghosts."

Possessions: The History and uses of Haunting in the Hudson Valley, By Judith Richardson, Harvard University, 320 pp., illustrated, $29.99

Tales Told in the Shadows of teh White Mountains, By Charles J. Jordan, Universtiy Press of New England, 224 pp., illustrated, $19.95, (paperback)
