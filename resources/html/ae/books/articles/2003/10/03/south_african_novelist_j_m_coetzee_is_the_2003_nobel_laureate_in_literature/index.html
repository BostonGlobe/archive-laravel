South African novelist J. M. Coetzee is the 2003 Nobel laureate in literature

By David Mehegan, Globe Staff, 10/3/2003

South African novelist J. M. Coetzee was named winner of the 2003 Nobel Prize in Literature yesterday.

Coetzee, long on the literary world's shortlist for the prize, is the second South African laureate. Nadine Gordimer won in 1991.

The Swedish Academy's citation praised Coetzee's novels for their "well-crafted composition, pregnant dialogue, and analytical brilliance. At the same time, he is a scrupulous doubter, ruthless in his criticism of the cruel rationalism and cosmetic morality of Western civilization."

"It came as a complete surprise," Coetzee (pronounced "kut-SEE-uh"), 63, said in a statement issued by the University of Chicago, where he teaches each fall semester. "I was not even aware that the announcement was pending."

Coetzee's best-known novels are "Waiting for the Barbarians," "The Master of Petersburg," "In the Heart of the Country," "Life and Times of Michael K.," and "Disgrace." The last sold more than 200,000 copies in the United States. His new novel, "Elizabeth Costello," is due from Viking this month. Viking spokesman Paul Slovak yesterday said bookstores are clamoring for more copies and that the book will almost certainly go back to press after the 33,000-copy first printing.

Besides his novels, Coetzee has written two memoirs and several books of criticism and has been a reviewer for literary magazines including The New York Review of Books. He is famously shy and rarely grants interviews. Wide-ranging in interests, he has written a book in defense of animal rights ("The Lives of Animals") and is working on an English translation of the works of several Dutch poets.

Born in Cape Town, John Maxwell Coetzee graduated with honors in English and mathematics from the University of Cape Town, worked for five years as a computer programmer for IBM in England, then earned a PhD in English and linguistics at the University of Texas. He taught at several American universities, including the State University of New York at Buffalo, Johns Hopkins, and Harvard. He was on the faculty of the University of Cape Town from 1984 until last year, when he moved to Australia to teach at the University of Adelaide.

Like that of his countrywoman Gordimer, Coetzee's fiction has an avid international audience. Though some of his work indirectly reveals the evil of racism, it is not overtly political, most often dealing with the underlying coldness, savagery, and lunacy of the human heart.

One of the two novellas combined in Coetzee's first published fiction, "Dusklands," titled "Vietnam Project," concerns an official who becomes insane as he works on an American psychological-warfare program in the war in Vietnam. The main character of the second novella, "The Narrative of Jacobus Coetzee," is an 18th century South African Boer who massacres a native tribe because they did not show him appropriate respect.

"Waiting for the Barbarians" is set in an unnamed modern empire in which a torturer is sent to a rural area to repress the native tribes. "In the Heart of the Country," seen by some as an allegory for the moral and racial struggles of South Africa, is told by an unreliable, possibly mad narrator, a provincial South African woman plotting the murder of her father. In "Disgrace," a professor is fired from his university position for a sexual dalliance and finds a kind of redemption working with animals on a remote farm.

The Nobel, which carries a prize of about $1.3 million, will be presented in Stockholm on Dec. 10.
