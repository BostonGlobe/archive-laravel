BOOK REVIEW

Oversize characters give weight to a busy first novel

By Renee Graham, Globe Staff, 10/6/2003

Heidi Jon Schmidt's ''The Bride of Catastrophe'' suffers from a niggling case of first-novel fever. It bounds along with a delirious busyness endemic to talented novelists eager to show off everything in their bag of literary tricks - and, at more than 400 pages, this is a very big bag of tricks.

Early on, when the book's narrator, Beatrice Wolfe, utters, ''If only my father could have managed our deaths better than he had our lives,'' one quickly realizes this is going to be a ride rife with bruising comedy and pathos. Still, if this acerbic story of a young woman's efforts to rise from the ruins of her dysfunctional family is occasionally too breathless for its own good, it certainly isn't boring, and neither are her oversize characters. As the story begins, it is 1974, and Beatrice is a student at Sweetwater College. She's smitten with Philippa Sayres, her comparative literature professor and, to the students' endless fascination, a lesbian.

''This gave her a mystique her more prosaic qualities couldn't dull,'' Beatrice says. ''It made her seem dangerous and exotic even as she amassed notecards in the back corner of the library.''

Philippa, in turn, takes notice of Beatrice, whom she regards with a kind of bemused interest and wonder. Beatrice notices Philippa staring at her hands, and this pleases Beatrice.

''I was proud of my hands - they were slender and pale and seemed to speak of virtue,'' she says. ''Someone had once told me they were perfect piano-playing hands, so they stood in my mind for all my undeveloped aptitudes, the possibilities latent in me.'' It's a lovely passage, and Schmidt, who has written two well-received short story collections, has a keen eye for tender details.

Soon, Beatrice and Philippa become lovers, their chatty consummation beginning with Philippa's taunting, ''Well, what about it? Wanna fool around?'' Like so much else in her life, the affair is a means for Beatrice to seek out her own identity and distance herself from her increasingly unstable family.

At the eye of the Wolfe family storm is her parents' rotten marriage. After 20 years, it isn't just that the thrill is gone; these two people never should have married in the first place. The failings of the parents stalk their children, who spend much of their time wounding and mending themselves.

If Beatrice's dull father suffers his disappointments in a kind of pained silence, her mother, Claire, is a bloody mess of regret and recrimination.

Claire is a woman of corrosive emotion who shares her miseries ''real and imagined'' with the world, including her four put-upon children. Careening from one melodrama to the next, Claire is the type of person who rarely drives, fearing the car may burst into flames. Even a minor traffic accident when Claire was pregnant with Beatrice convinces Claire that her husband wanted them all dead.

Yet there is an undeniable force to Claire, and somewhat like Hannibal Lecter in ''The Silence of the Lambs,'' whenever the focus shifts away from her tantrums, the novel sags a bit. Even Beatrice's descriptions of her mother crackle with energy.

Looking at Claire, Beatrice notes she was ''wearing a halter top and a pair of cutoffs, though a decade of childbearing had exhausted her body. Her hair was raked back by her angry hand, her crow's-feet must have been deepening right then, so narrowly was she squinting; and there was such determination in her stance that she was absolutely beautiful.''

Still, Claire treats Beatrice, her oldest child, as if she owes her mother everything; Beatrice's father married her mother because she was pregnant with Beatrice.

''She had bought my life at the price of her own, for my sake she had married - well, calling her relationship with him a marriage was like calling Hitler's march on Poland a parade!'' Beatrice says. Hence, she spends as much of this novel trying to win her mother's approval, as if to prove herself worthy of Claire's perceived sacrifices, as trying to get away from her.

At times, ''The Bride of Catastrophe'' mimics the ambitious sprawl of such John Irving novels as ''The Hotel New Hampshire'' and ''A Widow for One Year'' - and can be just as infuriating. Mishaps, disasters, and tragedies befall the book's characters in an almost biblical torrent - one character even gets zapped by lightning through a phone line while talking to her estranged mother. But there is something touching about these people, and as self-involved, self-delusional, and self-destructive as they are, one can't help but root for them to find some lasting happiness.

The Bride of Catastrophe

By Heidi Jon Schmidt

Picador, 422 pp., $24
