Greed is not good

How the US economy went from boom to gloom during one dizzying decade

By Robert Skidelsky, 10/26/2003

''The Roaring Nineties: A New History of the World's Most Prosperous Decade''

ADVERTISEMENT

By Joseph Stiglitz

Norton, 379 pp., $25.95

''The Roaring Nineties'' is the story of the forces driving the American economy to frenzy in the 1990s and collapse in 2000. It is a much better book than Joseph Stiglitz's previous offering, ''Globalization and Its Discontents.'' That was largely a rant against the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank. Diatribe is not absent from this book. But it is much more solidly rooted in his own path-breaking work on the economics of risk and information, for which he received a Nobel prize in 2001. Stiglitz is not an elegant or even punchy writer. But when he can relate the politics of the 1990s to the economics he knows well, the discussion becomes exciting.

In Stiglitz's view, markets suffer from pervasive ''failures,'' mainly to do with ''imperfect and particularly asymmetric information,'' which, unless corrected by governments, can make them seriously unstable. His account of the roaring '90s focuses on the failure of the Clinton administration to put the right incentives in place to curb these misshaps.

Instead, misguided deregulation, misguided accounting practices, and misguided tax policies all produced perverse incentives to boost the prices of stocks ahead of their real value, thus fueling a bubble that was bound to collapse. The chief gains from this fraudulent bonanza accrued to corporate executives, whose compensation packages skyrocketed while those of their employees stagnated. The stock market ''correction'' of 2000-2002 - with the bankruptcy of giant companies like Enron and the exposure of huge accounting frauds - marked the belated onset of reality.

This is not the whole story. There was also an element of what Alan Greenspan in 1996 called ''irrational exuberance,'' which was largely independent of any particular set of incentives. In this respect, the dot-com bubble of the late 1990s resembles the South Sea Bubble of 1720 and the railway booms of the 19th century.

Since Stiglitz was a member and later chairman of President Clinton's Council of Economic Advisers throughout the roaring nineties', '90s, he cannot absolve himself, or the president he was proud to serve, from all blame for what went wrong. His defense is that the political and economic forces in favor of letting the market rip were too strong to contain. The old balances in the economy, particularly markets and government, were swept away by the ideology of the free market, the capture of commanding positions in politics by the Republicans, and, of course, by the explosive force of the new economy itself. There is something else. While an unsustainable boom is building up, there are any number of people who know it cannot last, but to say so publicly and or, even more, to do something which that might moderate it, seems a kind of treason against prosperity. So, as in the 1920s, almost everyone was complicit in the fantasy till the moment of its collapse.

Stiglitz's main attack is directed against the deregulation mania that swept through the United States in the '80s and '90s. Deregulation of telecommunications in 1996 led to an overinvestment bubble, which burst in 2001. Deregulation of electricity in California led to market manipulation, which hugely inflated energy prices to consumers (and Enron's profits) at the expense of Silicon Valley, heart of US innovation. Deregulation of banking - notably the repeal of the Glass-Steagall Act of 1933, which had separated investment from commercial banking - set up conflicts of interest between the two branches and led to corrupt synergies between banks and companies, highly profitable to both sets of executives, but often damaging to their depositors and shareholders. Lax regulation of the accounting sector led to the boom in share options, which greatly boosted managers' pay and artificially inflated company profits. (By 2000, American CEOs were being paid 500 times more than the average employee, up from 85 times in 1990.)

Finally, the frenzy was fed by the temporary cut in capital gains tax of 1997, which Stiglitz calls ''one of the most regressive tax cuts in America's history.'' All in all it is a sorry tale, in which the retreat of the state from social protection made it more difficult to shield people from the consequences of collapse, even as business deregulation made collapse more likely.

The indictment seems damning, but one must remember that this is the case for the prosecution. Stiglitz is a strongly partisan Democrat, and it is often hard to separate the scientist from the preacher. Thus he attributes the failure of energy deregulation in California to bring down consumer prices to market manipulation by Enron, but fails to note that this was a predictable consequence of deregulating wholesale, but not retail, prices. This was a government, not market, failure. In Britain, a better-managed deregulation caused prices to consumers to plummet. There is no reason to think up fancy explanations whenever the market works more or less as common sense would lead one to suppose it would.

The preacher is very much in evidence when Stiglitz contemplates the effects of US advice and policy to developing countries. His theme is that America (often via the IMF and World Bank) pushed free-market policies on the rest of the world - ''Cut that budget. Lower that trade barrier. Privatize that utility'' - that it would not have dreamt of following itself. The indictment is partly true. But there is another side. Stiglitz talks about what government needs to do, but there is no such thing as government, only particular governments, which differ enormously in their capacity. Things governments can and should do in some countries would be the road to ruin if they did them in others. The right balance between government and markets shifts between countries and also from period to period. Stiglitz's chapter on Russia in his previous book is vitiated by a failure to understand this point.

Like Keynes, to whom he has been likened, Stiglitz overemphasizes stability at the expense of dynamism. The ability of capitalism to ''roar'' is part of its virtue, even if the roaring does contain the ''seeds of disaster.'' And we should not be misled by such mixed metaphors to exaggerate the scale of the ''disaster'' that struck in the new millennium. Between 1929 and 1931, the US gross domestic product contracted by over 25 percent. Between 2001 and 2003 the US GDP grew by 1.8 percent. Even subliminal comparison with the Great Depression is way off target.

Nevertheless, corporate executives should take a break from analysts' reports to read Stiglitz's book. It explains how so many of them became fabulously rich; and it warns them that they have been rumbled.

Lord Skidelsky is professor of political economy at Warwick University, England.
