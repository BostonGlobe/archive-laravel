FOR CHILDREN

When the world was young

By Liz Rosenberg, 10/26/2003

''The Pond God and Other Stories''

ADVERTISEMENT

By Samuel Jay Keyser

Illustrated by Robert Shetterly

Front Street, 96 pp.,

ages 11 and up, $14.95

''God Went to Beauty School''

By Cynthia Rylant

HarperTempest, 56 pp.,

ages 10 and up, $14.99

''Master Stitchum and the Moon''

By Mickle Maher

Illustrated by Spiro Dousias

Bollix, 85 pp.,

ages 8 and up, $19.99

If anyone asked me to guess what interesting new trends I'd see in children's books this fall, books of poems about gods or God wouldn't make my Top 100 list. Yet here I am, looking at ''The Pond God and Other Stories'' and ''God Went to Beauty School'' - two fine, eccentric books for young readers.

''The Pond God,'' by Samuel Jay Keyser, is a series of loosely linked poems about the adventures and misadventures of various gods - ''The Frightened God,'' ''The God With Ears Big as Sails,'' and the like. The tone is that of parables or wisdom teachings, somewhere between Kahlil Gibran's ''The Prophet'' and ''Aesop's Fables.''

Like Aesop, Keyser generally punctuates his poem-stories with a moral - ''Given a choice between two natures, it is best to choose your own'' - but the lesson is seldom straightforward. Rather, it leaps out of the body of the poem like the Zen master's stick, startling the reader. A snail befriends a god, becoming his second pair of eyes: ''From that day on, the crooked god walked as if he were upright,/and before long the gods forgot that he was crooked/And that is how two can change what one cannot.''

Some are old tales told in a new way, like ''The Old God and the Very Young God,'' in which ''youth and age are burdensome/only to those who have neither.'' The style is plain but not prosey.

''The Prophet'' enthralls many young teenagers with its exalted language and teachings. ''The Pond God'' may have similar appeal for young readers old enough to want not only to be smart, but also wise.

Newbery medalist Cynthia Rylant goes bravely out on a limb in ''God Went to Beauty School.'' She will, alas, be denounced for her irreverence: ''He went there to learn how/to give a good perm/and ended up just crazy/about nails.'' Rylant posits a God who enters his own created world - gets a dog, buys a sofa from Pottery Barn. ''God Went to Beauty School'' is certainly an eccentric look at God, and it won't pass muster with every religious group: ''He was surprised./Because, really,/He'd just been winging it.'' Not by a long shot: ''He'd gotten such a bad rap/all these years/for being pissed off/ all the time.'' Yet the tone of the poems increases cumulatively toward prayerfulness. God writes a book: ''Just one./He read it to the boy/at bedtime/because the boy couldn't sleep./So God read him a book./The boy grew up. He became a writer./Which one? Not telling.''

This is characteristic of Rylant's sly humor, which can be laugh-out-loud funny, as when God writes fan letters to a country music singer: ''Finally He wrote/one last time.../He could answer her prayers./Well - one at least./And finally finally/she wrote back./And she said,/'Dear God, I pray/you will get a life.' '' What shines through these poems - which read more like good, tight prose chopped into short lines - is something recognizable as worship: ''God's blood was love./His bones were love./His eyes, his heart,/his kidneys were love.'' If nothing else, ''God Went to Beauty School'' is bound to provoke lively discussion and response in its young readership.

''Master Stitchum and the Moon'' is neither a book of poetry nor a book about God or gods, exactly - though it contains poems, song lyrics, and heaven and angels, and a suspiciously all-powerful, cigar-smoking character. Author Mickle Maher begins boldly: ''Of all the world's stories, there's only one story called Master Stitchum and the Moon. And by an amazing coincidence, that very story is the one right here in this book.'' Based loosely on two Grimm Brothers tales, the story starts when the world was ''so young that the mountains only came up to a person's chin and snowflakes came in just twenty-three shapes.'' Master Stitchum, hero of the book's title, is a tailor who ''never did any tailoring.'' He's too busy correcting faults: ''as the sun would set (with a few too many pinkish hues, he thought) he'd encounter a clump of dirt, sitting the wrong way on a hill.'' His brother Pitchum is ''the town slop shoveler,'' but there's no slop, and their little sister Helga - well, she spits.

The world is so young there isn't any moon, and people stumble around at night, bumping into things. It's up to Stitchum, Pitchum, and Helga to change the course of history, thanks to author Maher, who lets his imagination run free - at times maybe a little too free. But the man can certainly write. ''All the houses were painted the same shade of deep, dusky blue - a sad blue that can only be found on houses where no one has lived for many years.'' The book comes with a CD, proving lyrics can be more powerful than melodies, an advantage when one is reading the book. Spiro Dousias's illustrations are appropriately skewed, light-handed, and childlike. They add life to an already abundantly lively work.

Liz Rosenberg is the author of more than two dozen books for young readers. She is currently working on a poetry anthology for teenagers, ''Going Crazy,'' and teaches English at the State University of New York at Binghamton.
