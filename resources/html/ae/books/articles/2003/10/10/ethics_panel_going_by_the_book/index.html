Ethics panel going by the book

By Yvonne Abraham, Globe Staff, 10/10/2003

It's a plot twist that might be right at home in one of his whodunits.

ADVERTISEMENT

As general counsel for the Massachusetts Board of Bar Overseers, Michael Fredrickson helps police lawyers accused of ethics violations. On the side, he also writes mystery novels about lawyers. But yesterday Fredrickson was hit with an ethics violation himself, for writing novels on state time.

"Fredrickson spent substantial time during his regular BBO office hours writing and preparing his novels for publication," reads the agreement that settled the case. He asked his administrative assistants to make photocopies and telephone calls and send faxes and mail related to the books, the settlement says.

While such developments might lead to life-threatening repercussions in a legal thriller, the fate of the respected lawyer who wrote "A Cinderella Affidavit" and "Witness for the Dead" has been somewhat less dire. The State Ethics Commission levied a $5,000 fine on the 58-year-old Fredrickson and ordered him to pay the Board of Bar Overseers $5,000 for resources used.

He remains firmly in the job he has held since 1989.

"We stand behind him and he's done an excellent job for us, and he has resolved the matter," said Ellen Carpenter, chairwoman of the board. "Mike's job performance has never suffered. He does an excellent job and he puts in many hours of work on board matters outside the office, so there is never a question that he is not performing his job."

"I read the first novel," Carpenter added. "It was excellent. I didn't figure out `who done it.' "

Michael Mone, Fredrickson's lawyer, said his client had reimbursed his employers for all office resources he used and had made up for the office hours he spent on the novel with plenty of unpaid extra work. But, according to the ethics commission report, "Fredrickson kept no records to document the asserted offset." He agreed to the fine only to spare the board embarrassment, Mone said. "He is one of the most honorable, straightforward people I know."

Fredrickson himself would not comment on the matter.

But if his employers were not fazed by Fredrickson's killing off his characters during office hours, why the ethics complaint? In the parlance of the genre, somebody snitched.

"The complaint arose in the context of an employee who had been terminated by the board and involved in litigation," Mone said.

In a 2001 interview published in the magazine American Lawyer, Fredrickson explained that when he had turned 50, he decided he simply had to start a novel.

"It was now or never," the magazine article read. "He began stealthily, writing in secret, hiding it even from his wife at first. `After a month or two, it became like a mistress I stole away to,' he says. After 18 months he had a draft."

But the article says his second novel took longer partly because of the demands of his job: "The second book took . . . about two years. But given that he does it while keeping his full-time job at the disciplinary board and trying to spend time with his wife and 9-year-old son, that's not bad."

While Fredrickson's novels found their way into his day job, his day job also found its way into his novels, and with happier results, according to his reviews. In "A Cinderella Affidavit," for example, a lawyer at a prestigious Boston firm messes up a criminal case, provoking a disciplinary complaint.

According to the book jacket, a drug bust gone wrong leads to "a search for the informant [that] will plunge lawyers on both sides of the case into the legal battle of their lives. High-placed politicians, Chinese mobsters, and Boston's power elite will be dragged into court, their fates riding on the identity of this mystery informant, an informant known only as Cinderella."

Publisher's Weekly proclaimed about that book: "Fredrickson's insight into the legal process adds authenticity to a fast-paced, intriguing, multi-faceted tale." His second book was based on the story of the FBI and its mob informant, James "Whitey" Bulger. Publisher's Weekly called that book "a savvy legal thriller exposing Boston's corrupt underbelly."

Fredrickson's books have done well in Boston, said Kathy Phillips, owner of Spenser's Mystery Bookshop, where his second book was her bestseller of 2001. "Witness for the Dead" was advertised as a Boston Globe bestseller.

"It's close" to John Grisham, Phillips said. "But Grisham is more of an adventure story set in the world of law, whereas Fredrickson really gives us the law in his books quite well. They're decent books, very readable."

Phillips, who is also a lawyer, was not surprised at the ethics committee ruling, she said.

"He works for the state," Phillips said.

"It's a dangerous thing to do. If he worked for a private firm, it would have been a piece of cake. Most people don't work a full eight-hour day. How long did you spend talking about the baseball today?"
