BOOK REVIEW

1960s radicals try to untangle the personal and the political

By Erica Noonan, Globe Staff, 10/16/2003

The Company You Keep, By Neil Gordon Viking, 406 pp., $25.95

ADVERTISEMENT

All parents are bad parents, Jim Grant tells his 17-year-old daughter, Isabel, in the first of nearly 50 lengthy e-mail messages that make up "The Company You Keep," Neil Gordon's third novel.

"All parents are bad parents, and the sooner you understand this, the easier it's going to be to decide what to do," he muses in his opening missive to the daughter he hasn't seen, or spoken to, in a decade.

Grant knows all about bad parenting. He is a longtime fugitive from justice who invented a fake identity, deserted Isabel in a hotel room when she was 7, and went underground to escape prosecution. But now, he wants his estranged daughter to help him redeem another bad parent, Mimi Lurie, who served a decade in prison for her role in a 1974 bank robbery in which a guard was killed.

"As bad as we were -- and we were very, very bad -- we were also as good as we ever could be, given the circumstances of our lives, which were dramatic, and were not circumstances of our making," Grant writes.

Isabel spends the next 400 pages wading through a series of far-ranging e-mails from members of "The Committee," a group of self-described balding ex-hippies, about their lives as members of the Weather Underground in the 1960s, and the radicalism that galvanized them then and that still haunts them in the year 2006 -- the year in which the book is set.

"The Company You Keep" is not a two-way dialogue, however. Isabel can't get a word in edge wise until the epilogue. The reflective, lengthy, one-sided accounts from Jim; his girlfriend, Molly Sackler; Mimi; and a newspaper reporter, Benjamin Schulberg, are what craft the complex, cross-generational rationalizations of the radical politics of the '60s.

Gordon skillfully interweaves the voices of his fictional narrators with many of the most important totems of the era: Vietnam, the shooting of Kent State students by Ohio National Guard members, and the bombing of a townhouse in Greenwich Village. While a bank robbery in Michigan that Grant and his friends are swept up into is fictional, it is clearly based on real-life events.

Their recounting of history gives way to an odd combination of regret and nostalgia. They want Isabel not only to understand them, it seems, but also to wash away their sins though her love and understanding. This is even more bad parenting from Jim and his friends, no longer unrepentant radicals, but diminished, middle-aged baby boomers whose fiery principles have been worn down by age and regret.

Gordon, author of two other thrillers based on history, "Sacrifice of Isaac" and "The Gun Runner's Daughter," has been criticized for what some have described as an apologetic view of '60s radicalism.

But his characters are so skillfully drawn that they remain likable and interesting, and their missives to Isabel are sincerely felt and compelling reads until the very last page.

There are loose ends. It is never exactly clear why Isabel's presence at a parole hearing is crucial, and Isabel herself turns out to be as apolitical and nonjudgmental as most of her generation.

But that matters little. What Gordon really wants to show us is how during one of the most tumultuous times in American history the political and personal meshed so thoroughly that they still cannot not be separated, even today.
