BOOK REVIEW

The death-defying lady and the tigers

By Bruce Allen, 9/28/2003

The Final Confession of Mabel Stark

ADVERTISEMENT

By Richard Hough

Atlantic Monthly, 430 pp., $24

The life and opinions of the greatest female tiger trainer in circus history are hilariously and touchingly re-created in this extravagantly entertaining tale, a veteran Toronto journalist's first novel.

As Robert Hough reveals in ''The Final Confession of Mabel Stark" 's concluding "Research Notes" and "Acknowledgements," he has used, reshaped, and expanded on materials located at Wisconsin's Circus World Museum, several circus-related magazines, and a probably ghostwritten 1938 "autobiography" entitled -- inevitably -- ''Hold That Tiger."

"Mabel Stark" was born Mary Haynie, circa 1890, into a soon-to-become-impoverished Kentucky tobacco-farming family. But her story proper begins in 1906, when teenage and now orphaned Mary is working as a trainee nurse in a Kentucky hospital. One of her patients, appendicitis victim Dmitri Aganosticus, is so taken with her soothing sponge baths that he importunately becomes the first of her five husbands.

Sexual incompatibility and terror send Mary to a lunatic asylum, where she's "vegetabilized and chilled" and later helped to escape by a sympathetic doctor. Then she hits the road, finding employment as a "cooch dancer" in a traveling carnival, then graduating to the Al G. Barnes Wild Animal Circus, later stardom (by which time she has changed her name to Mabel Stark) as the creator of an innovative and risky "twenty-two tiger act" with Ringling Bros., and -- after repeated maulings, serial marriages, and her own intractable cussedness have taken their toll -- living out her middle and old age with the indignities of scaled-down performances, "handling" cats at the seedy animal park JungleLand, and supporting herself as a pitchwoman selling kitchen utensils.

All this is narrated from the vantage point of 1968, when Mabel is about to turn 80 and feistier than ever. She tells her story in chronological order, mainly (as Huck Finn would say), but with exhaustive digressions and side trips. As Mabel puts it, "There'll be times when I take liberties with this thing called order," as she relates her history "the old person's way, gumball style, the bits and pieces all mixed up and swirled together."

One subject brings another to mind, and Mabel will frequently leap ahead from the glory days of the 1920s to later years when she has rejoined the Barnes Circus, or settled in at JungleLand, or lives alone in her trailer, subsisting on spartan meals and "Gilligan's Island" reruns.

The byproduct of this method is considerable repetition, but Mabel's forceful personality brings every page to swaggering confrontational life. Early on she admits to "my not being the world's greatest fan of people in love with their own authority." The strength that sustains her resides in the contrasting patience and empathy that mark her relationships with animals ("I know I'm not a tiger," she muses, "but for some reason I can think like one, in fact there are times when I think maybe I was one in a former life").

Mabel's gift for plain speech takes numerous beguiling forms, whether she's insulting her employer Al G.'s Junoesque bimbo wife Leonora, airily dismissing things that interest most women (fashion, to her, is "something intended not to make sense"), slandering her young rival Clyde Beatty's "pitiful excuse for an act," or raging against the limitations and compromises that old age imposes on her. If Thomas Berger's "Little Big Man" Jack Crabb had run into Mabel during her salad days, they'd have made a fine fractious pair, akin perhaps to W. C. Fields and Mae West.

Mabel's relationships with men in fact define her with brassy clarity. Too inexperienced to deal with first husband Dmitri's amorous demands, she has little trouble detaching from his successor, wealthy Texas oilman (and secret sexual deviant) James Williams III. Her next marriage -- to her Ringling colleague, "nervous little Hungarian" lion trainer (and mentor) Louis Roth -- seems predestined ("We were both a mass of bite and claw marks") but falls apart when he suspects her of preferring tigers to men (as, in truth, she does).

Mabel moves on, wedding Ringling accountant Albert Ewing, who embezzles company funds, gambles compulsively, and abandons her. Then she makes her most satisfying human connection, with cat handler Art Rooney, whose considerable charms she praises less than rhapsodically ("I'm technically a bigamist and a fugitive and a woman who's been locked up for nervous problems, so I'm hardly one to be picky").

But the real center of Mabel's life is Rajah, the cub given to her as a wedding present, who grows into a majestically beautiful 550-pound Bengal tiger possessed of "a regalness the other tigers lacked. . . . He moved slowly with his head up and his shoulders back, not unlike a deb learning to walk. Dignified, he was."

The novel peaks in lavish descriptions of Rajah's growth and training, his "wrestling" with Mabel in the ring (a highly sexually charged form of "play," as she quickly realizes), and his rivalry with her men, which reaches a critical point when Rajah understands that his mistress genuinely loves fifth husband Art.

There are many other pleasures in store for readers who enter this captivating novel's richly detailed world. Hough makes you feel the heady excitement of how an itinerant circus takes over its venues, and he creates a vivid sensory picture of the Ringling Circus's variety, energy, and truculent personality clashes.

Best of all, he has reawakened the spirit of Mabel Stark, a forgotten woman who's not likely to be forgotten again soon. The most appropriate tribute one can pay to her and rambunctious book that celebrates her exploits is to say that reading it is more fun than going to the circus.

Bruce Allen is a frequent reviewer of new and classic fiction who lives in Maine.
