Teetering between fame, failure

Writer recalls harrowing losses

By DeWitt Henry, 9/28/2003

The Los Angeles Diaries

By James Brown

ADVERTISEMENT

Morrow, 200 pp., $21.95

This remarkable memoir by novelist James Brown ("The Final Performance," "Lucky Town," etc.) rises above the commonplace that each family is unhappy in its own way to the true art of comprehended pain. Brown's cadenced, plain prose sings with a deadpan grace, at once hard-eyed and vulnerable, eschewing either false emotion or frigidity. His Los Angeles of the mind is as spiritually polluted as Nathanael West's in "The Day of the Locust." However, Brown's story is one of redemption, against a background of alcoholism, drug addiction, and despair.

When Brown was 21, his older brother, Barry, a film actor, committed suicide; when Brown was 39, his surviving sibling, an older sister who had also aspired to an acting career but had lost her ambition under the burdens of marriage and parenting, leapt to her death. Brown's own crisis spans some seven years from his late 30s into his 40s. He is married with three children. He has published four novels. He teaches fiction-writing full time in different colleges. But he too is in despair, and in his addictions he too is approaching suicide. The tension throughout, as Brown alternates chapters in the adult present with chapters from his family past, is that of deciding to be or not to be. "Change or die," Brown tells himself at the end, and his "story comes to rest at a moment of beginning."

The demon isn't Hollywood itself, Brown insists, although his despair is intensified by trips from his teaching and domestic life in "the desert of San Bernadino" into Hollywood offices where he hopes for success as a screenwriter and an ''escape" from the classroom and where, of course, he encounters humiliation and rebuff. He can't sell out his art, even if he wanted to. His instinct for reality is "too dark" for the mass marketplace.

Nor is the demon Brown's alcoholic and battling parents. The close bond of brothers and sister is partly a defense against the parents, as is their collective addiction. "I've been using and drinking since I was nine years old," Brown writes. When Barry's career takes a dive, the 21-year-old Brown writes to him: "I'd like to blame Hollywood and all it stands for, but I know it's not that simple and blame isn't even what it is about," and Barry replies simply, "It's killing me if I don't drink," and soon after shoots himself.

There are touching moments as well as wrenching ones, and sustained comic episodes, where Brown himself becomes the butt.The two most extraordinary chapters, however, are "Facts" and the climactic "Midair." The former presents harrowing images of Brown's helpless guilt at age 31, as he systematically betrays the sacred core of his life. He teaches his literature and writing classes while stoned. He promises to be home for dinner, but continues to binge until he creeps home to his basement office at dawn. Throughout the chapter he has been stalked by a fugitive self. Now he hears a noise, and we get this confessional epiphany: "A tall maple grows at the curb, and I see something duck behind it. . . . I don't know if I am hallucinating. Or just paranoid. . . . Paranoia, I tell myself, is a man with the facts. . . . And the facts, for this man, are these: I am no kind of father. I am no kind of husband. No kind of teacher. I am instead that man I see and don't see and he is watching me, as I am watching him, both of us afraid to step out from the darkness."

When Brown tries to revisit his brother's suicide, he fails to penetrate the inner circumstance, even though he will later write a novel about it. The astonishing breakthrough comes when he does fully imagine his sister Marilyn's suicide. Second-person narration serves both as his loving address to her and as a way to evoke her inner life, to voice her as art: "You are forty-four years old the night you storm out of the house in the heat of another fight with your husband, the last fight you will ever have, and end your life-long battle with alcohol and drugs." She jumps from a bridge over the dry aqueduct of the Los Angeles River. In detailing the suffering of her life and the relentless progress toward despair, Brown recognizes that "what we share, you and I, is a sadness for our brother and . . . this craving to somehow make ourselves feel better." This is naturalism at its best. Marilyn marries an unsuitable older man. She is in and out of rehab. She has sex in exchange for dope. She is raped in a park after falling off the wagon. "The shame and remorse builds. The load grows heavier as we age and I understand now how every day you find yourself closer to that overpass above the LA River." She calls him, drunk, while he is trying to stay sober and get through his divorce. She takes his kids for a weekend. Then she jumps. "I have no right to judge what you are capable of enduring," writes Brown. "But in my dreams, I am standing beside you . . . 'Don't worry,' you say. 'There's nothing to be afraid of anymore.' . . . Then you squeeze my hand and let go and I watch you fall, again and again, as I will for the rest of my life. There is no more pain. No impact. In my dreams you are suspended in mid air, the wind rushing up around you, captured in the moment of flight."

Brown chooses life and takes the pain of it "if not happily, like a martyr, at least willingly, like an heir" (to quote Edward Lewis Wallant). The hallmark of his prose is gravitas. His truths are definitive.

DeWitt Henry is the author of ''The Marriage of Anna Maye Potts" and editor of ''Sorrow's Company: Writers on Loss and Grief." He teaches at Emerson College.
