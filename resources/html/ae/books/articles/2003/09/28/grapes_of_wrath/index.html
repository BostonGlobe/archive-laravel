Grapes of Wrath

They Marched Intom Sunlight powerfully conveys the bitter harvest of Vietnam, at home and on the front lines

By Bob Kerrey, 9/28/2003

They Marched Into Sunlight: War and Peace, Vietnam and America, October 1967

ADVERTISEMENT

By David Maraniss

Simon & Schuster, 572 pp., illustrated, $29.95

On two consecutive days in the fall of 1967, a pair of seemingly unrelated, impossibly far-apart events took place: an ambush of a US battalion, in Lai Khe, Vietnam, and an antiwar protest in Madison, Wis. David Maraniss has woven the lives of the American and Vietnamese participants together expertly and sympathetically in ''They Marched Into Sunlight," a book that broke my heart and caused me to begin asking others my age: ''Where were you on Oct. 17 and 18, 1967?"

Maraniss was entering his second month as a freshman at the University of Wisconsin. He was witness to a student protest against the university policy of allowing Dow Chemical, manufacturer of napalm, to interview and recruit potential employees on campus. Had he merely told this story, his book would have been worth writing and reading, and well matched with Bruce Weigl's war poem ''Elegy," the basis for the book's title.

The choices made by many students who protested the war, by students who supported it but chose deferments rather than selective service, by those who ran the university or were responsible for maintaining order on campus, were as hemmed in by circumstances as those made by the men ambushed that same month, the Black Lion battalion of the US Army's First Infantry Division. Sunlight warmed the face of these youths, promising to illuminate the path ahead. But the certainty of brightness blinded. They could not see the ambush.

Such was the tragedy of Vietnam as recounted by Maraniss. Everyone seemed to be marching into sunlight: President Johnson and his generals, advisers, and aides; the soldiers who were shipped blindly into combat; the students who led or followed in the antiwar movement; the university administrators who clung to their dreams of a perfect academic environment; and the North Vietnamese regiment who ambushed the Americans. Absolute certainty became the crippling weakness of everyone who thought they knew the lay of the land.

Maraniss is interested in unusual and unexpected human connections more than the geopolitical arguments, which still swirl around discussions of this war. And he finds enough to give readers a chance to feel unity with former opponents. My guess is that such unity will be elusive and rare, and that this book will provoke cries of protest characterized by the same kind of blind confidence that produced the Vietnam War in the first place.

This speculation is based on the unusual honesty and detail that Maraniss uses to piece together the drama of those who organized the demonstration against Dow. I knew of the ignorance, indecisiveness, cowardice, and confusion of President Johnson and his team. ''They Marched Into Sunlight" breaks new ground by revealing the same conditions among the protesters, which will allow supporters of the war to tell us how right they were.

In the midst of these debates and the general sadness one feels reading Maraniss's book, it is worth noting that the story of the relationship between Vietnam and the United States had a happy ending. We were able to get over the bitter feelings of our defeat and made our peace with Vietnam in the 1990s. The process began with the first President Bush, who negotiated an end to the fighting in Cambodia and helped design a path toward normalization with Vietnam. It ended with President Clinton's executing the details of that plan.

Without this effort Clark Welch, one of the heroes of this book, would not have been able to travel back to Vietnam to visit the place where his company was ambushed. Nor would Terry Allen's daughters have been able to kneel at the exact spot where their father, himself the son of a famous World War II general, had died. Nor would Welch have been able to meet and talk with Vo Minh Triet, the commander of the North Vietnamese regiment that killed Terry Allen and 60 other young Americans that terrible day.

From the commander in chief in Washington, D.C., down through the commander of military forces in Vietnam and the Black Lion Battalion, the pressure was on to get out in the field and get results, no matter what the consequences. In this case a heavily armed and very experienced regiment of North Vietnamese soldiers was waiting for the battalion. No amount of firing and maneuvering could overcome the tremendous advantage of surprise, numbers, and experience.

Military superiors would not allow the word ''ambush" to be used in reporting what happened that morning. Their reports tried to present the incident as a victory for the Black Lions. But the size of the casualty list exposed the lie.

On the field of battle Maraniss describes young men who exhibit every emotion imaginable and some unimaginable: bravery, cowardice, confusion, terror, and anger. Private Joe Costello, for example, pinned to the ground during the firefight, is helpless to save a mortally wounded comrade. ''Try as he might, he could not call up the man's name. He remembered that the guy had a candy-apple-red '62 Chevy that he was proud of and a young wife he loved very much and that he didn't care for Costello at all and Costello wished that he had." From the vantage of nearly 40 years on, the deaths of people we have come to know feel tragic. Bitter is this ending.

I finished the book wishing I knew more about Vo Minh Triet and the Vietnamese he led. Maraniss succeeds in bringing their humanity into view but does not give us the same gritty detail we get with American participants. The ordeal and suffering of Americans paled in comparison with that of the Vietnamese. Not only did North and South Vietnamese have to face the superior firepower of the French and the Americans, but they did not have the option of protest or deferment. They were ordered into battle and knew they could not stop until the war ended or they were killed.

Jim George, Gregory Landon, Mike Troyer, Francis Dowling, Harold ''Pinky" Durham, David Halliday, Santiago Griego, Peter Miller, Don Holleder, Jack Schroder, Doug Tallent, Terry Warner -- the names of these American soldiers are familiar. Their stories are familiar. Their families and hometowns are people and places I know. I grieve for the tragedy of their experience and the loss of their lives.

We will need to feel the same pain of recognition and loss when we hear Vietnamese names, if we are to realize the hope of purpose and reconciliation captured in the words of Terry Allen's widow, Jean Ponder Allen Soto, and read by his daughter Consuelo in 2002 at the site of the battle: ''Turn that ground/to wide forgiveness/And heal and free/those left behind/deep peace find/that passes understanding."

''They Marched Into Sunlight" is also a story of reconciliation and forgiveness. Between Welch and Allen, his battalion commander. Between Allen's wife, another clear hero of this book, and the community that was horrified by her betrayal of her husband. And between the children of the antagonists during the war itself. Time heals, this book seems to say, if we will only embrace rather than running from the possibility. ''We set these structures that we are so deadly serious about," says Soto about the mind-set of the Vietnam era. ''And they becomes issues of life and death and matters of betrayals and hatreds, and yet somehow, sometimes, that is what in the end creates the possibility of the two sides joyously embracing."

It was a terrible time, October 1967. And things would get a lot worse before they got better. A lot more dying, a lot more killing, a lot more bravery, and a lot more cowardice. ''They Marched Into Sunlight" shows us how human beings who are frightened, disoriented, or weakened by fatigue sometimes choose right and sometimes choose wrong. Even when they choose right the results can be fatal. As such, this is a cautionary tale. But tucked into the connections between relative strangers are the living sparks of hope. As I wept, considering the tragedy of this story, I also hoped these sparks will warm those who believe mankind has the character to live in peace.
