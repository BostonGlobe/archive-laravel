BOOK REVIEW

An overnight sensation and her hungry heart

By Anna Mundow, 9/28/2003

Personality

By Andrew O'Hagan

Harcourt, 311 pp., $25

ADVERTISEMENT

Andrew O'Hagan's remarkable first novel, ''Our Fathers," set this young Scottish writer apart from his more subversive contemporaries. With his restrained poetic style and melancholic reverence for the past, O'Hagan seemed to have more in common with William Trevor than he did with James Kelman. The story too was recognizably traditional -- a young man visiting his dying grandfather becomes immersed in memories of Socialist and family struggle. Yet the novel leaked not one whiff of pastiche; its passion and considerable wit were entirely original.

Earlier this year O'Hagan appeared on Granta's ''20 Best of Young British Novelists" list, and now his second novel, ''Personality," reinforces the impression of an unashamedly compassionate novelist unapologetically drawn to the past, even when his novel examines the modern diseases of celebrity and anorexia.

''People make out the world is all what happens tomorrow and the day after that," one character remarks toward the end of ''Personality." And from the outset -- a mysterious 1940 prologue notwithstanding -- the novel itself seems committed to advancement. The entire Tambini family, indeed the entire population of Bute, the Scottish island on which the Tambinis have lived since the 1940s, dreams of propelling 13-year-old Maria to stardom. Which in 1977 means singing on a television talent show called ''Opportunity Knocks" in London. Which may as well be Mars.

''The last ferry left for Wemyss Bay. . . . The neon signs above the doors of the pubs flicked on as the sea went pink for half an hour, the darkness seemed to come quickly after that, then just as quickly came to life as music started pouring from the pubs."

Bute has its immigrants. Maria's mother, Rosa, runs the chip shop, her uncle Alfredo is the island's hairdresser, and Maria's best friend, Kalpana, is the daughter of Bute's esteemed Indian doctor. It has its entertainments. The Amusement Arcade, for example, where ''pale light from outside crept like an illness over the carpet with its smell of spilled orangeade." When Dr. Jagannadham lectures an audience of 12 on the history of sugar, Michael, a young shop assistant, contemplates the rain-lashed windows and thinks, ''It was like the whole of the Civic Centre was going through a car wash."

O'Hagan's fine descriptions of the island's landscape, weather, and imposed intimacy reveal how far Maria must travel carrying not only the island's hopes but also the dark thoughts that shadow her mother and grandmother. ''Every day they went about their housework as if it were an act of violence," the older woman tormented by an appalling wartime memory, her daughter by a youthful mistake and a lifetime of disappointment.

Maria, however, is young, talented, and, for the moment, sunny. She triumphs on ''Opportunity Knocks," becomes a teenage superstar, performs and tours like a veteran yet lives like a child in the care of her London manager. O'Hagan cleverly uses different narrators to illuminate various strands of his story. We see much of Maria's progress through the eyes of Hughie Green, the oily host of ''Opportunity Knocks," and the device is entertaining for those of us who remember Green's noxious patter but otherwise irritating, the only false note in the novel.

Far more successful is the childish, inevitably doomed correspondence between Maria and Kalpana, which is superseded by creepy letters and recorded messages from Kevin, an obsessed fan. ''When I left school I wanted to be a cook," he writes at first. ''This is a difficult job and not very suited to me. I now would like to be in television or the radio. I think you need qualifications but I have written off anyway. Maybe you could give me some advice." Kevin quickly loses his grip, and by that time Maria is overworked and in trouble. ''Crouching by the bed . . . she sucked a piece of toast and then spat it back onto the plate. . . . [She] went into the closet where she found a bag with a furry pencil case in it. She counted out twenty laxatives and swallowed them with the water."

Even good writers stumble on descriptions of illness, love, sex. But O'Hagan pares them to their core. ''Their love wasn't a choice, it was a recognition . . . and so they seemed to enjoy something new yet something familiar, while fearing the worst the world had to offer," he writes of Maria and Michael, who meet again as adults.

Michael is always more engaging than Maria, who can develop only into a celebrity-cipher. The most memorable section of the novel, in fact, has nothing to do with her. Michael finds a job in London with the St. Clare's Review, a magazine published by a charity founded in 1915 to care for soldiers blinded in action. The office scenes are very funny. But Michael also takes blind soldiers walking on the South Downs, in Sussex: ''The last time they had seen the world it was full of smoke and flying dirt . . . now they wanted to be guided to peaceful places."

They hold on to a long pole while walking and touch one another's shoulders for guidance when they sit on the grass. ''They all liked to talk about being blind. For each of them it was the great subject of their lives. Norman could grow breathless telling you about a mustard gas attack. 'That's the last thing I knew,' he said. . . . 'It stings so powerful you wouldn't believe. Then the eyes went out.' " Every character in ''Personality" lives to some degree in the past, and only a writer of O'Hagan's clear-eyed grace could confer such nobility on the condition.

Anna Mundow, a freelance journalist living in Central Massachusetts, is a correspondent for the Irish Times.
