BOOK REVIEW

Made in Detroit, imitated around the world

By David Farber, 9/28/2003

Wheels for the World: Henry Ford, His Company, and a Century of Progress

ADVERTISEMENT

By Douglas Brinkley

Viking, 858 pp., illustrated, $34.95

Company histories don't lend themselves to lyrical prose or pulse-racing narratives. Big corporations, almost by definition, are risk-averse bureaucratic behemoths dedicated to slowly expanding market share by safeguarding their business environment. Douglas Brinkley's centennial history of the Ford Motor Co. fights the genre's intrinsic boredom by capitalizing on company founder Henry Ford's legendary escapades and by wringing a reasonable amount of drama out of the company's breakthrough model launches and marketplace ups and downs. Based on prodigious research, "Wheels for the World" suffers from a mild case of CEO-itis (i.e., company history as seen through the eyes of its top executives) but pulls the reader through some 800 pages with clever character sketches, well-informed accounts of automobile engineering, and a balanced view of Ford's role in American society.

Not surprisingly, Henry Ford carries the best part of the book's action. The most famous man in the industrialized world in the 1920s, Ford created the modern automobile business by mass producing the assembly-line-built, high-quality, low-cost Model T. He revolutionized American society by offering his workers unprecedented wages of $5 a day (roughly double the prevailing industrial wage in the World War I era). In exchange for withstanding a mind-numbing regiment "on the line," auto workers received wages that allowed them to buy what they made and join the ranks of America's burgeoning consumer society. As much as any one individual, Ford created the American alternative to a class-riven society. And around much of the world, from Calvin Coolidge's America to Lenin's Soviet Union to Hitler's Nazi Germany, Ford was viewed as an economic and social hero for proving that large-scale industrial production could provide both good wages and consumer boons.

Brinkley does not ignore the less charming aspects of Ford's character. Most infamously, Ford spent a great deal of money and energy during the 1920s distributing reams of vile anti-Jewish propaganda. Adolf Hitler later rewarded Ford with Nazi Germany's highest civilian award, and Ford took pleasure in following the Nazis' campaign against European Jewry. Ford's anti-Semitic efforts took place at a time when the Ku Klux Klan enjoyed a membership of well more than 2 million in the United States, so Ford was, alas, comfortably within the American mainstream (though not in Boston, where in 1922 Mayor James Michael Curley banned public Klan activity). For both good and ill, Ford's life makes for a vivid story, and Brinkley tells it well.

The last 60 years of Ford's history are less enthralling. Brinkley is honest enough to quote economist John Kenneth Galbraith, who glumly noted: "The reality of Ford Motor in the 1950s was that it was a commonplace, somewhat dull corporation. . . . Nobody really wants to hear the drab details about the organization." Brinkley tries to divert attention from that dullness by recounting the efforts of the various Fords and assorted others who ran the company after Henry, but none, except Lee Iacocca, really have the personality or insight into the automobile business to carry the narrative. Real "motorheads," however, will get a kick out of Brinkley's sharp, if brief, accounts of the introduction of the Mustang, the Pinto debacle, and the rise of the SUV. He might have been better off writing less about the Ford men and more about the Ford machines.

Brinkley does perk up his narrative with neat snippets about Ford autos' special place in American popular culture. The Model T was perhaps the first American consumer durable that made newspaper headlines and starred in comedians' monologues: "It's easy to gauge your speed in a Model T. When you go ten miles an hour, your lamps rattle . . . at twenty-five miles, the windshield begins to rattle, and when you go faster than that, your bones rattle." From Chuck Berry lyrics (check out "Maybellene") to Jack Kerouac's "On the Road" homage to the '49 Ford and the Beach Boys' love affair with the classic T-Bird, Brinkley provides dozens of sparkling examples of how intertwined Ford automobility became with American life.

The Ford Motor Co. has had its hits and misses, but for a century now it has played a massive role in shaping the international economy and global culture. Brinkley, on occasion a little friendlier to the company than some readers might appreciate (he enjoyed open access to Ford's archives and is a little too sweet on the current Ford executive team), provides a compelling account of the company's incredible success story.

A coda: Over the past few years a number of popular historians have been properly raked over the coals for plagiarism. Given the problem, it's worth noting that Brinkley does a great job pointing out the multitude of scholars to whom his work is indebted. Not only does Brinkley give praise and credit where it's due, but he also lets readers know where to go if they want to learn more about a topic. Brinkley, a well-known TV commentator and professor of history and director of the Eisenhower Center at the University of New Orleans, demonstrates that good, honest scholarship can produce lively popular history.

David Farber is the author of "Sloan Rules: Alfred P. Sloan and the Triumph of General Motors." He is writing a book about the Iran hostage crisis of 1979-81.
