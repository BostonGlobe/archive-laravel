BOOK REVIEW

The birth of an electrified world, and its midwives

By Steve Weinberg, 9/7/2003

Empires of Light: Edison, Tesla, Westinghouse, and the Race to Electrify the World, By Jill Jonnes, Random House, 416 pp., illustrated, $27.95

ADVERTISEMENT

In an era of pathography as biography, it is inevitable that longtime cultural heroes will be unmasked as flawed. Although historian Jill Jonnes is not intent on exposing the flaws of hero Thomas Alva Edison, her approach in "Empires of Light" makes him seem frequently greedy, jealous, and downright petty: in other words, an unmistakable man of clay and dust, rather than solely a genius inventor.

This might seem a strange point to begin with because "Empires of Light" is not being marketed as a biography. But no matter how it is marketed in stores and cataloged in libraries, the human element prevails, with Edison looming especially large.

Set mostly during the 1880s and '90s, "Empires of Light" explains how businesses, residences, and public thoroughfares came to be lighted by that seemingly magical phenomenon called electricity. It explains, then explains some more. Jonnes, a Johns Hopkins University historian, examines the spread of electricity in the United States from just about every conceivable angle. Is this a history of scientific achievement? You bet. Is this a history of corporate rapaciousness? Sure is. Is this a history of consumer demand? Right again. Is this history as biography? Jackpot.

In previous books, Jonnes, a rare academic who strives to be accessible to generalists, chronicled the history of the South Bronx ("We're Still Here: The Rise, Fall, and Resurrection of the South Bronx"), then the history of illegal drugs spreading throughout the United States ("Hep-Cats, Narcs, and Pipe Dreams: A History of America's Romance With Illegal Drugs"). Those books contained character sketches, to be sure. This time around, Jonnes does more than sketch the major characters; she brings them to life through cumulative biographical detail.

Her three protagonists are named in the subtitle: Edison, famous inventor during the Gilded Age; Nikola Tesla, Serbian immigrant engineer; and George Westinghouse, corporate empire builder.

The life stories of all three men have been told in previous books, as Jonnes wisely acknowledges. Regarding Edison, she gives special credit to Paul Israel's 1998 biography. Regarding Tesla, she offers praise to Marc Seifer's 1996 biography. Westinghouse, on the other hand, had fallen victim to a biography gap, with nothing appearing since Henry G. Prout's 1926 work. Jonnes also gives a nod to previous books about the way commercially viable electricity changed towns and cities throughout the United States during the final decades of the 19th century and the early decades of the 20th century.

Part of the reason for the book's success is Jonnes's ability to make physics, chemistry, engineering, and other "hard" sciences accessible. One narrow but meaningful way to view her book is "Electricity for Idiots." The nonscientists among her audience will be grateful.

A second reason for the success is the inherent drama concerning whose vision will prevail. Edison, Tesla, and Westinghouse were geniuses who appreciated each other's skills, but they were also fierce competitors as inventors and businessmen. Edison seems to have won when viewed from the perspective of 2003, because he is so much better remembered than Tesla and Westinghouse. But from the vantage point of a century ago, the case could be made that Tesla and Westinghouse, who became allies, prevailed in the electricity wars.

A third significant reason for the book's success is the decision by Jonnes to sometimes put aside her biographical focus on her three protagonists -- a risky move in life writing because readers might bail after repeated digressions -- and turn the spotlight on consumers. Jonnes does this especially well with William H. Vanderbilt, railroad tycoon and multimillionaire beyond measure. As Edison, Tesla, and Westinghouse tried to harness electricity, the Vanderbilt family members became willing guinea pigs. During the early 1880s, Vanderbilt was supervising construction of the most fabulous (or ostentatious) mansion in all of New York City. He wanted electric light throughout the house -- for practical reasons and for prestige. Not to give away Jonnes's punchline, let's just say the project could have gone more smoothly.

The book takes other detours. For example, Jonnes chronicles how law enforcement authorities wanted to harness electricity to execute condemned murderers in her chapter "The Horrible Experiment." Always, though, the protagonists emerge, as Jonnes sees their obsessions with electricity through to their dying day. For Westinghouse, that day came in 1914, for Edison in 1931, and for Tesla in 1943.

In a lengthy epilogue, Jonnes summarizes the achievements of the three men and those they hired to make their visions come true. By the final page, however, it becomes clear that Jonnes's number-one hero is the electricity phenomenon itself:

"Electricity unleashed the Second Industrial Revolution, bestowing on man incredible gifts -- the untold hours once lost to simple darkness, the even greater hours lost to drudging human labor, and the consequent freeing and flourishing of the human mind and imagination."

Steve Weinberg is a biographer in Columbia, Mo.
