ON AUDIO

Something suspenseful this way comes

By Rochelle O'Gorman, 9/7/2003

The Face
By Dean Koontz
Random House Audio; unabridged fiction; 16 CDs; 19 hours and 30 minutes; $59.95; read by Dylan Baker. Also available unabridged on 12 cassettes; $49.95; or for downloading at www.audible.com; $32.95; both read by Baker. For information, call 800-541-5525.

To the Nines
By Janet Evanovich
Audio Renaissance; unabridged fiction; five cassettes; eight hours and 30 minutes; $29.95; read by Lorelei King. Also available unabridged on seven CDs; $37.95; read by King.

The Mysterious Affair at Styles
By Agatha Christie
Audio Renaissance; abridged fiction; two cassettes; three hours; $12.95; read by Hugh Fraser.

You usually can't go wrong with best-selling authors. You know what to expect and are rarely disappointed. A case in point is Dean Koontz's latest novel, "The Face."

At the core of the story is Channing Manheim, one of the most dazzling stars in the Hollywood firmament. He has power, money, prestige, and astonishing good looks. What he does not have is time for his son, Aelfric, a sweet but precocious 10-year-old who is left home on a Hollywood estate with a gaggle of servants. The entire estate is under the watchful eye of Ethan Truman, head of security. Enter Corky Laputa, an English professor who has dedicated his life to anarchy by creating chaos everywhere he goes. Hoping to prove that anyone is vulnerable, he plans to kidnap the well-protected Aelfric. Unbeknown to Corky, supernatural forces in the guise of a very unlikely guardian angel have conspired to protect little Aelfric and well-meaning Ethan. In an especially sly bit of mystical meddling, Aelfric receives what he believes to be crank calls, but they are actually ghostly warnings.

That supernatural element makes this fable quite different from most of Koontz's suspense novels. His love of language is readily apparent and Koontz writes with a skillful musicality that works exceptionally well on audio. Though the lengthy story is in need of some trimming, his writing is as clever as always and his dry sense of humor is a foil for the more frightening elements.

Narrator Dylan Baker has an interesting, malleable voice, the kind associated with character actors. His voice is filled with personality, though it is a little on the high side. The actor speaks with a similar voice for both Truman and Laputa, but he picks up accents for most of the surrounding characters, so you are apt to hear a passable Scottish brogue or a change of pitch for a particularly menacing person. Baker is very good at changing his attitude and his tone, so Aelfric sounds younger, more innocent, and eventually more frightened than the rest of the characters.

Janet Evanovich's Stephanie Plum mysteries are much lighter fare. Like the rest of the series, "To the Nines" is fast-paced, funny, breezy, and delightfully escapist. Plum is a bounty hunter operating out of Trenton, N.J. While bringing in scumbags and chasing down leads, she slowly realizes that she is the target of a serial killer. Her devastatingly sexy and kind of scary co-worker, Ranger, assigns a platoon of bodyguards to protect her. However, she brings bad luck, not to mention broken bones, to these burly men sworn to keep her from harm.

The usual eccentric Evanovich characters are here, and they are as comical as ever. In fact, this is one of the few continuous mystery series in which each entry guarantees laughter. Narrator Lorelei King does a fabulous job, as each character is assigned a distinctive voice, thus milking the humor for every drop. Lula is especially enjoyable. She is a brassy, sassy, and opinionated black woman, and that is exactly how she sounds. Co-worker Connie has a tough, unrefined Jersey accent, while the men in the story have lower voices and a slower pacing that distinguishes them.

King's only misstep is Plum's grandmother Mazur. She, and every other elderly woman in the audio, come off as screechy and over the top.

Although Agatha Christie died 27 years ago, her mysteries are still bestsellers. According to her official website, run by her only grandchild, more than 2 billion of her books have been sold, in more than 45 languages.

"The Mysterious Affair at Styles" is a typical Christie offering. Mrs. Inglethorpe is murdered at her lovely English estate in the Essex countryside, putting a host of visitors under suspicion. Was it her gold-digging, much-too-young second husband? Or perhaps one of her guests slipped that strychnine into her coffee? Belgian detective Hercule Poirot is called in to investigate, which he does, of course, with much panache.

Though first released in 1929, this murder mystery is every bit as entertaining now as it was then. Christie allows us to meet the characters and travel through twists and turns before we know who did it. An added plus is the now-historical aspect, as the lifestyles of the British gentry are far removed from the hustle and bustle of modernity. It is akin to popping another era into your stereo.

This is just one in a series of audio adaptations from Audio Renaissance, the only US publisher approved by the Christie estate to adapt her stories for audio format. Sixteen titles have been released, with eight more slated for production. The price is extremely reasonable, and the careful adaptation of this story did not hinder its ebb and flow.

Hugh Fraser was well chosen as the narrator. He has a moderately deep voice with clear diction and a melodic English accent. He easily slips into various British accents, relying on distinctions of region and class to identify various characters, though he makes no change for female characters. Best of all is the Belgian accent he uses for Poirot. Not only is the accent spot on, but Fraser speaks with a flourish and a lightness in tone that befit the brilliant, if preening, little detective.

Rochelle O'Gorman is publisher and editor in chief of audiobookstoday.com.
