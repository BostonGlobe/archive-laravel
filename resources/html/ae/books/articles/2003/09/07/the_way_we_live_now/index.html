BOOK REVIEW

The way we live now

Julie Hecht's The Unprofessionals takes ironic aim at the age of dysfunction, addiction, and distress

By Gail Caldwell, Globe Staff, 9/7/2003

The Unprofessionals, By Julie Hecht, Random House, 238 pp., $23.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Having long thought of her as a cross between Dorothy Parker and a character out of "Twin Peaks," I've been trying to figure out precisely why Julie Hecht is so funny. She is fond of comma splices and non-sentences, the latter following her, sans verbs, into a sort of desultory, neurotic inertia. Her crises are varied but exacting: bad shoulder pads, an electrologist with too many mangy cats, untoward strangers who approach her with advice in all-night drugstores. The world is too scary for her to go far -- she thinks the school milk program may be a conspiracy -- but she can make a trip to CVS into an expedition worthy of a Himalayan trek, returning home with little bags of chocolate and Xanax. Fey anthropologist of contemporary culture that she is, she scarcely avoids alienation and despair. When she confides in her husband that she feels alone and estranged, he answers, from the depths of his crossword puzzle, "That's man's fate." Clearly, he's become accustomed to his partner's singular modern horrors.

But I'm lapsing into the common error of confusing Hecht here with her first-person protagonist -- a nameless photographer in her late 40s who narrates both of Hecht's works of fiction, and who, like Hecht, lives year-round in island communities resembling the Hamptons and Nantucket. Because her 1997 story collection, "Do the Windows Open?," and her novel, "The Unprofessionals," roam these war zones with the same full-throttle, up-close angst and deadpan comic sensibility, one often fails to differentiate between character and creator. Well, whatever, as Hecht would say. Or wouldn't say. Or would say but then explain she had only said it because she'd heard it somewhere but didn't actually like the expression, and . . . oh, never mind. Perceiving life as daily hand-to-hand combat, Hecht is funny the way the original "M*A*S*H" was funny -- on the battlefield, death-knell irony is the only way to survive.

Granted, a lot of readers won't find the Hecht doppelganger's problems exactly a state of siege, or even worthy of ironic war simile. Still, it's the side-angle vision of her sorrows -- her running commentary on the little disturbances of man -- that provides her work with much of its heft. Death is all over the place in Hecht's fiction, but you usually can't see it, because it's obscured with the neurotic displacement of the narrator's pain. "It was the second month of living without a soul and I was getting used to the feeling," begins "The Unprofessionals," with the usual Hechtian understatement. The caches of chocolate and Xanax follow, and it is only later, and half-parenthetically, that we realize the narrator is mourning the death of her father -- a subject that comes up as a brief mention in the novel, but hovers throughout its bleak comedy of life's ongoing errors.

Much of the subject matter of Hecht's previous stories concerned her narrator's quest to photograph a world-renowned reproductive surgeon named Dr. Arnold Loquesto, whose young sons she befriended along the way. In the novel, a decade has passed, and the boy who was 11 in "Do the Windows Open?" has finished college -- he's an upper-middle-class Republican who irons his khakis and, perhaps not coincidentally, is a heroin addict. He is also our narrator's closest friend -- a young man she refers to as "the boy," talks with for hours by phone between his trips to rehab, and with whom she collaborates on a worldview dominated by effete irony. Together, they deconstruct pop culture (bad), psychiatry (dangerously bad), and medication ("I hate Ambien!" they say in unison), developing an intimacy deepened by their shared distress. When the father, now relocated in southern California, calls the narrator, it is either to grill her or to provide updates on the boy's drug addiction and recovery. When the boy calls her, more and more, it is a conversation filled with mania, lies, or rue and regret.

"The Unprofessionals" takes its title from what the boy and the narrator think of the psychiatric and helping professions, and in many ways this novel is about the futility of consciousness -- about how ineffective insight can be, whether yours or someone else's, in getting to the other side of trouble. Still, the narrator relies on the old Freudian prescription; "Work and love," she tells the boy about what makes life worth living, "the same old two things." Mostly, though, she listens -- and worries, and leaves messages on the boy's cellphone that are never returned. Unfolding back and forth through a sort of eternally present tense of hindsight, the novel begins to take on more meaning in retrospect -- the narrator's concerns, and worse, qualms, seem less driven by anxiety than by plain old love.

This is a weird, deeply likable novel -- hardly a surprise, given Hecht's weird and deeply likable story collection, which almost immediately took on a cult following. But "The Unprofessionals" is more claustrophic; because it follows the arc of a friendship with its own limitations -- the telephone and addiction being two of them -- it relies almost entirely on dialogue and the narrator's ruminative analysis. This can get old fast when it isn't perfectly conceived, and several passages in the novel feel more like filler than any planned effort to further or enhance the narrative.

Like most brilliant comic writers, Hecht employs restraint as well as deadpan wit to deliver her best lines. But the real basis of her gifts is not that she's so wacky -- it's that she's so much like the rest of us, taken to the extreme. And beneath all that unapologetically desolate, funny, disaster-mode living is a tender grasp of the human condition that seems brave and resolute. There's a beautiful, awful moment in the novel when the narrator focuses on the English rose pattern of an old upholstered chair -- precisely the kind of paralyzed myopia that people tend to engage in under similar circumstances. She worries over those roses, then thinks that "probably the British know all the terrible things in life that can happen on their rose-covered chairs and they take them in stride." Julie Hecht does, too.
