BETWEEN THE LINES

With Martha Ackmann

Rediscovering a tale of thwarted dreams

By Robin Dougherty, 9/7/2003

At the dawn of the Cold War era, America's obsession to go to the moon forced the careers of some its best aviators into eclipse. From the start of the space program, female pilots vied to be taken seriously as astronaut candidates. Although they excelled at the rigorous physical and psychological tests created for Mercury astronauts John Glenn and Scott Carpenter, the women with the right stuff were pushed aside.

Now, anyone searching for the missing links between Amelia Earhart and Sally Ride need look no further than "The Mercury 13: The Untold Story of Thirteen American Women and the Dream of Space Flight." Martha Ackmann's book tells the story of the baker's dozen of female pilots who should have been among our first space travelers.

Ackmann, who teaches women's studies at Mount Holyoke College, talked from her home in Western Massachusetts about the women she rescued from their undeserved place as footnotes to history.

How did you piece together the story of the Mercury 13?

It was about 1997, with John Glenn [about to go up] on the space shuttle. . . . I saw an article that said "Jerrie Cobb is not too happy about it." . . . I grew up in a space family in St. Louis in the '60s when McDonnell had the contract to build the Mercury capsule. Every father on the block worked on the capsule . . . [but] I never heard of Jerrie Cobb or this story. I'm always interested in writing about American women who do accomplish unusual things. . . . Because I had that background, I wrote a series of op-eds around the time of the Glenn flight, saying Jerrie Cobb is still waiting for her chance to go up.

In some ways, the story begins with Randy Lovelace, the chairman of NASA's Life Sciences Committee, who helped design the tests for the Mercury astronauts. In 1959, it occurred to him that women might be better suited to space travel than men.

I think he was fueled by medical curiosity. He was one of those large human beings who . . . didn't buy into assumptions or stereotypes. He . . . simply wondered what would happen. . . . Medical tests always used men. The rest of the world thought it was a waste of time and money [to test women]. And certainly when they got to the stage where they had to have the cooperation of the Navy and military, people thought, "Well, we are not tying up machines to test women." Jerrie Cobb pointed out they had money to test monkeys.

There were many women pilots in the 1950s and early '60s. Some had been in the Women's Airforce Service Pilots during World War II.

From Earhart's time on, there have always been women who have been intrigued by the sky. I asked Janey Hart [one of the Mercury 13, and the wife of former Michigan senator Philip Hart] if she could describe to me why. . . . She said [it was] the absolute freedom of being up there. It's a freedom coupled with confidence. . . . It's very technically complicated and requires courage and will. I heard that in variations from lots of other women. . . . There were women who, like many women, really wanted to see how far they could go. We pay a terrible price for racism and sexism and don't let people go as far as they can. The door was literally shut for them.

What were some of the aspects of women's physiologies or psychologies that made them good candidates for space?

This was at a time when every ounce of a payload mattered, so if someone weighed less and used less oxygen and less food, all this could be viewed as an advantage. . . . When the test results [from] the isolation chambers proved that women could withstand deprivation better -- it was ironic, tragic, and profound at the same time.

How did the women score on the tests?

They found out on that first round that Cobb performed at a very high level, as well as the best of the [male] astronauts. She had strength, stamina, resilience, contrary to many assumptions that women couldn't push through. She did as well as perfectly physically fit military test pilots who were the cream of the crop. There were people [in 1960] saying, "No, this is a special order of human beings, and women can't do this."

The testing was secret. The women came in separately and were not in touch with each other. Why?

Like the men pilots, they were sworn to secrecy. No one knew how it would turn out. It was conceivable that no one would pass the tests. That was just scientific protocol. . . . They originally wanted to test two dozen women but not all accepted the invitation or they couldn't get off from work. . . . They went [to Lovelace's Albuquerque lab] at various times, not as a group. Lovelace was shoehorning their testing around other tests.

After Jerrie Cobb passed the flight simulation phase, further tests on women were suspended and none of them was ever hired by NASA.

Once the question shifted from being a medical one and it became a political one, the question was "Do they have a right to be astronauts?" Cobb and Hart directed their work to petition Congress. When they appealed to LBJ [then vice-president], he wrote "Let's stop this now" on a memo. There were so many moments when things could have changed during the hearing if Glenn and Carpenter -- two of the biggest heroes in the US -- said there's no reason women can't be astronauts

. . . .

It was another 20 years till Sally Ride became the first woman astronaut. And not until 1999 was there a woman commander on the space shuttle -- Eileen Collins. She is the first to say she stands on the shoulders of the 13.

Robin Dougherty, a writer and critic, lives in Washington, D.C. She can be reached at inkrd@aol.com.
