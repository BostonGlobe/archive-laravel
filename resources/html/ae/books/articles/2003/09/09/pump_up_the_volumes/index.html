Pump up the volumes

Facing slumping book sales and a shortage of new writing stars, the publishing world is hoping author John Grisham will help ...

By Mark Feeney, Globe Staff, 9/9/2003

What the summer is in Hollywood, the fall is on Publishers Row: the season that generates the most income and determines the year's financial success or failure. The upcoming fall season is even more important than usual. This year has not been a good one in the book business.

"Even with Harry and Hillary, and the renewed Oprah's Book Club," says Owen Teicher, chief operating officer of the American Booksellers Assocation, an industry trade group, "many retailers have fought just basically to stay even with where they were a year ago -- and a year ago wasn't the best of years. It's been tough times." "Harry" is, of course, Harry Potter. As expected, "Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix," the fifth volume in J.K. Rowling's series, has had phenomenal sales. Less expected was the success of Hillary -- Hillary Rodham Clinton's "Living History," which dominated the nonfiction bestseller list in the late spring and early summer. The other big seller of the past few months was a paperback, John Steinbeck's

"East of Eden," which enjoyed that status thanks to its selection by Oprah Winfrey's book club. If a few months ago Harry, Hillary, and Oprah came to the rescue of publishing, sort of, is anyone likely to do it this fall?

No one book is expected to stand out the way the new "Harry Potter" or "Living History" did. But publishing experts cite several titles they expect to do well.

One candidate is John Grisham's "Bleachers," which arrives in stores today. Grisham's legal thrillers long ago made him a brand-name author -- so much so that even when he ventures outside the courtroom, as with "Skipping Christmas" and "A Painted House," he still jumps on the bestseller list.

"Bleachers" has a first printing of 2 million copies. That's 800,000 fewer than the standard first printing for his legal thrillers. But its story of a former high school football star who returns home for his coach's funeral offers intriguing possibilities for promotion, says Suzanne Herz, executive director of publicity at Doubleday, Grisham's publisher.

"It gives us a completely new market to explore for John," says Herz. "We're hitting up the sports market wherever we can."

"It's not a bad combination: Texas, football, nostalgia, and Grisham," says Gayle Feldman, a contributing editor at Publishers Weekly magazine and author of "Best and Worst of Times: The Changing Business of Trade Books, 1975-2002."

Feldman notes that unlike such other brand-name authors as Tom Clancy and Stephen King, Grisham's sales have remained fairly steady.

Yet a problem for the industry is that a new generation of brand-name authors -- those writers whose name appearing on a dust jacket can guarantee a book's making the bestseller list -- hasn't really emerged. The thriller author James Patterson is the only writer in the past few years to attain that status. His most recent novel, "The Lake House," came out in June.

"Patterson is really the one," Feldman says. "You look at Dan Brown [author of the current bestseller `The Da Vinci Code']. Is he going to be able to do it again? We don't know."

Feldman points to a number of titles that look promising for fall sales. Heading the list is the memoir by former Iraqi prisoner of war Jessica Lynch, written with Rick Bragg, the Pulitzer Prize-winning former New York Times writer.

Toni Morrison has "Love," her first novel in more than five years. Former Secretary of State Madeleine K. Albright hopes her memoir, "Madame Secretary," can imitate the success of "Living History." If Mitch Albom's first novel, "The Five People You Meet in Heaven," even approaches the sales of his previous book, "Tuesdays With Morrie," it'll be a smash.

Unless the author is a public figure, like Albright, predicting future sales is almost entirely based on looking at past sales. That tends to limit the pool of possibilities. As Feldman notes, "What's reassuring is the huge book that comes from nowhere, like [Alice Sebold's] `The Lovely Bones' and [Laura Hillenbrand's] `Seabiscuit.' "

Both independent and chain retailers are professing optimism. "I'm expecting a fabulous fall," says Frank Kramer, owner of Harvard Book Store in Cambridge. "We expect sales to strengthen through the rest of the year," says Anne Roman, corporate affairs counsel at Borders Group Inc., which owns the Borders and Waldenbooks chains.

Whether that optimism is justified remains to be seen. "It's still a lot of guesswork," says the ABA's Teicher about predicting sales. "You just don't know. If people in the publishing business knew the answer to that question, they'd be a lot richer."
