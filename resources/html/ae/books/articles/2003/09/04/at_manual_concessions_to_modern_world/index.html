At Manual, concessions to modern world

By David Mehegan, Globe Staff, 9/4/2003

When my brief career as a book publisher's editor began 31 years ago, I was handed my fat, heavy copy of an orange book titled A Manual of Style. Published by the University of Chicago Press and long known to editors as "the Chicago manual," it was considered the holy writ of book editors. More than a writing and editing style guide, it dealt with copyright law, book production, galley- and page-proofing, indexing, typographical handling of math, foreign languages, tables and charts, and on and on. It filled more than 700 pages.

Naif that I was, I imagined that I must memorize the book, or at least absorb and master it. For weeks, I pored over it on buses and subway trains, scrutinizing its magisterial canons like a Jesuit seminarian studying "The Spiritual Exercises."

I never mastered it, of course, but it did become my core reference, an authority I could cite in any difference with an author about capitalization, hyphenation, or bibliographical order. "Well, it is Chicago style," one might say, as if only a philistine need hear more.

There were other styles -- the Modern Language Association's and American Psychological Association's, not to mention the Associated Press's and those of individual newspapers -- but in the book world Chicago's position was the ne plus ultra.

This memory came back as the 15th edition -- swollen to 956 pages -- appeared this summer. Since the 13th edition, in 1982, the book has been titled simply The Chicago Manual of Style. The new edition, said to be a more extensive makeover than 1993's 14th edition, tries to accommodate the vast changes in printing technology, computer-aided researching, writing, editing, and typesetting. It throws out a lot of antique stuff about letterpress, linotype, and monotype; linotype is not even in the glossary.

It prescribes the proper form for footnote citation of websites. It even has a guide for the typographical treatment of American Sign Language.

But of most interest to a manuscript editor are the subtle yet noticeable changes in the 15th edition's preferred styling of abbreviations, punctuation, capitalization, numbers, and conventions of usage.

In capitalization, Chicago style was famously "down" with titles -- i.e., favoring lowercase -- which seemed strange to many eyes: the queen of England; the pope; Abraham Lincoln, president of the United States. Explaining its severely "down" style with titles of nobility, the 13th edition acknowledged: "British usage favors a more liberal use of capitals." The 15th edition has gone somewhat over to the British side. The earl of Shaftsbury, for example, has happily regained his capital E. In some cases "the queen" now can be "the Queen."

There are usage concessions, sometimes grudging. Consider "hopefully," which barricade-building grammarians denounced for decades: "The old meaning of the word (`in a hopeful manner') seems unsustainable; the newer meaning (`I hope' or `it is to be hoped') seems here to stay." However, it adds this little grumble: "But many careful writers deplore the new meaning."

I took it to be long-settled law that "it cost over a hundred dollars" was wrong, that "over" meant above in space and that correct usage required: "It cost more than a hundred dollars." The 15th shrugs: "As an equivalent of more than, [over] is perfectly good idiomatic English." You mean "good idiomatic" is not an oxymoron? Even split infinitives are "sometimes" acceptable.

Other changes: Chicago long held out against US Postal Service two-letter abbreviations of states -- "MA" and "CA" -- but now admits that that usage has all but supplanted "Mass." and "Calif." A venerable Houghton Mifflin editor I knew loathed "MA" and "CA." I can still hear her fuming, as her sharp blue pencil descended like a cruise missile, "Those are post office abbreviations!"

In ordinal numbers, it's now "2nd" and "3rd," where it was "2d" and "3d." In the past, "13 December 1883" -- a dying form -- was stoutly preferred, but the new edition prefers "December 13, 1883," since it is "the style most commonly used in the United States."

From my 12th edition, I learned what I took to be the correct way to handle ellipsis points, used to denote elisions in quoted text: essentially, three dots for an elision in midsentence, four for an elision at the end or for several elided sentences. In the 15th, the writer or editor is offered three different systems of ellipsis points to choose from.

Feelings have made their claim. The introduction to the numerous exceptions to the rule for plural possessives has a curious admission of the importance of editorial emotion: "Since feelings on these matters sometimes run high, users of this manual may wish to modify or add to the exceptions."

Hitherto, when Chicago said something was preferred, it seldom said why. But as in the last example, the tone of authority may seem slightly eroded in the 15th edition. In all previous editions, going back to 1906, decisions were made by the University of Chicago Press editors. The new edition, however, was prepared with help from an outside advisory board that even included a newspaper person: Mary Knoblauch, writing coach at the Chicago Tribune.

I make no complaint about any of these changes. Be details as they may, The Chicago Manual remains at heart what it always was: a detailed application of the principle that consistency is a good thing, that fine distinctions matter. "In any age," writes Bryan A. Garner, author of the chapter on grammar and usage, "careful users of language will make distinctions; careless users will blur them."

It's clear to anyone who hears or reads that the English of advertising, business, and especially politics is pretty well debased and seldom careful, except to ignore or cover up inconvenient distinctions. (Are "weapons programs" the same thing as "weapons"?) But with our Chicago Manuals at the ready, those of us who are writers and editors, nothing more, will hang on stubbornly to the conviction that, in language and life, a careful distinction can make all the difference.
