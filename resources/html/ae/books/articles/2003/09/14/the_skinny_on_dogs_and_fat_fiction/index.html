A READING LIFE

The skinny on dogs and fat fiction

By Caroline Leavitt, 9/14/2003

Getting married or divorced? Contemplating sisterhood or parenthood or home? Whatever your pleasure - or your pain - if you wander through any bookstore, you're almost certain to find an anthology calling out your particular name.

I've never truly been a dog person (I'm a cat and reptile woman myself), but the book ``Dog Is My Co-Pilot: Great Writers on the World's Oldest Friendship,'' from Bark magazine editors Claudia Kawczynska and Cameron Woo (Crown, $25), is so immediately irresistible that my particular pet preference doesn't matter. Maybe it's the playful title, or the bright, fetching cover drawing of a dog. Maybe it's the anthology's pedigree - Bark has been described as The New Yorker of dog magazines, and these reprints are from such literary luminaries as Caroline Knapp, Rick Bass, and Ann Patchett. For whatever reason, this is a book I have to read.

The anthology is separated into sections: ``Beginnings,'' where human meets dog; ``Pack,'' where human and dog bond; ``Lessons,'' where dog teaches human; and ``Passages,'' where loss rears its head. But the demarcation feels a little arbitrary to me since many of the stories cover the same territory - i.e., how a particular canine takes over a household and changes someone's life with a variety of furry lessons. I'm not wild about the design of the chapter openers - the opening sentence spans the two columns of type beneath it - which makes for a jarring reading experience. Too, a few sentiments are also a paw or two over the top. In ``God Is Dog Spelled Properly,`` Lama Surya Das's otherwise engaging tale, he claims dogs ``are holy because they are wholly themselves.'' Bonnie Jo Campbell's story, ``My Dog, Roscoe,'' while wonderfully written, is a tad heavy on the whimsy as it offers up a woman who believes her dog is the reincarnation of a past lover.

But these are small quibbles when you consider how much I love the ebullient and melancholy ``Ten Things My Dog Taught Me That Made It Possible for Me to Get Married,'' Pam Houston's funny and rueful meditation on how ``loving unconditionally doesn't mean you are a self-annihilating fool.'' Also magnificent is Lynda Barry's ``One Hundred Demons: Dogs!,'' which cartoons the story of how her emotionally abusive upbringing was healed by a compassionate teacher - and how she uses those same lessons to nurture a battered dog. ``All she needed was to find the right home,'' Barry says. ``But that's true for all of us, isn't it?''

We all yearn for love without limits, for total acceptance, which is really what this splendid collection is about. And if we get that adoration from four-legged animals more often than we do from two-legged ones, is it any wonder we might elevate our dogs to godliness? ``It was our soul's job to give him a soul,'' Tom Junod says about his pet in ``Dog Years,`` and in reading this anthology, I can truly understand why.

I've been slender all my life, but ``What Are You Looking At?: The First Fat Fiction Anthology,'' by Donna Jarrell and Ira Sukrungruang (Harvest, $14), like the dog anthology, draws me in with an immediate in-your-face appeal. The title's a funny challenge, and the cover art - a corpulent beach beauty defiantly admiring herself in a hand mirror - is frankly divine. The book seeks to detail and reveal ``the fat experience.`` Because we're such a thin-obsessed culture, I know all the punishing cliches. Food is a substitute for love or sex or happiness. The plump are uncomfortable in their bodies - unless, of course, they're excessively jolly.

These stories, essays, and poems, written by and about fat people, say otherwise - and they say it deliciously, gleefully, and heart-wrenchingly, sometimes taking the stereotypes and giving them an edge, or simply turning them on their head altogether.

My favorite story is Junot Diaz's electrifying ``The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao.'' Oscar's a New Jersey romantic, a Dominican whose heart is as huge and generous as his size. Dizzy in love with a prostitute he meets in Santo Domingo, he risks everything for her - even the deadly wrath of her gangster boyfriend. Full of brilliant riffs on love, sex, and race, and told in slangy, white-hot prose, Diaz's story isn't so much about fat as it is about the miraculous and dangerous weight of love.

Monica Wood's ``Disappearing'' brims with eerie, staccato rhythms. It tells the story of an overweight girl who obsessively diets because vanishing is the only way she can imagine being able to stop the disapproving stares she collects. In Andre Dubus's ``The Fat Girl,'' a woman named Louise diets for the coveted prize of husband and home, but when she gets them, she discovers that her life is now as thin and unsatisfying as her new body.

Unapologetic and triumphant, these stories don't preach or offer solution as much as they simply illuminate and claim a specific human condition - and by doing so, universalize it.

These two wonderful anthologies are about so much more than their designated topics. Yes, the themes, the narrowing of the focus, had an impact on me. I now notice and appreciate dogs, and I consider the cost of calories even as I savor my sundaes. But I'm more aware of the larger concerns. The very human need for love. The wonder and relief of acceptance - no matter how or where or from whom you might receive it. The truths that these anthologies reveal, their ability, as William Blake said, ``to see a world in a grain of sand'' - or in a single topic, be it hot fudge or chocolate Lab - is what makes these collections, and any good writing, transcendent.
