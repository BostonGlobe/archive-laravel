BOOK REVIEW

War's unnerving sounds of silence

By Laura Ciolkowski, 9/14/2003

Deafening, By Frances Itani, Atlantic Monthly, 378 pp., $24

ADVERTISEMENT

When Pat Barker's prize-winning World War I trilogy raised the bar on the literary-historical depiction of war and human suffering in the modern age, a wealth of imaginative space was opened up for writers like Frances Itani, whose impressive debut novel, "Deafening," takes the silent world of a deaf girl and the deadly sounds and devastating psychological vibrations of trench warfare as its subject. Like Barker, Canadian author Itani does not shy away from war's unflattering profile at the same time that she eagerly exploits for her own literary purposes the raw power and complexity of life on the battlefield and on the home front.

At the heart of "Deafening" is the figure of Grania O'Neill, the third child of a family of Irish immigrants, who came into the world on the night of the Great Fire of 1896 with a head of red hair reminiscent not only of the flames that devastated her hometown of Deseronto, Ontario, but also of the scarlet fever that left her deaf at the age of 5. With the help of her grandmother and her sister Tress, Grania learns to see the sounds she cannot hear and to replicate the shape and texture of the spoken word. "But words have no sound. Not for Grania. Only feeling, as they form inside her mouth and vibrate against the lining of her throat." Grania's frequently imperfect understanding of the spoken language that she struggles to imitate -- her battle to master the difference between "sea," "see," and "C"; her puzzlement over the distinction between "taken back" and "taken aback" -- helps to transform her world into a potentially menacing place, governed by the uncertainties and curious misconceptions of a girl locked in silence.

When Grania is 9, things change dramatically. She is sent away to the Ontario Institution for the Deaf and Dumb, where she is taught to "blend in" to the hearing world around her. But the hearing man she grows to love, Jim Lloyd, quickly comes to covet Grania's protected world of silence. Soon after they are married and he departs for the Belgian front to serve as a stretcher-bearer and medic in the Great War, Jim grows desperate to escape the unbearable sounds of death that Grania cannot and will never hear. He desires shelter from the roar of shells that is "so constant he crouches and squints, as if crouching and squinting will protect his body and close his ears." He wonders what it would feel like to "shut his ears to sound -- knowing that sound will enter him anyway, through his body," and he envies Grania her inability to hear the unnatural cries of a dying soldier who has been shot through the face and makes a "terrible sound . . . from the place below the hole where his lips and mouth should be." In "Deafening," Itani deftly links sound and silence, war and deafness, bravely confronting the need for human connection in times of war and, in turn, the acute pain of sustaining such connection.

Itani's stark vision of war and her uncensored images of mangled bodies and physical and psychological suffering ultimately overpower Grania's more modest story. The Great War is quite simply an awesome presence in the novel. It not only represents the stunning clash of nations that inspires Jim with the feeling that he has "been caught up in a great, moving, humming machine such as the world had never seen." It also represents a collection of individuals whose haunting personal tales of hopelessness, bravery, or death disturb Jim's dreams and dog his every step through the battlefields. Jim must live with the image of the severely wounded but still-living boy he had been ordered to set down in the yard of dead soldiers on his first night at the front; he is unable to shake off the picture in his mind of the clenched hands of the dead or the unfortunate soldier who shot himself through the head after he was mistakenly issued two left boots and forced to march 22 miles through the mud. Itani strikes a delicate balance between the large and the small, the macro and the micro, evoking with great sensitivity the collective and individual pride, fear, and human tragedy associated with world war.

Jim eventually returns to Deseronto, as does Tress's husband, Kenan. But Kenan's return brings the unsettling physical signs of war to the homefront. Injured in battle, Kenan is left with a dead arm, a mangled face, and only one seeing eye. He has also temporarily lost his capacity for speech, leaving him to suffer alone with the nightmares he has brought back with him. The sharp tension between sound and silence and the crucial role of language in Kenan's efforts to define himself and to find his place in the world finally reconnect the story of war to the story of the courageous deaf girl. When Grania attempts to draw Kenan back to the world he has abandoned by teaching him how to speak again, returning to the lessons and the drills she learned at the Ontario Institution, the two primary narratives of "Deafening" are gracefully joined together. Itani has written a moving first novel that expresses in human terms the healing power of language and the unspeakable horrors of war.

Laura Ciolkowski teaches literature at New York University.
