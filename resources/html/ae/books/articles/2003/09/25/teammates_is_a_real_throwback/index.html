BOOK REVIEW

'Teammates' is a real throwback

By Bill Nowlin, Globe Correspondent, 9/25/2003

The Teammates: A Portrait of a Friendship, By David Halberstam, Hyperion, 217 pp., illustrated, $22.95

ADVERTISEMENT

The two top-selling baseball books of 2003 are "Moneyball," Michael Lewis's look at modern baseball and its reliance on the numbers, and David Halberstam's "The Teammates," an appreciation of the special friendship among four Red Sox players: Dominic DiMaggio, Bobby Doerr, Johnny Pesky, and Ted Williams. The two books represent opposite views of baseball and America.

Lewis's book reads like a paean to both Billy Beane, Oakland's successful general manager (a former ballplayer), and the statistics-based approach he exemplifies in constructing the modern baseball team, composed as it often is of the interchangeable parts that are today's ballplayers.

"The Teammates" offers something gentler -- a look back two or three generations to a day when players traveled by train and the Red Sox' farthest destination was St. Louis. Those were the days when players were teammates, not rent-a-roster mercenaries. That stars such as DiMaggio, Doerr, Pesky, and Williams would play for the Red Sox from 1940 through 1951 -- Doerr arriving as early as '37 and Williams soldiering on through the 1960 season -- is nearly inconceivable today, and will almost certainly be impossible henceforth.

Halberstam was inspired by an October 2001 road trip made by DiMaggio and Pesky, along with their friend Dick Flavin, from Boston to Florida to visit the gravely ill Williams. They spent three days on the road, talking about baseball and life, then spent three days visiting their Red Sox teammate of days gone by. What modern ballplayers would travel that kind of distance by car -- and do the driving themselves? Doerr would have joined them from Oregon, but he was constrained by another love and loyalty. His wife, Monica, has multiple sclerosis, and for years Doerr has traveled little so he can remain at her side.

All four were raised on the West Coast -- Pesky in Portland, DiMaggio in San Francisco, Doerr in LA, and Williams in San Diego. All four had Pacific Coast League backgrounds, three as players. Then there was Pesky, the former Portland Beavers clubhouse kid who readily tells interested parties, "I used to pick up Ted Williams's jockstrap."

Pesky was the only one who ever played for any other major league team (briefly for Detroit and Washington). And yet over the years, it's he who has earned the title "Mr. Red Sox" for his long service, in so many different capacities, with the Boston ballclub.

The bonds among these very different individuals constitute a rare and precious phenomenon -- a friendship lasting six decades. This quartet includes two members of baseball's Hall of Fame and two others (DiMaggio and Pesky) who have better stats than several of those enshrined there.

For many years, every year, they thought they had a chance to win it all. Something always went wrong, but that's certainly no surprise to Red Sox fans. These men shared baseball dreams, and together they bore the yearly dashing of those dreams as well.

This was Ted Williams's "real family," Halberstam says, with Williams's upbringing firmly in mind. Williams's parents had little in common; they divorced in his rookie year. Williams later had three unsuccessful marriages and was admittedly not the best of fathers. For Halberstam to describe him as "unwanted, unloved," though, goes too far. There is ample evidence that Williams's mother loved him very much, though it's clear that he was ribbed by his friends over her work on the streets for the Salvation Army. She had other priorities, and one can see a lot of Williams in his mother.

Halberstam's book may at times suggest a more idyllic picture of this great friendship than truly existed. Nevertheless, the four men's relationship grew closer as the years passed, and they did indeed spend a lot of time riding the rails from city to city throughout the decade they shared. Once the train pulled into the station, though, Doerr was the only one that Williams spent much time with. Should a third person join Williams and Doerr, Williams would often excuse himself and seek solitude. More often than not, he would eat in his hotel room. When in Boston, he stayed in a different hotel.

Though Williams often seems the centerpiece, Halberstam's appraisal of these four men is smart, perceptive, warm, and sensitive. No one who has had the pleasure to know one or more of these players -- Beantown's best -- would fail to recognize the accurate portraits of the players in question. The book is only slightly marred by a very few inconsequential errors. It may lean toward the sentimental, but that is also part of its appeal. It leaves us with the reassuring feeling that these men represented the best of a bygone era. We all enjoy an old movie classic once in a while, and Halberstam's gently crafted work is in touch with the era it represents.
