Detours on the road west

By Gail Galdwell, 9/21/2003

Where I Was From, By Joan Didion, Knopf, 226 pp., $23

ADVERTISEMENT

Joan Didion's voice tends to be as dry and dangerous as a Santa Ana wind, a climatological phenomenon she described some 40 years ago in her classic California essay, "Some Dreamers of the Golden Dream.'' It was there, at the beginning of "Slouching Toward Bethlehem,'' that she carved out a reputation as consummate stylist and laid the groundwork for a generation of writers: the Hemingwayesque, loaded-for-bear silent sentence, the killer adverbial clause. And it was there, in her devastating portrait of a state founded on greed and amnesia, that she gave us a piece of California so contradictory, ominous, and unforgettable that it would seem truer than the place itself. "This is the California,'' she wrote about the San Bernardino Valley, "where it is possible to live and die without ever eating an artichoke, without ever meeting a Catholic or a Jew. This is the California where it is easy to Dial-A-Devotion, but hard to buy a book. ... The future always looks good in the golden land, because no one remembers the past.''

Several books and decades later, Didion has decided to remember the past, though she concedes at the outset of "Where I Was From'' that her perception of her birthplace remains full of "misapprehensions and misunderstandings.'' This is not so much a disclaimer as a point of origin; like a lot of those frontier crossings almost two centuries ago, "Where I Was From'' is more about momentum than actual destination. It is an odd book: part family history, part socio-political analysis, part scattershot indictment that never really succeeds. Didion's last several books have angled more toward the political _ toward conspiratorial netherlands and general anomie _ than her earlier work; the problem with this literarily is that it makes for a weird marriage between style and subject. Without the human drama _ with only, say, the convicting statistics of industry and financial growth, unemployment, population booms _ her work begins to read like actuarial tables as conceived by Don DeLillo: darkly suspicious but unintelligibly dull. When she pierces the California saga of (again) greed and amnesia with the personal stories that confirm her thesis, her writing is as riveting as ever. When she relies solely on a sort of faceless economic and political history, the book seems half-hearted and desultory _ a pastiche of old state histories, paintings, diaries, and California novels (Frank Norris's "The Octopus,'' Jack London's "The Valley of the Moon,'' even her own "Run River'') in search of a defining character.

The difference between the Deep South and the West, or California, as Didion sees it, is the former's concession to history. "In the South,'' she writes, "they remained convinced that they had bloodied their land with history. In California we did not believe that history could bloody the land, or even touch it.'' This freedom, or blindness, is what Didion perceives as California's defining force; from the moment those wagonloads of settlers launched what, for some, would be a cross-country death march, they were shedding their entire pasts and identities. "Where I Was From'' opens with crossing tales, those of women as far back as Didion's great-great-great-grandmother, women who knew how to shoot and build fences and butcher a steer. They also had a tendency toward madness, or being "nervous,'' or "different,'' those telling 19th-century euphemisms for women who couldn't quite bear the lot they'd been handed.

Didion, of course, has written famously of such telltale differences in her own life, and her comprehension of the stark hardship these women faced _ cholera, starvation, children buried on mountain passes -- sets up her story with commendable force, though it can hardly deliver the analysis her thesis requires. Tracing the character of California's settlers to the same forward-looking grit that drove them there, she sees the state's historical blueprint as a unique mix of artifice, aggression, and the willful denial that can be a legacy of manifest destiny. The unstoppable drive of her ancestors would emerge in the giants of agribusiness and development a century later, she argues; the ruthless boosterism of the survivor instinct would surface in future decades everywhere from anti-immigration sentiment to the state's grandiose efforts to make its desert a watery paradise. The sad outcome of this regional temperament culminates here in the 1993 notoriety known as the Spur Posse scandal, when a gaggle of white boys in Lakewood, Calif., starting attacking women and keeping score. A town created on false promises and class-mobility dreams, Lakewood sounds ominously like that artichokeless, bookless California that Didion described in "Some Dreamers of the Golden Dream." Her withering indictment of the community's hooligans -- "There were the dead eyes, the thick necks, the jaws that closed only to chew gum" -- can be devastating, and it reminds one of what Didion does with a few bulletproof descriptors. The connective narrative of "Where I Was From" then wanders from Lakewood to the California prison system, a new growth industry reliant on "selling the future of the place we lived to the highest bidder." This leap suggests why the entire tapestry of the book is more a product of stitched-together remnants than it is whole cloth. Much of the text appeared in earlier versions in The New York Review of Books, Esquire, and The New Yorker, which is unfortunately how it too often reads -- as discrete essays joined by a strained narrative overlay.

Didion's mother died two years ago, at the age of 90, and the author devotes her final pages to that passage: to the sorrows of the family, cultivated and inherited, and to the crossing stories that defined them, including her mother's last frontier. This brief section is eloquent, spare, and rendered without sentiment; it is also, for my money, more compelling than every dark mini-narrative about

McDonnell Douglas or Boeing or Lakewood that precedes it. "This is not a book about my family,"

Didion protests, in an extraordinary few paragraphs written as promotional material to accompany the image of a train plowing across the jacket of "Where I Was From." "This is a book about an historical nihilism that determined my life as surely as it determined their lives, and where that nihilism came from." That's an

intrepid conceit and a surefire definition of what this hybrid

socio-history wants to be; and I wish the book lived up to its ambition. But like too many myths about the West that took hold from the beginning, Didion's locomotive of history is fueled more by speculation than by measured truth.
