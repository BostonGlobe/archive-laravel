Torn by doubt and two loves, a writer reflects

By Mameve Medwed, 9/21/2003

Shipwreck, by Louis Begley. Knopf, 244 pp., $23

ADVERTISEMENT

Louis Begley's seventh novel, "Shipwreck," is so enthralling that the temptation is to let a thousand glowing adjectives bloom.

Alas, paragraphs of "fabulous," "terrific," "wonderful" do not a review make, never mind the English major's rule that too many adjectives drown a sentence faster than loose lips sink ships. All that's left, then, is to pull up the reviewer's socks and put it another way: This tale of a writer riddled by doubts about the quality of his work, shamed by his unbridled envy and narcissism, and set on a self-destructive course to ruin everything good in his life will pin readers to their chair right up to the final page. What's more, in the course of these revelations, the author and his protagonist dissect both the creative process and the writer's soul.

The first chapter starts with a brilliant conceit. John North, a novelist "of considerable literary reputation," arrives at a Paris cafe ominously called L'Entre Deux Mondes. He introduces himself to the unnamed narrator. "I will tell you a story I have never told before," he tantalizes. Fascinated, the narrator embraces the role of ideal listener. All the while, the question of his identity adds tier upon tier of speculation to an already multilayered construct. That North "is a man so like me in appearance and demeanor," the narrator notes, makes us wonder if, in fact, this audience of one is North's alter ego. Or is he, perhaps, the blank page facing all writers who wrestle to temper its whiteness with life experiences that can be developed into fiction?

North's story within a story begins at another cafe. Upon publication of the French translation of his newest, acclaimed novel, the author starts to distrust his celebrated genius. Home alone, he riffles through his novels, only to find that his sentences "lay on the page like so many vulgar, odious pieces of shattered glass. His books all belonged to the same dreary breed of unneeded books. Novels that are not embarrassingly bad but lead you to wonder why the author had bothered." In such a mood, North ends up at the Cafe Flore in Paris for an interview with a young woman from French Vogue. When the girl, Lea, praises his work, he's embarrassed. Still, he's determined to impress her. Soon, with authorial displacement in high gear, he decides to sleep with her; "that was my project, not my book."

Once declared, this desire segues into scenes from his marriage. His wife, Lydia, a physician specializing in childhood kidney diseases, has devoted herself to his muse. The only blip is her pregnancy at age 39. To North, "children have little place in a writer's life. My books were my real children, and my first duty was to them." Their daughter, however, is stillborn. Despite this paradoxical tragedy and the inconvenience of Lydia's job-related absences, their marriage hums along like a well-oiled machine, thanks to Lydia, who indulges him with the legendary selfless devotion of wives nurturing genius. Until his obsession with Lea turns the faithful husband into "a man preparing to commit murder . . . murder of my adored Lydia." The affair that follows spirals out of control in a series of progressively rough sexual encounters. These are described with a frankness not found in the novels of Henry James, to whom Begley has often been compared.

Enriching the larger themes of lust, adultery and self-destruction, more discreet Jamesian lagniappes abound. Like his mentor, Begley peels away the subtle trappings of class: "The Vineyard people we know fall into two categories: New Englanders I have known since childhood, and our New York friends who happen to have houses there," North observes. Neither North's own aged, demented WASP parents rattling around their Georgetown Federal house nor his intimidating in-laws, the Jewish liberal philanthropic Franks, escape his flinty eye. Lea herself could be James's Daisy Miller in reverse: "Like certain other fine products of France, [Lea's] charm didn't travel well." Throughout, he offers up a sybarite's tour of the watering places of the rich and famous -- East Hampton, Paris, Martha's Vineyard, a Greek island, Hollywood. Even more, he provides enough insights into the technique of writing to send any teacher, writer, or wannabe jumping for a highlighter. North knows art and literature, wine, food and clothes. "Sleeves that are too short are somewhere near the top of my list of betes noires," he complains. Against a finely rendered landscape of privilege, a marriage disintegrates and a talent goes to waste.

Mameve Medwed's third novel, "The End of an Error," was published in June.North's arrogance is such that he imagines he can get away with conducting a life on two plains, with two women. But as he's tugged in opposite directions, the center cannot hold. "Lying by Lea's side . . . it seems to me that I had been split in two. One half was Lydia's husband, whose limitless and unreserved love for her was like a vital organ of his body. The other was an unserious man, besotted by this girl's body and what she was willing to do with it . . . Why couldn't these men coexist . . . so long as I kept them apart, so long as Lydia never found out about Lea, and I succeeded in shielding Lydia even from the abstract humiliation of having others know?"Can he keep them apart? And how? Humiliations and embarrassments of a particularly upper-class sensibility multiply. Among other slights to North's sense of comme il faut is Lea's public practice of using "the familiar tu in every sentence although I pointedly used the neutral vous." As the story unwinds, twisting and curling back on itself like a double helix, events build toward a compelling conclusion. In this dazzling novel of manners, class, sex, and art, the ending is a tour de force that contains, like the creative process itself, mystery and ambiguity.
