A READING LIFE

High stakes, hard luck, and hungry hearts

By Caroline Leavitt, Globe Correspondent, 8/17/2003

Mention gamblers, and I think of sleazy, beefy guys in polyester shirts or sleek high rollers in Armani. Not people I'd necessarily want to know personally, but I wouldn't mind getting a "you-are-there" sort of glimpse into their lives. So this month, I picked up two books about the wagering life, and as with most gambles, I didn't get what I expected. "Games brought us together," Katy Lederer says about her fractured family in her wonderful memoir, "Poker Face: A Girlhood Among Gamblers" (Crown, $23.95). New England semi-respectable and Salingeresque dysfunctional, the Lederer family is schooled in the art of gamesmanship, be it her teacher father's innovative wordplay, her alcoholic mother's excellence at crossword puzzles, or the kids' skill in pilfering her purse and watering down her liquor. Intelligence is prized in this clan, but what matters most is winning at games. And win they do.

Howard, the eldest child, is slated for Columbia University, but his love of cards catapults him into the high-stakes world of sports betting. Sister Annie becomes a poker champ. After years of ferociously battling about money, the parents separate, the father to become the celebrated author of a series of irreverent books about language, and the mother to keep her kids' accounts.

Only Lederer doesn't seem to have a talent to grab on to, so she follows her siblings' money-laden trail to Vegas and becomes Howard's apprentice. But she's not after the cash as much as she longs to be closer to her family, and poker seems the sure bet. Alas, though, she's only an acceptable player, and she soon discovers that prose can provide her with the kind of wins poker can't. She gets into the famed Iowa Writers' Workshop, and instead of playing cards, she begins to write about them.

Lederer, though, never truly captures the essence of what it's like to be a gambler or why someone might risk all to stay in that world. Part of the problem -- a fact that later becomes her salvation -- is that she isn't really a gambler at all. She doesn't have the temperament, and she admits she can't really empathize with any of the players, which creates a distance when she writes about them. None of the gamblers really comes alive here, and even her sister, Annie, seems to disappear from the narrative.

Still, Lederer is abundantly talented. She writes movingly about her family, and her yearning for acceptance is palpable, making "Poker Face" compulsively readable, a book not so much about gambling, but about belonging and family. And as such, it's wise, rueful, and undeniably on the money.

Unlike Lederer, Bruce McNall was indeed a gambler. One of the most high-rolling players of all time, he was a millionaire coin collector with stakes in horses, hockey teams (he owned the Los Angeles Kings), and movies ("The Fabulous Baker Boys") who lost it all when he was sent to prison for fraud. "Fun While It Lasted" (Hyperion, $24.95, co-written with Michael D'Antonio) is his heady rise-and-fall-and-redemption account.

It's great fun to read how McNall parlays his boyhood love of coins into a world-class business, encouraging the most jaded Hollywood celebrities to fall under the coins' spell -- and his. But then he branches out, and the fun gives way to something more serious. He begins to play fast and loose with other people's money, trading in horses and movies, leading an opulent lifestyle that carries a kind of fame all its own. Finally, in the 1990s, he becomes so overextended that his debts begin to mount. Scheming, he defrauds where he can, using the same racehorse for multiple loans, keeping nine different sets of books, until his carefully constructed house of cards begins to tumble. Inevitably, he's caught, and sent to prison for five years for bilking investors out of $236 million.

So what happened here with his life -- and why? Like Lederer, McNall gambles because of family dynamics. The child of an emotionally distant father, and prone to portliness, McNall wants love more than money, and he's certain that's just what money can buy. He's determined to be one of the rare circle of speculators who he claims have ambition, intelligence, and personality. And over and over again, he reminds us that he made it to that circle. He keeps spotlighting his accomplishments, lavishly dropping celebrity names (Wayne Gretzky, Michael Eisner, Ronald and Nancy Reagan) like sprinkles on ice cream.

Even though McNall did go to jail, he still wants us to find him likable. Maybe he smuggled rare coins, but he insists all rare coins have been illegally acquired, whether people know it or not. Maybe his terrible frauds made headlines, but he claims they were the kind of press that got people cheering, "that brightened the aura" around him. Organizations were what he defrauded, never people. When he's in court, what sustains him is that hundreds of people come forward to vouch for him, even those who lost money. Goldie Hawn and Kurt Russell even try to visit him in prison. "I was still loved," he proclaims.

McNall's story is as fascinating and poignant as Lederer's. These two books are insider tales, not of the rough-and-tumble world of gambling as much as the yearning need to belong. Who ever thought that gambling would be the answer to the question "What did I do for love?"?

Caroline Leavitt's novel "Coming Back to Me" will be issued in paperback this September. Her new novel, "Girls in Trouble," will be published in January. She can be reached at www.carolineleavitt.com.
