Women on the verge

Moon Tide revolves around a matriarch, her granddaughter, and her housekeeper as a time of crisis approaches

By Sandra Shea, Globe Correspondent, 8/17/2003

Moon Tide, By Dawn Clifton Tripp. Random House, 285 pp., $24.95

ADVERTISEMENT

The hurricane that laid waste to southern New England and Long Island in 1938 was especially devastating not just because of its strength, but because no one saw it coming. Meteorologists expected the tropical storm that began off the coast of Africa to veer out to sea once it reached Cape Hatteras, N.C.; instead, it gathered speed and force and slammed into the Northeast while the weathermen were still forecasting a rainstorm. The storm's death toll of 600 surely would have been smaller had the population been better prepared.

By the time that same hurricane arrives in the novel "Moon Tide," we are all too prepared. Dawn Clifton Tripp begins her story 20 years before the storm, and the coming disaster hovers like a dark promise over all the smaller squalls, both natural and human, that precede it.

"Moon Tide," set in Westport, charts the stories of five principal characters. It is 1918, and elderly matriarch Elizabeth Lowe presides over Skirdagh, the big house she named after the Irish town she left as a girl. Her young granddaughter Eve, a summer visitor to Skirdagh, has been shattered by the recent death of her mother. Maggie, a recent arrival to town with gypsy-like powers, lives in Elizabeth's root cellar, where she concocts herbal potions and tends to the old woman, as well as to the house, its gardens, and a few of the local men. One of those men, Wes Wilkes, often confuses passion and violence; his younger brother, Jake, is a gentler soul who is captivated by Eve.

Though presented in alternating chapters that interweave these characters' stories, Elizabeth is the true eye of their storms. She is in decline, but she doesn't miss much of what's going on around her. She sees how unhinged the grieving Eve is becoming; she sees that Jake, to whom she has opened her library, is infatuated with Eve; she sees Maggie and Wes circle around each other. She filters these observations through her own lens of memory, her history of loss and regret.

Tripp is adept at illuminating how age shreds the fabric of both memory and consciousness: "[Elizabeth] knows that her mind has grown sloped, that everything passing into it rolls on a slight downhill, cannot find grip, and is lost." And Tripp cannily uses Elizabeth to help explore the class dynamic particular to summer towns in New England, of those who own the big houses, those who are hired to work for them, and those interlopers who come into town to exploit and develop it. "Moon Tide" traces the path of destruction (and creation) wrought when all these elements converge.

The convergence is, alas, often too slowly paced. We are to understand that Jake is focused on the inscrutable Eve for 20 years, based on little more than a glimpse and a single encounter. And although Wes and Maggie's relationship of connection, estrangement, and reconnection has violence at its core, it seems strangely muted.

It's the hurricane, so far off in the distance, that gives the story pace and tension, infusing the novel's atmosphere like the musty yellow light that will fill the sky on the September morning of the storm. In fact, the last third of the novel, its strongest, is taken up with the account of the hurricane, and Tripp writes wonderfully of the characters' dawning awareness of the storm's magnitude.

In Tripp's hands, the storm becomes a complex piece of music that builds note by note, swelling to its deadly crescendo. Her description is both sensual and visceral: "She can feel the pierced shrieking of the wind, the squall of the rain, the deep bass distant howl of the sea, and then among them, on a middling pitch, the steady moan of the storm itself, long and sonorous, like the groan of an old and heavy door drawn slowly open."

There are many such moments of poetic clarity throughout; Tripp has crafted each passage carefully, with an ear for lyricism. But not all of these moments succeed. Often, the images are not so much poetic as peculiar. A sampling: "Elizabeth fell in love with unanswered boulders lying in the middle of a field." "Maggie buys her hens from Ben, and he tells her that the sky is a table and when the clouds pass low over the earth, they are hungry and will take a woman in her sleep." "His body holds the smell of the red weed, this man who was a riverbed once." "She meets him now in the form of a stranger, with glib words and a distance that tastes like grass."

What are unanswered boulders? How was a man once a riverbed? And exactly how does distance taste like grass? False and mannered notes like these muddy the atmosphere that Tripp has worked hard to create and, at worst, call to mind a recent criticism leveled at literary fiction. In "A Reader's Manifesto," a 2001 Atlantic Monthly essay, B. R. Myers took to task the excesses and opacity of much of literary fiction. He claimed that "it has become fashionable . . . to exploit the license of poetry while claiming exemption from poetry's rigorous standards of precision and polish."

Ultimately, poetry should illuminate; Tripp's language too often obscures. A little more precision would have better served this book, and helped distinguish its characters. As it is, the characters all seem to share the same intuitive powers for tasting things like grief, for hearing each other's memories, or seeing into each other's dreams. Yet they spend the novel rarely communicating.

But this is a fine first effort, impressively researched and often hypnotic. It's not a beach book, but a perfect book for late-summer reading, when the sunny promise of June has passed, and the darkening skies of September begin to make their presence known.

Sandra Shea is an editorial writer for the Philadelphia Daily News and author of "The Realm of Secondhand Souls."
