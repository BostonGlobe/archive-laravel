An exuberant life of Cagliostro, the count of many callings

By Matthew Price, Globe Correspondent, 8/17/2003

The Last Alchemist: Count Cagliostro, Master of Magic in the Age of Reason, by Iain McCalman. HarperCollins, 272 pp., illustrated, $25.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Freemason and mystical magician, rascal and raconteur, Count Alessandro di Cagliostro was one of the most extravagant, eccentric characters of the 18th century. Cagliostro's celebrated exploits took him from the back alleys of Palermo, Italy, to the ruling circles of St. Petersburg and revolutionary Paris, where he dazzled the brightest men and women of the age with his potent psychic powers. Still, he was a divisive figure, and his long list of powerful enemies included Casanova, Goethe, Marie Antoinette, Pope Pius VI, and Catherine the Great.

Since his death, there has been little consensus about Cagliostro. Was he a charlatan or misunderstood genius? In a blustery assessment of Cagliostro's career, Scottish man of letters Thomas Carlyle concluded he was nothing but a fraud. On the other hand, Marxist cultural critic Walter Benjamin saw him as a symbol of "the repressed magical origins of science,"

as Iain McCalman notes in his spirited new biography of Cagliostro. "The Last Alchemist" is an exuberantly picaresque account of the count's life, which, unfortunately, reads like an overheated boys' adventure story; Cagliostro's excesses have inspired a fit of rhetorical overkill in his biographer, a professor at the Australian National University. McCalman writes as a devotee of the count,

not as a debunker, even though much of the count's outlandish trickery cries out for skeptical illumination. (To be fair, the author concedes Cagliostro was a knave, but he argues that he was also a "great occult healer.") McCalman is decidedly reticent about the nature of Cagliostro's powers. Often we are left wondering: How did he do that? The count's origins were hardly aristocratic: He was born Giuseppe Balsamo in 1743 in the slums of Palermo. Thanks to a relative's financial intervention, the bright and curious Balsamo went to seminary school, and then briefly trained as an apothecary under the friars of Fatebenefratelli healing order, where he honed his alchemical skills, and dabbled in the darker arts of black magic. The young Balsamo was an unruly fellow, preferring thievery to theology. After a bungled 1764 robbery, Balsamo made his way to Malta the following year. He found work there as a servant to the Knights of Malta, who provided him with credentials, which Balsamo exploited to the hilt when he returned to Italy a few years later. Apprenticed to a Roman master criminal, he added forgery and fraud to his repertoire.

By the early 1770s, Balsamo was passing himself off as Joseph Pellegrini, a Spanish colonel and freelance diplomat. An ever restless man on the make, he toured France with his nubile young wife, colliding with Casanova, who thought him a world-class phony. A crucial trip to London in 1776 proved decisive. There, he joined a Masonic lodge, and found his calling. "Masonry became the crucible of his genius," contends McCalman. "The artist, actor, and choreographer in him thrilled to the Masonic taste for pageant and theater."

Entry into the mysterious world of Freemasonry gave Balsamo, now calling himself "Count Cagliostro," access to a network of Masonic circles across Europe. McCalman rightly notes that many educated noblemen and middle-class merchants saw Masonry as an appealing alternative to the rigorous austerity of the Enlightenment's cult of reason, which little satisfied the spiritually needy. Needless to say, the Catholic Church saw Masonry as a distinct threat to the social order, not to mention outright heresy.

Crisscrossing Europe to deliver the liberating message of his mystically infused Masonry, Cagliostro huddled with Masonic noblemen of the Russian court in early 1779, arousing the attention of Catherine the Great, who loathed the Masons. With Catherine attempting to banish him, the count took his case to the people, setting up as populist healer in the streets of St. Petersburg.

Word of his powers spread across the continent, and "Cagliostromania" soon took hold in Paris. But the count found himself embroiled in a scheme to steal a diamond necklace from Marie Antoinette. Though he managed to escape trial, the game was soon up -- on a trip back to Italy in 1789, his wife betrayed him to the Catholic Inquisition, which quickly locked up the troublemaker in a mountain fortress, where he raved and raged until his death in 1795.

What is the legacy of Cagliostro's quackery? McCalman argues it is everywhere. Alas, he is right. Just take cable TV, the gaudy arena for the psychics of our electronic era; I bet the count would be quite pleased with "Crossing Over With John Edward."

Matthew Price is a freelance writer in Brooklyn, N.Y.
