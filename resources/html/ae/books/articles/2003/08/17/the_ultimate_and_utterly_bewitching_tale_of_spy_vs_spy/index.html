WORTH READING

The ultimate, and utterly bewitching tale of spy vs. spy

By Sam Allis, Globe Staff, 8/17/2003

Tinker, Tailor, Soldier, Spy, by John le Carre. Pocket, 379 pp., paperback, $14

ADVERTISEMENT

I got a lump in my throat when I ran into them a couple of weeks ago - Jim Prideaux, Peter Guillam, Connie Sachs, Toby Esterhase, Bill Haydon, Karla, Ann, and, of course, the master spy Ann cuckolded, George Smiley. I hadn't heard from them in years, and it took but a second to realize how much I'd missed them. Old friends.

And when I found them again, of course, I traveled back to our first meeting, in 1974, when John le Carre's ``Tinker, Tailor, Soldier, Spy'' burst onto the scene. I infuriated a woman by choosing to finish the book in lieu of her company one evening in Bermuda.

I did it again recently. (Not in Bermuda.) I couldn't help it. I was rereading the definitive spy story of our time. She observed, ``I really didn't exist while you were reading it.'' I said, ``No, you really didn't.''

If you're going to read only one spy book in your life, this is it. ``Tinker, Tailor'' is le Carre's big masterpiece (``The Spy Who Came In From the Cold'' is his small one). He will be remembered, at his acme, as the purveyor of Cold War truths, and this is the tale of the Cold War. Neither he nor anyone else has written anything better in the genre since.

``Tinker, Tailor'' is based on the penetration at the highest reaches of British intelligence by Kim Philby, one of a band of well-born Cambridge undergraduates who were recruited by the Russians during the '30s, when the elite flirted with Communism. These men never had money as a motive, unlike grubby American traitors such as Aldrich Ames. A committed Marxist, Philby believed to the end that the British class system - and Western society in general - were odious and indefensible. He died, in Moscow, a general in the KGB.

Philby did inestimable damage to British and American national security until he disappeared from a Beirut dinner party in 1963, one step ahead of Brit counterintelligence, which had been suspicious for years. The Americans had been too. James Jesus Angleton, Washington's obsessive counterintelligence chief, had worked with him before his uncovering and spent the rest of his career on a ruinous search for a mole in our midst that rivaled Captain Queeg's strawberry hunt in ``The Caine Mutiny.''

The book spawned words that are now in our spy lexicon - ``lamplighters'' and ``scalp-hunters.'' Its two poles of espionage were the British ``Circus'' and ``Moscow Centre,'' run by Smiley's ascetic nemesis, Karla. Many credit le Carre for coining the word ``mole.'' But in an introduction he wrote in 1991, the author refers to a letter from a reader, who cites page 240 of Francis Bacon's 17th-century ``Historie of the Reigne of King Henry the Seventh'' for first usage: ``Hee had such Moles perpectually working and casting to undermine him.''

``Tinker, Tailor'' is a seamless tale of a great comeback. Smiley is brought out of forced retirement to find a lethal mole who has wrecked the British Secret Service (and his career). We come upon Smiley in the London rain this way: ``Small, podgy, and at best middle-aged, he was by appearance one of London's meek who do not inherit the earth. His legs were short, his gait anything but agile, his dress costly, ill-fitting, and extremely wet.'' Alec Guinness tattooed this man indelibly into our psyches in the memorable BBC series based on the book.

The story carries no fat, and it moves like a freight train from the opening page to its denouement 379 pages later (a nice length compared with the doorstops we face today). There is no decent reason to put it down. I read it in one sitting with a break for dinner.

The writing is simply marvelous - never better than in the scene between Smiley and Connie Sachs, the brilliant, ditsy researcher with the encyclopedic memory who was canned with Smiley and drinks too much sherry alone in her Oxford flat. She never married; her life and career had been her ``lovely, lovely boys'' at the Circus.

He comes to her for information one rainy night, and she begins her offering, memorably, with ``Once upon a time there was a defector called Stanley ...'' She deplores the way the Russians execute spies. ``He was shot, poor lamb,'' she says about a British spy. ``They never hang, do they: too impatient, the little horrors.''

And it is Connie who provides the epitaph for the British ruling class and its hold on the Circus. Listen to Connie, in her cups now, pathetic, heroic:

``Poor loves. Trained to Empire, trained to rule the waves. All gone. All taken away. Bye-bye, world. You're the last, George, you and Bill. And filthy Percy a bit. ... I want to remember you all as you were. Lovely, lovely boys.''

Veterans: Reread this book. To paraphrase the corn flakes ad, taste it again for the first time. As for the neophyte: Get thee to a bookstore. You can all thank me later.

Sam Allis is a member of the Globe staff.
