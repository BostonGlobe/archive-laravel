A heroine with a nose for the authentic

Sharp-eyed Evelyn a compelling narrator

By Mameve Medwed, 8/24/2003

The Center of Everything, by Laura Moriarty. Hyperion, 291 pp., $22.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Growing up is hard to do. What's harder still is for a novelist to put a fresh twist on yet another coming-of-age chronicle. Laura Moriarty works such magic with "The Center of Everything." Never mind that legions of her literary antecedents have limned wise children on the cusp of adulthood often enough to risk reader backlash. Been there, done that, you might protest, pointing out Carson McCullers's Frankie, Harper Lee's Scout, Salinger's Holden. But wait. Amid such heavy hitters, Evelyn Bucknow, the 10-year-old narrator of Laura Moriarty's enchanting debut novel, more than holds her own. In her struggles to make sense of nonsense, to mark the disconnects between words and deeds, to parse friendships, family, sorrow, and the human heart, Evelyn shows how one tiny backyard can stand in for the whole world.

And it's a small world after all -- particularly for a child stuck in a hicksville Kansas town that, on a map, lies smack dab in the center of the United States. Living with her never-married mother in a shabby apartment surrounded by confusing but colorful grown-ups, she tries to sort things out. When her evangelical grandmother threatens fire and brimstone, Evelyn's innate skepticism doesn't keep her from agonizing over dying a doubter. Nevertheless, she's much more concerned about her drooping eyes. "Brad Browning says I look like the plastic basset hounds at grocery stores that you can put quarters in to give money to the Humane Society. There is a chance that I could grow up to be ugly, and this is one of many things I worry about."

In addition to her soul and her looks, there's a lot more for Evelyn to worry about. Her mother, Tina, for example, a child herself, irresponsible, given to the vapors, always on the brink of insolvency. Looking for love in all the wrong places, Tina's having an affair with her boss at Peterson's Pet Food who gives them his cast-off Volkswagen. The car, which keeps breaking down, "is okay from the outside, but on the inside, it's no good," notes Evelyn. Her mother's boss "likes to do tricks for me, like pretending he can pull his thumb off with one hand, then put it back on. I am too old for this. I know his thumb is really tucked behind his hand, that things can look one way and be another, depending upon where you're standing."

If things are never what they seem, they are also out of their natural order. Evelyn mothers her mother; it's the daughter who's smarter, reads faster, frets over where the money is going to come from, how to get the car fixed. She's wary, on alert. "Anything could happen now. The Earth could spin out of its orbit and crash into the moon." Despite the looming apocalypse, however, the smaller worries cause the most pain. Fathers leave. Tina's boss takes off when he finds out she's pregnant. Boyfriends fall in love with other girls. Not to mention high school hell. Evelyn's nemesis, Traci Carmichael, pretty, popular, smart, and rich, lives in a house with a porch swing and "twelve different windows on just the front side." While Traci pays for her own lunch, Evelyn is so poor her peanut butter sandwiches are humiliatingly free. What wouldn't Evelyn give for Traci's three sweatshirts with palm trees on them, her red Keds, and her gold necklace? But if Traci represents all that's unfair in the world, there are compensations: a science prize, a comeuppance beyond imagining, and, especially, the appealing bad boy, Travis Rowley, "thief, breaker of locks, my own dark avenger and first true love." Besides, what's high school without that rescuer of trapped children with potential -- a great teacher? In this case, two: Mr. Goldman, a young Jewish man from New York City, and the spinster Ms. Jenkins, who "reads magazines that don't have any pictures" while she eats and dares to teach evolution in the Bible Belt of creationism.

Though such people populated our own high school dreams and nightmares, here they never seem caricatures. Neither are they so familiar we're not eager to revisit those terrible years just for the sheer pleasure of seeing them through Evelyn's eyes. And what flinty eyes they are. At every turn, you can count on Evelyn's impeccable instinct for the authentic; she reads Anne Frank's diary and comments: "You know the whole time she's going to die. If it weren't a real diary, but just a story that someone made up, she would have lived, and maybe married Peter. But since it's a true story, anything can happen, even the worst."

The worst does happen. A death. The birth of a retarded brother. Tina's despair. Unrequited love. Not that we'd expect less, for when does the course of adolescent love ever run smooth? That Travis pines for dumb but beautiful Deena is an injustice that breaks Evelyn's heart yet helps her college plans. "Everything will be okay," Tina tells her daughter. "Everything will not be okay, I know," Evelyn replies. To grow up means to grow away from Mom. "I've drawn a line between us, the difference between her and me. It's like one of the black lines between states on maps, lines between different countries on the globe. They don't really exist. . . . But everyone knows where they are, and they are important, keeping one state separate from the other, so you can always tell which one you're in."

When, at last, Evelyn realizes where she is, she's ready to leave. Reluctant to let her go, we can't help but cheer her on. By the end of this beautifully evocative novel, we realize the paradox: Nothing and everything happens here. Evelyn's voice illuminates the most mundane observations, turning the ordinary extraordinary. As the child nears adulthood, her intelligence so widens and transcends her horizon that the reader, like Evelyn Bucknow herself, is not in Kansas anymore.

Mameve Medwed's third novel, "The End of an Error," was published in June.
