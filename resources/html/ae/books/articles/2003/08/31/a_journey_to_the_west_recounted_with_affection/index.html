A journey to the west, recounted with affection

By Thrity Umrigar, Globe Correspondent, 8/31/2003

Brick Lane, by Monica Ali. Scribner, 369 pp., $25

ADVERTISEMENT

Monica Ali's "Brick Lane" is as crisp and urgent as a headline, and yet the novel tells the story of the lives of a marginalized people who don't often make the news. Set in a working-class London neighborhood populated mainly by Bengali immigrants from Bangladesh, it traces the geographical journey of its heroine, Nazneen, from her homeland to England, as well as her metaphysical journey from dutiful wife to independent woman, from a victim of fate to the controller of her destiny. Along the way, Ali gives us a vivid picture of what it means to be a Muslim living in the West in the post-Sept. 11 world.

Simply told, "Brick Lane" is the story of how an undereducated, traditional woman finds her voice and her way in modern London. Nazneen comes to London as the young bride of a much older man, Chanu. As he repeatedly reminds her, Chanu is educated and considers himself superior to the "illiterate" immigrants who populate their neighborhood. Over the years, Nazneen grows into a sharp, independent woman who increasingly sees through her pompous and ridiculous husband but also grows to appreciate his fundamental goodness and sincerity. And after a lifetime of subsuming her life to the demands of fate, she at last discovers the thrill of free will.

But this simple description fails to capture the nuances and complexity of this wonderful novel. In addition to telling Nazneen's story, "Brick Lane" is a compelling portrait of the immigrant ghetto, which imprisons its men as much as its women. Ali's intelligent, sympathetic portrayal of how even moderate Muslims become radicalized in a political climate that isolates and ghettoizes them, of an insular and isolated urban landscape where even educated young men begin to talk of jihad and government conspiracies, makes for sad, if not chilling reading. It also serves to remind us of how reductive our public debates about Islamic militancy have become in the past two years. Or perhaps novels can illuminate the subtleties of the human condition in ways that newspapers can't.

But despite its serious subject, the novel reads like a certified page-turner. Ali has the eye of a portrait painter. Her understanding of human foibles and contradictions is supreme and results in characters so realistic that one can almost hear them breathing on the page. Her portrait of Chanu, Nazneen's bombastic, idealistic husband is filled with sly humor, compassion, wit, and dead-on insight.

There is a subplot that involves Nazneen's younger sister, Hasina, who elopes with the man she loves and falls on hard times when that marriage ends. We come to know Hasina only by the letters she writes her sister in London, and perhaps it is because of this plot device that Hasina never quite comes alive, as do even secondary characters. If there is a weak link in the novel, it is Hasina's letters, which are written in a kind of pidgin English that begins to grate after a while. What is perhaps the novel's most remarkable feature is the gentle humor that underlines it. Despite their diametrically different milieus and concerns, Ali, like Jane Austen before her, observes the antics of her characters with a wry bemusement that never devolves into condescension. Thus, Chanu's friend Dr. Aziz is not so dignified that he can resist taunting Chanu by periodically asking him about his long-forgotten petition to bring a mobile library to the immigrant neighborhood. It is the kind of humor born of compassion and a helpless love for the follies of human beings.

"Brick Lane" is not a didactic novel, but it makes many trenchant observations that may help Western readers understand the alienation and isolation and poverty that attract many young Muslims living in the West to concepts like jihad and Islamic fundamentalism. In that sense, it is as topical as the day's headlines. But the true pleasure of the novel comes from its timeless sense of wonder and affection for the haplessness of human nature.

Thrity Umrigar is the author of the novel "Bombay Time" and the upcoming memoir "First Darling of the Morning." She lives in Ohio.
