ON CRIME

The lingering legacy of violence, both personal and political

By Jim Fusilli, Globe Correspondent, 8/31/2003

An act of terrorism is the specter that haunts the maddeningly real characters in Janette Turner Hospital's brilliant novel "Due Preparations for the Plague." In September 1987, a group of Iraqis with ties to the US intelligence community hijacks a plane in Paris and threatens to kill all on board.

The method the group proposes for eliminating the passengers is ingenious, and the covert manipulation of the passenger list by the terrorists' leader makes it all the more sinister. And yet this compelling premise is somehow a backdrop for an even more undeniable tale: that of the victims' family members, including some children who are released by the terrorists and, 13 years later, continue to grope to understand the seemingly inexplicable yet oddly logical events. It's disturbingly and frighteningly unclear where their desperation will lead.

At the heart of the story is Lowell, who was 16 years old when his mother was killed on the flight, and Samantha, who at 6 was among the children released by the hijackers. When Lowell's father, an American intelligence agent, dies suddenly, Samantha and Lowell form a reluctant partnership to investigate the senior Lowell's involvement in the horrific affair.

Corrupted by death and psychosis, obsession and innuendo, the pair's memories and perceptions are unreliable. And yet their suspicions may not be wrong: Through the author's deft manipulation of time, we meet the terrorist Sirocco and the mysterious Salamander, the principals of the '87 event, and thus are present at the birth of the terror plot known as Operation Black Death. We also meet several vividly drawn passengers, American and French alike, whose actions raise suspicions, whether or not they are warranted. Trust in our own perception is something we lose very early in this tale.

With terse, erudite, yet affecting prose, Hospital permits us to live through the rippling ramifications of such acts and to know that the plague for which we must prepare has been created by our brethren. With its deliberate echoes of Homer, Defoe, and Boccaccio, her novel also helps us better understand the events of our time by allowing us to share the anguish of those who died and those who are left behind.

In "A Fistful of Rain," petulant rock star Mim Bracca's bad day seems her life in microcosm: She's been kicked out of her band and, after flying home to Portland, Ore., from Australia, is kidnapped by a man who terrorizes her by forcing her to strip to the skin. He releases her almost immediately, but the act proves a harbinger: Shortly after the reappearance of her father, who's been doing time for killing their mother, the stalker raises the stakes and Mim's drug-dealing brother is murdered. Is it any wonder she goes through Jack Daniel's by the barrel?

Author Greg Rucka put aside his Atticus Kodiak series (and the Batman illustrated novels he's written) to tell Mim's tale, and he does so by pressing the action and using the Pacific Northwest to wonderful effect. As for Mim, an unsympathetic victim she may be, but she's a victim nonetheless of her mother's murder, a gruesome incident that cast her into a foster system that proved both cruel and kind.

Though his tale meanders at times, Rucka understands that the likes of Mim aren't going to be restored through a single event, no matter how monumental. Thus, as bullets fly and blood flows, we wonder not only if Mim will survive, but whether it's possible she can be saved.

Coincidence makes a story worth telling, and in Peter Abrahams's "Their Wildest Dreams," coincidence is king. Folks from near and far wind up in Agua Fria, a seedy town on the US side of the Mexican border. Compelled by their varied and almost always unsightly ambitions, they collide in a series of jarring, entertaining confrontations.

Mackie Larkin's crumbled marriage has burdened her with staggering debt. To meet her obligations, and support her teenage daughter Lianne, she decides to dance at a strip club owned by Buck Samsonov, a Russian entrepreneur who has warmed to capitalism with a zeal only a former communist could muster.

Meanwhile, Lianne has taken up with a shady cowboy who's got his criminal mind on one of Samsonov's other properties. Dropped amid this potentially explosive situation is Nick Loeb, a Brooklyn, N.Y.-based mystery novelist who's decided to research the life of Agua Fria's seemingly laconic police chief. The fumbling Nick seems the perfect spark to light this combustible mixture. "Do you believe in cosmic deals?" asks Mackie at one point. In Agua Fria, the answer better be yes.

A Cape Cod resident, the talented Abrahams builds suspense as he keeps the story moving by jumping among the flawed protagonists Mackie, Lianne, and Nick, and teasing us by forestalling the inevitable. When it finally erupts, he manages to confound us with an unlikely and thoroughly satisfying conclusion. Given his skills, that's no coincidence.

Jim Fusilli is a novelist and critic.
