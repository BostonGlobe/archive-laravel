BOOK REVIEW

'Stella Descending' stays with you long after her fall is over

By Jules Verdone, Globe Correspondent, 8/21/2003

Stella Descending, by Linn Ullmann, translation by Barbara Haveland. Knopf, 256 pp., $23

ADVERTISEMENT

Maybe you could chalk it up to the influence of "Memento," and it's a delayed reaction at that, but the mail gods recently brought two new novels told chronologically in reverse. One swiftly became tedious. The other, Norwegian writer Linn Ullmann's "Stella Descending," is practically magical in its imagery, despite its somber story.

It won't ruin anything to give away (as the title hints at) that Stella is a woman who falls from an Oslo roof, nine stories up, mere pages into the book. She was out there with her husband, Martin, and witnesses can't say whether she fell accidentally, jumped, or was pushed. Were the couple arguing? Dancing? Something else?

We back up (though the sequence zigzags a bit), hearing from a number of narrators, among them an elderly friend and former patient of Stella's, a police investigator, three witnesses, Stella's 15-year-old daughter, her husband, and ultimately the title character. Ullmann gives each a pronounced voice and uses some imaginative devices to good effect: The prologue is a transcript from a videotape Martin and Stella made of themselves the night of her death, and a few of her daughter Amanda's chapters read as if they've been lifted from her diary.

Ullman's writing is sparse but not dry, sober without being humorless. Some of the characters are characters, especially Axel Gruff, probably in his 80s and a crank, one who sees himself clearly, aside from the nature of his feelings toward Stella. "I was a small man," he states. "I was a pathetic man. I was the kind of man other men would have spat on. . . . But I was not a bad man." His descriptions of his ailments and his quotidian routine are understated but lively. His insistence that he's not afraid of death and that he would have gladly died in Stella's place are endearing.

The image of Stella falling, endlessly falling, repeats throughout the book. Ullmann uses it as an almost comforting distraction from any picture we might conjure of the woman actually hitting the ground. In fact, Amanda tries to reassure her young half sister, the quiet, owl-eyed Bee, that their mother is still in midair, that she is somewhere in the world, still descending. Though Amanda doesn't delude herself, Ullmann points to the girl's earlier fascination with games of Nintendo: "Mamma, there's this beast in the forest that I have to kill," Stella recalls her saying, "and then the princess'll fall down into the next world." It's a metaphor that could ring as heavy-handed but somehow doesn't.

Stella's no angel, nor is she a villain. She's a loving and flawed mother -- even neglectful at times. She's a sometimes hypocritical friend, and she's confused to the point of near spinelessness in her relationship with Martin. And though there's doubt as to whether he's a murderer, his conversations with the crusty investigator Corinne make it obvious that he's a louse, even if he's not completely unsympathetic. What Ullmann sketches is the portrait of a screwed-up family, maybe not wildly dysfunctional or damaged, but probably on the way there, especially after Stella is gone.

But the author may have misstepped slightly by introducing Stella as a narrator more than halfway through. Her perspective shines some light on earlier puzzles, but oddly, the novel's tone becomes less dreamy, even though we're hearing from a woman who's been dead for 140-odd pages. Still, some of her passages are memorable: "Is it right that the dog's nose is warm and dry? Is it right that Bee never cries? . . . Is it right that Amanda has to fall from world to world? Is it right that this house is so quiet? . . . Is it right that I have a mother who would rather be a tree? . . . Is it right that Martin and I never sleep?"

The extraordinary thing Ullmann does in "Stella Descending," her second novel, is create a sense of spaciousness, the plot's dark premise notwithstanding. Her story is almost cinematic in its visual strength, and it's easy to imagine it adapted as a screenplay. (That thought surfaced before it was evident to me that the author is the daughter of Liv Ullmann and Ingmar Bergman. Honest.) The picture of Stella falling, continuously falling, ever falling as the novel folds and unfolds, is one that won't go away anytime soon. As one witness narrates, "Over and over again it happens -- [Stella and Martin] embrace and she falls -- for this is a moment that is bound to be relived time and time again, and there is nothing I can do to prevent it."

Stella Descending
By Linn Ullmann
Translated from the Norwegian by Barbara Haveland
Knopf, 256 pp., $23
