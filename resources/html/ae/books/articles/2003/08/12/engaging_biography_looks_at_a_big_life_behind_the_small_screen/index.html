BOOK REVIEW

Engaging biography looks at a big life behind the small screen

By Renee Graham, Globe Staff, 8/12/2003

Ball of Fire: The Tumultuous Life and Comic Art of Lucille Ball, By Stefan Kramer. Knopf, 361 pp., illustrated, $25.95

ADVERTISEMENT

They didn't always love Lucy.

Critics and audiences liked Lucille Ball enough as she rose from glamour girl to promising starlet to second-lead scene-stealer in dozens of films in the 1930s and 1940s. But they didn't love her in that sublime way that transforms

BookReviewa working actress into a screen goddess. So, by the early 1950s, there was little reason for a 40-year-old B-movie actress facing a slow fade in throwaway films not to turn her talents from big screen to small. At a time when only 8 million Americans had TV sets, she decided to star with her husband in a new series about the comic foibles of a Cuban bandleader and his scatterbrained wife.

As television finally came to define Ball, so, too, would Ball come to define television. She was one of its earliest stars, and more than 50 years after "I Love Lucy" premiered in October 1951, she remains one of its greatest and most enduring icons. With the series still airing on classic-TV cable channels, many know the episodes as well as their own family snapshots. Still, there was more to this complex, driven woman than the manic eyes, dyed red hair (which, even in black and white, somehow seemed as coppery as a new penny), and the slurred brilliance of "Vita-meata-vegemin."

Especially since her death in 1989, so many books have been written about Ball that there would seem to be little need for yet another treatise on the legendary comedienne. Most probably know about her stormy marriage to Desi Arnaz, her sometimes indifferent parenting of her two children, and the hellish behavior behind the scenes of her various TV shows.

Still, when a book as engaging and immensely readable as Stefan Kanfer's "Ball of Fire" comes along, it hardly matters if we've heard it all before. Kanfer makes use of previous books on Ball, Arnaz, and "I Love Lucy," as well as the two performers' autobiographies. But this isn't a hasty cut-and-paste job. Kanfer brings sharp, even moving insights into Ball's "tumultuous life and comic art," as the book's subtitle advertises, without lapsing into sycophantic hyperbole.

A large part of this book's appeal is Kanfer's ability to make Ball's early years -- her birth in Jamestown, N. Y., in 1911, her itinerant childhood following the typhoid-fever death of her beloved father shortly before her fourth birthday, and the solace she found in the fantasy of clothespin dolls -- as compelling as the "I Love Lucy" years that dominate more than a third of the book.

He captures Ball's feverish desire to succeed in Hollywood; she was even among the many actresses who auditioned to play Scarlett O'Hara in "Gone With the Wind." Her ambition was an asset as an ingenue, but it became a detriment when her need to be publicly adored overwhelmed her ability to be kind to those in her personal life.

One person who pierced Ball's armor was Arnaz, a performer nearly six years her junior, whom she met in 1939. The attraction was instant, and a year later they married and dubbed their first home "Desilu," later the name of their production company. But their union was rocky from day one. Arnaz was a notorious womanizer, and work kept them apart for prolonged stretches. After less than four years, Ball filed for divorce. Though it was granted, it was immediately null and void because Ball and Arnaz slept together right after the court hearing, and according to California law at that time, during the one-year waiting period before the final decree, the involved parties could not have sex.

After "I Love Lucy" completed its run, the marriage unraveled for good in 1960, marking both a personal and professional change for Ball. She would become one of the first women to run a major Hollywood studio as president of Desilu Productions, and she would have other TV hits with "The Lucy Show" and "Here's Lucy." (Her lowest professional moment came in 1986 with the ill-conceived "Life with Lucy," which ABC canceled after only eight episodes.) Work always sustained her in ways her personal life could not, even after she married mild-mannered former comedian Gary Morton. There remained a sadness to Ball's life that all her success could not shake.

But then Ball isn't the only American institution with a messy back story. It is to Kanfer's credit that at the end of this elegant, entertaining book, we still love Lucy.

"She was funny as a girl, funnier as a young stage actress and as a Hollywood starlet. She was professionally hilarious in films, yet never achieved iconic status until she was reduced in size," Kanfer writes. "As a sixty-foot image on a screen, the actress was only a journeywoman performer; as a sixteen-inch TV image, she turned into a superstar."
