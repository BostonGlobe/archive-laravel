BOOK REVIEW

Engaging terrorists in captivating conversation

By Scott Bernard Nelson, Globe Staff, 8/27/2003

Terror in the Name of God: Why Religious Militants Kill, By Jessica Stern. Ecco/HarperCollins, 368 pp., $27.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Death comes quietly for some, gently rapping at the chamber door. But Harvard University professor Jessica Stern was convinced that mortality had come pounding loudly, outside her Pakistani hotel room at 2 a.m. during a recent trip as she tried to arrange interviews with leaders of a militant Islamic group. Rather than an assassin, Stern learned after calling hotel security in a panic, the knock at the door was a man returning her freshly pressed laundry.

Such is life if you follow dangerous people around the globe and ask them to sit for coffee and frank conversation. Especially if you happen to be an American Jewish woman who has worked for the US National Security Council and the Council on Foreign Relations. " `As a result of their inspection, they have determined that you work for the CIA,' my guide informs me, seemingly bemused," Stern writes of a discussion with her Pakistani translator on the night of the laundry scare. No matter what she tells him, the translator probably believes the same thing. "How can you persuade someone you're not working for the CIA?" she asks.

Being a poster child for the nonadministration point of view of postwar policy in Iraq isn't a bad place to start. If Stern was ever a functionary for the Central Intelligence Agency, which seems unlikely, she's off the reservation now. She has become a fixture over the past three months on National Public Radio and cable television criticizing the US-led government there as it continues to flounder. She penned a New York Times editorial Aug. 21 arguing that the average Iraqi feels he or she has more to gain from Al Qaeda these days than from the US military.

In fact, Stern's second book, "Terror in the Name of God," is everything conservatives hate when it comes to discussions about terrorism. She has long, rambling conversations with religious militants, trying to understand what makes them tick and what they have in common across religious and national boundaries. It's the kind of Freudian look at bad guys and their mothers that's sure to send Bill O'Reilly and Rush Limbaugh into apoplectic fits.

It's also one of the most interesting books you'll ever read on terrorists and terrorism. For more than four years, Stern pigeonholed a series of unsavory characters, from convicted Jewish terrorist Yoel Lerner to antiabortion crusader and doctor killer Paul Hill to Fazlur Rahman Khalil, leader of the group suspected of killing Wall Street Journal reporter Daniel Pearl, to Al Qaeda foot soldier Khalfan Khamis Mohamed, who was convicted of helping to plot an embassy bombing in which 224 people died.

Along the way, Stern opens an incredibly intriguing window into the minds of those who use God to justify violence against others. One caveat, she acknowledges up front, is that from these interviews it remains unclear who was using whom.

"Here is the unspoken bargain between us," Stern writes of one face-to-face meeting, although it could have applied equally to all. "I make myself vulnerable and they will not harm me. I must strive not to reveal fear, and to trust that they won't hurt me, despite their machismo and manufactured rage. And they, in turn, will consider telling me the truth, but only half-truths. That is our bargain."

It's a bargain that benefits readers, whatever its limitations. Stern is at her best when she quotes these violent individuals at length, as they rationalize their actions and explain their beliefs. The interviews humanize men who are more commonly demonized or lionized (depending upon where you're sitting) and add layers of understanding to the discussion of how to stop others like them.

Stern is less compelling when drawing broad conclusions and making policy recommendations. At that point, "Terror in the Name of God" devolves into a standard text on terrorism and foreign policy, subjects Stern teaches at Harvard's John F. Kennedy School of Government. The analysis is fine, but it doesn't differentiate this work from any number of others.

The book can also be annoyingly academic when it comes to structure, with an introduction to each chapter describing what's ahead and a conclusion summarizing the learning points. Making matters worse, the book is divided into two sections -- one on grievances that give rise to terrorism and one on types of holy war organizations -- that come off as too pat, too standard to fit the array of voices the dozens of terrorists represent.

All of that said, Stern has an original take on an issue likely to be the most critical one faced by America for decades to come. With apologies to O'Reilly and Limbaugh, anybody interested in foreign policy and current events should read this book sooner rather than later. The interviews alone are worth the price of admission.
