BOOK REVIEW

'Confronting' analyzes the politics of the '60s draft resisters

By Michael Kenney, Globe Correspondent, 8/19/2003

Confronting the War Machine: Draft Resistance During the Vietnam War, by Michael S. Foley. University of North Carolina, 449 pp., illustrated, $49.95, paperback $19.95

ADVERTISEMENT

On March 31, 1966, four young men burned their draft cards on the steps of the South Boston District Courthouse. Though they were rushed by members of a hostile crowd, they were pulled to safety inside the courthouse.

While this was not the first action to protest the Vietnam War, its particular focus -- the draft -- shaped the antiwar movement in the Boston area.

But as historian Michael S. Foley writes, "draft resistance may be one of the least understood phenomena of the period."

Foley writes that draft resistance, which included such actions as publicly turning in or burning draft cards, refusing induction, and otherwise "meddling" with the Selective Service System, was so poorly understood that the fact that it may have been a significant aspect of the antiwar movement or that it influenced government policy is rarely considered.

In "Confronting the War Machine," Foley, a professor at the City University of New York's College of Staten Island, relies not only on documentary sources but the recollections of some three dozen draft resistance activists voiced during a weekend reunion in October 1997 at the Arlington Street Church.

Foley's book, in which well-chosen anecdotes are matched by solid analysis, rings true to a reviewer who was a friend of many of those involved in the resistance movement, as well as an observer of antiwar actions in Boston.

Boston was an obvious focus for the draft resistance movement. Along with "a long heritage of disobeying authority," Boston had a "ready-made base for protest" in its large -- and largely draft-deferred -- student population.

As Foley notes, several US Supreme Court decisions "evolved out of Boston draft cases." The "widely-followed" 1968 trial of Dr. Benjamin Spock and four codefendants, who faced conspiracy charges for advising people to resist the draft, was held in Boston. And US District Judge Charles Wyzanski's decision in the case of John Sisson, a resister from Lincoln, acknowledged the right to a "selective objection" to the Vietnam War (rather than a general objection to all wars) -- "the most common argument among draft resisters."

Foley traces the movement through its two-plus years of fervid activity until its collapse from internal political divisions and the impact of external events.

Counselors at the Boston Draft Resistance Group and similar organizations were often as knowledgeable about Selective Service policies as were members of local draft boards. But their ability to manipulate the system to obtain deferments for generally middle-class clients was often seen, Foley writes, as undermining the strategy of "challenging the draft on moral and ethical grounds."

Foley's account is particularly strong in its discussion of that political quandary. He notes that while BDRG counselors "advised men to take advantage of the system any way they could," its leaders also cautioned counselors that "every deferment that you get for someone else, for yourself, just means that another ghetto cat who can't get deferments gets drafted" -- a fact bluntly demonstrated with particular reference to Boston-area draftees by Christian Appy in his 1993 book, "Working-Class War."

A rival strategy was advocated by a group called Resistance that came out of California's New Left, and, Foley writes, was to become the new "driving force of Boston's antiwar movement."

Resistance argued that to "cooperate" with the draft by obtaining a deferment, fleeing to Canada, or seeking conscientious objector status was "to effectively abet the war." Instead, it called for young men to refuse induction and thus directly confront the government.

Compounding the internal strife was the burgeoning split within SDS, Students for a Democratic Society, between advocates of long-term community organizing and those for immediate action against the war.

Ironically, as Foley notes, "the draft resistance movement's momentum peaked" on April 3, 1968, the day of its largest antidraft demonstration on Boston Common at which 235 young men turned in their draft cards.

Just three days before, President Johnson had announced that he would not seek reelection, forced out of the race by the rising opposition to his war policies. And on April 4, the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated.

"The sunny, exuberant, self-assurance that resisters and their supporters had cultivated . . . began to unravel," Foley writes, "as events beyond their control pushed and pulled the movement in new directions."
