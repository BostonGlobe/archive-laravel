Rachael Ray cooks up a storm

By Joe Yonan, Globe Staff  |  December 14, 2005

Rachael Ray's ''365: No Repeats" is at the top of The New York Times advice-book bestseller list, the magazine ''Every Day With Rachael Ray" debuted with a smash, and in addition to her four shows on the Food Network, she has landed a deal for a daily talk show produced by Oprah Winfrey that will debut next fall.

But none of that means that she gets some flunky to do her Christmas shopping. When the Globe caught up with the quick-meal queen by phone, she was buying presents at a DKNY in Manhattan for a friend (''a fabulous outfit"), for one of her Food Network directors (''a big furry vest"), and for her mother (''a cozy, 'cause she's a very cozy woman"). In a husky voice showing signs of fatigue, Ray, 37, who is coming to Boston for a book signing on Friday, talked about writing ''365," cooking for her husband, John, and the risks of overexposure.

Q. In the new book you thank your mother and your husband ''for loving me even when I am cranky and tired -- as this is most of the time." Tell the truth: As the Kelly Ripa of cooking, do you ever really get cranky?

A. The world sees this chipper face, but by the time I get home, I'm just toast. I make dinner, and that's all the energy I have. I say, ''John, can you do this, John can you do that?" Poor man.

Q. Somehow I bet it's a perky cranky.

A. No, I'm just cranky cranky. When I get really tired my voice starts to go, and I start to feel rundown, and when I'm around people I love and trust, I bark things. I mean, it's not shameful, I'm not cussin' at the people I love, but I do get bossy. And I beg to be babied. [She puts on a whiny voice.] ''I can't find the clicker. Can you get the phone?"

Q. Sounds like this book was particularly exhausting.

A. The first few days it seemed like a really good idea, but after a few months I thought this was the dumbest thing I've ever done. I tapped everybody out. I included recipes inspired by my dogs, I got recipes from every single one of my friends and family. I tapped out my entire posse.

Q. You must drink a ton of coffee.

A. It's several pots a day. I'm not proud of it, but it's true. And I can't make coffee, that's the hysterical part. That cranky girl I was telling you about, while she was writing this book she was getting her husband out of bed at 3 in the morning to make the coffee. I just can't get it right. I can't bake and I can't make coffee.

Q. Is it that problem where you can't make the coffee because you haven't already had the coffee?

A. That is a vicious cycle, isn't it?

Q. The book's premise is not repeating any dinners for a year, but it strikes me that you might have the opposite problem -- wanting to cook something that you like a second time but never getting the chance to.

A. That's exactly the case. There are things my husband literally begs for because he only gets to see them once. I made a chicken sausage deglazed with German beer and slaw salad and it was absolutely delicious. And I wrote that down, but we won't have it again for like three years.

Q. Are you cooking on the tour?

A. No. About two years ago, the crowds became such that there were too many people to even see what I was doing, and it gave us less time for signing. People were leaving with kids in tears. We had to start handing out wristbands so we'd have a numbered cutoff that we'd guarantee, and then try to do more as time allows. But even 350 wristbands is about 1,000 to 1,200 books because people buy multiples. That's about all I can do in 2 1/2 hours.

Q. Do any crazy fans show up?

A. I had one scary stalker a few years ago where I had off-duty police living in my house, and I turned in all my mail to them before opening it. But other than him, nobody's weird at all. The other direction, actually, the exact opposite. You want to spend more time with them than you have. Everybody gets their hugs and whatever they need written. I never just sign a book, I put things like ''Yummo," ''Keep your pots hot," something to give them a little encouragement.

Q. With the new magazine and new book and new show, do you worry about backlash, a Rachael Ray fatigue?

A. Let's examine what my job is. I get to chat, make supper, travel, travel some more, and then write, which I love to do anyway. I come from a long line of people who loved to work, even if they didn't love what they did. So I do get tired, but it ain't digging ditches, so there's no reason to feel sorry for me.

Q. Actually, I was talking about overexposure.

A. The business that has my name on it has taken off, but it will live out its course, and when it's done, I get to keep being me, and that's fine. Who could complain? I don't get too afraid of people getting sick of us because when people come to talk to me at a signing, they're not wanting to talk about me, they're talking about themselves. I've got an annoying voice, so sometimes I can't imagine people not getting sick of me, but it's not about me, it's about them. Besides, I've got a great group of people helping me try new things all the time.

Q. Well, if you're looking, next year I hear there'll be an opening for a new ''Apprentice" host. If The Donald called about ''The Apprentice: Rachael Ray," what would you say?

A. [She laughs.] I'd say I'm a lousy boss. I couldn't fire anybody. I'd tell everybody, ''You're more qualified for my job than I am." I could never have an apprentice because I am the apprentice.

Rachael Ray will sign copies of ‘‘365: No Repeats’’ on Friday, noon to 2 p.m., at the Barnes & Noble in the Prudential Center, 800 Boylston St.
