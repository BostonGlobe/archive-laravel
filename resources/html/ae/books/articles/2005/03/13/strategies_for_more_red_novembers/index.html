Strategies for more red Novembers

By Nina J. Easton  |  March 13, 2005

It's My Party Too: The Battle for the Heart of the GOP and the Future of America
By Christine Todd Whitman
Penguin, 247 pp., $24.95

Winning the Future: A 21st Century Contract With America
By Newt Gingrich
Regnery, 243 pp., $27.95

The Republican Party's big tent stretches thin. From the Rev. Jerry Falwell at one end to California governor Arnold Schwarzenegger at the other, the party's reach is broader -- and potentially more fragile -- than its liberal foes dare admit. The strains become especially apparent on reading two books side by side: ''Winning the Future," by former House speaker Newt Gingrich, and ''It's My Party Too," by former New Jersey governor Christine Todd Whitman.

Gingrich and Whitman -- both onetime stars of the Republican Party establishment -- share some ideological ground. Both are fiscal-minded tax cutters unlikely to be found hobnobbing at religious right meetings. Whitman spends most of her book detailing the political dangers posed by her party's vocal antiabortion wing, activists that she labels ''social fundamentalists." As he did with his famed 1994 Contract with America, Gingrich simply ignores these folks: Abortion is cited only once, in a passing historical reference.

But then the contrasts begin. Gingrich is a provocative bomb-thrower, a history professor and longtime backbencher in the once Democratic-controlled US House who rose to power on his mantra that politics should be conducted like ''war without blood." Whitman, the boarding-school-bred granddaughter of the man who built Rockefeller Center, brings to mind images of the ladies in pearls and red suits at the 1964 Republican Convention, tsk-tsking the rise of that ''extremist" cowboy, Barry Goldwater.

Whitman's book is the angrier of the two, while Gingrich's is pied-piper in outlook, a call to arms for the 21st century. That's probably because Whitman feels shut out of the GOP power structure, while Gingrich's views are confidently ensconced at the party's top.

Whitman, who served as chief of President Bush's Environmental Protection Agency until resigning in frustration in 2003, fears that divisive and extremist social fundamentalists have taken over the party and are endangering its future electoral success. ''A clear and present danger . . . is that the party will now move so far to the right that it ends up alienating centrist voters and marginalizing itself," she writes.

She recounts her own history with antiabortion forces in the late 1990s, in which her support for including a women's health exemption in laws banning so-called partial birth abortions drew denunciations from conservative commentators and stoked protesters who labeled her a baby killer. Whitman complains that ''social fundamentalists took advantage of every opportunity to brand me as the abortion extremist."

On the environment, Whitman offers an inside account of what led to her resignation from the Bush administration. She describes how her efforts to bring moderation and common sense to environmental regulation -- an approach she was convinced Bush wanted -- were undercut by leading Republican senators and, in particular, Vice President Dick Cheney. As a result, she writes, the party has ceded its long history of environmental protection to corporate interests and ''extreme antigovernment ideologues."

Whitman calls for the rise of ''radical moderates" who aren't afraid of seeing the nuance and complexity in issues. Her analysis is an important one: It's next to impossible for a moderate to capture the GOP nomination for president without the blessing of the ''social fundamentalists" that she derides. But, in focusing on threats from the right, Whitman manages to ignore the fact that the biggest stars in the party now are precisely the kinds of centrists she yearns for: Schwarzenegger, Senator John McCain of Arizona, and former New York mayor Rudolph Giuliani among them.

Whereas Whitman's book feels defensive, Gingrich's style is, characteristically, all offense. His writing is so manifesto-like you almost expect a sign-up sheet attached at the end. His book is filled with the same kinds of gimmicky marketing labels (like ''patriotic stewardship") that infused his Contract With America, which he used as a rallying point to help elect Republicans and take over Congress a decade ago.

Gingrich opens his latest missive with a bone-chilling recitation of the terrorist threat facing America. He claims that a potential terrorist pool of 39 million to 52 million young Muslim men exists and predicts that the war on terror could last until 2070. He then calls for a tripling of the nation's intelligence apparatus and says the war must be fought politically and culturally, not just militarily.

At home, he argues that patriotism and a sense that America is a ''unique civilization" must be reinfused into the culture. Whereas Whitman worries about social fundamentalists on the right, Gingrich frets over liberal elites on the left, their support of ''politically correct multicultural drivel" and their ridicule of ''what little American history they are forced to teach."

While much of the book is devoted to support of the Bush agenda, Gingrich reserves his passion for condemning liberal academics and courts. His historical recounting of how God went from being front-and-center in American life to being shoved aside shows why it's often a treat to read this ideas-loving professor, whether you agree with him or not.

Gingrich ends his book with a suggestion that tourists stop at the Library of Congress to see the statue of Moses holding the Ten Commandments. Whitman ends by asking readers to visit her website to offer support to centrist candidates.

In the 2004 election, Bush managed to hold on to support from voters of both persuasions -- those in tune with conservatives such as Gingrich and those who ascribe to Whitman's centrism. That will be the same tough test facing the candidate the party nominates for president in 2008.

Nina J. Easton is the Globe's Washington deputy bureau chief and author of ''Gang of Five."
