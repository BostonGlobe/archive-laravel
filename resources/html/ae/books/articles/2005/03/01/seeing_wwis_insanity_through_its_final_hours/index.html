BOOK REVIEW

Seeing WWI's insanity through its final hours

By Robert Knox, Globe Correspondent  |  March 1, 2005

Any excuse to tell the gruesomely cautionary tale of World War I is a good one. In ''Eleventh Month, Eleventh Day, Eleventh Hour," Joseph E. Persico's hook is showing the outrageous waste of life in meaningless attacks on the war's last morning as a fitting symbol for the four-year killing spree that took more than 8 million lives and wounded another 21 million. The war itself was a diplomatic shoving match that got out of hand. None of the combatants had a casus belli that justified the resulting carnage, but once the game of international chicken began, none could find a way to back out.

Best known for his World War II-era books ''Roosevelt's Secret War" and ''Nuremberg: Infamy on Trial," Persico makes the story of World War I new by examining in detail the events of Nov. 11, 1918 -- Armistice Day to the rest of the world, but another day on the killing fields of the Western Front for the combat units sent over the top by their map-room commanders.

For the generals, war was a chess game and casualties an actuarial abstraction. Though the beaten German army pledged to withdraw from conquered territory in France and Belgium and both sides knew the armistice would go into effect at 11 a.m., business was business to the top Allied brass, and they opened the store as usual when dawn broke in Mons and the Meuse-Argonne. Allied commanders who had lost towns in the war's first years wanted to win them back before the end.

Persico puts a human face on these strategic calculations, using letters and journals to weave individual stories through his account of the final day's mayhem and the four years that preceded it. As the news of the armistice trickled through the ranks, men who had somehow survived the long odds of the death lottery in the trenches counted the hours with new hope. When officers told them planned attacks on fortified positions would go forward as scheduled, the men thought it must be a joke. But the cruel joke of war continued, resulting in 10,944 casualties (including 2,738 deaths) on Nov. 11, 1918 -- a higher figure than D-day in World War II.

Some of the men Persico tells us about at the book's beginning are inevitably no longer with us at the end. Men who watched comrades die in a failed attack that morning compared the order to go ahead to murder.

The war on the Western Front is an old but still powerful story, especially well told here because of Persico's ability to capture the characters of the men who fought it. A frightened Italian immigrant is shamed into enlisting in the US Army by his girlfriend. A desperate young Frenchman fears he will never know the embrace of a woman. A perspicacious German figures the odds and bets on a fugitive train ride to neutral Holland. England's Douglas Haig never loses confidence in his own wrongheaded military notions no matter how many hundreds of thousands he sends to death.

Some of the faces we meet in the trenches do survive. The English poet Siegfried Sassoon, whose battlefield daring was equaled only by his conscience, died in his 80s. Stretcher bearer Pierre Teilhard de Chardin became a major 20th-century Catholic theologian, struggling to reconcile the ways of God to the disasters of man.

Those of us for whom the train wrecks of civilization hold a horrible fascination will appreciate the author's struggle to explain the spell the trenches cast over so many who served in them. Some veterans would remember the war as the great adventure of their lives and even the best of times, when life was lived at a higher pitch and a common purpose lifted them above selfish concerns. Addicted to the adrenaline, they saw war as an antidote to the tedium of dull jobs.

You may end up feeling, as I did, that if the relentless, pointless, well-chronicled butchery of the Western Front did not cure mankind of its taste for war, nothing ever will. Persico faces this problem in his own conclusion, arguing that, given our history, humankind's best hope is that wars will be fought for good reasons rather than bad ones. When Veterans Day (our contemporary remembrance of the day) rolls around each year, the pointless blood-spilling of that first Armistice Day should give us more than enough to think about.
