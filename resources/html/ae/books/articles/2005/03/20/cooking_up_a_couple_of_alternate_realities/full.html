A READING LIFE

Cooking up a couple of alternate realities

By Katharine A. Powers  |  March 20, 2005

We don't usually seat Charlotte Brontë and Betty Crocker together in our thoughts. Still, the public images of both women were ever-changing creations of manipulation and wishful thinking, and both underwent inversions: Charlotte, the real person, was transformed into fiction; Betty, the fictional one, set up as real. This pair came together as I read, back to back, ''The Brontë Myth," by Lucasta Miller (Anchor, paperback, $15), and ''Finding Betty Crocker: The Secret Life of America's First Lady of Food," by Susan Marks (Simon & Schuster, $23).

The only biography of any Brontë I ever read was Elizabeth Gaskell's ''The Life of Charlotte Brontë," and I was so depressed by it, by all the illness, madness, death, and endurance, that I thought I would just leave matters there and simply reread the novels. They, too, are filled with all the above, but also with stormy emotion, independence, comeuppance, and redemption through pluck. I still don't feel like reading another biography of any member of this doomed family, but thanks to Miller's penetrating book, I see the ulterior motives and perversions of fact that went into Gaskell's melancholy ''Life."

The Brontë sisters first published their poetry and novels under male pseudonyms, that their work might be judged as literature and not as curiosities from the pens of women. Eventually, Charlotte, the most ambitious of the sisters, allowed it to be known that she was the mysterious Currer Bell, author of ''Jane Eyre." The novel, which had been condemned as ''coarse," now seemed more reprehensibly so in being the acknowledged work of a woman. What sort of vicious, unsexed female would -- indeed, could -- produce such shocking stuff? Charlotte made it her business to confound titillated expectations by acting the part of the demure, drabbish country parson's daughter -- though one, to be sure, through whom art spoke.

After the writer's death, her friend, Mrs. Gaskell, a novelist herself and, as such, concerned for the profession's respectability, treated Charlotte's protective coloration as genuine, and went further, creating, as Miller puts it, ''a saintly icon of female suffering." ''Jane Eyre," with its unedifying element of sexual passion, dismayed Gaskell, and she sought to explain it away as being the product -- somehow -- of Charlotte's living an isolated, spinster's existence between graveyard and desolate moor, tending her dying sisters, and serving a tyrannical father and a libertine, dipsomaniacal, drug-crazed brother. The biographer's depiction of this morbid milieu owed more to her skills as a novelist than to fact, but the book fulfilled its aims brilliantly: The artist and her indelicate art were suppressed and ''a valiant woman made perfect by suffering," as author Charles Kingsley declared admiringly, was invented.

This version of womanhood and not Brontë's novels became the proper subject of contemplation. Miller follows the fortunes of the domestic saint, which endured for half a century, to its dismantling with the publication of Charlotte's passionate letters to a married teacher. Still, fictional Charlottes continued to abound to serve many causes and whims: She was thrust forward as a ''virgin priestess of art," a victim of men, a victim of an ''Electra Complex," a hysteric, a proto-feminist, a dry and bitter old stick compared with her sister Emily, and so on, depending on the requirements of her interpreters.

Even now, Miller points out, when so many facts of Charlotte's life and circumstances have been winkled out and the record set straight, the artist is still seen not for the art she accomplished, but in the context of her family, whose dynamic and centrality derive from past invention. This is an outstanding account of mythmaking and a masterly analysis of its motives.

According to Susan Marks, the Washburn Crosby Co., later General Mills, did not deliberately mislead people into believing that Betty Crocker was a real person -- and if you buy that, perhaps you'll buy the recipe for Betty Crocker's ''Colorvision Cake," freshly baked from a box. In fact, Marks doesn't believe what she says either, admitting elsewhere that ''Betty's secret" was carefully guarded. But it becomes increasingly clear in reading ''Finding Betty Crocker" that its author doesn't like to dwell on Betty's admirers being played for suckers or, for that matter, on General Mills' part in debasing American cooking. This is a hagiography of one of the great heroines of hydrogenation, a priestess of processed provisions, the diabolic demiurge of domestic anxiety, the golem of galloping consumerism, and -- whew! -- just the thought of her clearly unhinges me.

Betty Crocker came into being in 1924 in Minneapolis, Minn. (my native state, for God's sake) as a voice on the radio, the creature of the Washburn Crosby Co. Played by different actresses on different stations across the nation, she peddled kitchen-tested recipes for kitchen-tested Gold Medal flour and soon enough was encouraging her faithful listeners to join her cooking school by, among other things, procuring the signature of their grocers certifying that they used only Gold Medal flour. In addition to dispensing culinary wisdom, Betty was free with domestic tips, marital counsel, and romantic advice not only over the airwaves, but in written answers to thousands of letters. (''If you load a man's stomach with soggy boiled cabbage, greasy fried potatoes," she observed on her first show, ''can you wonder that he wants to start a fight, or go out and commit a crime?")

Eventually Betty's spirit was manifest chiefly in the voice of Marjorie Child Husted on radio, and later, on television, incarnate in the person of Adelaide Hawley. At the same time, Betty's portrait changed over the years, going through, to date, at least eight versions, each distilling the very essence of ersatz for its period.

Betty's public citizenship in giving advice on frugality during the Depression was, as a company historian put it, ''spontaneous and unselfish." Same deal during the war. Nor did postwar prosperity hamper this paragon's public-spiritedness. With her ''Designs for Happiness," the great age of convenience food began. The bible of this regime was -- and is, I suppose -- ''Betty Crocker's Picture Cook Book," also known as ''Big Red." Published in 1950, it was, according to Marks, ''primed to set the new standard for cookbook excellence."

Never mentioning flavor or wholesomeness, Marks shows how Betty Crocker changed with the times she help change. But Marks's attitude is so positive and credulous, so ''moist" and ''goozly," as Betty might say, that, by book's end, I felt I had been fed a whole ''Dutch Pantry Pie": easy to make with Gold Medal flour, Wesson oil, Spam, Carnation evaporated milk, and ''Praised by our Home Testers from Coast to Coast!"
