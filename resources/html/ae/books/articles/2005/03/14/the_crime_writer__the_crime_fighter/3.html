The crime writer & the crime fighter

March 14, 2005

Page 3 of 3 -- The book, which is largely a compendium of stories, also tells of Joe McCain Jr. and his struggles within the Somerville Police Department, and of Atkinson's own brief experience as a private eye, while researching the book, with the McCain agency (which is now closed). But the heart of the book is the old man's story.

There's the time in 1965 when McCain arrested Joe Barboza, a vicious killer (himself the victim of a mob hit in 1976), for beating up a patron at a Revere Beach bar. In the jail, Barboza insulted McCain, saying he wasn't so tough without his gun. On the spot, the cop unbuckled his gun and nightstick, stepped in the cell, and invited Barboza to ''take a shot." He declined, and McCain said, ''I thought so."

But it was the 1978 Revere child-sex ring, which McCain stumbled on through a tip, that disturbed him most.

''Even with all the murderers and bad guys that he put away," Atkinson said, ''he would go home at night, kiss his wife, walk the dog, and sleep like a baby. But when it came to these well-heeled guys, with the imprimatur of upper-crust society, secretly molesting children, it sickened him so that he could not sleep. He was completely repulsed. He would rather spend time with some killer who had done 20 years in Walpole."

Honoring old-school heroes
It isn't surprising that Joe McCain would appeal to Jay Atkinson, a sports lover who coaches youth hockey in Methuen. Atkinson says he has always been drawn to the interaction of the intellectual and the concrete lives of regular people, especially those without the airs and polish of elite education.

''That has been a big part of my career," he said, ''balancing the solitary intellectual pursuit of the writer with the active life. At school I would be studying philosophy with kids who never went outside, and then I was hanging around with rugby players." He also wrestled and played soccer, and plays with the Boston Rugby Club.

The oldest of five children, Atkinson was the first member of his extended family to go to college. He majored in philosophy and English literature at Acadia University in Nova Scotia and earned a master's degree in creative writing at the University of Florida. Besides writing and coaching, he teaches at Salem State College and Merrimack College in Andover. He is divorced and has a 10-year-old son.

Atkinson says McCain, whom he never met (though he listened to many hours of tapes the detective made for a never-written autobiography), ''was a lot like my old man." James Atkinson, with little more education than McCain, ran a small insurance agency in Methuen. He died of a heart attack in middle age.

McCain's moonlighting amazed Atkinson. ''That is so old-school, something my father would do," he said. ''He would have a second job when we were kids because he felt, coming out of the Depression, that you had to work all the time. Otherwise you weren't putting money away for your family, things could go bad, and you could end up with nothing."

He sees his book, in part, as a meditation on ''what a hero has become in our society. If a kid runs in front of a car and someone pushes him out of the way, he'll be looking for a book deal or to be lionized. Joe McCain would do that on his way home from work, pull a kid out of the river, and never mention it to anybody."

The real deal

This sort of dubious bargaining drove Joe McCain nuts. One of his favorite sayings was ''You can trust a thief once in a while, but never trust a liar." When he heard that [Joe] Barboza had negotiated a plea agreement and entered witness protection, McCain remarked, ''Would a murderer lie? Hell, you've got the big score already. You've got Mr. Shooter. Put him in the electric chair. Get rid of him. You're gonna make a deal with this guy? For what? There's no redeeming value to him."

And he was right. Just a few years later, Joe Barboza, while still under witness protection, killed a man named Ricky Clay Wilson out West over $300,000 in stolen securities.

But McCain was comforted by the fact that he and [Leo] Papile had taken Joe Barboza off the street twice, saving untold lives in the process.

From ''Legends of Winter Hill: Cops, Con Men, and Joe McCain, the Last Real Detective," by Jay Atkinson
