Idyll and illusion in a rural life

Away from it all, a marriage eventually founders

By David Maloof  |  March 6, 2005

Losing the Garden: The Story of a Marriage
By Laura Waterman
Shoemaker & Hoard, 275 pp., $24

A compelling memoir demands a precarious dance between the universal and the unusual. The life described must in some way be extraordinary, yet familiar in its truths. Otherwise, why would someone read it, and believe it?

Laura Waterman's memoir of her marriage -- and her attempt to explain not only how and why it ended with her husband, Guy's, suicide, but why she was complicit in that act -- attempts that dance. The result is a very slow waltz during which the dancers hardly touch.

The setup is promising: In 1973, Laura and Guy Waterman leave their New York lives to homestead on 38 acres outside East Corinth, Vt. The outdoors, particularly climbing, had helped bring them together and sustain them while living more conventional lives. Now they are attempting what others dreamed of, few attempted, and fewer accomplished.

They succeed in that they lived at Barra, as they called their homestead, until 2000. Their 512-square-foot cabin has no telephone, electricity, or plumbing, but room for Guy's grand piano and shelves of their books. But because there is no geographical cure, they also bring along their personal histories.

As a young man, Guy spent time in a psychiatric ward. He is an alcoholic who stopped drinking before their move. The words ''shame" and ''blame" echo throughout the story, and Guy questions his abandoning two careers, first as a Senate aide in Washington, D.C., and then as a jazz pianist (after which he became a corporate speechwriter), even as he and Laura find some success as writers. His greatest pain comes after the death of his son Johnny, who suffered his own mental illness before disappearing on Mount McKinley. Another son, Bill, is presumed dead, and Guy is estranged from his third son (all from his first marriage).

Laura Waterman is the child of alcoholic parents. She long ago became adept at secrecy, passivity, and retreat, making an unfortunate dovetail with Guy's desire to retreat, control, and hide. The resulting problems are evident in both her marriage and this book, as emotional issues are skirted: A story leading to Guy's marriage proposal falls flat, the proposal never revealed. Laura's decision to not have children (following Guy's firm declaration that he wants no more kids) is explained briefly, academically: ''I felt no empirical urge to become a mother."

Guy's response to his pain is not only to distance himself, but to control the controllable. He is a fiend for 3-by-5 cards, apparently carrying them everywhere. He plans their days (a schedule card awaits Laura each morning on the breakfast table) and is a compulsive namer and counter. Playfully, desperately, he names their homestead, its surrounding trees, 160 seedlings (their names chosen from a telephone directory), and a jar used to hold harmful worms plucked from their garden. Over the years, he also compulsively counts, including the blueberries that they and friends would pick, not by the bushel, or by the pint: by the blueberry. Guy escapes to intellectual worlds, creating a game combining baseball and Milton's ''Paradise Lost," and he and Laura regularly shift from opportunities to consider their own selves into intellectual comparisons with literary characters.

The relationship, and to some extent this book, are encapsulated in a moment when they divide record-keeping responsibilities. Guy opts for statistics; Laura is assigned the diary. Yet she hesitates, recalling the time her brother found her childhood diary and its professions of young love. In response, Guy advises her on how to keep their narrative: '' 'Just say what we did every day.' 'Okay,' [Laura] said. 'No personal stuff.' "

Waterman has chosen to structure her story topically rather than chronologically, which offers thematic clarification at the chapter level but saps drama from the overall narrative. The death of Guy's son Johnny is mentioned as occurring during a ''watershed year" -- then abandoned for 25 pages. The pages leading to Guy's suicide offer the most sustained drama, driven by urgency and inevitability. But after the climactic moment of Guy's final climb, Waterman backtracks to the time leading to that point, like a hiker who has forgotten her backpack at a trailhead.

A scene in which Laura worries about Guy on a solo hike begins ominously -- we know from the first pages that Guy will one day kill himself -- yet ends anticlimactically: He returns as usual, rather than ending up ''benighted" (a hiking term meaning stranded after nightfall). Instead, it's the reader who is too often benighted -- stranded in the dark.

The book does gain power in its last quarter, which covers the 18 months of planning for Guy's suicide. At one point Laura, returning to the cabin five nights after Guy went off to die, takes care not to obliterate his snowshoe tracks. The image and her motivation are moving, yet soon shadowed by old habits: ''I was unaware," she notes, ''that I was crying."

Again and again Waterman mentions or shows Guy's distance, and her retreat. Again and again she admits being unaware of one thing, not understanding another. While memoir typically shows a narrator discovering what she knows now but did not know then, the effect in ''Losing the Garden" is a frustrating redundancy.

The title suggests a kind of paradise lost -- the Garden of Eden -- as well as the literal garden outside Guy and Laura's cabin. While their love for each other is undeniable, there never seems to have been a paradise to lose, as the gardens -- literal and figurative -- are far more peripheral than central. In ''Losing the Garden," the familiar -- flawed love, the desire to start over -- becomes so extreme that it overshadows the positively remarkable. Waterman now knows what happened, and how to lead us there; but too often she takes the easy route rather than bushwhacking through the difficult truths.

David Maloof is a writer living in Western Massachusetts.
