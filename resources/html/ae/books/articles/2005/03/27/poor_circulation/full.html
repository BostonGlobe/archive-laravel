THE OBSERVER

Poor circulation

Shortage of new books at BPL hits browsers

By Sam Allis, Globe Columnist  |  March 27, 2005

My friend Mark called recently to report that the shelves of the New Books section of the Boston Public Library are all but empty. Now Mark's memory isn't what it used to be, so the Observer went to investigate on the chance the guy drew that conclusion in the shampoo aisle of a CVS.

ADVERTISEMENT

Sure enough, the New Books offering is anemic. The shelves look skeletal. The library is putting books horizontally on shelves to consume more space, much the way bookstores do to mask dwindling stocks as they go out of business. It's a sad spectacle.

''This is the worse I've seen it," confirms Barbara Gaffney, examining the paltry selection of new adult fiction. Gaffney has been coming to the BPL for decades and consumes four or five of its books a week.

''The first time I noticed it, I thought it was a fluke," she adds. ''That was a couple of months ago. They try to trick you by putting the books in like that."

So what gives? Browsing, after all, is the lagniappe of the library experience. Go in for one book and leave with four others. It's the essence, too. Loyalists like Mark and Barbara pour in regularly to wander through the New Books section, hungry for titles to devour.

What gives is the BPL is no longer buying its books the way it used to. As of last November, it stopped leasing books from a supplier called McNaughton Books that provides titles to cover unforeseen spikes in popularity. (Think ''The Da Vinci Code.") At one point in 2002, all but 29 of the 123 copies of David McCullough's ''John Adams" were leased from McNaughton.

But McNaughton charges about $22 a book -- plus another $5 if the library decided to keep it, according to Mary Frances O'Brien, Central Library Services manager. As of November, the BPL has moved to an outfit called Baker & Taylor, from which it buys books for about $14 for a savings of $8 a pop.

Since November, the BPL has taken thousands of books leased from McNaughton off the shelves to be returned to the supplier, accounting for the sorry state of affairs. And, surprise, circulation in the central library dropped 4 percent for each of the last two months of 2004. (For now, the switch is a pilot project limited to the main library in Copley Square.)

The move, obviously, is about money. The BPL budget has averaged a 7 percent hit each of the past two fiscal years and is level funded for 2005. ''The choice was to cut heavily into the materials budget to avoid staff layoffs and keep all the branches open," O'Brien explains. She knows that browsing has been hit hard. New adult fiction, the lifeblood of grownup browsers, has taken the biggest wallop. She prays the budget situation will improve. Prayer is great, but the city's finances are not.

To avoid layoffs, the BPL lost about $300,000 in state funding this year by failing to devote the required 12 percent of its budget to materials (acquisitions.) The loss would have been much larger had it not been for a partial waiver from the state. The institution may lose the state funding again next year for the same reason. That's a lot of money down the drain.

Mayor Tom Menino wants the BPL to spend the $200,000 in a fund built largely from its restaurant revenues to buy more books, now. He recently relayed this message to BPL President Bernie Margolis, who as a result will go to the trustees for approval.

Menino is strapped for dough, but somehow needs to steer more bucks to the place. It's our crown jewel, the oldest publicly funded municipal library in the country. And well-heeled Bostonians must feed the acquisition budget. This is a classic opportunity for private largesse in a city that bills itself as ''the Athens of America."

O'Brien notes that almost 1,000 new titles have been bought since November, on par with the number leased from McNaughton in a similar period. What has diminished is the number of copies of each bought.

This means that new adult fiction may never make it to the shelves because the copies will always be out on loan with waiting lists. If new titles don't make it to the shelves, browsers will have no way of knowing they exist in the library's circulating collection. And there's currently no place on the BPL website to find out about new acquisitions. Big mistake.

Then there's the enduring mystery at New Books. Loyalists are baffled. They ask librarians about the situation and walk away clueless.

What Margolis needs to do is endow his floor staff with the facts and then put up some signs explaining what the hell is going on. Just say the BPL is switching from leasing to buying and it's going to take time to rebuild the roster. And let the browsers know that the library will buy almost any book they ask for.

The BPL struggles in the face of tight city finances and a sketchy relationship between Menino and Margolis. It's a tough sell when competing for dollars against affordable housing and crime prevention. That said, it simply can't lose loyalists like Mark and Barbara. Without them, the place is doomed. Browsing is breathing.
