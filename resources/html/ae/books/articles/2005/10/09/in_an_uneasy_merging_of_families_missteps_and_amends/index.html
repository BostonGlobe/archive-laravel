In an uneasy merging of families, missteps and amends

By Gail Caldwell  |  October 9, 2005

The English Teacher
By Lily King
Atlantic Monthly, 242 pp., $23

Everyone who ever suffered or triumphed through high school English has known a teacher like Vida Avery. The wounded but severe protagonist of Lily King's second novel, she's a 40-ish instructor at a tony New England private school who has much of Thomas Hardy's ''Tess of the d'Urbervilles" committed to memory -- and who finds far more solace in the labyrinths of literature than in the messy unpredictabilities of real life. Her 15-year-old son, Peter, has grown accustomed to his mother's eccentricities, with his need for her expanding to fill the vacuum of a father he never knew. When Vida agrees to marry a kind widower named Tom Belou, Peter's ingrained unhappiness gives way to exuberance -- now he can have a normal life, he thinks, with a kitchen and stepsiblings and a TV in the living room. Now he can break free of the shackles that define him: his singular attachment to a mother who's secure only when she stays in the margins of life. For 15 years the two have lived on campus at Fayer Academy, eating cafeteria food and stepping carefully around each other's emotions. They go to York Beach, Maine, each June for three days, where Peter swims and Vida reads; when her son presses for something more dramatic, Vida begs off, insisting that she has to rethink ''King Lear."

''The English Teacher" is a character study: of Vida, who retreats into tragedies within her control; of Peter, who has internalized most of his mother's fears; and of the extended half-battered family they marry into. King is also the author of the widely admired ''The Pleasing Hour," and she has the intuitive touch of a writer whose primary interest is the inner world of her creations. We glimpse Vida's profound ambivalence about her son -- and thus about the rest of life -- by the third paragraph of the novel, when the sight of him on the stairs is greeted with a mix of relief and love and a modesty bordering on alienation. This strange concoction of feeling extends to Tom Belou as well. A tailor whose family has lived in the neighboring town of Norsett for three generations, he recently lost his wife to cancer and has three children still staggering to find their place in this new motherless world. His love for Vida seems complete -- seems real and driven by his goodness, and not just compensatory -- but Vida's reaction to him careers between a mild, semisweet relief and downright horror at what she has gotten herself into: ''She figured that all marriages, if they lasted, ended up here in the land of quiet regret." That's a grim, if not uncommon, prognosis, but for Vida, it occurs within weeks of the wedding.

The treachery of Vida's past is apparent from the outset of ''The English Teacher," and the arc of the novel is spent exploring it -- alluding to the father Peter never knew, to the flight Vida made to Fayer from Texas years ago, to the skin-crawling responses she has to the new husband she tries to love. Some of this secret's excavation is heavy-handed: The word ''disassociation" should be stricken from contemporary fiction, particularly when it appears on Page 2. And for no other reason I could discern beyond the obvious captive analogy, the novel is set in 1979 during the Iran hostage crisis. This has an oddly unmooring effect; you keep expecting other time signifiers to emerge, but there are none: no songs, clothes, or other tip-offs that tell us we are in a particular era for a particular reason. The ensuing feeling is that we are stuck 30 years in the past with a plot that could have happened anytime in recent history within the provincialism of a New England campus, so that the specific date hangs from this family portrait like a dangling thread.

King is far more adept at tracing the interior routes of her characters, especially when the narrative shifts its point of view. Despite her fears and consequent flattened affect, Vida appears to us, through her own perspective, as a capable, decent-hearted woman trying to go it alone. There have been clues along the way that this may not be the whole story -- she fails abominably at dealing with a close friend -- but it is only when we get to Tom's house, with his three grieving children, that we begin to see Vida through more objective eyes. She smokes without apology, burns the dinner she's resented having to cook, buys a bottle of bourbon as a reward for herself, and finds it nearly empty at the end of the night. The woman we saw eloquently teaching her young charges is a four-star mess when she's off the clock. Peter takes refuge by peering into the photograph of his new stepsiblings' dead mother -- an inert image where the comfort is ongoing and where change is still possible, at least for him. Even in the classroom, the Vida who won all those teaching awards in years past eventually begins to seem unhinged, hurling Hardy's grim determinism at her students as though it were a billboard forecasting their own impending deaths.

It is to King's credit that ''The English Teacher" winds up neither as catastrophic as ''Tess" nor as ever-after cheerful as Tom's daughter's beloved ''The Thorn Birds." Instead the novel embarks on a journey of reparations, one that heads toward less circumspect possibilities where the tyranny over Vida's heart might loosen its grip. In spite of attempts to create narrative tension regarding Vida's past, there are no real surprises unveiled. What the novel delivers is something subtler and far more compelling: a look at the illusions we carry throughout life, through love or chaos, most of them in service of what we couldn't bear to see.
