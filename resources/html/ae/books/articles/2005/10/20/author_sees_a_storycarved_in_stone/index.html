ARTS

Author sees a storycarved in stone

Book traces marble cutters of Italy

By Denise Taylor  |  October 20, 2005

Behind every great stone sculptor is a great rock. And in Eric Scigliano's new book, behind every great rock is a great story.

Next week, the Seattle author -- and onetime Newton and Wellesley mail carrier -- is in town to discuss ''Michelangelo's Mountain: The Quest for Perfection in the Marble Quarries of Carrara." In it, he writes not only of Michelangelo and the marble mountains where the artist ''learned to think big," but also of the quarrymen who for millenniums have wrestled some of the world's finest marble from perilous peaks.

Scigliano, 52, is descended from cavatori (quarriers) and stone carvers in Carrara, and his grandmother often spoke of the marble mecca. Scigliano's great-grandfather immigrated to Boston, where he carved bas reliefs and figures for the Boston Public Library and the Metropolitan Theatre (now The Wang Theatre).

''So, I was always curious about Carrara," he said, adding that in 2003 and 2004 he tracked down and visited a relative still working in the stone business there. ''I was just astonished both at the landscape and at the depth of history and the work the people were doing."

Scigliano spent seven months living in the town beneath what he calls ''the largest trompe l'oeil effect ever shaped by human hands." Even in the heat of summer, the white marble peaks look as if they are capped by snow. While there, he excavated Italian archives and studied a little-explored part of Michelangelo's life.

''He's one of the most written about artists of all time, but there isn't any biography that goes into the broader context of his time in the quarries," said Scigliano, who said he is the first to explore how the Medici family opened rival quarries and forced Michelangelo to abandon Carrara and use their inferior stone.

In his book, Scigliano details Michelangelo's struggle to create his masterpiece ''David" despite mounting obstacles, how a rival stole his commission for the giant ''Hercules," his harrowing expeditions in search of the purest stone for his ''Pieta" and ''Moses," his dream to carve his own monument into Carrara's cliffs.

But the master artist's story does not overshadow the stoic, rough-hewn folk who have quarried the hills from the days of the Etruscans to the era of luxury countertops.

''I was trying to capture the layered experience of living in a place like that," said Scigliano.

''There will be these guys having their little francesi, their glass of wine, at the end of the day, and they will talk about Michelangelo as if he was a peer, arguing about which stone he used and where he got it. The past and present mingle in a place like that where you have almost continuous experience going back to Roman slaves."

Next week's author readings at Newton Free Library and Wellesley Booksmith mark a sort of homecoming for Scigliano, who delivered mail in the area some 30 years ago (his parents, Robert and June, lived in Needham until retiring five years ago). This visit, he has slightly headier work to do.

''I think you have three main characters in this book: the marble, the place, and Michelangelo, and understanding each of them illuminates the other," he said.

Eric Scigliano speaks at 7:30 p.m. Monday at Newton Free Library, 330 Homer St. (617-796-1360 or www.ci.newton.ma.us) and at 7 p.m. Tuesday at Wellesley Booksmith, 92 Central St. (781-431-1160 or www.wellesleybooksmith.com). Both events are free.

HIGH NOTES -- Two concert series start their seasons with a high note this week. Soprano Linda Mabbs joins the Newton Symphony Orchestra and tenor Nathan Granner opens the MetroWest International Concerts series in Framingham.

Mabbs, whose prestigious resume spans the globe, will reprise a piece she last sang with the National Symphony Orchestra: Strauss's ''Four Last Songs."

Newton Symphony Orchestra conductor Jeffrey Rink heard Mabbs perform the piece (in the 2003-2004 season) and ''was just so taken with the maturity of her as an artist" that he decided instantly to invite her to Newton.

''This is only the second time she's performed in Boston in her career and it's been a long time, so I think people will be really excited to hear this artist," he said.

This year marks the 40th anniversary of the orchestra, and now is the time to purchase season tickets for all four shows. January's show is a concert performance of Donizetti's comic opera ''Don Pasquale." March brings a guest appearance by violinist Polina Sedukh, who Rink says has ''the eye of the tiger in her performance." And May concludes the series with pianist Randall Hodgkinson performing Beethoven.

Meanwhile, Granner, a rising talent who makes up one-third of the Sony Classical Recording trio The American Tenors, performs in Framingham with guitarist Beau Bledsoe. The charismatic duo favors a mix of light opera and American songbook classics, and they seem to have a good time doing it. No stiff-collar types, they tend to joke and get mischievous as well.

''The goal of MetroWest Concerts is to provide people with quality entertainment they can afford," said its president, Ruth August of Framingham. A $50 season ticket covers four concerts (violinist Stephen Shey, The Pied Pipers vocal quartet, Gordon College Concert Choir) as well as free admission to the five remaining shows of Nashua Community Concerts and three shows of Concord (N.H.) Community Concerts.

Granner performs at 4 p.m. Sunday at Nevins Hall in the Memorial Building, 150 Concord St. in Framingham. Tickets are $20; season tickets $50. Call 508-879-1612. Mabbs and the NSO perform 7:30 p.m. Sunday at the Rashi School Auditorium, 15 Walnut Park in Newton. Tickets are $25-$30, discounts for seniors, students, and children; season tickets $105-$85, or $80-$95 for seniors and students, $35 for children. Call 617-965-2555 or visit www.newtonsymphony.org.

ARTS JACKPOT IN NATICK -- The numbers look good for the arts in Natick this weekend: 50-plus artists, two days, two events, 11 locations. Both the fourth annual Natick Artists Open Studios and the eighth annual Arts & Artisans craft and boutique show are taking place.

Open Studios includes artists with regional and national acclaim, such as painter/printmaker Mary Spencer, whose works are held by the DeCordova Museum and the Boston Athanaeum. Also participating are Danforth Gallery alum painters Nan Rumpf, Betty Glick, Jodi Colella, and Jean Weicha.

Three group studios and seven home studios are on the route, including the backyard barn where ceramic artist Suzanne Stumpf, who was named one of nine 2005 Emerging Artists by the international magazine Ceramics Monthly, will show her functional wares and sculptures. Jewelry, stained glass, cards, and more, including Helen Bellamo's cigar box and fabric handbags, can be found at other stops.

Over at TCAN's firehouse, about 25 artists, including furniture painter Kate Clancy and jewelry-for-strong-women maker Debra Sayre, both of Natick, take part in the craft show. (Sayre's wearable self-esteem builders are bold, big designs meant to exude the kind of confidence no delicate little pendant can.)

Sayre coordinates this annual gathering of regional artisans to benefit Kids Connect, an after-school program she runs for Natick youth. Best of all, some of the ''art" is edible. So you can buy a bottle of, say, Shootfly Hill chocolate sauce (the show marks the debut of a dark chocolate sauce) and chalk it up to a good cause.

Natick Artists Open Studios is Saturday and Sunday noon-5 p.m. Free admission. For maps and information, visit www.natickartists.com. The Arts & Artisans show is noon-5 p.m. Sunday at The Center for the Arts in Natick, 14 Summer St. Admission is $2, or free for seniors and children under 10. Call 508-652-4211.

DON'T MISS -- Director Carol Hayes has one thing to say about playwright Ray Cooney: ''He's a master, an absolute master of farce."

Through Oct. 29, you can see for yourself as the cast of ''It Runs in the Family" tries to keep a straight face. The show, presented by Calliope Productions in Boylston, begins in Britain as the pompous Dr. David Mortimore (Bruce Adams of Worcester) prepares for the speech of his lifetime. Surely, he thinks, this will be so big he'll be knighted. But his ex-lover and their grown son he never knew about both show up one hour before his moment of glory, and he spends the rest of the play trying to keep them hidden from his wife, colleagues.

''Cooney is a brilliant writer," said Hayes. ''I first saw this play outdoors on the hottest of summer days and it was miserable, but there was no way I would have left. It's can't-catch-a-breath funny."

''It Runs in the Family" runs through Oct. 29 at Calliope Performance Center, 150 Main St. in Boylston. Tickets are $15, or $12 for seniors and students. Call 508-869-6887 or visit www.calliopeproductions.org.
