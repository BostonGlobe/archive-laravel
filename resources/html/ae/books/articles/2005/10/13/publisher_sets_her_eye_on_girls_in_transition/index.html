Publisher sets her eye on girls in transition

Books tap issues of growing up for ages 9 to 13

By Tania Ralli, Globe Correspondent  |  October 13, 2005

LEXINGTON -- Addie Swartz wants to make it easier for girls to stay young for just a little longer.

Three years ago, as the older of her two daughters approached her teens, Swartz looked around and did not like what she saw.

''Marketers think girls are so sophisticated at age 7," she said. Images of starlets like Jessica Simpson, Britney Spears, and Lindsay Lohan saturate the media. So, to stall the push into adulthood and revealing clothes, Swartz started B*tween Productions, which publishes a series of books for girls for ages 9 to 13.

The books focus on the time when girls give up toys and start to face issues of growing up.

The Lexington-based company has just published ''Lake Rescue," the sixth book in the Beacon Street Girls series. By following the adventures of five main characters -- seventh-grade girls who attend a fictional school in Brookline -- the books have grappled with issues such as bullying, being the new kid in school, and divorce. The stories model healthy behavior and focus on emotional and physical development.

Swartz's enthusiasm for each character is evident. With bright eyes, she talks fast, filling in the details that make each character unique. Charlotte loves to write, Avery's parents adopted her from South Korea, and Maeve struggles with dyslexia.

Pat Byrne, the children's book specialist at BookEnds in Winchester, said the series is very popular with girls. Customers find the books appealing because ''the characters support each other, and it supports the fact that you don't need friends that are exactly like you."

The latest book deals with childhood obesity and the difficulties overweight children face in school. One of the book's characters, Chelsea, is overweight and diabetic, so she hides in the school locker room during gym to avoid ridicule.

Through the stories of Chelsea and other girls, the book encourages healthy diet and exercise. ''We wanted to make sure our information was based on the latest research," Swartz said. She invited a panel of doctors, psychologists, and nutritionists to review the book before publication. Each offered suggestions, and updates to the book were made based on their feedback.

''You want girls to love it and parents to be excited by the content," Swartz said.

The series as a whole tries to help girls face their many challenges. They are concerned with fitting in, wearing the right clothes, and coping with school pressures. Sitting alone in the cafeteria is mortifying. And with so many mixed messages about what it means to be a woman, vulnerability sets in, child development specialists say.

Lisa Machoian, a child and adolescent psychologist and author of the book ''The Disappearing Girl: Learning the Language of Teenage Depression," said self-esteem in girls tends to drop as they approach their teens.

''Sexualized images are a lot more pervasive than ever before," Machoian said. Girls are increasingly judged on their looks, and they have to deal with their bodies changing. With few age-appropriate images, girls are catapulted in womanhood before they are ready.

''Childhood is getting shorter," said Machoian, who added it is important to engage girls in open dialogue and conversation.

But one of the greatest hurdles in communicating with adolescent children is getting through to them in the first place. That's where B*tween Productions comes in. ''The key is to get them in their own language," said Swartz.

The books are filled with contemporary references. The characters send e-mails and instant message one another. Each of the five main characters -- Katani, Maeve, Avery, Charlotte, and Isabel -- comes from a different background and has a distinct passion that draws in a broad range of readers. (One reader wrote to the company's website that even her brother loves the books.)

''The whole philosophy of the company is to use media in a positive way," Swartz said. She works with a seven-member staff who conceive, edit, and design the books. Several outside writers write the stories under the pseudonym Annie Bryant.

B*tween Productions is not the first company started by Swartz, who earned a master's degree in business administration from Northwestern University's Kellogg Graduate School of Management and worked in marketing and product development for several major companies. While on maternity leave in 1993, she started BrightIdeas, an educational software company, out of a spare bedroom. Three years later she sold the company to Addison-Wesley.

To start B*tween Productions, Swartz raised $2.1 million from investors hoping to make inroads into the ''tween" market. According to a 2003 report by MarketResearch.com, an industry research firm, tweens' spending power is estimated at $43 billion annually.

When she started the company, Swartz conducted focus groups with girls up and down the East Coast. Calling them ice cream parties, she got girls together to talk about what they would like to read and characters they would like.

She recruited her older daughter to find a name for the Beacon Street Girls' dog. Armed with a clipboard at camp, she surveyed 150 campers and settled on the name Marty.

The early groups also helped B*tween Productions shape the characters' looks -- Charlotte got new glasses and Maeve got different shoes.

The Beacon Street Girls website allows readers to keep up the interaction. Girls can read more about the characters and play games. Readers can also join the BSG Club, which has grown to more than 1,050 members in 24 countries.

Club members can read new book chapters before they're published. They can e-mail the characters and take surveys about topics that interest them for future books. One survey asked girls to choose the club's logo, an embellished pink shield.

''It lets them have a voice in helping build the world they're excited about," Swartz said about the clubs.

B*tween Productions also offers a range of Beacon Street Girls merchandise. There are bags, stuffed animals, and diaries that tie into the series, though books still account for the largest part of the company's revenue.

Swartz declined to specify the revenue of the privately held company, but said that in the last nine months, more than 100,000 copies of the books have been sold, and 15,000 copies of the new book sold before it even went to print.

This fall, the company plans to launch a writing contest, and has plans for a cartoon contest as well.

Girls can flex their creative muscles and express themselves. Because at the end of the day, Swartz said, ''It's really all about a fun world for girls."
