Fight from within urged to close pay equity gap

By Kimberly Blanton, Globe Staff  |  October 11, 2005

Evelyn Murphy is no revolutionary.

The former lieutenant governor of Massachusetts may be on a crusade to rectify pay inequities among women, who earned 77 cents for each dollar a man earned in 2004, according to US Census Bureau figures.

The title of her new book, ''Getting Even: Why Women Don't Get Paid Like Men -- and What to Do About It," may sound radical. Its pages may chronicle tales of women who took employers to court -- and won -- because they were not promoted, were denied tenure, were not paid what male co-workers earned, or were fired for getting pregnant or speaking up.

Murphy's thesis is unvarnished: Employers are unfair to women.

But the 65-year-old insurance company director's solutions may come as a surprise. She argues women must work from within their workplaces, confronting employers about pay disparities and requiring them to change.

To build on the book, she recently formed a nonprofit organization, The Wage Project -- an acronym for ''Women Are Getting Even" -- to mobilize women and link them with YWCAs and women's professional groups around the country to support their efforts.

''Litigation is not the way to go," Murphy said.

Others who have fought these battles for years might call Murphy a Pollyanna who is unrealistic about corporate America.

''In most workplaces, you don't know what your male co-workers are being paid. You don't have any way of knowing you're being underpaid," said Boston employment attorney Nancy Shilepsky, a partner in Shilepsky O'Connell. ''Sometimes asking nicely isn't going to get there. It's why people form unions or bring lawsuits."

Even former John Hancock chief David D'Alessandro, in a recent Boston Globe opinion piece, said men in high places shut out women because ''they want it that way."

Murphy, who sits on the board of directors of SBLI USA Mutual Life Insurance Co. and holds a doctoral degree in economics from Duke University, calculates the stakes of lower pay: Over their lifetimes, women earn between $700,000 and $2 million less than men with comparable educations.

''Evelyn's book brought back lots of memories of when I was coming up the ladder," said June Rokoff, former vice president of worldwide services for Lotus Development Corp.

The book recounts numerous personal tales by women who feel the sting of bias at work, the money lost and opportunities denied.

Her favorite success story took place six years ago, when 15 women faculty members at Massachusetts Institute of Technology approached then-president Charles Vest for more lab space and pay -- and got it.

''I do believe if you start this groundswell of activism, CEOs are going to pay attention in a way they never have before," Murphy said this week, while signing 500 books at the downtown Commonwealth Institute, which she helped found eight years ago to promote women entrepreneurs.

As women streamed into the workforce in the 1970s, the male-female wage gap began to close. Cornell University economist Francine Blau, a leading authority on women's wages, said the gap shrank in the 1980s, stagnated in the 1990s, but has improved slightly for women since 2000.

Curious about the wage gap throughout her career, Murphy approached an agent about writing the book in fall 2002. Agent Jill Kneerim of Kneerim & Williams in Boston said publishers were keen on Murphy's proposal when the women took a train to New York to present it in early 2003. Eight publishers competed to print it, and Murphy received a hefty advance, Kneerim said, though ''I'm not going to tell you what it was."

Murphy expected her book would take a year or two to write. Writer E.J. Graff, while listening to Murphy speak at the Women's Studies Research Center at Brandeis University, where both were resident scholars, had doubts about her timetable. She offered to help, and they wrote ''Getting Even" together. The book was published this month by Touchstone, a Simon & Schuster imprint.

Murphy, whose book tour this month has included interviews on Fox television and ABC radio and has taken her up and down the East Coast, sensed she'd struck a chord with audiences. Even men, Murphy said, ''realize it's about their daughters and wives."

Author is just one more title in Murphy's eclectic career. The soldier's daughter learned early to adapt to changing circumstances while moving frequently with her Army family -- Rome, northern Virginia, North Carolina, back to Virginia. After earning her doctorate from Duke, she moved to Boston to work for a nonprofit consultant to the federal antipoverty program. Her first raise came only after her boss advocated for her. ''I didn't know to speak up for the raise," she said.

In her early 30s, Murphy left that job in 1971 to start a government consulting firm, which she sold to a British company. She says she had no desire to become a politician: Politics found her. After Michael Dukakis was elected governor the first time, in 1974, she received a call from a business contact who had helped on the campaign. He asked her to interview with his transition team for one of three Cabinet posts.

During the interview, she recalled telling the team, '' 'I am a PhD economist, so [Secretary of] Economic Affairs seems most sensible.' The interview team said, 'No, that's for a guy.' " She became secretary of environmental affairs. When offered the job, she thought, ''I might as well try my hand at it. There was no grander design than that. I was very fortunate."

After her first, unsuccessful run for lieutenant governor in 1982, Dukakis named her economic secretary. The competitive Murphy again ran for lieutenant governor in 1986 and was the first woman elected to statewide office. In 1990, Murphy ran for governor in the Democratic primary during tough economic times, but she dropped out of the race because she failed to raise enough campaign funds to battle deep-pocket candidates, former attorney general Frank Bellotti and Boston University president John Silber.

She took a job as managing director of a downtown law firm and joined numerous boards, including Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Massachusetts.

Blue Cross hired her in 1993 as executive vice president of legislative and media affairs. She left Blue Cross in 1998 to write the book.

Murphy had no preconceived ideas of how to remedy the pay gap. Her research and interviews with women who sued employers led her to encourage women to work with their employers.

Suing one's employer brings such ''harsh treatment and a no-win situation," she said. ''The only reason women hang in and keep suing isn't that think they'll ever be satisfied by it but that they'll leave a better workplace for the women after them.

''They said that over and over. 'I thought I could make it better for the women after me,' " Murphy said.
