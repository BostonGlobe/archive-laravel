A fiery novel of race relations

Southern gothic meets urban wit

By Carlo Wolff  |  May 15, 2005

Angry Black White Boy or, the Miscegenation of Macon Detornay
By Adam Mansbach
Three Rivers, 335 pp., paperback, $12.95

The resolutely transgressive "Angry Black White Boy" gives new meaning to the term black humor. A singularly jittery blend of urban wit and southern Gothic, it's a book of buoyant rhythm and dark material. Resolution is not the point; provocation is author Adam Mansbach's purpose.

"Angry Black White Boy" is about social change and turning racial stereotypes on their head. Unlike authors such as Richard Wright, Malcolm X, and Claude Brown, who wrote about a black-and-white world, Mansbach writes of one in living, dangerous color. Think Eminem, not Ralph Ellison. Macon Everett Detornay, the self-conscious, expressive focus of this jarring novel, is a white Jewish kid from suburban Boston who comes into his own at Columbia University when, spurred by friends Andre and Nique, both black, he founds the Race Traitor Project. It's an outgrowth of Macon's anger at the ambivalent racial attitudes of his parents and peers. Macon releases his rage by robbing and hectoring passengers in the cab he drives part-time through Manhattan. One night he picks up a tall white woman:

"Thank God," she exhaled, leaning back into the seat, hands tucked beneath her thighs. "I thought I'd never find a white cabdriver."

Macon's body stiffened. "What?"

"I'm not taking any chances with that maniac on the loose." Her eyebrows arched at his silence. "You haven't heard? It's all over the news. He's some sort of black militant wacko or something." The woman shook her head. "I'm not a prejudiced person. But this guy. . . he robs white people and the cops can't find him. Nobody knows what he looks like." She shuddered. "I don't want to get raped."

"Better safe than sorry," Macon heard himself respond. His brain was foaming, overflowing like an ill-poured draft. He'd done it: found some kind of worm hole in the white psyche, some uncharted reflex, and here he stood, divorced from his own color by the violence and conviction of his actions."

When Macon visits Columbia's Black Student Union to challenge its sham agenda, New York city cops arrest him for the taxicab robberies. Bailed out by Andre and Nique, Macon becomes a cause, a spokesman, and a brand known as "the Franchise." He calls for a Day of Apology, psychological reparations in which whites apologize to blacks for the racism embedded in American culture. That notion threatens to spur a riot and is among the most confrontational in this highly provocative book.

"Angry Black White Boy" is a novel of ideas above all. It interprets and bemoans a culture that angers and disappoints Mansbach. The book covers expansive intellectual and geographical terrain as Macon travels from suburban Newton to Manhattan to the South, where he learns a whole lot more about race. Even though the novel lurches occasionally, its drive never falters. And if it's short on detail -- it's not spelled out that Macon's charged with the robberies when the cops pick him up at the Black Student Union meeting -- the book's momentum and color carry it.

By the novel's end, this dark, talky entertainment takes a hard right turn into seriousness. Mansbach gets us there by creating a tense world whose figures often are at cross-purposes: Macon has differences with Andre and Nique, who can't understand Macon's attraction to the slight, driven white girl Logan, and Conway Donner, a consultant who specializes in racial makeovers, can't persuade Macon to abandon his convictions.

Mansbach skewers pretensions expertly, reserving some of his sharpest barbs for academia. Take this snapshot of a trendy Columbia class:

"Intro to Black Studies, Room 415 Hamilton Hall, 4:45 p.m., Associate Professor of American Studies Umamu Shaheed Alam presiding: three hundred plus two dime-sacks of esteemed chocolate-brown scholarship poured into an expertly tailored girth-streamlining double-breasted suit, accented with Armani eyewear and compromised by rubber-soled load-bearing shoes."

Macon, a.k.a. the "Franchise," considers Alam a phony. The impression solidifies when the former Boyd Randow tries to wheedle his way into the Race Traitor project:

"Give me a call," Alam said, reaching up to pat Macon on the forearm as Andre edged the Franchise away. "We'll talk persona commodification. I'll introduce you to my agent."

Mansbach namedrops and analyzes so trenchantly it's easy to overlook some shallowness in his characterizations. The women are particularly sketchy, little more than vehicles to drive the plot. The male characters are fuller; the hillbillies who take Macon on an Alabama trip evocative of the 1964 killings of three civil rights workers in Philadelphia, Miss., are especially well drawn.

"Angry Black White Boy," like the truest expressions of hip-hop, graffiti and jazz, is daring and original. It stings like its hero, Macon Detornay, a self-described scorpion of race.

Carlo Wolff is a freelance writer in Cleveland.
