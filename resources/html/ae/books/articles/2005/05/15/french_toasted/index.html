French, toasted

By Jim Fusilli  |  May 15, 2005

The Arrogance of the French: Why They Can't Stand Us -- and Why the Feeling Is Mutual
By Richard Z. Chesnoff
Sentinel, 187 pp., $23.95

Vile France: Fear, Duplicity, Cowardice and Cheese
By Denis Boyles
Encounter, 162 pp., $23.95

Perhaps we're exiting the era of sanctioned French-bashing, given that President Bush has taken lately to quoting de Tocqueville and Camus, that ''French Women Don't Get Fat: The Secret of Eating for Pleasure" by Mireille Guiliano is a bestseller, and that the United States imported nearly $32 billion in goods from France in 2004, an increase over the previous year. If so, what to make of ''The Arrogance of the French" by Richard Z. Chesnoff and ''Vile France" by Denis Boyles?

The subtitles of these books by France-based American journalists promise that the authors and publishers believe there's no deadline on promoting xenophobia and that their slim volumes will be deliberately infuriating and occasionally silly. They deliver, at times embarrassingly so.

Chesnoff's book is far less churlish than Boyles's snarky diatribe. A contributor to U.S. News & World Report and a columnist for the New York Daily News, Chesnoff presents a case that France is on the edge of chaos, crippled by corrupt leaders, a morally bankrupt foreign policy, growing racism and rampant anti-Semitism, and an unproductive workforce wallowing in the benefits of an entitlement society. The preeminent position and influence of the United States irks the French to the point of all but obliterating from memory how US troops died in defense of their country in two wars. To summarize Chesnoff's conclusion, France, though wonderful in certain aspects of quality of life and culture, is no longer great. Only its citizens and their leaders fail to acknowledge this is so.

This is awfully harsh stuff, and yet Chesnoff readily, and at times artfully, bolsters his opinion, particularly in the areas of internal affairs and immigration. In the case of France's ''self-deceptive diplomacy," he references the country's short-sighted and ultimately self-defeating appeasement of Libya, Iraq, and other nations that harbored terrorists, and its embrace of Hezbollah; and, he says, France's trust of radical groups may result in a growing sleeper network of Islamic extremists among the 10 percent of the country's citizens who are Muslim. France's duplicity in the United Nations' oil-for-food scandal, he claims, illustrates the government's willingness to seek billions in profit through nefarious means, and its failure to support the United States in the attack on Saddam Hussein's regime can be seen as a business decision, rather than a geopolitical one.

Chesnoff hints at some kind of explanation for what we see as French arrogance, citing the philosophies of René Descartes as the source of French intellectual life and its educational system, though he reduces Cartesian logic and its systematic approach to knowledge to ''don't let someone else's bothersome opinion or even ugly facts get in the way."

Add to that the following, quoted by the author: In the late '60s, Charles De Gaulle reportedly told David Ben-Gurion, Israel's founding prime minister, ''France has no friends, enemies or allies. It only has interests." For France-bashers, the definition of its arrogance in foreign affairs and elsewhere might be a blend of the two: for the French, only their opinions and their interests matter.

As for Boyles's book, after defining France as an incidental country in his opening sentence he informs us that he's authored a polemic and subsequently makes little attempt at rational discourse. It's a 162-page rant, and if it's supposed to be funny, it isn't.

''Vile France" is culled from Boyles's articles for National Review online, and the poorly edited text makes the same points time and again, often in almost identical language. The best writing in the book comes from excerpts of articles by other American journalists. Boyles may have come honestly to his conclusions about the country, but it's hard to see through his blizzard of bile to know if he's dealing in something other than opinion. He quotes French journalists yet tells us they're useless, and on a few occasions cites ''a friend" as the supplier of an anecdote. His vitriol is such that one grows squeamish at his seeming smugness over the death of some 15,000 elderly French men and women in the heat wave of 2003. It's a shameful performance.

But when it comes to France-bashing, there's plenty of shame to go around. Books such as these -- Chesnoff's to a lesser degree, to be fair -- are intended to inflame rather than inform, as is much of today's political discourse, where plenty of what's said is blatantly untrue. The French aren't oblivious to, unconcerned about, nor so arrogant as not to question their relationship with the United States and their country's place in the world, as suggested by many top-selling books in France, including the pro-US ''The Anti-American Obsession" by Jean-Francois Revel and Nicolas Baverez's ''France Is Falling."

In order to accept the premise of either book -- that they hate us and we ought to hate them -- we must agree to confuse government with its citizens and see official policy as the unanimous opinion of some 60 million French people. Certainly and rightly, we Americans resent it when we are viewed in this simple-minded way. Accordingly, we know that reducing people to the declarations and actions of their government is beneath us. Chesnoff and Boyles, and publishers of such inflammatory rhetoric, ought to as well.

Jim Fusilli is a novelist and critic whose mysteries include ''A Well-Known Secret" and, most recently, ''Hard, Hard City."
