LBJ and MLK: with eyes on the prize

Natural opposites, the two leaders compromised to get civil rights legislation passed

By Eric Arnesen  |  May 8, 2005

Judgment Days: Lyndon Baines Johnson, Martin Luther King Jr., and the Laws That Changed America

ADVERTISEMENT

By Nick Kotz

Houghton Mifflin, 522 pp.,illustrated, $26

The civil rights movement of the 1960s remains widely celebrated in our popular culture for its hard-won accomplishments. A century after the abolition of slavery, the movement helped to overturn legalized segregation and win voting rights for blacks in the American South, as many books, articles, and documentary films have powerfully chronicled. Studies of grass-roots organizations, individual civil rights campaigns, and city- and state-level struggles for equality fill multiple shelves in many bookstores, supplemented by numerous biographies of movement leaders, their white allies and antagonists, and national political leaders. For students of civil rights history, there is abundant scholarship to draw upon.

Nick Kotz's ''Judgment Days" is the latest book on the 1960s civil rights revolution aimed at a nonacademic audience. Adopting a biographical focus, Kotz recasts the momentous events of the '60s through the perspectives and actions of two larger-than-life figures, the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr. and President Lyndon Johnson. These men were ''unlikely partners," Katz argues, two ''indispensable leaders" whose ''synergy" decisively shaped the movement's legislative outcomes -- namely the 1964 Civil Rights Act and the 1965 Voting Rights Act.

Much of the ground Kotz covers is familiar territory, ably charted over the years by King biographers David Garrow and Taylor Branch, Johnson scholar Robert Dallek, and numerous others. What Kotz does exceptionally well is convey the drama -- and the contingency -- of key developments in the 1960s.

He draws fruitfully on the large and rich historical literature, as well as on newly released archival materials and many hours of interviews he conducted with movement and government principals. The result is a compelling and often exciting narrative that, while breaking little new interpretive ground, vividly re-creates the behind-the-scenes dynamics informing civil rights politics.

Whatever their differences in style and substance, Kotz suggests, King and Johnson needed each other to accomplish their shared goal of winning the passage of civil rights legislation. Upon becoming president following John Kennedy's assassination, Johnson seized on civil rights as a moral testament to the late president, which would also serve, crucially, as a vehicle for winning liberals' respect and securing Johnson's own place in history. For his part, King needed Johnson, the politician, to transform the civil rights movement's moral crusade into concrete legislative victories. Both men were ''master political jugglers performing a tenuous high-wire act before a national audience," Kotz concludes, and they decisively shaped the civil rights revolution.

From the beginning, their relationship was a tenuous one that grew shakier over time. Behind the scenes, J. Edgar Hoover, director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation, who could hardly restrain his hatred for King, fed Johnson a steady stream of secret anti-King reports alleging (falsely) communist control over King and detailing (accurately) King's extramarital affairs. For his part, Johnson used Hoover to collect covert intelligence on King and other civil rights leaders. During the 1964 Democratic National Convention, for instance, Johnson authorized an ''unprecedented program of illegal political espionage" against his civil rights critics. Phone wiretaps, bugged hotel rooms, and ''strong-arm tactics" provided him up-to-the minute information that enabled him to outmaneuver those who sought to seat an integrated delegation of the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party -- which might have prompted a walkout of other white Southern delegations -- instead of the segregationist Democratic regulars.

It was the Vietnam War, more than any other issue, that shattered the relationship between Johnson and King. To King, Johnson's pursuit of war in Southeast Asia diverted desperately needed resources from the war on poverty at home and was a highly immoral venture that cast the US government as the ''greatest purveyor of violence in the world." Johnson fumed at, and was deeply hurt by, King's growing criticism of US involvement in Vietnam. By 1967, each man had effectively given up on the other. Once rivals pursuing common goals, they now became outright adversaries.

''Judgment Days" offers a hard-headed, no-nonsense look at these two towering figures of the 1960s. Kotz devotes considerable attention to their large egos, insecurities, and vulnerabilities, their willingness to negotiate and compromise, and their personal tendencies toward depression. But Kotz seems more than a little in awe of Johnson and King. Repeatedly he reminds us that they were ''united by one uncommon belief . . . a passion for equality and a dedication to improving the lives of those left out of America's affluent society." Often taking rhetoric at face value, he neglects to examine closely what these lofty words meant to each man and how the programs they proposed would have accomplished their stated goals.

How do we assess the contributions of the countless participants in the civil rights revolution? Kotz acknowledges that ''without tens of thousands of demonstrators demanding change . . . the shining moment of the 1960s never would have become even a possibility." He leaves the impression, however, that ultimately it was Johnson and King, working in creative tension, who transformed the ''opportunity into a realized American dream." ''Judgment Days" is a story well told, but it gives short shrift to the broader cast of characters -- other civil rights leaders, politicians, and less prominent or anonymous frontline activists -- whose contributions, as much as those of the indispensable King and Johnson, transformed civil rights law and, in the process, the nation.

Eric Arnesen is chair of the History Department at the University of Illinois at Chicago. He is writing a biography of civil rights and labor leader A. Philip Randolph.
