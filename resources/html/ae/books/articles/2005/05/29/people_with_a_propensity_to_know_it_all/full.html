A READING LIFE

People with a propensity to know it all

Corrective views on other climes, kitchen science

By Katherine A. Powers  |  May 29, 2005

Bossiness and a know-it-all attitude belong to the firstborn, though others less favored (one thinks of second-born Donald Rumsfeld and third-born Joseph Ratzinger) have been known to muscle in on the territory. Be that as it may, as the eldest of five siblings, I take a special interest in books that display these problematic qualities. Indeed, I have one before me that approaches the sublime in its peremptory dictate and overweening all-knowingness. I am referring to ''The Clumsiest People in Europe, or: Mrs. Mortimer's Bad-Tempered Guide to the Victorian World," which consists of selections from three of this principled lady's geography books for children, edited and introduced by Todd Pruzan (Bloomsbury, $19.95).

The volumes were first published between 1849 and 1854, which is to say shortly after the revolutions of 1848, just as the Crimean War was beginning, while the Great Game was in full swing, and right between the two Opium Wars. The world was a dangerous place, but for Mrs. Mortimer, the perils attendant upon political and economic conflict were as nothing compared with the threats abroad of being scalded by hot springs, crushed by avalanches, buried in hot lava, murdered by Sicilian or Mexican robbers, drowned in Amsterdam's canals, or touched by dirty Icelanders. Take Switzerland, a beautiful deathtrap: ''If you are afraid of going up steep paths, you had better not go there, for you would have to travel in high places by the side of terrible precipices."

Wisely, Mrs. Mortimer did not travel much herself, but drew her measureless knowledge of other parts and peoples from books. Nonetheless, the decisiveness and moral certainty with which she passes on information (Greeks ''do not bear their troubles well; when they are unhappy, they scream like babies") and the ruthlessness with which she reduces everything to its barest essentials (''Denmark is flat, but not nearly as flat as Holland, nor as damp, nor as ugly") are all hers. ''What is politeness compared to truth and honesty!" she asks, raising knuckle-rapping intolerance to an art form. I must say, aficionada of eccentric and fanatic Victorian writing though I am, I have never read anything quite like this. Mrs. Mortimer is so dreadful, so bigoted, so insular, so evangelically dismayed at the world's fallen ways, that she is in a class of her own and may even have been nuts.

Pruzan introduces each country with a brisk description of its historical circumstances at the time Mrs. Mortimer was wielding her punitive pen -- making for a most curious contrast -- and in his introduction provides some evidence of possible lunacy, or at least of monumental intransigence. He also offers a glimpse of Favell Bevan Mortimer's unhappy life. Born in 1802, she was one of five daughters -- though which in birth order is, alas, not mentioned -- of a Quaker banker. She became an Evangelical and developed a passion for a man some years her junior with whom she studied the Bible and corresponded on spiritual matters with great ardor. He, in turn, distressed her by marrying another, and may possibly have unhinged her when he converted to Roman Catholicism, becoming, in the fullness of time, Cardinal Manning.

Cardinal Manning! Champion of papal infallibility, no less. It staggers the imagination. Certainly Mrs. Mortimer's Evangelical Protestantism alone had equipped her with as robust a loathing for Rome (''a wicked city. . . . Here the Pope lives") as any righteous person could ever need. But to think how it had touched her personally, safe at home though she was; to think that her erstwhile flame had abandoned himself to Rome's sacrilegious rites and iniquitous superstitions. The enormity almost loosens my own screws. Manning embraced the Church in 1851 and, ever ambitious, was ordained to the priesthood the same year. Knowing this allows one to feel a deeper current in Mrs. Mortimer's 1854 observation that ''the people in Brazil are not so wicked as those in Mexico. One reason may be, there are not so many priests."

In addition to being a steadfast foe of Catholicism, Mrs. Mortimer was no friend to the tippler, the gambler, the unwashed, the lazy, the ugly, the Sabbath breaker, and damp conditions. No, sir. There again, she deplored slavery, the English import of opium into China, and predatory states (''Austria, Russia, and Prussia. . . . have divided Poland between them. This was very wrong"). We cannot like her; she is a prodigy of prejudice; but one need only turn to the airwaves to hear today's rabid version of the same thing.

There is nothing the person with a propensity to know it all likes so much as sitting down with a book of obscure but authentic facts. Not trivia -- that is for nerds and bores -- but down-to-earth information on, say, what cream of tartar is, or why certain cured meats have a rainbow sheen, or what precisely an emulsion is and how it comes to be. You know what I mean: valuable ammunition for putting down ignorance and error. Robert L. Wolke's ''What Einstein Told His Cook: Kitchen Science Explained" and the recently published ''What Einstein Told His Cook 2: The Sequel" (Norton, $25.95) are the sort of books I'm talking about. Wolke, who is a chemist, among other things, writes a know-it-all column called ''Food 101" for The Washington Post, and these two books are filled with its fruits. More than that, they have the further essentials of being both highly opinionated and occasionally, in those opinions, quite wrong.

Oh, yes, this is paradise: It brings the bossy beast frisking out of its lair. One example will have to suffice. In his truly revelatory discussion of onions and garlic and all their little chemical ways, Wolke objects strongly to the term ''caramelize," which he believes has erroneous associations, and wishes to replace it with ''goldenize." Off he goes: ''In hopes of banishing" -- we always like the word ''banishing" -- ''the use of the word caramelizing for the gentle sautéing of onions, I hereby offer to the world the word goldenizing. I realize that goldenize has only three syllables to caramelize's four (and for heaven's sake, people, it's CAR-a-mel-ize, not CARM-el-ize)" -- that's the spirit! -- ''but what it may lack in grandeur it more than makes up for in accurate imagery." Well, really! This is the same fellow who does not bat an eye at the term ''sweating" onions (we say ''melting" in our family, for we learned early that only horses sweat). But goldenizing?! No, no. That is all wrong, and if you wish to know why, it is because I say so.
