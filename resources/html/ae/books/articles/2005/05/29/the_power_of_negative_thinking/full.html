The power of negative thinking

Seeking happiness? Forget the 12 steps, embrace pessimism, and tone down the self-esteem, counsels Paul Pearsall.

By Diane White  |  May 29, 2005

The Last Self-Help Book You'll Ever Need: Repress Your Anger, Think Negatively, Be a Good Blamer, and Throttle Your Inner Child

ADVERTISEMENT

By Paul PearsallBasic, 243 pp., $24

Paul Pearsall's provocative ''The Last Self-Help Book You'll Ever Need" lives up to the promise of its title. Those who might benefit most from reading it probably won't, but if they happen to, they'll never look at Dr. Phil the same way again.

Pearsall is a self-help heretic, a psychologist and motivational speaker who argues that the platitudes of self-empowerment popularized by the self-help movement actually prevent people from fulfilling their potential for satisfaction and happiness.

Pearsall doesn't buy the notion that men are from Mars and women are from Venus. He doesn't believe that you have to love yourself before you can love others. He thinks that under certain circumstances it makes sense to lose hope, to settle for less than the very best, to quit seeking your soul mate. Pearsall believes that a little pessimism is a good thing, that high self-esteem can be damaging to yourself and others, that you're not a victim of anything except thinking you're a victim. And he thinks your inner child needs a swift kick in the behind.

''The Last Self-Help Book You'll Ever Need" isn't a total debunking of the self-help movement. Pearsall writes that in the beginning, the self-help genre was based on sound psychological principles. Over time it has been hijacked by celebrity ''experts," many of whom lack relevant education or training, practicing simplistic pop psychology with no basis in scientific research. According to him, ''Everyone from politicians and professors to teachers and talk-show hosts parrots the platitudes of self-empowerment as if they were facts of life."

Pearsall uses the findings of legitimate scientific researchers to point out the weaknesses of celebrity self-help gurus and the flaws in their menu of McMorals, ''tenets about life that go down easily but aren't good for our long-term well-being." The self-help industry, he writes, with its relentless emphasis on the self, is creating a nation of self-centered, self-involved, narcissistic, childlike adults who doggedly pursue ''personal growth" but who never grow up and mature.

Self-help terms and bromides have become accepted wisdom and part of our vocabulary. We don't think twice when we hear people talk about ''getting in touch with your feelings," ''living in denial," ''codependency," ''learning to love yourself," ''being in recovery," ''quality time," etc. Pearsall invites readers to step back from the familiar jargon and think about the meaning and implications of these phrases. Consider, for example, the popular self-help idea that true love must be unconditional. Not so, says Pearsall. ''Healthy, lasting love is conditional, not unconditional. No one, including you, is love-worthy if she or he does not behave lovingly. Love is something you earn, not something you deserve."

Pearsall is hardly the first mental-health professional to take a dim view of the self-help industry. However, his objections may be particularly pointed because they are rooted in personal experience. Sixteen years ago he was ill with stage four lymphoma. He wasn't expected to live. Friends and family, trying to help, filled his hospital room with self-help books and tapes to the point that all the books actually began to interfere with the operation of his hospital bed. Desperate for something that would help him regain control of his life, he abandoned his scientific training and, as he puts it, ''entered the world of self-help."

The more self-help ideas he tried, the more they exhorted him not to give up hope, the more pressured and helpless he felt. He wondered what was wrong with him because, ''with all the pain and death surrounding me on the cancer ward, the prescriptions for a positive attitude, for mind over matter, for unwavering hope began to ring hollow and to feel almost sacrilegious."

His experience led him to suspect that telling people who are suffering never to lose hope can make them conceal their pessimism and fear, emotions that are as much a part of the healing process as hope and optimism.

No one, he writes, gave him a book about the joys of hopelessness, so he put his mind to it. He began to understand that constant hoping might cause him to sacrifice today for the illusion of a better tomorrow. He decided to concentrate on the present, and he believes that focus provided the three things he needed most: meaning, comprehension, and management.

That experience was the genesis of all his doubts about the self-help movement. Pearsall advises readers to try to develop what he calls a ''contrarian consciousness," to be skeptical when it comes to the claims of self-help gurus, himself included. He wants us to learn to think carefully and critically. Readers may indeed question some of Pearsall's views, for example, his assertion that most of what the self-help movement terms addictions are merely lifestyle choices. (''You're not a 'sexaholic,' you're just having too much reckless sex because it feels good.") Or his solution: ''The best way to deal with something you're doing that you shouldn't be doing is the one-step program: Stop it!"

Pearsall cites scientific research to underscore many of his arguments, but, like the self-help author that he is, he also tends to generalize from his own experience. ''The Last Self-Help Book You'll Ever Need" is a passionate, personal manifesto, occasionally cranky, sometimes sloppy, but nearly always engaging. It offers helpful suggestions about living a mindful life and raises countless questions about our values.

Diane White writes regularly for the Globe.
