A string of small epiphanies

From Robinson, stories that get at emotional truths

By Gail Caldwell  |  May 1, 2005

A Perfect Stranger and Other Stories
By Roxana Robinson
Random House, 235 pp., $23.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Roxana Robinson is a welcome practitioner of old-fashioned irony, the sort that prevailed until postmodern nihilism got ahold of the term and turned it into something weary and chic. By irony I mean what Jane Austen meant and what Robinson delivers: a central incongruity between the situation of a story and the emotional expectations of its characters. If a woman, summering in France with her husband, believes he loves her wholly, then discovers his passion was only stoked by the lover he left at home -- well, that's dire and unfortunate, maybe, but it's still dramatic irony. Or in the title story of Robinson's third story collection, ''A Perfect Stranger," the female protagonist thinks to herself, weary with relief, that her distinguished weekend guest will be leaving soon: ''It was practically over." That's our first sign, of course, that it isn't.

But then this is territory where irony is as common as stone-paved driveways: Robinson travels a paler version of Cheever country, those enclaves of privilege outside New York where the citizenry prefer the word ''country" to ''suburbs." These people know fine wine and boarding schools; they frequent Europe enough to feel at home in London or Provence, though such easy freedoms hardly guarantee a carefree inner life or inoculation against unhappiness. Their domestic skirmishes can be as devastating as they are private, with glossy life portraits giving way to reveal rage and disappointment. The narrator of the brief, fiercely constructed ''Intersection" describes a pastoral scene replete with deer and canopied forests; it is only after we have been lulled into this sleepy atmosphere that a woman appears with a knife dangling from her side, and the story is transformed into one of suicidal anguish. ''Family Christmas" is the account of a holiday at a young girl's wealthy grandparents' -- people of such insulated status that they insist they be called ''Grandpere" and ''Grandmere." The ruin of the confectionaire's fantasy will be the harder truths exposed by the couple's hired help -- by kind, trustworthy Molly, feeding the children in the kitchen; or by the mysterious, Dickensian child who peers down upon them all from another world, on the stairs leading to the servants' quarters. Only a child could see the world as simply as the girl narrator, who knows that ''you had to be careful in talking to grown-ups, it was like talking to foreigners." Her age allows her to eavesdrop on adults without the pretense of mannered emotion; when she comes up against other people's misery, she has the sense and innocence to feel shame.

Robinson is a fluent writer who has produced two novels as well as a biography of Georgia O'Keeffe, but she is at home with the form of the short story, where she knows enough to go after elliptical victories -- the smaller epiphanies of half-lit rooms and offstage conversations. Her descriptions can be startling and evocative: A young mother marches down a dirt road, wheeling a baby carriage and singing loudly, the baby inside ''heavy and radiant as a star." Or the chivalrous, well-intentioned father of ''The Football Game," putting up his dukes against a young rascal in the parking lot, trying to shake off his wife and daughter ''like a bear with terriers." Robinson has the feel of where a story should go -- how far its emotional parameters can extend -- and in her best stories, she presses these boundaries just enough to give us a vision beyond the story itself.

Her misfires, then, are a surprise, and there are several in ''A Perfect Stranger." In ''At the Beach," a man and woman who've been quarreling watch helplessly as a mother searches frantically for her lost child, and the story's ending feels contrived, its sentiment maudlin and unearned. ''Pilgrimage" follows a woman on a quest to find a mysterious antique dealer and her store in Paris; it's meant, one assumes, to explore obsession, but because we have no sense of the woman beyond her hunger for these exotic antiques, she just seems irritating and her story inconsequential -- she's Emma Bovary without any background.

Oddly, conversely, the portrait of a woman stricken with illness in ''The Treatment" at first seems flat, but in fact it is the claustrophobic reality of the narrator's physical condition that defines the story -- she can't get out, and we can't either, particularly after she has had a hideous encounter with a visiting nurse. The powerlessness of illness here is rendered with subtle precision; it was a fearless decision to offer no happy endings and no martyred triumphs, either -- just a sick woman's grabbing on to another day.

My own bias lies with the title story, told in the cross-cutting perspectives of an aging Englishman and his younger American hostess, and with the riveting account of a man coming to terms with enormous loss in ''Blind Man." The protagonist is an academic on his way to give a lecture somewhere in Connecticut; as he ducks and weaves his way through traffic on a frightening highway, we gradually learn the circumstances of the sorrow that now defines him. The story is masterful and quiet, and its ending offers a piece of transient grace, its haphazard kindness reminiscent of Raymond Carver's ''A Small, Good Thing." Bad things happen all over the place in ''A Perfect Stranger," the fates caring little for whose address they stop at, and that bittersweet even-handedness can begin to feel like a solace of sorts. In the end, consciousness itself is the one thing Robinson the writer hoards. There is the life, the swift uncertainty of action, and then the cool, long gaze of knowledge or regret.
