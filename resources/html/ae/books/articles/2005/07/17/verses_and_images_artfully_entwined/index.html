Verses and images, artfully entwined

New poetry for children explores animal Edens, companionship, and grief

By Peter F. Neumeyer  |  July 17, 2005

Song of the Water Boatman and Other Pond Poems
Written by Joyce Sidman
Illustrated by Beckie Prange
Houghton Mifﬂin, 32 pp., ages 6 and up, $16

A Kick in the Head: An Everyday Guide to Poetic Forms
Written by Paul B. Janeczko
Illustrated by Chris Raschka
Candlewick, 61 pp., ages 8-11, $17.99

Zoo’s Who
By Douglas Florian
Harcourt, 46 pp., ages 5-10, $17

Boris
By Cynthia Rylant
Harcourt, 74 pp., ages 10 and up, $16

Michael Rosen’s Sad Book
Written by Michael Rosen
Illustrated by Quentin Blake
Candlewick, 32 pp., ages 10 and up, $16.99

Take it as a premise that, although there are many poems that are beyond the ken of children, any poem intended for a child ought to be a good poem by adult criteria too.

By that measure, Joyce Sidman's ''Song of the Water Boatman" measures up fine -- for children and adults. Employing a literary tradition as old as classical Greece, Sidman takes us on a poetical voyage through the rural year in a Minnesota pond she knows well. From spring peepers to late autumn's hibernating painted turtle, Sidman observes specific fauna, flora, and microorganisms, and describes each in language that is both precise and accessible. But then -- and this is what poetry is and does -- by the deft use of evocative language, image, and verse form itself, Sidman renders each creature in a poem that expresses the very spirit of, say, the fierce ''predaceous water beetle," or the evanescent green darner dragonfly. A good poem can make you say ''Yes, yes, that's exactly how it feels." And that's the achievement of this fine poet, who, not incidentally, allots to each creature a different elegant verse form carried out with metrical exactitude. Beckie Prange's big, bold colored woodcuts are exactly suitable to the north woods setting of wood ducks and caddis flies.

Metrical virtuosity is a mature technical achievement, hardly a gripping subject for children. And yet Paul B. Janeczko's ''A Kick in the Head" is a ''guide to poetic form" that, with Chris Raschka's ebullient illustrations, is so colorful, jolly, and appealing that whatever lessons there are will not be noticed, and the wit of Ogden Nash's ''world of mules [where] there are no rules" couplet will go down like applesauce.

Douglas Florian, who by now has created a veritable Eden of poetical animals, essentially invents his own verse forms. Each animal defines itself in short rhymes, occasionally rising to the level of poetry. And each is accompanied by a picture made of -- you name it: watercolor, tinfoil, candy wrappers, brown paper bags. Florian's latest -- ''Zoo's Who" -- again delights with dense, punning, pithy rhymes that interplay with illustrations of such wit and elegance that one is reminded of the worlds of Paul Klee. Florian's well-insulated penguin schusses down an Antarctic wave of specifically labeled direction and temperature, while elsewhere, in the gaping maw of his great blue shark, we may discover the indiscriminating oceanic marauder's diet of fish, pliers, camel, and basilica. Simply put, Florian is an inexhaustible poet/painter/creator of wonder worlds, who can sophisticatedly entertain children even as he happily rewards the closest study by grown-ups.

Newbery Medal winner Cynthia Rylant, author of over 100 books, needs no introduction. In what is ostensibly an extended free-verse homage to a brave, virile, and self-possessed cat, the narrator of ''Boris" actually reveals herself to be a deep and subtle fictional character whose relationship to Rylant we don't need to know. Much of the fun lies in what we may extract from her 74-page monologue. She loves animals. She has three dogs, and the cats, Boris and his sister, whom she rescued from a storefront animal shelter. She is attuned to the nuance of words, and she mimics the silly cliches of an annoying neighbor (who is not all that offensive), and pungently tells her to buzz off. Once upon a time, she recalls, she lived in a house with yards and fences and children, but then the time came to move ''one state south." But even though she speaks of ''we" in that past life, still, somehow she always felt that ''something . . . was missing." Whatever it is, she has forever been seeking it, ''never . . . without tears." Now, at this stage in her life, bravely she moves on. ''Every new place is worth trying," she says to Boris, with ''hope in our hearts [and] three happy dogs in tow."

Poetry, like music and painting, is an attempt to express a feeling that lies beyond words, beyond charts, graphs, and statistics -- to express what it feels like to be happy, in love, or grief-stricken. And grief-stricken is the condition of the poet Michael Rosen when he thinks about his dead son, Eddie. In fact, grief-stricken he is whether he thinks about Eddie or not. He cannot not think of him. Rosen's sadness is pervasive and omnipresent. That is the subject of ''Michael Rosen's Sad Book," an account of the crazy and meaningless things Rosen does -- banging spoons; making his cheeks go whooph, booph, whooph; doing bad, bad things -- in an attempt to cope with the sorrow. But no matter what, he's always recalling the events of Eddie's life, Eddie with his buddies in the street, Eddie horsing around, but especially birthdays -- Eddie's birthday, everybody else's birthday, and the candles. Always the candles.

Although words alone fall short in rendering ''thoughts too deep for tears," in the ''Sad Book" we are afforded also the dazzling virtuosity of Quentin Blake, who, by line, color, and creative genius, articulates concepts that the text alone could not. As though there were accompanying music, Blake establishes tone for Rosen's words: For the pages depicting Rosen's depression, a dispiriting gray wash; for life as it is actively lived, even for Rosen's recollections of Eddie's teeming days, we have the variegated colors by which we know our world.

Consider this example of what the finest illustrator can do. How could you possibly illustrate the expression of rage, despair, and impotence in the face of death, as when Rosen exclaims:

Sometimes this makes me really angry.I say to myself, ''How dare he go and die like that?How dare he make me sad?". . . Eddie doesn't say anything,because he's not here anymore.

For this seemingly unillustratable rumination, Blake has devised a seven-panel double spread in which he renders the life of Eddie -- from baby bath with duckie, to joyful play with teenage buddies. That's the sort of interplay that goes on in a superb picture book, so you don't know whether the text leads the illustrations or the illustrations lead the text -- all the way to the heartrending and wordless conclusion.

I can't imagine how books ostensibly for children can get any better than these five.
