POP LIT

Diamonds are a sovereign's best friend

By Diane White  |  November 13, 2005

The Inheritance
By Annabel Dilke
St. Martin’s, 371 pp., $24.95

The Diamond
By Julie Baumgold
Simon & Schuster, 307 pp., $24

The Vow
By Denene Millner, Angela
Burt-Murray, and Mitzi Miller
Amistad, 334 pp., $23.95

Three engaging new novels range all over the map of popular literature -- a subtly satirical family saga, an absorbing historical novel, and a frisky chick-lit romance.

Annabel Dilke's ''The Inheritance" is a wryly subversive story about class wrapped in an entertaining portrait of the decline of an aristocratic British family in the 1960s. The Chandlers live at Edgerton, a magnificent -- if drafty and a bit shabby -- country house that has been in the family since the 16th century. Charming Sir Harry has never felt the need to work and lives off the family fortune, idling away his days riding, playing tennis, rereading his favorite novels, and seducing his friends' wives. His wife, Felicity, manages both the household and their four children with chilly efficiency while doing her best to ignore her husband's flagrant affairs. Eve, the family intellectual, is at Oxford, where she's acquired a working-class boyfriend. Beautiful younger sister Alice is about to marry, in lavish style, a man she doesn't love. Hugo, the pampered son, has just started at Eton, and little Kathy is still at home.

Alice, her father's adored ''turtledove," is marrying good old Edward, who was ''so kind" after her heart was broken by handsome, disreputable Marcus. Alice had all but begged her father to advise her against marrying Edward, to marry only for love, but as Sir Harry tells her, marriage isn't about love but ''safeguarding property and continuing bloodlines." One of his discarded mistresses sizes him up: ''You're cold as ice, Harry. You have no heart. None."

Alice's wedding is the turning point in the fortunes of the Chandler family. After that it's betrayals, divorce, abortion, more affairs, an illegitimate child, death, suicide, casual cruelty, class conflict, financial ruin, and disillusionment all around. Dilke's restrained style and arch sense of humor keep ''The Inheritance" from waxing histrionic. She has a wonderful eye for telling details of place and person, and a brisk way of summing up a character in a line: ''He was the sort of man who arranged romantic evenings for beautiful girls only to have them weep on his shoulder over someone else."

In ''The Diamond," Julie Baumgold has fashioned a fascinating, fast-moving historical novel around a real diamond, the Régent, a huge, beautiful, much-coveted gem that, at various times, hung from Marie Antoinette's neck and adorned Napoleon's sword, and ended up in the Louvre. The 426-carat raw diamond was discovered in India in 1701 by a slave and became the property of Thomas Pitt, governor of England's East India Company. ''Diamond" Pitt entrusted it to his son Robert, who had it cut to a brilliant 136-carat cushion of 58 facets. The cutting process took two years. Pitt had a difficult time finding a buyer for the expensive gem, but eventually sold it to the duke of Orleans, regent for the boy king Louis XV.

The narrator of this fiction is a historical personage, Napoleon's biographer Count Las Cases, who accompanied the emperor to his exile on St. Helena. ''The Diamond" places Las Cases on that remote island, researching and writing the history of the Régent. Baumgold's portrayal of the relationship between the biographer and the defeated, mercurial Napoleon is one of the many delights of this novel. The diamond is a device to recount two centuries of social upheaval, from the coffeehouses of 18th-century London, to the courts of Louis XIV, Louis XV, and Louis XVI, through the horrors of the French Revolution, to Napoleon's triumphs and defeat. Baumgold follows the diamond through court intrigues, power struggles, and love affairs, as well as wars and revolution, smoothly combining historical detail with humanizing portraits of famous figures from the past.

''The Vow" has three authors: novelist Denene Millner and magazine editors Angela Burt-Murray (Essence) and Mitzi Miller (Jane). That's one for each of the fictional narrators of this romantic novel about three smart black women seeking success and love in Hollywood. Multiple authors can make for awkward prose, but in this case their distinct voices move the story along convincingly.

Amaya, Trista, and Vivian are typical chick-lit heroines, feisty but vulnerable, sophisticated but naïve about some things, successful in their careers but unlucky in love. Trista is driven, an ambitious talent agent at a big-time firm. Saucy, risk-taking Amaya is an actress on the verge of a major career breakthrough. Down-to-earth single mother Vivian is an entertainment reporter for an LA tabloid. The three college sorority sisters get together at a friend's wedding and make the vow of the title, pledging that by the same date the following year they will have found men who will love, respect, and marry them.

''The Vow" unfolds in glamorous Hollywood settings as the three friends pursue and are pursued by various men, among them a basketball star, a music-industry executive, a male model, a closeted gay attorney, a hip-hop artist, and a plastic surgeon. There's plenty of Jackie Collins-style conspicuous consumption, and status symbols galore. Famous names are dropped relentlessly, particularly names of the black entertainment elite. And there's sex, explicitly described, because this is a very modern romance. Ultimately, though, what makes this story work isn't glitz or passion or even romance but the convincing portrayal of the genuine -- if sometimes rocky -- friendship among these women.

Diane White writes every month about new light and popular fiction.
