Traces of Dogtown offer grim evidence of lives on the edge

By Joel Brown, Globe Correspondent  |  November 13, 2005

It's warm for November, with blue sky and bright foliage. But up in the scrubby, rock-strewn woods of Dogtown, the shadows are long and the breeze is chilly, and the only sound is the rustle of leaves.

The feeling is that you're ''far away," says novelist Anita Diamant. ''You think people are going to leave you alone."

Of course, it's not really far away at all, a piece of Gloucester less than a mile from the nearest convenience store and maybe a five-minute drive from Route 128. But an afternoon's hike among the stone walls and cellar holes that remain offers an unmistakable echo of the isolation and poverty known by the last residents of Dogtown, a tiny Cape Ann community, which faded away in the early 1800s.

Diamant, author of the bestselling ''The Red Tent," has spent a lot of time thinking about their harsh lives for her new novel, ''The Last Days of Dogtown," published by Simon & Schuster.

''You think about what life was like for them, how dark it was at night, how dark it was if you just had the fire and one candle," Diamant says. ''If you really stop and think about what that looks like and feels like, it's a very small life you lead."

This patch of rocky high ground in the middle of Cape Ann had its heyday as a farming community in the 1700s. ''It peaked early and then slowly bled its life out," says Diamant, who lives in Newton but has a weekend cottage in Rockport. ''People started moving back down to the harbor where it was just easier."

By the early 1800s, only a couple of dozen residents were left, outcasts mostly, war widows and freed slaves and ''witches," ostracized by the good people of the surrounding towns. Originally known as the Commons Settlement, Dogtown apparently got its name from the dog pack raised by the women there for protection. Diamant says it's hard not to feel the privations of those who trudged all the way to Gloucester Harbor to barter a bit of home-churned butter or some herbs for the barest essentials.

''Walking up here in the winter is painful. Your skin hurts," she says, recalling a midwinter visit. ''And I'm wearing three layers and Gore-Tex and gloves and a hat and real shoes. And then you're talking about poor people, women in skirts -- how cold it is!"

The 3,000-acre site is now owned by the city of Gloucester. The main attraction is the Babson Boulders, a series of large rocks -- also known, ironically, as ''eccentrics" -- inscribed during the Depression with a series of inspirational slogans at the direction of millionaire Roger W. Babson. The maze-like trails are crisscrossed by mountain bikers and hikers. In warm weather, they attract the occasional teenage beer party and vagrant camp.

Diamant got interested when she found the pamphlet, ''Dogtown: A Village Lost in Time," by Thomas Dresser, in The Bookstore in Gloucester a decade ago. It had brief sketches of residents such as Easter Carter and Black Ruth.

''The names and the little thumbnails . . . of them were just vivid. They were eccentrics," Diamant says. ''And then I went looking for the source materials, and I looked in the histories of Gloucester, all the big, thickbound books, and they all refer to Mann's little 31-page book."

That would be ''In the Heart of Cape Ann or the Story of Dogtown" by Charles E. Mann, published in 1906.

''It's very charming, and it's full of illustrations of witches, and it's very bad history," says Diamant. ''Reminiscences of elderly people remembering childhood stories. And that is referred to by all the histories as the Dogtown source. With that as the resource, I decided, 'OK, I'm going to make it up.' "

So she took the names and the rough outlines of these hardscrabble lives and wove them into an episodic story of Dickensian brutality and kindness, circa 1810-1830. She tells of Easter Carter, who ran a saloon in her home; Black Ruth, who dressed as a man and called herself John Woodman; and Oliver Younger, who survived a miserable boyhood in service to a crazy aunt. All these are based on Mann's accounts. But Diamant introduced a plot surrounding the murder of an African servant girl -- a tale taken from a different time in history. A squalid brothel is her invention. So is the silent, secret love of freed slave Cornelius Finson and the lonely, white Judy Rhines.

A few characters find the courage to live happily ever after. But the pages are filled with hunger, penury, drunkenness, crude sexual behavior, racism, and violence, plus the most barbaric bit of dentistry since ''Marathon Man." Diamant's ''Dogtown" is vividly bleak.

''That'll sell a lot of books," she says with a laugh.

Still, she has deep sympathy for the people of Dogtown, even when she's in her more comfortable quarters over in Rockport.

''I'm lying in bed up here in the winter, in my snug bed, listening to the wind on a really cold night on Cape Ann, imagining what it would be like to be in a wooden shack that wasn't insulated, with everything you own piled on top of you [for warmth] -- it's humbling, you know? It makes you count your blessings."

Anita Diamant will be discussing ''The Last Days of Dogtown" at 6 p.m. Tuesday at the Boston Athenaeum, 10 1/2 Beacon St., Boston; at 7 p.m. Dec. 1, at the Peabody Institute Library, West Branch, 603 Lowell St., Peabody, sponsored by the North Suburban JCC (sold out); and at 7:30 p.m. Dec. 7, at the Newton Free Library, 330 Homer St., Newton.
