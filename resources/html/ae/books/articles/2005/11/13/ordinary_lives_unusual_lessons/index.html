FOR CHILDREN

Ordinary lives, unusual lessons

By Liz Rosenberg  |  November 13, 2005

Kamishibai Man
By Allen Say
Houghton Mifﬂin, 32
pp., ages 5 and up, $17

Mama’s Window
Written by Lynn Rubright
Illustrated by Duane Smith
Lee & Low, 89 pp., ages 8-12, $16.95

The era of cinematic tearjerkers is nearly at an end. A popular TV show has as its gritty motto ''No hugging, no learning." For those of us who have held on to our hankies, though, books still provide the needed savor of salt, and anyone who can get through this month's duo of books without weeping is a hard-hearted Hannah indeed.

Allen Say's best books for young readers have the atmosphere of a high-wire walk; there is tremendous agility in books such as ''The Bicycle Man" or ''How My Parents Learned to Eat," tension between comedy and sorrow, simplicity and complexity. These masterworks are all picture books for older readers. Some of Say's works have shaded down toward grimness, but in such books he succeeds less brilliantly, for he shines at the sharp edge between darkness and light. His palette too is that of dawn and dusk. Say's finest books are mercurial, poetic, ceremonious, and wry. They remind one of Yasunari Kawabata's tiny ''palm of the hand" stories, in a few words conveying whole histories. His newest picture book, ''Kamishibai Man," not only reminds one of Say's early promise and genius, but claims rich new territory. It is that rarity this year, a perfect picture book, beautiful in the unity of all its effects, down to its smallest exquisite details.

Kamishibai (kah-mee-she-bye) was a type of Japanese street art popular in the 1930s and '40s. As Say explains, ''Every afternoon, the kamishibai man came on a bicycle that had a big wooden box mounted on the back seat. The box had drawers full of candies and a stage at the top. We bought candies and listened to the man's stories."

''Kamishibai Man" begins like the folktales the kamishibai would tell: ''Not so long ago in Japan, in a small house on a hillside, there lived an old man and his wife. Even though they never had children of their own, they called each other 'Jiichan' and 'Baachan' [Grandpa and Grandma]." One morning the former kamishibai man realizes ''how much I miss going on my rounds." His wife prepares candies for him to sell, and the old man sets off on bicycle over a rickety bridge in countryside that echoes great Japanese landscape art, with mountains, river, and fields undulating in ethereal tints of rose, blue, and green.

One turn of the page brings us smack into modern urban life: gray skyscrapers, brown storefronts, a truck blasting its horn at the old man on his bicycle. Folktales often begin with characters suddenly transported from the real world to a magical one; in ''Kamishibai Man" this process is reversed, and the old man jumps instantly from the magical pastoral to drab reality. Bewildered, he pulls into a vacant city lot and mechanically begins to unload the tools of his trade, murmuring to himself about the past. In ''the old days," he recalls, children would come running at the sound of his wooden clappers, ''bright faces, clasping coins in your little hands, so happy to hear my clappers, so happy to see your kamishibai man!"

The remembered faces appear like story cards on the wooden kamishibai stage -- the past has become the realm of fairy tales. He remembers the poor boy, in patched clothing, always listening at the back of the crowd. He recalls people clustered around a shop window to watch a new device called television, its moving pictures ''all jerky and blurry . . . no colors at all." Soon after, TV antennas spring from rooftops ''like weeds in the springtime." And one sad day a child shushes the old man for making too much noise. The present is a gray blur surrounding the old man, the long-ago past bright with sharply etched details; the more recent past becomes increasingly cloudy again, washed with dream color.

In the end, a new crowd gathers around the kamishibai man, as hungry for his stories as they are for their own remembered childhoods. It's a scene destined to bring even a cynic to his knees. The poor boy who always stood in the rear makes a surprising reappearance in a new guise. Back home, the old wife has seen her husband on the TV evening news. They sit under shining lamplight to eat their dinner, talk of the future, and ''shut the television off."

''Mama's Window," by Lynn Rubright, is another moving story about lives of austerity and dignity. (The well-to-do may often weep, but seldom move us to tears.)

Eleven-year-old ''Sugar" Martin lives with his uncle out in the Mississippi Delta swamp after the death of his mother. Uncle Free is crippled and gruff, a ''swamp rat" who ekes out his living catching fish and turtles and gigging for frogs. ''Sugar's mama had worked for years to raise money for the window in the new church," a beautiful stained-glass window ''with black angels going up a staircase to heaven," and the novel's action takes place around this church. Now Sugar works on Sundays, '' 'cause you gotta eat on Sunday,' " as Uncle Free reminds him. Sugar sleeps on an iron cot, covered by his mother's old quilt; he is frightened of the water, and hates the spooky swamp. Things only take a turn for the worse when he learns that the church members have decided to use Mama's stained-glass-window fund to build the church out of bricks.

It's Sugar's bravery and pigheadedness that endear him to us, along with the homely details of his new life with Uncle Free and the affection that begins, gradually and convincingly, to bind them together. The ''surprise" ending won't surprise many adult readers, but that won't get them through the closing scenes without tears. Rubright writes with the clarity and commitment of a true storyteller. An afterword explains that some details of ''Mama's Window" come from true events in the life of the Rev. Owen H. Whitfield, a visionary union organizer who brought together the black and white sharecroppers of the Mississippi Delta. Duane Smith's subtle pencil drawings add immeasurably to the atmosphere of the novel; it seems shameful that he is overlooked both on the cover and flap copy.

Liz Rosenberg teaches English at the State University of New York at Binghamton. Her newest book for young readers, ''I Just Hope It's Lethal," is newly released from Houghton Mifflin. She is chair of this year's National Book Awards for Young People's Literature.
