Clashes and change, above the rim

How two huge talents reshaped their sport

By Brion O'Connor  |  November 6, 2005

The Rivalry: Bill Russell, Wilt Chamberlain, and the Golden Age of Basketball
By John Taylor
Random House, 421 pp., $25.95

There are sports figures -- heroes -- who seem impervious to the passing years, their legend forever preserved in our social consciousness. It's not a long list. Olympians Jim Thorpe and Jesse Owens, diamond stars Babe Ruth and Jackie Robinson, Maurice ''Rocket" Richard and Bobby Orr on the ice, or the ubiquitous and wildly gifted Babe Didrikson. In basketball lore, no two giants stand taller than Wilt Chamberlain and Bill Russell. Long before Larry Bird and Magic Johnson revived a moribund National Basketball Association, these two Hall of Famers -- one the ultimate winner, and one the ultimate package of pure basketball talent -- literally saved the fledgling league in the late 1950s and early '60s.

In his superb book, author John Taylor offers fresh insights and sparkling descriptions of the players and the game that go beyond the tired stereotypes and cliches that so many sports tomes fall victim to, including uncanny portrayals of Russell's gut-wrenching intensity, and Chamberlain's prodigious skills and appetites. Throughout ''The Rivalry," we're treated to a remarkable cast of characters who either profoundly influenced the game itself, or shaped Russell and Chamberlain, from penny-pinching owners and self-serving reporters to vagabond coaches and players both rough cut and wondrous, including Bob Cousy, Tommy Heinsohn, Dolph Schayes, Elgin Baylor, K. C. Jones, Oscar Robertson, ''Jungle Jim" Loscutoff, Jerry West, and Clyde Lovellette.

Taylor's exhaustive reporting ensures that casual fans will come away with a deeper understanding of not only the epic clashes between Russell and Chamberlain, but also professional basketball and its complex history. Today's multimillion-dollar sports industry seems so pervasive and powerful that it's easy to forget that most NBA teams teetered on the edge of bankruptcy in the league's early years, and many fell over. Even proposed relocations to the West Coast, which made perfect sense given the success of the Los Angeles Dodgers and San Francisco Giants, were actually frowned on due to added travel costs. Taylor's workmanlike prose -- liberally seasoned with some of the best sports quotes of the day -- can be a little disjointed at times, but in many ways reflects the starts and stops of a game in transition, at times fast-breaking, at times stalling. It also mirrors the uncertainly of the league itself, as most franchise owners struggled to make ends meet while trying to sell basketball, and their teams, to a fickle fan base.

Hoop aficionados will learn a thing or two as well. For example, how many fans can answer, with any certainty, that the famous, leather-lung shout of ''We love you, Cooz" during the March 17, 1963, send-off of the retiring back-court great was bellowed by 32-year-old city worker Joseph Dillon? Or that the 24-second clock was the 1954 brainchild of Syracuse Nationals owner Danny Biasone, who decided that the most entertaining games featured each team taking at least 60 shots (Biasone therefore divided the 48-minute game by 120, or 24 seconds per shot)? Or that Russell, a gangly kid from Oakland, Calif., by way of Louisiana, was so lightly regarded coming out of high school that he received only one scholarship offer?

In fact, it's almost unnerving, especially for a Boston sports fan, to learn how often Lady Luck smiled down on the Celtics franchise long before the famed leprechaun took up residence on the team logo. Taylor reveals that Red Auerbach, a quarrelsome kid from Brooklyn, landed a basketball scholarship to George Washington University only after the coach, Bill Reinhart, agreed to a scrimmage between GWU and Auerbach's junior college squad. Likewise, Russell was ''discovered" by University of San Francisco alum Hal DeJulio, who was scouting another player.

''That [Auerbach] eventually became one of the greatest professional coaches in mid-century America owed more than a little to that chance encounter with Reinhart," writes Taylor. ''The life of Auerbach's greatest player, Bill Russell, turned on a similarly fateful minor moment, and it gave both men a ferocious determination to fight to keep what chance had granted them -- a determination that was missing in many men who, at the same point in their lives, were much more obviously talented."

The story line of ''The Rivalry," by necessity, bounces between Russell (San Francisco and Boston) and Chamberlain (from the University of Kansas, Europe with the Harlem Globetrotters, Philadelphia, San Francisco, back east to Philadelphia, and finally back to Los Angeles), chronicling two parallel universes as they unfold, and occasionally collide. The book opens with the first meeting between the two, a titanic battle at Boston Garden on Nov. 7, 1959, that actually matched the wild pre-game hype. Taylor then flashes back to the league's beginnings, setting the stage for Russell and Chamberlain, and tracking their successes and failures (and those of the league) through their final, thunderous collision in the 1969 championships, and Russell's last game.

To his credit, Taylor doesn't shy away from the social ills of the day. Race, and racism, played an intrinsic role in shaping not only the league but many of its budding stars as well, particularly Russell and Baylor. This may have been the ''Golden Age of Basketball," but it certainly wasn't the golden age of race relations. African-American players were often refused service at hotels and restaurants in Southern cities, leading to boycotts. Boston isn't spared either, as Russell's tribulations with the Garden's largely white audience and his neighbors in Reading are duly recorded.

And, contrary to the nostalgic ''Happy Days" ideal that the 1950s and '60s were a more ''innocent" age in sport, Taylor lays bare the fact that most players were treated like livestock, signing one-year contracts without any pension or health and medical benefits, and fans were ruthless. He also skewers the notion that the pampered athlete is a modern marvel: Chamberlain, the individual phenom stuck in a team game, is painted as the poster child for today's pouting superstars. But basketball did blossom in the two decades that spanned the careers of Russell and Chamberlain, evolving from a plodding, set-shot affair to the fast-paced, airborne game we know now.

Poignantly, the book concludes with Russell speaking at a memorial service for Chamberlain, the adversary who became a close friend, after Wilt's untimely death, in 1999. At that moment, the greatest Celtic tried to deflect the notion of a rivalry, saying each attained their goals: Russell got the championship rings, and Chamberlain amassed his mind-boggling records. More than anything, he said, they loved the battle. ''The fierceness of that competition bonded us for eternity," said Russell. True. But as in most of their extraordinary confrontations, Russell had the final say.
