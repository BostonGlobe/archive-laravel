Ringed victory, on eve of war

Two works weigh in on the epic struggle of Louis-Schmeling, and its meaning

By Gordon Marino  |  November 6, 2005

The Greatest Fight of Our Generation: Louis vs. Schmeling
By Lewis Erenberg
Oxford University, 274 pp., illustrated, $28

Beyond Glory: Joe Louis vs. Max Schmeling, and a World on the Brink
By David Margolick
Knopf, 423 pp., illustrated, $26.95

As marginalized a practice as it might be, boxing has a way of transcending sports. For example, many observers believe that the most important date for African-Americans between Appomattox and the outbreak of World War I is Dec. 26, 1908, the day that Jack Johnson became the first black man to win a world title. Pugilism went beyond the ropes again in June 1938, when Joe Louis knocked out Max Schmeling in a fistic prequel to World War II. The American, Louis, was the heavyweight champion. The German contender, Schmeling, was, perhaps reluctantly, Hitler's champion. Two stirring books -- ''The Greatest Fight of Our Generation" and ''Beyond Glory" -- tell the tale of the night that Joe drove Max down.

In the mid to late 1930s, interest in the heavyweight division was as tepid as the Depression economy. The color line had been breached and then sealed again after the flamboyant Johnson fiercely challenged America's racial status quo. Promoters could, however, be very forward-looking if it meant better ticket sales. Seemingly indestructible, Louis was a fan magnet and a messiah-like figure to other blacks.

On a sharp trajectory to the title, Louis signed to fight former heavyweight champion Schmeling. After two devastating defeats, the aging Schmeling seemed on the back side of his career. The man who could have been a double for Jack Dempsey was, however, a very able sweet scientist. When Schmeling watched his future opponent, he noticed that as Louis threw his steel girder of a jab, he would bring his left hand back low, leaving himself open to a right-hand counter.

When the two fighters first locked up in June 1936, the Brown Bomber strafed Schmeling for a few rounds, but then Max started to time strong right-hand counters. In ''Beyond Glory," David Margolick records that Louis absorbed over 100 power shots before being stopped in the 12th stanza. According to the racist misunderstanding of the day, Schmeling's upset victory was promulgated as a triumph of brain over brawn and instinct.

But Louis was not down for the count. A year after the Schmeling debacle, he faced off for the title with the ''Cinderella Man," James Braddock. Lewis Erenberg, in ''The Greatest Fight of Our Generation," explains that in order to get the champ into the ring, Louis, who ended up destitute, signed a contract promising ''10 percent of his net profits for the next decade should Braddock lose, as was expected." Louis knocked Braddock out, and then defended his title twice but steadfastly maintained, ''I won't consider myself champion until I beat Schmeling."

The second volume of the Louis-Schmeling saga was scheduled for June 22, 1938, in Yankee Stadium. There were strident calls for a boycott because of Schmeling's connection with the Nazis. But as one activist put it, ''This was just too good of a fight to pass up." And there were fervent hopes that Louis would make his own protest in the ring.

It is not easy to turn the tables on someone who has separated you from your senses. In addition to the pressure of trying to overcome a knockout, Louis, who had been partly raised in the Jim Crow South, had ironically enough become the champion of the free world. On fight night a large portion of the inhabitants of Planet Earth were sitting on the edge of a chair next to a radio.

This time, Louis had no intention of letting Schmeling find his groove. When the bell rang, he attacked as though he were possessed and in just 96 seconds broke the ''Black Uhlan" 's vertebra and his hopes of ever regaining the title. Louis went on to set a record of 25 title defenses. Schmeling was through as a fighter. Shortly after their second battle, both men were called upon to play their complex roles as soldiers in a war that was anything but symbolic.

After the war, Schmeling floundered for a while and then landed one of the first Coca-Cola franchises in Germany. He became both a minor mogul and an icon of the new Germany. Hounded by the IRS and bad decisions, Louis ended up in almost as dismal a condition as Johnson, who literally became a sideshow act. Their similar fates are enough to make me believe that both of these gallant gladiators may have been driven near daft by their endless struggles with the hydra of racism in America. The king of the heavyweight kings passed on in 1981. Schmeling died last year, at age 99.

''Beyond Glory" and ''The Greatest Fight of Our Generation" are equally powerful studies. Margolick is more successful at re-creating the excitement of the second bout. Though I have Louis-Schmeling II memorized, I was turning the pages of ''Beyond Glory" as though to find out who won. Erenberg's account wins the footnote contest. If the two authors differ at all, it is in their verdict on Schmeling.

According to the Hollywood version, Schmeling detested the Nazis and ended up bosom buddies with Louis. However, Margolick does not let the reader forget that Schmeling never resisted the embrace of Joseph Goebbels and company when it suited his interests. He also insinuates that Schmeling used his postwar relationship with Louis to restore his own image.

While Schmeling may not have been moral hero enough to speak out against the Nazis, which would, of course, have been the end of his livelihood, it should be registered that at some personal risk, he saved the lives of two Jewish boys during Kristallnacht. Margolick devotes but a sentence to this important fact. It's also significant that Schmeling never joined the Nazi Party, and as the most famous athlete in Germany there had to have been enormous pressure on him to do so.

A professor of history, Erenberg is less judgmental and recounts a number of stories in which Schmeling used his celebrity status to help Jewish friends in trouble with the Gestapo. Erenberg is also a mite more successful at revealing the details of Louis's quiet but substantial contributions to the civil rights movement. Though Muhammad Ali once committed the egregious sin of tabbing him an Uncle Tom, Louis often spoke out against racism, and as a soldier in World War II he gave great impetus to the movement to integrate the armed forces.

In the end, both authors deserve praise as much for their literary execution as for vividly reminding us of the night when the world held its breath waiting to find out who would come out on top in a boxing match.

Gordon Marino is a boxing trainer and a professor at the University of Florida's College of Health and Human Performance.
