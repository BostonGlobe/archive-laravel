Getting to the bottom of things

A decade after 'Civil Action,' writer's digging yields a new page-turner

By David Mehegan, Globe Staff  |  November 24, 2005

NORTHAMPTON -- In his old house near the center of this college town, Jonathan Harr leafs through a massive art book, looking for his favorite paintings by the Italian master Caravaggio. ''Here," he says finally. He holds the book open to ''The Conversion of Saint Paul" and ''The Crucifixion of Saint Peter," painted around 1600, now in the church of Santa Maria del Popolo in Rome. ''The Conversion" shows the dismounted saint lying on his back in a trance, his arms extended, his nearby horse holding one hoof in the air. ''It is an unbelievably powerful painting," Harr said. ''The vividness and drama of it. It's cinematic. This is when Caravaggio became Caravaggio."

Northampton is a long way from Rome, and Jonathan Harr, 57, is no art connoisseur or art historian. The writer of the nonfiction bestseller ''A Civil Action," which became a movie starring John Travolta and Robert Duvall, has taken 10 years to publish his second book. ''The Lost Painting" is a different kind of page-turner, but it shows the same dogged reporting and minute detail as its predecessor, and 90 minutes with the writer makes clear how, when a first book is so successful, the second can be so long in coming.

Harr is a classic journalistic type: impatient with academic credentials, often unsure of his intentions or plans, but relentless in pursuit of a story. Born in Wisconsin, he dropped out of the College of William & Mary after one year, served as a Vista volunteer, spent two years at Marshall University in West Virginia and dropped out again, drove a cab in New York, worked for a literary agency, fell into drink and drugs. He was sure of one thing: ''I wanted to be a writer from the time I was 12." Eventually, to make a living, he got into newspaper reporting, as a freelancer, then at the New Haven Advocate and later at the Valley Advocate in Northampton, where he eventually became the editor. Later he became a staff writer for New England Monthly magazine.

In the mid-1980s, he got the idea of writing a book about a long-running civil suit against two companies accused of polluting the groundwater in Woburn and causing leukemia in several children. The key character was Boston lawyer Jan Schlichtmann, who pursued the case obsessively, even when he was broke and creditors tried to seize his assets.

On the strength of a proposal, Harr got a contract and an $80,000 advance from Random House: half at the start and half when the book was published. It sounds like a lot of money, but not when divided by seven, which is how many years it took Harr to write the book, an exhaustively detailed (some critics said too detailed) narrative with many characters on both sides of the case. Before it was finished, Random had advanced Harr all but $15,000 of the $80,000.

Why did it take so long? ''Partly it was the case," Harr said. ''I started following it, through the trial, but then the case went on and on and didn't end until the mid-1990s." But it was also his method of total immersion in facts and lives, down to the tiniest details. ''I had to go back and read all this stuff that had gone before, all the depositions," he said. With each twist and turn of the appeals, he would rework the manuscript.

''Then there was the writing, which is very hard for me," Harr said. ''There were a lot of characters. I wanted depth beyond just names. I wanted them to be recognizable to people, to have the texture of fiction."

The book was published in 1995. Sales were slow at first but picked up when the book became a National Book Award finalist and then took off with the paperback edition. And the movie helped. All told, up to the present, ''A Civil Action" has sold 1.8 million copies, making Harr and his wife, Diane, rather well off.

But what to do next? With the success of ''A Civil Action," Harr was under no pressure. While following the twists and turns of the Woburn case, he had published an article in 1994 for The New Yorker about the discovery of a 1603 painting by Michelangelo Merisi (1571-1610), better known as Caravaggio. Called ''The Taking of Christ," the painting shows Jesus set upon by soldiers in armor as Judas embraces and kisses him. As he often did, eerily, the artist appears in the scene, holding a lantern over the struggling figures. There were extant copies, but the original was lost for 200 years.

''My agent said, 'What are you going to do after ''A Civil Action"?' I said, maybe a book about this painting," Harr recalls. ''He asked how much money I would need, and I said, 'Probably $100,000 to $150,000. I'll have to go to Europe and spend some time there.' He said, 'No publisher will ever give you that.' "

In 2000, Harr was offered a fellowship at the American Academy in Rome. To take it, he needed a project. He returned to the idea of the lost painting.

In 1990, ''The Taking of Christ" was discovered, completely by accident, by Sergio Benedetti, a restorer at the National Gallery of Ireland, in a Dublin Jesuit residence. Harr had written up Benedetti's discovery in his New Yorker piece, but the untold part of the story was an intensive earlier hunt for the painting by two Roman graduate students in their early 20s. Francesca Cappelletti was the more active of the two, tracing the painting as far as Edinburgh in 1921, where the trail grew cold. Harr said, ''Her story was much more interesting because, unlike Benedetti, who stumbled across the painting, she had been actively looking for it."

Harr knew the project would take years -- he knew little about art, but then he had known little about industrial pollution or tort law before he started ''A Civil Action." He decided to learn Italian, threw himself into it during his three-month stay at the American Academy, and continued to study thereafter. Fortunately, Cappelletti spoke English.

''I met her for coffee and told her I wanted to do the book, and she said, 'Sure, why not?' " She was teaching at the University of Calabria and agreed to let Harr go there with her by train, watch her teach, and follow her around while peppering her with questions. It was the beginning of the kind of total immersion he had employed with ''A Civil Action."

''She was wonderful, a very brilliant woman," said Harr. ''I'm following her around, asking very detailed questions, and about her love life, too -- she's young and attractive, which comes into the book later on. It's not like sitting across a desk from somebody -- it's much harder to know a person that way. I like to see people in action, interacting with others, and I got a sense of her character."

In 2001, he got a new contract with Random House -- this time, without an advance.

Harr spent almost five years -- from his initial term at the American Academy to the publication of ''The Lost Painting" -- researching and writing what reads as a kind of detective story, thick with characters, twists, and turns. He became proficient in Italian, and he and his wife kept an apartment in Rome.

While all the main characters love art and Caravaggio, each is also after some kind of personal triumph. Behind them all is the mysterious painter himself, a brawling, drinking egoist, for years on the run for a murder charge. He was also the only main character Harr could not meet. ''I would love to have half an hour with him," Harr said, ''just to observe him, drinking in a bar somewhere. It would give so much life to the book. There are literally a thousand questions I could ask."

In all, it was 10 years between books. Asked if he was ever tempted to press Harr to hurry up, his Random House editor and friend Robert Loomis said, ''With people like Jon, that doesn't work. He isn't going to turn it in before it's ready. He doesn't want anything in there that does not seem perfectly natural, and that is one reason in his books it seems like you are with these people."

Harr does not know what his next book will be, but it might be fiction. He wrote one novel, unpublished, years ago. He and his wife just returned from four months in Italy, which they both now love madly, and while there he wrote some short stories. He said, ''At 57, you get tired of following people around and having one interview after another and trying to reconstruct these things."

''I like the idea that my nonfiction reads with the texture of fiction," Harr said. ''I want characters who are vivid. Characters are the driving force of a book."
