An inspiring kind of justice

Judge's novel for teensdraws on his early lifeunder segregation

By David Mehegan, Globe Staff  |  November 15, 2005

BROOKLINE -- Books aplenty have come from the pens of sitting judges: legal tomes, histories, memoirs, moral or political treatises. But few have been novels, and perhaps no judge before has written a novel for teenagers.

Julian Houston, 61, has for 15 years been associate justice of Middlesex Superior Court, and before that he was judge of Roxbury District Court. In his long Massachusetts career, which began when he came to Boston University in 1962, he has been an activist on behalf of youth in New York and Boston, trustee of the Boston Symphony Orchestra, and founder of the Justice George Lewis Ruffin Society, dedicated to improving understanding between minorities and the criminal justice system. He is distinguished and respected, and with his wife, Susan (director of the Massachusetts Alliance for Economic Development), has raised two children.

Still, something was missing. Since his days in Roxbury District Court, Houston had wanted to write fiction, and in particular a story near to his heart and life: that of an African-American boy sent out of the segregated South to an elite New England boarding school. ''New Boy," his first book, is based in part on Houston's own journey.

Seated in his living room, with his poodle snoozing on the rug, Houston explained why he wrote this story. He has a soft voice and chooses his words carefully, like a judge enunciating a ruling, and at times was drowned out by the Beacon Street trolley passing by the open window. ''One reason," he said, ''was to preserve my memories of what it was like to live under segregation in the South, because my sense is that young people today have very little understanding of what it was like. They may know about Martin Luther King, about Rosa Parks, but they don't understand the details."

He understands them. They're seared in his memory.

A separate life
Growing up in Richmond, Va., in the 1950s, he lived in a wholly segregated world, long after Brown v. Board of Education supposedly outlawed segregation. He was raised by his dynamic mother, who for 44 years taught literature at several schools including Bethune-Cookman College, Howard University, a high school in Richmond, and Douglass College in New Jersey. She had been the first black applicant to the University of Virginia, in 1934, and her rejection led to an unsuccessful lawsuit by the NAACP, argued by Thurgood Marshall. Later, she received a master's degree at Columbia. Houston's parents divorced when he was a toddler. When he was in high school, his mother married a doctor.

''We knew we were living in a society that was terribly wrong," Houston said. ''This was 1959. The Greensboro sit-ins [of 1960 in North Carolina] hadn't taken place, and we didn't know what to do about this society that was so morally off course. We came from homes that had television, our parents could take us on trips to New York, and we could see the way blacks lived in other parts of the country. They didn't have to live with being called racist names and be denied access to places. The courts were slow," Houston said. ''There was this terrible sense on the part of a lot of people, 'Let's get out.' "

In 1958 Houston's mother took her son and drove north, looking for a boarding school away from the blight of segregation. ''We visited several schools," Houston said, ''just knocked on the doors and introduced ourselves. A number of them were interested. I applied the following year and was accepted by most of them" -- by the Loomis Chaffee School and the Hotchkiss School, both in Connecticut, and George School and Westtown School, in Pennsylvania. They chose Hotchkiss, which offered the best scholarship. ''My friends were so jealous."

''New Boy," expected to be in bookstores this week, tells the story of Rob Garrett, a 15-year-old Richmond boy sent by his parents to the fictional Draper School, a Connecticut boarding school for boys. When he gets there, he befriends Vinnie Mazzerelli, an Italian-American from New York, and watches helplessly as Vinnie is ostracized and cruelly abused by the other boys, many of them sons of wealthy alumni, destined for Yale, Wall Street, and elite law firms. In a weird echo of segregation, Vinnie is taunted about his skin -- he has acne -- and driven to move out of his dormitory and live separate from the other boys, in the school infirmary. The administration does nothing to help him. Rob is his only friend.

Most of the scenes and events in the book are fictional, but not the Vinnie story. ''He was set upon by my classmates soon after we arrived," Houston said, ''and tormented mercilessly. They made his life horrible for most of the year. By the spring, he wasn't around anymore. I guess his parents came and got him, and by then he was pretty depressed. I was so bothered by the lack of leadership on the part of the adults. As far as I could tell, this was business as usual." Thinking back over this as he wrote the novel, Houston said, ''I felt that it was important, as we say in the court system, to make a record."

As the rest of the novel unfolds, Rob is torn by his desire to excel at Draper and make his parents proud, and his desire to go home and join his boyhood friends, who plan a sit-in at a local segregated lunch counter. His father, a dentist, takes a dim view of activism and wants Rob to concentrate on his studies. Indeed, differences among African-American characters of differing ages and classes are a key theme in the book. Rob's sexual feelings, meanwhile, are stirring. The book zips along, with scenes in Harlem and appearances by Malcolm X and Joe Louis.

Getting involved
Julian Houston never joined a Richmond sit-in, but at Boston University in 1965, he and fellow students staged a peaceful sit-in at the US attorney's office in Boston, demanding that President Lyndon B. Johnson send troops to Selma, Ala., after the attack on the freedom march led by the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr. The US attorney was W. Arthur Garrity Jr., later a federal judge, who issued the 1974 court order desegregating the Boston schools. Eventually the students were ejected. After graduation, Houston went on to BU Law School and became a trial lawyer before he was appointed a district judge in 1979.

He was asked to come back to the Hotchkiss School last year to address the student body on Martin Luther King Jr.'s birthday. The school today has a sizable minority enrollment. In 1959, Houston was one of three. He agreed to speak to the 600 students, but ''I was nervous," he said. ''I had stayed away for 42 years. Everybody was very gracious, but after my experience there, I just don't feel very close to it."

Houston said he had not intended to write for young readers, but his literary agent, Boston-based Wendy Strothman, convinced him that such a book would have a bigger impact, and now he's happy with it. He took a sabbatical in 2003 and wrote the novel, which had been in short-story form, in about three months. ''It was like a dream," he said. ''I loved writing it."

''The '50s are so remote to young people today," Strothman said in a telephone interview, ''and the book brings that era alive, with telling details about life under segregation and a great boy at the center." Houston's style is ''limpid," she said. ''Its matter-of-fact tone gives it power. If someone screams, we tend not to listen."

Houston will be speaking and reading at bookstores and other settings and plans to continue to write fiction. Asked if there was anything he hoped young readers would take away from ''New Boy," he said: ''that everyone has the ability to seek justice, even though it may not come immediately. The worst thing is to sit by and allow injustice to take place, and to do nothing."
