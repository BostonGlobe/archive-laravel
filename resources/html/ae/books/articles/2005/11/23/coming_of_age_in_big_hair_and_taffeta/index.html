Coming of age in big hair and taffeta

The book 'Bar Mitzvah Disco' captures a generation's rite of passage

By Douglas Belkin, Globe Staff  |  November 23, 2005

Sara Solfanelli will own up to the MC Hammer-inspired baggy pants and the ''Thriller"-era Michael Jackson vest. As embarrassing as they seem to her now, they were, back when she wore them to her 1990 bat mitzvah dance party, the height of middle school couture. But the dress she wore to the ceremony -- a silky floral print with puffy sleeves and mock turtleneck collar that made her look like a 5-foot-tall bouquet with hairspray -- Solfanelli insists, was her mother's doing.

''We went to every dressmaker in town," Solfanelli said. ''My mother had this vision for that dress -- we couldn't find it anywhere -- so she finally had it made. But I swear to you, it was all her idea."

And so, carefully glued into her oversize scrapbook, Solfanelli sits, 13 years old, hair sprayed high, swathed in flowers, a poster child for adolescent awkwardness.

It is, by all rights, a picture that should be carefully guarded -- taken out only among the closest of friends and shown covertly to family members in whose intimate confidence such a moment can be trusted.

But a couple of years ago Solfanelli, now an attorney at a white-shoe Boston law firm, was approached by a college friend. Jules Shell was assembling a collection of bar and bat mitzvah photos for a kitschy website. She remembered Solfanelli's story about the dress and the look that came over her face when she told it. Would she donate a photo?

Solfanelli hemmed and hawed but finally relented. When she did, she unwittingly began her international modeling debut while hitching a ride on the latest spasm of big-hair-era nostalgia to pulse through Generation X.

The website, put up by Shell and friends Roger Bennett and Nick Kroll, was quickly picked up by Gawker.com along with a tag line asking '70s- and '80s-era bar and bat mitzvah participants to submit their shots. Thousands of pictures arrived, and three years later about 300 have been published in ''Bar Mitzvah Disco," a new book that is quickly becoming a cultural touchstone for the 20- and 30-somethings raised in the suburbs, Jewish and gentile alike.

''I think there's a statute of limitations on humiliation," Solfanelli said. ''At some point you have to say, hey, that's not really me anymore, right? But then you look close, and of course it's me."

Constructed like a coffee table book with a series of short essays about the million adolescent heartbreaks and paper cuts that heal but never quite go away, ''Bar Mitzvah Disco" is an examination of crossing cultural currents. In the 1970s and '80s, second- and third-generation American Jews were moving out of cities and into the suburbs. Their children were, in many cases, the first generation raised in integrated, largely secular communities without the overhanging threat of anti-Semitism. Bar and bat mitzvahs, the religious rite of passage made by Jewish boys and girls, became a way for parents to express their upward social mobility in increasingly over-the-top celebrations of excess.

And there, capturing it all for posterity: the bar mitzvah photographer. Big, bad hair; girls awkwardly towering over their dance partners; braces; acne; fat ties; skinny ties; Dolly Parton impersonators; and so, so much taffeta. Among its charms, the book is a record of a last gasp of relative innocence for a generation whose primary cultural currency became a deep and abiding sense of irony.

''It's about bar mitzvahs but it's not about bar mitzvahs," said Bennett, an executive at a Jewish philanthropy in New York. ''It's the perfect prism into who we are and how we got to be that way. It's about adolescence and pop culture and the move to the suburbs, and it's about A Flock of Seagulls and the social pressure in 1982 that everyone who grew up at that time felt."

The book is divided into chapters titled with songs from the day. Interspersed are quotes from adults looking back at the photos from their youth, recalling the first time they ''got to second base," covertly slurped down a Tom Collins, or tried to break dance. Unmistakably entwined, a palpable sense of relief that's it's over.

''I was a giant pair of lips attached to gangly legs topped off by my huge, naturally curly hair, with an enormous bow," wrote Jill Barnett, who was bat mitzvahed in Ohio in 1987. ''I looked like a Q-tip. As a public service, my parents should have kept me indoors."

''I think my generation, which came of age in the '80s, were just more awkward than kids today," said Abbey Orlofsky, who grew up in New York and has lived in the Back Bay for the past five years. In the book she is pictured wearing a taffeta gown and matching homemade taffeta socks. ''You had to have the socks -- it was all about the socks," she said. ''I can laugh now, but we had no idea how horrific we looked. Today the kids just seem, I don't know, more manicured."

The introduction for the book sets the tone. It is, fittingly, written by the Village People, authors of that epic '80s theme song ''YMCA," who economically summarize the book's transcendence of religion. ''When we were asked to write the foreword of this book we were both honored and a little confused," the band wrote. ''First of all, while we are all artists with a wide array of creative talents, none of us is an author. Second, none of us had a Bar Mitzvah. Third, not one of us is even Jewish."

Among the Sharis and Stacys and Aarons and Davids who grew up and contributed photos, there is a short essay by Jonathan Safran Foer, author of ''Everything Is Illuminated," about the fleeting nature of adolescent social standing and trying to cop his first feel. Comedian Sarah Silverman adds her recollections about kissing on the dance floor and her father's concerns that she might get a reputation.

Seeking to get the book out in the public eye, Bennett called up Dov Charney, owner of American Apparel, to ask if he'd stock a few copies in his stores. Charney fell in love with the photographs and is using them as window displays across the country and in Europe. He said he has no doubt they will be well received overseas.

''These photos are Jewish-American, but they're really just American," Charney said. ''It's part of who we are. It's how we learned to party."

Look closely at the photos on display in the American Apparel store in the Back Bay and among them you'll see one of 13-year-old Sarah Solfanelli, entombed in flowers, posing grandly next to a bouquet her mother picked out so it would match her dress.

Is she embarrassed? ''I think it's hilarious. It's the sort of thing that's embarrassing but you can laugh at it. If you're not embarrassed by what you were wearing when you were 13 you probably weren't having too much fun."
