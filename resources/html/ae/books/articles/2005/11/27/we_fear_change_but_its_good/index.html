SELF-HELP

We fear change. But it's good.

By Naomi Rand  |  November 27, 2005

Remember when you dreamed of leading a life of adventure? Perhaps you wanted to climb Mount Everest, or hitch out to Hollywood and wait tables in the hopes of getting that big break. Then real life got in the way. Don't despair. Your life isn't nearly over. Read ''You Can Do It!: The Merit Badge Handbook for Grown-Up Girls," by Lauren Catuzzi Grandcolas. Inside are instructions that can help even the most anxious couch potatoes flex their fledgling wings. This handbook gives straightforward advice for those who want to become, well, just about everything, from skydiver to stand-up comedienne. In between you can learn how to rock out and start your own business. No, you're probably not going to become the next Ellen DeGeneres, but this book assumes, rightly, that getting there is half the fun. There are self-awarded merit badges and loads of supportive ''you can do it" talk. It's a fairly weighty read, but then, there are lots of dreams out there. So if your daily life is a round of gulping down coffee and heading in to work, only to head home again and pass out in front of the tube, buy a copy and put it on your coffee table. Leaf through at your leisure and see what inspires you. It's likely that even the most iconoclastic vision of who you can be if you dare is sandwiched inside these extremely snappily designed covers. Or, as Lauren's sisters put it (Lauren died on 9/11, and this book, her brainchild, was completed as a tribute), ''Allow yourself to think bigger than life because even the most fantastic dreams have something to tell us about who we are -- and who we may yet become."

''It's Called a Breakup Because It's Broken: The Smart Girl's Breakup Buddy," by Greg Behrendt and Amiira Ruotola-Behrendt, encourages a wholly different kind of transformation. In this advice-laden volume, the Behrendt husband-and-wife team bucks you up by giving you a dose of harsh reality. No, the supposed love of your life really doesn't want to hear from you. So please stop e-mailing, calling, and/or showing up at his front door. This is a self-help book for the self-flagellator. The Behrendts insist that the best thing you can do is keep your distance -- literally. Thus the no-contact rule: 60 days of going without seeing or speaking to your ex. According to them, it's ''necessary for total recovery." Of course many who suffer from willful blindness about the nature of their non-relationship will find it excruciating to go even a day without contact. They'll do anything, including picking up the dry-cleaning and housing their ex-boyfriend's mom to maintain the illusion of intimacy. This book is for them. Even though the title redundantly appears as advice in literally every chapter, it's still right on target. If you or someone you love is going back again and again to that very dry well, read this book. It just may help get you or yours back on track.

And now for something completely different, although the title is deceptive. When I picked up ''The Sanctity of Marriage Handbook," by Bryan Harris, I wasn't clear what I was getting into. But once I began to read, I was hooked. This is a self-help book for blue staters. Inside are biographical notes on certain well-known conservatives. All, according to Harris, have ''cast the first stone." These are ''the men and women who have won seats in Congress and won the hearts (and votes) of evangelicals across the country, the media pundits who have saturated the radio and television airwaves with their hypocrisy." He takes them apart nimbly, pointing out the huge divide between what is said to the public and what is done in private. Then he totes up their transgressions and gives each and every one their own ''Morality Score." Perhaps it's not a relationship solver, but it would still make a great stocking stuffer for that conservative friend of yours who hates duplicity. And it does have one thing in common with the other two books: Harris also believes that change is better than stagnation, and if he were asked, I believe he'd tell you, ''Real change begins at home."

Naomi Rand's third Emma Price mystery is ''It's Raining Men."
