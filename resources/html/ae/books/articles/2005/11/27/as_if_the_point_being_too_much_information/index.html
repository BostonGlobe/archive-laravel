As if. The point being? Too much information

A cultural critic takes aim at the catchphrases that infect our speech. Not that there's anything wrong with that.

By Richard Eder  |  November 27, 2005

Slam Dunks and No-Brainers: Language in Your Life, the Media, Business, Politics, and, Like, Whatever
By Leslie Savan
Knopf, 340 pp., $23.95

''Don't go there," Leslie Savan warns her baby, who is on a crawl toward the garbage can.

Garbage can? Pandora's box, more likely. A mother's literal injunction releases a swarm of American pop phrases in her writer's mind. They buzz, they gnaw into her speech and ours in a jargon somewhere between intellectual and emotional shorthand and a quick-fix jolt.

First, in her ''Slam Dunks and No-Brainers," a partial list: I don't think so, go for it, bling-bling, hel-lo?, you're history, get a life, and -- no longer a kitchen-floor crisis but a dismissive, airy injunction -- don't go there. A page or two later, list turns into paroxysm:

''Yo! It's the buzzphrases, stupid. Is this a no-brainer, or what? What we need is an in-your-face, wake-up-and-smell-the-java look at why literally gazillions of us talkmeisters are talkin' the talk -- big-time. I mean, get real. These words and phrases and attitudes are eating us for breakfast. We're toast. And we're eating their dust. Or is it their shorts? Bottom line, we need to ask, What's wrong with this picture?"

Savan writes not so much in judgment as in testimony -- testimony by someone agitatedly brainwashed in part, and in part resistantly unwashed. It is more performance than exposition; a kind of minatory St. Vitus dance that conveys illness by its jiggles and, in 300 pages of examples that start bright but come to seem interminable, is apt to get the reader jiggling illy, as well.

Surfacing in the writer's tumultuous chop, though, is a dorsal fin of argument, perceptive and witty, about the damage done by our easy and percussively catchy pop language.

''An army of brave new words is occupying our social life with coast-to-coast attitude," she writes. ''The catchwords, phrases, inflections, and quickie concepts that Americans seem unable to communicate without have grown into a verbal kudzu, overlaying regional differences with a national (even an international) pop accent that tells us more about how we think than we think."

She presents it as a McLanguage: fun, reassuring, and, like fast food, aiming for the thought equivalent of the basic flavors -- sweet, salty, fat -- and eliminating all in-between nuance. That there is a wit, a bite, a speed to pop phrases is evident; Savan compares older clichés to the new ones: ''when all is said and done" replaced by ''net net," ''old hat" by ''so five minutes ago."

Seemingly irreverent and individual, though, their nationwide usage, ''each modular phrase is part of a franchise deal," points to a deeper need for connection. ''The attitude -- or, more precisely, the platitude as attitude -- is emboldened by the knowledge that, if properly phrased, it will resonate with millions. Whether biting or benign, what all pop phrases have in common is the roar of a phantom crowd. They always speak of other people having spoken them."

There is nothing new about the use of the equivalent of pop language to convey an attitude or mark a vogue. Savan goes back to Shakespeare and comes forward: ''tacky" is 100 years old, there was ''unreal" in the 1920s, ''cheesy" and ''hot" in the 1930s. A difference is the vastly expanded range of the media, and the instant seizing upon of pop turns of phrase by commercial advertising.

She writes of the immense contribution of black English: ''kick back," ''dig," ''do your own thing," ''tell it like it is." She notes its eager appropriation by whites in need, at least superficially, of an aura of edge and verbal dissidence. Looking, as she puts it, for ''their inner black." Perhaps she takes her train of thought a stop or so too far as she recalls Senator Patrick Leahy's ''Chill, Orrin," to Senator Hatch, his Senate Judiciary Committee opponent. ''One white guy was momentarily able to harness and aim some soulful black power at the other white guy."

She writes of ''halo" pop -- the jargonish complacency of ''community," ''empowerment," ''giving back" -- and macho pop. She goes on at wearying length to illustrate her points by television, film, and even movie trailers; the effect can be interment by examples.

To better effect, she writes of weapons pop: ''what part of . . . don't you understand?," ''excuse me," ''whatever" (as dismissal -- it can also be conciliatory). They carry armor; Savan calls them conversational SUVs. ''They can make drivers feel insulated from life's potholes, and they block the vision of those traversing streets or sentences by less bulked-up means." In its sharp-edged simplification, its either-or quality, pop language is part of a broader digital phenomenon; eliminating the nuances, the wanderings, the associations of an analog world. Nowhere does she argue this more tellingly than in an allusion to President Bush's digitalization of international choice.

''If you're not with 'us,' " she writes with an awful quietness, ''chances are actually rather high that you're not with the terrorists."

Richard Eder reviews books for several publications.
