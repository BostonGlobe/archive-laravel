ON SPORTS

Passion, salvation, and the souls of Ravens

By Bill Littlefield  |  November 20, 2005

The Lion in Autumn: A Season With Joe Paterno and Penn State Football
By Frank Fitzpatrick
Gotham, 299 pp., illustrated, $26

Next Man Up: A Year Behind the Lines in Today’s NFL
By John Feinstein
Little, Brown, 502 pp., illustrated, $25.95

Only With Passion: Figure Skating’s Most Winning Champion on Competition and Life
By Katarina Witt with E. M. Swift
PublicAffairs, 168 pp., illustrated, $23.50

Golﬁng With God: A Novel of Heaven and Earth
By Roland Merullo
Algonquin, 274 pp., $23.95

Joe Paterno is not a representative college football coach. Most college football coaches don't have libraries on their campuses named after them. Most college football coaches don't remain employed at the same university for half a century; even if they've won national championships, most college football coaches don't survive even a few disappointing seasons in a row. And very few head coaches laboring in the belly of the college football beast would say, as Paterno has said, ''If I were a kid today and someone said to me, 'You're good enough to play big-time college football,' I'm not sure I'd do it."

''The Lion in Autumn," Frank Fitzpatrick's unauthorized biography of Joe Paterno, finds Paterno battling through one of those disappointing seasons. In 2004 the team finished 4-7, and though Paterno maintained that Penn State was just a player or two short of glory redux, Fitzpatrick had no trouble finding alumni who wanted the 77-year-old icon fired.

Given Penn State's giddy ascension to gridiron respectability this fall, it's tempting to conclude that the once again lovable ''JoePa" is enjoying the last laugh. On the other hand, it's hard to imagine the driven soul Fitzpatrick gives us in ''The Lion in Autumn" laughing about anything involving a football.

While Fitzpatrick was nosing around Happy Valley, home of the Nittany Lions, pursuing a head coach who wouldn't talk to him, John Feinstein was spending the year with the considerably more hospitable Baltimore Ravens of the NFL. Feinstein picked the Ravens for the subject of ''Next Man Up" in part because general manager Ozzie Newsome and head coach Brian Billick offered him greater access than any other team he considered, and most of the players and coaches are candid with the author. Feinstein reciprocates by concluding that even the Raven who pleaded guilty to obstructing justice in a murder investigation and the one who went to jail for drug offenses are really good guys.

Like Penn State, Baltimore rewarded the writer along for the ride with a disappointing season on the field. But the history of the Ravens provides Feinstein with some intriguing vignettes, such as the day when Billick lectured the media on its lack of legal expertise, concluding that no reporter had any right to ask about any player's criminal activities. ''I have the podium," the coach tells the press, ''and you-all are here to listen to me."

Then there's the tale of the fierce battle between two rival evangelical sects for the souls of the Ravens -- a contest that threatens to upstage football altogether until the coach sides with the team chaplain and boots his competition out of the locker room.

Feinstein acknowledges at the start that he's been a fan of the NFL since childhood, and maintains that the pro game always ''has had an aura about it." Some readers may wonder if a writer less enamored of the league's aura would have taken a harder look at a culture where almost nobody retires uninjured, and lots of players routinely get shot full of painkillers so they can wobble out to the field and demonstrate their determination to keep their jobs.

Maybe you remember Katarina Witt as the skater who won gold medals at both the 1984 and '88 Olympics. Maybe you remember her as the 27-year-old who made one last Olympic appearance in 1994 to skate a program designed to protest the destruction of Sarajevo. With the help of Sports Illustrated senior writer E. M. Swift, Witt recalls those days in ''Only With Passion." Witt tells the story of her career and her life to a fictional girl she calls Jasmine, a skater having trouble deciding whether she wants to devote her life to the sport. The book is entertaining and not without surprises, such as when Witt maintains that hatred for her coach was important to her development as a competitor, and admits she would pray before important competitions as follows: ''God, please, please, let me win. I'll go to church then, I promise."

I wonder where the dueling evangelical groups in the Baltimore Ravens locker room would have come down on that pre-game ritual.

Not surprisingly, God plays a major role in Roland Merullo's ''Golfing With God." Merullo's heaven, wherein a fair portion of the novel occurs, is a delightful place, featuring ''several thousand Guinness breweries" and ''no chapels or churches . . . the idea being that everything is already holy and so it doesn't make sense to single out one place or another for worship."

''Golfing With God" may strike some readers as a little out of bounds when the narrator contends that golf is his ''route to salvation," but there is comfort in the passage where he learns that he's earned afterlife points in part because he has loved his work. ''You devoted yourself to it," he is told. ''God doesn't forget a thing like that."

Of such fragile, glimmering strands is hope woven.

Bill Littlefield hosts NPR's ''Only a Game" each Saturday from WBUR in Boston.
