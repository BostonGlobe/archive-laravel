The Mozart of the game plan

By Alan Berger  |  November 20, 2005

The Education of a Coach
By David Halberstam
Hyperion, 277 pp., $24.95

As the old communists would say, it is no accident that professional football has become an allegory of social Darwinism in the globe's sole surviving superpower. More than baseball, basketball, or stock car racing, the game played on Sunday afternoons and Monday evenings in our autumns and winters dramatizes the American credo of competition. The French critic who said that to know the heart and soul of America one had to understand baseball was talking about a bygone era. Football has become the sporting paradigm of the present. Global competition waged by organizations with intricate and rapidly shifting analyses of the playing field is the reality reflected on the gridiron.

The marketing merlins who have made the NFL into a fabulously lucrative business organized on semi-socialist principles, at least in their sharing of TV revenues, have also engineered matters so that last year's winners have the lowest picks in the college draft and have to play the hardest schedules. Consequently, what happens over the course of a football season resembles the Afghan sport of buzkashi more than the genteel pastimes of baseball or cricket. Of their traditional contest, which is played on horseback by riders fighting to take possession of the headless carcass of a calf or goat, Afghans say: ''In buzkashi only one man wins -- and he not for very long."

It may be annoying to anyone in America who is indifferent to the mystique of our special secular holiday, Super Bowl Sunday, but that spectacle has become a celebration of globalized capitalism triumphant. So it is no accident that in ''The Education of a Coach," David Halberstam, author of books about America's wars, institutions, and sports, would turn his reporter's curiosity on the most successful professional football coach of the current era, Bill Belichick.

Sagely, Halberstam never reaches for the allegorical meaning of Belichick's success. He doesn't have to. Suggestive hints flare up from the straightforward story the author tells. Halberstam mentions in passing that the Patriots' coach, who despises the all-distorting glare of the media limelight, does give well-paid talks on leadership to the most gullible of groups in a nation of self-improvers: corporate CEOs. What could the captains of postindustrial society possibly hope to learn from the militantly uncharismatic Belichick, whom sports reporters cited by Halberstam describe as a ''man of chalk"? No doubt, they hope to hear how he motivates his workers to be the most productive in their league, how he persuades so many disparate individuals to sacrifice their personal interests for the good of the collective, perhaps how he rids the payroll of overpaid stars and stocks his roster with ardent, hard-working team players.

But it is one of the many sensible qualities of Halberstam's portraiture that he never pretends to penetrate the inner sanctum of Belichick's profession. Readers who are also Patriots fans and who may be peeved at the failure of some beat writers to appreciate the coach's winning qualities are sure to savor Halberstam's assessment of Belichick's virtues. Among these are an adamant refusal to play the role of a General Patton, the persona that some team owners and too many knights of the keyboard have come to expect in a coach; his appetite for hard work; his analytical realism about his players, their opponents, and his own coaching; his mentoring of young coaches; his self-discipline; his ability to stay calm on the sidelines in the heat of a game; and his stubborn rejection of the cult of celebrity that others are ceaselessly trying to foist on him.

Nonetheless, these are mere personality traits; they are not the keys to Belichick's success. Halberstam repeats what he has been told about those keys by the coach's colleagues and peers. So we know, in layman's terms, that nobody is more deft at breaking down film of his own team or of opponents. We are told that from the start Belichick has had his own signature approach to the study of film. Whereas other coaches searched for an opponent's weakness and ways to exploit it, Belichick has inveterately sought out the source of the enemy's strength and then devised schemes to take that strength away. In biblical terms, this may be described as the Delilah formula.

The book lives up to its title, recounting the experiences and phases that formed the education of a coach. The family history sketched by Halberstam includes the instructive life of Belichick's immigrant grandfather, who came to this country from Croatia and worked in the steel mills of Youngstown, Ohio. Hard work and a seriousness of purpose were not abstract ideals in Belichick's background but the very tools of survival. Unlike some sportswriters who have treated Belichick as a precious preppy, Halberstam discovers the grandson of a steelworker who was a strong union man, a legacy that helps explain why Belichick does not suffer gladly the fools who flock around a celebrity coach. At the age of 9, Belichick was helping his father, Steve, a longtime coach at the Naval Academy, break down game film. The subject of Halberstam's portrait, who may be the hardest-working monomaniac in a profession of workaholics, is also depicted as a Mozart of the game plan.

The great value of Halberstam's exploration of the coaching profession and Belichick's place in it is the writer's refusal to strike the facile pose of a cynic. Yet this is not a work of hero worship. Halberstam is trying to understand and make understandable how Belichick became, at least for a short while, the best at a devilishly difficult profession. The writer cannot take the reader into the film room to explicate the mysteries of disguised zone defenses, but he does paint a persuasive portrait of a man who has become the coach of coaches, the embattled horseman who for the moment clasps the headless goat carcass on his saddle.

Alan Berger is a Globe editorial writer.
