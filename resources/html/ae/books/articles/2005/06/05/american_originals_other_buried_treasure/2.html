American originals, other buried treasure

By Katherine A. Powers  |  June 5, 2005

Tucked amid the blockbusters of yesteryear and all the pifﬂe of the past infesting the bookshelves of summer cottages lie inconspicuous treasures, the objects of esoteric and fanatical veneration. Have these potent volumes been planted there by crafty proselytizers and cognoscenti, or simply abandoned by the innocent? We may never know. What is certain, though, is that just the title of such a book uttered in the presence of another initiate will cause eyes to blaze and lock, pulses to frolic in companionable zeal, and voices to join in a babble. They are not quite cult books, but curious works of genius, gems lost in the sand that have been stumbled upon and treasured, stockpiled and disseminated, by their ardent admirers over the years.

Katherine A. Powers, a writer and critic, lives in Cambridge. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@earthlink.net.

Masters of Atlantis
by Charles Portis (Overlook, paperback, $14.95)
First published in 1985, this is one of our country's greatest comic novels by one of its greatest living writers, and yet only the faithful few know about it or him. Portis wrote "True Grit," a dandy little book upon which the movie most people have seen was based. But "Masters of Atlantis" is the place to start for the would-be illuminati, for it, itself, is about a cult, a strange and dozy one, with a certain Mr. Jimmerson of Indiana as its founder. Beyond that it is a tour de force of American idiom, a festival of bathos, and a celebration of the melancholy that boosterism, get-smart-quick schemes, and the spirit of commerce can never conquer.

The Third Policeman
by Flann O'Brien (Dalkey Archive, paperback, $12.95)
This surreal murder mystery was written around 1940 but not published until 1967, after the Irish author's death. It is a comic novel of fantastic conceits, representing Irish science at full boil. Its footnotes are dexterously satiric and concern the philosopher de Selby, who argued that night is the result of "accumulations of 'black air.' " Indeed, theories of every sort abound throughout, and one in particular will cause you to think twice about riding your bicycle. O'Brien's prose is a peerless combination of deft Gaelic syntax and bureaucratic stuffiness, while the book's central mystery is, to borrow a certain Sergeant Pluck's formulation, "a conundrum of inscrutable potentialities, a snorter."

The Diary of a Nobody by George and Weedon Grossmith (various publishers; also available for download at onlinebooks.library. upenn.edu) First published in 1892, this is the faithful record of the triumphs, travails, exigencies, and bons mots of one Mr. Pooter, an uxorious, house-proud accountant, recently moved to the Laurels, Brickfield Terrace, Holloway. It was, with the exception of his own work, the only book that Hillaire Belloc would read in his vast old age. A miracle of controlled absurdity, Mr. Pooter's life embodies, in its small and minutely detailed way, man's primordial struggle to maintain his dignity. Pooter's trials are many, a refractory foot scraper being the first and his son Lupin, with his fast ways and "the Daisy Mutlar business," the most enduring.

How to Do Things Right: The Revelations of a Fussy Man by L. Rust Hills (Godine, paperback, $15.95) This brings together three books, published between 1972 and 1976, under the title, roughly, of the first, the other two being "How to Retire at Forty-One or, Life Among the Routines and Pursuits and Other Problems" and "How to Be Good or, The Somewhat Tricky Business of Attaining Moral Virtue in Society That's Not Just Corrupt but Corrupting, Without Being Completely Out-of-It." It is the only self-help book anyone should ever need. The essentials are here, including "how to cut down on smoking and drinking quite so much" and how to lead an examined, orderly life, and not shrivel up and fade away from loneliness and despair.

Flashman: From the Flashman Papers, 1839-1842 by George MacDonald Fraser (Plume, paperback, $14)
Published in 1969, this is the first and one of the very best in the ever-lengthening series of historical novels starring Harry Flashman, coward, cynic, cheat, lecher, and indefatigable opportunist. Here he is thrown into England's first Afghan campaign -- a disaster described with historical accuracy and magnificent verisimilitude. (Topical and illuminating, this is the stuff out of which summer-evening conversations are made.) Flashman's dyspeptic view of the world and his supreme caddishness lead him to shirk his duty at every juncture and, if need be, betray his fellows. But such is his luck that here, as at other crises of British history, old Flashy ends up celebrated a hero.

The Long Day Wanes: A Malayan Trilogy by Anthony Burgess (Norton, paperback, $14.95)
Published between 1956 and 1959, the trilogy is set in the last days of British rule in Malaya (now Malaysia). The profitable result of his own time in the country, it is Burgess's best and funniest work, though, inexplicably, not always in print. Snap it up now and meet the unforgettable Nobby Adams, "six feet eight inches of thirst" with "eagles of debt and drink pecking at his liver." Meet, too, Victor Crabbe, the main character, an English schoolmaster and civil servant whose marriage is deliquescing in the heat and squalor. The novel has an enormous, wickedly drawn cast of characters of diverse races, religions, nationalities, proclivities, and peccadilloes. It is splendid.

From a Darkened Room: The Inman Diary edited by Daniel Aaron (Harvard University, paperback, $17.95)
This distills 155 volumes of journals kept by the odious, addictively readable Arthur Inman from 1918 to his death, in 1963. His object was to describe honestly everything he saw, did, and thought, with curiosity and loathing his dominant emotions. Selfcentered and hypochondriacal, he kept to his darkened room in Boston's Back Bay, exasperating those who looked after his endless needs. He paid people to tell him about their public and intimate lives, often enough engaging with the women sexually. All is recorded, as are his observations on national and world events, creating a teeming, idiosyncratic picture of America unlike anything you will ever read.

My Dog Tulip by J. R. Ackerley (New York Review Books, paperback, $12.95)
"Dogs read the world through their noses and write their history in urine," writes the author of this passionate memoir of Tulip, a female German shepherd. This is the funniest, most poignant, and -- consider yourself warned -- preeminently disgusting of all the great dog books. First published in 1965, it portrays in the most affectionate terms what the dogless tend to consider outrageous transgressions. After recounting in unsparing and festive detail a series of early befoulings of private and public spaces, Ackerley smooths it all over indulgently. After all, Tulip "was young and a shade irresponsible and our love was new."

Ride a Cockhorse by Raymond Kennedy (Hardscrabble, paperback, $15.95)
Here the humdrum workings of a community bank are blown sky high by a megalomaniac middle- aged loan officer. Mrs. Frankie Fitzgibbons finds, almost overnight, that she is possessed of a gargantuan sexual urge, powerful eloquence, and love of dominion. She takes a teenage drum major as her lover and, at work, scythes through the staff and instills a spirit of demented finance capital. "This is the actual world," she lectures her dowdy, social-activist daughter. "There is no hole in the ozone layer here, or baby seals being murdered. . . . We're not killer bees." Well, not quite. First published in 1991 and written in snappy, exuberant prose that positively soars, this is a genuine American original.

Dance of the Dwarfs by Geoffrey Household
Though esteemed by a committed minority, this novel, published in 1968, is in limbo right now -- only available used, from libraries, or, of course, from summer-cottage shelves. Exquisitely disturbing and downright terrifying in places, it purports to be the journal of a British agronomist whose skeleton has been found entwined with that of a young woman at an experimental station in Colombia. We begin to take in the horror of how this man met his end in his record of increasing unease. Villagers are frightened but won't talk. He feels he is watched. A corpse is found in the jungle, two holes in the base of its skull. Dwarf-like creatures are glimpsed . . . dancing -- or what?
