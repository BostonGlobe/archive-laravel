Field guide to the words

Identifying the season's best reading, for escapist and serious-minded alike

By Gail Caldwell  |  June 5, 2005

Silly with techno-fatigue, the hedgehog all but staggered from his den. He had been trying to allay his anxieties -- he worried a lot about the destruction of the rain forest -- by playing mindless computer games, and now he was churlish and cotton-headed, and not a little ashamed of himself. His tumble into the brighter world landed him at the foot of the wise old owl, whom he was delighted to see and who gazed upon the little hedgehog with serene, if superior, countenance: Our hopeful thing with feathers spent his own leisure time in an owl-like version of pranayama.

''Read me a poem!" cried the hedgehog, hungry for the music of the spheres.

''Whoooom do you prefer?" asked his erudite friend, who prided himself on quoting, from memory, Wendell Berry's ''The Peace of Wild Things." ''Whitman, to electrify you? Larkin or Bishop, to shake your soul from its torpor? The sonnets of Neruda, or the majesty of Rilke? Or perhaps you prefer something simple yet elegant, like Keats's 'Ode to a Nightingale,' or" -- here the owl feigned humility -- ''Baudelaire's 'The Owls'?"

Properly humbled, relieved to be free of his own layabout impulses, the hedgehog nestled in. ''Anything you choose," he replied. ''I need only the consolation of words -- words that actually mean something, and that sound as mellifluous and well-intentioned in their pairing as the rain upon my den."

Now, the owl was impressed by the hedgehog's use of simile, however sentimental, and so he decided to reward his friend with a double reading: poetry for the heart, and a good detective novel for the head -- perhaps Mitch Cullin's homage to an aging Sherlock Holmes, ''A Slight Trick of the Mind." This combination, he felt sure, would satisfy the hedgehog's escapist needs and keep him, at least for a while, off the games.com website.

Some readers may remember the hedgehog and his literary mentor from occasional summers past, when the two have emerged to light the reading path for the months ahead. The hedgehog, of course, is a straight man for the high-flying rhetoric of the owl (who, let's face it, is a bit of a show-off). Together, their tastes and idiosyncrasies help us through the maze of summer reading that awaits.

Because of their kindness and affection for one another, though, our duo will have no need of Mike Albo and Virginia Heffernan's cruel and delicious ''The Underminer: The Best Friend Who Casually Destroys Your Life." The season is rife with that new subgenre of self-help that feigns something higher (our favorite title is ''You: The Owner's Manual"), with particular gender slots being filled by James Atlas (''My Life in the Middle Ages: A Survivor's Tale") and Sue Shellenbarger (''The Breaking Point: How Female Mid-Life Crisis Is Transforming Today's Women"). Readers who prefer plain old dysfunctional family memoir, where they can revel in someone else's problems, might look to Jeannette Walls's ''The Glass Castle" or Francine du Plessix Gray's ''Them: A Memoir of Parents." John Sacret Young, who produced the heartfelt Vietnam TV series ''China Beach," has written a memoir about the war's effect on his own family in ''Remains: Non-Viewable."

Still, the lion's share of summer reading will belong to fiction, where all bets are off and where utter invention, without fear of reprisal, rules the day. The last few months have offered exquisite choices, none darker than Scottish writer A. L. Kennedy's harrowing ''Paradise," told in the first person by a woman alcoholic headed toward the cliff. The happier end of the road belongs to Ian McEwan, who, in ''Saturday," has captured the universe like a firefly in a glass -- one man, one day, contemporary London.

With its post-9/11 grappling with history and tragedy, Jonathan Safran Foer's ''Extremely Loud and Incredibly Close" is the most imposing novel of the season, though some will find the narrative antics of its precocious boy protagonist both wearying and erudite. In ''Beyond Black," British writer Hilary Mantel writes a comic send-up of the psychic circuit in England. Pleasing some reviewers and confounding others, Kazuo Ishiguro has written a moody, melancholy novel starring lots of clones in his ''Never Let Me Go." And Nicole Krauss, in an accomplished acrobatic feat of interweaving three stories, follows a hidden and forgotten novel to its authorial mecca in ''The History of Love."

Peter Pouncey, too, entwined stories inside one another in his debut novel, ''Rules for Old Men Waiting," though his tale of an aging Scot facing death is one of commendably simple elegance: a war story hidden within the memories and regrets of a fine old soldier. Sue Miller easily inhabits the domestic realm of her best fiction in ''Lost in the Forest," while Sue Monk Kidd rewards faithful readers of ''The Secret Life of Bees" with another piece of family magic and conflict in ''The Mermaid Chair." Alice Hoffman's particular corner on the intimate stuff of dreams is in full display in ''The Ice Queen," and Jacquelyn Mitchard turns up the heat a little on family melodrama in ''The Breakdown Lane."

But if every unhappy family, as Tolstoy famously wrote, is unhappy in its own way, that particularity of misery becomes even more pointed through the eyes of a child. Two novels relayed from this place of innocence are Alicia Erian's ''Towelhead," about a 13-year-old girl of a cross-cultural marriage, and ''Me & Emma," by Elizabeth Flock, the story of a girl's escape from familial abuse through an alliance with her little sister. Even these sorrows, though, are no match for the drama witnessed by the 17-year-old sniper in Scott Simon's ''Pretty Birds," a novel drawn from the NPR correspondent's time covering the war in Sarajevo. Novelist Philip Caputo has made a career out of his fearless writing about war, and ''Acts of Faith," set against the civil war in Sudan, is his most impassioned in years.

After all this sturm und drang and firepower, some readers will prefer the tamer, sparer realm of short story collections, and the season has been generous with them: James Salter's slim, grim ''Last Night," Roxana Robinson's oblique sadnesses in ''A Perfect Stranger," Amy Hempel's finely tuned ''The Dog of the Marriage." Ann Beattie, whose voice can be meticulous in the story form, displays that gift in her new collection, ''Follies." And in ''Mother of Sorrows," Richard McCann braids together tales of a gay boy's coming of age with bittersweet regard.

Summer used to be known as the off season for publishers, who believed they should have their best and brightest in the stores by May. That no longer holds true, and some of the year's most awaited titles will appear in the next eight weeks. Michael Cunningham's eagerly awaited ''Specimen Days" (see review on Page D8) follows parallel stories, as did ''The Hours," though this novel takes Walt Whitman as its beacon instead of Virginia Woolf. Nick Hornby returns with his fourth novel, ''A Long Way Down," in which he has managed to write a witty, captivating novel about four strangers who meet as they're each plotting to end it all. John Burdett returns with a sequel to his much-lauded ''Bangkok 8"; this one, ''Bangkok Tattoo," is more labyrinthine and less rewarding. Melissa Bank, who charmed readers a few years ago with her debut, ''The Girl's Guide to Hunting and Fishing," delivers a family saga through a young woman's identity crisis in ''The Wonder Spot."

July brings John Irving's ''Until I Find You," an 800-plus-page novel about lost fathers and tattoos. The Paris Review is generally good at cooking up odd anthologies, so we are particularly looking forward to the summer's happily macabre collection, ''The Paris Review Book of People With Problems." Fans of Sara Paretsky will be glad to see her ''Fire Sale" arrive midsummer; the V. I. Warshawski novel has its female P.I. coaching a girl's basketball team. Cormac McCarthy's ''No Country for Old Men," scheduled for late July, will bring us a dark-hearted tale about blood and honor along the Texas-Mexico border. Finally, August promises a new collection of stories from Aimee Bender (''Willful Creatures") as well as a novel from Bobbie Ann Mason: ''An Atomic Romance." Perhaps because they themselves are fictional creations, the hedgehog and the owl take occasional respite from the demands of their imagined lives by burrowing into nonfiction -- a genre that, from their vantage point, is a bit like travel reading. The owl, naturally, has Malcolm Gladwell's ''Blink" on his reading list, though its subtitle -- ''The Power of Thinking Without Thinking" -- seems rather obvious to him. Still, humans will appreciate Gladwell's usual scattershot intelligence applied to the cognitive process and the unconscious.

Thomas L. Friedman, who has wowed readers for years with his command of the global situation for The New York Times, has a primer for the new, new way of life in ''The World Is Flat: A Brief History of the 21st Century." Stellar geographer Jared Diamond, whose Pulitzer Prize-winning ''Guns, Germs, and Steel" has given us a broader way to grasp the world and our heavy tread upon it, is back with ''Collapse: How Societies Choose to Fail or Succeed," which is both wide and deep enough to occupy plenty of people for the rest of the year.

Two books that will doubtless be spotted on beaches from Gay Head to Boothbay Harbor this summer are Stacy Schiff's account of Franklin's Francophile period, ''A Great Improvisation: Franklin, France, and the Birth of America," and David McCullough's ''1776," capturing the rise of the nation through the chronicle of a single year. Local notable Megan Marshall has focused on a less apparent cornerstone of American history in her commanding intellectual biography, ''The Peabody Sisters: Three Women Who Ignited American Romanticism." A spate of books have appeared recently on Robert Oppenheimer and the Los Alamos group; certainly the biggest and most wide-reaching is ''American Prometheus: The Triumph and Tragedy of J. Robert Oppenheimer," by Kai Bird and Martin J. Sherwin. For a look at the group of physicists sequestered together in their little New Mexico community that would change the world, there is ''109 East Palace: Robert Oppenheimer and the Secret City of Los Alamos," by Jennet Conant.

And finally, two selections from our little chorus of two who started this entire story. Despite a somewhat tempestuous youth, the owl has long been a confirmed bachelor, and so decided to peruse the peculiar world of Stephanie Coontz's ''Marriage, a History: From Obedience to Intimacy, or How Love Conquered Marriage." The hedgehog -- he, too, needs no one but his sidekick to keep him out of trouble -- has chosen more of an apologia for his own scalawag pursuits. Last spotted, he was immersed in Steven Johnson's ''Everything Bad Is Good For You: How Today's Popular Culture Is Actually Making Us Smarter." Johnson's hip polemic gives the hedgehog all the ammunition he needs to lose himself in video games -- ah, but we are happy to say, now we have tricked him into reading about his weakness instead.

Gail Caldwell is chief book critic of the Globe. She can be reached at caldwell@globe.com.
