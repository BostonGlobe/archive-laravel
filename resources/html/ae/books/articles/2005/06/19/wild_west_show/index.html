Wild west show

The rowdy adventures of two 19th-century celebrities

By Michael Joseph Gross  |  June 19, 2005

The Colonel and Little Missie: Buffalo Bill, Annie Oakley, and the Beginnings of Superstardom in America
By Larry McMurtry
Simon & Schuster, 245 pp., illustrated, $26

After Prince Albert's death, Queen Victoria spent 26 years in mourning, during which time she never attended a public entertainment. (When she wanted to see a performance, she invited players to Windsor Castle.) Then, in her Jubilee year of 1887, she emerged -- to see the London engagement of Buffalo Bill's Wild West, the traveling show starring the former frontiersman William F. Cody and the sharpshooter Annie Oakley, and featuring several dozen Indians (among them Mr. and Mrs. Cut Meat and Double Wound), 160 horses, 16 buffalo, some bears, elk, deer, and a 36-piece band.

In ''The Colonel and Little Missie" Larry McMurtry quotes from an account of Queen Victoria's visit, written by a Sioux dancer in Cody's troupe named Nicholas Black Elk:

''We stood right in front of Grandmother England. She was little but fat, and we liked her because she was good to us. After we danced she spoke to us. She said 'I am sixty-seven years old . . . I have seen all kinds of people; but today I have seen the best looking people I know. If you belonged to me, I would not let them take you around in a show like this.'

''We all shook hands with her. Her hand was very little and soft. We gave a big cheer for her and then the shining wagons came in and she got into one of them and they all went away."

The pleasures of ''The Colonel and Little Missie" (its title marries the two primary subjects' nicknames for each other) are found in such passages. McMurtry has an eye for vivid detail, and he sometimes prods a reader's imagination to bring a distant age alive.

However, the book gives little guidance for making sense of such material. McMurtry takes small stabs at explaining why Victoria emerged from mourning to see Buffalo Bill -- for example, she didn't want the animals messing up Windsor Castle. But he bypasses the key political question that could help readers understand the magnitude of Cody's celebrity. (Two million Britons paid to see Cody's show at Earl's Court.) What did the Wild West mean to Victorians? And which of Cody's personal qualities did they covet, or admire?

McMurtry states that ''The Colonel and Little Missie" is not ''a straight-birth-to-death biography" of its subjects. Instead, he seems to want the book to be a cultural history, ''to explain how the two became world celebrities." As the author of revisionist Western novels such as the Pulitzer Prize-winning ''Lonesome Dove" -- and as something of a celebrity himself -- McMurtry would seem an ideal candidate to analyze the particular kind of ''superstardom" that emerged from the West.

His consideration of Oakley is pithy and seems on the mark. Her sharpshooting skill was freakish: She could blast with almost perfect accuracy while riding a bicycle, ''lying full-length on her horse's back, and even . . . standing up on her mount." Her appearance was a paradox of purity and seductiveness (she skipped around arenas like a girl until she was 62 years old, and she often let her ''long, lustrous hair" hang loose). And like the greatest stars, she kept most of her private life secret.

McMurtry's greater interest is in Cody. He devotes about a third of the book to painstaking investigation of the historical accuracy of the stories that Cody told about himself, which became tropes of his traveling show. (Most of these claims -- that he killed his first Indian at age 11, made the third-longest ride in the history of the Pony Express, and killed Tall Bull in 1870 and Yellow Hair in 1876 -- were, at best, questionable.) In so doing, McMurtry demonstrates that Cody's celebrity did not depend ''on the solid facts of his life."

''Cody's fame depended, instead," McMurtry contends, ''on his truly smashing appearance, which only seemed to get better once his hair began to turn white." In addition to this oddly languid insight, McMurtry notes that Cody had the luck to come of age when reliable and rapid transportation became available, and he had an able manager and publicist.

Still more sawhorses are dragged out: Cody ''recognized . . . that 'farewells' always play" -- even his first show was announced as a farewell tour. His show offered ''an escape from reason, common sense, and the daily grind," he used patriotism to boost his appeal, and he presented an idealized past as history.

But Cody's celebrity makes no sense without consideration of the concerns of the people who sustained it: his fans. Why did Buffalo Bill's audience need the idealized past that he offered them? Perhaps, some have argued, Cody embodied the singular, grand myth of the self-made man that Americans wanted to believe in during the rapid industrialization of the late 19th century.

McMurtry steers clear of such issues, instead offering that ''all serious commentators on Cody's career agree on one thing: his shows succeeded -- as, for example, did the TV miniseries of my own novel Lonesome Dove (125 million viewers) -- because of the immense, worldwide appetite for anything pretending to portray life in the old West."

Despite the author's bizarre parenthetical about his own Nielsen ratings, the reader may still hold out hope for some probing answer to the question of why so many stars have won our hearts by pretending to ride the range. After all, since ''The Last Picture Show" in 1966, McMurtry has created a body of work that has depicted the contradictions between American fantasies and realities with uncommon grace.

But in this book, McMurtry raises a dispiriting question about whether his relationship with readers has changed over time, and about his publisher's decision to sell this slender book.

''The thing to remember about this appetite for Westerns and the West," McMurtry writes, ''is that the millions who possess it are entirely uncritical. They'll take anything in buckskins, literally."

Michael Joseph Gross is the author of ''Starstruck: When a Fan Gets Close to Fame." He lives in Los Angeles.
