ON CRIME

Sleuths battling lies, betrayal, and their own senses

By Hallie Ephron  |  April 24, 2005

There's an embarrassment of riches this month for the discriminating crime fiction reader, starting with Peter Abrahams's riveting new thriller, ''Oblivion."

As private investigator Nick Petrov is leaving a Los Angeles murder trial where he's just testified, he's accosted by a young woman who begs him to investigate the disappearance of her teenage daughter. Nick agrees. Over the ensuing weekend, he begins his investigation, and we get a terrifying, insider's view of his mental deterioration. What starts as a ''penny-sized headache" ends with his complete collapse.

Days later, Nick wakes up in a hospital after surgery to remove a malignant brain tumor. The last thing he remembers is the murder trial and the smell of perfume from the witness who testified before him. ''In between stood the lost time, like some dark forest in a fairy tale."

Nick knows that during the forgotten weekend, he began looking for someone, but he can't remember who or how to decode his case notes. Physically he's been transformed. He's lost weight and his right side is weakened, but his intuition about others' thoughts and feelings has sharpened, and he's suddenly able to connect with his teenage son. He experiences everyday actions and sensations as if for the first time. Here, he wakes up from a nap: ''Nick rose up from way down, a plantlike sleep, rooted in rich earth." The description is so original that the reader, like the character, experiences it anew.

Slowly Nick pieces together the clues he has -- a photograph, a volleyball, the name Amanda. They float like unconnected dots, tantalizing and infuriating, until finally he realizes that he was hunting for a lost girl. He vows not to undergo life-saving but mind-dulling radiation therapy until he's found her. Nick's search for answers leads inexorably back to a case of a serial killer he put behind bars for murdering seven women, and suspense builds because the reader knows what Nick himself can't remember.

''Oblivion" is a satisfying and life-affirming tale and a bravura turn on amnesia.

Vince Camden has a past he'd like to forget. The two-bit crook with a murky past and a dubious future makes a compelling hero in Jess Walter's ''Citizen Vince."

On the eve of the Reagan-Carter election, Vince is in the witness-protection program, starting life over in Spokane, Wash., making doughnuts and working the same credit-card scam that got him into trouble in New York City. ''So you construct a life from what's there," he muses. ''Patterns emerge -- fry, frost, and fill with jelly -- and comfort comes from order, especially on a day when you can't stop counting dead people."

Life veers from the orderly when the man who dummies up credit cards for Vince gets murdered, execution-style, and Vince realizes that the contract killer in town is gunning for him, too. Is his old life catching up with him? Vince returns to New York to make peace with the heavy he thinks is out to get him.

This is crime fiction with plenty of laugh-out-loud plot twists and literary panache. I reveled in descriptions like Vince's first view of Manhattan crossing the 59th Street Bridge: ''The ivied, old-money town houses of Sutton Place holding back the army of glass and steel crowded onshore and behind -- the needles of Chrysler and Empire and the sulking twin towers, a full skyline, a riot of buildings, a revolution of brick and steel and stone and glass, crawling with people, sick with people, cars surging down long gashes of avenues and short, packed cuts of cross streets and . . . the world."

''Citizen Vince" is fresh and different -- a gritty story of betrayal, and an extended riff on life, death, and politics. Walter is a literary talent writ large.

If you have a taste for a murder mystery set in Elizabethan England and told with tongue planted firmly in cheek, then by all means, hie ye to Kathy Lynn Emerson's latest Lady Appleton mystery, ''Face Down Below the Banqueting House."

Lady Appleton is a happily widowed gentlewoman, an herbalist who is carrying on with Nick Baldwin, her handsome neighbor and copesmate (lover). Fortunately this book comes equipped with a glossary, which you will need, plus a pages-long cast of characters.

Trouble brews when Queen Elizabeth takes a notion to visit her humble subjects, and sends her nefarious yeoman Bryan Tymberley to inspect Lady Appleton's Leigh Abbey to see if it will provide appropriate lodging for her and her prodigious court. Tymberley has a sideline in blackmail, and he finds no shortage of victims, since virtually every character has secrets ripe for plucking. Blackmail turns to murder when Tymberley's ruthless manservant is found, his neck broken, beneath a banqueting hall constructed in the branches of Lady Appleton's prize oak tree in anticipation of the queen's visit.

I confess, I began reading this book not expecting it to be my cup of posset. But its bounty of interesting characters, surfeit of period detail, and gentle, self-deprecating humor won me over.The story is laced with real places and historical figures. Curl up with this one some dark and stormy weekend.

Hallie Ephron is co-author of ''Guilt," the fifth Dr. Peter Zak psychological mystery thriller by G. H. Ephron.
