ON NEW ENGLAND

Mountains, meals, and a paper in Maine

By Michael Kenney  |  April 17, 2005

Wandering Home: A Long Walk Across America's Most Hopeful Landscape: Vermont's Champlain Valley and New York's Adirondacks
By Bill McKibben
Crown, 157 pp., $16

The House on Ipswich Marsh: Exploring the Natural History of New England
By William Sargent
University Press of New England, 141 pp., illustrated, $24.95

Journalism Matters
By Peter W. Cox
Tilbury House, 376 pp., paperback, illustrated, $20

America's Founding Food: The Story of New England Cooking
By Keith Stavely and Kathleen Fitzgerald
University of North Carolina, 395 pp., illustrated, $34.95

Should you have forgotten, over the late, long, unlamented winter, there is a grand New England waiting outside to be rediscovered.

Bill McKibben, who has explored the major environmental issues facing the world, sets out, in ''Wandering Home," from where he lives in Ripton, Vt., on a midsummer ramble through ''one of the world's few great regions," the country on either side of Lake Champlain. It is a place that is both ''complete" in the sense of an understood past, and ''full of future promise."

While his account is animated by the sights along the way and the people he visits with, McKibben is interested in trying to understand ''how this place can be successfully inhabited." The region, he writes, is full of environment-friendly dreams -- ''sweat-equity community forests . . . diners serving local ham and eggs [and] ferries running on local biodiesel." But turning those dreams into reality involves ''interfering with history" -- ''farmland [turning] into second homes [and] the march of big box stores."

Among McKibben's prescriptions for addressing that conflict are finding ways to reconcile ''the wild and the pastoral," and setting ''land aside from our own use simply to prove that we can do it."

But should the reader think there is too much speculation here, McKibben tells us that ''the good thing about philosophical speculation is that it can carry you right up a mountain, even a steep-sided, mossy-rocked one" like Giant Mountain on the lake's New York side -- at 4,627 feet, ''the literal high point of the journey."

William Sargent, who has also written extensively about the world's environment, instead in ''The House on Ipswich Marsh" ventures barely a half-dozen miles from his home to discover ''a quest that could last a lifetime." ''I am walking," he writes, ''through an extraordinary plot of meadows, marshes, pastures, and islands." Since it is early spring, ''frost edges the creeks" and ''scudding blankets of fog obliterate the outside world," making it feel more like ''a tundra pool high above the Arctic Circle" -- until ''the call of a distant pheasant breaks the illusion."

Sargent's path takes him into the marshes and along a causeway built in the 1600s for the early settlers to mow the salt marsh hay, which, by providing fodder for horses, cows, and sheep, fueled the economy of Colonial America (note Boston's Haymarket).

The causeway ''is an extraordinary piece of engineering." At full-moon high tides, it is under several feet of water, but when the tides recede, ''it remains the same. You could drive an Abrams tank over the causeway and it would still stand" -- something ''few of our recently built highways could withstand."

There is a mockingbird that fools him with its mimicking call and a newborn fawn, taken by its mother to the middle of the marsh, ''all day safe from the prying eyes of fox and coyote, safe from all except the nosy naturalist."

Those are just his springtime observations. When readers get to winter, they will find a naturalist's take on why the New England Patriots beat the Miami Dolphins.

Few newspapers explored the intersection of development and environment as thoughtfully -- and persistently -- as Maine Times. The alternative weekly, founded in 1968, folded in 2002, and Peter W. Cox, its visionary co-founder, died last November. To read Cox's memoir, ''Journalism Matters," is to be made aware that the advocacy journalism that he practiced, along with his co-founder, the late John N. Cole, and an eager crew of young reporters, indeed matters. The battles it fought and won created the Maine of today.

In older New England -- and still today for cooks who favor local produce -- cooking was very much a seasonal thing, as Keith Stavely and Kathleen Fitzgerald, librarians in Fall River and Newport, detail in ''America's Founding Food," their recipe-free history of the region's cooking.

Along the region's rivers, ''the spawning of the shad signaled the welcome coming of spring," so much so that the fish gave its name to flora and fauna -- shadbush, shad fly, and shad frog -- that appeared along the spawning route. Once so abundant as to be hauled off by the cartload as fertilizer, it is now a seasonal delicacy, served, of course, with fiddleheads.

May was the traditional election season in New England, prompting the baking of election cake, a fruit- and spice-filled loaf. As Harriet Beecher Stowe put it in ''Oldtown Folks," on Election Day, ''the Governor took his seat with pomp and rejoicing and all the housewives outdid themselves in election cake."
