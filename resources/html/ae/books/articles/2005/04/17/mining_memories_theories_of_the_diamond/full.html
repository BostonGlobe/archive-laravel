A READING LIFE

Mining memories, theories of the diamond

By Katharine Powers  |  April 17, 2005

The joy we Red Sox fans feel at having been granted at long last a much-yearned-for triumph seems these days to be contrasted chiefly with the continuing deprivation of Chicago Cub fans. Their team hasn't won a World Series since 1908 and hasn't even played in one since 1945. But what of the eight major-league franchises who have never won even one World Championship? And what of the six who haven't to this day hoisted even a measly league pennant? If the World Series were baseball's main attraction, it would be pretty grim for great multitudes of the American people. But it's not. Soccer fans will not agree -- nor will people who are always wrong about everything -- but baseball is quite simply the greatest game that has ever existed.

Buzz Bissinger's ''Three Nights in August: Strategy, Heartbreak, and Joy Inside the Mind of a Manager" (Houghton Mifflin, $25) goes a long way toward proving this. Bissinger takes a three-game series played in 2003 between the St. Louis Cardinals and the Cubs to examine the managing approach and intellect of Tony La Russa, one of baseball's most inspired and aggressive strategists. Among the matters treated here are La Russa's batting-order philosophy (he likes lead-off power), his views on throwing at batsmen (situations require it), and his rejection of the gospel of statistical probability as promulgated by ''Moneyball," by Michael Lewis. There is an excellent treatment of the hit-and-run play, its possible gains and awkward losses, the opponent's expectations and defensive tactics, and all the hugger-mugger that surrounds giving the nod. Some managers avoid the tactic, but its aggressiveness suits La Russa -- though his calling for it earlier in the month had culminated in the execution of a rare unassisted triple play against his team.

Some of La Russa's thinking is explored between pitches themselves, as is a great deal of other business -- often to an exasperating extent. One of Albert Pujols's appearances at the plate stretches to almost eight pages though it involves only three pitches. Between them Bissinger gives Pujols's life story, a description of his determination, work habits, and reliance on video, and an account of the influence of batting coach Charley Lau on hitting today. The book's other weakness is that it occasionally taps into that seam of highfalutin baloney that so many writers on baseball, from Bart Giamatti on down, seem drawn to. Of a Kenny Lofton hit Bissinger writes, ''He gets a little piece without any particular inspiration. The ball meanders in the air, a halfhearted ennui, the kind of existentialist hit that would keep Camus or Sartre in the money if they had played baseball."

Still, the book is worth the occasional annoyance. In addition to illuminating the mass of research and myriad of decisions that go into managing a baseball team, Bissinger describes La Russa's overall view of the qualities essential to a top-flight player. It comes down to talent and character, to possessing the tools, the savvy, and, above all, the will. La Russa feels that the cosseting of gifted athletes from adolescence on, and the complacency that big money can bring, spoil many players, holding them back from greatness. Jose Canseco is, of course, the perfect example. La Russa, who managed the superstar in Oakland, heard him grumbling to his teammates about a lengthened work year, complaining, ''Why is it always us that has to go to the playoffs?"

Yes, that's the author of ''Juiced: Wild Times, Rampant 'Roids, Smash Hits, and How Baseball Got Big" (Regan, $25.95) all right. Suffice it to say that this crowing, whining, snitching, and relentlessly crude embarrassment of a book makes David Wells (Red Sox pitcher, alas, and author of ''Perfect I'm Not") sound like Lord Chesterfield.

Jonathan Eig's ''Luckiest Man: The Life and Death of Lou Gehrig" (Simon & Schuster, $26) is a wonderful old-fashioned biography of one of the greatest players of all time. Called ''Biscuit Pants" as a rookie because of his sumptuous posterior, Gehrig is shown also to have been under the mighty sway of his mother, Christina, who fed him up good and sabotaged his chances of getting married for years. Finally Eleanor Twitchell entered the scene, a bit of a high-stepper, now intent on marriage, and more than a match for the fearsome Christina. The two married though they were not entirely compatible, as Eleanor liked the high life and Gehrig was a tightwad and not given to words; indeed, Eig scouts the possibility that Eleanor may have had an interlude with Babe Ruth. The book is immensely satisfying as a picture of baseball and the Yankees during the 1920s and '30s, and is moving in its account of Gehrig's courage and determination in fighting the disease that bears his name.

''Negro League Baseball," edited by Antony Decaneas with a foreword by Willie Mays and text by Daniel Wolff (Abrams, $35), is a magnificent collection of photographs taken by Ernest C. Withers in the late 1940s and early '50s. Withers, photographer for the Memphis Red Sox, took these pictures of players, owners, and fans in the declining era of the Negro League. In the stands ''pool sharks sat side by side with saints of the church," while on the field baseball and clowning were jointly pursued. The game, as is made clear both from the unsentimental photos and text, was very much concerned with making money. Mays expresses the exuberant entrepreneurial spirit the photos convey, writing that playing baseball was ''a chance to make money doing something I was good at."

In an article I wrote on books about the Red Sox last week I gave only a few words to Steve Buckley's ''Red Sox: Where Have You Gone?" (Sports Publishing LLC, $19.95) but still managed to attribute the book to ''Steve Bailey." This is the sort of error that makes one ponder decay. On the other hand, Buckley's book is a tonic for melancholy thoughts, for he has tracked down 50 onetime Red Sox players and interviewed them to see what they've been up to and what they really thought of their playing careers. Along the way he has uncovered a good deal of unlikely information. Some things we all know: that Jim Lonborg became a dentist and that Bruce Hurst wished he'd never left Boston. But what of Bill Monbouquette, Mel Parnell, Bobby Doerr, and Oil Can Boyd? What of the Sturm und Drang of Tommy Harper? What of Earl Wilson? And why is Gene Michael in these pages? In that, as throughout this book, lies a great story.

Katherine A. Powers, a writer and critic, lives in Cambridge. Her column appears on alternate Sundays. She can be reached by e-mail at pow3@earthlink.net.
