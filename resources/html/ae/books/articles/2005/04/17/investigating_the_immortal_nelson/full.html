Investigating the 'immortal' Nelson

A detailed look at a man central to Britain's national identity

By Andrew Lambert  |  April 17, 2005

Nelson: A Dream of Glory, 1758-1797
By John Sugden
Holt, 943 pp., illustrated, $35

ADVERTISEMENT

Few historical figures have been subjected to as many biographies as Horatio Nelson, and yet as we approach the bicentennial of his crowning achievement and tragic death the number is increasing. Do we really need more big books about such a well-known life? The answer depends on the quality of the book, and the continuing significance of the subject. John Sugden's ''Nelson" passes both tests.

Born in 1758, the son of a village clergyman, Nelson escaped his humdrum background through the patronage of his maternal uncle and by the age of 21 was a captain in the Royal Navy, with nine years of service under his belt. He made his name in the War of Independence, striking deep into Nicaragua on a forlorn amphibious raid and capturing numerous New England merchant ships. He also demonstrated a talent for attracting the love of almost every officer or man he ever worked with.

After the war Nelson stopped illegal American trade with the British West Indies, but his career only prospered with the outbreak of the French Revolutionary War in 1793. Commanding ships, squadrons, and fleets in the Mediterranean, Baltic Sea, and English Channel, he displayed the highest professional skills, mastering strategy, diplomacy, logistics, and coalition warfare. He also lost his right arm and the sight of an eye. Finally, on Oct. 21, 1805, he achieved immortality, annihilating the imperial dreams of Napoleon Bonaparte off Cape Trafalgar, close to the Straits of Gibraltar, at the crossroads of naval power. Struck down by a musket ball early in the battle, he lived long enough to learn the extent of his victory. Nelson left an example of unequaled ability, courage, loyalty, and devotion that has transcended his age and country.

In life Nelson was worshiped by a frightened and desperate nation; he was their talisman against the bogey of Napoleon. His great victories at the Nile, Copenhagen, and Trafalgar exceeded all previous triumphs in scale and consequence. Not only did they define the art of war at sea, they kept Britain British. In death Nelson was buried like a Roman god, and then his twice-life-size effigy was raised high above London's central square. He was, quite simply, the greatest Englishman and the most brilliant naval commander of all, a point acknowledged by every navy, and stressed by Captain Alfred T. Mahan, who used Nelson's life in his 1897 book to teach US naval officers about war, strategy, and leadership. Nelson remained Britain's national talisman in times of trouble: When the nation faced war, invasion, or threat, his name, image, and example were a source of comfort and confidence. In the darkest days of 1940, Prime Minister Winston Churchill filled his speeches with Nelson, and helped write the film ''That Hamilton Woman" in which Laurence Olivier and Vivien Leigh portrayed the hero and his mistress for an American audience. Although less frequently called upon in times of peace, Nelson remains central to the British identity, and while his statue stands in Trafalgar Square his country will never be subsumed into Europe.

Sugden has excellent credentials. His award-winning biographies of Elizabethan naval hero Sir Francis Drake and iconic Native American leader Tecumseh established his scholarship, accuracy, and engagement. This time his subject escapes the bounds of a single, massive volume. The book ends with newly promoted national hero Rear Admiral Sir Horatio Nelson facing a shattering defeat at Tenerife and the loss of his arm. The sheer scale of the book allows Sugden to reconsider Nelson's early life, a period that all previous biographers have hurried through. Sugden argues that this undue haste has perpetuated many myths, telescoping Nelson's career into a simple linear trajectory.

To support such revision Sugden has gone back to the original sources and widened his search for fresh evidence to government departments and the careers of Nelson's colleagues. His attention to details is remarkable: The myths that accumulate on any heroic reputation faster than barnacles on a battleship are removed with energy and overwhelming force. Every stage of Nelson's career receives fresh analysis, and many of them will be changed forever by this exhaustive treatment. Sugden's book will be the first resort of any scholarly research on Nelson's rise to glory for decades to come, a monument to application, industry, and comprehension. Nor is the journey of discovery anywhere near as long as the 800-page text might imply. Apart from a few overlong asides, the story possesses the same drive as its astonishing subject.

However, there is a price to pay for presenting the central character in such fine detail. In revealing exactly how the glory was won, who else was responsible, and how much of the old version was mythic, Sugden has lost something of the magic that made Nelson the idealized hero worshiped by Byron and his generation. Sugden's Nelson is a thorough professional, but the mainsprings of his success are dreams of glory. It was not for such commonplaces that his countrymen called him ''immortal" and raised him above them. They worshiped Nelson because his genius reminded them of an eternal truth. Men can aspire to be gods, and in the slight, battered form of Nelson one man came as close to the divine as any warrior in history.

Nelson has always been open to conflicting interpretations. ''Nelson: A Dream of Glory" is a resounding opening salvo in the debate about his meaning in the 21st century and is among the top three Nelson studies. If Sugden completes the story and adds a little magic, he might produce the best biography of them all.

Andrew Lambert is Laughton Professor of Naval History in the Department of War Studies at King's College, London, and author of ''Nelson: Britannia's God of War."
