Into the breach

Wounded in World War II, Bob Dole returned to Kansas and found that the battle had only begun

By Bob Kerrey  |  April 17, 2005

One Soldier's Story
By Bob Dole
HarperCollins, 287 pp.,
illustrated, $25.95

Since I fought in a war that was the product of the thinking of the Greatest Generation, I have always had a little difficulty with the airbrushing that occurs when the story of this remarkable cohort is told. However, there is no doubting the gargantuan size of the cumulative impact of their individual acts. Former senator Bob Dole's memoir, ''One Soldier's Story," gives us enough of the life of one of those individuals to understand why it is they inspire us.

The early sections of the book are compelling. Investor Warren Buffett has written that the most important event of his life was the circumstances of his birth, and that the most important influence was the fact that his two most important heroes -- his mother and father -- never let him down. Obviously, the same could said by Dole about his parents.

''One Soldier's Story" would be worth the read simply as a text for how to be a good parent. And if there were just a few more like Doran and Bina Dole, the answer to the question asked by Thomas Frank's popular political treatise ''What's the Matter With Kansas?" would be ''Nothing."

These sections are marred somewhat by patriotic and self-help clichés, which began to accumulate like so many fingernails on a blackboard. This repetition made me start to regret I'd agreed to do write this review; I served in the Senate with Dole, admire him greatly, and wanted to like this book. But my attitude changed abruptly as he described walking from his family's home in Russell, Kansas, downtown to Dawson's Drugstore, where he worked as a boy, more than a year after he was seriously wounded in the closing days of the Second World War. The wounds reduced an athlete who seemed to run everywhere to a disabled man who considered movement in his toes as exciting progress.

In ''The Hero With a Thousand Faces," Joseph Campbell writes that the voyage of every hero begins with the crossing of a threshold that separates the safety of home from the danger of the outside world. The hero crosses the threshold with the purpose of retrieving a boon, which he or she takes back to help those who remain at home.

Bob Dole crossed two thresholds -- the first when he went off to war, and the second when he walked to Dawson's that day in 1946. His next stop was college, to pursue an understanding of what was for him the ultimate boon: the law. He did not stop until he reached Washington, D.C., where he served his hometown and state for 35 years in Congress.

Other readers, especially those who presume that we must dislike our political opponents, may not buy the association with Campbell's hero myth. However, it will take a pretty hard heart not to be moved to tears as the 23-year-old Dole keeps moving slowly down the street toward Dawson's while singing Rodgers and Hammerstein's ''You'll Never Walk Alone."

Dole stays on the surface of his emotions for most of this memoir, though he does walk close to the edge of his grief twice. The first time, he is describing how he felt when he knows his right arm will never function normally again. His list of losses includes basketball and golf. But they also include pulling his future wife ''close to me as we drifted off to sleep" and ''[tossing] my future daughter into the air above my head and [seeing] that look of sheer exhilaration and trust in her eyes as she waited for me to catch her." I believe facing the fact and the consequences of this kind of loss is a must. Without this, hope is amputated from acts of daily living.

The second moment is even more revealing. After his injuries, Dole was horrified to see how wasted and damaged his body was the first time he saw his image in a mirror. In the senator's words: ''It's been more than sixty years since I first saw that image in the bathroom mirror. In the past sixty years, I've glanced at my full body in a mirror less than half a dozen times. I don't need any more reminders." It is a need the hero must deny him- or herself in order to continue the voyage.

Dole's time in war, leading a platoon in the 10th Mountain Division in Italy, was short. It ended in the second lieutenant's near death three weeks before Germany surrendered, ending a terrible war in Europe. But these endings were just the beginning for Dole and his generation.

In a lovely coincidence that makes this point emphatically, two of Dole's 11,000 fellow patients at Battle Creek, Mich.'s, Percy Jones Army Hospital were future Democratic senators Phil Hart and Dan Inouye. Inouye was gravely wounded just miles and a week from Dole. They were among the final casualties of the campaign to drive the Germans out of Italy.

''One Soldier's Story" is worth reading not because it is great writing but because it tells a great and important story. Properly read, it just might help others be inspired to not let life get them down. To my way of thinking that's about as good as it gets.

Bob Kerrey is the president of the New School University, New York.
