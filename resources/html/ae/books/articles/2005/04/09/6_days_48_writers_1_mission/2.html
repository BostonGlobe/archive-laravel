6 days, 48 writers, 1 mission

April 9, 2005

Page 2 of 2 -- Working with a small platoon of volunteers and the backing of the William Joiner Center for the Study of War and Social Consequences at UMass-Boston, Rodriguez invites authors who are published, soon-to-be published, or simply aspire to be published one day. This year, he's expanded his roster of writers to include some from overseas. Among them is Oscar Soto, a Spanish cardiologist, who will read excerpts from his book ''El Ultimo Dia de Salvador Allende" (''Salvador Allende's Last Day"). The book details the day of the 1973 coup that ousted the elected socialist Chilean president.

Ten of the participating authors will also conduct writing workshops for students in Boston area public schools, including East Boston High, the Amigos School in Cambridge, and the Boston Adult Technical Academy in Roxbury. Rodriguez said he hopes the workshops will help young people find their way into literature by seeing real-life examples up close.

Many of the writers taking part in the weeklong event were born outside the United States or represent first or second generation Latinos and a multiplicity of backgrounds. They are lawyers, local schoolteachers, producers, professors, and college students who are drawn to the readings by a common need to share their stories with one another and to be heard.

The undercurrents of their stories peel back layers of who today's Hispanics are. They may not necessarily speak Spanish. They may not necessarily be immigrants. They may be racially mixed, with no appropriate census box to check off. They may be Dominican, Mexican, Cuban, Puerto Rican, or Salvadoran. They may yearn to write but don't earn enough to make a living from it.

They are writers such as Francisco Stork, a 51-year-old MassHousing attorney in downtown Boston. Writing mostly on weekends, he's published one book, ''The Way of the Jaguar," a novel that follows a prominent Boston lawyer whose life falls apart as he begins a search for a lost love in his hometown of El Paso. This year Stork plans to read from his upcoming second novel, ''Behind the Eyes," which chronicles a 16-year-old Chicano boy who gets mixed up with gang life in Texas.

Stork, who was born in Tampico, Mexico, and moved to Boston to study law at Harvard, says writing gives him the opportunity to use his imagination in a way that he doesn't get to do at work. ''It's a way of self-discovery, too, because you learn things about yourself and about the world. Writing has a way of at least making you confront the important things in life."

Magda Alvarez-Donis, a Hyde Park schoolteacher for 23 years, was so struck by the positive reaction her students had to visiting local authors that she began volunteering with Rodriguez to help cultivate a larger event in the school system.

''It's important for the community to see that there are writers among ourselves. It opens so many cultural doors, " said Alvarez-Donis, who plans to read from her 50-page children's book that dissects Afro-Antillean poetry and its relationship to Latin jazz and salsa music. ''The Latino experience is very cultural, and most of the struggles we have gone through have been channeled through writing,"' said Alvarez-Donis, 46. ''It's only natural that the Latino community here also uses writing as a venue for the continued development of our Latino identity."

A Hispanic accent on writing

Hispanic Writers' Week, which takes place Monday through next Saturday, is a six-night event that will feature 48 local and international scribes. Readings begin each night at 6 p.m. at the Egleston Square branch of the Boston Public Library, 2044 Columbus Ave., Roxbury. For information, call the Joiner Center at the University of Massachusetts, 617-287-5850. The writers will read from their works, published or soon-to-be published, in either English or Spanish.

Among the writers:

Raquel Ortiz: Cambridge producer and author of ''The Silk Purse"

Oscar Soto: Spanish cardiologist and author of ''El Ultimo Dia de Salvador Allende" (''Salvador Allende's Last Day")

Francisco Stork: Boston lawyer and author of ''The Way of the Jaguar," and the upcoming novel ''Behind the Eyes"

Andres Torres: director of the Mauricio Gaston Institute at University of Massachusetts at Boston and author of ''Between Melting Pot and Mosaic" and ''Nuyorican Signs."

Charley Trujillo: author of ''Soldados: Chicanos in Viet Nam" and ''Dogs From Illusion."

Raul Ybarra, professor at University of Massachusetts at Boston and author of ''Learning to Write as a Hostile Act for Latino Students" and the upcoming memoir ''I Am a Man."
