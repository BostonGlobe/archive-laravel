Untangling a web of politics, murder, and war

By Jonathon Keats  |  August 28, 2005

The Breaking Point: Hemingway, Dos Passos, and the Murder of Jose Robles
By Stephen Koch
Counterpoint, 308 pp., $24.95

For a few months in early 1937, a battered hotel in war-torn Madrid was the most fashionable destination in the world. Errol Flynn was there, as were Antoine de Saint-Exupery and Andre Malraux. Every day in the atrium, you could find the duchess of Atholl and the dean of Canterbury Cathedral. And then there were Ernest Hemingway and John Dos Passos.

With its beguiling mixture of glamour and wreckage, the Hotel Florida is a fitting setting for viewing radical chic between the world wars. Besieged by the fascist militia of Generalissimo Franco, the fledgling Spanish Republic was the leftist cause celèbre of the 1930s, and Madrid was the city where popular resistance to Franco was fiercest. As Stephen Koch meticulously documents in ''The Breaking Point," the behavior of Hemingway and Dos Passos was emblematic, and in the collapse of their friendship can be seen the misunderstandings and duplicities that turned the leftist idealism of the era so mean and ugly.

Both authors were virtually at the height of their celebrity when their paths crossed as the two most famous, and well-compensated, correspondents at the Hotel Florida. Hemingway was the acclaimed author of ''A Farewell to Arms," while Dos Passos had recently been pictured on the cover of Time magazine, in tribute to his epic trilogy, ''USA." They also had been good friends for some 15 years and had practically learned to write together, reading passages of the Bible to each other for the rhythm of the prose.

In the beginning, there wasn't any political disagreement. There hardly could have been, given the difference in their personalities: ''Hemingway shrugged off not just radical politics, but all politics," Koch writes. ''Dos Passos on the other hand felt the Old World -- old Europe and old America, too -- shaking on its corrupt foundations. He just couldn't stop thinking about radical change, and justice, justice, justice."

Naturally, their books reflected that distinction. While Hemingway's early works held certain leftist assumptions, particularly in their depiction of war, Dos Passos was a fictional muckraker who wrote novels as radical case studies. His books were enormously popular. Hemingway was still struggling as Dos Passos became a household name, the darling of the leftist intelligentsia and, by extension, of the Popular Front.

Koch rightly calls the Popular Front ''the single most successful propaganda campaign ever mounted by the Soviet state." The idea was to promote Stalinism within the Western democracies by presenting it as the only viable alternative to the fascism and Nazism sweeping across Europe. Essential to this mission was its secrecy: Support for Stalin must appear spontaneous, disinterested, if it were to be persuasive.

Dos Passos was perfect for this function, given that his communist sympathies were genuine, and he had a large American audience. The only problem was that his style was as radical as his substance, tending toward fractured storytelling interrupted by bursts of ticker tape. Praiseworthy in the days of the Soviet avant-garde, by 1934, such experimentalism was expressly condemned by Stalin. Lest there be any ambiguity on the matter, two names had been given, at the First All-Union Congress of Soviet Writers, as examples of bourgeois formalism: James Joyce and Dos Passos.

Hemingway, on the other hand, was looking like a better Popular Front mouthpiece all the time. There was his soaring fame, and his charm. Most of Hemingway's friends were leftists. Some were even, unbeknown to him, Soviet agents. With his gritty characters and blunt prose, Hemingway was suited to serve the cause, except that, as Koch notes, ''he didn't trust causes." If Hemingway had one core principle, it was individualism. And, if he had one core practice, it was selfishness.

So the Popular Front simply exploited that. Hemingway was already drawn to the Spanish Civil War, which was being fought heroically, tragically, like the plot of one of his novels. All it took to make him take sides was to make it advantageous to his career. How? In a masterstroke, by pitting him against politically passe Dos Passos with a showdown in the Hotel Florida, and a chance to accomplish literary dominance.

And that is where Jose Robles enters the picture. From an old Spanish family, Robles was a radical who served the Republican cause close to the Soviet center of power, only to end up dead, executed for an unspecified crime by an unknown party. Robles happened to be an old friend of Dos Passos's. Hemingway was told, through the Soviet propaganda machine, that Robles had faced the firing line because he was an undercover fascist. That put the cause in Hemingway terms: personal terms, with professional implications. On the one hand, there was the righteous Republic. On the other, there was, as Koch puts it, ''Dos -- noble Dos -- the protector of treason."

Of course, this is a gross simplification of an enormously complex situation. Also in play were anarchists, literary critics, and, naturally, the Hemingway women. Koch delves into all of this. If anything, he does so too much, muddling his story with needless background. More significantly, like those whose stories he tells, he lets the literature stand too often as an afterthought.

''The Breaking Point" is a deft study in political cynicism, with earnest Dos Passos as hero and Johnny-come-lately Hemingway as bogeyman. But the selfish individualism that made Hemingway so problematic personally and politically was what gave the characters in his novels their extraordinary autonomy. Even in his finest books, the three thick volumes of ''USA," all that Dos Passos could muster was dogma awash in melodrama, occasionally enlivened by technical innovation. There is a good reason Hemingway is still so admired, and Dos Passos barely read. If art has no place in politics, politics has no place in art.

Jonathon Keats is the author of ''The Pathology of Lies." His new novel, ''Lighter Than Vanity," will be released next month.
