The path of a family defined by silence

By Amy Kroin  |  August 7, 2005

Remains: Non-Viewable
By John Sacret Young
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 272 pp., $24

John Sacret Young grew up in a sprawling family of repressed New Englanders. Within the Young clan were golfers and alcoholics and train enthusiasts who made an art form out of evading the truth. Favoring physical exertion over heart-to-heart confessionals, the Youngs clobbered each other in annual tennis tournaments and played Thanksgiving Day football games that dragged on until nightfall.

And, on occasion, they fought with vigor. Young recounts a particularly nasty skirmish in his memoir, ''Remains: Non-Viewable." The book focuses on the intersecting relationships of four men in the Young family: the author, his closemouthed father, his larger-than-life uncle, and his cousin Doug, who was killed in Vietnam. Doug had a tumultuous relationship with his father, George, a talky, towering figure and the family's alpha male. One morning a seemingly innocuous tiff about household chores culminated in Doug's swinging a pair of garden shears at his father's face. Young knew that his cousin and uncle had their struggles, but in a family of inveterate secret-keepers it was impossible to uncover the roots of the feud.

Young is best known as the co-creator, writer, and executive producer of ''China Beach," the Peabody Award-winning television series about a triage hospital in Vietnam. Young did not serve in Vietnam, but that war and others have long been a presence in his screenplays and teleplays. ''A Rumor of War," the first television drama dealing with Vietnam, profiles a court-martialed soldier; the film ''Testament" explores the aftermath of a nuclear attack; ''Romero" celebrates Archbishop Oscar Romero, who was assassinated for his opposition to the El Salvadoran death squads; ''Thanks of a Grateful Nation" examines Gulf War syndrome. In ''Remains" Young shifts his attention from global calamities to private ones, probing the tiny fissures and ruptures that lie within his family.

Doug's death focuses the narrative, which shuttles back and forth between past and present. Early on, Young explores the moment the family learned of Doug's death. We see the terrible days spent waiting for Doug's casket to arrive home to the United States; then Young makes us wait another 100 pages or so before introducing the cheerful aspiring mortician who delivers the body. In between, Young exposes Doug as he was in life: a prankster, an unwilling participant in the family tennis matches, an up-for-anything playmate. The book's final pages, which document Young's unsuccessful attempt to determine the cause of Doug's death, are among its most poignant.

''Remains" has the feel of a meandering yarn told by an uncle with time to spare. Anecdotes beget anecdotes, and tangents that inevitably circle back to the original point abound. Young is, for the most part, a deft and assured storyteller. He offers rich and nuanced portrayals of various family members, in particular his father -- a onetime pillar of the community turned caustic alcoholic -- and George, a man who remained a pillar of the community until the very end. How these two men progressed from inseparable childhood pals -- known by one name, ''Bill and George" -- to fathers of such opposing character is left unresolved. Indeed, this is one of the many questions Young is unable to provide a tidy answer to. There are revelations galore in this book, but they are of the small, day-to-day variety. Young refuses to simplify his family's experience; he knows that silence defines the Youngs, but he does not know why.

The narrative momentum falters when Young strays from storytelling to deliver important messages about important matters like depression and the nature of memory. The pace slows further when he takes a peck of words to say what could have been expressed in a pint: ''She had a remarkable knack of asking questions that didn't seem to pry, but that came from a rich vein of a word that's easy to denigrate and was a reason she was so alive -- interest." Moments like these cry out for a more attentive editor.

There is also a curious divide in this book between the writing on family -- which is generally absorbing and unflinching -- and the writing on romantic and sexual matters. Young's teenage infatuation with his cousin Samantha is depicted in appropriately complex terms; one can't say the same for his take on his relationship with Dana Delany, the lead actress on ''China Beach": ''She was good but she wanted to be bad -- and she had been bad. . . . When she did let go she caromed and ricocheted through ravenous and risky wildness." This girls-gone-wild prose jars, as does the stagy nature of the exchanges between Young and his estranged wife, Jenny. Their all-too-charged conversations have the feel of television dialogue -- a carry-over, perhaps, from Young's day job.

Fortunately ''Remains" transcends these missteps. The book's title, a reference to the military's classification of the Vietnam dead into remains ''viewable" and ''non-viewable," is a handy metaphor for the Young family, for these men who were chatty about everyday matters -- and silent about what really matters.

Amy Kroin's reviews have appeared in The Washington Post and The New York Times, among other publications.
