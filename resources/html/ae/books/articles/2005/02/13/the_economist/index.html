The economist

Profile of Galbraith as a principled political force is burdened by detail

By Joseph Rosenbloom  |  February 13, 2005

John Kenneth Galbraith: His Life, His Politics, His Economics
By Richard Parker
Farrar, Straus & Giroux, 820 pp., illustrated, $35

Any biographer of John Kenneth Galbraith should, of course, read his oeuvre. That's easier said than done. Galbraith, who is 96 years old, has written 42 books. At last word he was working on a 43d.

Then there is the mass of books, magazines, and newspaper articles that bear on Galbraith's incarnations as Harvard professor, Fortune magazine writer, State Department bureaucrat, speechwriter for Adlai Stevenson, ambassador to India, and top adviser to John F. Kennedy and Lyndon Johnson. The list goes on.

And what about all the relevant records in the archives at Harvard, the State Department, various presidential libraries, and other places too numerous to mention?

Eye strain is an obvious risk. A bigger risk is the likelihood that, no matter what one writes about Galbraith's life, there is a good chance that he has already said it in one of his books and said it better. Books by Galbraith about Galbraith -- including a 563-page memoir, ''A Life in Our Times" -- are prominent among his many bestsellers.

Making a run at the high bar is Richard Parker. As Galbraith's authorized biographer, Parker has had unique access to the man, his papers and family, his colleagues and friends. Galbraith sat for more than 200 hours of taped interviews conducted over five years, according to Parker. He even turned his Cambridge house over to Parker one summer so the biographer could rummage through boxes of personal papers stored in the basement.

The result is ''John Kenneth Galbraith," a prodigiously researched valedictory for a man on the last lap of an extraordinary career, and an unabashed valentine.

That Parker, who heads the economics and journalism program at Harvard's John F. Kennedy School of Government, finds much to admire in Galbraith is not surprising. Parker is a kindred political spirit whose liberal credentials include the launching of Mother Jones magazine and fund-raising for the likes of George McGovern and Ted Kennedy.

Although Parker's emphasis is not on the details of Galbraith's personal life, he paints a reasonably complete and vivid portrait of him. Galbraith emerges as a man driven by a fierce work ethic, imbued with old-fashioned civility, and susceptible to wide mood swings, which a psychiatrist once diagnosed as latent bipolar disorder.

In conducting the obligatory tour of Galbraith's books, Parker is at his best. He lucidly reprises the trilogy published in a 15-year span starting in 1952, ''American Capitalism," ''The Affluent Society," and ''The New Industrial State." The three volumes set forth most of the arguments for which Galbraith is best known, not the least his clarion call to right the ''imbalance" of private over public goods in the American economy -- ideas that have become cornerstones of liberal orthodoxy.

Parker delves deeply into the historical contexts surrounding Galbraith's every job and political allegiance. The book's impressive display of scholarly diligence exacts a cost: tedium. Ditto the effect of Parker's esoteric attention to the battles between, say, economists who subscribe to the ''neoclassical synthesis" and those adhering to ''post-Keynesian eclecticism."

Parker's heavily footnoted, workmanlike prose relies extensively on his subject's writings, and he quotes large swatches of it. The juxtaposition, alas, does not always reflect well on Parker's literary gifts. Nor does Parker's digging yield anything startlingly new about Galbraith. It does, however, augment the record in some interesting ways.

If the theme of Galbraith as whipping boy of American conservatives is nothing new, Parker documents several extreme examples, notorious episodes in which Galbraith's enemies on the right have vilified him.

In 1947, when Galbraith came up for tenure at Harvard, several overseers objected on the grounds that, among other things, he had not been sufficiently respectful of the American military during his post-World War II investigation of US aerial bombardment. Galbraith had concluded that the air campaign had not greatly impaired Germany's economy. Only when Harvard's then-president James Conant put his own job on the line did the overseers back down, allowing the tenure appointment to go through.

Likewise, audiotapes obtained from President Nixon's presidential library show the venom that Galbraith's name elicited in the Oval Office circa 1971. Enraged about Galbraith's congressional testimony, in which he had accused Nixon of mishandling the economy, the president erupted. He instructed his aides to wage a public campaign to ''blast" Galbraith as a socialist and the economic spokesman of the ''ultra-liberal wing" of the Democratic Party.

Galbraith has written at length about his own campaign within President Kennedy's White House to head off what became a juggernaut of the military and national security establishment behind a plan to dispatch US troops to Vietnam. Galbraith failed, and Kennedy ordered thousands of troops to Vietnam.

However, in memos and personal appeals to Kennedy, Galbraith flogged the case for reversing course and withdrawing the troops. Drawing on freshly declassified State Department documents, Parker concludes that the memos were having the intended effect. Kennedy's reshuffling of the top posts at State just days before his death signaled a shift toward Galbraith's views and, Parker implies, meant that the president would have brought the troops home.

If that interpretation construes the facts in a light favorable to Galbraith, Parker is consistently so inclined. Yes, he concedes, many economists consider Galbraith not really one of their own in a discipline that extolls mathematical models and aspires to the scientific rigor of physics. For example, Parker quotes MIT's Robert Solow, who terms Galbraith ''fundamentally a moralist."

But Parker sees nothing wrong with Galbraith's blend of economics and moral conviction. On the contrary, he would put Galbraith in a pantheon with John Maynard Keynes, whom Parker describes as the ''model of the economist as an engaged and politically purposive intellectual."

Joseph Rosenbloom is a senior correspondent for The American Prospect.
