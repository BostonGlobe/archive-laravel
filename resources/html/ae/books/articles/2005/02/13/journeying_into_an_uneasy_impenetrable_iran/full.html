Journeying into an uneasy, impenetrable Iran

By Richard Eder  |  February 13, 2005

In the Rose Garden of the Martyrs: A Memoir of Iran
By Christopher de Bellaigue
HarperCollins, 283 pp., $26.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Are there plans to use force to bring about regime change in Iran? At least one report suggests there are. Secretary of State Condoleezza Rice says not ''at this time." And contingencies are surely being studied somewhere in the Pentagon -- by definition a structure built upon obtuse angles.

If ''look before you leap" retains any currency in our government, though, a look at ''In the Rose Garden of the Martyrs" makes it clear that any leap would amount to setting up a munitions dump in a still-active volcano.

Christopher de Bellaigue, an English writer, hurled himself into the volcano five years ago. He married an Iranian woman, settled in Tehran, learned Farsi, and committed himself to experience a society whose nature -- always difficult for a Westerner to grasp -- is now unable to grasp itself, being caught in a shifting magma of revolutionary and post-revolutionary trauma.

De Bellaigue's book is a pontoon crossing, neither securely attached to its subject on one end, nor offering readers a secure foothold on the other. To do either, it would have to be superficial; it would have to pretend to understand more than can be understood, and explain more than can be explained.

If the author can't quite attach his pontoons, he does better, perhaps: He clings to them, swaying, slipping off, climbing back on. The turbulent motion provides the reader with a sense of being there, of understanding by not understanding. ''In the Rose Garden" is a series of portraits, brilliant but unfinished at the edges, of vivid set pieces that refuse a design, of snatches of recent history that reveal, reverse, and trail off.

De Bellaigue tells, briefly, the story of the revolution that toppled the shah, the Islamist theocracy that took over under the late Ayatollah Khomeini, the reformist tide that installed Mohammad Khatami as president, and the retreat of this tide under pressure from religious conservatives. At the end there is a suggestion that, sooner or later, time and a younger generation will make the retreat inconclusive.

There is much detail about the political struggles, the maneuverings, the repressions, and there is an intriguing account of the repercussions inside the country of the Iran-contra scandal. But de Bellaigue is after a society's nature more than its doings.

Riding in a crowded taxi, he hears talk of drought, the unmatched quality of Iranian rice, the bad character of a nearby town. He observes ta'aruf (a ritualized courtesy not to be taken seriously) in action. Twice the driver refuses payment from a woman passenger. She insists, he names a price, she is outraged and offers half. He is outraged, holds to his price, gives up. He pats de Bellaigue on the shoulder: ''Whatever you do, don't marry an Iranian."

There are other vignettes, but de Bellaigue is mainly interested in a darker story: that of a lost generation. Revolutions liberate what is hidden (until the liberating grows despotic and stultified). In Iran this was a passionate Islamic current that had been held down for a half-century by the last shah and his father, and that found expression in the populist upsurge led by Khomeini.

De Bellaigue sets the tone with the annual commemoration of the death of the Imam Hossein, grandson of the Prophet and precursor of the Shia branch of Islam, at the hands of enemies whose descendants would become the Sunnis. It is a pageantry of wild grief. Huge crowds gather, weeping, beating their breasts, calling out Hossein's name. A pantomime is performed of the 13-centuries-old killing. And between the intervals of lament the crowds stroll, chat, and gossip. ''I reflected on the grief, and the entertainment that people made of that grief," de Bellaigue writes.

Martyrdom is a passion equivalent to love or patriotism and, for those who gore their hearts celebrating it, a vicarious escape. The heart of ''In the Rose Garden" is the author's encounter with middle-aged men who in their youth were fierce activists in the Islamist Revolutionary Guard and, later, veterans of the disastrous -- and to them religious -- war with Iraq. They are settled now, and many know in some fashion that their idealism was betrayed by the blindness of a leadership that substituted zealotry for military expertise, at horrible cost. And far worse, by the corruption and joyless oppression that followed the high Khomeini days.

The author's principal interlocutor is Mr. Zarif, once an extremist street fighter, later a war veteran, then a student in a Shiite seminary, and now a prosperous family man. He and his wife visit regularly with de Bellaigue and his Iranian wife. He is interested in literature; he keeps a journal and talks freely about his radical past.

Except that it is not entirely his past: The zealotry remains alongside the sophistication, and pulses with the pain of faith wedded to despair. Mr. Zarif's moderation is a scab over an unhealed wound, a bridge that goes only partway over the gulf that neither de Bellaigue nor his readers -- let alone the liberation medicine men in Washington -- will ever really cross.

Richard Eder reviews books for several publications.
