Former spy chief plots a thriller

February 10, 2005

"When I joined MI5, it was very male dominated," she says. "Some of the men there, it never occurred to them we might actually want to do this work. So it was both breaking through the perceptions and seizing the moment. I was around at that time and, in fact, was the first woman who was allowed to be trained to be an 'agent runner,' as we call it. And, of course, women are actually good at this work."

A sentence from Rimington's 1970 employee evaluation gives a sense of what MI5 was like back then: "She is a most acceptable, warm-hearted and engaging colleague, even though she is an upholder of women's rights."

Rimington thrived on the work, but that didn't make balancing the demands of office and family any easier. "At some stages," she says, "particularly when the children were young, I felt that I was running very, very fast, and things sorted themselves out by the skin of their teeth, really. You had to make instant decisions, particularly when children are ill or something odd happens like that."

Once, Rimington was about to go negotiate with a prospective defector when she got a call saying one of her daughters was having convulsions and on her way to the emergency room. The hospital was near the safe house where the meeting was to be held. So Rimington was able to make the meeting and go the hospital -- though she had to borrow money from the Soviet agent to pay for a taxi. (Perhaps unnerved by being asked for a loan, he decided not to defect.)

Such difficult moments didn't stop Rimington's ascent at MI5. After she was named director-general, there would be questions about her role in agency surveillance of striking miners in the mid-'80s. And her decision to publish her memoirs after she retired raised official eyebrows. But overall her stewardship of the agency drew high praise.

"In the conduct of her office," says Twetten, "she was more open than anyone had ever been." And Rimington is widely credited with shifting the agency's priorities from fighting the Cold War to battling terrorism and organized crime.

Thanks to the Irish Republican Army, Rimington has a long record of experience with counterterrorism. She cites two keys to success: "the importance of [interagency] coordination and the importance of proper, cool assessment."

Rimington has no illusions about the terror threat diminishing. "Because of my experience," she says, "I think terrorism isn't going away. There's always going to be terrorism from some cause or another because it's clearly so effective at bringing whatever cause it is to the world's attention."

In saying that, Rimington speaks as an interested spectator rather than a participant in the war on terrorism. "When you're out, you're out," she says. Intelligence work is so all consuming, she adds, that once it's left behind it should stay that way. And Rimington now has fiction as an outlet.

"They're similar in that, to write a novel, you have to be fairly analytic. You have to think through plots and people as you have to when you're an intelligence officer. The other thing, I think, is that a big part of intelligence is getting information from people. To do that, you've got to understand how people work. To that extent, they're similar as well."

Apparently, Rimington finds the similarity congenial. She's already begun work on a second thriller, featuring the heroine of "At Risk" and several of the same characters.
