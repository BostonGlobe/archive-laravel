Inside soccer's dream team

The phenomenon of Real Madrid, Beckham's new gig

By Bill Littlefield  |  February 6, 2005

White Angels: Beckham, Real Madrid and the New Football -- The Inside Story
By John Carlin
Bloomsbury, 340 pp., $24.95

ADVERTISEMENT

When John Carlin claims on the first page of the introduction to ''White Angels" that ''with the arguable exception of the outbreak of war in Iraq . . . the news story with the greatest global impact of 2003 [was] the transfer of the world's most glamorous footballer to the world's most glamorous football club," he may lose numbers of US readers. Lots of folks in this soccer-challenged nation probably know David Beckham as the name in the title of a movie about women who play the game.

But readers who aren't put off by Carlin's inclination toward overstatement will find throughout his book evidence that Beckham and Real Madrid, the star-studded team for which he plays, constitute a phenomenon about which businessmen like New York Yankees owner George Steinbrenner and National Basketball Association commissioner David Stern can only dream.

Consider, for example, that on July 29, 2003, a large crowd of fans in China paid the equivalent of $20 to $100 per ticket to watch a scrimmage between Real Madrid's first team and the club's reserves; that following Beckham's transfer from Manchester United to Real, a Japanese television station began running a weekly five-minute bulletin on developments in Beckham's life; that in the year following Beckham's arrival in Madrid, sales of Real jerseys climbed from 900,000 to over 3 million; that surveys have indicated that Real Madrid (which plays, of course, in Spain) is the most popular soccer team . . . in Italy.

The man most responsible for creating the phenomenon of Real Madrid is the team's president, Florentino Perez, whom Carlin credits with ''smashing long-standing orthodoxies, altering the whole conception of the game." Perez has operated on the assumptions that the best way to keep fans interested in his team is to sign the best players in the world, no matter what they cost, and that their artistic and commercial value will make them bargains in any case. As a result, Real Madrid currently employs not only Beckham but Ronaldo, Zinedine Zidane, Luis Figo, and Roberto Carlos. Together, the club's stars are known as ''Los Galacticos," and Carlin maintains that they have created a level of artistic achievement and fan enthusiasm unprecedented in the history of the game. As evidence he offers more remarkable anecdotes. While pursuing a story about genocide and AIDS in Rwanda, he finds that all the doctors and patients want to talk about is Real Madrid's joyous style. While trolling the Internet, he comes across a story in The Jordan Times by a Lebanese journalist, Hala Jaber, who claims to have witnessed ''young martyrs-in-waiting" with the Palestinian Al-Aqsa Martyrs Brigade chanting ''Allahu akbar!" when they got the news that Beckham had scored two goals.

That's not to say that Perez has no critics. According to Carlin, Uli Hoeness, the general manager of the German club team Bayern Munich, accused Perez of ''turning his football club into a circus act," and Carlo Ancelotti, coaching in Milan, attributed to Real Madrid ''an excessive appetite for the aesthetic." Beyond that, in Beckham's first season with Real Madrid, the club faltered so spectacularly during the final part of the season that Carlin, fan that he is, is compelled to acknowledge that ''never had a team fallen so far, so fast, and so hard from such a great height." At the end of the season, Real had not won a championship.

But Carlin would maintain that it doesn't matter. Winning, as he and Perez see it, is not only not everything, it is inconsequential unless it is accomplished with style and beauty. The triumph must constitute art. That is one of the principles that has driven Perez in his mania for collecting Galacticos, and as far as Carlin is concerned, the undertaking is noble and brave. Real Madrid has, he maintains, ''generated more excitement and fun and dreams around the world than any other group of people engaged in any other activity anywhere."

The team has also generated extraordinary wealth, including an endorsement agreement with Adidas that, according to Real's marketing manager, ''is worth double the deal Reebok has with the entire National Basketball Association," allegedly somewhere over 300 million euros. Would sponsors such as Adidas like Real Madrid to win championships and cups? Of course they would. But here, too, the association with the breathtaking dance of the game at its most magnificent, the unforgettable goal scored when Zidane meets a 40-yard Beckham pass in the air and slams it past a bewildered keeper from 30 yards out, these delights transcend mere winning, at least for the fans and the sponsors of Real Madrid.

Such music will fall on lots of tin ears in the United States, where even people who argue that ''it's how you play the game that counts" are referring to sportsmanship rather than art. But readers here who are curious about this phenomenon the rest of the world knows as ''the beautiful game" have an energetic and enthusiastic guide in Carlin.

Bill Littlefield hosts NPR's ''Only a Game" each Saturday from WBUR in Boston.
