ON CRIME

Dealing with the legacy of the lost

By Hallie Ephron  |  February 27, 2005

The Forgotten Man
By Robert Crais
Doubleday, 342 pp., $24.95

ADVERTISEMENT

Son of a Gun: A Sydney Sloane Mystery
By Randye Lordon
St. Martin’s, 278 pp., $23.95

Busted Flush
By Brad Smith
Holt, 320 pp., $24

Family ties are mangled, and one generation casts a shadow over the next, in this month's crop of mysteries.

The forgotten man of Robert Crais's novel is PI Elvis Cole's long-lost father. As a kid, Cole ran away repeatedly to search for this man, described by his mother, in a moment of lucidity, as a ''human cannonball." Touches like this, bordering on the absurd, make Crais's story hilarious and, at the same time, moving.

Cole's hopes of finding his father are rekindled when he gets a phone call from LAPD detective Kelly Diaz. She tells him of a bandy-legged vagrant, covered with ornate, self-inflicted religious tattoos, found bleeding in an alley. The dying man told her that he was searching for his son, Elvis Cole.

Cole is nonplussed. Curious and skeptical, he goes to the morgue to view the body, looking for a family resemblance. ''The dead man had a head like a praying mantis and I had a head like a rutabaga," he observes with characteristic wry humor.

In this riveting novel with a vivid sense of place -- a Southern California of endless highways, homes perched on tenuous canyon walls, and seedy pink-and-turquoise motels -- Crais sets four different characters in motion. Like freight trains on a collision course, they move forward, relentlessly gaining momentum. The climax doesn't feed off the fireworks of bombs, gun battles, or car chases; what fuels it is the puzzle pieces coming together, and the explosion is realization, by this reader about a half-page before Cole reveals a powerful payoff to 300-plus pages of suspense.

Fans of Lee Child and Robert B. Parker, and anyone who enjoys a well-written, fast-paced, noirish thriller with a great aha! moment, shouldn't miss ''The Forgotten Man."

The subject of ''Son of a Gun," Randye Lordon's latest Sydney Sloane novel, is a bad seed, a child spawned by a violent, incestuous rape. Grown to adulthood, he returns to wreak vengeance on the mother who he feels abandoned him. Lordon's series examines what it means to be family, and this one dissects the ties that bind mother to child, and sibling to sibling.

The mother is Peggy Dexter-Cannady. Already in crisis -- her police-officer husband clings to life in intensive care in the aftermath of a shooting -- Peggy receives a phone call from someone who claims to be the son she gave up for adoption. ''Are you there, Mommy?" he asks, in a parody of filial devotion. He tells her he's been watching her and her daughter Lucy, ''and I am about to make your life a living hell."

Sydney, a New York private investigator who also happens to be a lesbian, is Peggy's good friend and godmother to Lucy. The carefully wrought plot leads Sydney and her colleagues into as many cul-de-sacs as revelations as they team with police to find Peggy's tormentor, whose threats escalate to kidnapping.

Lordon writes riveting stylized violence that is utterly believable. There are affectionate portraits of New York neighborhoods. Characters, even her villains, are fully human. People matter to Sydney, and her wit and intelligence put her in a class with Boston's own Carlotta Carlyle, the sleuth of Linda Barnes's novels.

The hero of ''Busted Flush," by Brad Smith, is running away from ties of any kind. In a first chapter that rivals the laugh-out-loud lyrics of classic country-and-western storytelling songs, Dock Bass departs Cooper Falls in the wake of a crooked poker game, a loveless marriage, and a real estate career that spectacularly self-destructs. The bulldozing of his family home to clear the view for a trophy mansion is the final straw.

He cuts his losses and heads for Gettysburg to collect an inheritance from an unknown relative. It's a ramshackle house and barn on 25 acres -- perfect for a guy who's trying to shed his skin along with the trappings of a material world. He's had it with cheats and hustlers, but what he finds is more of the same, and they come crawling out of the woodwork when he discovers an invaluable trove of vintage Civil War material in a hidden room that's been sealed like a time capsule.

''Busted Flush" alternates between Dock and the far less likable hotshot investigative reporter Amy Morris. She's glamorous and ruthless, a compulsive liar, and ''the love in her life" is a Porsche Cayenne Turbo. Her philosophy is summed up thus: ''Success, maybe, was all about owning things one didn't need." She arrives in Gettysburg with a scam of her own up her sleeve.

The story is funny and compelling, despite a pointless detour into a Chicago Cubs-White Sox World Series (Amy has to cover it) and somewhat overblown characters, including a wisecracking Hungarian professor and a scurrilous Civil War reenactor who conjures visions of an unwashed Hagrid, the Hogwarts gamekeeper of the ''Harry Potter" books. It's the irresistible Dock Bass who keeps it all together. The epitome of laconic wit, he replies when his wife asks what people find so fascinating about history: ''It doesn't change." You've got to love this guy who keeps plugging along, rebuilding that house, nail by nail and board by board, oblivious to greedy charlatans storming its walls.

This is one to kick back and read with a cold beer and Johnny Cash wailing away in the background.

Hallie Ephron is co-author of ''Guilt," the fifth Dr. Peter Zak psychological mystery thriller by G. H. Ephron.
