A READING LIFE

Wriggling out of the ties that bind

By Katharine A. Powers  |  February 20, 2005

Life and the problematical "self" look fairly dismal when viewed through February's gray, pitted lens. What is life? One is born, goes through various entanglements with others, arrives at predictable stages of awareness, then one dies. And self? It is merely the sum of life's parts at any instant. The first inkling of this inessential, ramshackle self, as well as the desire to transform it, to make it not reflexive but free, arises in one's teens or 20s. Exactly when depends, I suppose, on how much one is given to sitting around staring into the middle distance. The discovery, as it seems, that human relations are fetters and that the will is unexercised or dormant -- that "the mass of men lead lives of quiet desperation," in fact -- causes many people to turn to existentialism of one stripe or another, or to peculiar religions. But existentialists, if they persist, turn into bores and bullies, and often enough, peculiar religionists do too.

Tim Guest had the misfortune to be born to an English mother who was not willing to accept the limitations of the human condition and the squalor of self. She sought liberation and fulfillment in LSD, hippie living, Jung, Laing, Marx, radical feminism, and life in an assortment of communes before she discovered the bright light of Bhagwan Shree Rajneesh. This character and his followers, known as "sannyasins," are famous for certain wonders of the 1980s: for the takeover of the town of Antelope, Ore.; for the strewing of salmonella bacteria in salad bars, thereby poisoning over 700 people; for a couple of foiled murder plots; and for those signs and symbols of transcendental accomplishment, Bhagwan's 93 Rolls-Royces. "My Life in Orange: Growing Up With the Guru" (Harcourt, paperback, $14) is Guest's moving, superbly written account of growing up in the midst of this cruel madness.

Convinced that her own restrictive Catholic upbringing had instilled in her a love that "enslaved her to the sufferings of others," Guest's mother, Anne, was open to Bhagwan's idea that people who had children did so to "avoid certain aspects of themselves" and that detachment was a prerequisite for growth. Anne, renamed Vismaya, ditched her son several times in the cult's group homes as she went off on various missions. She felt bad about this at times, but it seemed the necessary price for an evolved soul. As one sannyasin put it on the subject of children: "How could I wish for them, as I did most of all, happiness, freedom, and continual growth, if I didn't allow myself to have it?"

How indeed?

I usually read accounts of crackpots and con men with a festive spirit, but my heart really did sink at the rigors of abandonment, neglect, and loneliness suffered by Guest in the cause of his mother's (and the world's) enlightenment. The children of the sannyasins were fed and clothed, to be sure. "There were hundreds of people joyously washing our clothes, cutting our tofu, rinsing our string beans," he writes. "But none of it was done for us personally." The idea was that intimacy, love, and personal history curdle into self and bind the spirit; but to be at the receiving end, so to speak, of such resolute detachment is a cruel fate for a child. "Bhagwan wanted us to let go; but it felt like all I'd ever done was let go. Bhagwan wanted the children of his communes to be free of history; but if I was to free myself, I needed a history." Unlike many of the children of the sannyasins, the author did not lose himself to drugs or madness, and has proved, in writing this extraordinary memoir, that he does in fact have a history despite Bhagwan's and his deluded mother's best efforts.

Mattia Pascal, the central character in Luigi Pirandello's "The Late Mattia Pascal" (translated, from the Italian, by William Weaver, New York Review Books, paperback, $14), has the great good fortune, as it initially appears, to pull the plug on a personal history that is nothing but trouble. In debt, unhappily married, and put upon by a termagant mother-in-law, he flees the scene of his misery with 500 lire sent to him by his brother for their mother's funeral. Fetching up in Monte Carlo, he makes a killing at the roulette tables and discovers, as he turns reluctantly for home, that he has been declared dead, the badly decomposed body of a suicide having been identified as his.

It occurs to this supremely and comically selfish man that he can create a new life and a new self. He is exhilarated: "I had never looked at mankind or at the world in this way; the air between them and me seemed suddenly free of mist; and the new relations that had to be established between us now seemed easy, light, since I now had to ask little of them to achieve personal satisfaction." He settles on another name, Adriano Meis, and with a good deal of artistic pleasure composes a fictional identity, pausing now and again to consider the difficulty of creating the impression of life's tangled threads without becoming entangled in life: "Witnessing the lives of others, observing them minutely, I saw their infinite ties, and at the same time, I saw all my own snapped threads. Could I take these threads and knot them to reality again? Who knows where they would have drawn me; perhaps they would have been immediately transformed into the reins of runaway horses, hurtling towards the abyss with the poor chariot of my imperative invention. No. Those threads should be tied only to my fantasy."

The problem, of course, is that the "unique, boundless freedom" of this new self is in fact isolation. "As long as I was willing to remain shut up in myself and watch others live, yes, then I could prolong the illusion that I was living a new life." Our hero realizes with disgust that it is his wife, whose history now includes the death of her feckless husband, who has, after all, been freed. I shall go no further into the plot; suffice it to say that paradoxes abound and problems proliferate: for life cannot truly be lived without creating a life, which is to say entanglement. The philosophic dimension of this novel is never a heavy presence. The story is animated and wry and agreeably retributive to its irresponsible hero. All in all, I call "The Late Mattia Pascal" a tonic against February's deforming refraction.
