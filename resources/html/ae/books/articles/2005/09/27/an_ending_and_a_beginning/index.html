An ending and a beginning

September 27, 2005

It is a beautiful truth that all men contain something of the artist in them.-- from ''Talk to an Art-Union (A Brooklyn fragment.)" Walt Whitman

Come, said the Muse,Sing me a song no poet has yet chanted,Sing me the Universal.-- from ''Leaves of Grass"Walt Whitman

Mr. Norwood Rhinehart Beauregard Boyd, age 88, philanthropist, philosopher, and maker of mystery, died on a June morning in Mabel, Mississippi, at home in his bed. His nearest neighbor, House Jackson, age 12, crackerjack baseball pitcher and strong, silent type, found the body, although he didn't tell anyone it was he who dialed Dr. MacRee's office from Mr. Norwood Boyd's telephone to report the death.

When Sheriff Taylor and the undertaker came to take Mr. Norwood Boyd to Snowberger's Funeral Home to be prepared for his laying out and eternal rest, they had to hack through a jungle of kudzu at the gates in order to get the hearse into the driveway, as no one had been through the wide front gates in years. House was crouched inside a giant honeysuckle bush beside the front porch, his heart pounding.

This was the second time in his life that he had seen a dead body and it put him in mind of what had happened six years before at his own home. It was cave-like inside the honeysuckle bush, and from this position, House watched through the leafy branches as Bunch Snowberger and Sheriff Taylor entered and later left the crumbling old house with the wrap-around porch and broken porch swing. They left with Mr. Norwood Boyd between them on a stretcher, covered with a white sheet from head-to-toe.

Mr. Norwood Boyd left behind a collection of black-and-white photographs, a library filled with musty books, and an ancient, pug-nosed, white dog named Eudora Welty. House took Eudora home with him. He told his little sister, Honey, that he had found the dog whining in a thicket, which was true. She had been inside the honeysuckle bush, her head between her paws, her bulgy eyes staring at House with a pitiful look. She had a note attached to her collar, which House stuffed into his pocket. It was addressed to him. Later, some folks would say it was the note that made all the difference for House. Others would say it was the dog. And yes, it was those things. But more than either of them, it was something else. It was the pageant.

Later that same day
''Darlings! Mes amours! Biquettes!" Finesse Schotz -- age 14, skinny as a porch rail, hair twisted into a wiry black propeller on top of her head, black capris stretching around her almost-curves -- sashayed into the crowd sitting under the chinaberry tree behind Hallelujah School and flashed a smile so bright it prompted House to put on his sunglasses.

Finesse stood in the dusty summer heat wearing high-heeled rhinestone sandals and welcomed an assortment of kids of all ages and colors to auditions.

''You are the chosen ones!" she warbled. ''My bijoux!" She dropped her straw tote bag and flung her arms out for effect. Her shiny silver shirt caught the sun and shimmered against her smooth-as-pecan-shell skin.

The chosen ones stared. House, who was sitting as far away from Finesse as possible, pushed his sunglasses up farther on his nose and blinked. Everyone fidgeted. Nervous laughter sifted through the crowd.

''That's right!" said Finesse. She motioned come-on! with her hands. ''Laugh! Cry! Carry on! C'est la vie! It's what we're here for!"

''Not me!" said Honey Jackson. She sat in the dirt near Finesse's painted toenails. She wore a pink tutu and had one arm wrapped around an old pug dog. The dog wore a tutu, too, also pink, around its neck. Honey craned her neck earnestly at Finesse as she stated her purpose: ''I'm here for the tryouts!"

Finesse clasped her hands to her chest and made a face that said her heart was aching with happiness. ''Mais oui! Of course you are!" With the tips of her fingers she slid her glasses onto her face -- they'd been dangling around her neck on a jeweled chain. They were black, shaped like sideways teardrops. She gazed through the lenses, wide-eyed, at the assemblage. She leaned toward them. They leaned back.

''Everyone gets a part," she declared in a confidential tone. ''Everybody! That's tout le monde in French!"

Tout le monde began talking, all at once.

''Of course we all get a part," said Ruby Lavender. ''We're signed up against our will!"

''Well, I'm not!" said Melba Jane Latham. ''Some of us want to help out our hometown! Look at Dove -- she doesn't even live here and she signed up!"

Dove Ishee, visiting from Memphis, pulled a small notebook out of the baggy pocket of her cargo shorts. ''Aunt Tot signed me up," she said, ''but I wouldn't miss this for anything. There's tons of anthropology here!"

Ruby unwrapped a piece of Dubble Bubble. ''I'm happy to help out," she said. ''I just don't want to be in some stupid county anniversary pageant. We should just stick with the operetta in August like we always do."

''We've got a 200th anniversary and that's even better," said Melba Jane. She gazed at Finesse, transfixed. ''And this year, we've got professional help!"

Ruby hooted. Melba Jane ignored her.

Finesse clapped her hands together in tiny motions. Dozens of wispy-thin bracelets clinked against one another on her arms and made a tinkling sound, like little bells that were calling everyone to order.

''Mes amis!" she said. ''Silence, please! Si-lence! Time to get started! Remember, everyone gets a part!" She spoke in crescendo tones. ''This is our pageant celebrating our town!" Her face turned angelic as she said in a come-to-glory voice, ''It's positively Thornton Wilder!" There was a smattering of applause. Kids looked around to see if there was anybody named Thornton Wilder among them, and Finesse made a tiny curtsy.

''She's the picture of poise!" whispered Melba Jane.

''Who is she?" Dove whispered back.

Ruby popped her gum. ''She used to be Frances Schotz." The bubble collapsed onto Ruby's nose. ''She went off to boarding school last year, took too much French and drama, and turned into Marie Antoinette. Where's the guillotine?"

''I think she's amazing," sighed Melba Jane.

''You would," said Ruby, rubbing pink off her nose.

Finesse pulled a clipboard out of her tote bag. ''I'll assign parts as they speak to me . . ."

''I don't want a part!" cried Honey Jackson. ''I want to be a dancer!" She stood up. Her legs looked like little potato sticks poking out from under her tutu. ''House!"

Finesse shaded her eyes with a jeweled hand and peered into the crowd. ''House? House Jackson!" Finesse blew a lofty kiss in House's direction. Boys in the back of the crowd laughed and shoved at House, who tugged his baseball cap lower onto his head.

''Are we still friends, House?" Finesse piped. ''Are you and all my former classmates mes amis, even though I've been absent for a whole year?"

House did not reply. Finesse had never been his friend. She'd been endured, by him and by everyone he knew. He skirted the edge of the dusty crowd. He'd had a matter of hours to get used to the fact that he'd seen a dead body, had made a phone call to report the death, and had adopted a dead man's dog. And now he had to put up with Frances Schotz, the former theatric terror of Mabel Middle School.

He reached for his sister. ''You can dance, Honey." He pulled his sunglasses down his nose a notch and forced himself to look Finesse in the eye. ''Right?"

Finesse winked at House and struck a modern-dance-like pose.

''Mais oui! But of course! We need les danseurs for the danse moderne !" Then she offered a dazzling smile to House alone and asked him, ''How's everyone in middle school, darling?"

''Recovering," said House. He picked up Honey and held her in front of him for a moment, as if he might use her as a shield. She threw her arms around her brother's neck and pulled him close. House's sunglasses popped off his face and landed in the dirt.

''Good garden of peas," said Ruby. She stood up and swiped dust from her overalls. ''I don't care if Mama signed me up for this, and I don't care if I get in trouble for not participating. I'm dancing outta here."

''Ruby!" called Finesse, striking a statuelike pose and pointing at Ruby. ''Is that you, Ruby Lavender? I haven't seen you since I baby-sat for you eons ago! How old are you now?"

''She's eleven!" shouted Honey. ''I'm six! Can I wear tap shoes?"

Ruby's face reddened to the color of her hair. ''Hey, Frances."

''Finesse!" said Finesse. She shined her brilliant white smile on the crowd. ''I have a nom de plume! I have change-ed my name, tout le monde! Murmurs of uncertainty rolled from kid to kid.

''She's worse than ever!" said Cleebo Wilson. ''Let's get out of here." He and the boys near the back of the crowd got to their feet carrying baseball gloves and the occasional bat. ''House!"

Finesse gushed passionate tears as she saw the boys rise. ''Mes danseurs!" she chortled, holding her arms out as if she were offering them the world.

''Not on your life!" said Cleebo. ''House!"

House squinted at Finesse in the noontime glare. His face was strawberry-red with sunburn and sprinkled with freckles. ''I just came to bring Honey, and they came with me."

Finesse looked stricken. The boys made up fully half the crowd. ''You mean, you're not staying? You're not going to help?"

''We don't dance," said House. He put Honey down, retrieved his sunglasses, and shoved them onto the brim of his cap. ''We play ball."

''Ball?" asked Finesse. ''Le baseball?" She made a tsk-tsk! sound. ''Don't tell me you still put together that little rag-tag sandlot team every summer? Honestly, at some point one must grow beyond running around after a silly white ball!"

The little rag-tag sandlot team glared at Finesse. ''House!" they hissed to their leader.

If House had had a silly white ball, he'd have thrown it at Finesse's silly propellered head with pleasure. Instead he threw a few words. ''We got to go."

''Don't leave me, House!" Honey clung to her brother's leg. She stared at Finesse with a tinge of terror in her eyes. ''I don't think she is who you think she is!"

Cleebo had a silly white ball. He tossed it up and up and up and let it thunk and thunk and thunk into the deep pocket it had worn into his glove.

''You've got a part, Honey," said House. ''You're a dancer. Time to go now -- you can come with me and the boys." He whistled at the pug. The dog grunted and struggled to her old feet, her tutu wobbling around her neck.

''I'll come, too!" called Ruby over the heads of a half-dozen others. She fairly leaped over Melba Jane and Dove. ''I'll just run get my glove."

House shook his head. ''No girls allowed."

''Honey's a girl!" said Ruby.

Honey's eyes filled with tears. ''Do I get tap shoes, House?"

''Sure you do," said House. He took his sister's hand. ''Let's go. I'll take you home."

''Aw, House!" called Cleebo. ''We ain't got all day!"

''I'll catch up," said House, even though he had no intention of playing ball this day. He needed to go back to where he'd started the day, back to Mr. Norwood Boyd's home.

Finesse had been studying the papers on her clipboard. Her face contorted in a moment of triumph. ''None of you is going anywhere!" she snapped in perfect, high-pitched English. ''Nobody moves! You're all on my list!"

Tout le monde sucked in its collective breath.
