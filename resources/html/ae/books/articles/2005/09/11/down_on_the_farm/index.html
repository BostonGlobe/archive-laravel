BOOKS

Down on the Farm

Can MaryJane Butters's back-to-basics lifestyle with a Martha Stewart twist really be all that romantic? And a Boston architect appreciates the Vineyard's houses.

September 11, 2005

IMAGINE MARTHA STEWART meets Little House on the Prairie, and you have a sense of the lifestyle movement that MaryJane Butters is trying to launch with the publication of MaryJane's Ideabook, Cookbook, Lifebook: For the Farmgirl in All of Us (Clarkson Potter, 2005, $35). Butters is a self-described "farmgirl," which she defines as a condition of the heart. Her book, however, is more a contrived marketing concept than a sincere study of life on the farm.

Butters says she was obsessed with becoming a farmer for 10 years before she saw a real estate ad reading: "Remote old farmstead, five acres, orchard, and well, $45,000." She bought the property in Moscow, Idaho, 18 years ago, and the investment has worked out well for her. She's raised hardworking children - whom she toilet-trained using an outhouse - as well as chickens, goats, cows, peas, hay, and wheat. She started the Pay Dirt Farm School, which trains farmers in organic-growing techniques, as well as the Farmgirl Connection, www.maryjanesfarm.com/farmgirl-connection, a website where women with or without acreage can share their thoughts.

She's tapped into the momentum generated by the increase in the number of women buying farms. In her introduction, Butters quotes former US agriculture secretary Ann M. Veneman, who has said that women are the fastest-growing segment of farm buyers. In 2002, 27.2 percent of agricultural producers were women, up from 12.6 percent in 1997.

Butters's book addresses connection to the community, environmental concerns, self-sufficiency, and other matters. In her recipe section, the food is so gloriously photographed that putting up the harvest seems a breeze and well worth a try. She proposes that chickens are spiritual as well as being child-friendly pets and advises where to purchase homes for urban chickens. Then there is a photo spread of a romantic wedding staged by the "farm chicks," Teri Edwards and Serena Thompson, two friends from Mead, Washington, who share a passion for antiques and junk that gave rise to a business (www.thefarmchicks.com). And for the kids, there are instructions for simple games such as kick the can and a listing of 10 jump-rope ditties.

While today's American women may not have the same know-how as their foremothers when it comes to keeping house, one has to wonder if this return to the so-called simple life would be all that rewarding. Do today's women want to turn back the clock, washing floors on their hands and knees, using a washboard for scrubbing clothes, or freshening up the out-house? If they were to do all that, would they have time to, as Butters suggests - with complete instructions - make an apron for a broom or fly swatter?

Is there a woman out there cleaning an outhouse who doesn't long for indoor plumbing? And, if so, would she or other farmgirls be willing to part with $35 for this book? -- Janice Byrd

Responding to Place

Boston architect Keith Moskow has summered on Martha's Vineyard for most of his life. He developed an appreciation for the woodlands, beaches, moors, and towns that eventually developed into a curiosity about how architects respond to the island, a subject he explores in his handsome volume The Houses of Martha's Vineyard (The Monacelli Press, 2005, $50).

Despite its celebrity visitors, the Vineyard embraces a simple aesthetic derived from Colonial farmhouses and Victorian cottages. Its signature color is the gray of the weathered cedar in which many of the 24 featured houses are clad.

Several of the houses - all were built between 1983 and 2004 - were designed to respect the landscape and live a bit lighter on the land than conventional homes do.

Peeking into other people's (second or third) houses is a voyeuristic pleasure, and plans and sketches suggest the designers' intentions. Nationally known architects are represented. There is a house by Steven Holl (a boxy structure inspired by whale bones), the Polshek Partnership (a cluster of gable-roof sheds), and Robert A. M. Stern (a dormered, hip-roofed house with broad porches).

Other architects, including local and regional talent, have designed homes for the island, too, with beautiful results. The Teller house by Fernau, Hartman is a creative composition of interlocking volumes united by a screened porch. The Vineyard Sound residence in Aquinnah by Charles Rose Architects is made up of three sleek, freestanding spaces that emphasize a connection to the landscape. The Gay Head house by Kalkin & Co. is the most eclectic; its raised perch and mix of materials break the restraint-in-cedar recipe. The 800-square-foot Pinwheel house by Frederick Noyes is inviting and, as Moskow puts it, "not trying too hard. It looks like a place you'd like to be."

In designing the Arbor house, Moskow and his associate, Robert Linn, were inspired by the site's grape arbor and by fishing nets once used by the Wampanoag. Copper roof shingles are laid in a netlike pattern. The architects calculated thermal mass, overhangs, orientation, and sun angles so that passive heating and cooling (and wood stoves) would suffice.

There are no people in the pictures, which is typical in architectural photography, and it's too bad. Traces help - a hat and towel on a deck - but a glimpse of how people enjoy these places would have conveyed even more of this island's special spirit. -- Kira Gould

Around Town

The American Townhouse by Kevin Murphy (Harry N. Abrams, 2005, $45) is a treat for anyone fond of Boston's architectural landmarks. Murphy, an authority on historic houses, includes the Nichols House, attributed to Charles Bulfinch, the Federal-style William Hickling Prescott House (both in Beacon Hill), and the Italianate Gibson House, a brownstone in the Back Bay, among 25 town houses from around the country.

According to Murphy, the term "town house" is commonly used to distinguish attached houses from freestanding residences. However, he writes that the designation applies to both large city houses that are attached to their neighbors but are separate entities and to some detached urban homes.

These elegant buildings developed in the United States during the Colonial settlement and remained popular through the building and expansion of cities right through the 20th century. The book traces design peculiarities and construction records and offers an engaging history along with stunning photographs of each building. Even if you don't live in a town house or follow American architectural history, Murphy's book is an interesting read. - Janice Byrd
