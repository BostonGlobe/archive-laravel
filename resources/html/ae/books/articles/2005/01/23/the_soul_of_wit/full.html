The soul of wit

By William E. Cain  |  January 23, 2005

Shakespeare After All

By Marjorie Garber

ADVERTISEMENT

Pantheon, 989 pp., $40

Somehow Shakespeare seems to have known in advance the predicaments that would absorb and vex us: the debates and arguments about sex, law, and morality that rack American society today. In "Measure for Measure" Shakespeare presented his own inquiry into versions of them with bitter, wrenching immediacy. The belief in the necessity for preemptive attack and assassination (a word that Shakespeare coined), and the horrors that a preventive war unleashes: Shakespeare depicted these in "Julius Caesar." He is sharp-sighted and quick-witted about every topic and issue, and across all cultures.

Marjorie Garber's book is informed by her appreciation for "the remarkable omnipresence of Shakespeare in our lives." For many years Garber taught a popular Shakespeare course at Harvard University, where she is a professor of English and director of the Humanities Center, and in "Shakespeare After All" she draws upon this experience to provide comprehensive essays on all 38 of the plays. A lifetime of learning has gone into the production of this massive volume. Indeed a number of chapters, including those on "Hamlet" and "King Lear," come close to being books themselves.

Spanning nearly 1,000 pages, however, the detailed coverage that Garber supplies may leave some readers feeling overwhelmed rather than stimulated. She also stretches out the book with long quotations, many of which could have been trimmed or cut altogether. Is it really necessary to quote Matthew Arnold's vapid sonnet about Shakespeare in its entirety and to list the complete "dramatis personae" for every play?

The space occupied by these extensive quotations could have been better used. Garber's opening sections on Shakespeare's life and times, and on the social and theatrical backgrounds, are sketchy and hurried. She doesn't adequately treat Shakespeare's affinities with and differences from Christopher Marlowe, Ben Jonson, and other dramatists of the period, and thus she inadvertently gives the impression that Shakespeare practiced his craft in splendid isolation. It's puzzling, too, that Garber chose not to examine Shakespeare's sonnets and nondramatic poems. That's a significant piece of this writer's achievement to leave out.

A very big book with a narrow focus, "Shakespeare After All" is intended as a reference tool, a compendium, the kind of book to which one might turn for a review of a play that one has not read in a long time or has not read at all. In this respect it will prove more valuable to newcomers and general readers than to critics and theatergoers who know intimately the plays and the key issues in them.

Garber is solid and responsible when she deals with familiar subjects, such as the fairy kingdom of "A Midsummer Night's Dream," the ghost in "Hamlet," the witches in "Macbeth," and sexual identity in "As You Like It" and "Twelfth Night." The basic information is all there. And she gives cogent accounts of historical contexts, as when she examines Shakespeare's representation of ethnicity in "The Merchant of Venice" and of race in "Othello." She also does a skillful job on "Titus Andronicus," "Henry IV Part Two," "Timon of Athens," and other second-tier plays in the canon.

Garber is sensitive to significant details in the language. She points out, for example, that in "Julius Caesar" Shakespeare portrays Mark Antony presenting to the crowd both the bloodstained robe worn by Caesar and the body itself ("marred, as you see, by traitors"), whereas in the source that Shakespeare is adapting, Antony displays only the robe. An actor as well as a playwright, Shakespeare knew that the presence of the slashed and pierced body would make the scene more shocking, dramatically compelling, and unforgettable. Garber astutely comments, too, on Shakespeare's amusing anachronisms, as when Paris in "Troilus and Cressida" refers to Helen of Troy as "my Nell," as though she were an English lass.

But on the whole, "Shakespeare After All" is surprisingly tame. Garber has a lively and risk-taking mind, and she has written a number of adventurous (and controversial) studies of such subjects as cross-dressing and the erotic thrill of "real estate." Here, however, she is unduly limited by the deliberate play-by-play structure and the obligation to cover everything that the consensus of critical opinion has declared to be important. I kept wishing that Garber would say something boldly provocative, even outrageous about some issue or question (e.g., are there any plays that she dislikes or hates to teach?), which would give "Shakespeare After All" the intellectual edge and flair that her other books put so prominently on display.

There's no escaping Shakespeare -- Garber is certainly right about that: He is remarkably present in our lives. His characters and stories illuminate fundamental facts and conditions of life, and we are dependent on him for many perceptions that we assume are our own. The nearly self-crippling labor of reaching an inescapable but apparently impossible decision: In "Hamlet" Shakespeare delineates this process. The lacerating cruelty that can overtake parents and children: Is there any grimmer rendering of this than "King Lear"? In a deep, pervasive sense, Shakespeare has given us the terms, the insights, and the feelings through which we perceive human experience.

In a sense everyone is a performer of Shakespeare, and that is because his images, words, and phrases saturate the English language. Eaten out of house and home; pomp and circumstance; foregone conclusion; full circle; neither rhyme nor reason; one fell swoop; seen better days; a sorry sight; a spotless reputation; strange bedfellows: Shakespeare furnished us with hundreds of phrases like these, and he introduced some 1,700 words into the English language. It is bracing, and perhaps unnerving, to realize that much of the time we speak and act in the world that Shakespeare has made.

William E. Cain teaches in the English Department at Wellesley College.
