BOOK REVIEW

Unflattering Pavarotti bio hits more low notes than high ones

By Richard Dyer, Globe Staff  |  January 20, 2005

The King & I: The Uncensored Tale of Luciano Pavarotti’s Rise to Fame by His Manager, Friend, and Sometime Adversary, By Herbert Breslin and Anne Midgette, Doubleday, 320 pp., $25.95

For 36 years the pushy publicist and manager Herbert Breslin served Luciano Pavarotti for love and for money. They parted company two years ago, and now Breslin has written a racy, amusing, vindictive, and unpleasant memoir in association with New York Times critic Anne Midgette.

Breslin's portrait of the most popular classical singer of the second half of the 20th century is intermittently sympathetic but seldom flattering. "The story of Luciano Pavarotti is the story of a very beautiful, simple, lovely guy who turned into a very determined, aggressive, and somewhat unhappy superstar," he writes.

The tenor comes off as demanding, insecure, childish, extravagant, gluttonous, lazy, superstitious, and vain. Fat, too. Diva Joan Sutherland puts him in his place. "Joan, we fat people know how it is," Pavarotti says. The Australian diva replies, "Luciano, we are not fat. You are fat. I am big." Breslin shares his privileged view of the tenorissimo in the steam room. "Believe me, it's quite a sight. It's very hard to sit there and imagine that this is a figure capable of any great sexual prowess. Put it this way: He's hardly the answer to a maiden's prayer."

Maybe maidens weren't interested in Pavarotti, but a lot of women were. Breslin is, for him, kindly toward the soprano Madelyn Renee, who served as Pavarotti's protge for a while; once, in a Symphony Hall recital, the tenor pulled her onstage to sing a duet. Breslin felt close to Pavarotti's first wife, Adua, and gives short shrift to some of the other women in his life, especially his current wife, Nicoletta. ("I think she's as dull as dishwater.")

The story he tells of Pavarotti's rise to superstardom is compelling and disturbing. Breslin is properly proud of his ideas -- of starting Pavarotti in the recital business, launching him in arena concerts, getting him on television through special events like the spectacular trio concert with Pavarotti, Sutherland, and Marilyn Horne. The movie "Yes, Giorgio" was not a bad idea, although it didn't turn out well, and Breslin devotes an amusing chapter to the adventures of the tenor in Hollywood. Breslin had less to do with the Three Tenors phenomenon, although he profited from it and hangs out all the dirty laundry for inspection. The fees rose to $1 million a night, and Breslin got his cut.

As the decades passed, Breslin had less to do with the direction Pavarotti moved in, and he deplores it without seriously questioning the role that big money played. There's no question that Pavarotti reached a vast audience of people unfamiliar with and uninterested in opera; some of those people joined the operatic audience, and that was a good thing.

But other aspects of Pavarotti's career have had a deplorable effect on the music business as a whole. Managers, including Breslin, tried to duplicate the Pavarotti effect with other artists ("The Three Sopranos"), and too many people grew besotted by the idea of how much money could be made in the business if you ignored its core values -- which is something Pavarotti didn't do. He soldiered on in opera long after he could have quit, and maybe should have. Breslin is not given to reflection and self-questioning; he told people how much his client was worth and made sure there was something in it for them both.

The book is full of viciously quotable remarks about other figures in the musical world. Conductor James Levine earns some respect, but not many others do. Kiri te Kanawa is "boring"; offstage, he says, the glamorous German soprano Elisabeth Schwarzkopf looked like she'd come to do the floors. In Breslin's view, Renee Fleming, a onetime client, has succeeded because of the absence of competition. Pavarotti's rival Placido Domingo lacked a high C -- and failed to listen to Breslin. "In his dreams, Placido never had a voice like" Pavarotti's.

All this -- 320 pages of it -- is entertaining and titillating, and Breslin is certainly a formidable personality. It's hard not to feel a sneaking admiration for a writer who doesn't care what you think of him and who will allow his subject a bit of one-upmanship. At one of his last meetings with Pavarotti, Breslin was accompanied by his coauthor. "I am writing a book," Breslin said. "You are not writing a book," Pavarotti replied, gesturing toward Midgette. "She is writing a book." With generosity, Pavarotti granted an interview to Midgette to conclude the book, and in more recent interviews has refused to be drawn into mudslinging, preferring with dignity to thank Breslin again for his role in advancing his early career.

And Breslin can give credit where credit is due: "that romantic voice he had . . . pulled you in with this incredible sound. To the best of my knowledge, that's something nobody else can do like Luciano: make love to you with the sound of his voice." Breslin fell under its spell, too, but not far enough. There are some things you should not do to people you have cared about.
