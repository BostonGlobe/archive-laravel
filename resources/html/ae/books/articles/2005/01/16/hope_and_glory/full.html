Hope and Glory

A flawed look at JFK's 1961 inaugural speech, touchstone for a generation

By Martin F. Nolan  |  January 16, 2005

Ask Not: The Inauguration of John F. Kennedy and the Speech That Changed America
By Thurston Clarke
Holt, 252 pp., illustrated, $25

ADVERTISEMENT

Since that cold, crisp day so long ago, 10 presidential inaugural speeches have filled the ether over Capitol Hill, forgettable sermons all. It was different on Jan. 20, 1961, when John Fitzgerald Kennedy, 43 years old, told America and the world that "the torch has been passed to a new generation of Americans."

Generations also pass. It is a demographic fact that those Americans who voted for Kennedy in 1960 are now eligible for Social Security. The literature about JFK and "the New Frontier" to which he summoned Americans has relied on memoir, revisionist argument, and rebuttal. A less traumatic and even less comprehensive approach is a worthwhile effort. Thurston Clarke offers an intriguing, flawed look at the era in "Ask Not: The Inauguration of John F. Kennedy and the Speech That Changed America."

Following two decades of war and cold war, Kennedy caused change by reviving hope and optimism. A French diplomat told columnist Drew Pearson after the speech, "We are so glad that the old America is coming back again." Clarke's wealth of such detail is no substitute for historical perspective. Gossip and trivia obscure the author's judgment of JFK's style and substance.

Whatever the faults of the speech, it was unlike most presidential inaugurals, free of cant and clich. Kennedy's role model was Winston Churchill, the World War II British prime minister who offered "blood, toil, tears and sweat." (Kennedy's father was US ambassador in London before the war.) Fred Dutton, a JFK aide, noticed Kennedy listening to a record of Churchill speeches in 1960. The result, Clarke, writes, was "pay any price, bear any burden, meet any hardship, oppose any foe," Kennedy's way of "replicating the cadences of 'we shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets.' "

Churchillian cadences also influenced policy. JFK's words, carved in granite above Kennedy's gravesite, foreshadowed counterinsurgency, the Green Berets, and escalating the US role in Vietnam. "The business of bearing any burden and taking on any foe was great at the time," Clarke quotes George McGovern as saying, "but looking back on it, I think it was really quite arrogant and extravagant." Tom Wicker, who covered Kennedy for The New York Times, later called the speech "bellicose."

Clarke's book focuses on the speech itself and on Kennedy's habit of picking the brains of Gore Vidal, Adlai Stevenson, Arthur Schlesinger, John Kenneth Galbraith, and others. Kennedy sought a quotable "master sentence" and found it in his call to sacrifice: "Ask not what your country can do for you: Ask what you can do for your country."

Every day at the JFK Library, visitors view with awe or nostalgia a streaming video of JFK's 1961 speech. The inaugural resonates with religiosity, providing enough biblical citations to alarm today's secular liberal Democrats. It ends with Kennedy "asking His blessing and His help, but knowing that here on earth God's work must truly be our own." As Clarke points out, "This was Kennedy's God, a deity who gives us freedom and judges us on how well we use it."

After the election, Kennedy, the first Catholic elected president, conspicuously invited Rev. Billy Graham, the Protestant preacher, to golf with him in Palm Beach, Fla. This raises the question of whether Sen. John F. Kerry should have invited Rev. Jerry Falwell to go windsurfing last summer.

Kennedy and his chief speechwriter, Theodore C. Sorensen, favored antithetical cadences, which Galbraith saw as a way to diminish the speech's bellicosity. "Galbraith's great contribution," Clarke writes, "was a stirring, Kennedyesque, antithetical statement: 'We shall never negotiate out of fear. But we shall never fear to negotiate.' " The phrase, slightly altered, set the path for future talks on arms control. Galbraith later complained that "a ghost-writer is like an unloved dog in a poor family. He must be content with scraps." As Churchill might have said: some dog, some scrap.

Kennedy did not fret about his "mandate." In 1960, 34.2 million Americans voted for him, a nationwide plurality of fewer than 115,000 over Vice President Richard M. Nixon. His inaugural affirmed Kennedy's belief that history is "the final judge of our deeds." This speech belonged to him, not to Sorensen, who provided a debatable amount of help to Kennedy's 1956 book, "Profiles in Courage." Clarke documents how witnesses like Hugh Sidey of Time magazine saw the president-elect writing his own speech.

After the speech, Norman Thomas, six times the Socialist Party's presidential candidate, sent the new president a telegram saying "It will go down in history with Lincoln's and Roosevelt's." JFK's presidency lasted only until Nov. 22, 1963, but the forecast was accurate.

What Lincoln's first inaugural called "the mystic chords of memory" echo for only a few inaugural speeches. Jefferson diminished factional strife after the election of 1800 by saying "We are all Republicans, we are all Federalists." Lincoln's "with malice toward none" in 1865 was not matched in memory until 1933, when Franklin D. Roosevelt said "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself." FDR's inaugural was filmed by newsreel cameras. By 1961, television focused on "the perfect man for a time when Americans were teetering on a balance point between image and word," as Clarke writes.

"Ask Not" is the product of much diligence, but not enough. Clarke's errors include placing FDR's "rendezvous with destiny" at an inaugural speech in Washington, instead of Philadelphia in 1936. Joe Kennedy threw a pre-inaugural party for his son at Paul Young's restaurant, not Joe Young's.

The author has made a valuable start by evoking the vivid spirit of that time and documenting a major moment in American oratory. But historians still must assess JFK's place in history. That legacy remains encrusted with grief and sentiment; but as he said that day, let us begin.

Martin F. Nolan is a former Washington bureau chief for the Globe.
