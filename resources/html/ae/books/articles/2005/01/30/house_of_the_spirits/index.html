House of the spirits

In 1940s Brooklyn, a group of artists shared a home, and an urgent desire to follow their muse

By Denis Donoghue  |  January 30, 2005

February House
By Sherill Tippins
Houghton Mifflin, 317 pp., illustrated, $24

Paul Muldoon's collection of poems ''Meeting the British" (1987) includes a poem called ''7, Middagh Street," a sequence of monologues in voices named Wystan, Gypsy, Ben, Chester, Salvador, Carson, and Louis. It soon emerges that these belong to W. H. Auden, Gypsy Rose Lee, Benjamin Britten, Chester Kallman, Salvador DalÃ­, Carson McCullers, and Louis MacNeice. The justification for assembling such a bizarre medley of people in one poem is that they lodged -- or at least visited -- at various times during 1940-41 in a rooming house, 7 Middagh St., Brooklyn Heights, N.Y. Kallman didn't live there, but he was a frequent visitor. Muldoon got most of his information, he tells me, from Humphrey Carpenter's biography of Auden and from ''Literary New York: A History and Guide," by Susan Edmiston and Linda D. Cirino. Now Sherill Tippins has written the story of the house in much more elaborate terms.

From June 1940 to December 1941 the house was a commune, colloquially called February House because several of the residents were born in that month. The true begetter of the commune was George Davis, a writer of sorts but a gifted editor when he put his mind to the job. He was fiction editor at Harper's Bazaar, always on the edge of quitting or being sacked but in the meantime helping good writers to get published. The first step was to give them a home away from home at 7 Middagh St. Then someone had to be persuaded to pay the bills: Often this was Lincoln Kirstein. Davis chose 7 Middagh St. because he had a dream of finding a house for rent, cheap and dilapidated but with possibilities, preferably in Brooklyn Heights, a place congenial to artists, notably homosexual artists. Tippins puts Davis at the center of the book, where he deserves to be. He was an irregular writer, but something of a visionary and a strong enabler. He was especially helpful to McCullers when she was trying to write ''The Member of the Wedding" and ''The Ballad of the Sad CafÃ©," and to Gypsy Rose Lee in her daytime hours writing ''The G-String Murders."

Auden, Britten, and McCullers were the major residents. In 1940 and thereafter Auden, Christopher Isherwood, and Britten were reviled in England as traitors and cowards who abandoned their country and lit out for peaceful America before Hitler's bombs started falling. In January 1939 Auden and Isherwood sailed on the Champlain for New York and new lives. Many English people still withhold forgiveness. Philip Larkin later claimed, with evident satisfaction, that Auden's poetry did not survive his becoming an American. Britten lived in Auden's shadow, but he was soon befriended by Elizabeth Mayer and other friends of the arts on Long Island and provided with pianos and quiet rooms. Auden and Britten worked on an ill-advised opera based on the life and deeds of Paul Bunyan. At 7 Middagh St., Auden was the chief presence. In clothes and personal hygiene he was a mess, but at least he intuited what the social graces would be if they could be elicited and sustained. Davis was the one who kept the house more or less intact, the rent more or less paid, while Auden contributed a constant mixture of anxiety, theology, wisdom, and lovesickness.

Kallman, no great shakes as a writer, was the love of Auden's life, but hopelessly promiscuous, always on the lookout for a good-looking sailor. Lee put in her days and nights doing what by all reports she was good at.

Tippins gives as much detail as anyone could want about these lives in that long year. She extends Muldoon's range of reference, telling us about the other artists who roomed in the house for various short periods. These included Britten's partner, Peter Pears; McCullers's often-estranged husband, Reeves; Richard Wright; June and Paul Bowles; Salvador DalÃ­; and Erika Mann. The list of visitors and dinner guests runs to Janet Flanner, Aaron Copland, Kirstein, Colin McPhee, Louis Untermeyer, and many more. Lively company, most of them, and Tippins's account of them is interesting. She hasn't much to say about communes, or whether such companies are really much of a help to the individual artist. She takes up the story of each personage as it arises, and tells it pretty well. Most of her sentences are respectable, but she often finds her style in Manhattan between the theater district and Fashion Avenue. (Marc Blitzstein's ''The Cradle Will Rock" was ''wildly successful," the first run-through of ''Paul Bunyan" was ''wildly improvised," the revival of Kurt Weill's ''The Threepenny Opera" in 1954 was ''wildly successful," John Latouche's ''Ballad for Americans" was ''wildly popular," many of the company at Middagh Street were ''dazzlingly famous," and Jane Bowles's ''Two Serious Ladies" is ''an utterly original, uniquely feminine literary work.") Sometimes Tippins fudges, extending the narrative beyond December 1941 and the house in Brooklyn. Britten did not get the idea of ''Peter Grimes" while he roomed on Middagh Street.

Tippins associates the end of the commune with the attack on Pearl Harbor and America's going to war. The several artists went their different ways. Britten and Pears went back to England, where their claims to the status of conscientious objector were accepted. Auden stayed in America, won the prizes, and became an American citizen. The house on Middagh Street was pulled down in 1945 to make way for the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway.

Denis Donoghue is University Professor and Henry James Professor of English and American Letters at New York University. His most recent book is ''Speaking of Beauty."
